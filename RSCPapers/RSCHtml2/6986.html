Abstract

Ultrathin layered double hydroxide (LDH) nanosheets are a promising candidate as the electrode material for energy storage due to the ultrafast mass diffusion and greater specific surface area. As a kind of emerging electrode material, metal nitride plays a vital role in improving the poor conductivity of most supercapacitors. Herein, we design a novel three-dimensional (3D) embedded hierarchical coreâ€“shell nanostructure by combining ultrathin CoFe-LDH nanosheets (âˆ¼2.5 nm) with porous Cu3N nanowire arrays (NWAs), in which the ultrathin CoFe-LDH nanosheets are grown from the interior of Cu3N nanowire cores supported on Cu foam (denoted as Cu3N@CoFe-LDH). The prepared Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWA electrode exhibits a high areal capacitance of 3078 mF cmâˆ’2 at a current density of 1 mA cmâˆ’2, excellent rate capacity (85.8% capacity retention at 20 mA cmâˆ’2) and superior cycling stability (93.9% retention after 10000 cycles), making it significantly superior to the related CuO@CoFe-LDH NWA electrodes. Additionally, the constructed asymmetric supercapacitor (ASC) devices deliver an ultrahigh energy density of 2.474 mWh cmâˆ’3 and a robust cycling stability (92.6% retention after 10000 cycles). The outstanding electrochemical performance can be credited to the significant enhancement of the specific surface area, charge transport, and mechanical stability by the ultrathin LDH shell, the highly conductive Cu3N core, and the distinctive embedded coreâ€“shell nanostructure.

1. Introduction

In light of the sharp increase in the consumption of fossil fuels globally and the growing environmental pollution problem, there is an urgent demand for clean, efficient, and renewable energy resources. Certainly, sustainable electrochemical energy storage devices, such as batteries and supercapacitors (also called electrochemical capacitors), have become the critical components for full utilization of the green and renewable but intermittent conventional energy sources such as solar, wind and tide.1â€“4 Among various electrochemical energy storage devices, supercapacitors have shown extraordinary prospects for application in hybrid electric vehicles, portable electronic devices and communication in recent years by the inherent virtue of high power density, rapid chargeâ€“discharge, and long cycle life.5â€“7 Based on the type of charge-storage mechanism, supercapacitors are further classified into electric double-layer capacitors (EDLCs) and pseudocapacitors. Compared with electric double-layer capacitors, pseudocapacitors show enhanced energy density by introducing a continuous and reversible redox (faradaic) reaction on the electrode surface.8,9 Additionally, pseudocapacitors generally combine non-faradaic electrostatic charge storage (electric double-layer) with redox reactions to get larger capacitance.10â€“13 As a kind of emerging pseudocapacitive electrode material, transition metal nitrides or carbides that are superior to metal oxides in terms of electrical conductivity (4000â€“55500 S cmâˆ’1) have successfully drawn plenty of attention from researchers in recent years.10,11,14 So far, there have been a lot of efforts devoted to investigating pseudocapacitive transition metal nitrides, including TiN,15,16 VN,17 and Fe2N.18,19

Cu based materials have drawn particular attention as promising pseudocapacitive electrode materials due to their unique physicochemical properties, low cost, abundance, easy preparation, and environmental friendliness.20â€“22 Especially, CuO and Cu2O with nanostructure arrays such as nanowires,23,24 nanorods25,26 and nanosheets27,28 have fast mass transfer and high structural stability as electrode materials. Unfortunately, their poor electrical conductivity and inferior rate capability limit their widespread commercial application.10,29 On the other hand, the limited specific surface area of nanostructure arrays further influences their electrochemical performance. Therefore, an appropriate design to improve the electrical conductivity of Cu-based materials and enhance the specific surface area with more active sites will be vital for pseudocapacitive materials. Cu3N is a cubic semiconductor material with an anti-ReO3 crystalline structure that is attracting increasing interest for application in multiple areas.14,30 Due to possessing the merits of superior electrical conductivity and structural stability compared with Cu-based oxides, Cu3N is expected to be the most promising pseudocapacitive electrode material. However, few reports on Cu3N-based materials as pseudocapacitive electrode materials are available. Layered double hydroxides (LDHs), which are composed of positively charged brucite-like host layers and charge compensating interlayer anions, have been used as promising candidates in electrochemical energy storage/conversion owing to their tunable chemical composition, cost effectiveness and environmentally friendly properties.31â€“34 Among them, Coâ€“Fe LDH as an electrode material has been drawing more and more attention, because the co-existence of Co and Fe ions in the host layers can provide abundant redox reactions due to their combined contributions during the electrochemical processes.35 The CoFe-LDH with the nanosheet structure shows remarkably higher energy density through multiple faradaic (redox) reactions owing to the high specific surface area and ultrathin thickness.36â€“38

Rational design of the electrode architecture is a powerful method to promote the electrochemical performance of supercapacitors. Typically, 3D hierarchical coreâ€“shell nanostructures grown on conductive substrates are of great interest due to their large surface area, efficient electron transfer, and intimate access to the electrolyte.39 Li et al. reported hierarchical CuO@CoFe-LDH coreâ€“shell NWAs supported on Cu wire as a self-standing electrode for pseudocapacitors.24 Compared with a CuO metal oxide core, Cu3N NWs are much more conductive and have a stable structure. Thus, we propose a novel 3D hierarchical composite nanostructure of Cu3N@CoFe-LDH coreâ€“shell NWAs, in which the inner porous Cu3N cores not only serve as highly conductive layers to provide reliable electron transport but also contribute partial capacitance as an electrode material, and ultrathin CoFe-LDH nanosheets (NSs) are used as a shell framework embedded in the Cu3N NWA interior to enhance the specific surface area and the stability of the whole structure. The synthesis process of Cu3N@CoFe-LDH coreâ€“shell NWAs supported on a Cu foam substrate is illustrated in Scheme 1. We can see that these 3D embedded Cu3N@CoFe-LDH coreâ€“shell NWAs possess better mechanical integrity since the Cu3N NWs are directly grown on the Cu foam, and the CoFe-LDH shell is firmly embedded in the interior of the Cu3N NWA core, ensuring good electronic transport. Additionally, the ultrathin CoFe-LDH NSs were vertically interlaced and distributed on the Cu3N NWAs, leaving lots of active sites and accelerating the diffusion of the electrolyte. Clearly, the 3D embedded coreâ€“shell nanostructures can provide large surface area with increased exposure of active sites, efficient electron transport from the inner Cu3N core to the surrounding CoFe-LDH shell, and stable mechanical architecture of the whole 3D coreâ€“shell system. Consequently, the as-obtained Cu3N@CoFe-LDH coreâ€“shell NWA electrode displays outstanding electrochemical performance with high areal capacitance (3078 mF cmâˆ’2 at a current density of 1 mA cmâˆ’2), superior rate capability and longer cycling lifespans. Finally, a flexible asymmetric supercapacitor was fabricated and it exhibited admirable areal energy density and cycling stability.

2. Experimental section

2.1 Synthesis of the Cu(OH)2 and Cu3N NWAs

The Cu3N NWAs were synthesized by two simple steps. Firstly, precursor Cu(OH)2 NWAs were prepared by a typical alkaline corrosion method.40 Then, a piece of high-purity Cu foam (1 Ã— 1 cm2) was pretreated with 2 M HCl solution, acetone, and absolute ethanol (each for 20 min) by ultrasonication, respectively, to ensure a clean surface. An alkaline oxidative etchant solution (AOES) was prepared by dissolving 1.826 g (NH4)2S2O8 in 44 mL deionized water (0.182 M) and mixing with 16 mL freshly prepared NaOH solution (10 M). Then the Cu foam was immersed into the above solution mixture. After 10 min, a light blue film, precursor Cu(OH)2 NWAs, appeared on the Cu foam surface; it was taken out from the solution, thoroughly rinsed with deionized water and dried in air. Subsequently, Cu3N NWAs on Cu foam were formed by annealing Cu(OH)2 NWAs at 350 Â°C for 2 h in a flow of NH3. For comparison, CuO NWAs on Cu foam were also fabricated by annealing Cu(OH)2 NWAs at 180 Â°C for 2 h in air. The mass loading of Cu3N NWAs on Cu foam was calculated to be about 1.04 mg per square centimetre by weighing the sample before and after HCl solution corrosion.

2.2 Synthesis of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH coreâ€“shell NWAs

The Cu3N@CoFe-LDH coreâ€“shell NWAs were fabricated by a facile electrodeposition method. In a three-electrode configuration, the Cu foam supported Cu3N NWAs were used as the working electrode, Pt foil was used as the counter electrode and an Ag/AgCl electrode was used as the reference electrode. A 50 mL solution mixture of FeSO4Â·7H2O (0.05 M) and Co(NO3)2Â·6H2O (0.05 M) was used as the electrolyte for electrodeposition of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH coreâ€“shell structure. The potentiostatic deposition was carried out at a potential of âˆ’1.0 V vs. Ag/AgCl at room temperature (25 Â°C), and the deposition time was in the range of 0â€“200 s. Subsequently, the Cu supported Cu3N@CoFe-LDH coreâ€“shell NWAs were taken out and rinsed with distilled water. The mass loading of the Coâ€“Fe LDH was determined by weighing the sample before and after coating.

2.3 Preparation of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWAs//AC asymmetric supercapacitor (ASC) device

A dip-coating method was used to synthesize the AC electrode. Typically, active carbon, acetylene black, and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) were mixed with ethanol in a mass ratio of 8:1:1 to produce a slurry. Acetylene black and PVDF were used as a conductive agent and binder, respectively. The resulting mixture was then painted onto the surface of a nickel foam substrate. Lastly, the manufactured electrodes were dried at 60 Â°C for 8 h in a vacuum drying oven. The mass-loading of the electrode material was controlled by repeating the above deposition process for several cycles. The Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWAs//AC ASC device (1 Ã— 1 Ã— 0.1 cm3) was fabricated by assembly of a thin PE filter (thickness: 150 Î¼m, Japan) membrane between the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWA positive electrode and the AC negative electrode face-to-face, followed by combination with 2 M KOH as the electrolyte and encapsulation with Kapton plastic film.

2.4 Materials characterization

The crystal structure information of these samples was obtained by X-ray powder diffraction (XRD, PANalytical Xâ€² PertPRO) and Raman spectrometry (LabRAM HR JY-Evolution, 532 nm). The morphological characterization was done by scanning electron microscopy (SEM, JEOLJSM-6700F) with an accelerating voltage of 20 kV, combined with energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) for elemental identification. The analysis of elements and valence states was done by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, EscaLab 250Xi). The microstructural characterization was done by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and images were recorded with Philips Tecnai 20 and JEOL JEM-2010 high-resolution transmission electron microscopes.

2.5 Electrochemical performance measurements

For the single electrode test, the electrochemical performance was evaluated on a CorrTest Electrochemical workstation (CHI660E) in a three-electrode system using a 2 M KOH solution as an electrolyte at room temperature. The Cu foam supported Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWAs (Cu3N mass: âˆ¼1.04 mg, CoFe-LDH mass: âˆ¼1.76 mg) served as the working electrode, and Pt foam and an Ag/AgCl electrode served as the counter electrode and reference electrode, respectively. The distance between the working electrode and the counter electrode was 2 cm. Cyclic voltammetry (CV), galvanostatic chargeâ€“discharge (GCD), electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) and electrochemically active surface area (ECSA) measurements were carried out. Finally, the electrochemical properties of the self-assembled ASC device were investigated employing a two-electrode system.

3. Results and discussion

Scheme 1 illustrates the synthesis strategy for Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWAs, and shows a facile three-step procedure. Firstly, the Cu(OH)2 NWAs were in situ fabricated on the Cu foam substrate by the typical wet chemical method with an alkaline oxidative etchant solution (AOES) at room temperature. Afterwards, the obtained Cu(OH)2 NWAs transform into Cu3N NWAs through an nitridation process under an NH3 flow. Subsequently, the CoFe-LDH NSs were grown uniformly on the Cu3N nanowire (NW) skeleton by an electrodeposition method, forming the embedded hierarchical Cu3N@CoFe-LDH coreâ€“shell nanostructure supported on the Cu foam substrate.

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) were then employed to investigate the morphological and structural evolution. As shown in Fig. 1a, the SEM images of the as-prepared Cu(OH)2 NWAs reveal vertical and high density nanowire arrays in situ growing on the surface of Cu foam, with a uniform diameter in the range of 150â€“200 nm and a length distribution of 9â€“10 Î¼m (Fig. S1aâ€ ). Significantly, the morphology of vertical nanowires almost doesn't change when the Cu(OH)2 NWAs transformed into Cu3N NWAs (Fig. 1b) by a nitridation procedure. It should be noticed that the formed Cu3N NWs are composed of nanoparticles, which results in porous Cu3N NWs as shown in Fig. 1c. Finally, after coating with CoFe-LDH NSs by an electrodeposition route with an optimized scheme, the Cu3N NWAs evolved into the target product, Cu3N@CoFe-LDH coreâ€“shell NWAs with a vertical and intercrossing ultrathin CoFe-LDH NS shell (âˆ¼120 nm in lateral size and âˆ¼2.5 nm in thickness) shown in Fig. 1d and e. The electrodeposition process of the LDH shell was further studied by controlling the reaction time. It is found that the electrodeposition time (0â€“200 s) of the LDH shell imposes a significant influence on the shell thickness and morphology (Fig. S2aâ€“dâ€ ): with increasing electrodeposition time, the LDH shells become much thicker and denser so that the sample surface is full of cracks when the electrodeposition time is 200 s. Thus, we selected an electrodeposition time of 100 s as the experimental condition for the following samples. TEM measurement was then performed to further confirm the Cu3N@LDH coreâ€“shell nanostructure. According to Fig. 1f and g, the coreâ€“shell structure with the Cu3N NW core consisting of nanoparticles and an ultrathin LDH NS shell was identified clearly. The diameter of the Cu3N NW core is âˆ¼100 nm and CoFe-LDH NSs have a lateral size of âˆ¼150 nm. Meanwhile, the thickness of ultrathin CoFe-LDH NSs is determined to be only 2.5 nm or even less than 2.5 nm in terms of the crimped part of LDH NSs as shown in the enlarged image (Fig. 1h). Since the thickness of a single layer LDH is about 0.8 nm,41 the CoFe-LDH NSs consist of around 3 layers or less in our samples. It is worth noting that the ultrathin CoFe-LDH NSs grew from the interior of the Cu3N NW core, which profits from the porous Cu3N NW composed of nanoparticles. This special growth mechanism stabilized the coreâ€“shell structure and enhanced the electron transfer from the shell to the core, thus improving the electrochemical performance. As shown by the lattice fringes (Fig. 1g inset), the d-spacing from high resolution TEM (HRTEM) is âˆ¼0.25 nm, corresponding to the (012) plane of CoFe-LDH. The selected area electron diffraction (SAED) image reveals a characteristic polycrystalline structure (Fig. 1i), which can be indexed to the (110), (101), and (100) planes belonging to trigonal hydroxide structures, and an oriented growth along the [001] direction.42,43 Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) provides the distribution of elements (Fig. S3â€ ), from which the elemental contents of Cu, Co, Fe and N of Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWs are found to be 62%, 5.5%, 4.87% and 27.61%, respectively. The atomic ratio of Fe to Co is very close to 1:1, and the Cu:N atomic ratio is close to 3:1, corresponding to molecular Cu3N.

As a significant gauge, galvanostatic chargeâ€“discharge (GCD) measurement was then employed in the potential range of 0â€“0.45 V to characterize the capacitance performance of the samples. Fig. 3c shows the GCD curves of CuO NWAs, Cu3N NWAs, and Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWAs at the same current density (1 mA cmâˆ’2), respectively. The chargeâ€“discharge characteristics of all these samples exhibit a typical pseudo-capacitive behavior, consistent with the results of CV. The corresponding areal capacitances are calculated to be 3078, 934, and 596 mF cmâˆ’2 for the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWAs, Cu3N NWAs, and CuO NWAs, respectively, demonstrating the high areal capacitance of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWA sample. The enhanced areal capacitance attained (for Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWAs) is much higher than those of similar supercapacitor electrodes in other previous reports (Table S1â€ ). Fig. 3d exhibits the GCD curves of Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWAs at different current densities (1â€“20 mA cmâˆ’2). At a current density of 1 mA cmâˆ’2, the calculated corresponding areal capacitance is 3078 mF cmâˆ’2 and a high areal capacitance of 2641 mF cmâˆ’2 (85.8%) is still retained at a high current density of 20 mA cmâˆ’2. Corresponding to the CV result, GCD also confirms the superior areal capacitance of Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWAs. In addition, the electrodeposition time of the LDH shell also has an evident impact on the supercapacitive performance of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH samples. The areal capacitance of Cu3N@CoFe-LDH increases with prolonging the deposition time of LDH and reaches a maximum of 3078 mF cmâˆ’2 at a deposition time of 100 s. However, as the deposition time continues to increase, the capacitance begins to decrease (Fig. S5â€ ), which can be explained by the fact that the caking of the LDH shell leads to a reduced exposure of active sites and hinders the charge transfer.

Rate capability is a key factor for evaluating the power applications of supercapacitors, and is the areal capacitance calculated as a function of the discharge current density. Fig. 3e shows the rate capability curves of the three samples derived from the GCD curves at various chargeâ€“discharge current densities. Obviously, over the whole current density range, the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH displays a much higher areal capacitance than Cu3N and CuO at a high current density of 20 mA cmâˆ’2 (Fig. S6â€ ); the areal capacitance of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH sample remains at 85.8%, which has rarely been reported before. In the case of the other two samples, however, only 73.8% and 61.2% of areal capacitance are retained for Cu3N and CuO, respectively. The superior rate capability for Cu3N@CoFe-LDH is mainly a result of the enhanced conductivity contributed by Cu3N NWs as the core and the ultrathin CoFe-LDH NS shell improved the mass transfer efficiency of the electrodeâ€“electrolyte interface. The enhanced electrochemical performance of Cu3N@CoFe-LDH is also confirmed by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements. Fig. 3f exhibits the Nyquist plots of the EIS spectra for Cu3N@CoFe-LDH, Cu3N, and CuO electrodes that were obtained at the open circuit potential in the frequency range of 100 kHz to 0.01 Hz, which consist of a partial semicircle at high frequency and an inclined line in the low-frequency region.50 In the equivalent circuit, the charge transfer resistance (Rct) reflects the results of the faradaic reactions of the electrodeâ€“electrolyte interface, related to the diameter of the semicircle. The Warburg impedance (Zw) related to the ion diffusion of the electrolyte is presented by the sloped curve.51 The Cu3N@CoFe-LDH has a smaller Rct value of 0.58 Î© than Cu3N (0.81 Î©) and CuO (1.4 Î©) electrodes, indicating a faster electron transport. Meanwhile, the straight line of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH is more vertical, revealing a more rapid ion diffusion rate in the electrolyte than that of Cu3N and CuO electrodes, which agree well with the superior rate capability.

As another important feature for supercapacitors, the cycling stability of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWA electrode was investigated by continuous chargeâ€“discharge measurements carried out at a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 over 10000 cycles (potential window: 0â€“0.45 V). The areal capacitance of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH electrode still remains at 93.9% after 10000 cycles (Fig. 3g), demonstrating superior capacitance retention compared to Cu3N and CuO electrodes, which exhibit only 87.1% and 71.7% capacitance retention, respectively, after 5000 cycles. In the last 20 cycles, the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH electrode displays very stable chargeâ€“discharge curves (Fig. S7â€ ), confirming the long-term electrochemical stability of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH electrode. It is believed that in the embedded coreâ€“shell nanostructure, the ultrathin CoFe-LDH NS shell arising from the interior of the Cu3N NW significantly improved the mechanical stability of the nanocomposites, which effectively reduced the shedding of CoFe-LDH NSs from the Cu3N NW and volume change of the Cu3N NW during the chargeâ€“discharge process. Corresponding to the above, the SEM image and XRD pattern of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH electrode after 10000 cycles is shown in Fig. S8,â€  from which it is found that the hierarchical coreâ€“shell nanostructure almost did not change and the crystalline structure was well preserved.

In order to elucidate the possible origins of such extraordinary performance, the electrochemically active surface area (ECSA) of the as-prepared Cu3N@CoFe-LDH and Cu3N NWA sample was also estimated by the double-layer capacitance (Cdl).52 The CdI of the two samples shown in Fig. S10â€  was calculated by the non-faradaic reaction CV region (Fig. S11â€ ), in which the CdI of Cu3N@CoFe-LDH is 927.5 mF cmâˆ’2 and 3.3 times higher than that of the Cu3N sample (281.1 mF cmâˆ’2). This higher ECSA of Cu3N@CoFe-LDH demonstrates the larger specific surface area and more exposure of the active sites achieved after the CoFe-LDH coating, benefitting the intimate contact with the electrolyte and the occurrence of more faradaic reaction, thus improving the electrochemical performance.

Based on the above discussion, the outstanding electrochemical performance of the Cu3N@LDH coreâ€“shell NWAs could be attributed to the following factors: (1) the ultrathin CoFe-LDH NSs contribute to increasing the specific surface area of the whole electrode, which is beneficial for exposing more redox reaction active area, and greatly reducing the mass transfer distance between the electrolyte and the substrate.24 Meanwhile, diffusion of reactive ions from the electrolyte into the inner part of CoFe-LDH is favorable, ensuring that the faradaic redox reaction is adequate. (2) The porous Cu3N NWs rooted into the Cu foam firmly grasp the shell CoFe-LDH NSs like a tree trunk, not only ensuring good electrical contact and mechanical stability, but also partly contributing to the total capacitance.10 (3) The unique 3D embedded coreâ€“shell nanostructure can effectively reduce the shedding of CoFe-LDH NSs from the Cu3N NW and volume change of the Cu3N NW during the chargeâ€“discharge process, improving the cycling stability. Also, it facilitates efficient electron transfer from the current collector Cu foam to the outer shell of CoFe-LDH NSs.

Asymmetric supercapacitors (ASCs) possess great potential for practical applications due to their high energy density and power density. For further demonstrating the potential application of the Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWA electrode material, a flexible asymmetric supercapacitor device was fabricated by using Cu3N@CoFe-LDH NWAs (supported on a Cu foam substrate) as the positive electrode, Ni foam supported active carbon (AC) as the negative electrode and 2 M KOH as the electrolyte, whose configuration is schematically shown in Fig. 4a.
