Abstract

The great success of graphene has encouraged the fast development of other two-dimensional (2D) nanosheets, which have attracted extensive attention in different scientific fields encompassing field effect transistors, lithium-ion batteries, and catalysis. With atomic-scale thickness, almost all of the atoms are exposed on the surface, providing an extremely high specific surface area, in conjunction with special physical, chemical, and electronic properties, owing to the quantum confinement effects, which enable their surface phase to be as important as the bulk counterparts. In this review, we have summarized and discussed the recent advancements of 2D nanomaterials beyond graphene, with an emphasis on their basic fundamentals, preparation strategies, and applications. We believe that this review supplies critical insights for exploring and understanding 2D nanomaterials and puts forward the challenges and opportunities for further developments, such as more precise morphology control, foreign atom doping and surface modification technologies, atomic-scale characterization, and finding wide applications in many different fields.

1. Introduction

Apart from the management of the compositions and arrangements of atoms in functional materials, dimensionality engineering also plays a pivotal role in determining their fundamental properties, and has exhibited much success and great progress in chemistry, physics, and material science.1 Two-dimensional (2D) nanomaterials are quite fascinating to the scientific community and have become the focus of intensive research in recent years. To date, 2D nanomaterials with sheet-like structure have been studied in more than 30000 published academic articles every year, suggesting that they are among the hottest topics in academic research and have great impacts on industry and our lives.2 After being exfoliated into ultrathin nanosheets, almost all of the atoms are exposed on the surface, making their surface phase extremely important. In addition, the dramatically increased surface areas originating from exfoliation not only enhance their physical and chemical reactivities, but also influence a 2D wave function from quantum confinement effects.3 Therefore, their unique 2D nanostructure with the dimensional confinement feature can offer abnormal electronic, photonic, magnetic, and catalytic properties compared to that of the bulk counterparts, endowing them with impressive behaviors in field-effect transistors, sensors, catalysis, supercapacitors, batteries, ferroelectricity, and thermoelectricity.4

The special layered feature is highly determined by the inherent crystalline structure. The most common crystal structures can be exfoliated through the top-down process, featuring neutral, single-atom-thick or polyhedral-thick layers of atoms that are covalently or ionically connected to their neighbors, in which the van der Waals force interaction along the third axis between these nanosheets can be weakened or even broken via the mechanical and chemical exfoliation process, to form the desired ultrathin 2D morphologies constructed by single- or few-layered nanosheets. Layered ionic crystals with charged 2D polyhedral layers (that are typically held together with strongly electropositive cations or strongly electronegative anions) can also be exploited and deposited as single to few layers via bulky organic cation/anion exchange, such as tetrabutylammonium/dodecyl sulfate.5 Beyond that, the confined growth method with precise control based on the bottom-up synthetic process can also facilitate the growth and assembly of 2D nanomaterials. In general, surface growth,6 solution-phase growth7 and vapor deposition8 strategies have been successfully employed. For instance, monolayers of silicon composed of a puckered hexagonal lattice with sp2 bonding configuration have been obtained by deposition on Ag(111) substrate.9 Transition metal sulfide nanosheets can be easily prepared through hydrothermal or solvothermal reactions with sulfur-containing precursors.10 Also, graphene and 2D MoS2 have been successfully synthesized via the chemical vapor deposition process with precisely controlled reaction conditions.11,12

Currently, a broad range of 2D nanomaterials have been prepared and evaluated in different applications, including graphene, metal dichalcogenides, metal carbides, metal nitrides, layered oxides and hydroxides, layered metal–organic frameworks, and their derivatives. Among them, the typical example is graphene, which is the first planar nanosheet revealed in nature and shows 3–5 orders of magnitude higher electrical and thermal conductivities along the basal plane than graphite, its three-dimensional counterpart. The discovery of graphene has ignited intensive interest in a wide range of 2D nanomaterials. Accompanied by fast development, 2D metal-free C3N413 and BN14 nanosheets and a series of metal compounds have been prepared and reported in succession, which exhibit enhanced performance, relative to their bulk counterparts, in energy conversion and storage, illustrating the great potential of the 2D nanomaterials family. Meanwhile, elemental 2D nanomaterials are attracting dramatically increasing interest, especially after the achievement of phosphorene15,16 and borophene17 nanosheets in experiment, which exhibit versatile physical, optical and electronic properties, and unexpected performance in a variety of applications. Some groups have reviewed the physical characters and chemistry of graphene, 2D transition metal dichalcogenides, metal hydroxides and their applications; however, the authors only place emphasis on graphene or its compounds.18–21 Herein, we provide a comprehensive review of the latest advances in elemental 2D nanomaterials beyond graphene and discuss their basic fundamentals and applications, with the hope of increasing the awareness of this novel promising structure and stimulating further exploration of new 2D nanomaterials, as well as their usage in more fields. The reports are organized based on elements, with the progress of both experimental and theoretical investigations being discussed. Finally, to conclude this review, a brief conclusion and perspectives on the future development of 2D elemental nanosheets will be addressed.

2. Group IV A

2.1 Silicon & silicene

As an analogue of carbon, silicon is expected to have certain similar structures and properties. It is the most commonly used material in the semiconductor industry and favors the equal sharing of four electrons, leading to a sp3-hybridized tetrahedral configuration and forming the bulk silicon structure. Lower electronegativity, larger atomic radius, smaller energy difference between the s and p orbitals, and the lower-lying d orbitals are the characteristics of silicon, compared to carbon.22 Recently, the rapid advances in graphene have increased the interest in speculating whether silicon can have low-dimensional allotropes; in particular, the fascinating option of creating a 2D hexagonal lattice of silicon, which is named silicene, is extremely appealing.23,24

Silicene has single atom thickness, similar to graphene, with the same honeycomb structure.25 As early as 1994, Takeda and Shiraishi put forward the question of what kind of 2D structures of silicon might be possible.26 Through quantum mechanical ab initio calculations, the authors have found that structures with minimum energies could be obtained if the two atoms in a unit cell were not in the same plane, resulting in a sheet that had dimples rather than both atoms being in the same plane as for graphene.27 To date, silicene has attracted extensive attention on the basis of both experiment and theory among scientists, owing to its fascinating properties and wide range of potential applications.28–35

Unlike carbon, which can effortlessly form sp2 hybrid orbitals, the sp3 hybridization configuration is more stable and energetically favorable for the common Si–Si bond, because of the poor π type overlap between neighbouring p orbitals at the distance imposed by normal s bonding, which causes its very unlikely independent existence and puts forward the great challenge to its free-standing preparation.36 Nevertheless, the wet chemical method, exploited through the top-down process, has accelerated the synthetic technology development and driven the access to 2D silicon nanosheets. In 2006, Nakano and his co-workers selected Zintl phase calcium disilicide, CaSi2, as a fundamental unit, which had a hexagonal layered structure consisting of alternating Ca layers and corrugated Si(111) planes with interconnected Si6 rings (seen in Fig. 1A).37,38 By treating the Zintl compound with ice-old concentrated aqueous HCl, the CaSi2 transformed into a green-yellow solid and hydrogen gas was released, leading to the Weiss siloxane Si6H3(OH)3 solid product, as shown in Fig. 1B.

Compared to CaSi2, the introduced OH and H groups in the interlayer of the silicon polymer sheet could significantly weaken the combination between layers, facilitating the cleavage in many layers throughout the Si6H3(OH)3 product and forming 2D silicon based nanostructures, as displayed in the transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image in Fig. 1C.37 Another pathway is to introduce magnesium. Because the electrostatic interaction between the Ca2+ and Si− layers in the ionic Zintl phase was too strong, magnesium doping could effectively decrease the charge on the negatively charged silicon layers, contributing to the preparation of tremendous silicon monolayer sheets through the chemical exfoliation of CaSi1.85Mg0.15. Fig. 1D demonstrates the hexagonal crystal structure of the magnesium-doped silicon sheet, with its typical TEM and atomic force microscopy (AFM) images shown in Fig. 1E and F, respectively, to confirm its ultrathin nature with the average sheet thickness of 0.37 nm.38

Although Nakano's contributions opened up the experimental 2D silicon research, the issue was that foreign elements were inevitably in existence and functionalized on the monolayer surface. Since then, tremendous progress has been made, and the breakthrough in experimental silicene research was achieved, with the fabrication of silicene nanoribbons on a silver substrate being recognized as a promising 2D material beyond graphene.39 The substrate interaction could effectively change the silicon atomic bonding configuration, to compete with the intrinsic sp3 hybridization, which would be taken as another influencing factor to control the structure during its growth process. Various substrates have been reported for rich phases and different morphologies of silicon preparation, including Ag(111),40–42 Ag(110),43–45 Au(110),46 Ir(111),47 MoS2,48 and ZrB2.9 Among them, Ag(111) surfaces with sixfold top-layer symmetry were utilized as the most accepted support for the formation of honeycomb silicon adlayers, and have attracted tremendous attention from experimental investigations as well as theoretical calculations.49,50

A home-built low-temperature scanning tunneling microscope (STM) with a base pressure of 5 × 10−11 Torr has been created by Wu et al. to deposit 2D silicene on a clean single-crystal Ag(111) substrate.51 They observed and reported the evolution of a several monolayer superstructure of silicon on the Ag(111) surface, which was dependent on the coverage and temperature. These structures were found to share the common building blocks of silicon rings, transforming from a fragment of silicene to the complete monolayer and multilayer 2D silicon nanosheets. When the deposition temperature was below 400 K, only clusters and disordered structures were observed. As the substrate temperature increased to 420 K, the less ordered phase with close-packed protrusions (marked as T) and the highly ordered phase consisting of honeycomb structures (labeled as H) were acquired, as illustrated in Fig. 2A and B, respectively. Fig. 2C proposes a model of phase H, which was constructed based on the high-resolution STM observation of the characteristic corner hole structure. Accordingly, the authors have built the model of phase T via removing half of the silicon rings in phase H, as depicted in Fig. 2D, leaving one hexagonal silicon ring per 3 × 3 unit cell. Their coexistence under the same conditions suggest that these two phases had similar formation energies and stabilities. Furthermore, after the substrate temperature reached 480 K, long-range ordered 2D silicon with moire pattern was obtained, covering the whole surface as shown in Fig. 2E, with its high-resolution STM image demonstrated in Fig. 2F. When the depositing temperature reached 500 K, the 3D STM image in Fig. 2G elucidated a one-atom-thick silicene sheet across the Ag(111) surface without losing the continuity of the atomic lattice, with the obvious honeycomb structure illustrated in the high-resolution image (Fig. 2H). These results present the detailed process of silicene growing on the Ag(111) substrate and provide insights for high-quality and large-scale 2D silicene preparation.

Okamoto et al. have shown that oxygen-free silicon nanosheets covered with organic groups can be obtained by the exfoliation of layered polysilane, as a result of reaction with n-decylamine and dissolution in organic solvent.53 Nakano and co-workers have prepared soluble 2D silicon nanosheets with surfaces modified by benzene-, naphthalene- and carbazole-containing amino substituents, which were obtained via dehydrogenative coupling of layered polysilane Si6H6 with the corresponding amines.54 In 2016, Rieger's group reported the functionalization of free-standing silicon nanosheets by radical-induced hydrosilylation reactions, and an efficient hydrosilylation of Si–H terminated 2D silicon nanostructure has been achieved through thermal initiation or the addition of diazonium salts with a variety of alkene or alkyne derivatives.55 Simultaneously, an efficient and economical route for the synthesis of porous 2D nanoplates of silicon was developed by Hwang et al. via the magnesiothermically induced phase transition of exfoliated clay 2D nanosheets, and the magnesiothermic reaction of precursor clay nanosheets prepared by the exfoliation and restacking with Mg2+ cations could yield 2D porous nanoplates that are composed of elemental silicon.56

Silicene can be described as a surface material that is truncated from the bulk silicon. When the truncation takes place, it is a serious problem to stabilize silicene because of the presence of dangling bonds on the surface.57,58 In addition to the above experimental preparations, Berdiyorov, Neek-Amal and their coworkers have proposed a novel route by using the graphene bilayer as a scaffold via molecular dynamics simulations. The buckled hexagonal silicene was observed, only weakly coupled to the graphene layers by van der Waals interactions, with a graphite-like interlayer distance of ∼3.4 Å. Based on the thermal stability investigation, the authors demonstrated that the silicene intercalated within bilayer graphene is stable well above room temperature, thus providing a favorable route to realizing silicene.59,60

A series of investigations on the physical properties of 2D silicene were made using the first-principles method, including structural and electronic properties,61–63 quantum spin Hall effect,64–66 ferromagnetism,67,68 thermal conductivity,69 and half-metallic properties.68,70 Due to the buckled structure, silicene has properties that are not found in graphene. Theoretical studies have indicated that the electronic structure and spin states in silicene are strongly influenced by applying an electric field in the perpendicular direction and an exchange field, making the properties well tuned under different potentials.71,72 It was found that the combined fields could bring a spin and valley texture to the particle-hole excitation spectrum and allow the formation of spin- and valley-polarized plasmons, whose lifetime and oscillator strength depend on the number of spin and valley type electrons that form the electron–hole pairs,73 and the properties of silicene are sensitive to lattice imperfections and adatoms. Creating in-plane defects opened the possibility for tailoring the electronic structures of silicene. Stone–Wales type defects could be formed within silicene with an energy barrier of ∼2.5 eV for the formation, which is significantly lower than that in graphene (∼9.2 eV). Silicene with Stone–Wales defects is semiconducting with a direct band gap of 0.02 eV, and this value depends on the concentration of defects, realizing band gap engineering.74 Pristine silicene was found to be stable up to 1500 K, and Peeters found that vacancy defects resulted in local structural changes in the system and reduced its thermal stability; depending on the size of the vacancy defect, the critical temperature decreased by more than 30%.75 In addition, adsorption of foreign atoms has also been regarded as an effective route for electronic property control.76 Sahin and Peeters reported that the adsorption of Be, Mg, and Ca turned silicene into a narrow gap semiconductor, and upon the adsorption of transition metals, depending on the adatom type and atomic radius, the system could exhibit metal, half-metal, and semiconducting behavior.77 Zhou and Zhong carried out density functional theory (DFT) calculations on the molecular adsorption of N2 and CO on silicene, seen in Fig. 4, as well as the atomic adsorption of nitrogen, carbon and oxygen.78 The obtained results demonstrated 0.085 and 0.255 eV adsorption energies for N2 and CO, respectively, exhibiting the higher reactivity of 2D silicon nanosheets towards CO chemisorption, compared to N2 physisorption.

It is widely accepted that silicon is the most promising anode material for the next generation of high-performance lithium ion batteries, and for this reason, 2D silicon based materials have been explored and have shown significantly improved lithium storage performance in experiment.79,80 Hong et al. synthesized free-standing silicon nanosheets from natural sand through the magnesiothermic reduction process, as illustrated in Fig. 5A, with their structures characterized by SEM (Fig. 5B) and TEM (Fig. 5C), exhibiting a leaf-like sheet morphology ranging from tens to several hundred nanometers.81 Moreover, the reduced graphene oxide could also be used to encapsulate 2D silicon, and both of them presented enhanced lithium capacity performance, compared to commercial nano-silicon, as measured in Fig. 5D. Meng et al. have employed first-principles calculations to investigate the interaction of lithium with silicon in the model electrode of free-standing silicene.82 The strong binding site for lithium adsorption and the energy barriers for lithium diffusion were identified, as displayed in Fig. 5E and F. Moreover, the authors have compared the obtained results with bulk silicon and nanowires, to assess the suitability of 2D silicon in the form of silicene layers for lithium ion storage.

Because silicon is a congener of carbon, silicene has also attracted tremendous interest in the science and technology field. Owing to the advances in physical and chemical properties, silicon nanosheets were developed as the building blocks for electronic devices in field effect transistors.83,84 Although the applications are still in infancy, 2D silicene has exhibited great potential and is expected to be explored in photo devices, sensors and other functional fields in the near future.

2.2 Germanium & germanene

Like carbon and silicon, the outer shell electrons of germanium are also made up of the s2p2 configuration, and thus it is expected to emerge as a strong contender in the realm of 2D materials. Each germanium atom is covalently bonded to three other germanium atoms, resulting in a simple hexagonal unit cell. Despite having a similar electronic configuration to carbon, germanium is more stable in the sp3-hybridized tetrahedral configuration than in sp2 hybridization. The Ge–Ge bond length is much larger than the C–C bond length, making the pz overlap and the π bond formation more difficult in germanium.85 Hence, converting the sp3 configuration into sp2via controllable physical and chemical modifications to form a 2D nanostructure has been raised and fueled by the fast development of graphene.

Germanene is designated as the germanium analogue of silicene.36 Its first prediction also originates from Takeda and Shiraish's first-principles total-energy calculations, which suggest that the germanium atoms prefer to form the corrugated aromatic stage and to construct the corrugated 2D layer-structure.26 Because of its special location relative to silicon in the periodic table of elements, germanene has also become the prologue of recent research based on nanosheets and has been regarded as the “brother” of silicene, further igniting the spark in the 2D materials family.86–91

The synthesis of 2D germanene and germanene-based materials was initiated by the study of Bianco et al. on the preparation and generation of stable, single-layered germanene, which is a single layer of hydrogen-terminated germanium.92,93 In their investigations, millimeter-scale crystals of a layered hydrogenated germanium have been firstly obtained from the topochemical deintercalation of CaGe2, as shown in the schematic illustration in Fig. 6A, which has alternating planes of covalently bonded germanium atom layers separated by ionically bonded interstitial calcium. The chemical conversion proceeded by placing a large prepared CaGe2 crystal into aqueous HCl at −40 °C for several days, and the designed hydrogenated germanium was purified through washing with Milli-Q water followed by methanol, and then dried at room temperature on a Schlenk line. The optical image in Fig. 6B demonstrated that the obtained crystallites of hydrogenated germanium were 2–3 mm in diameter and less than 100 μm in thickness. The 2D few layered nanosheets could be acquired by simple exfoliation of the layered van der Waals solid, which exhibited good stability in ambient environment and was only slowly oxidized in air over the span of several months. Fig. 6C shows the corresponding TEM image, which confirms its layered morphology. Furthermore, AFM analysis indicated its ultrathin nature, as shown in Fig. 6F and G, where few-layer and even 2D single-layer-thick germanium-based samples were observed. Meanwhile, its silver-black appearance suggests a broad absorption over visible wavelengths, with a linear approximation of the absorption edge indicating a band gap of around 1.59 eV (see in Fig. 6D). Simultaneously, the HSE-06 theory calculations predicted a 1.56 eV direct band gap for an isolated single layer of hydrogenated germanium, which matched well the experimental observations, demonstrating the great potential for a wide range of optoelectronic and sensing applications (Fig. 6E).

Ab initio calculations were effective for the study and prediction of the potential physical properties as well as electronic structures of free-standing 2D germanene, especially when its experimental development was fairly limited.100–108 The structural stability,109 metallic behavior,110 strong excitonic resonances,111,112 carrier mobility,113 Dirac features,114,115 photon properties,116,117 negative thermal expansion,118 spin polarized electronic transport,119 magnetic properties,120 many body effect,121 infrared adsorption,122,123 vibrational properties124 and thermoelectricity125 have been discussed and predicted theoretically in recent research. Moreover, it was found that modification by haloid elements or hydrogen could lead to the quantum spin Hall effect,126,127 highly anisotropic optical responses,128 half-metallic ferromagnetism,129 and topological insulators.130 Besides, foreign element doping could lead to superconductivity131 and magnetism132 on 2D germanene, as well as change its electronic and magnetic properties.133 It has also been predicted that applying an electric field was able to tune the band gap,84 control the topological phase transition,134 and induce half-metallicity135 in buckled germanene. For example, it is known that germanene is zero-gap semi-metallic, and its charge carriers are massless fermions due to the linear contact of its π and π* bands at the Fermi level. The atoms in the lattice will become inequivalent under the influence of a vertical electric field, leading to a direct band gap opening at the K point (Eg = 0.06 and 0.12 eV under the vertical electric field of 0.51 and 1.03 V Å−1, respectively), which provides the opportunity to fabricate field effect transistors at room temperature.84

In addition, based on the experimental achievements, 2D germanene supported by different substrates have also been studied by first-principles calculations. For instance, Liu et al. have investigated the structural, electronic, and hydrogenation-induced properties of Al(111) supported germanene, and found that despite having a different lattice from the free-standing germanene, the supported counterpart on Al(111) still possessed Dirac points at high-symmetry K and K′ points. More remarkably, they discovered that the one-sided semi-hydrogenated germanene turned out to be a 2D half-semimetal, representing a novel state of matter that was simultaneously a half-metal and a semimetal.136 Recently, Pirri and co-workers calculated the electronic structure at the germanene/Al(111) interface, and disclosed a charge transfer from the Al surface atoms to the germanene, and the existence of three non-equivalent germanium sites with three different atomic Bader charges, which were in agreement with the photoemission measurements.137 Simultaneously, Meng's group carried out a comprehensive first-principles study to investigate the geometric structures, electrical properties, and optical properties of the 2D Ge/BeO heterostructure. The energy gap of around 49 meV was obtained, which could be further adjusted in the range of 14–382 meV by the interlayer distance, external strain, as well as the external electric field.138

Theoretical investigation of the adsorption of atoms and organic molecules on 2D germanene, which had an ability that graphene did not, was also an interesting topic. Ciraci et al. showed that a germanium adatom adsorbed on germanene could push down the host germanium atom and form a dumbbell structure, as illustrated in Fig. 8A, and the exothermic process could occur spontaneously, causing new phase formation.139 3d transition metal atom adsorption could provide a route to modifying the electronic and magnetic properties of 2D germanene for the applications in nanoelectronics.140,141 Kaloni addressed the structural, electronic, and magnetic properties of 3d transition metal atom adsorbed germanene, and found that these metal atoms preferred to occupy the hollow site for all the cases.142Fig. 8B shows the calculated charge density difference isosurface for two Mn atoms at a distance of 15.47 Å, with ferromagnetic coupling on the 2D germanene surface. Besides, Zhang et al. studied the germanene adsorption with several small organic molecules, which led to a large enhancement of the band gap on germanene, with sizeable values of 3.9 (acetonitrile)–81.9 (ammonia) meV, due to the sublattice symmetry breaking (summarized in the table in Fig. 8C).143 In addition, Yang et al. investigated the common gas molecules adsorption, and reported that N2, CO, CO2 and H2O were physisorbed on germanene via van der Waals interaction, while NH3, NO, NO2 and O2 were chemisorbed on germanene via strong covalent bonds, providing a feasible way to exploit chemically modified 2D germanene.144

2.3 Stannum (tin) & stanene (tinene)

The stannum element is located in a deeper position in group IV A in the periodic table, and also has unusual and potentially useful electronic properties. It is emerging as a competitor for the miracle material, and its progress makes things even more interesting.145 The 2D allotrope of stannum is named stanene, which is composed of a bi-atomic layer of Sn(111), in which two triangular sublattices stack together, forming a buckled honeycomb lattice. It has potential for being a topological insulator, owing to its special electronic structures and outstanding properties, and is regarded as a new cousin of graphene.146 According to the predictions made by Zhang et al. in 2013, 2D stanene was found to be a quantum spin insulator with extraordinarily large bulk gaps of 0.3 eV, which is sufficient for practical applications at room temperature, spurring great interest among both physicists and chemists.147

Unlike the successful realization of silicene and germanene in experiment, the synthesis of 2D stanene has proved to be a tremendous challenge for a long time. In 2015, Jia et al. overcame this difficulty and fabricated 2D stanene by molecular beam epitaxy, and its atomic and electronic features were characterized and confirmed using STM and angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES).148 Bi2Te3(111) substrate was selected for stanene deposition. Compared to the topography of the substrate, the overall surface morphology was unperturbed on the large scale after stanene growth, as shown in Fig. 9A and B, in which the sharp Bi2Te3 steps with 1 nm height could be always observed. Moreover, the dispersion of the inner hole band was measured via the polarization-dependent experiments and the corresponding ARPES spectra near the Γ point were presented. It was obvious that there were two hole bands under p-polarization crossing the Fermi level (dotted lines), with the conduction bands and valence bands of Bi2Te3 also being observed (arrows), shown in Fig. 9C. In contrast, the Bi2Te3 bands were strongly suppressed under s-polarization, seen in Fig. 9D, where the two hole bands originating from stanene remained and the inner hole band was enhanced. After that, Shukla et al. reported the synthesis and investigation of the optical properties of 2D stanene up to a few layers. The typical high resolution TEM demonstrated its hexagonal arrangement of tin atoms, with the inter-planar d spacing calculated using the selected area electron diffraction pattern to be around 0.25 and 0.16 nm for d1 and d2, respectively (Fig. 9E). Fig. 9F represents the edge of a large stanene flake, with the height profile indicating the presence of 4 layers and confirming its 2D morphology.149

Additionally, the derivatives of stanene functionalized by hydrogen, halogens and organic clusters have also aroused great awareness and showed a promising platform for further realistic applications. The results indicated that the half-hydrogenation broke the π-bonds of stanene, leaving π electrons in unsaturated tin atoms localized and unpaired.173 The semi-hydrogenated stanene nanosheets possessed the ferromagnetic ground states with magnetic moments of 1 μB and high Curie temperatures under different strains.174 Besides, Chuang et al. predicted that hydrogenated ultra-thin films of stannum harbored a new class of 2D topological insulators.175 Stanene sheets functionalized by halogens, especially by fluorine, could efficiently tune the electronic structure and drive the quantum spin Hall effect.176,177 Noticeably, ethynyl-derivative functionalized stanene, especially with chlorine, bromine and iodine, have been found to be quantum spin Hall insulators with a bulk gap around 0.22 eV, shedding new light on the future design and fabrication of new quantum devices with topological phenomena, which could operate at room temperature in spintronics.178,179

2.4 Lead

As researchers continued downward in the study of group IV A of the periodic table, lead, at the bottom in the carbon group, was also considered, whose outer shell electrons were made also up of the s2p2 structure. As far as we know, there was no experimental report on the successful preparation of 2D ultrathin lead nanosheets, and even its theoretical investigation was still in infancy. Recently, Barraza-Lopez and co-workers indicated that lead could stabilize an optimal 2D high-buckled phase – a hexagonal close packed bilayer structure with nine-fold atomic coordination, which was demonstrated by energetics, phonon dispersion curves, and the structural optimization of finite-sized samples. Through the analysis of structural stability and the discussion of its electronic properties, it was shown that lead was not a viable route towards stabilizing topological fullerenes.180 In addition, Zhang et al. predicted a novel class of 2D quantum spin Hall insulators in X-decorated 2D Pb monolayers (PbX; X = H, F, Cl, Br, I) with extraordinarily giant-sized bulk gaps from 1.03 eV to a record value of 1.34 eV. The topological characteristics of 2D PbX generally originated from s–px,y band inversion related to the lattice symmetry, while the effect of spin–orbital coupling was only to open up a giant gap.181

3. Group V A

3.1 Phosphorus & phosphorene

In general, phosphorus has several allotropes including white, red and black phosphorus, which are taken as common materials with industrialized production and have been extensively used in matchsticks, fireworks, chemical fertilizers, and the napalm bomb.182 Among them, black phosphorus (BP) has attracted significant attention in recent years as an emerging material due to its 2D morphology exploration.183 The first fabrication of BP could be traced back to 1914, when Bridgman applied high hydrostatic pressure at a suitable temperature to transform white phosphorus into black phosphorus, with a high density of 2.69 g cm−3.184,185 On seeing the success from graphene, phosphorene, a single layer of BP, was prepared from the bulk counterpart using scotch-tape-based microcleavage in early 2014, and was regarded as the second elementary single-layer material after graphene that can be exfoliated from bulk crystals.15,16,186 Its electronic bandgap has been found to increase from 0.3 to 1.3 eV when exfoliated from the bulk to the monolayer limit, conferring on it the typical characteristics of semiconductors and suggesting significant potential for used in electronic and photonic devices.187 Phosphorene has a layered structure consisting of puckered hexagonal rings in an orthorhombic crystal structure and shares the same in-plane translation symmetry with bulk BP. There are five valence electrons in the 3p orbitals in the phosphorus atom and each atom is saturated by making a covalent bond, giving rise to sp3 hybridization with the phosphorene structure.188 For the few layered case, each individual layer consists of two atomic layers, with the shorter in-plane P–P bond of 0.2224 nm connecting the nearest phosphorus atoms and the longer out-plane P–P bond of 0.2244 nm combining the phosphorus atoms at the interface between the top and bottom of a single layer.189 It is well known that phosphorene is unstable in the presence of the moisture and oxygen in air, and a few-layer sample without protection can degrade within hours. As a result, oxidized aluminum, poly(methyl methacrylate) and other protections have been proposed as a passivation layer, which can effectively reduce the degradation of phosphorene.190,191

According to the report from Ye et al., 2D few-layer crystals of phosphorene have been prepared and introduced by the scotch-tape-based microcleavage of the layered bulk black phosphorus, which have an inherent, direct and appreciable band gap and are regarded as an unexplored p-type semiconducting material. Fig. 11A addresses the AFM image of an exfoliated single-layer phosphorene crystal, with a step height of around 0.85 nm, confirming its single-layer structure. Based on the photoluminescence measurements as shown in Fig. 11B, an obvious response was observed in the visible wavelengths on the exfoliated phosphorene, while no photoluminescence signal was obtained for 10 nm thick BP flakes. Subsequently, the authors have employed the 2D exfoliated phosphorene in the field effect transistor to demonstrate its p-type characteristics and reveal its good performance in devices (seen in Fig. 11C and D).15 Chen and Zhang have also fabricated field effect transistors based on few-layer black phosphorus crystals with thicknesses down to a few nanometers. The carrier sign inversion and electrical conduction were detected on their device as displayed in Fig. 11E and F, to demonstrate that 2D phosphorene is a candidate for future nanoelectronic and optoelectronic applications.16,192–195

Liquid exfoliation is also a very promising typical method for 2D material fabrication, which normally provides high yield with rather low cost.197 The first report on phosphorene preparation via liquid exfoliation was according to Brent et al.'s work,198 in which, BP crystal was immersed into the organic solvent, N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone, with strong bath ultrasonication to produce three to five-layer phosphorene nanosheets with significant lateral dimensions. After that, exfoliation in different conventional solvents such as dimethylformamide, dimethyl sulfoxide and N-cyclohexyl-2-pyrrolidone were carried out for 2D phosphorene fabrication with high yield, confirming the tremendous potential for liquid exfoliation in 2D materials synthesis.199–201

The properties of phosphorene highly depend on its layered structures. Photoluminescence and Raman spectra have proved to be effective at providing useful information for the interlayer coupling in phosphorene, whose peak position correlated substantially with the layer number, type and size of strain and other factors.202–205 This characteristic confirmed its semiconductor nature and conferred 2D phosphorene with controlled response to light irradiation, making it a good candidate for use in photoelectronics, involving photo-detectors,206 solar cells207 and photosensitizers for singlet oxygen generation.208

Based on the combination of experiment and theoretical simulations, a series of properties and the performance of 2D phosphorene have been systematically studied, including electronic structure,209–212 magnetic behavior,213 magneto-optical transport properties,214 band structure influenced by layers215 as well as by strain and orientation,216 mechanical flexibility,217 blue phase,218,219 thermal properties,220 thermoelectric efficiency,221 anisotropic excitons,222 anisotropic electrical conductance,223 anisotropic thermal conductivity224,225 and the conversion from a normal insulator into a topological insulator via electric field.226 The influence of strain has been discussed by Peeters et al., where applying a compressive biaxial in-plane strain and perpendicular tensile strain can lead to a topological phase transition.227 Moreover, they have reported that applying tensile strain can significantly improve electron transport along the zigzag direction of phosphorene and biaxial strain is able to tune its optical band gap from 0.38 eV (at −8% strain) to 2.07 eV (at 5.5%), which appears as a promising technique to design novel photovoltaic devices.228

Furthermore, the development of 2D phosphorene has been applied in gas sensing. For instance, a field effect transistor sensor device has been fabricated based on phosphorene nanosheets by Cui et al., which exhibited an ultrahigh sensitivity to NO2 with a sensitivity up to 190% at 20 parts per billion at room temperature.229 On the other hand, Kou et al. studied the adsorption of CO, CO2, NH3, NO and NO2 gas molecules on a monolayer phosphorene via first principles calculations.230 Their results predicted its superior sensing performance compared to graphene and MoS2, which was driven by the higher charge transfer and stronger interaction between the molecules and 2D phosphorene surface.

Considering the extremely high surface areas of phosphorene nanosheets and the successful application of its cousin graphene in batteries, it was also anticipated to explore phosphorene as a new anode material in lithium and sodium ion batteries. To the best of our knowledge, the experimental development of using isolated phosphorene in this field was limited, while much progress has been made by density functional theory calculations.231–233 Zhang and co-workers have indicated the ultrahigh diffusivity of lithium on monolayer phosphorene along the zigzag direction with the shallow energy barrier of 0.08 eV, in Fig. 13A and B.234 The lithium atoms were loaded on the phosphorene surface then diffused along the armchair or zigzag direction. Obviously, the diffusion of lithium on phosphorene was highly anisotropic with the zigzag direction being highly energetically favorable, while along the armchair direction was almost prohibited. Moreover, Wu et al. have found the high theoretical sodium storage capacity and low diffusion energy barrier of single layer phosphorene.235Fig. 13C addresses a high sodium intercalated structure, corresponding to NaP2, in which all of the sodium atoms remained isolated and kept their positions above the centers of the hexagons, suggesting that large sodium concentration might be expected in phosphorene, owing to a rather small Na–Na repulsion with a large Na–Na distance of 3 Å. It was shown that sodium diffusion on phosphorene was fast and anisotropic with an energy barrier of only 0.04 eV (seen in Fig. 13D). Therefore, 2D phosphorene was regarded as a very promising material due to its interesting features and good performance in various domains.

3.2 Arsenic & arsenene

Arsenic also has several allotropes, in which the gray bulk arsenic constructed by buckled honeycomb layers is denoted as the most stable phase. There are five electrons in its atomic outer-shell which are composed of s2p3 structure, making arsenic crystals tend to form a 2D layered structure with single-layer thickness of about 1.35 Å.236 Meanwhile, arsenene has been put forward to describe a single-atomic layer of gray arsenic with a rhombohedral structure, which inspires tremendous research interest in the field of 2D materials. In the early part of this year, Tsai and Liang successfully prepared multilayer arsenene (thickness is about 14 nm) on an InAs substrate using the plasma assisted process, in which the thickness could be controlled by the plasma exposure time and the bandgap of multilayer arsenene was estimated to be around 2.3 eV.237

Based on a comprehensive theoretical investigation on the electronic structure, 2D arsenene seemed to be an ideal direct bandgap semiconductor,238,239 and its electronic properties could be further effectively modulated by staking, patterning and applying strain.240 Allaei and his co-workers studied the lattice thermal conductivity of the 2D honeycomb structure of arsenene using ab initio calculations and predicted a highly anisotropic thermal conductivity of 30.4 and 7.8 W m−1 K−1 along the zigzag and armchair directions, respectively.241 Chen et al. reported that the suitable strain modulation could result in a unique 2D topological insulator with a sizable bulk gap that could be characterized and utilized at room temperature.242 Yang and Liu have investigated the dielectric functions of atomically thin arsenene as a function of temperature, and concluded that with large energy gap, interband transitions dominate the optical absorption of arsenene sheets, but are reduced with the lattice vibration becoming stronger, including a redshift and decreased absorption peaks.243

Introducing defects into 2D materials, including doping and adsorption, is of fundamental importance because this process could enable a wide range of nanoelectronic devices by modulating their electronic properties. Foreign atom doping was investigated by means of first principles methods, indicating the obvious influences of impurity atoms on the structural, electronic and magnetic properties in 2D arsenene.244–247 For instance, Fig. 14A displays the band structures of B-, C- N-, O-, and F-doped models with the substituted formats, where B-, N-, and F-doped systems exhibit semiconducting characteristics, while C- and O-doped cases manifest metallic behaviors with their Fermi levels crossing the electronic states.248 Besides, the adsorption of metal atoms,249 hydrogen,250,251 halogen,252 and other molecules253,254 on arsenene nanosheet surfaces showed promise as candidates for practical room temperature applications in electronics and photonics. Taking Ma's work for example, the optical properties of the F decorated arsenene monolayer were calculated as illustrated in Fig. 14B and C, which suggested its pronounced visible light absorption and potential usage in photovoltaics, and also provided an instructive platform for the design of new controlled bandgap 2D elemental materials.255

3.3 Antimony & antimonene

Antimony is in a much lower position in group V A and is also composed of s2p3 electronic structure with the five electrons in its outer-shell orbitals. The largest applications for metallic antimony are as an alloy with lead and tin, and the lead antimony plates in lead–acid batteries. Similar to arsenic, the gray antimony constructed by bulk honeycomb antimony layers is the most stable phase among all its allotropes. When exfoliated down into a monolayer structure, it is denoted as antimonene, exhibiting semiconductor behavior with a theoretical bandgap up to 2.28 eV.256 The fast development of its experimental fabrication as well as theoretical calculations point toward a hopeful electronic structure with a bandgap suitable for optoelectronics applications, which has attracted significant interest.

Zamora et al. have demonstrated that single layers of antimonene could be readily produced using mechanical exfoliation combined with a controlled double step transfer procedure.257Fig. 15A depicts the sample transferring process, and the AFM and TEM images in Fig. 15B and C have addressed the 2D sheet-like morphology composed of well-resolved terraces with a thickness in the nanometer range. Meanwhile, the same group has reported a fast and simple liquid exfoliation method to produce few-layer antimonene nanosheets without any surfactant.258 The Faraday–Tyndall effect was obvious for the suspension with the corresponding AFM image in Fig. 15D, indicating its high yield and efficient exfoliation. The observed Raman spectra reflected the high thickness dependence (Fig. 15E), which was rationalized by the theoretical calculations as displayed in Fig. 15F. Apart from these, 2D antimonene with few layered structures have been grown on different substrates, involving InSb,259 Bi2Te2Se,260 Bi2Te3 and Sb2Te3,261 which exhibited some special electronic properties and paved a material foundation for further study and commercial applications.

3.4 Bismuth & bismuthene

Bismuth is a pentavalent post-transition metal. It is located at the bottom of group V A in the periodic table, and chemically resembles its lighter homologues, arsenic and antimony. Similar to the above discussions, bismuthene is used to describe the single layer bismuth, which is also composed of the 2D hexagonal lattice with washboard structure.

In 2005, Nagao et al. reported the unusual high-quality film growing of bismuth to form a 2D structure on a silicon surface with atomic level surface smoothness, which put forward the concept of 2D growth of bismuthene.275 After that, Kumar et al. developed a novel and convenient, template-free synthesis approach based on a low-temperature reduction route for the fabrication of high-yield ultrathin bismuthene nanosheets through bottom-up self-assembly.276Fig. 16A and B are the representative TEM characterizations of the obtained sample, which demonstrate its nanosheet-like morphology and ultrathin nature. The phase was confirmed by X-ray diffraction patterns, XPS and Raman spectra, and the achieved productive yield could be as high as 80%. According to the report from one group at Princeton University, Drozdov et al. carried out STM experiments that showed that a subset of the predicted bismuthene bilayer edge states were decoupled from states of bismuth substrate and provided direct spectroscopic evidence of their one-dimensional (1D) nature. Based on further detailed analysis, they claimed there are 1D topological edge states at bismuthene bilayers.277

When the substrate temperature increased to 300 °C, ordered monolayer boron islands were obtained with their STM images shown in Fig. 17E and F demonstrated the corresponding high-resolution STM configurations, with parallel and ordered rows of protrusions along the [−112] direction of the substrate. In contrast, when the annealing temperature approaches 380 °C, it would transfer into another ordered phase as shown in Fig. 17G and H, where the protrusions along the rows were obviously divided into sections with alternate brighter and darker parts.

At the same time, Tai's group also prepared the thin boron films by using their home-made two-zone chemical vapor deposition furnace.286 As shown in Fig. 18A, the source zone T1 was set to 1100 °C to get the diboron dioxide vapor via annealing a mixture of B and B2O3 powders. Simultaneously, a 25 μm thick piece of Cu foil was placed at the substrate zone T2, with the annealing temperature of 1000 °C for the boron film growth. Finally, the atomically thin 2D γ-boron nanosheets were achieved, which featured orthorhombic γ-B28 cells with a unit cell of 28 atoms (a = 5.054 Å, b = 5.620 Å, c = 6.987 Å) and the space group of Pnnm. Fig. 18B and C are the typical AFM and TEM images, which demonstrate its ultrathin nature of 0.80 nm in thickness and the whole covering feature on copper substrates. In addition, the UV/Vis absorption spectroscopy characterization was conducted, as shown in Fig. 18D; the optical band gap with a direct excitonic transition was estimated to be around 2.25 eV. Moreover, the authors have also presented the fist-principles calculations, and the obtained band structure suggested a 2.07 eV band gap (seen in Fig. 18E), which is in conformity with the experimental observation.

In order to understand the nature of 2D boron nanostructures and the experimental results, a string of theoretical simulations have also been carried out to study the structures and features of the free-standing boron nanosheets.287–289 The mechanical,290,291 thermomechanical,292 work function,293 magnetic294 properties and hydrogen storage capacity295 have been analyzed and discussed, respectively, indicating good performance in wide applications. Specifically, the conventional superconductivity has been predicted on the 2D boron polymorphs with the estimated critical temperature in the range of Tc about 10–20 K.296 This property arose from multiple phonon modes, owing to the electronic states of the Fermi surface originating from both σ and π electrons.297 Furthermore, due to the superconductivity achieved on lithium-decorated graphene, Li et al. established the lithium-incorporated boron monolayer nanostructure and evaluated its structural diversity, as well as the energetic stability.298 Compared with the pure boron sheet, some 2D lithium–boron monolayers could exhibit photon-mediated superconducting properties above the liquid helium temperature. These results suggest the attractive potential applications of 2D boron based nanomaterials in power generation, electricity transmittance and energy storage.

The interaction between 2D boron and its substrate was also a critical issue; as discussed above, its preparation was commonly achieved through CVD method on a metal substrate that catalyzed the reactions and supported the sample. By employing the cluster-expansion method combined with first-principles calculations, Yakobson and co-authors have systematically investigated the preferred 2D boron on different metal surfaces, which were usually taken as deposition substrates in the CVD process.299 They showed that a layer composed of icosahedral boron was unfavorable on copper and nickel, but unexpectedly occurred on gold and silver, which opened a possibility to guide 2D boron formation through controlled boron–metal interactions. Recently, Shu et al. also studied the atomic structure and electronic properties of borophene on the Ag(111) surface, revealing that the energetically favorable 2D monolayer boron structures on silver substrate occurred with slight surface buckling (around 0.15 Å) and relatively higher hexagonal vacancy density.300

Besides, the electrochemical hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) have been envisaged on a 2D boron sheet, which are the two branches of the water splitting reaction, and are able to produce promising, clean and affordable hydrogen energy in a green and sustainable way. Similar to the doped graphene for catalysis,301 Jha et. al. functionalized the monolayer boron sheet with different elemental dopants like carbon, nitrogen, phosphorous, sulfur, and lithium, and calculated their electronic strictures, as well as determined the adsorption energy for each case based on a density functional theory framework.302 The free energy calculations demonstrated that the carbon dopant was the best candidate for good HER and OER activities.

4.2 2D iron

Iron is the fourth most common element in the Earth's crust and pure iron metal is relatively soft. Body-centered cubic and face-centered cubic are the two common crystal structures of iron, and these non-lamellar features make it difficult to prepare ultrathin 2D iron configuration. Fortunately, Wu et al. developed a general strategy to fabricate free-standing 2D iron and other metallic nanosheets at room temperature.303Fig. 19A demonstrates the schematic of the process to advance from thin metals toward their 2D nanolayer structures. Iron and aluminum metal sheets were stacked first, followed by repeated folding and calendaring for 20 times with a rolling machine. After that, the iron and aluminum layers were stacked alternately, without any alloy phase detected. Aluminum layers were selectively etched from the as prepared binary metal system using alkaline media, to obtain the final free-standing 2D iron nanosheets, with their SEM and TEM images shown in Fig. 19B and C, respectively. This strategy was universal, and in this report the authors have claimed the successful fabrication of other 2D metallic nanosheets including nickel, copper, silver and gold within this method at room temperature.

4.3 2D cobalt

Cobalt is an important and typical transition metal element that has been widely studied. The crystal structure is hexagonal close-packed, and it usually displays a hard, lustrous and silver-gray appearance. Recently, Xie et al. have synthesized 2D inorganic ultrathin cobalt nanosheets, which were four atomic layers in thickness and exhibited metallic properties.304 The fabrication process was depicted in Fig. 20A, with n-butylamine for controlling the growth and assembly direction of cobalt, and dimethylformamide for gradually reducing cobalt ions. After a series of characterizations including XRD, elemental mapping and Raman spectroscopy were carried out on the obtained nanosheets to determine the phase and composition. Furthermore, the obtained cobalt nanosheets could be partially oxidized for 3 h, during which the 2D ultrathin morphology was still retained. HRTEM and AFM images shown in Fig. 20B and C have confirmed the [001]-oriented hexagonal cobalt nature with average sheet thickness of 0.84 nm. Furthermore, the fabricated 2D cobalt nanomaterials with and without oxidization were loaded onto a glassy carbon electrode and tested in CO2-saturated 0.1 M Na2SO4 electrolyte, respectively. Fig. 20D addresses the catalytic performance data, in which both samples display CO2 reduction response. The partially oxidized 2D cobalt could generate an enhanced current density of 10.59 mA cm−2 at −0.85 V versus a saturated calomel electrode, roughly 10, 40 and 260 times larger than the current densities obtained with the pure cobalt nanosheets, partially oxidized bulk cobalt and bulk cobalt, respectively, illustrating the great advantages of using 2D cobalt based nanosheets for electrocatalysis.

In 2007, Li et al. employed a facile hydrothermal/solvothermal method to control the shape of cobalt nanostructures, obtaining 2D cobalt hexagonal nanocrystals with well-defined planes.305 The underlying formation mechanism was proposed as follows: the cobalt hydroxide colloid precipitate was first produced when sodium hydroxide was added into the cobalt chloride solution. Then, the cobalt hydroxide was reacted with H2PO2− to form metallic cobalt in the presence of sodium dodecylsulfate under hydrothermal conditions, while the product inherited the 2D morphology during the reducing process. This strategy has been applied in fabricating other 2D metal with anisotropic structures, and according to their reports, 2D nickel and copper have also been achieved. The temperature-dependent magnetization and hysteresis loop curves of nickel nanoplatelets suggested typical ferromagnetic behavior at room temperature, with the blocking temperature exceeding 300 K, which was different from the superparamagnetism reported on nickel nanoparticles at room temperature. UV-Vis absorption spectra measured on copper nanoplatelets displayed two adsorption peaks at 540 and 610 nm, a little different from previous reports, which was probably because of the high aspect ratio on the prepared copper nanoplatelets.

4.4 2D nickel

Nickel is also a typical and widely studied transition metal, belonging to the face-centred cubic crystal structure, and often has a silver-white lustrous appearance. Although the non-layered crystal structure makes it a great challenge to prepare 2D nickel nanosheets, Sun's group recently designed a simple in situ reducing method to simultaneously synthesize the atomically thick 2D nickel nanocrystal.306 The ultrathin nickel nanosheets were prepared via reducing a Ni(OH)2 nanosheet array on a metal substrate under mild reducing conditions, as depicted in Fig. 21A. The phase transition from Ni(OH)2 to Ni was evidenced by XRD measurements. It was found that the kinetics behavior was critical for the conversion and morphology control process, and the reaction temperature should be kept around 160 °C. In Fig. 21B and C, the SEM and TEM images show the thin array morphology and ultrathin structure. Fig. 21D displays a vertically laid nickel nanosheet, with the inset HRTEM image exhibiting the thickness of the nanowall in the range of 5–6 nm (i.e. 10–12 nickel layers). The obtained 2D nickel nanosheet array has been tested in the hydrazine oxidation reaction, in which it displayed a dramatic enhancement on both activity and stability relative to platinum. This work has provided a new method for the fabrication of 2D metal nanostructures.

4.5 2D copper

Copper has a reddish-orange color and face-centred cubic crystal structure. It is a soft, malleable and ductile metal with high thermal and electrical conductivity. Recently, Shaik and Chakraborty reported the synthesis of stable, large 2D copper nanostructures using fast reduction at room temperature.307 The copper nanosheets were obtained through reducing Cu(OH)42− with hydrazine hydrate in the presence of cetyltrimethylammonium bromide at room temperature. Fig. 22A–C illustrate their typical TEM images in different solvents, demonstrating their 2D thin morphologies. In addition, the authors found the size of the nanosheet was highly dependent on the concentration of alkaline media and reducing agent, providing reference for other 2D material preparation.

4.6 2D ruthenium

Ruthenium is a rare transition metal with the hexagonal close-packed crystal structure, and belongs to the platinum group in the periodic table. It is inert to most other chemicals and has been broadly used as a catalyst in many reactions. According to Yan et al.'s report, ultrathin 2D ruthenium nanocrystals could be obtained through a fast growth process, with a high concentration of ruthenium chloride and poly(vinylpyrrolidone) as the precursors. The obtained ruthenium nanosheets were less than 2 nm in thickness, containing less than five layers of ruthenium unit cells.308

Recently, our group reported the synthesis of free-standing 2D Ru nanosheets using a facile solvothermal method, in which Ru(III) was reduced via self-decomposition of the Ru(acac)3 precursor and grew into ultrathin nanosheets with the aid of isopropanol and urea.309Fig. 23A demonstrates its 2D growing mechanism, with the corresponding attachment growth process confirmed in Fig. 23B. Fig. 23C and D show that the product was constructed primarily of very thin, sheet-like nanostructures of a few hundred nanometers in size. The hydrogen evolution activity via electrochemical water splitting is recorded in Fig. 23E. The 2D ruthenium exhibited excellent HER activity compared with its powder counterpart, demonstrating its great potential in chemical catalysis.

4.7 2D rhodium

Rhodium is a member of the platinum group elements and belongs to face-centred cubic crystal structure. It is one of the rarest and most valuable precious metals, and usually appears with silver-white, hard, and chemically inert features. In 2014, Li et al. reported the fabrication of poly(vinylpyrrolidone)-supported single-layered rhodium nanosheets based on a facile solvothermal method.310Fig. 24A is an image of 2D rhodium nanocrystals supported on silica wafer, showing that the thickness of the rhodium nanosheet is less than 4 Å. The TEM characterization shown in Fig. 24B demonstrates its sheet-like features, which are consistent with the above TEM image. The obtained single-layer 2D rhodium could expose full atoms with unsaturated coordination, significantly improving its atomic efficiency and overcoming the limited reserve issue. When tested in both hydrogenation and hydroformylation reactions, in which rhodium is commonly used as catalyst, ultrathin 2D rhodium nanosheets exhibited much enhanced activity, compared to rhodium nanoparticles and commercial rhodium/carbon catalysts, suggesting its great advances in chemical and material science.

Zheng's group was very efficient at shape control during the crystal growing process, especially for designed noble metal synthesis. They demonstrated the CO-confined growth strategy to effectively prepare single-crystalline rhodium nanosheets with atomic thickness and controllable size.311 The TEM image in Fig. 24C has illustrated the typical 2D ultrathin structure. They also revealed that the formation process involved the formation of small rhodium carbonyl clusters at the early stage, followed by their aggregation assembly into 2D nanosheets to minimize their surface energy. Moreover, Kuang et al. synthesized an ultrathin 2D rhodium nanostructure with a thickness of approximately 1 nm.312 During the preparation process, formaldehyde was regarded as the shape controller, which could produce CO and H2via its decomposition, playing key roles in the formation of 2D ultrathin nanostructures. The obtained sample had a clean surface as well as plentiful exposed atoms, providing excellent activity toward the catalytic oxidation of CO.

4.8 2D palladium

Palladium is a rare and lustrous silvery-white metal, belonging to the face-centered cubic crystal structure. It has excellent catalytic behavior and has been widely used in many reactions, converting a large number of harmful chemicals into less noxious substances, which is very important for industrial production.

Zheng's group has made a lot of contributions to the shape control of palladium nanocrystals. In 2011, they reported a general CO-confined growth method to facilely synthesize free-standing hexagonal palladium nanosheets that were less than 10 atomic layers thick.313 CO was taken as a surface confining agent, and the obtained 2D nanocrystals were blue in color, as displayed in Fig. 25A. Upon near infrared irradiation, the palladium samples created a significant photothermal effect and could effectively kill cancer cells; see Fig. 25B and C. Moreover, the 2D nanosheets exhibited enhanced activity for the oxidation of formic acid that was 2.5 times higher than commercial palladium catalyst, displayed in Fig. 25D, suggesting the great potential of 2D palladium nanostructure.

In 2013, they demonstrated a facile synthetic method to prepare surface-clean uniform palladium nanosheets.314 The [Pd2(μ-CO)2Cl4]2− complex was firstly prepared by stirring a solution of PdCl42− in anhydrous N,N-dimethylformamide solution under CO atmosphere. CO was removed and a small amount of water was added to the reaction mixture, with the solution color turning from light yellow to dark blue. The obtained 2D palladium nanocrystals had a thickness of approximately 1.8 nm, and the mechanism study indicated that the CO ligands in the complex served as both reductant and capping agent for the 2D structure formation. Based on the above work, the authors further investigated the self-assembly behavior.315 It was found that introducing trace amounts of countercations could facilitate the above surfactant palladium nanosheets assembled into one dimensional (1D) superlattice nanowires as shown in the TEM image in Fig. 25E. The countercations would shield the negative charges on 2D palladium nanosheets, and thus electrostatic repulsion was greatly reduced, leading to their 1D assembly. These newly obtained palladium superlattices that were composed of 2D nanostructures could exhibit unique surface plasmon resonance properties, as well as excellent performance in hydrogen sensing.

Yang's group have also contributed a lot to the 2D palladium field. In 2014, they reported the ensemble structures of multilayered 2D palladium nanosheets that had shared growth centers.316 As shown in the TEM images in Fig. 25F, the nanosheets were hexagonal with the diagonal distance of ∼2 μm and rotationally mismatched in their in-plane orientations. The competitive adsorption of the Pd4(CO)4(OAc)4 complex and CO on palladium surfaces was proposed as the critical factor determining the anisotropic growth of palladium nanosheets. Moreover, the authors have prepared flexible palladium nanosheets by using short chain thiolated carboxylic acids and amines.317 The as-prepared positively charged cysteamine and negatively charged 3-mercaptopropionic acid functionalized 2D palladium nanocrystals exhibited around 80% and 30% cell death at 100 ppm, suggesting the potential for using ultrathin metal nanosheets as carriers for drug delivery and targeted release.

4.9 2D silver

Silver is a rather unreactive metal and belongs to the face-centred cubic crystal structure. It is soft, white, as well as lustrous, and has long been valued as a precious metal. Fang et al. reported the synthesis of 2D silver nanostructures through a facile self-seeding strategy.318 Based on a series of control experiments, poly(vinylpyrrolidone) and sodium dodecyl sulphate were found to regulate the size and morphology of the silver nanosheets, which could built the microreactor together and serve as a synthesis template. The reduced silver atoms were driven to grow into anisotropic 2D silver nanosheets under the influence of poly(vinylpyrrolidone) through selective adsorption onto different crystal facets to control their growing rates. Besides, Song and co-workers reported the large-scale synthesis of micrometer-sized silver nanosheets (several tens of nanometers in thickness) via a facile, template- and surfactant-free electrochemical deposition in an ultra-dilute electrolyte of low electrical conductivity (less than 50 μS cm−1).319 The 2D structure growth involved three stages: polygonal island formation, faceted nanowire growth, and planar growth of the nanosheet with the faceted nanowire, which was also expected to contribute to the development of other functional 2D nanomaterials.

4.10 2D platinum

Platinum is one of the least reactive metals and widely employed in catalysis, jewelry, electrodes and other laboratory equipment. The crystal structure of platinum is face-centered cubic, resulting in the significant challenge of preparing it with 2D sheet-like morphology. In 2015, Gautam et al. induced the mechanochemical transformation of tellurium template nanorods to synthesize 2D free-standing platinum nanostructures under the influence of external deforming forces exerted by magnetic stirring.320 The obtained nanosheets had a thickness of about 27 nm and uneven surfaces, as shown in their SEM (Fig. 26A) and TEM (Fig. 26B) images, which could provide high catalytic active surface area and exhibit efficiency toward the electrocatalytic oxidation of small fuel molecules. In addition, Matsumoto et al. reported the preparation of monolayer platinum nanosheets by exfoliating layered platinum oxide and adjusting the reducing conditions, with the AFM measurement displayed in Fig. 26C.321 They also demonstrated that the platinum had high oxygen reduction reaction activity, compared to commercial platinum/carbon catalysts. Moreover, by using a molten salt (KOH–NaOH) and through an in situ chemical vapor reaction, Yang et al. have presented the synthesis of platinum nanosheets via bubble breakage.322 There were no toxic gases and organic solvents involved in the developed synthetic method and the as-prepared 2D platinum nanocrystals had good activity toward oxygen reduction, implying its potential application in fuel cell electrodes.

4.11 2D gold

Gold is a bright, slightly reddish yellow and ductile metal, and normally belongs to the face-centered cubic crystal structure. As is well known, it has been used for jewelry and coinage throughout recorded history. Zhang's group has done a lot in the preparation of 2D gold nanostructures.323 As early as 2011, they presented the first in situ synthesis of dispersible hexagonal close-packed gold square sheets on graphene oxide sheets by slowly reducing chloroauric acid with oleylamine under mild conditions (as seen in Fig. 27A).324 Furthermore, the authors have claimed that these gold square sheets could transform from the hexagonal close-packed to the face-centered cubic phase via exposure to an electron beam during TEM characterization, while remaining in their original 2D configuration (as shown in Fig. 27B).325

Fig. 27D shows the TEM image and indicated the large lateral dimensions of the obtained gold nanosheets, which exhibited excellent catalytic activity and stability towards the selective and solvent-free oxidation of C–H bonds using molecular oxygen as the oxidant. Jeong et al. have presented the synthesis of large, thin gold nanosheets with L-arginine as a capping ligand and mild reductant in aqueous solution; the SEM image is shown in Fig. 27E. The nanosheets can be further processed into electrode film, and demonstrate high electrical stability at 100% strain during the repeated stretching cycles.327 Moreover, it was reported that by microwave heating of chloroauric acid in the ionic liquid, 1-butyl-3-methylimidazolium tetrafluoroborate, without any additional template agent, large-sized single crystal 2D gold could also be successfully prepared on a large scale.328 This synthetic procedure was fast and simple, and might be a versatile approach to the fabrication of some other 2D metal nanosheets.

5. Conclusion and perspectives

In summary, this review article has supplied an overview of the recent development in elemental 2D nanosheets beyond graphene, involving their physical, chemical and structural properties, as well as the reported applications, which are based on both experimental and theoretical investigations. Dimensionality engineering has achieved excellent success with graphene, which gives people great hints and drives us to look for and design more new elemental 2D nanocrystals. Because of their location in the same group as carbon and having the same s2p2 electronic structure, other group IV A elements have also attracted great concern, including silicon, germanium, stannum and lead. The first three have experimental reports on their 2D nanosheets preparation, while as we know, there is still no report on the 2D lead fabrication, and only a few theoretical simulations. Group V A elements, including phosphorus, arsenic, antimony and bismuth are gaining attention especially after the successful synthesis of 2D phosphorene nanosheets and the report of their applications in field effect transistors. To date, all four elements have been confirmed to have their own 2D allotropies via experiment. Combining with theoretical investigation, it has been shown that these new 2D nanostructures have good semiconductor features, implying their potential applications in electronic and photonic devices. Moreover, boron and other metals as listed in the review have also exhibited promising properties and excellent behaviors in their 2D morphologies, such as in catalysis and biomedical science, which not only enrich the elemental 2D nanosheets family, but also provide valuable reference for exploring more functional 2D nanomaterials. Although some progress has been achieved in this field, some challenges still remain. One of the greatest difficulties for the synthesis of 2D elemental nanomaterials is to achieve the large-scale yield with high purity. CVD and high vacuum deposition are the universal methods for 2D nanostructure preparation, but the cost is too high and the yield is far from practical use. Although liquid exfoliation has been successful in the fabrication of some nanosheets, foreign atoms or byproducts are usually introduced on the 2D sheets, which will inevitably reduce their quality. In addition, developed characterization techniques should be explored, to help us get more precise images of the details on these novel elemental 2D materials, which are crucial for us to understand their formation mechanisms, as well as to further strengthen their performance improvement.

Rational design and synthesis of elemental nanosheets with desired structural features are anticipated for specific applications. Thickness, size, crystal phase, defects and other factors of a material play an essential role in determining its physical, chemical, and electronic properties. It is expected that materials with different structures should exhibit different properties, which are applicable to their utilization in all fields. From an application point of view, field effect transistors and other electronic devices require elemental 2D nanomaterials have larger lateral size and suitable bandgap, while catalysts, especially electrocatalysts, should possess large exposed active sites and good conductivity. Hence, the highly controllable synthesis of elemental 2D functional nanosheets with desired structures is worth developing further.

As mentioned above, electronic modification via doping foreign elements has recently been receiving considerable attention. The introduced atoms with stronger electronegativity will snatch electrons from the elemental sheet base, while introducing less electronegative atoms will donate electrons to the 2D nanomaterials, forming p- and n-doping formats, respectively. Controllable doping is an effective measure for regulating band structures, serving as electronic and photonic devices, and simultaneously, this strategy can induce interesting local high spin/electronic states on the elemental nanosheets' surface, to create special active sites for catalytic application. For example, high solar light absorption can be realized by precise bandstructure tailoring via doping and defect engineering on 2D nanomaterials, and at the same time, efficient catalysts may also be created, owing to the induced local high spin/electronic states on the surface, which will form a photocatalytic system to convert sunlight into useful chemicals, contributing to the energy storage and energy conversion, such as in the research of solar-water splitting. Therefore, the doped species, concentrations and distributions are our focus, and even realizing the controlled doping down to a single atom is one of our further research directions. Furthermore, in situ characterization is also appealing. For example, it is expected to identify the specific active sites and understand the catalytic mechanisms by virtue of scanning probe microscopy technology in liquid media.

Alloying is also an important direction for future research. With matching crystal structures, different elements can be alloyed in the same lattice to form new 2D alloyed nanomaterials. For instance, it is possible to form a novel nanosheet composed of iron and nickel, since they both have face-centred cubic crystal structures and are very close in atomic size. Because iron and nickel are active for different catalytic reactions, this newly prepared alloyed nanosheet can have bifunctional or multifunctional catalytic activities and the synergistic effect induced between these two elements may enhance their original catalytic behavior. One example is the oxygen evolution reaction, in which iron doping is well accepted as an effective step to improve the oxygen evolution activity on nickel based catalysts.

To fully resolve these problems, systematic theoretical simulations are also needed to establish a clear relationship between their structures and properties in the near future. As for the layer number dependence, much more research should be carried out to understand the property change caused by reducing or increasing the layers of these 2D nanosheets. All in all, there are numerous exciting opportunities in developing the elemental 2D family, and bearing these in mind, we believe the elemental 2D nanomaterials will be further extended and contribute to our daily life.
