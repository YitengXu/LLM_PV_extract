Abstract

Photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting is a very attractive approach to produce clean hydrogen using abundant natural resources such as solar energy and (sea)water. As its primary step, light absorption controls the available amount of photons for the subsequent processes. Light management in PEC water splitting is essential to obtain high performance and efficiency. Herein, light management strategies via selective material choices and device engineering are comprehensively reviewed from two aspects: (1) improving light trapping ability; and (2) extending light absorption range. From a material design standpoint, morphology control of the photoelectrode components and application of surface-plasmon resonance are priorities. Besides these design priorities, approaches and strategies such as doping, solidâ€“solution formation, and photon up-conversion will be briefly discussed. From a device engineering standpoint, tandem PEC devices are proposed to extend light absorption. The concentration of light is involved as an effective route to regulate the photon flux. In addition to experimental work, simulations of light distribution and absorption in PEC water splitting are examined to provide new device-oriented recommendations. This work features realistic and practical light management approaches for efficient and stable PEC water splitting materials, systems and devices by considering the synergistic effects of light absorption, charge transfer, and surface reactivity.

1. Introduction

Photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting has gained increasing attention in the past decades as an attractive approach to convert solar energy into clean hydrogen.1â€“5 Similar to photosynthesis in nature, the basic idea of PEC water splitting is to utilize photoexcited electrons and holes in the light-absorber to trigger the water reduction and oxidation reactions. Three typical processes are included during PEC water splitting: light absorption; charge generation, separation, and transfer; interfacial chemical reaction.6,7 The overall solar-to-hydrogen (STH) conversion efficiency depends on the efficiency of each process. The implementation of PEC water splitting relies on the development of efficient, stable, and cost-effective photoelectrodes. Diverse semiconductor materials, e.g. metal oxides, metal sulfides, organic semiconductors, IIIâ€“V group materials, have been examined as light-absorbers for PEC water splitting.8 To improve the stability, a protective layer is generally coated on the surface of the light-absorber to prevent the direct contact with the electrolyte.9 Electrocatalysts for hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) are loaded to promote charge transfer and accelerate the interfacial reaction. Although the IIIâ€“V group materials-based PEC cells have achieved STH efficiencies higher than 19% under one-sun irradiation, their high price and the low reserve of IIIâ€“V group materials restrict large-scale applications.10 On the other hand, the STH efficiencies of low-cost and robust metal oxide-based PEC devices still do not meet the performance criteria for wide-spread applications.11

To improve the PEC efficiency for water splitting, different modification approaches have been implemented for the photoelectrodes, such as morphology control,12â€“14 element doping,15â€“17 and forming heterojunction.18,19 The device optimization and electrolyte engineering can also play a positive role in improving the STH efficiencies.20,21 A major goal of these approaches is to simultaneously improve light absorption, promote charge separation and transfer, and accelerate the redox reactions. As the first step of the PEC reaction, the light absorption determines the number of available photons for the subsequent processes. Therefore, the light management in PEC water splitting is essential to achieve high efficiency.22â€“24 As shown in Fig. 1, light management can be implemented from two directions: improving light trapping and extending light absorption range. Improving light trapping means to enhance light absorption by reducing the photon loss from reflection or transmission without the compromise of charge transfer properties. It should be noted that improving light absorption by simply increasing the film thickness is not a light trapping strategy, because it can lead to severe charge recombination. Extending light absorption range means to broaden the utilization of solar spectrum and make use of relatively low-energy photons (visible or infrared light) for PEC water splitting. In the aspect of material design, the morphology control of the photoelectrodes should be a feasible approach to trap light by increasing the optical path length through multiple scattering. Applying plasmonic materials has recently emerged as an attractive means to increase light trapping and utilize the wide-spectrum light for PEC water splitting. Other modification routes such as element doping, solid solution, and photon up-conversion, can also be used to extend light absorption range, which has been extensively studied by previous researches.8

In terms of device engineering, extending light absorption range can be achieved by constructing tandem PEC devices, in which the short-wavelength light can be absorbed by the front photoelectrode and the rear unit absorbs the transmitted long-wavelength light. In such a way, the light utilization can be maximized.25 Up to date, one-sun irradiation is generally applied to test the PEC properties for water splitting, giving rise to the photocurrent density at the scale of mA cmâˆ’2. The low photocurrent density indicates that large land space is indispensable to the practical PEC water splitting, which would inevitably increase the overall cost. In fact, none of the current approaches for solar water splitting (photoelectrochemical or PV-electrolysis) are currently economically viable for large-scale applications, with the STH efficiency being a determining parameter to reduce the H2 generation cost.26 Applying concentrated light should be a viable approach to increase the photocurrent intensity. The high flux of photogenerated charge carriers under concentrated light irradiation could potentially increase the photovoltage, prolong the charge lifetime, and overcome the reaction barrier. Meanwhile, the thermal effects induced by the concentrated light could provide the â€œhot spotsâ€ to accelerate the surface chemical reactions and strength the mass transfer.27â€“29 Despite these aspects highlighting the implicit potential of using concentrated light, some materials such as perovskite solar cells can see their stability dramatically compromised by those illumination conditions, making it necessary a further validation of this approach. In addition to the experimental approaches for light management in PEC water splitting, theoretical simulation is one effective tool to investigate light absorption in the PEC materials and cells, which can provide some forward-looking suggestions on the design of PEC devices with optimized light harvesting.22

Considering the important role of light management in PEC water splitting, an adequate review of recent progress in this field is required. In the following sections, the basic principles of PEC water splitting and light management are first introduced. The strategies for material design and device engineering to promote light utilization are respectively summarized. Particularly, the morphology control of the photoelectrodes and adopting plasmonic materials are focused on the aspect of material design. Constructing tandem PEC devices and regulating the light trapping by concentrating are involved in the aspect of device engineering. The simulation methods of light absorption in PEC water splitting are then discussed. A brief summary and outlook of efficient strategies for light management in PEC water splitting is finally presented.

2. Basic principles of PEC water splitting and light management

To develop efficient PEC systems for overall water splitting, it is important to comprehensively understand the basic principles. Forming a semiconductorâ€“liquid junction (SCLJ) is crucial to actuate PEC water splitting, and a clear description of the semiconductorâ€“electrolyte interface will help to understand the mechanism. When the photoelectrode immerses into the electrolyte, electrons will migrate through the semiconductorâ€“liquid interface due to the difference between the redox potential of the electrolyte and the Fermi level of the semiconductor, until that the equilibration is reached.30 As shown in Fig. 2a, a â€œband bendingâ€ appears in the semiconductor owing to the electron flow. A typical region in the semiconductor, where the majority carriers are depleted, is called as the depletion layer.31 The band bending takes place in the depletion layer. The n-type semiconductors generally work as photoanodes since the upward band bending makes photoexcited holes inject into the solution. As for p-type semiconductors, a similar manner also happens. They work as photocathodes, in which photoexcited electrons migrate toward the semiconductorâ€“solution interface to drive water reduction due to the downward band bending. The semiconductors will yield non-equilibrium electrons and holes after photoexcitation. The electrochemical potential of photoexcited electrons or holes under light illumination could be determined as the quasi-Fermi level. An electric field close to the photoelectrode surface would be produced due to the difference between the electron quasi-Fermi level (EF,n) and hole quasi-Fermi level (EF,p). The generated voltage is defined as the â€œphotovoltageâ€.32

For the overall water-splitting reaction, the redox potential difference is determined as 1.23 V from the free energy change. Therefore, the photon energy for driving water splitting should be larger than 1.23 eV. As shown in Fig. 2a, the photoexcited charge carriers in the semiconductor first undergo separation. The minority carriers migrate toward the surface of the photoelectrode, and the majority carriers transport to the counter electrode. Water redox reactions take place at the electrodeâ€“solution interface, leading to the generation of hydrogen and oxygen at the separate electrodes. The valence band and conduction band positions of a semiconductor should straddle the potentials for OER and HER to drive water redox reaction. Considering the energy loss from the overpotentials for OER and HER, the photon energy required for PEC water splitting is generally larger than âˆ¼1.7 eV for a single light-absorber photoelectrode, corresponding to the light wavelength of âˆ¼730 nm.33

To effectively utilize the absorbed photons, the light absorption and charge transfer should be balanced in PEC water splitting. For a semiconductor photoelectrode, the light penetration length is determined to be Î±âˆ’1, where Î± is the optical absorption coefficient of the photoelectrode material at the wavelength Î». For instance, the value of Î±âˆ’1 is âˆ¼118 nm at 550 nm for Fe2O3.34 In the light of the Lambertâ€“Beer law, the light intensity is exponentially reduced when it transmits through a material. The film thickness of a planar photoelectrode is generally more than 2-fold of the penetration length to assure that the incident light is efficiently harvested.30 For the minority carriers, the effective charge collection length is the sum of the depletion layer width (W) and the carrier diffusion length (Ld). Compared to the light penetration length Î±âˆ’1, the values of W and Ld (âˆ¼2 nm for Fe2O3) are much smaller. This mismatching gives rise to low charge separation and transfer efficiencies in the planar photoelectrodes.35 So, it is not a bright idea to improve the light absorption of the photoelectrodes by simply increasing the film thickness. Alternatively, constructing nanostructured morphology should be an effective route to increase light absorption and charge migration at the same time. For instance, one-dimensional and two-dimensional nanostructures can improve the light trapping by increasing light scattering among different units. The charge transfer distance from the semiconductor to the electrolyte can be considerably decreased by introducing the nanostructures, and the reactive surface area can be greatly enlarged. In addition, integrating the anti-transmittance and anti-reflection structures is also a feasible way to improve the light trapping.36

Besides the material choices, the absorption and utilization of the incident light can be regulated using PEC device engineering. To decrease the cost and system complexity, developing PEC devices without the assistance of external electric energy is required for the practical application. However, a single photoelectrode usually could not fulfill the critical demands for overall water splitting. Instead, developing the photoanodeâ€“photocathode PEC tandem devices and PEC/photovoltaic (PV) tandem devices is an applicable way to maximize the utilization of solar energy and achieve enough photovoltage for water redox reaction.37

In the photoanodeâ€“photocathode PEC tandem devices, the photocathode and the photoanode would drive HER and OER, respectively (Fig. 2b). Usually, the two photoelectrodes are arranged in a tandem illumination mode (mode T), where the front photoelectrode harvests the short-wavelength light, and the back photoelectrode absorbs the transmitted long-wavelength light. In such a scenario, the utilization of solar spectrum can be maximized. Another configuration of PEC tandem device is a parallel irradiation mode (mode P), in which both photoelectrodes are illuminated by the full-spectrum light. There are two ways to make ohmic contact between the two photoelectrodes in the PEC tandem cell. One is the wired configuration in which a conductive metal wire is used to connect the photoanode and the photocathode. The other is the wireless configuration where the photoanode and photocathode stand back to back with a transparent conductive oxide as a middle layer. The wired configuration is simple to assemble, while the wireless one owns the highest integration level.31

In the PEC/PV tandem device, the photoelectrode can drive one of water splitting half-reactions, and the PV cell provides the photovoltage regarded as a bias to promote the other half-reaction over the counter electrode (Fig. 2c). Generally, the short-wavelength light can be absorbed by the photoelectrodes. Meanwhile, the long-wavelength light transmitted from the photoelectrode is harvested by the PV cell (mode T). In addition, similar to PEC tandem devices, the mode P can be employed in PEC/PV tandem devices. According to different assembly methods, the tandem devices are classified into three categories, including wired, wireless, as well as separated configurations. Applying a wire to couple dark electrode and integrated photoelectrode/PV cell is called as the wired configuration, which is the most broadly applied. In the wireless configuration, the electrodes and PV cell are in contact through ohmic layers. The separated configuration corresponds to the modular system in which the PV component and PEC cell are separate units. The potential corrosion of the PV cell can be avoided in the separated device, because it is placed outside the eletrolyte.38

As discussed above, the wide-spectrum light response of photoelectrodes is generally needed to achieve high PEC property. However, for the single light-absorber system, the theoretical STH efficiency is first increased and decreased with the decreased bandgap owing to the tradeoff of light absorption and photovoltage. As shown in Fig. 2d, the maximum practical STH efficiency can be predicated to be 11.2% for a bandgap of 2.26 eV. The combination of dual light-absorbers to construct the tandem devices enhances the available free energy for PEC water splitting. For the tandem devices with mode P, the maximum practical STH efficiency can be improved to 15.5% with the same bandgap of 1.59 eV for two light-absorbers (Fig. 2e). Because the dual light-absorbers in tandem devices with mode T can use the incident light with different wavelength range, the STH efficiency can reach a maximum of 22.8% with the front and rear light-absorbers respectively having the bandgaps of 1.84 and 1.23 eV (Fig. 2f).39

3. Material design for improved light harvesting

Modifying the morphology of the photoelectrodes is an effective way to improve the light trapping. Various nanostructures have been constructed for PEC water splitting, e.g. nanowires, nanosheets, and nanotrees.35 To broaden the light absorption range of the PEC materials, several approaches, including doping, forming solid solution, and utilizing surface-plasmon resonance effects, are widely employed. Typically, surface-plasmon resonance effects have emerged as a research focus. In the following sections, various approaches to increase the light harvesting of photoelectrode materials will be discussed.

3.1 Morphology control

The morphology of the photoelectrode materials plays an important role in determining the PEC performance.33,40 It can not only affect the specific surface area and the charge separation efficiency, but also has a direct influence on the light absorption capacity.41â€“44 The morphology of utilized semiconductor materials to construct the photoelectrode can be classified to 0-dimensional (0D), 1-dimensional (1D), 2-dimensional (2D), and 3-dimensional (3D) nanostructures.45Fig. 3 presented the schematic and scanning electron microscope (SEM) images of 0D, 1D, 2D, and 3D nanomaterials. 0D nanostructures generally represent nanoparticles, and 1D nanostructures include nanorods, nanowires, nanotubes, and nanofibers. For the 2D materials, the nanosheets and nanowalls are widely applied, which can be vertically or horizontally aligned on the substrate. The 3D structures allow the materials to grow outside the nanoscale from arbitrary directions, which include nanotrees, 3D ordered porous structures, etc.46

For 0D nanomaterials, the photogenerated charges have to pass through the grain boundaries of the stacked nanoparticles to reach the interface, which usually leads to severe charge recombination and low PEC performance.45 Therefore, the assembled 0D nanomaterials as single photoelectrode are not the research priority for PEC water splitting. However, varied examples of systems incorporating 0D nanomaterials on bulk semiconductors can be found in literature, in which these materials act as quantum dots and have an overall effect on the light absorption. A typical example is the incorporation of carbon quantum dots (CQDs) on metal oxide photoanodes such as BiVO4. For instance, Ye et al. deposited 5â€“7 nm CQDs on BiVO4 photoelectrodes, thus extending the light harvesting in the wavelength range of 500â€“800 nm and giving rise to applied bias to photon-to-current efficiency of 2.29% at 0.6 VRHE, while a value lower than 1.5% was obtained with the material with only electrocatalyst.47 Similarly, Fe2O3 and WO3 photoanodes decorated with CQDs have been reported, having not only optical effects reflected on the incident-photon-to-current efficiency (IPCE) values in the range of 450â€“600 nm, but also playing a role on the enhanced charge transfer processes.48

Next, we firstly take the nanowire as a model to study the effect of 1D morphology. Due to the short diffusion distance, the nanowires own excellent charge transfer property.49 Meanwhile, the nanowire array can efficiently trap the incident light. As shown in Fig. 4a, the multiple light scattering among the nanowire array can occur, which prolongs the light travel time and length inside the photoelectrode. Compared with the planar and bulk materials, the nanowire structure is a better light-absorber for PEC water splitting.50,51 For instance, the longer Ti and Sn doped Î±-Fe2O3 nanowires could absorb more incident light. The photocurrent density of the nanowire photoanode with the length of 2.5 Î¼m could reach 1.3 mA cmâˆ’2 at 0.7 VAg/AgCl.52 Considering the advantages of the nanowire structure, the ITO (In:SnO2) nanoarrays were introduced to prepare the Î±-Fe2O3 coreâ€“shell nanowires. Due to the increased light absorption, the photocurrent density of the Î±-Fe2O3 coreâ€“shell nanowires could reach 1.1 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 VRHE.53 Zhang et al. proposed that the controllable diameter of the nanowires was a critical factor to influence the PEC efficiency. The activities of single silicon nanowires with different diameters from 200 to 2000 nm were compared. It was shown that the small diameter was in favor of generating the resonance effect, and the larger diameter could make more efficient utilization of long-wavelength light. The optimal performance was achieved at a diameter of 1415 nm.54 Pihosh et al. prepared the WO3/BiVO4 coreâ€“shell nanostructured photoanode for water oxidation. The combination of BiVO4 and conductive WO3 nanorods could enhance light trapping and decrease charge recombination. After loading the CoPi cocatalyst, a high photocurrent density of 6.72 mA cmâˆ’2 was obtained at 1.23 VRHE.55

Guan et al. prepared p-GaN-based nanowire arrays for seawater splitting in the absence of any external bias or sacrificial agent. Compared to hydrogen production from pure water splitting, the improved hydrogen production performance was achieved in simulated seawater due to the chlorine oxidation reaction as a favorable intermediate process and the increased solution conductivity. Under concentrated light irradiation, the nanowire arrays showed a STH efficiency of 1.9%.56 They further loaded water oxidation and reduction cocatalysts on the GaN-based nanowire arrays to improve hydrogen production. A remarkable STH efficiency of âˆ¼2.7% and an excellent stability beyond 580 h were achieved for pure water splitting without the external bias.57 In addition to nanowires, nanorods and nanotubes are also widely examined in PEC water splitting due to their favorable light absorption properties. Jeon et al. used a series of strategies to modify the hematite nanorod arrays, consisting of hydrogen treatment, depositing TiO2 passivation layer, and loading CoPi cocatalyst, and a high photocurrent density of 6 mA cmâˆ’2 was achieved at 1.23 VRHE.58 Graphynes have received increasing attention as newly emerging 2D carbon materials for PEC applications owing to their large surface area, high stability, and higher carrier mobility than graphene. It was shown that the vertical alignment had a significantly higher light absorption capacity, because of the increased light scattering among the nanosheets.64 The thickness of 2D materials determines the charge diffusion length, which would affect the PEC performance. Large CuInS2 nanosheet films first grew on the substrate, and then small SnS2 nanosheets were epitaxially deposited on both sides of the CuInS2. The photoelectrode with 3D nanostructure has a complex geometric morphology, which can exhibit special optical features, such as the optical waveguiding. Additionally, compared to the 1D and 2D structures, the 3D nanostructure has a larger surface area and better charge transfer property.69 Cho et al. compared the PEC performance of TiO2 nanoparticles, TiO2 nanorods, and hierarchically branched TiO2 nanorods (Fig. 4c). Constructing a 3D hierarchical heterostructure is an effective approach to further increase the light absorption, surface area, and multiple interfaces. Han and coworkers fabricated the Î±-Fe2O3/TiO2 3D hierarchical nanostructure to promote the PEC performance. The 3D hierarchical nanostructures consisted of Î±-Fe2O3 nanoflakes and TiO2 nanotubes, which could expand the absorption region from the UV to Vis light. In such systems, textured substrates are used for growing the semiconductor, thus displaying significantly improved light management. Inverse opals (IO) are a clear example of such systems: BiVO4 grown on IO-AZO (Al doped ZnO) and BiVO4/TiO2 heterojunctions on IO-FTO (F-doped SnO2) have displayed between 60% and two-fold improvement in the photocurrent density.77,78 Double-deck WO3/BiVO4 IO fabricated by Ma et al. demonstrated enhanced in-plane light scattering, thus significantly improving the light absorbance (around 90% between 350â€“475 nm), leading to high IPCE values (above 60% at Î» â‰¤ 450 nm).79 A similar strategy has also been applied to CuFeO2 photocathodes, boosting its intrinsic low near-bandgap quantum efficiency and reaching a photocurrent density of 5.2 mA cmâˆ’2 at âˆ’0.1 VRHE.80 Following a different strategy to the IO preparation, Zhao et al. reported the fabrication of photoanodes consisting in textured polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) substrates with different nanostructures covered by ultrathin BiVO4/SnO2/Au layers, able to induce resonant light trapping and light retrapping processes, causing a 10% photocurrent increase in the textured substrate compared to a planar one, only related to light absorption improvement.81

Briefly, the morphology control is an effective way to improve light trapping in the photoelectrodes. 0D nanoparticles have been widely used as photocatalysts in the suspension solution. However, when they are assembled into a photoelectrode, the severe charge recombination at the interparticle boundaries would restrict the PEC performance. Meanwhile, the photoelectrode with 0D nanoparticles barely showed advanced light absorption properties. As a comparison, the delicately designed 1D, 2D, and 3D nanostructures are beneficial to light trapping, charge transfer, and surface reaction, which give rise to apparent improved PEC activities. The enhancement of light trapping in the 1D architecture can be simply tuning the geometry. For instance, leaky mode resonances can take place with appropriate nanowire interspacing or diameter, which leads to increased light absorption. The light absorption improvement at a specific wavelength can be controlled by adjusting the nanowire diameter.45 Compared with 1D nanostructures, 2D nanostructures possess two exposed surfaces, which can be engineered as active sites for catalytic reactions and provide a platform to design heterojunction structures through the efficient face-to-face contact. The 3D nanostructure can be considered as the assembly of 0D, 1D, and 2D nanostructures, which have more possibility to design the morphology, heterojunction structure, and textured substrate. 3D ordered structures also own some unique properties. 3.2 Surface-plasmon resonance effects

Combining plasmonic materials can improve the optical properties of the photoelectrodes, because plasmonic materials have tunable absorption in wide spectrum and large absorption cross section.82 The attachment of plasmonic materials to the semiconductor photoelectrode can widen the light absorption range, enhance the electromagnetic field, and promote the catalytic and photoinduced processes.83,84 The surface-plasmon resonance (SPR) effect is the interaction between electrons and photons that vibrate freely on the metal surface, generating electron density waves that propagate along the metal surface.85 The main favorable effects of plasmonic metals on solar conversion efficiency can be divided as (i) light scattering/trapping, (ii) plasmon-induced resonance energy transfer (PIRET), and (iii) hot electron injection.86

When the size of plasmonic materials is larger than 100 nm, the light trapping is induced by multiple scattering and reflection. With the particle size smaller than 20 nm, the light scattering is weak, but a strong local electromagnetic field with intensity 100 times higher than that of incident light can be created.87 Plasmonic coreâ€“shell Au@TiO2 nanoparticles were used to modify the photoelectrodes for water splitting. Compared with the photoelectrode without the Au@TiO2 nanoparticle layer, the photoelectrode with the Au@TiO2 nanoparticle layer could dramatically enhance the IPCE from the UV to the IR region (Fig. 5a). The enhancement in the UV region was induced via the light scattering of plasmonic Au nanoparticles (Fig. 5b). The increase in the IR region was attributed to the plasmon-induced resonance energy transfer.88 In addition, the periodic structure of plasmonic materials can introduce the incident light along the lateral direction and trap it. With the careful nanostructured design of the plasmonic materials, the SPR could concentrate light around the surface.89

For the PIRET, the energy transfers from the plasmonic metal to the semiconductor by dipoleâ€“dipole coupling. It allows the photons with energy smaller than the bandgap of the semiconductor to participate in the PEC water splitting, thus extending the absorption spectral range. Notably, the PIRET process does not need an intimate contact between plasmonic materials and the semiconductor.87 Even if the thickness of the insulating layer between the plasmonic materials and the semiconductor is larger than 25 nm, the PIRET still could occur. However, it was pointed out that the efficiency of the PIRET depended on the distance and spectral overlap between the metals and semiconductors.90 The hematite nanorodâ€“Au nanohole array was prepared to enhance the light harvesting. Above the band edge of hematite, the light enhancement was ascribed to the surface plasmon polariton of Au nanohole array, which could launch a guided wave mode inside the hematite nanorods with these nanorods as miniature optic fibers (Fig. 6a and b). The PIRET helped to enhance light absorption below the band edge of hematite, and the local plasmonic field could decrease charge recombination (Fig. 6c). Therefore, the greatly increased PEC water splitting performance (i.e. IPCE) was achieved by incorporating hematite nanorods into the plasmonic Au nanohole array (Fig. 6d).91 Li et al. proposed a new plasmon-driven photoanode based on plasmonic metalâ€“insulatorâ€“semiconductor (PMIS) heterostructure (Au@SiO2NP-decorated Î±-Fe2O3 nanorod array) for water splitting. The PEC performance of PMIS structure was greatly improved due to the enhancement of localized SPR electromagnetic field via the PIRET mechanism. Meanwhile, the thin SiO2 insulating layer could eliminate the negative influence of interfacial states and increase the metalâ€“semiconductor barrier height, which could suppress charge recombination.92

Hot electron injection is a direct electron transfer from the plasmonic materials to the semiconductors, which is also called as direct electron transfer (DET).90 Compared to the PIRET, the DET requires close contact between the plasmonic metal and the semiconductor, and the hot electrons need to overcome the Schottky barrier.93 Among various plasmonic metal/semiconductor composite materials, plasmonic metal-photonic crystal (PC) semiconductor heterostructures emerged as an efficient combination to extend the light absorption, because both plasmonic metals and PC semiconductors are sensitive and tunable light-absorbers. The spectral overlap of electronic bandgap, localized SPR, and incident light source could also lead to superior PEC performance.94 Au nanoparticles with a size of 10 nm were coupled with a TiO2 PC layer, and the PEC water splitting performance was apparently enhanced owing to the energetic hot electrons and holes generated in Au nanoparticles. By tuning the pore size of the TiO2 PC structure, the slow photon region at the red edge of the photonic bandgap could overlap with the localized SPR region of Au NPs, which would intensify the SPR response. Considerable hot electrons were thus generated under visible-light irradiation, which were then injected to the TiO2 PC structure for efficient PEC water splitting.95

3.3 Other approaches

Besides the morphology control and surface-plasmon resonance effect, other approaches such as doping, forming solid solution, and photon up-conversion could also improve the absorption spectral range of the photoelectrode materials, which has been extensively studied by previous researches.8 For instance, doping Ge ions into Î±-Fe2O3 could induce the non-isovalent impurities, which led to better absorption from 250 to 800 nm and a shift of absorption onset to longer wavelength. The improved light absorption after Ge doping was an important factor for the enhanced photocurrent.96 With the aid of forming the solid solution, the bandgap and band positions of semiconductors can be adjusted. Li et al. prepared (GaN)1âˆ’x(ZnO)x nanowires for water splitting. The increased content of ZnO from 10.3% to 47.6% in the (GaN)1âˆ’x(ZnO)x solid solution would lead to the bandgap tailoring from 3.08 to 2.77 eV. Owing to the narrower bandgap and the favorable morphology of nanowires, the PEC property of (GaN)1âˆ’x(ZnO)x photoanode was greatly enhanced.97 Because the up-conversion can utilize two low-energy photons to generate a high-energy photon, the Vis or IR light can be converted into UV light to excite large bandgap metal oxides for PEC water splitting. As shown in Fig. 7a, Barawi et al. respectively applied the 2,3-butanedione (BA) and 2,5-diphenyloxazole (PPO) as the emitter and sensitizer in the tripletâ€“triplet annihilation systems. The upconverted energy of BA/PPO overlapped with the TiO2 absorption bandgap (Fig. 7b). Therefore, the photocurrent of the TiO2 photoelectrode cell with up-conversion materials could be increased 40 folds compared to that without up-conversion materials.98 For the single light-absorber photoelectrode, most of current PEC systems can only utilize the light with the wavelength shorted than 600 nm. If the light response is extended to âˆ¼730 nm (corresponding to the bandgap of âˆ¼1.7 eV), the photogenerated charge carries in the single light-absorber still have enough redox ability for water splitting. When the plasmonic and up-conversion materials are applied, the incident light with even longer wavelength can be utilized. In terms of the tandem device with dual or multiple light-absorbers, the band position of each light-absorber only needs to meet the criterion for HER or OER, so semiconductors with narrow bandgaps can be used. As discussed in Fig. 2f, the STH efficiency can reach a maximum of 22.8% with the front and rear light-absorbers respectively having the bandgaps of 1.84 and 1.23 eV. Broadening the light absorption range may lead to the accumulation of photoexcited charges and cause increased charge recombination. Nevertheless, the charge separation and transfer can be accelerated through many strategies such as morphology control and loading cocatalysts. Eventually, the PEC activity can be improved by extending the light absorption range through appropriate optimization.

4. Device engineering for optimized light utilization

A single light-absorber PEC device is hard to achieve efficient unbiased water splitting. Alternatively, the tandem configuration is generally applied to construct viable PEC devices, containing photoanodeâ€“photocathode PEC tandem devices and PEC/PV tandem devices. Importantly, the absorption of incident light can be optimized through dual or multiple absorbers, leading to higher theoretical STH efficiency.102 To obtain efficient PEC water splitting, the management of light absorption between dual light-absorbers should be optimized. Typically for the tandem devices with mode T configuration, the light absorption and transmission of the front photoelectrode should be balanced to guarantee the sufficient light absorption by the rear unit. Therefore, selecting appropriate photoelectrode and PV materials, as well as constructing effective device configurations is crucial.25,103 In addition, the current PEC water splitting is generally studied under one-sun irradiation. Inspired by concentrated photovoltaic (CPV) cells, PEC water splitting under concentrated light have received increasing attention as an efficient light trapping strategy. It is expected that the high photon flux from the dramatically increased light intensity can give rise to favorable properties of charge generation, separation, transfer, and injection.

4.1 Photoanodeâ€“photocathode PEC tandem devices

The utilization of a photoanode and a photocathode to construct the tandem device is an attractive approach for unbiased PEC water splitting without complex power supply. The efficiency is determined by the operating current density (Jop) from the intersection of currentâ€“potential curves of the photoanode and the photocathode. Both the onset potentials and photocurrents of the two photoelectrodes will affect the performance of the PEC tandem devices.103 The ideal Jop is required to approach the plateau photocurrent densities of both photoelectrodes.104 Besides the current matching, the optimization of the tandem device not only depends on the selection of the two photo-absorbers, but also on other parameters affecting the individual performance for each half-reaction. Several models and predictions have been proposed in literature,31 suggesting ideal combinations based on the materials bandgap which is one of the main aspects determining the overall STH. However, as demonstrated by Hu et al., the STH depends on the interplay of all the components, including individual fill factor, electrocatalysts, electrolyte, etc.105 A web-based approach for modeling tandem systems developed by Seger et al. (initially focused on water electrolysis but extendable to other PEC reactions), has shown for instance that besides the top- and bottom-layer bandgap, decreasing the optical thickness of the top absorber (i.e. photon matching) is theoretically a good strategy to gain in the STH for some tandems with specific combinations.106

Currently, different semiconductors such as metal oxides, metal sulfides, IIIâ€“V group materials, have been used to construct PEC tandem devices. Among them, IIIâ€“V materials-based devices have exhibited the best activities due to their remarkable photoelectric properties. Kainthla et al. reported an STH efficiency of 8.2% for the tandem device composed of InP photocathode and GaAs photoanode. The efficiency and stability were greatly improved with the assistance of the manganese oxide protective layer and Pt HER cocatalyst.107 Kang et al. fabricated a series-connected layered tandem cell including GaAs photoelectrodes with an STH efficiency of 13.1%.108 In these printed bifacial GaAs photoelectrodes, sunlight irradiated from a glass substrate to reach the photoelectrode, while the water redox reactions occurred on the opposite side of the incident light (Fig. 8a). The decoupled light absorption and electrocatalysis could avoid the parasitic absorption from electrocatalytic nanoparticles and the protection layer, leading to efficient utilization of incident photons by the light-absorbers. Compared with conventional wafer-based systems, the resulting bifacial GaAs photoelectrodes with well-matched energy band performed improved light absorption and charge transfer property, as well as good stability (Fig. 8b).

4.2 PEC/PV tandem devices

Although the photoanodeâ€“photocathode PEC tandem devices are good options for unassisted water splitting, they need proper matching. To mitigate the requirements of photoelectrodes, PV units can be introduced to provide additional photovoltages, and the generated PEC/PV tandem devices lead to self-biased water splitting. Diverse semiconductor photoelectrodes and PV units can be integrated, which provides versatile combinations. Similar to PEC tandem devices, the efficiency of the PEC/PV devices is determined by the properties of each component, and generally limited by the low photocurrent of the photoelectrodes.102,125 The solar spectrum also needs to be reasonably distributed between the photoelectrode and the PV device to achieve a balance.126

The IIIâ€“V semiconductor photo-absorbers have shown remarkable performance in PEC/PV tandem devices. The efficiency modeling was carried out to guide the bandgap matching of two light-absorbers. 4.3 PEC water splitting under concentrated light

The concentrated photovoltaic (CPV) cells have been maturely used to improve the energy conversion efficiency and decrease the consumption of the raw material to lower the operational costs and to reduce the large area requirements.137 Inspired by the advantages of CPV cells, the utilization of concentrated light in PEC water splitting has recently drawn increasing attention for efficient light trapping.138,139 It is expected that the high flux of photogenerated charge carriers under concentrated light irradiation could improve photocurrent and photovoltage, extend the charge carrier lifetime, and overcome the potential barrier for PEC water splitting.27,28 Additionally, the thermal effects can be induced by the concentrated light. The increased temperature is favorable to the surface reaction and mass transfer. For typical metal oxide photoanode materials such as BiVO4 and Î±-Fe2O3, the electron transfer property is limited by the small-polaron hopping. Enhancing the temperature can significantly improve the transportation of small polarons, which is thus beneficial to the PEC reaction.29

The incident light intensity is closely related to the PEC performance. Bell et al. increased the light intensity to 52 suns for PEC water oxidation over the TiO2 photoanode. It was found that the photocurrent and the light intensity had a sub-linear relationship, and the photocurrent was proportional to the light intensity with an exponential value of 0.627.140 Generally, the generation rate of photoexcited charge carriers is faster than the charge transfer and surface reaction rate.141 Therefore, the relationship of the PEC performance with the light intensity should be affected by the charge transfer and surface reaction properties of the photoelectrodes. HernÃ¡ndez et al. found that after increasing the incident light intensity, the photocurrent enhancement of TiO2 nanotubes was more obvious compared to that of ZnO@TiO2 coreâ€“shell structures. The electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) analysis revealed that these material-dependent behaviors were determined by their different charge transport and interface reaction properties.142 Segev et al. changed the light intensity from 0.5 to 25 suns for PEC water splitting. It was shown that the photocurrent density of Î±-Fe2O3 photoanode increased linearly with the light intensity (Fig. 12a), and the photovoltage scaled logarithmically with the light intensity, displaying a steeper rate than in photovoltaics. Therefore, the energy conversion efficiency could be improved under high light intensity. The improvement of the charge injection efficiency at the interface and the decreased charge recombination rate were considered as the main reasons for the dramatically increased efficiency of Î±-Fe2O3 at high intensity.28 The charge carrier dynamic study by ultrafast spectroscopy revealed that the increased light intensity could extend the lifetime of minority carriers in the Î±-Fe2O3 photoelectrode.143,144 Chen's group also demonstrated that the high light intensity could accelerate the electron transfer from the semiconductor nanostructure to cocatalysts for HER through thermionic emission.27

Briefly, the one-sun irradiation and the room temperature are not always the best reaction conditions for PEC water splitting. The improved light trapping and increased temperature brought by concentrated light can alleviate the problems of unfavorable charge transfer, slow interfacial reaction, and large mass transfer resistance in the PEC water splitting. Therefore, the concentrated light can dramatically improve the photocurrent density. Meanwhile, the PEC system under concentrated light has a great significance to promote the practical application by reducing the materials requested. The current experimental study on PEC water splitting using concentrated light are mainly focused on metal oxide photoelectrodes. The effect on other semiconductor photoelectrodes is still an open question and deserves more investigation in the future.

5. Simulation of light distribution and absorption in PEC water splitting

Theoretical simulation is one effective tool to investigate light distribution and absorption in PEC water splitting, which can provide forward-looking suggestions on the design of efficient materials and devices to promote the light harvesting.22 Maxwell's equations are classic equations to describe the optical field distribution,150 which can be solved by many numerical methods, e.g. the finite element method (FEM),151 the finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) method,152 and the rigorous coupled wave analysis (RCWA).153 The FEM is a basic numerical method to solve partial differential equations (PDEs). The space domain is first discretized into several small elements, and then the PDEs are solved in each small element. The solution in each element is finally connected to form the solution for the studied domain. Usually, the FEM can be applied to solve the electromagnetic phenomenon in complex geometry.154 The FDTD method is based on the direct solution of Maxwell's equations,155 which can calculate electromagnetic scattering and absorption from the time and space scale.152 The RCWA can study the spatio-temporal change of the electromagnetic field. It is a semi-analytical numerical method of solving Maxwell's equations in the Fourier domain.156 In the following sections, the application of these methods for simulating the light distribution and absorption in PEC water splitting will be introduced.

5.1 Finite element method (FEM)

The FEM has been widely applied to simulate the electromagnetic field, because it can employ the flexible mesh in the complex geometries and structures. Haussener and coworkers investigated the effects of a wedge shape on the light absorption of the Î±-Fe2O3 photoanode. As displayed in Fig. 13, the light distribution in the unit wedge structure was simulated and the floquet periodicity was applied as the boundary condition. By solving Maxwell's equations, the electromagnetic field distribution was simulated to determine the optimal wedge shaped photoanode with different reflective layers, wedge angles, and aspect ratios. The FEM simulation results suggested that the wedge shaped Î±-Fe2O3 photoanode would generate both the resonant and geometric light trapping.157 JÃ¤ger and Strehle employed the FEM to evaluate the light absorption of the silicon arrays. In the model, the analytic wavelength of the incident light ranged from 300 to 800 nm. The mesh was finer to 14â€“30 nm, which was far smaller than the minimum studied wavelength. Through the simulation, it was found that the light absorption was affected by the diameter, pitch, length, and shape of the nanoarray. The silicon nanoarray exhibited an excellent optical performance than the silicon film.158 Chen et al. introduced the localized plasmon-induced effects by loading the Au nanoparticles on the ZnO nanorod array to improve the PEC property. To explain the mechanism of plasmon-induced effects, the FEM was applied to simulate the electromagnetic field distribution. It was shown that the plasmonic nanoparticles with the smaller size were the proper and optimum condition for both plasmon-induced effects and PEC activity.159

5.2 Finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) method

Although the FEM can be applied to an arbitrary geometry, it is quite awkward in the simulation of electrically large structures and broadband solar irradiation. These limitations of the FEM can be compensated by the FDTD method.160 Qiu et al. prepared nanoporous Mo doped BiVO4 layer on the cone-shaped nanophotonic structure for water splitting, and investigated the effects of the nanostructure on the light absorption using the FDTD method. The ultraviolet-visible absorption spectra indicated that the light absorption on the nanocone substrate was much higher compared to that on the FTO-coated glass substrate (Fig. 14a). The simulation results revealed that the nanocone structure could enhance the electromagnetic field around them due to the light diffraction and redistribution in the nanocone area (Fig. 14b). Namely, the Mo doped BiVO4 layer could absorb more light on the nanocone substrate than that on the flat substrate, which led to significantly improved PEC performance. By combining the modified nanocone/Mo:BiVO4 photoanode with a PSC, the tandem cell exhibited a high STH of 6.2% for unassisted water splitting.161 Yalamanchili et al. used the arrays of TiO2 nanocones to transmit more than 85% of incident light to the underlying Si light-absorber. The 3D FDTD simulation over the spectral range from 400 to 1100 nm revealed that the TiO2 nanocones could act as the waveguide to transmit the broadband light to the semiconductor, and reduce the light interaction with the metal catalyst layer.162

Braun and coworkers prepared Si/WO3 hierarchically structured coreâ€“shell microwire arrays as the photoelectrode for water splitting. The outer shell was made of WO3 with a porous structure, and the core consisted of Si microwires. By the FDTD simulation, it was shown that the porous WO3 shell could effectively reduce the light reflection. It was revealed that the CdS/Au PTP photoanode could achieve high light absorption in wide incident angle and broadband. The surface plasmon effects at the CdSâ€“Au interface contributed to the optical absorption enhancement above 450 nm, while the optical absorption enhancement below 450 nm resulted from the photonic modes.165

5.3 Rigorous coupled wave analysis (RCWA)

Compared to the FEM and FDTD methods requiring large amount of computational resources, the RCWA is a simplified method and could be applied to some specific cases, such as periodically structured films.166 Song and coworkers employed both the FDTD method and the RCWA to simulate the electromagnetic field distribution in planar, cylindrical, truncate cone, and cone GaN nanostructures. The RCWA was used to simulate diffraction efficiency under the periodic situation, and the FDTD method was used to calculate the electromagnetic field in the broad computational region. From the RCWA simulation, it was indicated that the nanostructured truncated cone could increase light absorption by reducing the reflection of the semiconductor (Fig. 15a). The FDTD results revealed that the light absorption was mainly concentrated on the bulk, except the truncated cone (Fig. 15b). The carrier separation and transfer in the bulk was unfavorable, but the truncated nanocone could increase the carrier utility by reducing the carrier migration length.167 The additional reflective layer and the surface roughness of the photoelectrode also affect the light absorption. The RCWA was used to model the light absorption in the BiVO4/SnO2 flat structure, BiVO4/SnO2/Au flat structure, and BiVO4/SnO2/Au textured structure. The PDMS is applied as the textured substrate. It was revealed that the Au reflective layer would increase the light trapping, and the textured film could produce the diffraction pattern and trap more light, which was coincident with the experimental results.81

Besides the above three methods, some groups also use analytic solutions or other numerical methods to calculate the light absorption in the PEC cell. Lee et al. studied the effect of the thickness of TiO2 hollow microspheres on their light absorption and scattering using the exact solution for the Mie theory. When the thickness ratio of inner core to whole particle radii was less than 0.4, the hollow microspheres could achieve the same light absorption capacity as the solid spheres. This finding not only helped to save the materials used for PEC reaction, but also provided a guide in the design of efficient hollow spheres.168 Zhao et al. used the ray optics to investigate the effect of the geometry on the light absorption in the microstructured photoelectrode. The simulation results indicated that the shadow produced by the micropillar geometry could lower the PEC efficiency when reducing the pillar pitch, and the microcone could avoid the negative effect of the shadow.169

In short, the FEM can be applied to the arbitrary geometry and structure with high accuracy, whereas it is not suitable to the electrically large structures and broadband irradiation. In contrast, the FDTD method is effective to calculate the electromagnetic wave propagation in electrically large structures and broadband irradiation. However, its accuracy is lower than the FEM, and it is restricted in the uncomplicated geometry and structure. The RCWA performs the worst accuracy for the electromagnetic field simulation among these methods, but it has the highest computational efficiency.

6. Conclusions and prospects

To conclude, several feasible light management approaches for PEC water splitting from the aspects of materials and devices were summarized. In terms of the material design, the morphology control is widely used to improve the light trapping, and the delicately developed 1D, 2D, and 3D nanostructures can attain a good balance of light absorption, charge transfer, and surface reaction. Under these conditions, improved PEC performances for water splitting are achieved. The surface-plasmon resonance effect is a promising approach to increase light harvesting, because plasmonic materials have tunable absorption in wide spectrum and large absorption cross section. The surface-plasmon resonance effects can improve the PEC through light scatting and trapping, plasmon-induced resonance energy transfer, and hot electron injection. Several other approaches, e.g., doping, forming solid solution, and photon up-conversion are also frequently employed to enhance light trapping or to extend the light absorption range. In addition to the material choices, device engineering is necessary to achieve efficient water splitting. To adequately utilize the solar energy, photoanodeâ€“photocathode PEC tandem devices and PEC/PV tandem devices are good choices because they can extend light absorption range by the frequency division absorption. The utilization of concentrated light in PEC water splitting can lead to high flux of photogenerated charge carriers and increased temperature of the reaction system. Accordingly, the increased photocurrent and photovoltage, improved charge transfer property, and accelerated surface reaction are achieved, making it as another efficient light management strategy for enhanced PEC property.

It should be noted that our ultimate goal is to design feasible PEC devices for the practical application. High-performance materials with good light absorption property are the foundation for a good PEC device. After applying the light management strategies from the material aspect, the single photoelectrode can achieve enhanced PEC property. However, when the optimized photoelectrode is assembled into a device, the overall PEC activity can be unsatisfactory due to the mismatching of different components in the device. Taking the photoanodeâ€“photocathode PEC tandem device in mode T as an example, the light absorption and transmission over the front photoelectrode should be balanced to ensure the sufficient light absorption by the rear photoelectrode. In this case, the property of the front photoelectrode in the device can be different from the optimal property when separately tested. Therefore, the light management strategies from materials and device engineering should be carefully coordinated to obtain high-performance devices, by considering the used material and device architecture.

Theoretical simulation is one effective tool to investigate light distribution and absorption in PEC water splitting, which can provide constructive suggestions on the design of efficient materials and devices. The FEM, FDTD method, and RCWA are the most studied methods for simulating the electromagnetic field in the PEC cell. The FEM can be applied to solve Maxwell's equations in complex geometry. The FDTD method is suitable to describe the electromagnetic field in broad ranges. The RCWA is a simplified method with low accuracy, yet, requiring less computational resources. To accurately determine the light absorption and optical properties of the photoelectrode, theoretical methods should be combined with experimental approaches, such as optical absorption spectroscopy and photoluminescence (PL) spectroscopy. For transparent materials, the light absorption spectrum can be acquired in transmission. When the material is non-transparent or suffers severe light scattering, the absorption property can be evaluated by the diffusion reflectance spectroscopy. The bandgap of the semiconductor can be determined from the absorption spectrum. Meanwhile, the complementary information about the bandgap can be offered by the PL spectroscopy. The light absorption property can be affected by the defect states. To determine the defect states, the absorption spectroscopy, PL spectroscopy, and electron paramagnetic resonance spectroscopy can be utilized. Furthermore, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy and ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy are useful methods to estimate the valence band positions. Typically, the light scattering of plasmonic nanoparticles can be measured by dark-field microscopic imaging and spectroscopy, which affords a feasible approach to uncover the working principle of plasmonic nanoparticles in the photoelectrode at the single-particle level.

To date, various strategies have been applied to promote the efficiency of PEC cells. However, practical applications of PEC water splitting still face many challenges, and the STH efficiency of low-cost and robust PEC devices have not yet achieved 10%, which would start to make them competitive. As the primary step of the PEC reaction, the light absorption plays a significant role in determining the overall efficiency. Based on the above discussion, some issues of light management in PEC water splitting can be addressed to further promote their performance in the future:

(i) The morphology control of the PEC materials can effectively promote light harvesting. Learning from nature to design tunable hierarchical structures for enhanced light trapping should be a feasible approach and deserves continuous studies. Combining optics theory into PEC water splitting is important to improve light harvesting. For instance, adding appropriate metal reflective layer could reduce the light transmission, and the different refractive indexes between the medium and the semiconductor could lead to multiple reflections inside the semiconductor light-absorber. By delicately tuning the configuration of tandem devices, the incident light can be repeatedly absorbed multiples times to minimize energy loss in the system. To optimize the light distribution inside the photoreactor, optical fiber can be used to guide the light propagation. However, the application of the optical fibers inside the PEC device still needs to be explored. The efficiency of PEC water splitting is based on the combined effect of light absorption, charge separation and transfer, and surface reactivity. Therefore, when developing effective strategies for improved light harvesting, the efficiencies of charge separation and transfer, as well as the surface reaction should be considered. Meanwhile, the modification on the photoelectrode materials and PEC devices should be simultaneously carried out to improve the overall efficiency.

(ii) Because the applicable photoelectrodes generally have multi-layer structures, including the light-absorber layer, buffer layer, protection layer, and catalyst layer, the light absorption and transmission of each layer should be well balanced to insure the sufficient absorption by the light-absorber. In some scenarios, the front and back illumination will lead to dramatically different PEC performances. A promising idea is to decouple light absorption and electrocatalysis, which would avoid the parasitic absorption from electrocatalytic nanoparticles and the protection layer, giving rise to efficient utilization of incident photons by the light-absorbers. When constructing tandem devices, special attention should be paid on the light absorption by the aqueous solution. The light transmission loss in the aqueous electrolyte will lead to the poor absorption of the component with narrow bandgap, thus decreasing the overall performance of the PEC device.

(iii) Although some progress in PEC water splitting under concentrated light irradiation has been achieved, the underlying mechanism is still unclear, especially for the properties of high-flux charge carriers. The relationship of the PEC performance with the light intensity and reaction temperature deserves deep investigation. Typically, the poor charge transfer property of metal oxide light-absorbers is mainly attributed to the slow-moving small polarons. The increased temperature and photon flux can potentially increase the conductivity of the charge carriers. To disclose the underlying mechanism, advanced in situ characterization methods such as in situ X-ray absorption spectroscopy, in situ Raman spectroscopy, and in situ electron paramagnetic spectroscopy would be conducive.

(iv) More theoretical studies in both material design and device engineering need to be carried out to assist the investigation of light-related properties in PEC water splitting. The theoretical analysis can help to visualize the advantages of different modification approaches, and ultimately offer guidelines for developing efficient PEC materials and devices. Because the PEC system involves different spatial scales, from microscale materials to macroscale devices, the simulation of light distribution and absorption should be varied depending on the spatial scale. For nanoscale materials in the photoelectrode, the wave optics theories can be utilized to simulate the electromagnetic field distribution. In the design of macroscale PEC devices, the geometric optics theories can be used to optimize the light absorption. In addition, the PEC reaction takes place in the multi-physical field. The light distribution and absorption can be affected by other physical fields such as the flow field and the electric field. Therefore, the multi-physical field information should be considered when carrying out the optical simulation.

By addressing the synergistic effects of light absorption, charge transfer, and surface reactivity, this review covers the most effective light management approaches, which act as comprehensive knowledge and guidance to develop and fabricate more efficient and stable PEC materials, systems, and devices for solar water splitting.
