Abstract

The study of adhesion behaviors on solid–liquid surfaces plays an important role in scientific research and development in various fields, such as medicine, biology and agriculture. The contact angle and sliding angle of the liquid on the solid surface are commonly used to characterize and measure the wettability of a particular surface. They have a wide range of values, which results in different wettability. It boils down to the adhesion of solid surfaces to liquids. This feature article is aimed at revealing the essence of the adhesion behavior from the aspects of controlling the chemical composition or changing the geometrical microstructure of the surface, and reviewing the natural sources, wetting models, preparation methods and applications of four kinds of typical solid–liquid surfaces (low-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces, high-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces, slippery liquid-infused porous surfaces (SLIPS) and hydrophilic/superhydrophilic surfaces). Last, a summary and outlook on this field are given to point out the current challenges and the potential research directions of surface adhesion in the coming future.

1. Introduction

Nature runs smoothly under the coordination of numerous species. In addition to maintaining the ecological balance, some of them have inspired human beings in functions and structures. Learning from nature is the eternal driving force of technological progress. The development of bionics has promoted the progress of various industries and improved people's lives. In nature, wetting of a solid by a liquid is a common interface phenomenon. Crystal clear water drops roll on a lotus leaf, taking away dirt and dust.1,2 Dewdrops on a rose sparkle on the surface of the petals.7 The mouth of the pitcher plant secretes and holds a smooth mucus, which makes the caught insects slip into the bottle.10 Aquatic plants, including Anubias barteri, reduce gas exchange and photosynthesis on the plant surface.13 There are diverse adhesion mechanisms between the liquid and solid behind these wonderful and unique wetting phenomena. With the rapid development of materials science, chemistry and other related disciplines, inspired by surfaces with special wettability in nature, and guided by the classical wettability model,15–18 the preparation and application of special wettable surfaces are becoming increasingly diversified, and they have penetrated into all aspects of people's daily life. This review focuses on four special wettable surfaces (low-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces, high-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces, slippery liquid-infused porous surfaces (SLIPS) and hydrophilic/superhydrophilic surfaces). The preparation of low-adhesion/high-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces is mainly via constructing rough microstructures of corresponding sizes and modifying with a low surface energy substance, and the preparation of SLIPS additionally requires the injection of low-viscosity fluids and low-volatility lubricants. A super-hydrophilic surface requires strong attraction between the surface and the liquid molecules. The applications of these special wettable surfaces involve self-cleaning, anti-fouling, anti-icing, oil–water separation, microfluidic sensors, etc.44,45,47,50,52–55

Hereinafter, this review reveals the adhesion nature of four surfaces from four aspects: reviewing natural sources, designing theoretical models, selecting preparation methods, and generalizing application areas (Table 1). Finally, the areas to be improved and the future development direction of this research field are discussed.

2. Low-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces

2.1. Sources of structure in nature

It is the traditional Chinese culture's praise of a virtuous person that the lotus grows out of the mud and is not stained with it. This phenomenon of self-cleaning has aroused great interest among researchers. With the development of scanning electron microscopy studies of biological surfaces, Barthlott and Neinhuis concluded that the water resistance of many terrestrial and aquatic plants represented by the lotus leaf is due to the surface microstructure papillae and special cuticle which is composed of soluble lipids embedded in a polyester matrix.1 These two factors give the plant such a surface with a high contact angle (CA) of more than 150° and a low sliding angle (SA) of less than 10°, called a low-adhesion superhydrophobic surface. The process that makes plants self-cleaning can be expressed as that epicuticular wax crystalloids provide plants with highly effective hydrophobicity,2 and then the rough papillary structure causes the droplet to have less contact with the plant surface, the droplet only stays at the top of the papillary epidermal cells, and, when the droplet rolls on the leaf surface, it takes away the contaminating particles. In a follow-up study, the researchers concluded two factors that contribute to the superhydrophobic nature of plant surfaces: a chemical composition with a low surface energy, and a geometric structure with micro–nano and micro-line structure.63,64

Fig. 1 shows water contact angle (WCA) measurements of some plant surfaces and insect body parts, as well as scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images. The lotus leaf is a representative plant with micro and nanostructure. It can be found from the SEM images that there are 70–100 nm hydrophobic waxy particles and lots of randomly distributed nanorod-like structures with an average size of 50 nm found in the grooves and ridges of 3–10 mm on the lotus leaf surface, which makes a WCA of about 161 ± 2° and a SA of about 3° (Fig. 1a). There are also plentiful superhydrophobic plants with diverse microstructures, such as the ramee leaf. It is interesting that the front surface of the ramee leaf has hydrophilic properties, while, for the reverse side, from the SEM image (Fig. 1b) it can be seen that fibers with a diameter of 1–2 mm are evenly distributed on the surface. This micro-line structure can also form a superhydrophobic surface, with a WCA of 164 ± 2°.64,65 Another common plant with a binary structure similar to the lotus leaf is the rice leaf, which has papillae with an average diameter of 5–8 μm and a WCA of up to 157 ± 2° on the surface (Fig. 1c), but the papillae are different from the lotus leaf. Arranged in a fixed direction, the SA in this direction is 4° and perpendicular to this direction is 12°, making it difficult for water droplets to roll. Besides rice leaves, the feathers of waterfowl or ducks also have anisotropy of water droplet rolling (Fig. 1d).66 Ducks often play in the water and forage for food, but their feathers will not be wetted by water. This is because the feathers have neatly arranged micron and sub-micron strips, which give the feathers good hydrophobicity and air permeability. And this orderly arranged microstructure can make it easy for water droplets to roll away along the strip to achieve directional drainage. For winged insects, including Odonata (dragonfly), Hymenoptera (bee), Trichoptera (moth), Lepidoptera (butterfly), Homoptera (cicada), Coleoptera (beetle) and Diptera (Mosquitoes, flies), etc, they have microstructures of various shapes distributed on their wings. The resulting superhydrophobicity makes the surface of the wings self-cleaning to maintain their flight ability.67,68Fig. 1e shows the microstructure and special wettability of the cicada wing surface. The CA of water droplets on the surface is as high as 145.6°. For water striders, they need waterproof legs to stand and jump on water. The leg of water striders is covered with lots of tiny chitinous setae, which are tilted about 20° relative to the surface of the legs. These setae are about 50 μm in length and less than 3 μm in diameter. It can be derived from the SEM image (Fig. 1f) that the surfaces of microsetae have tiny nano-grooves to accommodate more air, and the legs have rough micro–nano structures. In subsequent research, Wei and Chen et al.3–6 measured the CA of water striders in contact with the deformed water surface at 168.8° and derived a mechanism related to the adhesion of water when they jumped on the water surface.

2.2. Wetting model and adhesion mechanism

Simplifying the structures of low-adhesion superhydrophobic plants and animals in nature into explicit wetting models is an essential step in biomimetic research to prepare materials with corresponding structures and functions. There are many factors that affect the wetting state of solids, including roughness, chemical heterogeneity, surface reconstruction, swelling and dissolution. Among these factors, the roughness of the structure has a large effect on the wetting behavior of the surface; it is necessary to construct theoretical models to explain and quantify the wetting behavior of the surface according to the wetting state.70 For low-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces, the effect of roughness on the superhydrophobic performance is that air remains trapped below the drop to form a nano-scale air layer, which causes the drop to sit partially on air.71 As early as 1944, this phenomenon was reduced to the Cassie–Baxter model.15 Before the Cassie equation and Cassie model are obtained, it is necessary to understand the generation of the Cassie contact angle. The actual surface is rough or uneven, on which the resting liquid droplets can be in a stable equilibrium state, that is, the lowest energy state, or can be in a metastable equilibrium state, which is isolated from its adjacent state by an energy barrier, and then hysteresis of the contact angle will occur.72,73 As shown in Fig. 2a, droplets on an inclined plane can explain this intuitively.
Fig. 2 (a) The droplet is on a tilted plate, θa is the advancing angle and θr is the receding angle. (b) Microstructural diagram of a droplet in the Cassie–Baxter state.

For a droplet with a stable CA on the horizontal plane, if the surface is ideally smooth and uniform, when a small amount of liquid is added to the droplet, the leading edge of the droplet perimeter expands forward, but still maintains the original CA. On withdrawing a small amount of liquid from the droplet, the perimeter front of the droplet shrinks backward, but still maintains the original CA. If the surface is rough or uneven, adding a little liquid to the droplet will only make the droplet higher and the perimeter will not move, which will increase the CA. The CA at this time is called the advancing angle θadv. If enough liquid is added, the perimeter of the droplet will suddenly creep forward, and the angle at which this sudden motion occurs is called the maximum advancing angle θadv,max. If a small amount of liquid is taken out of a droplet, the droplet becomes flatter without the perimeter moving, and the CA becomes smaller. The CA at this time is called a receding angle θrec. When enough liquid is pumped away, the perimeter of the droplet will suddenly contract. The angle at which this sudden contraction occurs is called the minimum receding angle θrec,min. In this process, the difference in the energy barrier between the forward and backward droplets causes contact angle hysteresis, which is also a measure of the energy dissipated when a droplet flows along a solid surface. The Cassie contact angle is caused by the heterogeneity of the solid surface. Because of the energy barrier, the front of the liquid often stays at the interface of the phase. The advancing angle often reflects the area with a lower surface energy, or the part of the solid surface with a weak affinity for the liquid. The receding angle often reflects the area with a higher surface energy or the part of the solid surface that has a strong affinity for the liquid. For a surface composed of two surfaces with different wetting properties, the area fractions are assumed to be f1 and f2, respectively, and the following relations are obtained:

 
cosθCB = f1cosθ1 + f2cosθ2(1)
Eqn (1) above is the Cassie–Baxter equation, where θCB is the apparent contact angle of the droplet on a multi-phase rough surface, and θ1 and θ2 are the intrinsic contact angles of the liquid on the first surface (S1) and the second surface (S2), respectively. The model of the water droplet wetting state in the Cassie–Baxter (CB) state is shown in Fig. 2b. In this state, the cavities on the rough surface are filled with air, and the liquid droplets cannot penetrate into the cavities. It can be seen from Fig. 2b that S1 and S2 are composite interfaces composed of solid–liquid and liquid–air. Therefore, fSL and fLA (fSL + fLA = 1) are used to replace f1 and f2, and cosθ1 is the intrinsic contact angle of the droplet on the rough solid surface; at the same time, the concept of phase area fraction r is introduced to deal with the effect of chemically heterogeneous surfaces on wettability.74–77 The CA between the droplet and the air is 180°, and thus cosθ2 is −1. The above parameters are introduced into eqn (1):
cosθCB = rfSLcosθ1 + (1 − fSL)cosθCB2
i.e.:

eqn (2) explains the mathematical relationship between the parameters in the Cassie–Baxter state. After understanding the theoretical model, the lotus effect can be explained more clearly, that is, there are many tiny papillae on the surface of the lotus leaf, and the concave part of the papillae is filled with air, so that a nano-thick air layer is formed. When dust and raindrops of a size larger than this structure fall on the surface of the lotus leaf, the water droplets form a spherical shape under the action of their surface tension. And the water ball absorbs dust and cleans the leaf surface during the rolling process, which is the so-called lotus effect.

2.3. Fabrication of low-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces

Inspired by the superhydrophobic phenomena in nature, researchers found that there are two main effective methods for preparing superhydrophobic materials: to prepare suitable roughness with a micro–nano composite structure on a hydrophobic substrate or to prepare the corresponding roughness and modify it with low surface energy substances.78–81 According to these two principles, researchers have adopted many methods to construct superhydrophobic surfaces, among which the commonly used methods are electrodeposition, chemical vapor deposition, sol–gel, layer-by-layer assembly, solution-immersion, the electrospinning method, etc.
Electrodeposition. Electrodeposition is a cheap, simple, efficient method and is not limited by the shape of the substrate. Liu et al.19 used a solution of cerium nitrate hexahydrate and tetracyclate dissolved in ethanol to rapidly prepare a superhydrophobic surface with a CA as high as 159.8° and SA less than 2° on a Mg–Mn–Ce alloy plate by electrochemical deposition (Fig. 3A and B). Yan et al.82 prepared an alkyl pyrrole film with an array structure on ITO glass. Due to the large roughness and low surface energy, this pyrrole film has good superhydrophobic characteristics. Wang et al.83 prepared a cobalt-nickel coating of tungsten carbide particles with a solid layered microsphere structure on a carbon steel substrate by electrochemical deposition. After a linear wear test, it was found that, when the content of tungsten carbide is 9.8 wt%, the CoNi/WC coating exhibits excellent superhydrophobicity and solves the problem that the superhydrophobic surface is limited by the mechanical durability in the application process (Fig. 3C). Although the electrochemical deposition method can quickly and easily prepare superhydrophobic coatings, the basic requirement that the materials must have electrical conductivity makes many materials unable to be converted into superhydrophobic materials by this method.

The chemical vapor deposition method (CVD). CVD uses a chemical reaction at a gas phase or a gas–solid interface to form a solid deposit (thin film). Due to the variety of deposits that can be deposited by this method, and the fact that the purity of the thin-film coating obtained by the reaction is high and the chemical composition and crystal structure of the coating can be controlled by adjusting the deposition parameters, the CVD method has received extensive attention in the field of preparing superhydrophobic coatings.20–23 Liu et al.39 first reported the use of CVD to prepare columnar, island, honeycomb, and other arrays of carbon nanotube films on quartz substrates, and showed that this array of micro–nano structures is the main reason for generating contact angles up to 164.3° and sliding angles less than 5°. Rezaei et al.87 used ethyl orthosilicate as a precursor, vinyl trimethylsilane as a modifier, and ammonia as a catalyst to prepare superhydrophobic surfaces on glass and aluminum sheets. It is worth mentioning that CVD is an effective method for preparing transparent superhydrophobic coatings.88–90 Mcgaughey et al.86 used the CVD method to coat two porous substrates (hydrophilic cellulose acetate (CA) and hydrophobic polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE)) with a cross-linked fluoropolymer to improve the moisture resistance of the membrane. The experimental results show that the coated membranes have higher moisture resistance than the uncoated membranes (Fig. 3G), which solves the problem of difficulty in increasing the hydrophobicity of porous membranes. In future membrane design, it provides a new way to replace perfluorinated membranes with environmentally friendly materials. Although the CVD method does not have high requirements for material selection, the required reaction temperature is 850–1100 °C, and many substrate materials cannot withstand high temperatures. At this time, plasma or laser-assisted technology must be used to reduce the deposition temperature, thus increasing the operating cost, and the method is not suitable for large-area preparation, and the prepared superhydrophobic coating has a weak binding force.
The sol–gel method. The sol–gel method involves the use of the target product as a precursor to carry out chemical reactions such as hydrolysis and condensation in solution to form a stable and transparent sol system. After the sol is aged, the colloidal particles slowly polymerize to form a gel with a three-dimensional network structure. In recent years, sol–gel technology has not only been successfully applied in the synthesis of glass, functional ceramic powders and composite oxide materials which are difficult to prepare by traditional methods, but has also been recognized as an ideal method for preparing superhydrophobic materials with controlled roughness structure.24–27 Xu and colleagues catalyzed the preparation of highly transparent and superhydrophobic anti-reflective coatings under alkaline conditions by using methyl triethoxysilane and ethyl orthosilicate as reactants through the sol–gel method.91 Li et al.84 synthesized a sol containing a CNT–SiO2 nano/micro composite structure, and then sprayed it on the surface of a glass substrate to form a film. After fluorination, the WCA was as high as 167°. The FTIR spectra showed that the surface of the nanotube was rich in hydroxyl groups (Fig. 3D), which can be condensed with the hydroxyl groups on the surface of silica to obtain a micro–nano rough structure connected by covalent bonds, which is superhydrophobic. Justin et al.85 prepared silica aerogels and xerogels using tetramethoxysilane (TMOS) and methyltrimethoxysilane (MTMS) sol–gels. After being prepared into a powder, a double-sided adhesive was used to bond to a glass surface to test the hydrophobic performance. The results showed that, when the value of MTMS was 0.1–0.75, the aerogel powder achieved a superhydrophobic effect in the entire range, and the WCA is 167–170° (Fig. 3E). Although the sol–gel method has good controllability and does not require high reaction conditions, there are still some shortcomings. For example, the use of organic materials as raw materials is harmful to health, the entire sol–gel method takes a long time, and the presence of a large number of micropores in the gel causes the surface to shrink during drying, affecting the stability of the coating.
Layer-by-layer assembly method. Apart from the preparation methods of these common low-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces, there are some methods that deserve attention. The research team of Sun reported a method of using layer-by-layer assembly to grow silica beads with a particle size of 220 nm onto silica beads with a particle size of 660 nm. The system is a raspberry-like binary micro–nano hierarchical structure. After grafting with a hydrophobic reagent, the CA of the system reaches 157°, and the SA is less than 5° (Fig. 3F).28
Solution-immersion method. Liu et al.29 prepared superhydrophobic surfaces of zinc, silicon and steel by a simple solution-immersion method. They immersed zinc, silicon or steel in 1H,1H,2H,2H-(perfluorooctyl)-trichlorosilane and PFTS/methanol solution, respectively, and controlled the immersion time (24 h) and immersion temperature (50 °C) to form micron-level balls (1.7–2 μm in diameter) on the surface of the substrate.
Electrospinning method. Sheng et al.92 prepared a polyacrylonitrile film using the electrospinning method, and introduced amino silicone oil as a low surface energy substance and silica particles with a multi-layer rough structure to prepare a strong superhydrophobic microporous film. It can be seen that there are still many problems to be solved in the industrialization process of superhydrophobic materials. The preparation process of many superhydrophobic materials requires complicated and time-consuming steps and expensive raw materials and equipment. The stability of superhydrophobic materials is also a bottleneck in the development of this field.

2.4. Applications of low-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces

2.4.1. Self-cleaning. Superhydrophobic surfaces have a rough micro–nano structure and a low surface energy substance, which makes them have the lotus effect. Pollutants such as dust and mud on the surface can be taken away by rolling water droplets, so that the surface of the material remains clean. The self-cleaning properties of superhydrophobic materials have great application value in fabrics, smart equipment, building exterior walls, and high-altitude glass that is difficult to clean.44,45 By spraying organosilane-modified alumina particles on conductive glass, Yoon et al.93 prepared a highly transparent self-cleaning superhydrophobic surface. When the droplets roll off, they can take away the carbon black powder with greater surface adhesion. Yao et al.94 used a perfluorosilane-coated titanium dioxide nanoparticle ethanol suspension as a coating to spray or immerse on the substrate to prepare a self-cleaning surface with strong mechanical properties that can also run in oil. Milles et al.95 prepared superhydrophobic pure aluminum textured self-cleaning surfaces by surface laser processing methods such as direct laser writing (DLW) and direct laser interference pattern (DLIP), and then contaminated the surface with manganese oxide and polyamide particles with sizes of 1 μm and 100 μm. Then they used nine drops of water (10 μL) for washing, and the results show that the residual pollution is as low as 1% (Fig. 4A).

2.4.2. Corrosion prevention. A superhydrophobic material on a metal substrate does not easily react with water and corrode due to a layer of air on the surface. Hydrophobic treatment of the metal surface improves the corrosion resistance of the material, extends the life of the material and saves economic expenses.46 Sun et al.96 constructed roughness on an aluminum sheet substrate by hydrochloric acid etching, and used polydimethylsiloxane as a low surface energy substance to prepare a superhydrophobic aluminum sheet with corrosion resistance. Liang et al.97 combined the traditional Stöber method with the in situ growth method to prepare a 55 μm-thick film layer on the surface of an aluminum substrate with a WCA of 155° (Fig. 4B). A corrosion test in 3.5% NaCl solution shows that the corrosion potential of the substrate has a large positive shift, showing superior corrosion resistance – the Bode plots of the untreated aluminum and superhydrophobic sample are shown in Fig. 4D. In general, the impedance behavior in the low frequency region reflects the properties of the protective layer in the electrolytic solution. As can be seen from Fig. 4D-a, the |Z| value of the superhydrophobic sample decreased gradually to approach the value of the untreated aluminum with the evolution of the immersion time. This was in good agreement with the result from the Nyquist plot and potentiodynamic polarization curve (Fig. 4D-b), indicating that the superhydrophobic coating film provided the aluminum substrate with excellent corrosion resistant properties even after immersion in 3.5 wt% NaCl solution for 1440 min. 2.4.3. Anti-icing. There is an air layer between the surface of a low-adhesive superhydrophobic material and the droplets, which makes it possible to delay freezing or roll the water droplets off the surface before freezing. Therefore, superhydrophobic materials have great significance in anti-icing applications of power systems, communication systems and roads.47 Li et al.99 prepared a 2–5 nm silica sol, and then dispersed 20–30 nm silica powder in the sol, sprayed it on the surface of a glass insulator together, and finally used perfluorosiloxane (PDTS) for surface chemical modification. A superhydrophobic coating with a WCA of 163° was prepared. Under the test temperature of −3 to 1 °C, relative humidity of 87–91%, and wind speed of 5.9–6.6 m s−1, only scattered ice appears within 5–6 h, showing excellent anti-icing performance. Farhadi and his colleagues prepared micro–nano rough superhydrophobic coatings by spin coating or dip coating, and used an organosilane, a fluoropolymer or silicone rubber as the top layer.100 The prepared material was placed in a wind tunnel at −10 °C for an anti-icing performance test. The results showed that, after up to 11 cycles of icing/deicing, the performance of the superhydrophobic material gradually declined, pointing to the research focus of the durability of the anti-icing performance. Later researchers prepared superhydrophobic surfaces based on the assembly of sprayed SiO2 nanoparticles. Tests have shown that when a slide with a superhydrophobic surface is exposed to an ultra-low temperature of −40 °C, the freezing time of water droplets is extended to 372.0 s. The superhydrophobic surface has a good free energy barrier and a low heat transfer rate, so that the surface has good mechanical durability while having ice resistance (Fig. 4F).101
2.4.4. Drag reduction. The air layer on a superhydrophobic surface can greatly reduce the resistance of the liquid to its movement, so that the superhydrophobic material has a good drag reduction and floatation effect, which is of great importance for reducing the water resistance during hull operation.48 Wang et al.48 prepared alkali-etched copper alloys and modified them with perfluorosilane to prepare superhydrophobic copper surfaces. A water jet system was used to study the frictional resistance of the liquid to its surface. The results showed that the superhydrophobic surface had a drag reduction effect of 40% at a low wall shear rate and a drag reduction effect of 20% at a high wall shear rate. Moaven et al.102 used butyl orthotitanate hydrolysis to prepare a sol. After dilution, it was spin-coated on a sandblasted aluminum alloy to form a film. The WCA was 157–160°, and the drag reduction effect for laminar and turbulent flow was 30% and 15%, respectively. Zhao et al.103 implemented ultraviolet laser texturing on a stainless steel surface, and then spin-coated a reagent to reduce the surface free energy, thereby obtaining a superhydrophobic stainless steel surface with a CA of up to 161.5°. After testing the sliding performance of the surface, it was shown that, when the laser-textured scanning interval is 80 μm, the drag reduction effect of the surface is increased by 29.7% (Fig. 4G).

3.2. Wetting model and adhesion mechanism

The superhydrophobicity of water droplets on a solid surface can be explained by two basic models: the air enters the texture groove, allowing the water droplets to freely advance and retreat at different contact line points – the Cassie–Baxter model; and the water droplets enter the grooves of the coarse structure with a larger size, so that the water droplets “stick” to the solid surface – the Wenzel state. In the Wenzel state, fSL can be changed to 1 in eqn (2) because the liquid and air contact parts are not considered.16 At this time, the Cassie–Baxter equation becomes the Wenzel equation (eqn (3)). The Wenzel equation illustrates the relationship between the change in WCA and roughness.
 
cosθw = cosθy(3)

In the above equation, θw is the Wenzel contact angle and θy is the Young contact angle. Since the actual surface has a certain roughness, the actual contact area is larger than the ideal surface, and r represents the roughness, that is, the ratio of the real area to the projected area. However, the Wenzel equation is not valid for porous rough surfaces; when the plant leaf is regarded as a solid filled with grooves, the solid grooves are wetted by the liquid, and the solid platform remains dry. This phenomenon is called the Cassie impregnating state. The wetting specific surface area of the solid surface in the Cassie impregnating state is smaller than that in the Wenzel state and greater than that in the Cassie–Baxter state. Combining the adhesion of the droplets in the three states, the conclusion can be drawn that the degree of hysteresis of the contact angle, that is, the adhesion of the droplets, depends on the wetting specific surface area of the solid surface. The greater the specific surface area of wetting, the higher the droplet adhesion. The Cassie impregnating equation is converted on the basis of the Cassie–Baxter equation.75,76,104 For eqn (1), f1 is still fSL, f2 = fLA = 1 − fSL, and θ2 is the contact angle of the liquid and air (θLA). Because in the Cassie impregnating state, the hole is filled with water (θLA = 0°, cosθLA = 1), so the Cassie impregnating equation is as follows:

 
cosθCB = 1 + fSL(cosθ1 − 1)(4)

The model of the water droplet wetting state in the Wenzel state and the Cassie impregnating state is shown in Fig. 6a and b. It is worth mentioning that, besides the periodic rough micro–nano structure grooves, the other factor affecting the droplets staying on the petal surface is the volume of the droplets. For small droplets, their weight is smaller than the surface tension, so they can adhere to the surface. When the volume of the water droplet is about 10 μL, its weight and surface tension are balanced, and the water droplet will fall.

A slippery liquid infused porous surface (SLIPS) has the functions of liquid repellency and self-cleaning like a superhydrophobic surface, but the difference is that a superhydrophobic surface repels the liquid by the air layer formed by the micro–nano structure, and a SLIPS is a low surface energy liquid-perfused porous rough structure, so as to obtain a homogeneous and stable solid–liquid membrane layer, thereby playing the role of lyophobicity.119 SLIPS, a peculiar structure, can also be found in nature – Nepenthes. 4.2. Wetting model and adhesion mechanism

The rough liquid injection surface represented by the inner wall of Nepenthes and the epidermis of earthworms can significantly reduce the sliding angle and hysteresis angle of droplets. Therefore, it has excellent self-cleaning and self-repairing properties, and has broad application prospects in the fields of self-cleaning coatings, marine antifouling, and biomedicine.124 It is very important to understand the model, construction mechanism and principle of SLIPS. The system on SLIPS involves four different phases, i.e. the solid, lubricant, working liquid impinging on the lubricant surface and surrounding air; the interaction of the complex interface between the four phases and the geometry of the solid play a decisive role in the effectiveness and stability of SLIPS.17

The geometry of a solid surface can be described by a geometric factor R (R = r − 1/r − ∅, the range of R varies from 0 on a smooth plane to an extremely rough surface 1); r represents the roughness of the solid surface, that is, the ratio of the actual solid surface area to the projected area, and ∅ represents the proportion of the solid surface in contact with the bottom of the impact droplet. In addition, the interface energy γ (taking γx and γxy as examples, γx refers to the surface energy between the x phase and the surroundings, and γxy refers to the interface energy between phases x and y), and the contact angle θ between the four phases need to be introduced to describe the interface interaction.

When the repelled liquid is completely filled in the rough solid surface structure, the surface energy EA equals Rγsa + γa, when the lubricating liquid is completely filled in the rough solid structure, the surface energy E2 equals Rγsb + γb, and when the lubricating liquid is filled in the rough structure and the repelled liquid spreads on the lubricating layer, the surface energy of the entire system E1 equals Rγsb + γab + γa; in this system, if the solid is preferentially wetted by the lubricating fluid, the repelling fluid is present on the lubricating layer, and the following two energy conditions (eqn (5) and (6)) must be met:33

 
ΔE1 = EA − E1 = R(γbcosθb − γacosθa) − γab > 0(5)
 
ΔE2 = EA − E2 = R(γbcosθb − γacosθa) + γa − γb > 0(6)
Among them, γab, γa and γb represents the interface energy of the repelling liquid and the lubricating liquid, the repelling liquid and the air interface, and the lubricating liquid and the air interface, respectively; θa and θb represent the contact angle of the repelling liquid and lubricating liquid with the solid surface when the system is in equilibrium. At this time, the model of the entire system is shown in Fig. 10a.


Fig. 10 (a) SLIPS system. The failure model of SLIPS predicted from the surface energy and extended parameters: (b) (Sba < 0) the droplet is covered with lubricant, and the lubricant is taken away when the droplet leaves the SLIPS; (c) (Sab < 0) the droplet spreads over the lubricating layer; (d) (Sbs > −γbR) the lubricant cannot enter the solid surface where air exists; (e) (Sbs(a) > −γabR) the lubricant cannot enter the solid surface where impinging droplets exist; and (f) (γab > 0) the impinging fluid is miscible with the lubricant, forming an infinite interface.123 (Copyright: 2018, Wiley-Blackwell.) The extended parameter S between the four phases can also be used as a criterion to determine whether the SLIPS is effective and meets the functional criteria. Preston et al.125,126 proposed five basic conditions to be met by SLIPS systems and failure models if the conditions are not met according to the surface energy and its extended parameters.

 
(I) Sba = γa − (γab + γb) < 0(7)
If condition (I) is not met, it means that the diffusion parameter of the lubricant on the droplet is a positive number. The lubricant spontaneously diffuses on the droplet, covering the droplet (Fig. 10b). When the droplet leaves the surface, it will take away part of the lubricant, and the lubricant will gradually disappear over time. Not only that, it will also contaminate the repelled droplet.
 
(II) Sab = γb − (γab + γa) < 0(8)
If condition (II) is not met, the impinging droplet will endlessly diffuse onto the lubricant to form a liquid film (Fig. 10c), making the SLIPS ineffective.
 
(III) Sbs = γs − (γbs + γb) > −γbR(9)
where S represents a rough solid, γs represents the surface energy of the solid in the surroundings, and γbs represents the interface energy between the lubricant and the solid. If condition (III) is not met, the lubricant will not be injected into the solid structure in the presence of ambient air (Fig. 10d).
 
(IV) Sbs(a) = γs − (γbs + γb) > −γabR(10)
Subscript (a) means the presence of the impinging droplet. If condition (IV) is not met, the lubricant will not be injected into the solid structure in the presence of impinging droplets (Fig. 10e).
 
(V) γab > 0(11)
If condition (V) is not met, the interface surface area between the lubricant and the impinging fluid will increase indefinitely to minimize the energy, which ultimately leads to miscibility of the two fluids (Fig. 10f).

These five conditions have guiding significance when constructing SLIPS. Similarly, Smith and his colleagues list 12 possible combinations between the four phases in a SLIPS system,127 according to the above condition (I), S < 0 (the droplet will not be covered by lubricating oil), and when condition (I) is not met, S > 0 (the lubricating oil diffuses onto the droplet and blocks the droplet33,125), each divided into six combinations: A3–W1, A3–W2, A3–W3, A2–W1, A2–W2, and A2–W3 (as shown in Fig. 10h and i, A1: not impregnated in the oil–solid–air state, A2: impregnated with emergent features, A3: impregnated with submerged features, W1: impaled in the oil–solid–water state, W2: impregnated with emergent features, W3: impregnated with submerged features). Fig. 10j shows the six SLIPS design constraints expressed in terms of the intrinsic contact angle, apparent contact angle, or spreading coefficient when the droplets are not covered by a lubricant, i.e. six ways for lubricants to wet the texture in the presence of air (vertical axis) and water (horizontal axis). (A3–W3) of Fig. 10j are the phase relationship of the qualified SLIPS, i.e. the stable configuration is the one with the lowest total interface energy, from which we can know that only when the lubricating film completely wets the texture is the lubricating film encapsulating the texture stable. The study of the thermodynamic model provides a theoretical basis for the design and preparation of a stable liquid-repellent SLIPS.

4.3. Fabrication of SLIPS

4.3.1. Lubricant selection. A SLIPS is composed of a rough micro–nano structure, base layer modification and an injected lubricating fluid. The construction of SLIPS needs to follow some principles: the lubricating liquid and the repelling liquid are incompatible with each other; the chemical affinity of the lubricating liquid and the solid substrate is higher than that of the repulsive liquid and the solid substrate; and the micro–nano structure on the solid surface should provide enough surface to adsorb and store the lubricant. These three principles are the guarantee to obtain stable SLIPS.33 The preparation of SLIPS does not simply involve injecting lubricating fluid into the rough solid structure, but involves four key steps: the selection of the lubricant, the preparation of the base rough structure, the modification of the substrate, and the lubricant injection process.

The lubricant is the phase of SLIPS that most directly contacts the outside, so the physical and chemical properties of the lubricant are the key to determining the characteristics of SLIPS. Mineral oil,128 perfluoropolyether (krytox),33 ionic liquid,129 silicone oil,130etc. have all been used as lubricants. Lubricants applied to SLIPS need to have low surface energy to enhance the anti-adhesion ability, and low viscosity to reduce sliding resistance and repair damaged parts, but also need to have low volatility to strengthen the coating durability. A lubricant used in the optical field must have transparency in order not to affect the optical properties of the material. Low surface energy of the lubricant is a necessary condition to impart SLIPS with liquid repellency. Sett and his colleagues reviewed the surface energy of common lubricants and low-surface-tension fluids at 20 °C.131 The surface energy of perfluoropolyether krytox is 17–19 N m−1. Due to the lower surface energy, perfluoropolyether is often used as the first choice for the preparation of lyophobic surfaces. However, low surface energy cannot be pursued blindly for liquid repellency. A too low surface energy will cause condition (I) in the above to be not met, resulting in the oil film blocking liquid droplets and the droplet movement being hindered, causing surface function failure or substrate erosion. When such a SLIPS is used as a condenser surface, tiny droplets are wrapped by an oil film, and then developed into a water film covering the surface of the condenser, which affects the heat transfer effect of the condenser.132 When selecting a lubricant, besides considering the surface energy factor, it is also necessary to consider the relationship with the impinging droplet. For example, isopropyl alcohol (IPA), pentane and hexane cannot condense into droplets on a surface wetted by perfluoropolyether, but form a liquid film, because they have large air–liquid surface tension, which makes them penetrate the lubricant and enter the solid rough structure.133 In addition, the dosage of the lubricant and the size of the droplets are also factors that affect whether the lubricant produces a cloaking behavior.134Fig. 11a–c show that, as the thickness of the lubricating layer gradually decreases, the state of the droplets in the lubricant is flooded, coated, and exposed, respectively. As can be seen from Fig. 11d and e, with the same lubricating layer thickness, as the droplets evaporate, the size decreases, and the possibility of the lubricant cloaking the droplets becomes greater. Fig. 11f is the curve of the dimensionless ratio of the ridge height (z) and drop height (h) of the lubricant in the three states of flooded, coated, and exposed with the time increasing. The states recorded at 38 s are shown in Fig. 11g and i, indicating that the lubricant takes the shortest time to complete the cloaking behavior in the flooded state. Therefore, in order to improve the stability of SLIPS and reduce the loss of lubricant, excessive lubricant should be removed to enhance the interaction between the lubricant and the substrate, and the volume of impinging droplets should be increased to slow down the production of lubricant cloaking behavior in consideration of the surface energy.

4.3.2. Preparation of the substrate. Rough micro–nano structures with different shapes on the substrate increase the contact area with the lubricant and provide capillary force for locking the lubricant.34 There are many methods for preparing rough micro–nano structures, such as the hydrothermal method (Fig. 12a),135 polymer crystallization method (Fig. 12b),136,137 anodizing method (Fig. 12c)138 and electrochemical deposition method (Fig. 12d).139 The preparation of rough substrates in SLIPS has many similarities with that of superhydrophobic surfaces. For example, etching methods, chemical deposition, sol–gel methods, and layer-by-layer self-assembly methods (LbL) can be used to prepare rough surfaces to construct SLIPS. Rykaczewski et al.133 used photolithography and deep reactive ion etching to form a patterned surface with a width of 10 μm, a depth of 10 μm, and a spacing of 5 μm on the surface of a silicon wafer, and then subjected it to hydrophobic treatment with octadecyltrichlorosilane, injecting perfluoro oil to obtain a SLIPS that can effectively promote the droplet condensation of a low surface tension liquid (Fig. 12e). However, it is not suitable for large-scale preparation due to its complex preparation process and strict requirements on surface materials and shapes. Hozumi et al.140 deposited 1,3,5,7-tetramethylcyclotetrasiloxane on a silicon wafer by chemical vapor deposition to obtain a rough structure, and then used triethoxysilane to increase the polarity of the surface, and injected ionic liquid 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazole bis-trifluoromethanesulfonimide salt ([EMI][TFSI]) to obtain a SLIPS (Fig. 12f). [EMI][TFSI] has a lower vapor pressure and higher thermodynamic stability and is less volatile than perfluoropolyether. Kim et al.141 used the sol–gel method to convert an aluminum sol into nano-scale boehmite (AlO(OH)), spin-coated it onto silicon wafers, and obtained a gel film after heat treatment at 400 °C. A stable SLIPS can be obtained after the membrane is fluorinated and lubricant is poured (Fig. 12g). The sol–gel method is characterized by economical efficiency and simple operation, and can prepare SLIPS on different substrates, but requires heat treatment and fluorination treatment. Vogel et al.142 used layer-by-layer self-assembly (LbL) to perform plasma treatment on the surface of a silicon wafer to make it negatively charged, then deposited polydiallyldimethylammonium chloride (PDADMAC) cations and negatively charged silicon nanoparticles to the desired number of layers, and then calcined to form a porous silicon layer. After fluorination to reduce the surface energy, Krytox100 is poured to obtain a thermodynamically stable SLIPS (Fig. 12h). Although LbL can build SLIPS on different substrates, it involves complex technologies such as plasma treatment and chemical vapor deposition, and is not suitable for large-scale production.

The above method for preparing superhydrophobic surfaces can also be used to prepare SLIPS, but the characteristics and roughness range of SLIPS substrates are still worth exploring. Philseok et al.143 came to the following conclusions by studying the difference between the lubricant loss, contact angle and sliding angle on smooth surfaces, micro-scale surfaces, nano-scale surfaces and micro–nano hierarchical surfaces (shown in Fig. 12i–l): for the superhydrophobic surface of bionic lotus leaf, the roughness of the hierarchical structure can provide the largest contact angle, which is conducive to improving the hydrophobicity. The nano-scale rough surface of the SLIPS can resist the shear force of the fluid to the greatest extent, which is conducive to the construction of a stable oil film. The nano-scale voids provide denser bump contacts, which can bear the impact of external forces and increase the stability. The capillary force generated can also better lock the lubricant without affecting the movement of droplets on the surface due to excessive roughness.

In addition to the effect of roughness on the stability of SLIPS, the open and closed state of the pores storing lubricant is also an important factor affecting the stability of SLIPS. A porous structure with interconnected pores will lose lubricant under the action of shear, while a closed pore structure (nanopores are separated from the adjacent nanopores by a membrane-like surface) can effectively maintain the lubricant and improve the mechanical stability. A closed-cell structure can be prepared by a template method using colloidal spheres as a template,144 hollow polymer sphere self-assembly methods,145 soft-plate printing,146etc. A closed-cell structure applied to a superhydrophobic surface makes the air trapped in the pores difficult to discharge, reducing the possibility of a transition from the Cassie model to the Wenzel model. For SLIPS, a closed-cell structure can not only effectively prevent the loss of lubricant and improve the mechanical damage resistance, but also provide adjustable transparency.17

4.3.3. Low surface pretreatment (substrate modification). For SLIPS, the micro–nano rough structure used to store the lubricant on the solid substrate after treatment with low surface energy groups will not only help the spreading and storage of the lubricant, but also enhance the hydrophobic property of the surface.35 On the contrary, if the substrate is not modified, the air trapped in the rough structure will bring resistance to complete filling.147 Common modifiers that can reduce the surface energy are fluorides such as fluorosilanes and fluorocarbon materials, because fluorinated functional groups have good compatibility with fluorinated lubricants, and can react to form precursors or miscible molecules.148 Caitlin et al.149 prepared a rough surface based on polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) by a sol–gel method. After fluorination, the lubricant was poured and the excess was discharged, and then (0.05 ± 0.01) g residual lubricant on the rough structure was detected, while only 0.003 g of lubricant remained on the surface without fluoride treatment. The results show that the surface modified by fluoride increases the adsorption of lubricants. Levkin et al.150 controlled the size of the micro–nano structure and the lubricity of the surface by controlling the composition of the polymer solution. They used UV-induced free radical polymerization to prepare a polybutyl methacrylate-ethylene glycol dimethacrylate P(BMA–EDMA) porous membrane, which was treated with methanol and dried, and then immersed in fluorinated lubricant (Krytox 100 and 103). An antibacterial SLIPS was prepared without fluoride treatment. Hashaikeh et al.151 took advantage of the fact that fluorine is present in polyvinylidene fluoride–hexafluoropropylene (PVDF–HFP), so no further fluorination treatment is required to reduce the surface energy, and sprayed PVDF–HFP acetamide solution on an aluminum surface by electrospinning to form a porous structure composed of fibers with a diameter of 100–500 nm, and then infiltrated Krytox-1506 to form SLIPS that can promote the condensation of droplets.

In addition to introducing low surface energy modifiers to enhance the adhesion of lubricants, the use of π-electronic interactions can also increase the stability of lubricant injection into the surface. The π-electronic interaction refers to the typical non-polar interaction between electron-deficient groups and electron-rich groups. For example, an oily liquid and –OH, –COOH, –CHO, and –CH groups are adsorbed onto a silanized smooth surface through the interaction of OH–π, COOH–π, CHO–π and CH–π, so as to achieve the purpose of forming a liquid layer even on a smooth surface without micro–nano structure. Togasawa et al.152,153 used the –COOH group in oleic acid to form OH–π interactions with the π-electrons in the base layer, so that oleic acid with a surface tension of up to 32.5–33.6 mN m−1 could also be fixed on a smooth surface to obtain a fluorine-free lubricating coating. What's more, by increasing the density of π-electrons in the base layer, the OH–π interaction between the lubricant and the base layer is enhanced, thereby improving the stability of the surface. Covalent immobilization of the lubricant can produce an effect similar to the interaction of π-electrons. Krumpfer et al.131 dip-coated silicone oil on the surface of aluminum oxide at 100 °C. After 24 hours, a lubricant gel surface with a low hysteresis angle was formed. The mechanism of action is that the silicone oil is attached to the surface through the Si–O–Al covalent bond, and the polycondensation reaction between the lubricant and Si–OH produces a low hysteresis phenomenon. There are many other elements whose inorganic oxides can produce covalent fixation, such as silicon, titanium and nickel.154,155

Delaying the loss of the lubricant inside the substrate can also be achieved by controlling the elastic contraction of the substrate. Yao et al.156 crosslinked perfluorinated acrylates and perfluoropolyethers and then cured them to obtain fluorine gel elastomers. The cross-linked network structure of the fluorine gel (as shown in Fig. 13a) can seal the lubricant into the substrate to make the surface have super slippery properties. It has a strong ability to repel proteins and blood cells, and has significant applications in medical fields such as dialysis and blood analysis. In the same way, Hozumi et al.157 used organic liquids with changeable composition and polysiloxane (PDMS) to cross-link to prepare organic gels with self-lubricating properties. By changing the composition of the organic liquid to control its compatibility with the PDMS network and the shrinkage rate of the gel substrate, they achieved the purpose of controlling the secretion of lubricant and slowing down the process of the SLIPS being transformed into a superhydrophobic surface due to the loss of lubricant.

4.3.4. Lubricant injection process. The modification of a substrate with low surface energy groups, physical or covalent interactions and altering the elastic contraction of the substrate mentioned above can enhance the adhesion between the lubricant and the substrate. The next step is to inject the lubricant into the substrate with a micro–nano structure, which is also the key to determining whether the lubricant can better bond with the substrate. Since the complete filling of the substrate cannot be guaranteed, simply immersing the substrate in the lubricant through the capillary force of the micro–nano structure and the surface tension of the lubricant cannot meet the requirements of preparing SLIPS.36 The injection of the lubricant has to solve two key problems. One is how to remove the air in substrates with different shapes so that the lubricant can completely infiltrate the structure. The available methods include vacuum infusion, which involves extracting the air from the hole and refilling the lubricant,163 and the pressure injection method, which creates a pressure difference between the inside and outside of the hole to drive the lubricant into the substrate.164 There is also a heating method, which involves heating the air in the hole to make it expand and escape. What's more, high temperature can reduce the viscosity of the filled liquid, which is suitable for high-viscosity lubricants.165 In addition, a solution exchange method with fast filling speed and complete filling can also be used. The micro–nano structure is filled with a small molecule solution first (e.g. ethanol solution), and then an intermediate (lubricant solvent) is used to replace the small molecule solution, finally replacing the intermediate with the specified lubricant.36

The second key issue is how to fix the lubricant. Besides covalent interactions (π-electronic interactions), modification with a low surface energy modifier35 and the control of the elastic contraction of the substrate156 mentioned above, a polymer can also be injected into the lubricant so that the lubricant is locked in the cross-linked network, and a continuous self-regulating secretion system of a supramolecular polymer–gel matrix (Fig. 13c and d) is introduced through the diffusion of the lubricant.159–161 Zhang et al.158 obtained a surface of a PDMS-infused organogel through heat treatment. Thermally responsive organogels are potential anti-icing materials that exhibit good long-term synergistic behavior when silicone oil is injected. Due to the compatibility with PDMS, silicone oil can be injected at the molecular level and locked in the PDMS (Fig. 13b). In the same way, it is possible to control the synergistic behavior of organogels by adjusting the compatibility of the PDMS network with different immersion organic liquids – by continuously and slowly releasing the liquid layer, the long-term performance of the liquid layer can be extended.166 Damle et al.162 embedded a network of periodically spaced channels (Fig. 13e) in the lubricant to connect the microchannels to the polymer surface to achieve rapid replenishment of the lubricant, and solve the problem of slow diffusion of silicone oil to the lubricating layer in PDMS.

4.4. Application of SLIPS.

By infusing a low surface energy liquid into the porous rough structure, a continuous, stable and homogeneous solid–liquid film layer can be formed on SLIPS. For SLIPS, it is the injected lubricant rather than the substrate that contacts with the droplets. Therefore, the characteristics of SLIPS mostly depend on the physical and chemical properties of the lubricant. The main properties of SLIPS are: Low sliding resistance. The low viscosity and surface energy of the lubricant make the object subject to less frictional resistance when moving on a near-molecularly smooth surface. The critical size of the droplet sliding on its surface is 20 μm, and the dynamic contact angle hysteresis is as low as 2.5°.167–169 Difficult for contaminants to attach. It is difficult for the lubricant film to provide nucleation and adsorption points, effectively reducing the condensation of droplets,147 ice crystal nucleation170 and scaling with deposition.171 Even if contaminants attach to the surface and nucleate, due to the fluidity of the lubricant, the attached droplets, ice crystals, fouling and microorganisms can be easily removed under the action of a small shear force.172 Fast self-healing. When the SLIPS is worn or impacted, the lubricant can quickly release and fill the “wound” within 150 ms, and a “wound” that has experienced multiple injuries can still heal automatically.173 In addition, SLIPS also have pressure resistance,174 anti-friction properties and adjustable light transmittance. Due to a series of excellent properties, SLIPS have important applications and influences in many fields.
4.4.1. Antifouling. Reduce the adhesion of droplets. The lubricant (such as silicone oil, olive oil, and ionic liquids) perfused on SLIPS has good repellency to water droplets and the surface has good sliding properties. The droplets on it can slide off at a small angle.175 Therefore, SLIPS can effectively reduce the adhesion of droplets.52 What's more, SLIPS can also resist biological adhesion and inhibit the formation of biofilms. They have great application prospects in the fields of medicine176 and marine antifouling.177,178
Anti-bioadhesion. Due to the low surface energy and fluidity of SLIPS, bacteria can be removed with a small shear force, which effectively inhibits the formation of biofilms. Alexander et al.179 prepared SLIPS with fluoroalkylsilane functionalized silicon as a substrate and a perfluorinated liquid as a lubricant, and then confirmed that the surface can prevent 99.6% of P. aeruginosa and 97.2% of S. aureus, and 96% of E. coli, showing superior anti-bacterial performance to the PEGylated substrate. Levkin et al.150 conducted UV-initiated free radical copolymerization on a glass substrate to prepare SLIPS, and then used bacteria such as P. aeruginosa, algae spores and larvae to conduct anti-fouling tests to simulate biological pollution in the real marine environment. The results showed that, when the culture time was less than seven days, the SLIPS showed resistance to algae spore settlement. A SLIPS prepared on a polymer or glass substrate is not suitable for anti-fouling of ships. To solve this problem, Wang et al.180 constructed an anode Al2O3 nanowire-like layer on an aluminum surface and poured a fluorinated lubricant. The SLIPS prepared can effectively inhibit the adhesion and growth of sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB) on metal surfaces. Similarly, Tesler et al.181 electrodeposited a nanoporous tungsten film on a steel substrate, and injected Krytox GPL-K103 after hydrophobic treatment to obtain a tungsten oxide SLIPS (TO-SLIPS). In the anti-pollution test, TO-SLIPS, a tungsten oxide superhydrophobic surface (TO-SHS) and an untreated steel surface were exposed to C. reinhardtii culture for eight days to grow a biofilm. The algae attached to the three surfaces are shown in Fig. 14A. The residual algae on TO-SHS and untreated steel are as high as 88 ± 4% and 99 ± 1%. Most of the algae on TO-SLIPS fall off easily, leaving only 7 ± 6% on the edge of the untreated surface (Fig. 14B), showing the superior performance of TO-SLIPS in marine antifouling.

Corrosion prevention. There are two main mechanisms for SLIPS as a protective layer to protect metal materials from corrosion. One is to repel corrosive microorganisms such as SRB through fluidity and low adhesion mentioned above; the other is to prevent the penetration of corrosive media such as water, ions and dissolved oxygen.182 For resistance to environmental corrosion, it is mainly attempted to construct a superhydrophobic rough structure on a metal surface and inject lubricant. Tuo et al.183 constructed layered double hydroxides (LDHs) on an aluminum alloy substrate by the hydrothermal method. After being hydrophobically modified with fluoroalkyl silane, perfluoropolyether oil was injected to obtain an anti-corrosion barrier. The key problem to be solved is that the loss of lubricant will cause the failure of the anti-corrosion coating. Tenjimbayashi et al.184 used phase separation technology to insert perfluoropolyether (PFPE) oil into two self-standing porous surfaces (SPS) made of poly(vinylidene fluoride-co-hexafluoropropylene) PVDF-HFP with an Fe plate (Fig. 15A)185 to obtain a lubricant-inserted microporous triple-layered surface (LIMITS) (Fig. 15C). PFPE wets the SPS to form a lubricating layer, and the lubricant sandwiched in the middle gradually secretes itself to the surface. The design of the LIMITS reduces the loss of lubricant due to contact with other liquids or evaporation. In the anti-corrosion test, the LIMITS shows excellent corrosion resistance (>45 days) compared with general SLIPS (Fig. 15B).

4.4.2. Anti-frosting/icing. The chemical homogeneity of SLIPS can reduce the nucleation temperature of supercooled water and reduce the formation of ice nuclei, thereby prolonging the freezing time, reducing the rate of ice formation in high humidity environments, and reducing the adhesion of ice. They are an excellent anti-icing material.33,53 Zhou et al.186 studied frosting on a pure and smooth PDMS film without pores, fluorination and lubricant (SF), a fluorinated porous film (FF) and a fluorinated porous film infiltrated with perfluoro polyether (Krytox from Dupont) lubricant (LF). As shown in Fig. 16, the ice layer on SF is thick and compact, and on FF is thick and granular, while the ice layer on LF is rough and sparse. Due to the various condensation states of water on the three films, their frosting time and frost layer morphology are different. It can be seen that the structure of the substrate and the characteristics of the lubricant are significant factors to be considered when preparing anti-icing SLIPS. SLIPS with micro–nano texture surface have plenty of space for absorbing and storing lubricant. This texture structure can be divided into ordered structure and disordered structure.187,188 Photolithography is usually used to prepare ordered structures with specific shapes.189 When preparing a random structure surface with a porous pattern, methods such as etching, plasma and spin coating are usually used.190,191 An orderly structure is more conducive to achieving anti-icing performance, but creating a disordered structure is more convenient and practical. However, a textured porous substrate will lose the lubricant and lose its anti-icing ability over time. Aizenberg et al.144 developed a closed-cell system using a composite material with a single layer of reverse colloid and a transparent nanostructure, which can trap lubricant in the texture structure. Similarly, Liu et al.192 used electrochemical etching and hydrothermal methods to prepare a slippery liquid-infused porous network surface (SLIPNS) with interconnected microchannels and cross-linked nanosheet structures (Fig. 17A), and compared its anti-icing performance with ordinary SLIPS by measuring the icing delay time of static droplets. The results show that when the water droplets slide 100 times at −10 °C, the water droplets on the SLIPS freeze at 547 s and remain on the SLIPS after the ice melts, while the water droplets on the SLIPNS freeze at 896 s and the melted droplets can slide off the SLIPNS spontaneously (Fig. 17D), which is due to the stronger oil storage and retention function of the SLIPNS (Fig. 17B and C).

Most lubricants are organic fluids with low surface energy and low fluidity. They can reduce the intermolecular interactions between ice and the solid surface, such as hydrogen bonds, thereby reducing the adhesion of the ice.193,194 Different from traditional SLIPS, researchers covered the surface with a hygroscopic polymer to prepare a self-lubricating liquid layer inspired by the sport of skating. The hygroscopic polymer will ionize in water, and the activity, melting point and freezing point of water will all decrease. When the hydrophilic side groups come into contact with ice and snow, they can be melted. Then the hygroscopic polymer will expand after absorbing water to form a water-based lubricating layer on the surface to replace oil-based lubricants.195–197

In order to improve the durability of icephobic materials, researchers have developed a new anti-icing surface using ferrofluid as a lubricant. The surface is exposed to a magnetic field, which can lock the liquid in the structure without micro–nano textures. This locking effect can reduce the adhesion strength between the surface and the ice, and its ice nucleation delay time is three orders of magnitude longer than other icephobic surfaces.198 However, this surface has the problem that the magnetic field cannot be maintained for a long time. There are other practical icephobic surfaces that have been developed. Rykaczewski et al.199 imitated the skin structure of poison frogs that can secrete venom and designed a SLIPS consisting of a porous superhydrophobic skin and a lower layer of nylon filled with wick-like lubricant propylene glycol. When high-speed impact droplets and condensed frost penetrate into the pores, the structure of the coating releases the stored antifreeze, which melts the ice and removes it.

4.4.3. Oil–water separation. Recently, SLIPS have provided new ideas for efficient oil–water separation.54,55 Aizenberg et al.200 used a capillary liquid as a reversible and reconfigurable gate. The liquid will seal the membrane pores in the closed state, and open to form an anti-fouling channel under pressure (Fig. 18A). It is introduced into the separation of complex multiphase liquids to achieve controllable separation. In addition, the multiphase fluid in the microfluidic and macrofluidic system can be quickly and reproducibly controlled by simply adjusting the system pressure (Fig. 18B and C), adding a hydraulically controlled porous membrane to the port of the microfluidic channel, and then setting the critical pressures of air and water to 2.3 kPa and 9.8 kPa, respectively. By setting different thresholds for each component of the three-phase air–water–oil mixture, pressure can be used to actively adjust and control the fluid passing through the hydraulic control membrane, and to prevent any component from escaping while achieving selective collection of different phases.

Enhancing condensation.56 Since condensed droplets have good mobility on the lubricant layer and easily gather and form nucleation points, a SLIPS can promote drop-like condensation. Dynamically regulating the fluid-infused surface57. The size of the micro–nano structure in the substrate of SLIPS is below the diffraction limit. Choosing lubricants with different refractive indexes and light transmittances can adjust the degree of light penetration or reflection, thereby adjusting the light transmission performance. Based on this, a gradual tensile force can be applied to the substrate. When the film is deformed, the pressure inside the pores drops, and the lubricant flows into the pores, which deteriorates the optical transparency of the film. When the force is removed, the lubricant overflows outwards and becomes a dynamic fluid-infused surface, which can be tunable between transparent and opaque by stimulating the substrate.

5. Hydrophilic/superhydrophilic surfaces

5.1. Sources of structure in nature

Several surfaces that distinguish wettability and adhesion according to the range of CA and SA have been described, and the surface to be introduced next is another extremely wettable surface in addition to a superhydrophobic surface – a superhydrophilic surface. In academia, there are two definitions of superhydrophilic surfaces. One is defined by the contact angle, i.e. a superhydrophilic surface means that the water droplets can be completely spread on the surface, and the CA is equal to or close to 0° (Fig. 20b).201,202 Some researchers have set requirements for the spreading time of droplets on superhydrophilic surfaces. Huang et al.203 pointed out that when superhydrophilic surfaces have a contact angle of less than 5°, the spreading time of the droplets must be less than 0.5 s. Another is defined by the mechanism. There are few smooth superhydrophilic surfaces in nature, and most of the complete wetting phenomena occur on rough porous surfaces. Therefore, a superhydrophilic surface is also defined as a surface with a roughness factor greater than 1 (r > 1) and droplets can be completely spread.204

Superhydrophilic plants in nature can be divided into two categories. One is permanently wet aquatic plants. Fig. 20c is the SEM image of the submerged aquatic plant Anubias barteri. It can be seen that the leaf has a relatively smooth plate-like or slightly convex cell surface.13 This surface structure reduces the gas exchange on the plant surface, which reduces the photosynthetic activity of plants. Another type of superhydrophilic plants absorb water over their surfaces and let water spread over the surface. The superhydrophilicity of plants is caused by various microscopic nanostructures. As shown in Fig. 20d, the spongy epidermal structure of sphagnum squarrosum consists of pores with an inner diameter of 10–20 mm. These pores formed by dead and collapsed cells can absorb up to 20 times their own dry weight. Fig. 20e shows the porous surface structure of Rhacocarpus purpurescens; the diameter of the pore for quickly absorbing fog, dew or rain is between 0.2 and 1 mm. Apart from pores and porous surfaces, the water-absorbing structure of the plant surface can also be absorbent hair, for example, the surface composed of multicellular water-absorbing trichomes of Tillandsia usneoides is shown in Fig. 20f.14 What's more, the surface of Calathea zebrina composed of uniform conical cells (Fig. 20g) enables the water droplets to spread to a contact angle of 0° within a few seconds. Fig. 20h shows the Ruellia devosiana surface of a composite structure with hair papilla, papilla cells, glands and some extended channel-like structures, whose superhydrophilicity is determined by the surface roughness and the hydrophilic substances secreted by the glands. Studies have found that a hydrophilic substance that can reduce the surface tension of water, promote the effective diffusion of water, and prevent the formation of biofilms is saponin.205

Since there are no roots that absorb water or a vascular system for water delivery, super-hydrophilicity is the basis for many lower plants to absorb water and nutrients. In addition, the superhydrophilic leaves of some higher plants such as Ruellia devosiana can accelerate the acquisition and evaporation of water by increasing the water–air interface, which is a good way to reduce the growth of microorganisms and increase the gas exchange with the environment.14

5.3. Fabrication of superhydrophilic surfaces

According to the mechanism and model, the key to preparing a superhydrophilic surface is roughness and porosity. There are two main ways to prepare it. One is photo-induced superhydrophilicity (PIH), which means that some metal oxides can exhibit superhydrophilicity under ultraviolet light.207 The mechanism is that, under irradiation with ultraviolet light, the hydrophobic organics on the surface undergo a photocatalytic degradation or a oxidation–reduction reaction.208 Taking TiO2 as an example, due to irradiation with ultraviolet light, the valence band electrons of TiO2 are excited to form electron–hole pairs, and then the oxidation–reduction reaction between holes and surface bridge oxygen ions forms oxygen vacancies (Fig. 22A-a), thereby adsorbing water in the air (Fig. 22A-b) and forming a hydroxyl adsorption surface (Fig. 22A-c) to further adsorb moisture in the air to obtain a physical adsorption layer. So it exhibits superhydrophilicity in a macroscopic view.

Another way to prepare superhydrophilic surfaces is to build micro–nano rough or porous structures on the surface of hydrophilic materials. The method used is roughly similar to the method to prepare superhydrophobic surfaces.

The sol–gel method37 involves changing the precursors of hydrolysis and polycondensation to obtain films with different morphologies and chemical compositions. The method requires low reaction temperature, has advantages of easy control of the process, a simple process, and convenient operation, and is one of the most common methods for preparing inorganic coatings. However, this method requires ultraviolet light generally, and is not applicable for the preparation of polymer materials.

In the phase separation method,38 phase separation of polymer solutions or polymer blends by changing factors such as temperature, humidity, solubility or pressure can form a rough structure, which can be used to prepare special wettable surfaces. The method is simple and easy to implement, and low in cost, and can be used to prepare large area superhydrophilic surfaces, but due to its poor adhesion to the substrate, the durability will be affected.

The vapor deposition method,39 which is a common method for constructing films, is divided into chemical vapor deposition (CVD) and physical vapor deposition (PVD). CVD has high production efficiency and is suitable for hard-to-evaporate materials, but requires high-temperature operation and will generate toxic or corrosive gases. The PVD preparation process is environmentally friendly and the prepared coating has better durability, but its equipment is expensive and the preparation efficiency is low.

The layer-by-layer self-assembly method (LbL)40 refers to the process of spontaneous formation of molecular structures with specific functions between layers by means of alternating the deposition layer by layer, with the help of the weak interaction between molecules of each layer. The method has no limitation on the film substrate, and a wide selection range of driving force, and is suitable for preparing transparent surfaces with special wettability. However, its efficiency is low, the process is complicated and it is difficult to use in industrial production.

The template-based method,41 which includes the natural template method and artificial template method, involves using an extrusion or pouring method to cover a mold (high molecular weight polymer) on the surface of the template and realize the replication of the template structure. The method has simple operation steps, high efficiency and diversified templates, but it is limited by the templates when preparing a large surface area.

The electrochemical method42 refers to using electrochemical methods (including electrochemical deposition, anodic oxidation, galvanic reaction, electrochemical polymerization, etc.) to treat the surface with a rough structure. The method does not require light, but requires special equipment. It is difficult to achieve large-scale preparation, and the superhydrophilicity can only be maintained for one to two days.

Electrospinning43 involves obtaining a fiber surface with nano-channels through the phase separation of the solution during the spinning process. The fiber has a multi-scale three-dimensional capillary effect, which makes the surface superhydrophilic. The method can continuously prepare polymer nanofibers with higher specific surface area, but the strength of the nanofibers is insufficient.

Combining two special wettable surfaces can prepare more functionalized surfaces, such as superhydrophilic/superhydrophobic reversible transition surfaces, superhydrophilic/superhydrophobic patterned surfaces, and superhydrophilic/superhydrophobic gradients surfaces. Guo et al.209 prepared a synergistic dual-responsive poly-L-lysine (PLL) surface by changing the conformation of the polymer chain segment (Fig. 22B). In this model, PLL mainly adopts a random coil conformation (state I) at low pH. Raising the pH allows the dominant existence of an α-helix conformation (state II). Heating up the surface destabilizes the α-helix conformation to produce aggregated β-sheet structures (state III). Lowering the pH and temperature induces a reversible conversion process from state III to state I. When the pH is less than the acidolysis constant of PLL, the polypeptide surface is superhydrophilic. When the temperature and pH are increased, the surface becomes superhydrophobic, realizing a change in wettability (Fig. 22C). Lin et al.210 used an inkjet printer to print patterns on a superhydrophobic coating based on a titanium-based oxide layer, and degraded some of the superhydrophobic molecules by ultraviolet radiation to obtain superhydrophobic/superhydrophilic micro-patterns. Then electrochemical methods are used to deposit calcium phosphate films (CaP) of different types on the superhydrophobic and superhydrophilic regions, and in vitro cell culture tests indicate a difference in adhesion between the superhydrophobic and superhydrophilic regions of MG63 cells (Fig. 22D and E). A gradient surface can also be obtained by selectively removing the hydrophobic substances on the superhydrophobic surface. Han et al.211 decomposed chlorodimethyloctylsilane molecules on a SiO2 surface into carboxylate, ether and carbonyl groups by ultraviolet light irradiation, and controlled the irradiation time to obtain a gradient wettable surface (the CA varies between 165° and 10°). Surfaces with different wettability have many potential applications in fluid control, immobilization of biomolecules, controlled growth of cells, and microreactors.212,213

5.4. Applications of superhydrophilic surfaces

5.4.1. Self-cleaning. With the development of preparation technology of superhydrophilic surfaces, they have more extensive applications in self-cleaning, biomedical fields, oil-water separation and other aspects. Water droplets can spread instantly on them, which is beneficial to take away the dirt on the surface, thus achieving a self-cleaning effect. Yang et al.58 used a microemulsion polymerization method and blended with modified silica gel to prepare a TiO2/PMMA (polymethylmethacrylate) composite surface, which exhibited superhydrophilicity under ultraviolet light irradiation. The gradual degradation test of methyl blue on the surface shows that it has self-cleaning properties. Since water droplets can completely wet the superhydrophilic surface, it effectively prevents water vapor from condensing into water droplets on the surface, thus having an excellent anti-fog effect. Based on this, superhydrophilic surfaces have important applications in life and industrial fields such as car window mirrors, solar cells, spacecraft observation windows and optical antireflection coatings for lasers.214,215 Guo et al.210 used a one-step self-masking reactive ion etching method to prepare a superhydrophilic quartz broadband antireflection film with good anti-fogging ability. The quartz glass covered by the film was placed on top of boiling water for an anti-fogging test, and the results showed that the water droplets spread quickly on the hydrophilic film and had no effect on the transparency of the quartz glass.
5.4.2. Biomedical applications. Studies have shown that superhydrophilic materials have good biocompatibility and bioactivity, so they have great application value in the field of biomedicine. For example, their good wettability is conducive to the movement of a catheter in the patient's body and makes contact lenses more comfortable.59 The superhydrophobic/superhydrophilic patterned surface mentioned in Section 5.3 enables biomolecules and nanoparticles to be immobilized in the superhydrophilic area, which is convenient for targeted drug delivery and monitoring of toxicity.216

However, the biggest contribution of superhydrophilic materials in the medical field is TiO2 biomaterials. A superhydrophilic TiO2 surface irradiated with ultraviolet light has better albumin and fibrin adsorption capacity, osteoblast adhesion and reproduction capacity, and is conducive to the formation of bone apatite layers. Therefore, it can promote the reproduction of bone cells on the implant, strengthen the combination of bone tissue and the implant, and facilitate the long-term use of the implant.217

5.4.3. Oil–water separation. Among many oil–water separation membranes, superhydrophilic and superoleophobic membranes have the advantages of high efficiency, anti-adhesion, and reusability.60 They mainly include two types: superhydrophilic underwater superoleophobic membranes and stimulus-responsive superhydrophilic superoleophobic membranes. The preparation of them can be divided into two types. One is to prepare a polyelectrolyte–fluorosurfactant composite coating solution first. The fluorosurfactant complex is composed of hydrophilic groups and fluoroalkyl chains. When subjected to static electricity, the hydrophilic groups and fluoroalkyl chains will be positioned on the surface of the material.60 Then water molecules can responsively pass through the fluorine-containing layer and enter the hydrophilic surface, while oil molecules will be hindered to achieve the effect of oil–water separation.

The other is to use surface grafting, surface spraying and self-assembly methods to coat a solution on various substrates, and after drying and UV irradiation obtain superhydrophilic and superoleophobic materials. Around these two types, researchers have developed many preparation methods. Kota et al.218 prepared a polymer blend composed of poly(ethylene glycol)diacrylate (x-PEGDA) and fluorodecyl polyhedral oligomeric silsesquioxane (POSS). The mesh coated with fluorodecyl POSS + x-PEGDA exhibits superoleophobicity and superhydrophilicity. Water droplets can penetrate the coating within a few seconds, while the coated mesh or fabric can continuously separate insoluble oil with a separation efficiency of up to 99% only under gravity drive. Yang et al.219 used dimethyl diallyl ammonium chloride as the hydrophilic part and perfluorooctane carboxylate as the oleophobic part to prepare a moisture-responsive superhydrophilic and super oleophobic membrane, which can change from superhydrophobic to superhydrophilic under humidity stimulation (the CA of water decreases from 165 ± 2° to 0°), while maintaining the CA of oil at 155 ± 1°. Li et al.220 used the interface produced by mixing 2-carboxylic acid ethyl acrylate, poly(ethylene glycol)diacrylate and silica nanoparticles as the hydrophilic part, and used 1H,1H-perfluorodecyl modified polyfunctional mercaptan (F-PETMP) as the oleophobic part, and then cured using ultraviolet to form a superhydrophilic and superoleophobic coating. Studies have shown that when the contents of F-PETMP and SiO2 are 30% and 50% respectively, the performance of the coating is optimal (the CA of oil is greater than 150° in air or in water, while the CA of water reached 0° within 10 s).

5.4.4. Other applications. There are many other applications for superhydrophilic surfaces. For example, the visualization under high humidity conditions can be used to prepare micro–nanofluidic devices and micro-reactors. Their rapid response to humidity can be used to make humidity sensors. And superhydrophilic surfaces’ superior absorbent and perspiration functions can be used on fabric to make clothes more comfortable.61 In addition, a superhydrophilic surface can provide the power for liquid replenishment and relieve the vapor film from hindering the process of liquid replenishment to the heating device. Therefore, it can significantly increase the critical heat flux density (CHF), thereby increasing the evaporation and condensation heat transfer coefficient.62

6. Summary and outlook

Based on the adhesion state of liquid droplets on a solid surface, four kinds of surfaces with typical wetting states (low-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces, high-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces, slippery liquid-infused porous surfaces and hydrophilic/superhydrophilic surfaces) are reviewed in this paper. Tracing the whole process – starting from their natural sources, with the development of optical and qualitative instruments, researchers use bionics methods to design wetting models with corresponding structures and chemical components, and then obtain artificial special wettable surfaces with needed properties through various preparation methods and apply them in industries such as life and production. Finally, we point out current problems and new research directions in the development of these four special wettable surfaces.

Low-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces inspired by the lotus leaf have broad application prospects in daily life, biomedicine and industrial development because of their self-cleaning, anti-corrosion and anti-fouling properties. They are mainly obtained by reducing the surface energy of the surface with a micro–nano rough structure and building a micro–nano rough structure on the low surface energy one, so that the water droplets cannot enter the air grooves while forming a spherical shape to obtain a low-adhesion superhydrophobic surface. The method of reducing the surface energy is mainly through modification with fluorine-containing silane, while fluorocarbons have high thermal stability and are difficult to decompose, and their extensive use will cause environmental problems such as water, soil and air pollution. The main methods for constructing micro–nano rough structures include the template method, chemical deposition method, phase separation method, sol–gel method, layer-by-layer self-assembly method, etc. These preparation methods rely on complex chemicals and sophisticated experimental equipment. Based on the current problems of preparation cost, environmental pollution and mechanical durability, the direction of future research on superhydrophobic surfaces is to find low-cost, green and energy-saving material systems while optimizing and simplifying the preparation process, design a preparation method suitable for large-scale industrial production, and, what's more, make superhydrophobic surfaces non-fluorinated and have the properties of strong mechanical stability and self-repair.

High-adhesion superhydrophobic surfaces inspired by rose petals have layered micro-papillae and nano-folds, which provide sufficient roughness while making the surface have high adhesion to water. By replicating the surface morphology of a petal, it is known that the high-adhesion superhydrophobic surface is in a Cassie impregnating state. Atomic force microscopy (AFM) has been used to analyze the difference between the micro–nano structure and a structure with the lotus effect and it was concluded that its P–B (peak-to-base) height and bump density are smaller, which is the root cause of high adhesion. The method of obtaining an artificial high-adhesion superhydrophobic surface is mainly based on its surface as a template to replicate polymer films with different periodic microstructures to meet the needs of different polymer precursors, which can realize large-scale manufacturing. In addition, it can also be prepared by constructing a superhydrophilic pattern on a low-adhesion superhydrophobic surface. This surface with adjustable adhesion can be used in broad applications of liquid transportation, microfluidic devices, biochemical analysis and targeted drug transportation. At present, researchers have not been able to accurately measure the size of the adhesion force, nor have they been able to quantitatively analyze the relationship between the adhesion force and the surface structure. In addition, improving the economics and practicality of the surface is a problem that we urgently need to solve. The research on the preparation of multifunctional, responsive and adhesive intelligent superhydrophobic surfaces with special materials is still in the early stage of development, and further efforts are needed.

SLIPS inspired by pitcher plants separate the external liquid from the solid substrate due to their lubricating layer. They have excellent properties such as resistance to organic and complex liquids, resistance to ice, good compression stability and self-repair. Therefore, they have great application prospects in the fields of anti-fouling, anti-frost, anti-icing, oil–water separation, etc. Designing and preparing SLIPS with stable performance requires an in-depth understanding of the four phases (solid substrates, lubricant film, impinging droplets and gas environment), as well as selection of suitable substrate roughness, and suitable surface energy and viscosity lubricants. However, the preparation of SLIPS has the problems of a complicated process, high cost, and poor stability and durability. In addition, the surface morphology, shear force, and the ratio of the viscosity of the external liquid to the lubricant will affect the stability of the lubricating layer. The future research directions of SLIPS are: looking for new materials, designing innovative structures to lock the lubricant layer and extend the service life of SLIPS, adding functional particles (bactericides, anti-icing agents, fluorescent substances, etc.) to lubricants to increase the usage scale of SLIPS and introducing smart materials (shape memory materials and phase change materials) to make a SLIPS adjust its parameters actively, in order to adapt to changes in the environment and achieve a wide range of industrial applications.

Superhydrophilic surfaces inspired by some aquatic plants and lower mosses can allow water droplets to be completely wetted and spread. Therefore, they have important applications in the fields of self-cleaning, anti-fogging, oil–water separation and biomedicine. In addition, combining surfaces with different special wettability (superhydrophobic and superhydrophilic) can be used for directional infiltration and microfluidic devices. The key to preparing a superhydrophilic surface is to control the surface microstructure and chemical composition. The problems faced are that the preparation technology is expensive and complicated, it is not easy to mass produce and it will pollute the environment during the preparation process, as well as the fact that the prepared surface is susceptible to external conditions such as light, heat, and oxygen, and has poor weather resistance and mechanical strength. At present, there are still controversies in the definition and mechanism of the research on superhydrophilic surfaces. With the increasing demand for functional surfaces in different fields, environmentally-friendly, functionally responsive, and durable superhydrophilic surfaces made by constructing microstructures and using light-induced mechanisms will surely develop by leaps and bounds.
