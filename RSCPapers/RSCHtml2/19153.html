Abstract

Two-dimensional (2D) Janus structures such as MoSSe and Janus graphene have grabbed global attention for their novel properties and interesting behaviors. In this study, via first-principles calculations, we have systematically explored the structural, electronic, and magnetic properties of Janus-functionalized Sc2CTT′ (T, T′ = H, O, OH, F, Cl). Interestingly, four Sc2COT′ were found to be ferrimagnetic half-metals with high predicted Curie temperatures, indicating their feasibility in spintronic applications. On the other hand, Sc2COHCl, Sc2CHCl, and Sc2CFCl are suitable photocatalysts for photocatalytic water splitting. In particular, Sc2COHCl with a bandgap of 0.99 eV is highlighted as an infrared light-driven photocatalyst. Sc2COHH, possessing an ideal direct band gap of 1.08 eV and noticeable optical absorption in the visible light region, is very promising as the donor material for solar cells. It is highlighted that the designed type II Sc2COHH/InS heterostructure solar cell could present a high power conversion efficiency of up to 21.04% by inducing effective photo-excited electron–hole separation, which is favorable for photodetection harvesting. We believe that our study will provide a feasible strategy for the design and application of MXenes.

1. Introduction

Two-dimensional (2D) materials have remarkable properties as compared with their bulk counterparts.1 The 2D transition metal carbides/nitrides (MXenes)2–9 are produced by etching out the A layers from MAX phases, where M is a transition metal, A is a IIIA or IVA element and X is C or N. MXenes have been actively studied for various applications including electromagnetic interference shielding,10 battery anodes,11,12 supercapacitors,13 photocatalysts,14–16 and composites,.17,18

In MAX phases, the M layers are nearly close-packed with the X atoms filling the octahedral sites, while the MX layers are interleaved with the A layers. Since M–A bonds are much weaker than M–X bonds, A layers can be selectively chemically etched by hydrofluoric acid (HF) without disrupting the M–X bonds, resulting in weakly bonded MX layers.4,5 The surfaces of MX layers are always covered with a mixture of functional groups after the etching process. Therefore, the general formula of MXenes can be described as MXTx, where Tx represents the surface functional groups such as oxygen (O), hydroxyl (OH), and/or fluorine (F).19,20 These functional groups play an important role in the properties and applications of MXenes. Very recently, MXenes with O, NH, S, Cl, Se, Br, and Te surface terminations were successfully synthesized,21 which exhibit distinctive structural and electronic properties. For example, the functionalized MXene Nb2C exhibits superconductivity.21 It is worth mentioning that graphene functionalized with O and H on opposite sides is found to act as a ferromagnetic metal, and when semi-functionalized with F on one side, it is an antiferromagnetic semiconductor.22 Such surface group engineering is expected to uncover new directions for 2D functional materials.

Inspired by the experimental achievements of Janus MoSSe and Janus-functionalized graphene (asymmetrically terminated by H and F),23–25 the synthesis of Janus MXenes with asymmetrically surface-functionalized groups is expected in the future. In our previous high-throughput research on MXene, Sc2C was proved to be metal, while Sc2CT (T = O, F, OH) turned out to be a semiconductor.26,27 Zha et al. demonstrated that one intermediate state during the conversion from Sc2C(OH)2 to Sc2CO2 is a bipolar magnetic semiconductor.28 Before the trial and error experiments, the comprehensive theoretical screening for the structural, electronic, and magnetic properties of Janus functionalized MXenes Sc2CTT′ (T, T′ = H, O, OH, F, Cl) is of great interest and importance. Based on first-principles calculations and materials screening, we show that the Sc2CTT′ are half-metals with ferrimagnetism when one side of the surface is covered with O terminations, while Sc2COHCl, Sc2CHCl, and Sc2CFCl are promising candidates for photocatalysts. In particular, Sc2COHCl exhibits good optical absorption performance in the infrared light wavelength region. Additionally, Sc2COHCl presents excellent optoelectronic properties and is very applicable as a donor semiconductor for heterostructure solar cells.

2. Theoretical methods

First-principles calculations based on density functional theory (DFT) were adopted by using the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP).29,30 For the electron exchange–correlation functional, the projector augmented-wave (PAW)31 of generalized gradient approximation (GGA) with Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE)32 formalism was employed. A vacuum layer larger than 20 Å was set in the z-direction to avoid the interaction between repeated units. The cutoff energy was set to 500 eV. The atomic structures were analyzed by the VESTA code.33 The k-points of 11 × 11 × 1 and 15 × 15 × 1, automatically generated by the Monkhorst–Pack scheme, were used for structural relaxations and static calculations, respectively. The relaxation convergence for both electrons and ions was 1 × 10−6 eV. The Heyd–Scuseria–Ernzerhof (HSE06)34 hybrid density functional was applied to calculate the band gaps. Time-dependent Hartree–Fock (TDHF)35 calculations based on the HSE06 were used to get accurate optical properties, which take the excitonic effects into account. The TDHF calculations were done in two steps. The first step is the HSE06 calculation. In the second step, based on the wave function from the first step, the dielectric function was evaluated by setting ALGO = TDHF. The PHONOPY code based on force constants obtained by the VASP-DFPT (density-functional perturbation theory) interface36,37 was employed to study the dynamic stabilities, for which a 6 × 6 × 1 supercell and a k-point mesh of 3 × 3 × 1 were used. The dipole correction has been considered for all the Janus structures during our calculations.

Having confirmed the magnetic ground state of Sc2COT′, we now turn to the electronic structures to gain further insight into the magnetic properties. Because Sc2COCl has the highest estimated Curie temperature, we take it as an example to study the electronic structure. The calculated spin-dependent electronic band structure and DOS are shown in Fig. 4. A remarkable feature of the band structure is the half-metallic character (majority-spin electrons show insulating behavior while minority-spin electrons show metallic behavior). The majority-spin electrons are insulating due to the existence of a wide bandgap as large as 3.46 eV. Thus, the electronic transport in such a system is completely spin-polarized, dominated by the minority-spin electrons. The half-metallic gap, defined as the minimum energy gap of majority-spin conduction and valence bands with respect to the Fermi level,50,51 is very important for guaranteeing the half-metallic character by preventing the spin-flip transitions. The calculated half-metallic gap for Sc2COCl is 0.98 eV, which is larger than those for chalcogenides and double perovskites.52,53 For the majority spins, the conduction band minima (CBM) are mainly contributed by the 3d electrons from the Sc atom and valence band maxima (VBM) are predominantly occupied by the 2p electrons from the C atoms. For minority spins, the 3d electrons from the Sc atom and the 2p electrons from the C atom are dominated at the Fermi level, resulting in the metallic behavior. The spin-dependent band structure and DOS for Sc2COF, Sc2COH and Sc2COOH are displayed in Fig. S4, S5 and S6,† respectively. Table 4 lists the calculated majority-spin bandgaps and half-metallic bandgaps of these four structures. Apparently, Sc2COF and Sc2COH exhibit very similar features to Sc2COCl, while for Sc2COOH, although the majority-spin bandgap is much lower, it holds a relatively high half-metallic gap of 0.61 eV. The above merits of Sc2COT′ make it very suitable for spintronic applications.

More interestingly, the calculated ΔEFM–AFM is negative from ε = 0% to ε = 4.8%, that is, Sc2COCl prefers the FM state in this strain range. As the tension increases, the value of ΔEFM–AFM becomes positive. Thus, the AFM state was the most stable one until the maximum tension (ε = 10%) was applied in our study. Remarkably, FM to AFM magnetic switching was achieved at ε = 4.8%. Fig. 5b and c give the spin-resolved density of states of Sc2COCl under 4% tensile strain for FM states and 5% tensile strain for AFM states, respectively. As is seen, Sc2COCl is an FM half-metal under 4% tensile strain, which is very similar to that under 0% tension in Fig. 4b. Sc2COCl transfers to AFM metal under 5% tensile strain, in which Sc 3d electrons and C 2p electrons are responsible for the metallic behavior.

3.3 Photocatalytic applications

According to the traditional water-splitting mechanism, the CBM of a suitable photocatalyst should be higher (more negative) than the hydrogen reduction potential (0 eV vs NHE) and the VBM should be lower (more positive) than the water oxidation potential (1.23 eV). Hence, the smallest bandgap for a semiconductor to be applied as a photocatalyst is 1.23 eV. However, such bandgap limitation has hampered the utilization of the infrared part in the solar spectrum, which consists of almost half of the solar energy. The Yang group has proposed a new mechanism for efficiently utilizing infrared areas.56 In this new mechanism, the bandgap of a suitable photocatalyst can be reduced to the infrared region (Eg > 1.23 − ΔΦ) by tuning the intrinsic dipole with an appropriate band position. Herein, ΔΦ is the electrostatic potential difference between different surfaces of the photocatalyst. Thus, if ΔΦ is large enough, i.e., ΔΦ > 1.23 eV, one can relieve the restriction on the bandgap requirement of the photocatalyst. Yang et al. designed a surface-functionalized boron-nitride (BN) bilayer (F-BNBN-H) system with an internal field for water splitting under near-infrared light. Coincidently, the outer layer of Janus Sc2CTT′ is saturated with different functional groups. Motivated by this, a blueprint came to mind, where Sc2CTT′ could be endowed with satisfactory characteristics as a proposed photocatalyst for hydrogen production, utilizing near-infrared solar energy.

For Sc2COHF, Sc2COHH and Sc2CHF, although their CBMs are more negative than the hydrogen reduction potential, the VBMs are higher than the oxidation potential of water, indicating the relatively poor performances of water oxidation. Significantly, the band gap for Sc2COHCl is 0.99 eV, which broadens the range of light absorption from the visible to near-infrared region, resulting in the dramatically improved utilization of solar radiation.

Another predominant factor that has a notable impact on photocatalytic ability is the efficient utilization of solar energy. Fig. 8 presents the calculated absorption coefficients of Sc2CFCl, Sc2COHCl and Sc2CHCl by the HSE06-TDHF method. Herein, Sc2COHF, Sc2COHH and Sc2CHF are not under consideration since they do not have favorable band alignments as discussed before. Sc2CFCl, Sc2COHCl and Sc2CHCl exhibit a quite broad optical adsorption from the UV light to the visible light region, where the absorption coefficients can reach the 105 cm−1 level, which demonstrates the highly efficient utilization of solar radiation for photocatalytic water splitting. In particular, the absorption coefficient for Sc2COHCl in the infrared light range is still up to 104 cm−1. Hence the infrared light that constitutes almost half of the solar energy can be used to produce hydrogen. Such advantage agrees well with our previous analysis. Similar results have been reported in Janus MoOTe and PtSSe as photocatalyst for H2 generation.57,61

Thereafter, we drew the schematic of the photocatalytic process in Fig. 9 to investigate the photocatalytic mechanism in Sc2CFCl, Sc2COHCl, and Sc2CHCl. After photon absorptions in these three Janus structures, shown in Fig. 9a, the excited electron–hole pairs are separated into free electrons and holes. Due to the internal electric field, free electrons will transport to the (001) surface for hydrogen production and holes will transfer to the (00) surface for oxygen generation. Thus the oxidation–reduction reaction occurs on different surfaces, reducing the free carrier recombination rate, which can dramatically improve the photocatalytic efficiency.56 We then examined the band-decomposed charge density in Fig. 9b, which shows that the charge densities of the VBM and CBM accumulated on different surfaces, agreeing well with the previous discussions.

3.4 Solar cell applications

Photovoltaic materials converting solar radiation directly into electricity are very hopeful for clean and sustainable applications of solar energy. Excitonic solar cells (XSC) are combined with 2D donor–acceptor semiconductors via the interlayer van der Waals (vdW) interactions, in which the free carriers are generated and dissociated at the heterostructure interfaces. Previous studies have proved that XSCs are promising candidates for highly efficient solar cell devices.62–66 However, finding an appropriate donor semiconductor with both high stability and ideal direct bandgap is still an urgent and challenging task. Amazingly, we found that 2D Sc2COHH is very suitable as a donor material, and the reasons are discussed in detail below.

The calculated band gap of Sc2COHH by the HSE06 method is 1.08 eV, which is very close to that of bulk silicon (∼1.16 eV), which is the optimum gap for solar cell applications. Moreover, the direct bandgap structure of Sc2COHH is beneficial for photovoltaic applications. Thus, we have calculated the absorption coefficients of Sc2COHH to evaluate the optical performance. Fig. 10 displays the absorption coefficients obtained from the TDHF-HSE06 approach, which has taken the excitonic effect into account. The absorption coefficients calculated by the DFT-HSE06 approach and the experimentally measured absorbance of intrinsic silicon are represented for comparison.67 As is seen, the absorption spectrum by the TDHF-HSE06 method is significantly higher than that from the DFT-HSE06 method, demonstrating the existence of the excitonic effect in the visible-light region. Sc2COHH shows dramatically higher light-harvesting than bulk silicon in the overall visible light spectrum range from 380 to 750 nm. The outstanding absorption properties make Sc2COHH a promising material for ideal solar absorbers.


Fig. 10 Calculated optical absorption coefficients of Sc2COHH by the TDHF-HSE06 method; the experimental spectrum of intrinsic silicon is also illustrated for comparison. The gray background demonstrates the reference solar spectral irradiance at Air Mass 1.5.68

Appropriate band edge positions of donor and acceptor semiconductors play a crucial role in the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of XSC. Given the CBM (−4.40 eV) and VBM (−5.48 eV) levels of Sc2COHH, displayed in Fig. 11a, we chose monolayer InS with CBM of −4.44 eV and VBM of −6.89 eV as a suitable acceptor semiconductor to form a type-II heterostructure solar cell. We plotted the density of states (DOS) of Sc2COHH and InS by setting the vacuum level to 0 in Fig. S11.† The type-II heterostructure nature can be observed in Fig. S11,† the VBM of which is contributed by the Sc2COHH layer, while the CBM is dominated by the InS layer. The donor bandgap Eg and the energy difference between the conduction band offset of donor and the acceptor ΔEc are marked in Fig. S11,† from which the very small ΔEc guarantees the high PCE of the heterostructure solar cell. In such a designed heterostructure, after the 2D InS contact with the H atomic surface of Sc2COHH, photo-generated electrons will transfer from the CBM (Sc2COHH) to the CBM (InS), while holes will transport from the VBM (InS) to the VBM (Sc2COHH) simultaneously since electrons and holes become stable through moving down and up, respectively.69–71 Such phenomena can efficiently separate the photo-excited electrons and holes, which is favorable for photodetection and energy harvesting.2,72–74

This PCE value is larger than that of the recently designed ZrS3/HfS3 (16–18%),76 MoS2/bilayer phosphorene (16–18%),75 phosphorene/MoS2 heterobilayer (17.5%),77 and HfTeSe4/Bi2WO6 (20.8%).78 From the above, Sc2COHH is a promising donor material for forming high-efficiency heterostructure solar cells.

4. Conclusions

In summary, based on first-principles computations, we have thoroughly investigated a novel class of Janus MXene Sc2CTT′ (T, T′ = H, O, OH, F, Cl) for multifunctional applications. The stabilities of these ten Sc2CTT′ monolayers have been confirmed by formation energy and phonon calculations. We highlight that Sc2CTT′ has non-magnetism or magnetism, depending on the different functional groups. Interestingly, four O-group-containing structures Sc2COT′ exhibit half-metallic features with ferromagnetic ordering, while the other six are non-magnetic semiconductors. Significantly, 2D Sc2COCl has a high half-metallic gap of 0.98 eV, the Curie temperature of which can be as high as 292 K. Such advantages allow for applications as spintronic materials. We have demonstrated that Sc2CFCl, Sc2COHCl and Sc2CHCl monolayers are promising candidates for photocatalytic applications due to the favorable band edge positions, large internal electric field, high and broad efficient absorption in the visible and UV light regions. In particular, Sc2COHCl with a bandgap of 0.99 eV can be applied in photocatalytic water splitting under the near-infrared light region. The 2D Sc2COHH, as characterized by a moderately direct bandgap and high optical absorption in the visible-light range, is a suitable donor material for constructing heterostructure solar cell devices. The proposed Sc2COHH/InS heterostructure can achieve a PCE of up to 21.04%. It is expected that these new 2D Sc2CTT′ monolayers can be applied in spintronic, photocatalysis and heterostructure solar cells. Our exciting results provide new insights into the possible applications of Janus Sc2CTT, and also pave the way for designing more Janus MXenes in this field.
