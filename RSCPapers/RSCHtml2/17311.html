Abstract

Manganese dioxide (MnO2) has been considered one of the most attractive materials for the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) due to its low cost, natural abundance, easy synthesis, multiple morphology and polymorph, environmental friendliness, and high potential theoretical catalytic performance. However, the unsatisfactory oxygen evolution reaction (OER) efficiency of MnO2-based materials has confined their practical applications. Fortunately, recently, enormous efforts have been invested to improve the oxygen evolution reaction efficiency and clarify underlying mechanisms. Here, the aim is to exhaustively summarize the recent advances in the application of manganese dioxide and its composites as electrocatalysts for the oxygen evolution reaction. Fundamentally, the mechanisms and correlatively critical parameters of OER catalysis are discussed. Moreover, the diverse preparation routes of MnO2 nanomaterials are itemized elaborately. The morphology control, structure construction, facet engineering, element doping, and construction of binary/ternary heterojunctions are presented. Their applications in OER catalysis are also highlighted. Finally, a proposal for the research gaps and perspective on the challenges and development direction for future research on nanostructured MnO2-based materials in the field of OER catalysis applications has been propounded. Therefore, this review provides basic guidance for the rational design and construction of high-efficiency MnO2-based catalysts for OER.

1. Introduction

With the rapid development of urbanization and science-technology, energy exhaustion, and environmental pollution have pushed renewable energy sources to the forefront.1,2 Therefore, it is essential to discover efficient and sustainable technologies for environmental purification and crises.3–5 Electrochemical water splitting has been recognized as a promising approach for efficient and clean energy conversion with zero carbon emissions.6–9

The oxygen evolution reaction (OER) is a critical process, with the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) for electrochemical water splitting. In particular, OER is very crucial in a wide range of energy conversion and storage technologies. The key issue is to develop highly active electrocatalysts to address the sluggish dynamics of OER. Tremendous research attention has been paid to this challenging conundrum. Noble metal oxides are regarded as the benchmark catalyst for the oxygen evolution reaction with the obtained rapid reaction kinetics.10–15 Unfortunately, the natural scarcity, inferior resistance to poisoning, and high cost of these noble metal catalysts remain major obstacles to their practical applications. Recently, noble metal-free electrocatalysts fabricated for oxygen evolution reactions include metal oxides,16 metal nitrides and oxynitrides,17 metal carbides and carbonitrides,18 metal sulfides,19 metal halides,20 hydroxides,21 and other carbon-based materials,22–27 organic–inorganic hybrid materials,28–32 zeolite,33 and nanoclays.34,35 Transition metal oxides have attracted a lot of attention as a class of electropositive oxides with abundant catalytic sites, as well as for their structural stability, chemical tunability, environmental friendliness, and simplicity of synthesis. Among multiplex catalysts, MnOx has gained widespread respect for its unique properties, such as earth-abundance, low cost, low toxicity, environmental compatibility, and large surface area. Since the discovery of PSII systems in nature, the search for active MnOx materials as artificial water-splitting catalysts has a long history and extremely high potential theoretical catalytic performance. Among the diverse MnOx, MnO2 is polycrystalline with various morphologies. Through the basic [MnO6] structural unit, different stacking and connection modes are formed into different tunnel structures, and then generate a variety of crystallographic forms and morphologies. The application of MnO2 in water-splitting systems is described in detail. Compared with the other 3d metal oxides such as Co3O4, NiO and Fe-based oxide, manganese dioxide (MnO2) has the advantages of high oxygen storage capacity, rich redox chemistry, self-repairing, and high surface-area utilization owing to different kinds of crystal structures (α-, β-, γ-, σ- and λ-MnO2) and nanostructures (nanorods, nanowires, nano-sheets, nanotubes, etc).36–38 However, most polycrystalline MnO2 exhibit limited electrocatalytic OER activity due to their poor conductivity, perfect lattice structure, and half-filled T2g orbitals. But Mn has a number of variable valences, which are favorable for facilitating charge transfer during electrolytic oxygen conversion. More importantly, the morphology, crystal structure, and surface chemical state of MnO2 can be tuned to further promote its catalytic properties. Strategies such as heteroatom doping, morphological tuning, heterostructure formation, conductor support, defect engineering, and valence regulation have been implemented to optimize the catalytic performance of MnO2.

However, several drawbacks still limit the catalytic efficiency of MnO2. Firstly, nanosized MnO2 agglomerates are prone to fragmentation due to their high surface energy during catalytic reactions. In addition, MnO2 is difficult to apply in aqueous media and recover due to its tendency to levitate in water. Secondly, the low catalytic activity at low temperatures limits further practical applications due to the sluggish chemical dynamics. Thirdly, the catalytic mechanism still needs to be implemented and the active sites should be clearly defined.

Hence, in the past decades, significant efforts have been made on MnO2-based materials, which offer considerable methods for high-efficiency water splitting applications. Although some reviews have reported MnO2-based materials on the topic of electrocatalytic water oxidation.39–42 A systematic overview of frontier scientific research on OER catalysis is still lacking. 2. Polymorphic phase structures

Generally [MnO6] octahedral units offer angles or edges to form crystalline MnO2 materials with various tunnels and layered structures.43 In nature, there are six main MnO2 types, namely, α-, β-, γ-, ε-, δ-, and λ-MnO2 (Fig. 1).44 Among them, α-, β- and γ-MnO2 belong to a 1D tunnel structure, δ-MnO2 is a two-dimensional (2D) layered structure, and λ-MnO2 is divided into the three-dimensional network structure. Specifically, α-MnO2 is made up of [MnO6] octahedral double strands, shared with a corner in adjacent double chains along the c-axis. Thus, α-MnO2 possesses (2 × 2) and (1 × 1) tunnel structures, in which the sizes of the tunnels are 4.6 Å and 1.89 Å, respectively. Most metal cations (K+, NH4+, Fe2+,…) and micromolecules (water, NH3, …) can be accommodated in the large (2 × 2) tunnel. In β-MnO2, the manganese (Mn) atoms occupy the center of the [MnO6] octahedron, while the oxygen (O) atoms are arranged in the six vertices with hexagonal close-packed structure. The [MnO6] octahedron of β-MnO2 exhibits a (1 × 1) tunnel, and the size is 1.89 Å. These narrow tunnels make only small ions (H+, Li+) to accommodate. γ-MnO2 grows with (1 × 1) and (1 × 2) tunnels alternately, forming a hexagonal closely packed structure. In γ-MnO2, disordered and irregular alternate growth exists in the tunnel, which results in poor crystals due to the defects and vacancies. The [MnO6] octahedron of δ-MnO2 consists of a 2D infinite layer, in which Mn atoms occupy the holes of the [MnO6] octahedron, and O atoms form a close hexagonal structure. The interlayer distance affords 7.0 Å. Alternately, empty layers can host holes rich in metal cations, water molecules, and other substances. While λ-MnO2 is a typical 3D spinel structure with a (1 × 1) tunnel. 3. Preparation method of manganese dioxide materials

In order to broaden the range of applications of MnO2 materials, various fabrication methods have been reported to produce MnO2 materials with different morphologies, polymorphs, high porosity, and large specific surface areas. Common preparation methods include electrodeposition techniques, hydrothermal methods, sol–gel methods, microwave-assisted methods, electrostatic spinning techniques, and template-assisted preparation methods, etc.

3.1 Electrodeposition technique

At present, the electrodeposition methods are mainly divided into electrochemical deposition and electrophoretic deposition. Electrochemical deposition is the process of depositing metal oxides from their aqueous solution by the action of an electric field. Electrophoretic deposition is the deposition of colloidal particles in a dispersion medium under the action of an applied DC power supply, which moves in a directional manner towards the cathode or anode, thus depositing on both poles. A combination of electrochemical deposition and electrophoretic deposition has also been used to synthesize such compounds. By controlling the deposition rate, MnO2 electrodes of different densities and morphologies were obtained by electrodeposition on anode or cathode substrates in a solution containing manganese ions. MnO2 prepared by electrodeposition techniques is simple and easy to manipulate, without the use of polymer binders, and is free from contamination, and mild reaction conditions. It has been widely used in the field of electrochemical energy storage.45,46 Depending on the deposition method, electrodeposition techniques can be divided into three categories, namely, constant-current deposition, constant-voltage deposition, and cyclic voltammetry. MnO2 prepared by different techniques present different morphologies and crystal structures, which directly affect their physical and chemical properties. For example, Chen et al.47 used constant-current electrochemical deposition technology to electrodeposit ε-MnO2 with a nanometer flower-like structure on the substrate in a three-electrode system using manganese nitrate and sodium nitrate as electrode liquid. The specific capacitance of the composite electrodes can reach 1230 F g−1. In addition, the prepared MnO2-CNT-sponge supergiant exhibits excellent capacitive properties and cycle stability with power and energy densities up to 63 kW kg−1 and 31 W h kg−1. Su et al.48 prepared MnO2-PeDOT:PSS composite by a one-step deposition process using manganese acetate and polyethoxythiophene (sodium styrene sulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS) as the electrolyte in a two-electrode system, in which MnO2 showed weak crystallinity. The specific capacity reaches 1670 mF cm−2 at the current density of 0.5 mA cm−2. Wang et al.49 prepared MnO2–NiFe electrode with high activity and cost-effective performance through a simple electrodeposition process, which showed excellent activity. Wu et al.50 took titanium dioxide nanoarray (TNAs) as the carrier and modified TNAs with manganese dioxide and Pd by two-step electrodeposition treatment. The prepared Pd–MnO2/TNAs composite structure showed excellent electrocatalytic activity. Studies have shown that a very short deposition time is detrimental to the formation of oxide particles, and a very long time can lead to excessive oxide loading and affect the stability of the product. It is important to note, however, that the structure of the MnO2 on the electrode can be designed in advance to meet the performance requirements of the electrode, such as high capacitance, excellent stability, and recovery capability. Efforts have been made to utilise this electroplating-based deposition method in order to easily obtain diverse and flexible structures, such as wires, sheets, or spheres of MnO2.

3.2 Hydrothermal method

In a closed system, the reaction is facilitated by using high-temperature and high-pressure conditions to increase the temperature to the saturation vapor pressure. The hydrothermal method has many advantages, such as it is not bound by the template, the initial reactants do not need to be soluble, the nanomaterials can be synthesised more easily by choosing the right temperature, time, etc. The synthesis is carried out in a confined space, minimising external interference and greatly improving the purity of the resulting products, which is one of the most commonly used synthesis methods in practice. Manganese dioxide nanostructures of various shapes and sizes can be produced by hydrothermal synthesis, which has the advantages of high purity, good dispersion, complete crystalline shape, and controlled grain size. However, it is more tedious than other synthesis methods due to the high temperature and pressure environment. At present, hydrothermal methods have been widely used to synthesize MnO2 with specific sizes and morphologies, for example, nanoparticles,51,52 nanosheets,53 flower-like structures,54 nanotube shapes,55 nanowire or rod shape structures,56,57etc. Wei et al.58 prepared a novel 2D long-range ordered β-MnO2 by a one-step hydrothermal method. The network structure, consisting of uniformly interwoven nanowires, allowed sufficient contact with the electrolyte and reduced the polarization of the electrodes. This material can be used as an electrode in supercapacitors with excellent electrochemical properties and a specific capacity of up to 453 F g−1. Lu et al.59 successfully embedding Fe3+, Co2+, and Ni2+ into the manganese dioxide layer containing nickel foam (NF) by a hydrothermal method and ion exchange method, and obtained Fe–MnO2/NF, Co–MnO2/NF, and Ni–MnO2/NF. Fe–MnO2/NF showed excellent performance with low over-potential (330 mV at 20 mA cm−2) and Taffeal slope (51 mV dec−1). Han et al.60 prepared porous Co3O4@MnO2/Ni nanomaterials by a hydrothermal method. At low charge/discharge voltages of 300 mA g−1 and 0.76 V, the catalyst exhibited a durable cycle lifetime of 170 cycles. Du et al.61 prepared MnO2/Co3O4 catalyst by a two-step hydrothermal method, which was used as a new air electrode material. Co3O4 nanoparticles were uniformly coated on the surface of MnO2 nanotubes. The combination of Co3O4 and MnO2 nanotubes effectively enhanced the cyclic stability and reduces the charging potential. Liao and colleagues62 prepared nickelsiron embedded K0.23MnO2 cubic nanoflower (Ni–Fe–K0.23MnO2 CNFs) through a hydrothermal reaction. Ni–Fe–K0.23MnO2CNFs-300 showed good OER activity, with an overpotential of 270 mV at 10 mA cm−2, significantly higher than Ni–Fe–K0.23MnO2CNFs (302 mV), K0.23MnO2Ns (355 mV) and IrO2 (297 mV). Xiong et al.63 prepared NF–Ni3S2/MnO2 through a simple two-step hydrothermal method. NF–Ni3S2/MnO2 perfectly integrated and optimized the advantages of multilayer Ni3S2 and MnO2, achieving the fast dynamics and superior performance required for overall water decomposition in alkaline media. Zhang et al.64 prepared core-double shell electrodes by coating manganese dioxide nanorods on carbon spheres (CS) by a hydrothermal method. MnO2 nanorods were further dispersed with Pd nanoparticles of 40–50 nm diameters, which acted as OER electrocatalysts. In the hydrothermal method, the final product properties depend on the hydrothermal conditions (time, temperature, solution concentration, pH value, pressure, and exterior reaction environment). Various MnO2-based architectures can be fabricated at pre-designed reaction conditions in different kinds of structures. However, the main challenge in hydrothermal synthesis is to achieve control of crystal growth, which determines the structure, size, and dimensions of the nanostructures, and this remains to be addressed.

3.3 Sol–gel method

As a common material synthesis process, the sol–gel method is based on the hydrolysis of condensed metal alcohol salts or inorganic salts and their gradual transformation into gels, which are then treated accordingly to obtain powdered materials. The products prepared by the sol–gel method are homogeneously dispersed on a chemical scale and have a high degree of purity. However, due to the long reaction time and agglomeration during calcination, it is often used in combination with other synthesis methods such as the template method to improve the sol–gel method and to produce more complete and regular nanomaterials. A technical method for converting a precursor solution containing Mn2+ into a solid MnO2 powder or film. The sol–gel method can effectively control the purity, composition, and homogeneity of the product to obtain high-quality MnO2. However, sol–gel reactions require the use of large amounts of organic solvents, which limits the large-scale preparation of MnO2 electrodes to a certain extent. Reddy et al.65 used sodium fumarate as an organic solvent to reduce sodium manganate and potassium permanganate, and successfully prepared two forms of MnO2 by a sol–gel method, which showed a maximum specific capacity of 130 F g−1. Kim et al.66 selectively electrodeposited Co3O4 and MnO2 on 3D porous nickel foam (NiF) by a simple agarose gel-mediated method. This new structure improved the catalytic activity of OER and ORR with little physical interference to the catalytic function.

3.4 Microwave-assisted method

Different from the traditional oil bath and hydrothermal method, microwave-assisted preparation can heat the material rapidly inside by microwave radiation, and the radiation frequency commonly used is 0.3–2.45 GHz.67 The highly homogeneous structure of MnO2 can be obtained using microwave-assisted methods. For example, Meher et al.68 successfully prepared α-MnO2 with a high specific surface area (155 m2 g−1), high crystallinity, large pore volume (0.67 cm3 g−1), and spherical porous graded form by the microwave-assisted method. In addition, the α-MnO2 prepared by microwave synthesis displayed better electrochemical performance than the traditional reflow method. The specific capacity of α-MnO2 can reach 168 F g−1, and the capacity retention rate of 91.4% after 400 cycles of charge and discharge. To better control the morphology, phase state, and size distribution of the product and to prepare MnO2 quickly and efficiently, the scientists combined microwave and hydrothermal methods. For example, Chen et al.69 prepared different forms (including flake, rod, and line) and different crystal forms (including α, β, and γ phases). Compared to single microwave and hydrothermal methods, MnO2 active material prepared in this way has better crystalline and electrochemical properties and significantly shorter reaction times.

3.5 Electrospinning technique

Using the principle of electrostatic repulsion between surface charges, MnO2 precursor solutions are uniformly sprayed onto the substrate surface at high voltage through an injection nozzle to form quantities of specific size and morphology. Electrostatic spinning technology is widely used in the preparation of flexible energy storage devices because of its simplicity, low relative cost, and large-scale production.70,71 Li et al.71 mixed PVP and manganese acetate tetrahydrate as the precursor solution and successfully prepared MnO2 nanofibers by electrostatic spinning technology. The flexible capacitor has a specific capacity of up to 645 F g−1 and 95% of the initial capacity was retained after 2000 cycles using an ordered population of 10 nm MnO2 nanoparticles as the electrode material.

3.6 Template-assisted preparation method

The template method is an effective bottom-up synthesis method that is widely used for the synthesis of nanostructures of various sizes and shapes. In general, the template-based synthesis process consists of different steps that can be broadly divided into three stages: (1) preparation of the template, (2) construction of the material of interest on the template surface by applying conventional synthetic methods (condensation, redox, and electrochemistry), (3) removal of the template by selective etching or calcination strategies. It is worth noting that these template methods produce target designs with specific shapes and sizes. Based on the different types of templates, the preparative methods can be divided into hard template preparation methods and soft template preparation methods. Among them, the hard template-assisted preparation method uses a number of materials with specific structures to support the synthesis of MnO2. Synthesis methods include the aforementioned hydrothermal, sol–gel, and electrodeposition methods to obtain MnO2 nanostructures of different sizes and morphologies. Common hard templates include mesoporous silica,72 silica spheres,73 porous anodic alumina (AAO),74 naturally grown diatoms,75 polycarbonate membranes,76 and carbon nanotubes.77 Li et al.78 prepared 3D hollow layered MnO2 by the template-assisted hydrothermal method. Using diatoms with three different growth forms as templates, the fabricated MnO2 electrode material fully reproduced the characteristics of diatoms with high porosity, large specific surface area, and low volume density, and exhibited electrochemical performances. In order to shorten the ion diffusion distance and speed up the kinetic reaction in the electrochemical reaction, Duay et al.79 synthesized 1D layered MnO2 complex array using template-assisted electrodeposition technology. With the highly ordered and porous structure of AAO (aperture range: 10–200 nm, length range: 1–50 mm, density range: 109–1011 cm−2), the diameter and length of the prepared MnO2 nanowires can also be controlled artificially, thus, effectively improving the electrochemical performance. Boyjoo et al.80 synthesized hollow MnO2 spheres using SiO2 nanospheres as sacrificial hard templates. The SiO2@MnO2 core–shell spheres were obtained by MnO2 coating on SiO2, calcinated at 300 °C, and removed the template by NaOH etching.

The method of soft template-assisted preparation involves inducing MnO2 to self-assemble by using soft templates, commonly used templates include surfactants, organic molecules, microemulsions, and block copolymers.81 The use of surfactants can not only reduces the surface tension of the material during the reaction but also effectively reduces the agglomeration effect and the preparation of MnO2 with a high specific area. Compared to the hard template-assisted preparation method, the soft template-assisted preparation method is simpler and cheaper. Liu et al.82 obtained monolayer MnO2 nanosheets by one-step synthesis of the redox reaction between sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) and potassium permanganate. During the reaction, SDS not only acted as a reducing agent to reduce potassium permanganate but also dominated the formation process of monolayer MnO2. The progressive hydrolysis process of the SDS controls the rate of the redox reaction, which is a key factor in the formation of the monolayer MnO2 nanosheets. Moreover, the monolayer MnO2 nanosheets prepared as electrode materials for supercapacitors showed excellent pseudocapacitance performance. In addition to SDS, surfactants have also been reported to include polyethylene glycol (PEG), polyethylene pyrrolidone (PVP), tetraoctyl ammonium bromide (TOAB) and cetyltrimethylamine bromide (CTAB).

There are various preparation methods for MnO2, and different preparation methods have their own advantages and disadvantages. The hard template-assisted preparation methods can be used to synthesize MnO2 with specific structure and morphology to better control the size and homogeneity of the product, but the subsequent removal of the template is complicated and costly. Soft template-assisted preparation methods by adjusting the type, and composition of the surface active agents can directly affect the product morphology and structure of MnO2, the mesoporous structure obtained was MnO2, but surfactants removal required a lot of time, and residual surfactant produced larger interfacial resistance, having a direct impact on the electrochemical properties of the electrodes. Electrodeposition technology, hydrothermal method, sol–gel method, microwave-assisted method, electrostatic spinning technology, and other methods do not need the use of the template, the preparation cost is lower, relatively simple and easy, and can regulate the composition and morphology of the product as required, as well as increasing defects and improve the performance of MnO2-based materials. It has more advantages in mass production and preparation.

Gao et al.83 proved that the template mesostructure can influence the amount and the kind of acid site, reduction ability, and influence the catalytic performance of the MnO2. The catalytic reduction of NOx into NH3 of MnO2 templated from 3D mesoporous material displayed better performance than that of the other MnO2. 2D-MnO2 and 3D-MnO2 were also prepared by Bai et al.84via the hard template method for low-temperature oxidation of ethanol. Zhang et al.85 prepared mesoporous MnO2 (M−MnO2) with SBA-15 as the hard template. The ordered MnO2 nanorods exhibited efficient catalytic ozonation of phenol due to the mass transport and active sites exposed with high surface area.

3.7 Ultrasonic method

Acoustic wave-based synthesis is a facile top-down method that has become an effective strategy for the fabrication of nanoparticles. This facile method is commonly used to reduce the size and thickness of 2D materials, resulting in nano-sized dots. Due to the weak van der Waals forces, these pre-engineered or commercially available multilayer 2D MnO2 sheets can be subjected to a simple ultrasonic treatment in ice water to obtain fewer layers of MnO2 than in larger MnO2 sheets. In addition to obtaining thinner MnO2 nanoflakes or even monolayers of MnO2, multilayer MnO2 nanoblocks were initially prepared and exfoliated into thin flakes. In this case, the precursors of the multilayer MnO2 nanoblocks and the etching process played a crucial role in the preparation of uniform-size MnO2 nanosheets. Balakumar et al.86 synthesized uniform α-MnO2 nanorods via the ultrasonication method using the ultrasonic bath at 20 kHz and 100 W. The α-MnO2 nanorods presented efficient catalytic conversion of methylene blue, one of the refractory pollutants. The catalytic conversion reached 99.7% efficiency with good reliability and catalytic cycling stability. Efforts have also been made to produce ultra-thin MnO2 nanosheets. Efficient ultrasonic methods can be used to prepare MnO2-based quantum dots. However, this method has the major disadvantage of low quantum yields and must be improved.

3.8 Gas–solid liquid method (VLS method)

In 1964, Wagner and Ellis proposed the mechanism of VLS crystal growth to explain the formation of silicon whiskers.87 In this method, the reactants are deposited on a low-temperature substrate and grown into a one-dimensional structure by gas transport, which is the traditional method for growing one-dimensional materials by gas reaction or physical evaporation of metallic organic compounds at high temperatures. Research show that nanoparticles react in a gaseous state at the first melting temperature of nanoscale droplet formation, forming low eutectic alloy droplets by transit and impurities (catalysts) at low temperatures, which later reach supersaturation as the concentration increases and the crystalline interfacial layer precipitates nucleation (or by heterogeneous nucleation) on the surface of the substrate. As the interfacial layer continues to absorb reactive molecules in the gas phase and in the crystal nucleus, the whisker continuously advances one step towards crystallization. The circular alloy droplets are then lifted upwards until they cool and form small solidified droplets. The results showed that the VIS growth method has the advantages of easy manipulation, easy control, and good product crystallinity. The VLS method involves the gas-phase transport of reactants at a constant temperature, the preparation of catalysts, and the growth of one-dimensional nanowires. Kim et al.88 synthesized rod-like spinel nanostructured LiMn2O4 through this method, and studied its related properties as cathode material of the lithium-ion battery.

3.9 Ion exchange method

Crystalline compounds of lamellar or tunnel manganites have a pore structure, which presupposes ion exchange. By controlling reaction conditions, organic ions or metal ions pass through the pore or interlayer vacancy area of the manganese oxide crystal compound in ion exchange or the REDOX mode and then obtained modified crystalline manganese oxide compound by the hydrothermal or calcination treatment.89,90 Djerdj et al.91 used birnessite manganese oxide as the precursor of the reaction, by exchanging Mg2+ into the precursor layer by the ion exchange method, and then combined it with hydrothermal treatment to prepare the 2 × 4 tunnel manganese oxide. Yan et al.92 made use of the differences in solubility of different substances to prepare metal oxides such as zinc oxide, copper oxide, alumina, and manganese trioxide with various micrometer and nanometer morphologies by the ion exchange method, and the morphology of different products such as pH value, temperature and reagent types accompanying the reaction system also changed accordingly. Yang et al.93 prepared MnO2 nanosheets embedded with transition metal cations (Cu2+, Co2+, Fe3+, Ni2+ and Zn2+) by a simple cation exchange method. Among them, Ni–MnO2 showed excellent performance. Compared with other metal cationic embedded MnO2 nanosheets, the activity of Ni–MnO2 was very low, and the overpotential (η = 330 mV) at the current density of 10 mA cm−2 was significantly lower than that of MnO2 (η = 581 mV).

3.10 Thermal decomposition method

Thermal decomposition methods are often referred to as high-temperature assisted cleavages of the chemical linkages established between precursors to create new structures. Similarly, at high temperatures, the chemical bonds in the Mn-based precursors can be broken and re-formed as MnO2. The classical thermal decomposition method was developed by LaMer and Dinegar,94 who believed that the preparation of monodisperse nanocrystals required a short, discontinuous nucleation process, and then the growth of crystal nuclei was controlled. Thermal decomposition at different temperatures produces MnO2 with different crystal structures, however, it should be noted that the appropriate temperature plays a key role in the purity of the desired end product. On this basis, Cheng et al.95 obtained nano-MnO2 with different crystal shapes and morphologies by the hydrothermal treatment of Mn(NO2)2 solutions with different concentrations. The precursor manganese citrate was prepared with manganese acetate and citric acid as raw materials, and then calcination of the precursor at 300 °C for a certain time to obtain a black powder of γ-MnO2. Characterization tests indicated that it had a tunneling structure, and electrochemical studies indicated a specific capacitance of as high as 109.76 F g−1. Zhang et al.96 prepared spherical nano MnO2 by decomposition of precursor manganese acetyl acetonate(II) by adjusting the heating rate, (2/3 < x < 1). Zhang et al.97 synthesized β-MnO2 nanorods by thermal decomposition of precursor MnOOH obtained by hydrothermal treatment of KMnO4 in ethanol solution. The prepared β-MnO2 nanorods demonstrated excellent catalytic oxidation of methylene blue dye via a Fenton-like reaction. Ye et al.98 synthesized FeC/MnO2 composites at a pyrolysis temperature of 800 °C and a ratio of 1/1 of Fe/Mn precursor. The reaction kinetics of the synthesized FeC/MnO2 was improved and the current density of LSV was better than that of the ruthenium dioxide catalyst. The solvothermal decomposition method has the following advantages: (1) high-temperature reactions can be carried out in organic solvents so that some substances cannot react at low temperaturesand may thermally decompose or initiate reactions in organic solvents. (2) The solvent reactants are likely to generate highly reactive intermediates to replace the solid phase in the soft chemical synthesis of some substances, metastable substances with special optical, electrical, and magnetic properties can be obtained by this method. (3) The reaction in the solvent can effectively prevent the pollution of oxygen in the atmosphere or inhibit the oxidation process of the product, which is very conducive to the preparation of high-purity material. However, it is worth noting that the effects of high temperatures on all reactants should be carefully considered.

Among the various synthetic strategies mentioned above, the hydrothermal method has the advantages of simplicity of operation, low cost, and rapid synthesis, which is the most widely used method for the preparation of MnO2-based nanostructures. Nevertheless, the main challenge of hydrothermal synthesis is to control the crystal growth and thus determine the nanostructure, size, and dimensions.99 While electrodeposition techniques are commonly used to fabricate MnO2 supercapacitor electrodes.100 In this regard, different types of electrodeposition, such as dynamic potential, constant current, and constant potential have been applied to prepare MnO2 layers with different crystal structures.101 It is important to note that the structure of MnO2 on the electrode can be designed in advance to meet the performance requirements of the electrode such as high capacitance, excellent stability, and recovery.102,103 Efforts have been made to obtain a variety of diverse and flexible structures conveniently using the electrodeposition technique. Although thermal decomposition, acoustic chemistry, microwave-assisted, electrospinning, and gas–solid liquid methods offer higher efficiency, obtaining morphologically homogeneous structures of manganese dioxide-based systems is very challenging because the generated products are too disordered. In addition, inhomogeneous-shaped products during the thermal decomposition reduce the mass yield, leading to difficult MnO2 separation and stringent temperature regulation. The size is adjusted by the regulation of crystal production. To meet the need to fabricate complex structures, such as hollow structures, the products of substrate-free redox methods undergo hydrothermal synthesis, which is regulated by crystal production to create more precisely controllable structures, in terms of size. For this purpose, electrochemical and template-based synthesis techniques often rely on substrates, which contribute to different structures and numerous designs consistent with further functional processes. Electrochemistry, sol–gel methods, microwave-assisted methods, template synthesis, and so on are usually dependent on substrates, which contribute to a variety of structures and numerous designs consistent with further functional processes. For example, hard template-assisted preparation methods can be used to synthesise MnO2 with specific structures, morphologies, and better control of the size and homogeneity of the product, but subsequent removal of the template is more complex and costly. Soft-template-assisted preparations can directly influence the morphology and structure of the MnO2 product by adjusting the type and composition of the surfactant, resulting in mesoporous MnO2, but surfactant removal is time-consuming and residual surfactant can generate high interfacial resistance, which directly affects the electrochemical performance of the electrode. However, substrate stability is highly considered during structure design, reaction conditions, and product isolation, which remains a major challenge in the fabrication process. Electrodeposition technology, hydrothermal method, sol–gel method, microwave-assisted method, electrostatic spinning technology, and other methods do not require the use of templates, have lower preparation costs, are relatively more simple and easy to use, and can be adjusted according to the need to control the composition of the product, morphology, and the addition of defects to improve the electrochemical properties of MnO2-based energy storage devices, more advantageous for large-scale production. To meet the need of fabricating complex structures, such as hollow structures and core–shell structures, combining multiple synthesis methods or developing new technologies can help fabricate more precise and controllable structures, sizes, and diversity, which are being prospected to bring new and flourishing opportunities.104

Until now, significant progress has been made, and the development of efficient and controllable scalable methods for the synthesis of micro/nano MnO2 is still necessary. To date, micro/nanocomposites with specific functionalities can only be prepared on a laboratory scale in low yields and in a time-consuming manner. Further research is focused on developing advanced synthesis techniques for structurally and compositionally controllable MnO2 materials, improving their stability and conductivity, revealing the relationship between morphology, crystallinity, composition, and properties, and further exploring the catalytic mechanism of MnO2 materials during the electrochemical processes. Although these challenges remain, the rapid development of nanotechnology has given such functional materials a bright future in electrochemistry. Through the continued research efforts on materials, scientists, chemists, and engineers in this exciting field, the practical application of MnO2 in electrochemical oxygen evolution reactions may eventually be realised.

4. Modification of MnO2 materials

MnO2 is undoubtedly one of the most promising materials for environmental applications. In the past few years, many encouraging strategies have been developed to modulate MnO2-based materials, such as morphology control (1D, 2D, 3D), structure construction (hollow structure, ultrathin structure, mesoporous structure), facet engineering, element doping (metal and nonmetal doping) for single MnO2 species, as well as the design and fabrication of MnO2-based composites by homojunction construction and binary or ternary heterojunction construction.

Based on the single-crystal chemical bonding theory, various thermodynamic and dynamical morphologies can be obtained due to the preparation method and the polymorphic nature of MnO2. Enriched morphology control and structure construction can increase the specific surface area, boost oxygen vacancies and surface defect formation, facilitate mass transport and light harvesting, accelerate charge motion, and improve the electron–hole pair separation efficiency. Das et al.129 synthesized δ-MnO2 flowery nanocomposites over the clay nanotube surface by light-assisted decomposition of the manganese precursor. The obtained δ-MnO2 flowery nanostructures induced enhanced photocatalytic activity of dyes. Although the crystalline form and morphology of MnO2 can be effectively controlled by adjusting the hydrothermal reaction temperature, time, molar ratio of precursors, pH value, and the concentration of surfactants, its bulk solid structure still suffered from disadvantages such as low specific surface area and few active sites. An effective solution to overcome these drawbacks is to replace the bulk solid structures with some advanced structures such as hollow structures, ultrathin structures, and mesoporous structures because of their structural and performance advantages.

4.1.2. Hollow structure construction. Further structural design has been executed in order to gain a deeper understanding of the influence of manganese dioxide morphology and the structure on performance. Hollow MnO2 spheres were prepared for the catalytic oxidation of formaldehyde.80 γ- and δ-MnO2 could be obtained by simple modification in the precursor addition protocols. The high surface area, hierarchical pore size, and the presence of oxygen vacancies due to the high mobility of oxygen species lead to catalytic activity. MnO2 hierarchical hollow nanostructures were prepared under controlled preparation conditions for water treatment.137 Hierarchical hollow microspheres and microcubes of MnO2 nanosheets can be obtained by self-assembly from different morphologies of MnCO3 precursors by adding the (NH4)2SO4 solution in the reaction system. Tan et al.138 prepared porous 3D MnO2 hollow microspheres by the self-template process for the degradation of bisphenol A. MnO2 porous hollow microspheres possessed enhanced activity owning to hollow-mesoporous shell spherical structure. Ultrathin MnO2 hollow nanoballoons with a large ratio interface to total atoms were synthesized by inflating MnO2 with CO2 followed by instant freezedrying.139 Ultrathin MnO2 hollow nanoribbons have been shown to be highly catalytic for the long-term durability and efficacy for the breakdown of methylene blue dye. Ge et al.140 synthesized hierarchical flower-like iron containing γ-MnO2 hollow microspheres for arsenic transformation. Yang et al.141 constructed ramsdellite MnO2 hollow spheres for the purification of benzene by a facile method. Closely stacked sheets constitute the resulting MnO2 hollow spheres. The highly efficient catalytic activity is attributed to the lattice oxygen activity inspired by solar irradiation. Li et al.142 also synthesized hierarchical hollow MnO2 microspheres for the oxidation of benzene. Discus-like nanoplatelets and nanorods are composed of hierarchical hollow MnO2 microspheres. Hollow urchinlike α-MnO2 nanostructures were synthesized by Zhou et al.143 with three types, namely, columnar nanorod clusters, tetragonal nanorod clusters, and tetragonal nanotube clusters. The tetragonal nanorod clusters show the best microwave absorption properties among the good matching of electromagnetic impedances.
4.1.3. Ultrathin structure construction. Wang et al.144 confirmed that single-layered MnO2 nanosheets were promising for next-generation paper-like spintronics. The single-layered MnO2 nanosheet showed halfmetallicity with a wide range of vacancy concentrations of Mn and O. Zhao et al.145 developed monolayer thick δ-MnO2 nanosheet arrays on nickel foam for bifunctional electrodes for water splitting. The bifunctional electrode displayed excellent oxygen evolution reaction and hydrogen evolution reaction activity since the ultrathin δ-MnO2 nanosheets contained abundant oxygen vacancies, giving half-metallic properties and strong H2O adsorption. Miao et al.146 constructed ultra-thin MnO2 nanosheets via H2O swelling followed by a cetyltrimethylammonium bromide-intercalation exfoliation. The ultrathin MnO2 nanosheets were 1–2 layers thick and had rich surface oxygen vacancies. These benefitted the oxidation of formaldehyde. Moreover, MnO2-pillared layered manganese oxide could be fabricated for capacitive behavior147 and ultrathin manganese dioxide (MnO2) nanosheets for HCHO adsorption and removal,148 and for catalytic activity in the water oxidation reaction.149
4.1.4. Construction of the mesoporous structure. Jiao et al.150 synthesized mesoporous β-MnO2 as the electrode for rechargeable lithium batteries for the first time. The obtained mesoporous β-MnO2 displayed highly crystalline walls with ordered pore structure. It accommodated invertible intercalated Li, preserving the crystal structure on the cycle and maintaining the ordered renormalization instead of switching to the spinel structure. This is different from electrochemical reactions with nanoparticulate β-MnO2 or chemical intercalation into the bulk β-MnO2. Mesoporous MnO2 were synthesized from mesostructured silicas (KIT-6, SBA-15, MCM-41) as hard templates.83 Mesoporous MnO2 templated from KIT-6 exhibited the best catalytic performance among the three types of mesoporous MnO2, inducing 100% NOx conservation from 75 °C to 275 °C, as well as good resistance of SO2 and H2O. Bai et al.84 also reported 3D mesoporous MnO2 showing catalytic activity for ethanol in low-temperature, primarily due to good reducibility, Mn4+ ions, and numerous surface adsorbed oxygen species. Meanwhile, mesoporous MnO2 was prepared for the catalytic ozonation of phenol.85 3D macroporous and mesoporous γ-MnO2 afforded the excellent catalytic performance of toluene.110 The macroscopic and enigmatic morphology provided accessible molecules to the catalyst and improved the mobility of the Olatt. Macro–mesoporous ε-MnO2 was observed for HCHO oxidation in good catalytic stability.151 This is due to the formation of meso/macroporous structures with high surface area and good accessibility to active surface sites.

In summary, manganese dioxide is available in many forms such as nanowires, nanotubes, nanosheets, nanoboxes, microflowers, mesopores, and hollow spheres, which can be effectively applied for electrocatalytic water splitting. Among different morphologies, mesoporous and hollow sphere nanostructures can provide more catalytically active sites due to their porous structure and large surface area. Therefore, the electrocatalysis of oxygen evolution reaction (OER) using α-MnO2 mesoporous or hollow spheres can provide low electrochemical polarisation as well as good cycling stability. The α-MnO2 showed higher OER performance, which can be attributed to the combined effect of its electrical conductivity and intrinsic tunnel size. The layered δ-MnO2 exhibited excellent OER catalytic activity due to its abundant O vacancies and associated Mn3+ active sites.

4.2 Engineering crystal facets

The activity of the exposed crystal facets directly influences the physical chemistry of the material. Crystalline materials with different exposed crystal faces usually have different physicochemical properties because their exposed crystal faces have different exposed atoms, distorted electronic structures, and different surface active sites. It is also important to realize the controllable synthesis of highly active surfaces for materials. Rong et al.152 synthesized α-MnO2 nanowires with majorly exposed high-index {310} with the assistance of a capping agent of oxalate ions for the catalytic oxidation of formaldehyde. The resulting α-MnO2 nanowires with exposed {310} facets endowed better activity for formaldehyde oxidation compared to α-MnO2 with low-index facets ({100} and {110}). 100% of 100 ppm of HCHO were mineralized into CO2 at 60 °C, which is better than some catalysts supported by Ag. The density functional theory (DFT) calculations also confirmed that the {310} facets with high surface energy can facilitate O2 and H2O adsorption/activation and benefit oxygen vacancies generation, which significantly enhanced activity for HCHO oxidation. α-MnO2 nanofibers also showed high adsorption capacity for As(III) depending on the (1 0 0) facet. The As(III) formed stable complexes on (1 0 0). The maximum adsorption capacity was higher than that reported for α-MnO2, β-MnO2, and γ-MnO2. Yao et al.153 controlled the morphology of β-MnO2 by K cations, which affected the α-K0.5Mn4O8 intermediate phase formation and the subsequent phase transition process. High concentration of K cations in the precursor gradually changed β-MnO2 from bipyramid prism ({100} + {111} facets) to an octahedron structure ({111} facets). β-MnO2 octahedrons with highly exposed {111} facets donated better rate crystals than the bipyramid prism structure as the cathode for Li-ion batteries. Besides, the team also found that β-MnO2 crystals with highly exposed {100} and {111} facets reduced the charge and discharge overpotential of the cathode catalysts in Li–O2 batteries.154 The solution mechanism was facilitated by {111} facets of β-MnO2 for the formation of Li2O2 toroids and the surface mechanism was catalyzed by the {100} facets for the generation of detrimental Li2O2 thin films. The difference in the LiO2 intermediate adsorption energies on the {100} and {111} facets led to different formation mechanisms. Chen et al.155 used first-principle calculations to investigate the electrochemical performances of different surfaces of β-MnO2. The {001} surface of β-MnO2 afforded superior surface adsorption pseudocapacitance and lower ion diffusion barrier, in which surface F-termination promoted a large percentage growth of the {001} surface in β-MnO2. Wei et al.156 synthesized column β-MnO2 [CM] with mainly the {110} facet and pincer β-MnO2 [PM] with {110} and {101} facets for the adsorption and oxidation of arsenic. The adsorption and oxidation of β-MnO2 was significantly depended on pH.

The integrated λ-MnO2 with numerous (311) facets with the functional groups of poly(mphenylenediamine) form core–shell MnO2@poly(m-phenylenediamine) (MnO2@PmPD). The distinct adsorption towards Pb ions from water by MnO2@PmPD was attributed to the synergistic effects between λ-MnO2 and PmPD, in which, Pb and O atom interacted on the surface of λ-MnO2. DFT calculations revealed the adsorption affinity toward Pb was in the order of (311) > (111) > (400) > (440) facets. The adsorption of λ-MnO2 toward Pb was facet-dependent. The high calculated adsorption energy reflected that the (311) facet was the predominantly adsorption site. δ-MnO2 flower-like nanospheres were prepared by Chu et al.157 through Mo doping. Mo–MnO2 (001) slabs could enhance the conductivity of MnO2 and activate the inert Mn sites to synergistically boost the NRR (N2 reduction reaction) and suppress HER.

Crystalline materials with different exposed crystal faces often have different physicochemical properties because their exposed crystal faces have different exposed atoms, distorted electronic structures, and different surface active sites. Therefore, much work has been done to prepare crystals with highly reactive exposed surfaces. Crystal facet engineering is an effective strategy for regulating the catalytic activity, exposing the corresponding special crystalline surfaces respectively, and thus modulating the oxygen vacancy concentration, so that they show significant differences in activity as catalysts for oxygen evolution reactions.

4.3 Metal and nonmetal doping

The morphology and polymorphism of MnO2 can be regulated by doping, which affects the specific surface area, surface species, electronic structure, oxygen–manganese bond strength, etc. The synergistic effect can modify the optoelectronic properties and improve the adsorption and catalytic properties. Hence, great efforts must be devoted to metal doping or nonmetal doping in MnO2. Generally, heteroatom doping can effectively modulate the intrinsic properties of MnO2, thus contributing to the modification of the adsorption and catalytic activity. The active centres in these systems can be oxygen atoms in the vicinity of the dopant or the dopant itself. Metal combinations are expected to improve the OER performance in terms of intrinsic activity or number of active sites, which has been strategically considered. To improve the intrinsic activity of the active site, the adsorption energy of the existing active site can be optimised by introducing exotic atomic doping to position the active site closer to its peak in the volcanic plots. In addition, the doping of the exotic atoms can provide additional active sites by activating inert atomic sites, leading to catalytic enhancement. In principle, the adsorption energy of an intermediate is determined by the electronic structure of the catalyst surface. Foreign atoms have two main effects on the electronic structure, the ligand effect and the strain effect. The ligand effect refers to the perturbation of the electronic structure caused by the charge transfer between adjacent metals. As multiple metals have different electron-giving capabilities, the charge transfer occurs via shared oxygen ions, thus changing the electronic structure of the catalyst surface. The strain effect arises from changes in the surface lattice parameters. The addition of foreign atoms inevitably alters the lattice parameters and thus the degree of overlap between atomic orbitals, resulting in a change in the electronic structure. Ligand effects and strain effects are usually inseparable as they occur simultaneously in polymetallic oxides. Therefore, the electronic structure of the modified manganese oxides is considered to be the combined result of these two effects. In the preparation of MnO2 by heteroatom doping, the doping of metal cations is easily achieved. In most cases, effective doping of the metal cations can be achieved by simply adding a cation salt to the MnO2 precursor solution. There is a large body of literature on the enhancement of the adsorption and catalytic activity of MnO2 by doping with various metal cations. However, few reports exist on the doping of MnO2 with non-metallic anions under harsh conditions.
4.3.1. Alkali metals doping. Umek et al.158 used the ion exchange process and subsequent thermal treatment to synthesize α-MnO2 nanorods with different potassium contents. The high potassium content increases the decomposition temperature of MnO2. Yin et al.159 synthesized different MnO2 structures by controllable cations. α-MnO2 was formed when K+ amount was higher than H+, whereas β-MnO2 structures grew when H+ amount was high. δ-MnO2 was obtained under the high K+ concentration. The specific capacitance strongly depended on the crystallographic structure, which followed the order α-MnO2 > δ-MnO2 > β-MnO2 at a current density of 0.5 A g−1. Besides, initially, the increasing content of K+-doped MnO2 was found to obviously improve the catalytic performance for HCHO oxidation160 and high-performance electrode for aqueous supercapacitor161 because of the considerable lattice oxygen activity enhancement. Kang et al.162 reported that the catalyst performance for formaldehyde oxidation can be improved by Na+ modification. Meanwhile, the remaining anion on the support acted as a mild poison by covering the surface active site of the manganese oxides. The enhancing effect of anions in the salts followed the order of CO32− > SO42− > NO3−.
4.3.2. Transition metal doping. Gao et al.163 synthesized transition metal (Fe, Co, Ni, and Cu)-doped α-MnO2 nanowires by the hydrothermal method as CO oxidation catalysts. Among them, Cu-doped α-MnO2 nanowires showed nearly 100% conversion of CO. Density functional theory calculations proved that Cu doping facilitated the oxygen vacancy formation in MnO2, which is the rate-determining step for the CO oxidation reaction, easier than that by Fe-, Co-, and Ni-doped and pristine MnO2. Fan et al.164 investigated Fe-doped α-MnO2 nanorods for the catalytic removal of NOx and chlorobenzene. Embedding Fe yielded (t2g)3(eg)1 configuration of Mn3+, which anisotropically distorted the a-MnO2 lattice and significantly increased the Mn–O bond length along the local z direction. Ge et al.165 fabricated hierarchical fower-like Fe-doped γ-MnO2 hollow microspheres assembled by thickness-tunable nanosheets for phosphate removal. The crystal structure, morphology, surface characteristics, and nanosheet thickness of γ-MnO2 could be changed by Fe doping, which further tailored the phosphate removal performance. In particular, the hierarchical MnO2 microspheres with 15 at% Fe incorporation achieved completely low concentration phosphate (below 10 ppm) removed at a dosage of 0.5 g L−1 with selectivity, ascribed to the increased surface specific area and fully exposed active sites of the hierarchical micro/nanostructures. Chen et al.166 constructed cobalt-doped MnO2 nanofibers for propane oxidation. The optimized MnCo0.2 nanofibers exhibited the propane conversion of 50% and 90% at 205 and 223 °C, respectively. Co2+ doped α-MnO2 also exhibited superior resistibility to water vapor, which was in comparison with the catalytic activity of some noble metal catalysts.167 The mobility of the active oxygen species and the redox capacity of MnO2 were enhanced by doped Co2+. Dong et al.168 found that nickel doping could improve the catalytic properties of α-MnO2. Significantly, substitutional Ni2+ doping in octahedra MnO6 frameworks not only improved redox properties but also promoted toluene adsorption by forming lattice oxygen mobility and more active lattice oxygen. The doping of W6+ into MnO2 was obtained for the ozone decomposition catalyst.169 Doping tungsten changed the morphology from nanorods to nanoparticles, which greatly increased the specific surface area, acid sites, and oxygen vacancies of MnO2. The W–MnO2 achieved 50% decomposition efficiency for 120 ppm ozone with high ability under dry conditions. Ma et al.170 also synthesized tungsten-doped layered MnO2 nanosheets (W–MnO2) for photo-assisted catalytic combustion of benzene. W doping induces abundant VO and intensified covalent interactions in the W–O bonds, which served as active sites for O2 chemisorption and facilitated O2 polarization and e− transfer, respectively. Yang et al.171 synthesized vanadium-doped MnO2 with poorer crystallinity, exposure of high-index facets such as (301) and (211), and larger specific surface area. Vanadium doping benefits ozone decomposition due to numerous oxygen vacancies and acid sites on the catalyst.
4.3.3. Rare-earth metal doping. Liu et al.172 reported the samarium doping todorokite-type MnO2 can boost the catalytic oxidation of airborne benzene. Sm0.01MnO2 (atomic ratio of Sm/Mn was 0.01) showed 100% benzene conversion for 229 ppm at 150 °C and above under 120 L g−1 h−1, and almost no residual benzene was detected at 200 °C and above, even at 240 L g−1 h−1. Li et al.173 revealed that Ti-doped δ-MnO2 showed good catalytic oxidation properties over benzene. Ti doping induces active oxygen formation and abundant pore structures, facilitating gas diffusion and reactions. La doping can improve the oxygen reactivity of MnOx.174 La doping clearly affects the crystal structure, lattice defects, surface phase composition, reduction properties, and apparent morphology of the La0.4–MnOx catalysts. Ce-doped α-MnO2 contributes to the low-temperature selective catalytic reduction of NO.175
4.3.4. Other metal doping. Hu et al.176 prepared Al-doped α-MnO2via the hydrothermal method with high mass-loading pseudocapacitor and improved conductivity. By modifying and optimizing the chemical composition, Al-doped α-MnO2 could achieve a high energy density of 2.42 mW h cm−3 and power density of 5.72 W cm−3.
4.3.5. Nonmetal doping. In addition to metal cation doping, much research has been performed on nonmetal anionic doping of MnO2, which also affected intrinsic properties.177 Ke et al.178 prepared nitrogen-doped manganese oxide (N–MnO2) for oxidative activity catalysts. N–MnO2 showed >99.9% selectivity of 2,5-diformylfuran and 100% conversion of 5-hydroxymethylfurfural. Nitrogen doping increases the sites of the surface defect and coordination of unsaturated Mn sites, greatly promoting oxidative activity. He et al.179 further studied the effects of N-doping sites, electronic structures, and valence states in MnO2 lattice. The interstitial N-doping MnO2 showed excellent catalytic activity for arcinogenic HCHO decomposition compared to the substitutional N-doped MnO2. This was attributed to the effective narrowing of the band gap and enhanced electron transport. B-doped MnO2 nanosheets were fabricated as electrocatalysts for the nitrogen reduction reaction.180 It has been shown that B-doping can induce abundant O-vacancy, which promoted conductivity and enhanced the intrinsic nitrogen reduction reaction activity of MnO2.

Doping can optimize the crystal structure of manganese dioxide, increase the defect sites, provide additional reactive oxygen species, and improve the structural stability of the catalyst. Different ions embedded in the interlayer or even replacing the atoms in the crystal cause local structural disorder in MnO2, producing a lattice distortion. The lattice distortion and surface-suspended bonds favor the generation of catalytically active *OOH intermediates, which is essential for the OER process. In addition, amorphous electrocatalysts have high structural flexibility due to the relatively loose stacking of atoms, allowing the catalytic active sites to adapt to the flexible geometry required for electrocatalysis.

5.1 Homojunctions heterojunction

Homojunctions are made of identical semiconductor materials. Due to their interfacial polarization or better impedance matching properties, the construction of homojunctions can promote enhanced photoelectrochemical properties of the material, as well as adsorption and catalytic properties. Therefore, coupling MnO2 components with different crystalline phases or crystalline planes or sizes to form MnO2/MnO2 homogeneous junctions can provide a new strategy to enhance adsorption and catalytic properties without relying on additional semiconductors. Various MnO2/MnO2 homojunctions have been developed for different applications. Ma et al.181 constructed hierarchical α-MnO2 nanowires@ultrathin δ-MnO2 nanosheets core–shell nanostructure, consisting of ultrathin δ-MnO2 nanosheets with a few atomic layers well grown on the surface of ultralong α-MnO2 nanowires. The first specific capacitance of the hierarchical α-MnO2 nanowires@ultrathin δ-MnO2 nanosheet nanostructure reached 153.8 F g−1 with a discharge current density of 20 A g−1, and the cycling stability was retained at 98.1% after 10000 charge–discharge cycles. The excellent rate capacity and stability were attributed to the stable structural backbone provided by the 1D α-MnO2 nanowire core, more reactive active sites created by an ultrathin 2D δ-MnO2 nanosheet shell, and the synergistic effects of different dimensions. The construction of heterojunctions is an effective strategy to promote the enhanced catalytic adsorption performance of MnO2. A variety of MnO2-based heterojunctions have been reported for different applications.
5.2.1. MnO2/metal. MnO2–Au NPs are decorated MnO2 nanowires with ultrasmall Au nanoparticles (3 nm) used for silane oxidation and H2 production.185 The catalysts displayed good stability and recyclability (Fig. 4a–f). Au NPs can facilitate oxidative processes and thus yield high performance towards the oxidation of hydrosilanes. The Auδ+ species and oxygen vacancies concentration at the catalyst surface represented highly catalytically active sites towards oxidation reactions. The ultra-small size of Au NPs at the MnO2 surface enabled the exposure of high-energy Au surfaces/facets, high surface-to-volume ratios, and their uniform dispersion. Single-atom Pt into MnO2 was synthesized by a one-pot hydrothermal process.188 100% conversion of 0.42 ppm toluene was achieved at 28 °C under the high gas-hourly-space-velocity of 300 L g−1 h−1 and 100% conversion of 10 ppm toluene was achieved at 80 °C and complete oxidation into CO2 at 220 °C. The surface and interface are the main sites for the electrocatalytic reactions, and the design of electrocatalysts by regulating the surface intrinsic structure and constructing heterogeneous interfaces can significantly reduce the overpotential and improve stability. 6. Fundamentals of the oxygen evolution reaction (OER)

The OER process consists of multiple electron transfer steps of electrochemical reactions with hysteresis. The catalytic mechanism has been frequently described in terms of the adsorbate evolution mechanism.

6.1 Mechanism of the OER

In the neutral and alkaline media, the four-electron pathway is as follows. Reactions 1–4:266–273
 
* + OH− → OH* + e−(1)
 
OH* + OH−− → O* + H2O + e−(2)
 
O* + OH− → OOH* + e−(3)
 
OOH* + OH− → O2 + H2O + e− + *(4)

Further derivation.

 
4OH− → O2 + 2H2O + 4e−(5)

In an acidic medium, OER is viewed as proceeding through the following four steps:

 
* + 2H2O → OH* + H2O + H+ + e−(6)
 
OH* + H2O → O* + H2O + H+ + e−(7)
 
O* + H2O → OOH* + H+ + e−(8)
 
OOH* → O2 + H+ + e− + *(9)

Further derivation,

 
2H2O → O2 + 4H+ + 4e−(10)
where * denotes active sites on the surface of the catalyst, OH*, O*, and OOH* are intermediates formed during the four OER steps. All these four-electron pathways are thermodynamically uphill.274,275 The biggest energy barrier is the rate-limiting step. The binding energies of the intermediates before and after each reaction determine the potential.267 When the energy barrier of the OH* → O* and O* → OOH* steps are equal, the OER overpotential is the smallest. Therefore, the activity of the catalyst is optimized when it can catalyze the OH* → O* and O* → OOH* steps with similar overpotentials.268

6.2 Parameters for OER

Some critical parameters are summarized to evaluate and compare the oxygen-catalytic properties of different catalysts.
6.2.1. Potential and overpotential. The theoretical equilibrium potential is 1.23 V for the OER. The overpotential (η) represents the potential at a certain current density minus 1.23 V.276 The current density at 10 mA cm−2 (j = 10) is usually regarded as a standard to assess the OER catalytic activity of different OER catalysts.277,278 Additionally, the onset potential (the potential at the current density of 1 mA cm−2) is another important potential parameter.279 Linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) can be generally used to obtain the above potential parameters.
6.2.2. Tafel slope. The Tafel plot is the curve of overpotential versus logarithm value of current density in the LSV curves, which reflects the electrochemical kinetics referring to an electrochemical reaction rate to the overpotential.280,281 The Tafel equation is an empirical formula, whose mathematical expression is η = α + b log(j), where η represents overpotential, j denotes current density, α is a constant, and b is called the Tafel slope. The Tafel slope obtained from the Tafel plot is related to the electrostatic dynamics. In general, a smaller Tafel slope indicates a faster electrochemical reaction rate.
6.2.3. Stability. The ability of the oxygen catalysts to maintain their catalytic activity refers to the change in the catalytic activity over time. The chronopotentiometry (CP) method was used to evaluate the stability of the oxygen catalysts for OER. The variation of the potential at a fixed current density after a long time was negligible, indicating robust stability. Besides, cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves can also be used to assess the stability of the oxygen catalysts at somewhat higher scan rates, which are obtained through LSV measurements. The small shift of the potential manifests good stability towards oxygen catalytic reactions.
6.2.4. Exchange current density. The exchange current density (j0) is another important descriptor of OER kinetics and reflects the intrinsic electron transfer rate between the electrode and the electrolyte. The exchange current density is the current density in the absence of net electrolysis at η = 0. Currently, exchange current density cannot be measured directly by electrochemical methods and can only be calculated by the Tafel equation. Typically, good electrocatalysts have a high exchange current density.
6.2.5. Turnover frequency. Turnover frequency denotes the intrinsic catalytic activity of a catalyst, defined as the rate at which the amount of product generated is normalised by the number of active sites. However, in practice, it is difficult to detect the exact number of active sites, especially on solid-state multiphase electrocatalysts. By counting the total number of active species involved in an electrochemical system, including chemically inert species or buried species, the number of active sites is often overestimated.
6.2.6. Faraday efficiency. Faraday efficiency is the conversion efficiency of the electron-generating products such as molecular O2 and H2. The Faraday efficiency of an OER is the ratio of the experimental to the theoretical value of oxygen production. There are two general methods to determine the Faraday efficiency of OER. In the first method, the theoretical amount of O2 produced was calculated by the integration of chronoamperometric or chronopotential analysis. The actual amount of O2 gas produced was measured by conventional water–gas displacement, gas chromatography, or fluorescence spectroscopy. Another method is based on a rotating ring-disk electrode in which a finely polished glass carbon disk and a platinum ring assembly are used. The same potential window of the OER is applied to the disk region.
6.2.7. Electrochemically active surface area. The electrochemically active surface area is highly dependent on the different electrochemical processes and catalytic materials. There are several methods for measuring the electrochemically active surface area of different materials, such as the redox peak area integration method, hydrogen underpotential deposition, carbon monoxide dissolution, and double-layer capacitance methods. The evaluation methods and results of the electrochemically active surface area often differ for different electrochemical reactions and materials. The electrochemically active surface area of the material should not be used as a key criterion for comparing the active properties of materials in different studies. Instead, it is recommended to use the electrochemically active surface area to compare similar materials in the same study.
6.2.8. Mass and specific activities. The mass activity, defined as the normalised current of the mass loading of the target catalyst at a specific overpotential, is becoming increasingly important when evaluating the catalytic performance of OER electrocatalysts, especially when considering the catalyst cost. Therefore, mass activity is often used as a key evaluation parameter for noble metal-based materials and less important for non-precious metal-based catalysts. Another key evaluation parameter for OER is a specific activity, normalised by the electrochemically active surface area of the catalyst. The normalisation of the specific activity value over the electrochemically active surface area is more reflective of the true activity of the catalyst and has therefore received more attention. Unfortunately, accurate measurement and comparison of the electrochemically active surface area of different materials is somewhat difficult, which makes the normalised specific activity on the electrochemically active surface area a less practical evaluation parameter. In addition, benchmark overpotentials should be defined to determine mass activity and specific activity for better comparison.

7. Applications of MnO2 and its composites for OER

In recent decades, MnO2 and MnO2 composites have been extensively used in electrocatalysis in recent decades. However, their relatively low conductivity, catalytic activity, and ease of recycling in electrolytes limit their wide range of applications as catalysts. Hence, great efforts have been made to partially compensate through structural optimization and composition design.

7.1 MnO2

Generally, different polymorphs of MnO2 have an obvious influence on the OER activity. Meng et al.282 have proposed that the electrocatalytic activities in OER are strongly dependent on the crystallographic structures, and follow the order of α-MnO2 > AMO > β-MnO2 > δ-MnO2. The overpotential of α-MnO2 arrived at 490 mV at 10 mA cm−2, and the stability reached 3 h. This can be further improved by adding Ni2+. The abundant di-μ-oxo bridges existing in α-MnO2 as the protonation sites, the mixed valence (AOS = 3.7), and the lowest charge transfer resistances (91.8 Ω at η = 430 mV) were attributed to the superior OER activity. Different crystallographic and morphologies of MnO2 nanostructures have been prepared by the facile template-free hydrothermal method.283 The OER activity follows the order α-MnO2 > α-MnO2/β-MnO2 > broken β-MnO2 > β-MnO2. The intrinsic Mn atom activity in α-MnO2 and large electrochemical active surface area induced by high BET surface area improved the catalytic activity in the oxygen evolution reaction. Mesoporous γ-MnO2 obtained at the low annealing temperature was found with catalytic activity in the oxygen evolution reaction due to the highly porous and defect-rich structure.284 The overpotential reached 427 mV at a current density of 10 mA cm−2.

Meanwhile, the catalytic properties of MnO2 are affected by the different morphology. Nano-wires, nano-tubes, and nano-particles α-MnO2 have been prepared with controlled α crystallographic forms as catalysts for the oxygen evolution reaction.285 Among the three α-MnO2 shapes, the nano-wire catalyst showed the best performance. The first principles-based density functional theory (DFT) calculations proved the affinity of (310) crystallographic planes toward the water molecule and its exposure to the reaction interface facilitated the adsorption of the reactant on the catalyst surface, showing superior catalytic activity. The effect of the morphology of α-MnO2 on OER activity and resultant electrolyser performance has also been investigated thoroughly by Gupta.286 Three distinct α-MnO2 shapes, namely nanotubes, nanorods, and nanowires were employed. MnO2 nanowires exhibited high OER activity due to the large specific surface areas, high electrical conductivity, low surface average Mn oxidation states, and rich content of the 211 facet (Fig. 12a–f). Two kinds of three-dimensional (3D) radial α-MnO2 (dandelion- and urchin-like) catalysts were readily fabricated via two different redox reactions through the hydrothermal method.287 As a result, the dandelion-like α-MnO2 displayed a higher electrocatalytic performance than urchin-like α-MnO2, with an overpotential of 90 mV lower for OER, higher mass activity, and lower Tafel slope. The superior electrochemical activities of dandelion-like α-MnO2 can be attributed to the relatively larger electrochemical active surface area, less charge transfer resistance, and richer defects. Porous β-MnO2 nanoplates with high surface area showed superior catalytic performance, reducing overpotential to 450 mV at 10 mA cm−2 and Tafel slop of 78.2 mV dec−1. The catalytic activity was greatly enhanced due to the increased density of active Mn in unique 2D morphology and porosity. Au loading blockage and deterioration of catalytic activity demonstrated the significance of surface-bound catalytic Mn sites of porous β-MnO2 nanoplates.288 2D (SBA-15, MCM-41) and 3D (KIT-6, MCM-48) silica-template-derived MnO2 electrocatalysts were prepared using a nanocasting route.289 The MnO2 nanostructure derived from KIT-6 showed comparable activity to the benchmark catalyst RuO2. This is attributed to the enhancement of mass transport and electronic mobility of 3D template-derived MnO2, favored by interconnected network structures compared to that of 2D MnO2 materials.

The buserite-type MnO2 thin film structure was fabricated via electrodeposition and the subsequent ion-exchange.302 Isolated Co2+ buserite produces fast electron transfer and high utilization efficiency, which is beneficial for OER catalysis. Lee and co-workers also found that V- and Co-substituted α-MnO2 materials rendered excellent bifunctional electrocatalytic functionalities with lowered overpotentials and enhanced current densities for oxygen evolution (OER) and oxygen reduction reactions (ORR).303 The charge transfer kinetics, electrical conductivity, and electrochemical active area were enhanced due to the local symmetry of partial substitution of V and Co ions into 1D α-MnO2 nanowires, which was beneficial for the oxygen electrocatalytic functionality. 7.3.5. The uniqueness of MnO2 electrocatalyst compared with other OER materials. Current OER electrocatalysts at the industrial level are highly dependent on noble metal catalysts, mainly iridium (Ir) and ruthenium (Ru) based catalysts, especially under acidic conditions. However, the high cost, low crustal reserves, and relatively poor stability of noble metal OER electrocatalysts have hindered their large-scale commercialisation. This provides a strong incentive to promote research into non-precious metal-based OER electrocatalysts. Overall, the stable operation of non-precious metal-based electrocatalysts in acidic media is a great challenge.340–345 Fortunately, some non-precious metal-based OER electrocatalysts, such as manganese oxides, earth-rich metal phosphates, and borates, have recently been reported to be able to catalyse OER under neutral, acidic, and basic conditions and to be comparable to noble metal-based electrocatalysts. Non-precious metal base metals and alloys often need to be stabilised by a host (e.g. carbon materials) or saturated with highly electronegative elements as they cannot survive directly in harsh acidic and alkaline media.345 A great deal of research work has been devoted to investigating the OER activity of metal oxide catalysts containing transition metals, and various metal oxide families such as chalcocite, spinel, and layer structure type families that exhibit significant activity towards OER.342 These transition metal-based oxides are low-cost, easy to synthesise, and environmentally benign.344 In addition, they are stable in alkaline solutions and show moderate conductivity, which makes them good candidates for electrocatalytic OER. For example, spinel-like compounds have good electrical conductivity and high anodic potential in alkaline solutions and can be used as electrocatalysts for OER. Most spinel oxides used for OER are iron-based (ferrite) and cobalt-based compounds doped with other transition metals/alkali metals such as Mn, Ni, Cu, Zn, and Li. In various studies, spinel bimetallic oxides have been shown to have better OER activity than monometallic spinel oxides, suggesting that the different metal ions in spinel metal oxides may play different roles. Furthermore, transition metal oxide catalysts can structurally possess a rich combination of transition metals, especially chalcogenide and spinel-type structures, as the transition metals can have different oxidation states and coordination environments, which leads to a fully tunable OER behaviour. Furthermore, compounds belonging to the family of layered structure types have been extensively studied due to their incredibly high OER activity. Among other things, doping is an effective way to promote the OER activity of compounds in this family. In addition to these three families of compounds, other metal oxides also have good OER activity, some of which are formed in situ and are suitable for OER in neutral electrolytes. Various efforts and systematic studies based on these metal oxides have provided insightful observations that contribute to the understanding of the underlying mechanisms of OER. However, the disadvantage of metal oxide electrocatalysts is usually their poor electrical conductivity. On the other hand, non-oxide catalysts such as metal-sulfide compounds, metal-nickel-based compounds as well as organometallics (metal coordination complexes) have also been reported as promising electrocatalytic materials for OER and may be good candidates for overall hydrolysis reactions. However, in contrast to metal oxide catalysts for electrocatalytic OER, non-oxide catalysts for OER have only recently been reported in small quantities. Metal sulphide compounds show good OER activity in alkaline electrolytes with low overpotentials and good long-term stability. In the case of metal-sulphur compounds, their OER overpotential is usually slightly higher than that of metal-sulphur compounds. Organometallic compounds are suitable for studying the mechanism of OER because of their tunable molecular/electronic structures.343 However, their low electrocatalytic activity towards OER usually hinders their development in practice. By dispersing organometallics on specific substrates (graphene or reduced graphene oxide), researchers can greatly improve their corresponding activity. In addition to these metal-based electrocatalysts, a number of non-metallic carbon-based electrocatalysts have also been investigated for OER with moderate OER activity. Carbon-based materials such as carbon black, carbon nanotubes, and graphene doped with N, O, and S are good conductors, chemically inert to acidic and basic electrolytes, and are used for OER activity.

Among these non-precious metal catalysts, manganese dioxide (MnO2) has received increasing attention due to its high earth abundance, low cost, balanced activity stability, high oxygen storage capacity, rich redox chemistry, self-healing properties, and high surface area utilisation. Manganese dioxide (MnO2) with different crystal structures (α-, β-, γ-, σ- and λ-MnO2) and nanostructures (nanorods, nanowires, nanosheets, nanotubes, etc.), were identified as the active centre for Mn3+ intermediates in MnO2 as an OER catalyst over the entire pH range. Strategies such as heteroatom doping, morphology tuning, heterostructure formation, conductor support, defect engineering, and valence modulation were employed to effectively optimise the catalytic performance of MnO2.15,39,41

In brief, an efficient electrocatalyst should be a good conductor and have moderate adsorption energy on the surface of the electrocatalyst for oxygen intermediates such as hydrogen peroxide and superoxide (the binding energy for these oxygen intermediates is neither strong nor weak). In addition, a good redox (ability to lose or gain electrons) centre may be an effective electrocatalyst and a highly active site would be required for catalytic ability. Furthermore, it is important for the electrocatalyst to be resistant to electrolyte corrosion, especially at high anodic potentials (i.e., no phase change) or where the transition phase is stable and does not dissolve in the electrolyte. Based on these criteria, several key strategies can be developed to design a good OER electrocatalyst. Firstly, the conductivity of the electrocatalyst must be very high. The conductivity of the electrocatalyst is important because one of the duties of the electrocatalyst is to transfer carriers from the metal electrode to the adsorbed intermediate. The introduction of exogenous elements, mixing with conductive materials, and changing the morphology have great potential to improve the conductivity of electrocatalysts, not only in terms of conductivity but also in other ways to improve the catalytic ability. Secondly, the electrocatalyst should be able to withstand redox reactions at high anode potentials in the electrolyte without phase degradation. This is due to the fact that during OER these electrocatalysts may transform into other phases and dissolve in the electrolyte or be gradually stripped from the electrode during OER. The addition of other elements to the electrocatalyst in order to improve the stability of the electrocatalyst is a good strategy for extending the electrocatalyst's lifetime. In addition, the decay of OER electrocatalysts can be attributed to phase changes that result in the newly formed phase falling off the electrocatalyst (due to lattice mismatch). In such cases, a buffer layer can be deposited between the electrolyte and the electrocatalyst to minimise the problem of exfoliation of the newly formed phase to enhance its OER durability.

7.3.6. Suggestion of the large-scale preparation with high consistency for Mn dioxide-based catalysts. Currently, the important challenge for Mn dioxide-based catalysts is the large-scale preparation with high consistency. In various synthetic strategies, manganese dioxide nanoparticles synthesised by hydrothermal methods have high purity and good dispersion, making it easy to obtain nanoparticles with controlled morphology. The process is also simpler and less costly, so the common method for preparing nanostructured materials is now hydrothermal. Examples can be synthesised including but not limited to nanoparticles, nanorods, nanowires, nano-urchins, and nanotubes. This method is widely used and can be applied to the preparation of different nanostructured materials, but the process requires attention to the reaction conditions: choosing the right reaction temperature, time, active filler, and solvent. For example, the addition of surfactants can reduce agglomeration and improve dispersion, and when polymers are used, the cross-linked structure of the polymers can lead to products with a special shape. The surfactant-added manganese dioxide nanoparticles have a large specific surface area and form special bonds between the manganese ions and the surfactant, resulting in growth in specific directions. In order to better control the morphology, phase, and size distribution of the products and to prepare MnO2 quickly and efficiently, scientists have used a combination of hydrothermal and other synthetic methods such as microwave methods. For example, Chen et al.69 used microwave hydrothermal methods to prepare MnO2 in different morphologies and crystalline forms in aqueous systems of manganese chloride and potassium permanganate, which resulted in better crystallinity and better electrochemical properties of the MnO2 active material than those prepared using the single microwave and hydrothermal methods, by significantly reducing the reaction time. To meet the needs of complex structures such as hollow structures, the products of the substrate-free redox methods are hydrothermally synthesised and adjusted by crystal production to produce more precisely controlled structures, sizes, and dimensions. Additionally, inspired by natural methods, a large number of natural microorganisms can be used to synthesise a wide range of metal nanoparticles, including intracellular and extracellular preparation strategies.346–348 Biological methods using microorganisms offer advantages over traditional chemical synthesis methods in terms of ease of synthesis, biocompatibility, and environmental friendliness. Microbial synthesis of MnO2 systemic structures usually involves reacting the residue of the microbial medium with the Mn precursor solution, as the microbial medium supernatant contains components with reducing ability. For example, washed brewer's yeast cell precipitates showed the ability to synthesise MnO2 nanoparticles involving various proteins and cell wall components.349 The results demonstrate that the synthesis of MnO2 nanostructures is due to the self-assembly and tight stacking of MnO2 spheres at the nanoscale. In conclusion, due to its eco-friendly nature, this innovative biosynthesis will undoubtedly open new opportunities for the extraordinary diversity of assembly modes to flourish.104

8. Summary and prospect

Exploring efficient and inexpensive oxygen catalysts is at the heart of one of the most crucial energy storage and conversion technologies. MnO2 is one of the most promising catalytic materials for water decomposition due to its unique properties. Encouraging breakthroughs have been made over the past decade. This review provides systematic guidance on the common preparation methods and modifications of MnO2. Meanwhile, it also offers an overview summary of the applications in OER catalysts.

Although abundant synthetic methods and regulatory strategies have been proposed for MnO2-based materials, the catalytic performance in the interrelated oxygen evolution reactions has been boosted. It is recognized that MnO2-based materials are still in their infancy for catalytic applications. Therefore, there is still plenty of challenge and space for a deeper understanding of the underlying mechanisms and further efficiency improvements in OER catalysts.

(1) Morphological design and polymorph control bring advantages to MnO2 materials such as augmenting specific surface areas, facilitating the surface defects and oxygen vacancies, accelerating mass transport, and facilitating charge motion. Although various morphologies and crystal structures have been developed for OER catalytic activity enhancement in MnO2 materials, mechanical stability is still lacking. Structural collapse results in poor catalytic performance in high-temperature or humidity environments. Therefore, more attention should be given to improving the stability of MnO2 under extreme conditions. The relationship between morphological and crystal structure diversity and catalytic activity of MnO2 is in urgent need of elucidation. The generated Mn3+ by the produced surface intermediates during the OER process is unstable in acidic solutions and undergoes self-disproportionation reactions to form Mn2+ and Mn4+. Mn2+ may dissolve in the electrolyte before oxidising back to Mn3+, which accounts for its instability in acidic electrolytes. Under alkaline conditions (pH > 9), this self-disproportionation reaction is inhibited due to the higher stability of Mn3+, resulting in a better OER electrocatalytic capacity for the application of δ-MnO2 in alkaline solutions (higher specific genus of Mn3+).

Although a variety of superior morphologies and advanced structures have been developed to improve the catalytic efficiency of MnO2 materials, their mechanical and thermal stability remains inadequate. During the adsorption and catalytic reactions (especially at high temperatures or high humidity), these advanced structures may undergo a certain degree of collapse, leading to a reduction in the specific surface area, a decrease in the number of exposed active sites, and ultimately an inability to adequately maintain high catalytic efficiency or even become inactivated. Although some are promising, strategies have been proposed to improve the mechanical and thermal stability of MnO2 nanostructures, such as adding a protective shell to the catalyst, but they have not been explored enough. In view of this, new strategies need to be explored to obtain better structures with superior mechanical and thermal stability. In future research, we should pay more attention to the latest reported strategies and techniques to improve the stability of MnO2 nanostructures using emerging technologies and methods. The instability of manganese dioxide-based catalysts consists of two main categories: material instability and reaction instability. Material instability is independent of the test conditions and is usually related to material corrosion. For manganese dioxide-based catalysts, material stability can be improved by compositional adjustments, elemental substitution/doping, and new stable structural innovations. To improve the mechanical and thermal stability of MnO2 nanostructures, several strategies have been proposed, such as the addition of a protective shell to the catalyst. Core–shell structured materials formed by encapsulating the manganese dioxide catalyst with part or all of the carbon shell provide stability and performance advantages due to the protective and conductive nature of the carbon shell layer. A highly graphitised carbon shell contributes to the stability of the material, manganese dioxide-based core–shell structured material catalysts. The degradation of the activity of this catalyst is closely related to the interaction between the carbon shell and the metal core. However, the increase in the current corresponding to the reduction of manganese dioxide during the potential cycling clearly shows the loss of the carbon shell and the exposure of the inner core. In this case, the loss of carbon is considered to be the main mechanism of catalyst degradation, since once the carbon shell is partially or completely removed, the manganese dioxide core of the catalyst can no longer be protected under the harsh OER conditions. The degraded core may weaken the interaction between the core and the carbon shell, thus hindering the modification of the electronic structure and reducing the inherent activity of the carbon shell. Additionally, MnO2 nanoparticles deposited on conductive carriers, such as carbon materials and nickel foam, can further enhance their activity and stability at different pH ranges (pH 1–14) and catalytic activity. Operational instability arises from the processes affecting the stability of the manganese dioxide-based catalysts during electrochemical testing, often arising from potentially-induced elemental dissolution and/or structural/phase evolution. A lower operational (anodic) potential is a possible way to mitigate catalyst degradation as it significantly prevents the catalyst from being exposed to higher anodic potentials. However, this requires a significant increase in OER activity, and therefore, high-activity OER catalysts should be developed to exceed the baseline catalyst and meet the requirements of high current densities at lower operating voltages.

(2) MnO2 with different crystallographic facets present divergent catalytic properties. The exposed facets are dominated by reaction conditions such as reaction temperature, time, concentration pH, and cations. However, the specified and precise exposed facets are still difficult to obtain in a controlled synthesis process. Thus, systematically studying different crystal facets and seeking efficient ways to synthesize specific facets is a great challenge. In addition, each facet of MnO2 is needed to verify its special functions.

(3) Doping is an effective means to modify the intrinsic properties of MnO2, affecting the crystal structure, morphology, chemical bond strength, and electronic structure. This reduces the formation energy of oxygen vacancies and thus facilitates oxygen mobility. The above-mentioned properties favor the OER catalytic properties of MnO2. Although great efforts have been made in doped MnO2, a systematic study of anionic doping is still lacking. Moreover, a deeper understanding of the dopant sites is needed for the interpretation of the effect of doping on MnO2 catalysts.

(4) Homojunction structures have been shown to be an effective strategy for the design and preparation of MnO2-based composites and for improving the catalyst performance. Although the construction of MnO2-based homojunctions for OER applications has been explored somewhat, almost all reported MnO2-based homojunctions for OER applications at this stage are composed of MnO2 with different crystalline phases, while few MnO2-based homojunctions composed of different crystalline planes or different sizes have been reported. Therefore, it is necessary and valuable to carry out research in this area. In addition, the currently-reported MnO2-based homogeneous structures are relatively simple, with only core–shell structures reported between the two types of MnO2, which poses difficulties in further improving their specific surface area and interfacial active sites. In view of this, there is an urgent need to develop more advanced structures between the two MnO2 types, such as ultra-thin 2D/2D structures, yolk structures, and hollow cages.

(5) Coupling different materials with MnO2 can highlight the OER catalytic properties of MnO2. Various materials have been compounded with MnO2, such as metals, metal oxides, and carbon. It should broaden the scope of MnO2 composites. For example, phosphates and sulfides, and even polycompounds (like quaternary or five-element compounds) are worth exploring. In addition, more tractable, efficient, and cost-effective methods are expected to be developed to construct high-quality MnO2-based heterojunctions with improved performance.

(6) Nanosized MnO2 particles are prone to agglomeration due to their high surface energy. They also tend to float in the water and are difficult to separate. This results in poor catalytic stability and recycling of MnO2. MnO2-based materials remain a significant obstacle for large-scale practical applications for water decomposition. Continued efforts will be made in the future to improve catalytic stability and recovery by leveraging the latest reported strategies and technologies using emerging technologies and methodologies.

(7) Surface/interface modulation strategies can be effective in enhancing the catalytic activity in oxygen evolution reactions. However, there is still room to research the following methods and techniques: the direct observation and evaluation of active sites at the catalyst surface/interface, whether experimentally or using a theory, the synergistic catalytic mechanism between different components in multi-component catalysts, the in situ characterisation of the catalyst surface/interface composition, structure and catalytic interaction in real-time catalytic processes, and the large-scale preparation of high-performance catalysts.

(8) Defect engineering has become a common and popular strategy for the preparation of high-performance manganese dioxide-based electrocatalysts. However, the precise preparation of MnO2-based electrocatalysts with specific defect types remains a challenge. In addition, the currently reported MnO2 defective electrocatalyst materials are not yet sufficient for practical industrial production needs. Therefore, methods for the large-scale preparation of high-performance manganese dioxide defective catalysts must be further investigated and developed. In contrast, the role of defects in the catalytic process remains unclear. Although commonly used characterisation techniques are able to characterise defects in defect-based catalyst materials, the presence of defects inevitably alters the local electronic structure, leading to unsaturated coordination states and modulated reactivity. Therefore, how to maintain the stability or reconfiguration of the defect structure during the actual catalytic process needs further investigation. On the other hand, the synergistic interaction between multiple defects, the dynamic evolution of the structure, and the reaction mechanism of the adsorbed intermediates are still unclear and need to be further investigated in depth using advanced in situ characterisation techniques (satisfying both temporal and spatial resolution).

(9) The effect of the chemical valence of manganese (Mn3+ and Mn4+) in manganese dioxide-based electrocatalysts on the catalytic reaction is also controversial. Some researchers believe that the presence of Mn3+ plays a negative role as it leads to the instability of MnO2. They suggest that the antibonding eg1 electrons of Mn3+ (t2g3eg1) lead to longer and weaker Mn–O bonds than those of Mn4+. These weaker Mn–O bonds are more easily broken, leading to the instability of MnO2. Other researchers take the opposite view. They believe that the presence of Mn3+ plays a positive role, as it creates an electrostatic equilibrium leading to the formation of oxygen vacancies and promoting catalytic reactions. Therefore, the mechanism of the catalytic oxidation of oxygen vacancies on the MnO2 surface needs to be further investigated.

(10) Currently, most of the mechanism-related studies are limited to the distribution and morphology of the discharge products. However, the mechanisms associated with the specific growth processes of these discharge products on the electrode surface have not been investigated in depth on MnO2-based catalysts. An in-depth understanding of these mechanisms could help explore more suitable catalysts. Therefore, it is of great interest to conduct corresponding studies based on superior in situ characterisation techniques and theoretical calculations.

(11) The water-splitting process involves an anode OER and a cathode HER at a theoretical voltage of 1.23 V. Of these, the OER electrocatalyst should have excellent catalytic activity under alkaline conditions, while the HER electrocatalyst requires an acidic solution to achieve this. Several studies have been reported on the application of MnO2-based nanomaterials as bifunctional catalysts for the whole hydrolysis process. Therefore, it is crucial to explore MnO2-based nanomaterials that can effectively promote faster kinetics of two opposite reactions at the same pH environment.
