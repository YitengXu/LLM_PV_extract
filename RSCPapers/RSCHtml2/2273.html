Abstract

In this research work, new types of ruthenium sensitizers used in dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), named S3 and S4, consisting of carbazoleâ€“diazafluorene based bipolar ancillary ligands, have been synthesized and characterized by 1H NMR, UV-vis absorption spectroscopy, and elemental analysis. The energy levels and band gaps of the sensitizers were evaluated using electrochemical measurements and density functional theory (DFT) calculations. Both sensitizers show blue shifted low energy metal to ligand charge transfer bands, which could be ascertained from the relatively large dihedral angle between the secondary electron donor (carbazole) and acceptor (4,5-diazafluorene) units. The frontier molecular orbital surfaces obtained from DFT calculations revealed that the highest occupied molecular orbitals (HOMOs) of both sensitizers are dominantly populated on the secondary electron donor carbazole antenna, indicating the increased spatial separation of the HOMO away from the TiO2 surface. The DSSC fabricated using sensitizer S4 exhibited a higher photon-to-current conversion efficiency (2.11%) and these results were compared to those obtained with commercial sensitizer N719. The implications of this study may contribute to the design of new molecular architectures to be applied in DSSCs.

Introduction

Solar energy emerges from the sun and it is the cleanest and most abundant renewable energy, which can be harnessed by means of photovoltaic (PV) devices to meet the energy crisis in the near future.1 Due to the significant advances in PV technology, many types of alternative PV devices are being developed, but still inorganic silicon based solar cells are the most efficient solar cells. On the other hand, dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) due to their low cost and easy fabrication techniques, with commercially realistic conversion efficiencies (11.18%),2 are often believed as an alternative to silicon solar cells. Seminal reports on DSSCs have stimulated extensive research interest in improving their efficiencies through the modification of the device components.3 Sensitizers used for harvesting solar radiation play a vital role in determining the conversion efficiency of solar cells. Several types of photo-sensitizers, including metal complexes,4â€“6 phthalocyanines,7,8 porphyrins9â€“11 and metal-free organic dyes,12â€“14 have been investigated for their use in DSSCs in the past few decades. Remarkably, N719 has been considered as a benchmark dye in DSSCs with its record efficiency (11.18%).2 In order to achieve high energy conversion efficiency, primary work has been focused on the preparation of ruthenium sensitizers exhibiting superior light harvesting properties and near infra-red metal to ligand charge transfer (MLCT) bands. Ancillary ligands, consisting of triphenylamine (TPA),15â€“17 carbazole,18â€“22 thiophene23 or ethylenedioxy thiophene (EDOT),24 have been used to prepare ruthenium photo-sensitizers with superior light capturing abilities to match solar radiation. Consequently, a conjugated bridge such as the ethylene group (â€“CHCHâ€“),21,22 or an electron donor spacer such as thiophene,16,20 between the bipyridyl and the electron donor antenna, enhances the molar extinction coefficient of the resultant photo-sensitizer and, in turn, allows for higher conversion efficiency. For instance, Cao et al.16 designed two photo-sensitizers named KW1 and KW2 without and with the electron donor spacer thiophene between TPA and bipyridyl for DSSC applications. Among them, KW2 exhibited a red-shifted MLCT band centred at 554 nm with a high molar extinction coefficient of 2.43 Ã— 104 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1, due to the high electron delocalization originating from the TPAâ€“thiopheneâ€“bipyridyl ligand. The photovoltaic device using the KW2 sensitizer showed high photovoltaic conversion efficiency (10.7%), which is nearly 20% greater than that of the Z907-based reference device under full sunlight irradiation (AM 1.5G, 100 mW cmâˆ’2). Hence, the spacer influences the photo-physical and electrochemical properties of the ruthenium complexes for efficient light-induced charge separation. Alternatively, NCS free Ru-sensitizers prepared using visible light absorbing dipyrrin chromophores25,26 and strong donor cyclometallating ligands such as phenylpyridine27 and pyrid-2-yltetrazolate28 were demonstrated as potential DSSC components. Another promising approach is to super-sensitize the dye molecule by the addition of a secondary electron-donor group such as triphenylamine in the ancillary ligand.29,30 Such a supramolecular system provides a potentially attractive approach for controlling the charge separation, and recombination dynamics in molecular/nano crystal devices. Haque et al.30 reported a supermolecular dye sensitized nanocrystalline TiO2 film which showed a remarkably long lived (4 second) charge separated state. This effect is caused by the increase in the spatial separation between the HOMO of the sensitizer and the TiO2 surface. Therefore, the introduction of secondary electron transfer functions into the sensitizer retards the charge recombination dynamics, while still retaining light induced charge separation at the semiconductor/TiO2 interface. Thus, an innovative and systematic design of ruthenium sensitizers to surpass the photovoltaic performance of commercial N719 dye is needed.

Introduction of spiro-conjugated donorâ€“acceptor ancillary ligands into ruthenium sensitizers is another way to introduce secondary electron donor substituents.31,32 As a continuation of our effort towards the design of improved heteroleptic Ru-based sensitizers,32 here we employ 4,5-diazafluorene with carbazole antenna units to synthesize new types of sensitizers that were named S3 and S4 (Fig. 1), with the aim of further enhancing the conversion efficiencies and of gaining new insights into the opto-electronic properties of these chromophores. Bipolar ligand precursor systems (L1 and L2), containing electron transporting 4,5-diazafluorene units and hole transporting carbazole units, were prepared via a simple one-step procedure (Scheme S1, ESIâ€ ). Bromination of L1 and L2 with N-bromosuccinimide (NBS) in dichloromethane (DCM) selectively produced the corresponding dibromo compounds Br-L1 and Br-L2. Subsequently, Ullmann coupling of carbazole with Br-L1 and Br-L2 in the presence of copper(I) iodide, 18-crown-6 and potassium carbonate in 1,2-dichlorobenzene at reflux temperature resulted in ligands L3 and L4 as white solids with high yields of 82% and 85%, respectively. Finally, the ruthenium complexes S3 and S4 were synthesized via a typical one-pot synthesis (Scheme S1, ESIâ€ ). The scope of the present synthetic strategy is to utilize the electron donating ability of the carbazole unit to increase the lifetime of the charge-separated state of the dye in a DSSC, thereby reducing the recombination of the injected electron at the dye/semiconductor interface. In order to gain insights into the electronic structure and properties of these sensitizers, computational calculations using density functional theory (DFT) have been carried out.

Analytical measurements

1H and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker 500 MHz and 100 MHz spectrometer, respectively. The reported chemical shifts were in parts per million (ppm) against tetramethylsilane (TMS). Dye-sensitized solar cell fabrication

The transparent titania electrodes (active area, 0.36 cm2; thickness, 10â€“12 Î¼m) were impregnated with a 0.05 M titanium tetrachloride solution and sintered at 500 Â°C and then cooled to 50 Â°C before being dipped into dye solution. The platinum counter electrode and the dye-coated TiO2 films were then put together. A thin transparent film of the Surlyn polymer frame (25 Î¼m) was sandwiched between these two electrodes. The drilled holes were sealed with a microscope cover slide and Surlyn to avoid leakage of the electrolyte solution.

Computational studies

The geometry optimization, electronic structure calculation and localization of the frontier molecular orbitals (MOs) for complexes S3 and S4 were carried out using density functional theory (DFT) calculations in DMF as a solvent. Calculations were performed using the Becke's three parameter hybrid exchange functional and Leeâ€“Yangâ€“Parr correlation functional (B3LYP method)33 in conjunction with the 6-31G(d) basis set for H, C, N, O and S atoms and the LANL2DZ34,35 basis set and pseudopotentials for the Ru metal atom. The optimized geometries were used to obtain the frontier MOs and to carry out time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT) studies at the same level of theory. Solvent effects were taken into account using the conductor-like polarizable continuum model (CPCM)36,37 as implemented in Gaussian 09 software.38 All electronic structure calculations were accomplished by using Gaussian 09W on a 64-bit processor (64 GB RAM) DELL server and the GaussView 539 interface program was used for visualization and analysis of chemical structures and their electronic properties.

Optical properties

The electronic absorption spectra of sensitizers S3, S4 and N719 measured in DMF are given in Fig. 3 and the peak wavelength and molar extinction coefficients are compiled in Table 1. The absorption peaks at 263 and 268 nm of sensitizers S3 and S4, respectively, are assigned to the carbazole-centered nâ€“Ï€* transition.40 The peak at 291 nm for both sensitizers S3 and S4 is mainly attributed to the Ï€â€“Ï€* transitions centred at the 4,5-diazafluorene core,41 whereas for N719 the bands at 303 and 310 nm are due to Ï€â€“Ï€* transitions arising from the 4,4â€²-dicarboxylic acid-2,2â€²-bipyridine (dcbpy) ligand. From a careful comparison with the homoleptic complex N719, other absorption bands in the UV region at 341 and 342 nm, with high molar extinction coefficients, were observed for sensitizers S3 and S4, respectively. This longer wavelength absorption band can be assigned to the photo-induced electron transfer (PET) that originates from the electron-donating carbazole moiety to the electron-accepting 4,5-diazafluorene moiety.42,43 This additional band merges with the broad band in the region between 380 and 385 nm, arising from a mixture of ligand-to-ligand charge transfers (LLCTs) and high energy metal-to-ligand charge transfers (MLCTs), as observed in the case of N719. The absorption maxima of the low energy MLCT bands of S3 (516 nm; 0.64 Ã— 104 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1) and S4 (518 nm; 1.03 Ã— 104 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1) are blue shifted and exhibit low molar extinction coefficients in comparison with the standard N719 sensitizer (1.47 Ã— 104 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1). This could be associated with the large dihedral angle between carbazole and 4,5-diazafluorene units (see Fig. 2) in the ancillary ligand that limits both the Ï€ conjugation and the intra-molecular interaction between them. This also suggests that the carbazole moiety substitution on the ancillary ligand has no influence on the molar extinction coefficient of the low energy MLCT absorption band. Therefore, the non-planar geometry of the ancillary ligand causes the MLCT band of the sensitizers to remain at higher energy levels.

DFT and TD-DFT calculations

To gain more insights into the relationships between the structural, electronic and optical properties of the studied molecular systems, DFT and TD-DFT calculations were performed on sensitizers S3 and S4. The electron density distributions of the frontier molecular orbitals HOMO, HOMOâˆ’1, HOMOâˆ’2, LUMO, LUMO+1 and LUMO+2 for complexes S3 and S4 are depicted in Fig. 5. The calculated ground state values for the HOMO energy levels of the optimized sensitizers S3 and S4 were âˆ’5.35 eV and âˆ’5.38 eV, respectively, which agree well with the electrochemically determined HOMO energy levels (see Table 2). It is observed from Fig. 5 that the electron densities of the next lower energy highest occupied molecular orbitals of S3, HOMOâˆ’2 and HOMOâˆ’3 (Fig. S1, ESIâ€ ), and of S4, HOMOâˆ’1 and HOMOâˆ’3 (Fig. S1, ESIâ€ ), are dominantly populated at the ruthenium atom and the two axial isothiocyanate (NCS) ligands. Markedly, the HOMO and HOMOâˆ’1 of S3 and HOMO and HOMOâˆ’2 of S4 are localized on the carbazole moiety of ligands L3 and L4, respectively. Although Ï€-clouds localized on the carbazole units are observed on HOMOs, the Ï€-orbitals are not delocalized over the 4,5-diazafluorene unit due to the large dihedral angle between both units. This also clarifies that the electron-transporting diazafluorene unit and hole-transporting carbazole unit ensembles a bipolar charge transporting molecule. Thus, the poor Ï€-bonding orbital overlap between the ruthenium atom, NCS ligands and electron donor units may be responsible for the blue shift and low molar extinction coefficients of the low energy MLCT absorption bands in the sensitizers. It is also worth noting that the highest occupied molecular orbitals HOMO, HOMOâˆ’1 and HOMOâˆ’2 (Fig. 5) are very close in energy in both sensitizers, with energy differences among them of less than 0.04 eV. This explains the different order seen for the MOs in sensitizers S3 and S4. On the other hand, the lowest unoccupied molecular orbitals LUMO, LUMO+2 and LUMO+3 (Fig. S1, ESIâ€ ) of sensitizers S3 and S4 have amplitudes mostly on the anchoring bipyridyl ligands, while the LUMO+1 in S3 and S4 are localized on the electron-deficient 4,5-diazafluorene ligands, L3 and L4, respectively. From TD-DFT calculations, the lowest singletâ€“singlet vertical excitation energies (E0â€“0) for sensitizers S3 and S4 gave values of about 1.87 eV (Table 2), which agree relatively well with the experimental values obtained from optical analysis. It is also possible to see that the ethyl and phenyl groups, which are linked to the nitrogen atom of the carbazole units, did not contribute to the energetics of the entire molecule, although there are small differences in their absorption intensities. On the other hand, using the relation, ESOPsinglet = EHOMO âˆ’ E0â€“0, the excited state oxidation potentials (ESOPs) for sensitizers S3 and S4 gave values of about âˆ’3.48 and âˆ’3.51 eV, respectively. These values are positively shifted relative to the conduction band edge of TiO2 (âˆ’4.04 eV),45 which indicates that electron injection from both sensitizers is thermodynamically favourable.

It appears that the specific molecular structures of S3 and S4, in particular, the presence of carbazole groups at larger dihedral angles, reduce the recombination rate, most likely by inhibiting the access by iodine/iodide electrolyte to the TiO2 surface. Also, the bi-polar ancillary ligand (electron donating carbazole group and electron accepting 4,5-diazafluorene unit) undergoes efficient photo-induced electron transfer upon light absorption (S3, 341 nm; S4, 342 nm, as shown in Table 1) and acts as a source of electrons in the excited state, reasonably stabilizing the hole produced after electron injection, thereby slowing down the charge recombination (Scheme 1). Despite this setback, both Voc and Jsc were significantly improved relative to sensitizers S1 (Voc = 0.49 V, Jsc = 1.09 mA cmâˆ’2, Î· = 0.36%) and S2 (Voc = 0.50 V, Jsc = 1.23 mA cmâˆ’2, Î· = 0.42%) documented in an earlier report.32 Moreover, from DFT studies, it is realized that the HOMO for both sensitizers S3 and S4 is shifted to the secondary electron donor carbazole moiety, potentially displacing away, from the NCS ligands and closer to the carbazole moiety, the positive charge (hole) in the oxidised form of the sensitizer. Hence, the HOMO located over the electron rich carbazole might offer indirect evidence that the carbazole moiety maintains the â€œholeâ€ produced away from the TiO2 surface after electron injection from the dye-excited state into the TiO2. This results in an increased spatial separation of the cation center of the sensitizer away from the semiconductor TiO2 electrode surface. As a consequence, the secondary electron donor carbazole antenna in sensitizer S4 slows down the interfacial charge recombination dynamics at the dye/semiconductor interface, as depicted in Scheme 1, and a long-lived charge separated pair could be formed at the interface region.29,30 It is obvious that similar effects may also occur in sensitizer S3. Hence, utilization of bipolar ancillary ligands can balance the charge carriers and broaden the recombination zone in the dye/semiconductor interface, thereby reducing the recombination process. This phenomenon plays a key factor in the operation of sensitizers S3 and S4 for the achieved photon-to-current conversion efficiencies. It is important to notice that the HOMO, HOMOâˆ’1 and HOMOâˆ’2 represent degenerate states in both sensitizers and therefore a more complex mechanism may also take place in the DSSC, where the electron holes may also be formed on the NCS ligands. In spite of this, it is expected that carbazole groups have the greatest contribution to the formation of long-lived charge separated states. Further computational studies where these dyes are linked to the TiO2 surface may shed more light on this point.

Although the efficiencies of sensitizers S3 and S4 are marginally higher than S1 and S2,32 they are still low when compared to the standard N719. As explained above, this might be attributed to the long-lived charge separated states which diminish the regeneration of the neutral dye by iodide, as regeneration of well separated charge species by iodide does not occur faster than recombination of the electronâ€“hole pairs produced.46 Therefore, these sensitizers suffered from poor regeneration of the oxidized dyes formed upon light absorption. Furthermore, it has been seen that triplet intra-ligand (IL) excited states coming from the ambi-polar ligands formed between carbazole and 4,5-diazafluorene43 lead to longer lifetimes, resulting in low radiative rates for the sensitizer,47 and thus slowing dye regeneration. In line with this, ruthenium complexes appended with triphenylamine and pyrene are dominant with intra-ligand excited states and so show low DSSC efficiencies.48 Hence, the poor conversion efficiency in sensitizers S3 and S4 may be attributed to the well separated charged species at the dye/TiO2 interface and IL excited states. The simple methodology proposed here for the supermolecular design of dyes is a versatile approach to control the interfacial charge transfer in molecular electronic devices.

Conclusions

We have successfully synthesized and characterized two novel sensitizers, S3 and S4, and demonstrated their optical, electrochemical and photo-voltaic properties. A carbazole antenna was introduced to function as a secondary electron donor system in the sensitizer, with the aim of enhancing the solar cell conversion efficiency. The non-planarity and a wide range of dihedral angles between the donor and acceptor units reduced the electronic communication and orbital overlap between them. It remains clear that a perpendicular arrangement of the secondary electron donor antenna units to the coordinating diazafluorene unit is crucial for the observed absorption spectra of the sensitizer. Whilst this low absorption is not ideal for use in a DSSC, the bipolar nature of the ligand in the sensitizer helps in slowing down the charge recombination at the dye/semiconductor interface. This phenomenon provides an ideal opportunity to study chromophores such as anthracene, pentacene, fluorene and pyrene, linked to diazafluorene units, which might help in stabilizing the hole produced and also play a role in improving the light-harvesting ability of the sensitizer. Thus the combination strategy of inorganic chromophores and organic chromophores offers an alternative way for the construction of more efficient sensitizers in which multiple electron donors and chromophores can be organised in an orderly fashion to facilitate panchromatic absorption for increased device efficiency. We believe that this highlight provides a complete overview of the state of the art in this particular field and will provide guidance for strategies to design efficient donorâ€“acceptor metal complex systems towards much improved DSSCs.
