Structures and properties of conjugated Donorâ€“Acceptor copolymers for solar cell applications

Abstract

With the rapid evolution of photovoltaic polymer materials, power conversion efficiency of polymer solar cells has been markedly improved in recent years, and is now approaching a landmark value of 10 %. This review focuses on Donorâ€“Acceptor (Dâ€“A) photovoltaic copolymers. Starting from briefly introducing the Dâ€“A concept, the fundamental donor and acceptor units for constructing polymer photovoltaic materials are introduced and classified. By summarizing the structureâ€“property relationships of typical photovoltaic Dâ€“A copolymers, the important design rules for such materials are highlighted. Several crucial aspects, including proper combination of Dâ€“A units, high planarity of the backbone and proper incorporation of side chains are particularly emphasized. A new Dâ€“A architecture, namely main-chain donor and side-chain acceptor is introduced and reviewed. Moreover, the role of the electron-deficient group in fine-tuning energy levels of low-bandgap Dâ€“A photovoltaic polymers is discussed.

1. Introduction

Bulk heterojunction (BHJ) polymer solar cells (PSCs) based on conjugated p-type polymers and n-type fullerene derivatives have been intensively investigated in recent years for generation of affordable, clean, and renewable energy.1 Advantages of the BHJ PSCs include low-cost fabrication of large-area devices, lightweight, mechanical flexibility, and easy tunability of chemical properties of the polymer materials. The bulk heterojunction of regioregular poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) and [6,6]-phenyl C61â€“butyric acid methyl ester (PC60BM) represents one of the most successful systems with reproducible efficiencies of 4â€“5 %.2,3 However, further improvement of its performance is intrinsically hindered by the narrow absorption spectrum (300â€“650 nm) and the high-lying HOMO (highest occupied molecular orbital) energy level (âˆ’4.9 eV) of P3HT. In the past years, great endeavours have been devoted to developing new conjugated polymer donors4,5 and new fullerene derivative acceptors in realizing high-efficiency PSCs.6â€“10 In this paper, we concentrate on donor materials.

An ideal polymer donor for PSCs should have the following characteristics: broad and strong absorption in the visible region (to harvest enough solar light), high hole mobility (to ensure fast hole transport), suitable energy levels well fitted with that of its fullerene acceptor counterpart (to achieve satisfactory Voc and sufficiently free electrons from excitons), and appropriate compatibility with the fullerene acceptor (to form nano-scale bicontinuous interpenetrating networks for efficient exciton dissociation). These properties can offer high values of short-circuit current (Jsc), open-circuit voltage (Voc) and fill-factor (FF) of the PSCs, and result in high power conversion efficiency (PCE) (PCE = (Jsc Ã— Voc Ã— FF)/Pin, where Pin is the input light power11,12).

An efficient way to broaden absorption of donor polymers is to narrow their optical bandgaps. There are three major ways to do so (Fig. 1): (I) enlargement of the Ï€ systems,13â€“17 (II) transition from aromatic to quinoidal structures,5,18 (III) integration of donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) functional units.4 Despite that they are very efficient in engineering bandgaps, both I and II have the detrimental drawback of pulling up HOMO energy levels (decrease in Voc). Method III overcomes this problem by integrating donor (electron-rich) and acceptor (electron-deficient or electron-poor) units into one system. Such Dâ€“A strategy is now widely used to design efficient polymer photovoltaic materials. In such Dâ€“A copolymer systems, the HOMO is mainly located at the donor unit, and the LUMO is mainly located at the acceptor unit (Fig. 2), thus both HOMO and LUMO energy levels and hence bandgaps can be well tuned.19â€“21 In this review, we list those important fundamental donor and acceptor units, and review typical Dâ€“A copolymers and their photovoltaic performances. By summarizing their structureâ€“property relationships, important principles for designing Dâ€“A copolymers are highlighted.

2. Donor and acceptor building units

Regioregularity is well known to be very important for intra- and intermolecular ordering of the conjugating polymer chains in solid-state films (ordered structure enhances carrier mobility), thereby symmetric donor and acceptor units are usually selected to construct Dâ€“A copolymers. This section reviews those widely used symmetric donor and acceptor units.

2.1 Donor units

Donor unit refers to an electron-rich unit. Thiophene and benzene are the two most basic donor units, and they are also the main fundamental blocks to construct new donor units. The chemical properties of benzene and thiophene are compared in Fig. 3. Donor units derived from dibenzene and dithiophene can be classified into three types (Fig. 4), namely dibenzene bridged units, dithiophene bridged units and thiopheneâ€“benzene fused units. For simplicity, we choose the most-used benzothiadiazole as common acceptor unit for all selected donor units, by comparing performances of their corresponding Dâ€“A copolymers, the electron-donating abilities (and other related properties) of those donor units are evaluated (Fig. 5 and Table 1).

The dibenzene based monomers, fluorene, dibenzosilole and carbazole can be regarded as two benzene units bridged by a carbon (C), silicon (Si) and nitrogen (N) atom, respectively. Because of its ability of fixing the dibenzene system in one plane, this tricyclic system is much more efficient than their parent dibenzenes in building highly-conjugated polymers. The bridging atoms also have considerable positive effects on electronic properties of the polymers, which can be gradually seen in the following. Due to the electron-deficient nature and good aromaticity of the benzene unit, polymers based on these donor units normally have low-lying HOMO energy levels, and consequently their photovoltaic devices generally show high Voc. This is especially evident in fluorene and dibenzosilone based polymers. Polyfluorenes (PF) was established as the first deep-HOMO, highâˆ’Voc class of photovoltaic polymers. The pioneering work of Andersson and InganÃ¤s and their co-workers paved the way for developing polyfluorene based photovoltaic materials.22â€“24 PCE up to 4.5 % was reported for P5a (Fig. 5) based devices.25 Obtained by replacing the fluorene unit in P5a with dibenzosilole, P5b (Fig. 5 and Table 1) exhibits a higher PCE of 5.4 %.26 Such improvement in photovoltaic performance is attributed to its higher hole mobility and stronger light absorption, which are directly resulted from the better Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking caused by longer Câ€“Si bond induced less steric hindrance (in comparison with the Câ€“C bond).26,27 Considering that the carbazole unit possesses stronger electron-donating ability than dibenzosilole and fluorene (due to delocalization of the lone electron pair of the nitrogen atom over the aromatic structure),28,29 Leclerc et al. systematically explored 2,7-carbazole-based Dâ€“A conjugated polymers. P5c based devices show a preliminary PCE of 3.6 %,28 where both photocurrent and FF are larger than that of P5a and P5b based devices. Later studies reported that PCEs of P5c based devices reach as high as 6.1 % by adding an optical spacer (TiOx) and 6.5 % by incorporating a conjugated polyelectrolyte interlayer.30

Because of the electron-deficient nature of the benzene units, the above mentioned donor units can be ranked as weak donors. In their corresponding Dâ€“A copolymers, the poor orbital mixing between donor and acceptor units results in relative large bandgaps and hence narrow light absorptions, which intrinsically limit their photovoltaic performances.

Another family of donor units is based on dithiophene units. In these donor units, bridging dithiophene units with an atom of C, Si and N afford cyclopentadithiophene (CPDT), dithienosilole (DTS) and dithieno[3,2-b:2â€²,3â€²-d]pyrrole (DTP), respectively (Fig. 4). Compared to dibenzene units, dithiophene units have stronger orbital mixings with acceptor units (because of its stronger electron-delocalization ability (Fig. 3)), and tend to produce Dâ€“A copolymers with higher planarity (because flanking five-numbered thiophene rings cause less steric hindrance with their neighbor blocks than six-numbered rings do (Fig. 4)).31

The first family of low-bandgap polymers with good photovoltaic performances is the class of CPDT-based polymers. P5d (in Fig. 5) has a band gap of 1.45 eV and exhibits good absorption properties.32,33 Although performances of P5d based solar cells were limited by unfavorable phase separation, an acceptable preliminary PCE of 3.2 % was achieved.34 By adding processing additives such as alkanedithioles in the blended solution (which plays a role of adjusting the nano morphology of the blend film), PCE was improved to 5.5 %.35 Further, Yang et al. developed P5e4 by substituting the CPDT with a DTS unit. Since the Câ€“Si bond is longer than the Câ€“C bond, there is less steric hindrance, thereby better Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking between polymer molecules is expected. Moreover, the interaction of the silicon Ïƒ*-orbital and the dithiophene Ï€*-orbital should lower the LUMO energy level, which could contribute to narrowing the bandgap. Without further optimization, PCE reached 5.1 %.4

Compared to P5d, P5f, which employs electron-richer nitrogen atom, spans an even wider absorption range (extended to 867 nm) due to its lower bandgap (induced by enhanced Dâ€“A orbital mixing).36 Although solar cells based on P5f show high Jsc (11.9 mA cmâˆ’2), PCE was dragged down to 2.8 % partially by the low Voc (0.54 V). The low Voc is attributed to the high HOMO energy level pumped up by the electron-richer nitrogen atom.

Because of the low oxidation potential of the dithiophene donor unit, these donor units can be ranked as strong donors (especially for DTP unit). A general rule is that a stronger donor unit produces narrower bandgaps (corresponding to higher Jsc) and higher HOMO energy levels (corresponding to lower Voc). This can be seen by comparing dithiophene based copolymers (for example P5d and P5f) and dibenzene based copolymers (for example P5a and P5c).

The combination of the electron-deficient benzene units and electron-rich thiophene units provides a solution to developing relatively ideal donor units. In benzene fused thiophene structures, parallel Ï€-orbital interactions are able to elongate effective conjugation length and facilitate Ï€-electron delocalization, and thus can narrow bandgaps. At the same time, the electron-deficient benzene unit is able to keep the HOMO energy level low. Also, at least two alkyl chains can be incorporated in every repeat unit on the backbone of the final Dâ€“A copolymer, thereby high solubility can be ensured. Moreover, coplanar geometries and rigid structures in such systems can suppress the rotational disorder around inter-annular single bonds and lower the reorganization energy, which enhances the intrinsic carrier mobility. P5g is a good example of such polymers,37 it has a low optical band gap of 1.76 eV (which results in a film absorption edge of 706 nm) and a deep HOMO energy level of âˆ’5.36 eV. Solar cells based on P5g show high PCE of 6.4 % (with a Jsc of 11.2 mAcmâˆ’2, a Voc of 0.85 V and a FF of 67.2 %). The silicon atom bridged polymer analogue P5h was independently reported by Jen38 and Ashraf39 and their co-workers, the absorption range of P5h is similar to that of P5g. Solar cells based on P5h exhibit similar Voc to that of P5g based devices, but FF and Jsc are lower. The poorer performance may be attributed to non-optimized device engineering. Like P5f (relative to its polymer analogues P5d, P5e), the electron-richer nitrogen atom brings P5i40 a lowest bandgap of 1.40 eV, and hence broadest absorption among its polymer analogues (P5g, P5h). However, the nitrogen atom elevates the HOMO energy level, and leads to a low Voc of 0.36 V.

Among this family of donor units, benzodithiophene (BDT) is most attractive. It is simple to synthesize, and its central benzene can be substituted with two additional alkyl thienyl groups42,43 or alkyl phenylethynyl substituents44,45 with the planarity of the whole unit almost undisturbed. This is useful for making building blocks to construct two-dimensional copolymers (such as P7i42 and P7l,43 see Fig. 7) as proposed by Liet al.13,14 Details on molecular engineering of bandgaps and HOMO energy levels of BDT based polymers, and their photovoltaic performances can be found in a recent review.46

2.2 Acceptor units

Acceptor unit refers to electron-deficient (or electron-poor) groups, almost each acceptor unit contains the electron-withdrawing imine (â€“CN) nitrogen (such as benzothiadiazole (BT),47â€“49quinoxaline (QA),50,51thienopyrazine (TP),52â€“54bithiazole (BTz),55â€“60thiazolothiazole (TTz),43,61â€“66benzobisthiazole (BBTz),20,67benzotriazole (BTA),68â€“71s-tetrazine (STTz),72,73naphtho[1,2-c:5,6-c]bis[1,2,5]thiadiazole (NT)74 or carbonyl groups (CO) (such as diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP),75,76[2,3-c]thiophene-4,9-dione (NTDO),77,78isoindigo (II),79â€“81 and thieno[3,4-c]pyrrole-4,6-dione (TPD)82â€“87). The chemical structures of these acceptor units are shown in Fig. 6.

Based on their electron-accepting abilities, acceptor units can be classified into three categories: weak acceptors, medium acceptors and strong acceptors. Fundamentally, the electron-accepting ability is judged by the LUMO energy level of the unit: the lower the LUMO energy level, the stronger the electron-accepting ability. Experimentally, such ability somehow can be evaluated by comparing optical bandgaps (absorption edges) of Dâ€“A copolymers with common donor units (such as DTS or BDT) and different acceptor units: the smaller the optical bandgap, the stronger the electron-accepting ability of the acceptor unit in the copolymer. In this way, electron-accepting abilities of various acceptor units can be compared and ranked empirically. Weak donorâ€“weak acceptor,56 medium donorâ€“weak acceptor,88,89 and weak donorâ€“medium acceptor68 combinations generally result in large bandgaps due to the poor Dâ€“A orbital mixings (thereby they should be avoided in material design). Dâ€“A copolymers with good photovoltaic performances (PCE > 5 %) are usually constructed by strong donorâ€“strong acceptor,4,90 strong donorâ€“medium acceptor64,65 and medium donorâ€“strong acceptor combinations.48,83,91,37,92,93 Among them, medium donorâ€“strong acceptor combination is believed to be most effective.48,94 All possible combinations of donors and acceptors and their products are listed in Fig. 7. Besides the electron-accepting ability, planarity of the acceptor unit is also critically important for properties of the Dâ€“A copolymer. The properties of side alkyl chain and fused ring can significantly affect the planarity of the acceptor unit and hence planarity of the Dâ€“A copolymer. This effect is described and summarized below.

Motivated by its rigid and planar geometry, several groups have selected thiazolo[5,4-d]thiazole unit (TTz) as an acceptor unit to construct Dâ€“A copolymers.61,62,95â€“97 Shi et al. reported a polymer containing benzodithiophene and dithienylthiazolo[5,4-d]thiazole with a C12 alkyl chain at the 3-position of the flanking thienyl unit (P7k), a PCE of 2.7 % was obtained.61,62 Lee et al. shortened the side alkyl chain to C6 to reduce steric hindrance caused by the alkyl chain, and copolymerized this modified acceptor group with a weak donor carbazole (affording P7p), PCE up to 4.47 % was obtained for P7p based device.63 Just as its length can, the position of the side alkyl chain can also affect the final steric hindrance. You et al. pointed out that the 4â€“position anchoring of alkyl chains on the flanking thiophene units has negligible impact on the steric hindrance relative to that of 3â€“position in the diâ€“2â€“thienylâ€“2,1,3â€“benzothiadiazole (see Fig. 8).98 Inspired by these findings, Liet al. moved the alkyl chains on thiophene units from the 3-position to the 4-position under the motif of P7a64 (see Fig. 7) in order to further reduce the steric hindrance. As expected, the synthesized copolymer P7b65 does adopt a more planar structure than P7a, and possesses higher hole mobility and smaller band gap (1.76 eV). PCE of P7b based solar cells reached 5.88 % with a high fill factor of 71.6 %.

Recently it is found that rigidity and co-planarity of the fused ring in the acceptor part significantly affect the planarity of the Dâ€“A copolymer and hence its photovoltaic performance. Zhang et al. have developed a Dâ€“A copolymer (Fig. 7) based on dithenosilole (DTS) donor unit and bithiazole (BTz) acceptor unit. P7f56 demonstrated an optical bandgap of 1.85 eV with a film absorption edge of 670 nm, a hole mobility of 3.07 Ã— 10âˆ’4 cm2 Vâˆ’1 s and a PCE of 2.86 %. Considering that thiazolothiazole (TTz) contains a more rigid and coplanar fused ring than bithiazole, BTz unit in P7f was replaced by TTz, which affords P7a.31 Owing to its higher backbone planarity and the resulting stronger Ï€ stacking, P7a exhibits one-order higher hole mobility (3.56 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2Vsâˆ’1) and a 15 nm red-shifted of film absorption edge (in comparison to P7f). These improved properties account for the much enhanced PCE of up to 5.59 % (with Jsc of 11.9 mA cmâˆ’2 and Voc of 0.77 V). The effect of fused ring on PCE properties can also been seen by comparing P7g and P7h (Fig. 7). The fused aromatic ring on qunioxaline units results in higher planarity,99 which offers a narrower bandgap (induces a 63 nm red-shift of absorption) for P7h. P7h based device exhibited a PCE of 6.24 %, higher than that of P7g based device (5.69 %).

To maintain high planarity of a Dâ€“A copolymer, another useful strategy is to insert a thiophene spacer to relieve the possible severe steric hindrance between the acceptor and donor units. For example, when benzotriazole is incorporated into the Dâ€“A polymers, severe steric hindrance can be set in by the peripheral H-atoms and the incorporated solubilizing alkyl chains. Compared to P7d, adding thiophene spacer in P7e improves planarity and increases effective conjugation of the polymer main chain, which are translated into broader and stronger absorption, higher hole mobility and better photovoltaic performance.100 The P7e based device demonstrated a PCE of 3.80 %, which is significantly improved in comparison with that of P7d based device (1.64 %).

3. New Dâ€“A architecture

The main-chain donor and side-chain acceptor Dâ€“A copolymers were derived from the extended concept of two-dimensional conjugated polymers proposed by Li. et al.13,14 compared with the well-developed main-chain donorâ€“main-chain acceptor Dâ€“A copolymers (Fig. 9), this type of polymer is expected to have several interesting features, such as isotropic charge transport and inside carrier transfer (from main train to side chain). Since the first successful demonstration of this concept by Huang,101 this family of copolymers has attracted great interests.102â€“108
Fig. 9 Construction of Dâ€“A polymers with conjugated side-chain acceptors.

Zhang et al. pioneered use of the concept of main-chain donor with pedant acceptors in photovoltaic materials and reported alternating copolymers of fluorene and triphenylamine with pedant acceptor groups of mono-cyano (P10a) or di-cyano (p10b).109 Limited by their weak absorptions, only moderate device performances were achieved for them (with PCE of 0.11 % for P10b and 0.22 % for P10a). By adding Ï€-conjugated side chains, Huang et al. developed P10c and P10d. The absorptions were broadly extended to the infra-red region,101 and they exhibit greatly improved photovoltaic properties (PCE as high as 4.74 % for P10c). Later, Zhang et al. conducted detailed studies on such types of polymersvia experiments and molecular modeling,110,111 they identified that the HOMOs for these type of polymers are located on the main chain while the electron density distributions at LUMOs become highly localized near the pedant acceptor groups, and the well-separated distribution of HOMOs and LUMOs indicates that the transition between them can be considered as a charge-transfer transition. Their DFT calculations further indicate that in such molecular architecture, the HOMO and LUMO energy levels are separately controlled by main-chain donor and side-chain acceptor, respectively. These results paved the way for developing this type of Dâ€“A copolymers. The following studies (Fig. 10 and Table 3) mainly concentrated on three topics: (I) replacing fluorene with dibenzosilole (P10h, P10i)102 or carbazole (P10iâ€“P10n)103,105,111 (to promote hole mobility); (II) replacing triphenylamine in the main chain (to improve the planarity of the polymer main chain) (P10jâ€“P10k);110,112 (III) employing stronger pedant acceptor groups (to promote charge transfer).106 Compared to linear Dâ€“A polymers, however, this family of polymers usually exhibits low FF values due to the poor packing of their polymer chains, more investigations are needed to further understand their structure-property relationship and improve their performances.

4. Application of electron-deficient groups in Dâ€“A copolymers

Some low-bandgap Dâ€“A copolymers exhibit broad and strong sunlight absorptions, and could be excellent solar materials. Unfortunately, their photovoltaic performances are self-limited by their high-lying HOMO energy levels (low Voc). Thus finding ways able to tune their HOMO energy levels down without sacrificing their absorption is highly necessary in order to transform them into better materials and further explore their potentials. It is found that wise incorporation of electron-deficient groups in these materials is able to achieve such goals (pull HOMO energy levels down without enlarging optical bandgaps). This section collects some typical examples (Fig. 11 and Table 4) of such chemical modifications. We believe they are helpful in further understanding the Dâ€“A strategy and the general structureâ€“property relationships of Dâ€“A copolymers, and could add valuable suggestions for material design.

You et al. reported the successful application of electron-deficient groups in Dâ€“A conjugated polymers. P11a demonstrated a Voc of 0.81V and a PCE of 5.0 %.116 Replacing the hydrogen atoms of the BT units with fluorine atom91 or replacing the benzene in the benzothiadiazole (BT) unit with pyridine48 in P11a affords P11b and P11c, respectively. Both modifications led to decreased HOMO energy level and improved photovoltaic performances (Jsc = 12.91 mA cmâˆ’2, Voc = 0.91 V, FF = 61.2 % for P11b; Jsc = 12.78 mA cmâˆ’2, Voc = 0.85 V, FF = 58.2 % for P11c). P11d was developed by Qin,113 the well distributed side chains promote planarity of the polymer backbone and hence render close packing of the polymer chains in solid state (in comparison to its polymer analogue P5c). Thus a high FF of 69 % together with a PCE of 5.4 % was achieved for the P11d based device. Under the structure motif, carbazole was replaced by electron-richer benzodithiophene, which affords polymer P11e.114 This produces a lower bandgap of 1.56 eV (a 20 nm red-shift of absorption edge). However, the strong donor elevates the HOMO energy level, resulting in a decreased Voc of 0.76 V and a lowered PCE of 4.02 %. Replacing the BT unit with electron-poorer BO (benzooxadiazole) affords P11f.117 This slight change in chemical structure effectively presses down the HOMO energy level while leaving the optical bandgap almost unaffected. As a result, larger Voc of 0.88 V and higher PCE of 5.7 % were obtained. Notably, the number of electron-deficient groups and their incorporated positions are also important influential factors that should be considered. In P11g, replacing of the thiophene units with furan units weakens the electron-donating ability of the donor part and hence lowers HOMO energy level. This effect produces a higher Voc (0.94 V), but a lower Jsc (induced by the narrowed absorption).

5. Conclusion

By selectively reviewing structureâ€“property relationships of typical photovoltaic Dâ€“A copolymers, we highlight the important rules for designing polymer photovoltaic donor materials with Dâ€“A strategy, particularly the following three: (I) proper combination of Dâ€“A units to achieve low bandgap and deep HOMO energy level. (II) High planarity of the donor/acceptor units and the whole backbone to enhance absorption and hole mobility. (III) Proper incorporation of side chains to offer good solubility without causing serious steric hindrance.

Here we would also like to mention two other factors which are significantly important for device performance and should be considered in material design: absorption coefficient and morphology of phase-separated blend film. Unfortunately, it is not well understood how the two are related to the material structures. Recent work by Bao52,118 and You98 suggested that absorption coefficient can be enhanced by improving the planarity of the polymer backbone. Morphology is generally believed to be closely associated with the chemical nature of p-type polymers and n-type fullerene acceptors: their solubility and mutual affinity, and some secondary interactions, such as van der Waals, electrostatic interactions, as well as Sâ€“N (Sâ€“O) and hydrogen-bonding interactions.119
