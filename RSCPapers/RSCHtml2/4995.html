Microwave-assisted synthesis of a Z-scheme heterojunction Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3 photocatalyst for efficient organic pollutant degradation under visible light

Abstract

A plasmonic Z-scheme photocatalyst comprised of Ag/AgBr-decorated BiOBr/Bi2O3 nanosheets was synthesized successfully using a rapid microwave-assisted one-step approach. The Ag/AgBr particles were generated simultaneously on the surface of the BiOBr/Bi2O3 nanosheets. This construction resulted in the formation of multiplex phenomena, including the surface plasmon resonance effect of Ag particles, the photosensitive role of AgBr in enhancing visible light utilization, and efficient charge transfer processes in the quaternary heterojunction photocatalyst. The as-prepared catalyst exhibited remarkable photocatalytic activity toward the destruction of tetracycline hydrochloride (TCH) (93.7%), methyl orange (MO) (96.6%), and rhodamine B (RhB) (100%) under visible light. The catalyst exhibited high photocatalytic stability under visible light irradiation, retaining 97% of its activity after nine cycles of MO degradation. This was attributed to the multiple synergistic mechanisms of the components following the Z-scheme mechanism, which facilitated rapid charge transfer and restricted the decomposition of AgBr to achieve high efficiency and stable catalysts.


Environmental significance

We have solved the environmental wastewater treatment problem economically using an efficient photocatalyst under visible light. We have synthesized a novel Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3 Z-scheme photocatalyst with the help of CTAB using a microwave-assisted one-step method under mild conditions. This catalyst exhibited exceptional photodegradation efficiency for organic pollutants, such as rhodamine B (100%) and methyl orange (97%), under visible light with small loading (0.3 g L1) and excellent stability (97% after nine cycles). The degradation efficiency with 0.5 g L1 loading reached 98% under solar light for difficult-to-degrade tetracycline. We have studied photodegradation theoretically using the TRPL and trapping experiments to confirm the Z-scheme and the predominant role of singlet oxygen in the degradation.

1. Introduction

Economic growth with uncontrolled industrial activity has imposed significant challenges to human life, especially water pollution. To overcome this environmental problem, photocatalysis is one of the best approaches for water treatment among various water treatment techniques available, such as adsorption, chemical techniques, and electrochemical technologies.1,2 The photoinduced advanced oxidation process is a green and relatively low-cost wastewater treatment owing to the utilization of abundant solar radiation.2 Titanium dioxide (TiO2) is a promising photocatalyst because of its nontoxicity, low cost, superior redox ability, excellent photocatalytic activity, and high chemical and biological photostability.3 On the other hand, TiO2 only absorbs the UV range of solar light because of its large bandgap (3.23.3 eV), harvesting less than 5% of solar light,2,4 and thus leading to the restriction of photo-efficiency. In addition, it has a high rate of photoinduced electronhole recombination. Therefore, considerable research has been conducted to find alternatives to utilize light effectively.

Recently, bismuth oxyhalides (BiOX (X = I, Cl, Br, F)) have attracted much attention in the photocatalysis field due to their high chemical stability, uniquely layered structures, and highly favorable photocatalytic performance under UV-vis irradiation.5 The BiOX family has a tetragonal matlockite-type structure with a unique layer structure of [Bi2O22+] slabs overlapped by halogen atoms.3 As one member of the BiOX family, BiOBr has been studied intensively because of its low bandgap (2.32.9 eV),6 superior photocatalytic activity and stability,7 and unique layered crystal structure.8 Although this layer structure is favorable for separating the electronhole pairs effectively because of the induced dipole,7 its photocatalytic performance is still hindered by the low light absorption ability.6

Bismuth oxide (Bi2O3) has a low bandgap (2.12.8 eV) and remarkable properties, such as high refractive index, low cost, visible-light activity, higher oxidation power of the holes,9 non-toxicity, and non-carcinogenicity.2 With these features, Bi2O3 is considered a potential photocatalyst. Despite these advantages, its photocatalytic activity differs significantly according to the crystal structures.10 Generally, water purification using a Bi2O3 photocatalyst is a poor choice because of the high rate of photoinduced electronhole pair recombination and photocorrosion.9 Therefore, many strategies have been designed to exploit its properties and enhance its photocatalytic performance effectively.2,4,9

Noble metallic Ag nanoparticles can act as a strong absorber for harvesting visible light owing to their surface plasmon resonance (SPR),6,11 whereas silver bromide (AgBr) plays an essential role as a light-sensitive material and a plasmonic photocatalyst.9 On the other hand, AgBr as a single-phase photocatalyst shows optical instability because it can partially decompose to Ag NPs under visible light irradiation.11 Thus, the coupling of Ag/AgBr would result in high photocatalytic activity and good stability under visible light.11,12 Nevertheless, the photocatalytic performance of the plasmonic Ag/AgBr composites could be hampered by their micrometer-scale particle sizes resulting in low surface area and the rapid recombination of electrons and holes.12

Several strategies for developing visible-light responsive photocatalysts have been studied widely to enhance their visible light-harvesting capability, photocatalytic activity, and photostability. For example, Yang et al. fabricated an AgBr/Bi2O3 catalyst using a two-step hydrothermal method to degrade methyl orange (MO).13 Li et al. prepared Ag-doped Bi2O3 nanosheets via co-precipitation (90 C, 24 h) and calcination (500 C, 4 h) routes for the treatment of MO.14 Dong et al. fabricated AgBrAgBiOBr composites by a hydrothermal route (160 C, 16 h) and calcination (450 C, 4 h) for the removal of MO.6 Yan et al. used a solvothermal approach (160 C, 10 h) to obtain Ag/AgBr/BiOBr hollow hierarchical microspheres for rhodamine B (RhB) degradation.15 Zhao et al. successfully prepared an Ag/AgCl/BiOCl nanocomposite by a solvothermal reaction (150 C, 24 h) for RhB and tetracycline hydrochloride (TCH) removal.16 Qiu et al. reported that Z-scheme SnS2/BiOBr photocatalysts synthesized using a hydrothermal method (180 C, 10 h) showed better activity in RhB decomposition than pure SnS2 or BiOBr.17 A Z-scheme Ag/AgBr/Bi4O5Br2 photocatalyst was also synthesized using a hydrothermal method (160 C for 12 h) to decompose acid orange II, 4-tert-butylphenol, and aniline.18 Several studies have been conducted to improve the photocatalytic activity of catalysts. Nevertheless, these fabrication routes posed obstacles for practical applications related to their high-energy consumption, lengthy production time, complicated synthesis procedures, and environmental problems from toxic solvents. Among the construction designs of photocatalysts, Z-scheme photocatalytic construction with the introduction of a solid-state electron mediator (noble metal components: Pt, Au, and Ag) is considered a practical design to improve the visible-light absorption ability, effective electronhole separation, and accelerated charge transfer at the interface.19

Heating with microwave has been employed widely in materials synthesis as an alternative to conventional heating because it is an efficient, rapid, and controllable method.20,21 Moreover, microwave heating is dependent on the dielectric properties of solvents. The use of water as a solvent in the reaction has several advantages, such as improved reactivity and selectivity, mild reaction conditions, and simplified workflow. On the other hand, using other solvents leads to toxicity risks, environmental pollution, and waste treatment problems.20 In addition, microwave-assisted synthesis under atmospheric pressure was successfully carried out in the previous work.22

Therefore, a Z-scheme quaternary heterojunction photocatalyst Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3 was prepared in this study using a low-temperature rapid microwave-assisted one-step hydrothermal method. Several characterization methods were employed to confirm the formation of the heterojunction catalysts. Different types of light sources were used to examine the photocatalytic activities of the as-synthesized catalyst under visible light, including a halogen lamp (in the overhead projector (OHP)), light-emitting diodes (LEDs), and a xenon lamp in a solar simulator. Three hard-to-destroy pollutants such as TCH, MO, and RhB were tested. Additionally, the excellent photocatalytic activity and the synergistic effects of the four components of the prepared catalyst were studied. The mechanism of pollutant degradation under visible light has also been discussed.

2. Experimental

2.1 Materials

Bismuth(III) nitrate pentahydrate (Bi(NO3)35H2O, 98%, Sigma Aldrich), hexadecyltrimethylammonium bromide ([(C16H33)N(CH3)3]Br, CTAB, 98%, Alfa Aesar), nitric acid (HNO3 70%, Daejung Chemicals), and silver nitrate (AgNO3, 99.8%, Daejung Chemicals) were used to synthesize the photocatalyst. RhB (90%, Sigma Aldrich), MO (Junsei Chemicals), and TCH (C22H24N2O8HCl, 96%, Alfa Aesar) were used as the target organic pollutants without further purification. Sodium hydrogen carbonate (NaHCO3, >99%, Daejung Chemicals), tert-butyl alcohol (TBA, 99.5%, Daejung Chemicals), p-benzoquinone (p-BZQ, 98%, Sigma Aldrich), and L-histidine (99%, Sigma Aldrich) were used as received.

2.2 Synthesis of Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3

The Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3 nanocomposite was synthesized using a microwave-assisted one-step approach. Typically, 0.075 g of CTAB was dissolved in 50 ml of DI water to obtain a transparent solution. Subsequently, 100 l of 70% HNO3 was added to the solution. Then, 0.2 g of Bi(NO3)35H2O was sonicated in the above solution in a sonication bath for 5 min at room temperature to obtain a milky suspension. The solutions of AgNO3 (0.1 M) were prepared separately. A particular volume of 0.1 M AgNO3 solution was dropped into the milky mixture and stirred for 30 min. The mixture was poured into a microwave reactor. The reaction was conducted at 180 W at 90 C for 30 min with constant stirring (2.45 GHz microwave, CEM, Discover System) (Scheme 1). After rapid cooling to room temperature, the final product was filtered and washed several times with DI water and dried overnight in a vacuum oven at 40 C. The experiments were carried out with different amounts of 0.1 M AgNO3 solutions (100, 300, 500, 700, and 1000 l denoted as S1, S3, S5, S7, and S10, respectively). A BiOBr/Bi2O3 sample was synthesized using the same procedure without adding the AgNO3 solution for comparison purposes. A pure AgBr sample was synthesized by a reaction between 0.1 M AgNO3 and KBr (molar ratio of 1:1) at room temperature.

2.3 Characterization

The crystallinity of the samples was analyzed by powder X-ray diffraction (XRD, PANalytical, X'Pert-PRO MPD) using Cu K radiation ( = 0.154 nm). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Hitachi, S-4800) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM, Philips, CM-200) were used to observe the morphology of the samples. The high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) images were collected using a TITAN G2 ChemiSTEM Cs probe electron microscope. The elemental composition and chemical state were analyzed by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Kratos, AXIS Nova) using monochromatized Al K radiation. A HORIBA scientific photoluminescence spectrometer (PL, HORIBA, iHR550) with a laser wavelength excitation of 325 nm was used to record the photoluminescence (PL) spectra.

The UV-vis diffuse reflectance spectra and UV-vis spectra of the samples were collected using a Varian Cary 5000 UV-vis-NIR spectrophotometer. Time-resolved photoluminescence (TRPL) was carried out at room temperature by time-resolved confocal fluorescence microscopy (MicroTime-200, Picoquant) with a pulsed single-mode diode laser (375 nm with a pulse width of 30 ps and an average power of approximately 5 W) as the excitation source. The total organic carbon (TOC) was measured using a TOC analyzer (multi N/C 2100S, Analytic Jena). The electron spin resonance (ESR) was also measured using an ESR spectrometer (EMX plus-9.5/2.7, Bruker). An exponential fit for the emission decays was accomplished using Symphotime-64 software (ver. 2.2).

In a typical run, a certain photocatalyst was added to 50 ml of a 10 mg L1 aqueous pollutant solution, resulting in a photocatalyst loading of 0.5 g L1. The reaction mixture was stirred in the dark chamber for 30 min to ensure the adsorptiondesorption equilibrium before irradiation. 2.5 Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements and photocurrent response measurement

EIS of samples was performed using an Autolab PGSTAT 302 N (Metrohm) instrument equipped with a conventional three-electrode cell system. The Ag/AgCl electrode, Pt electrode, and working electrode (coated with the as-prepared catalyst) were set up in a quartz cell in the presence of 0.5 M Na2SO4 electrolyte. The working electrode was prepared using the drop-casting technique by depositing the active material on fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) glass (1 cm 1 cm active area). Briefly, 2 mg of the as-prepared catalyst was mixed with 1 ml of ethanol and 10 l of Nafion to obtain a slurry. Subsequently, 15 l of this mixture was drop-cast onto an FTO glass substrate and dried in a vacuum oven for 12 h. The EIS spectra of the samples were recorded over the frequency range, 0.01 to 105 Hz.

The photocurrent response of samples was also measured at 0.5 V using the same instrument. The photocurrent response was measured under several on/off irradiation cycles for 20 seconds using a solar simulator as the light source. The working electrodes had the same illuminated area of 1 cm 1 cm.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Characterization

XRD characterized the phase of the as-prepared samples. As shown in Fig. 1, the XRD pattern of Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3 shows peaks for the BiOBr, Bi2O3, AgBr, and Ag components in the nanocomposites. The BiOBr component showed characteristic peaks at 11.0, 25.2, 32.1, 39.4, 46.4, 50.7, 57.3, and 77.0 2 (JCPDS #01-1004). The peaks at 25.2, 32.1, 46.4, and 57.3 2 corresponding to the (011), (110), (020), and (212) planes confirmed the presence of the tetragonal structure of BiOBr (Fig. 1a).8,23,24 XRD peaks also confirmed the presence of AgBr at 26.7, 31.0, 44.3, 55.1, and 73.3 2 corresponding to the (111), (200), (220), (222), and (420) planes, respectively. These peaks were indexed to the JCPDS #06-0438 profile, which belongs to the cubic structure of AgBr (Fig. 1a and c). Moreover, the nanocomposite showed Ag peaks with weak signals at 38.0 and 64.7 2 for the (111) and (220) planes (JCPDS #01-1164) and cubic phase Bi2O3 at 32.3, 67.7, and 77.0 2 corresponding to the (200), (400), and (420) planes (JCPDS #52-1007), respectively (Fig. 1a). Because of the strong-acid weak-base salt, the Bi(NO3)35H2O reagent would be hydrolyzed to BiONO3 in the acidic media (eqn (2)). The BiONO3 species were then hydrolyzed to BiO+ according to eqn (3). In this reaction, CTAB acts as a bromide source for the formation of BiOBr and AgBr. As a result, the generated BiO+ species reacted rapidly with Br to form BiOBr with a nanosheet structure (eqn (4)). The same reaction would occur with Ag+ to produce AgBr (eqn (5) and (6)). CTAB is also a reducing agent that can produce electrons under microwave conditions to form Ag species on the surface of the BiOBr nanosheets (eqn (7)). Ultimately, while contributing to the production of BiOBr, BiONO3 would participate simultaneously in two reactions to form Bi2O3 (eqn (8) and (9)). The formation of Ag and Bi2O3 cannot be avoided under the microwave environment for the short-time reaction. On the other hand, Ag and Bi2O3 contributed to the separation of electronhole pairs in the photocatalytic system, resulting in excellent photocatalytic activity under visible light.

3.2 Opto-electrochemical properties

The light absorption property is one of the critical properties of a photocatalyst because it affects photocatalytic performance. Therefore, the light absorption of the as-prepared samples was measured using a UV-vis-NIR spectrophotometer in the range of 200 to 800 nm, as shown in Fig. 5a. The Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3 sample recorded a red-shift in the absorption edge and strong absorbance at a wide range of wavelengths from 200 to 800 nm compared to the BiOBr and BiOBr/Bi2O3 samples. Ag and AgBr enhanced the light absorption ability of the Ag-containing-catalyst due to the contribution from the SPR phenomena of Ag and the visible-light absorption of AgBr.11,13,33 This behavior would allow the composite to harvest light effectively over broad wavelengths for photocatalytic reactions. Furthermore, the BiOBr/Bi2O3 sample showed no response to visible light due to the effect of the Bi2O3 content,13 but its light absorption was more substantial at wavelengths below 400 nm than BiOBr. Although the AgBr sample exhibited the most prominent absorbance from 400 to 800 nm, its photocatalytic activity during the degradation process was poor, corroborated by the ineffective separation of the photogenerated electronhole pairs. The photoluminescence (PL) spectrum at a laser wavelength excitation of 325 nm is an effective technique for evaluating the rate of photogenerated electronhole pair recombination in nanocomposites.16 Basically, an increase in the PL intensity represents the higher recombination rate of charge carriers.1Fig. 5c shows the room-temperature PL spectra of the as-prepared samples. The emission intensity of the AgBr sample showed the highest intensity compared to the other samples, indicating the high recombination rate of electrons and holes. While the BiOBr/Bi2O3 sample showed a weaker emission intensity than the BiOBr sample, the co-existence of Ag and AgBr on the BiOBr/Bi2O3 produced the lowest emission intensity. This behavior was attributed to the effective separation of charge carriers by incorporation of Ag and AgBr particles on the surface of BiOBr/Bi2O3. The Ag particles generate electrons due to the SPR effect and act as a solid-state electron mediator in the catalytic system,16,19 providing many electrons and facilitating rapid electron transfer between BiOBr/Bi2O3 and AgBr. This rapid migration of charges significantly decreases the recombination rate of electronhole pairs, which further results in excellent photocatalytic activity under visible light irradiation.

EIS and photocurrent response measurements were implemented to prove the separation and transport of electrons and holes and provide a better understanding of the transport of charge carriers. In the Nyquist plots, the semicircle at the high frequency represents the interfacial charge transfer property at the electrode/electrolyte interface and is related to the charge transfer resistance.34 The smaller arches indicate lower charge transfer resistance. The relative arch sizes of the as-prepared samples were identified in the following order (Fig. 5d): AgBr > BiOBr > BiOBr/Bi2O3 > Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3, verifying that the constructed heterojunctions of Ag, AgBr, and BiOBr/Bi2O3 have low charge transfer resistance due to the rapid charge transport and effective charge separation at the interface of the catalyst.34,35 These results confirm that many photogenerated electrons and holes with longer lifetimes were photogenerated. This formation initiated the generation of oxidizing agents for the photocatalytic reactions that degraded the contaminants on the catalyst surface. The photocurrent response measurement was examined at a fixed bias of 0.5 V vs. Ag/AgCl with successive 20 s on/off cycles of solar light irradiation to acquire more information on the kinetic process of separation and transportability of these photoinduced electronhole pairs. The photo-excited electrons generated from semiconductors (BiOBr, Bi2O3, and AgBr) under solar light irradiation would implement electron migration to the FTO substrate to produce photocurrents.1,36 A higher generated photocurrent density represents faster charge transport and higher separation efficiency of photoinduced electronhole pairs. The AgBr sample showed the lowest photocurrent density of 0.71 A cm2 (Fig. 5e). Although AgBr showed outstanding absorbance in the visible range, the rapid recombination of electrons and holes in the AgBr catalyst resulted in a weak photocurrent response, leading to poor photocatalytic activity. Furthermore, the photocurrent densities of the BiOBr and BiOBr/Bi2O3 samples were 1.30 and 1.81 A cm2, respectively, showing the interaction between the two components (BiOBr and Bi2O3) resulted in better charge carrier separation than BiOBr.1,3,34 The strong charge separation ability in the Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3 sample was recorded with the highest photocurrent density of 2.10 A cm2. Therefore, this proves that the synergistic interaction among the components contributed considerably to the high separation efficiency and longer lifetime of the photoinduced electrons and holes. These results show that integration between Ag, AgBr, and BiOBr/Bi2O3 formed the multichannel transport for photoinduced electrons and holes, resulting in highly efficient photocatalytic activity.

3.4 Photocatalytic reaction mechanism

The main oxidizing agents involved in the photodegradation process and the catalytic reaction mechanism were investigated by radical-trapping experiments using various scavenging reagents. The oxidizing species were identified from the photocatalytic reactions under identical conditions in the presence of sodium hydrogen carbonate (NaHCO3), tert-butyl alcohol (TBA), p-benzoquinone (p-BZQ), and L-histidine as scavengers for the holes (hVB+), hydroxyl radicals (OH), superoxide radicals () and singlet oxygen (1O2), respectively.1,28,45,46

The trapping experiments disclosed the roles of each oxidizing agent (hVB+, OH, , and 1O2) in the photocatalytic degradation (Fig. 10a), evidenced by lower degradation efficiency with the introduction of scavengers in the reactions. In particular, a significant decline in RhB degradation efficiency to approximately 7.9, 35.6, and 69.5% was observed after 60 min under OHP light irradiation after adding L-histidine, NaHCO3, and p-BZQ, respectively. In contrast, the degradation efficiency was 99.1% within 30 min, and complete decomposition occurred after 60 min with the addition of TBA, which was similar to that observed in the absence of scavengers (control experiment). The results showed that singlet oxygen (1O2) plays a significant role as a dominating oxidizing agent while OH species do not contribute to the photocatalytic decomposition in this system. On the other hand, hVB+ and radicals contribute to pollutant degradation.

Scheme 2 presents the proposed Z-scheme mechanism of the electron transfer process occurring on the catalyst surface under visible light excitation, which explains the efficient charge carrier separation of the catalytic system. Two typical charge transfer processes, including band-to-band transfer between BiOBr and Bi2O3 and the Z-scheme mechanism transfer between Bi2O3 and AgBr with Ag as an electron mediator, might co-exist. When the catalyst was exposed to light, BiOBr, Bi2O3, and AgBr were excited to generate photoinduced electrons and holes (eqn (13)(15)). These photogenerated electrons and holes tend to recombine to reach a stable energy state. On the other hand, the charge carrier separation resulting from the two charge transfer processes hindered the recombination and accelerated charge transfer on the nanocomposite surface.

Photogenerated electrons at the CB of BiOBr (ECB = +0.31 eV vs. NHE)17 easily transferred to the CB of Bi2O3 (ECB = +0.34 eV vs. NHE)47 following the band-to-band transfer mechanism while electrons in the CB of Bi2O3 and electrons from plasmon-excited Ag particles (eqn (16)) conducted charge transfer to the VB of AgBr (EVB = +1.09 eV vs. NHE) through the Ag bridge via the Z-scheme transfer mechanism, as illustrated in Scheme 2. The Ag particles served as electron mediators, accelerating charge transfer and capturing electrons at the CB of BiOBr and Bi2O3. Therefore, the corrosion of Ag is prevented, and the stability of the catalyst system is maintained.12 The trapping experiments showed that the photoinduced electrons in the CB of AgBr (ECB = 1.25 eV vs. NHE) could reduce the O2 dissolved in the aqueous solution to generate (eqn (17)) because it is more negative than the standard redox potential of O2/ (E (O2/) = 0.33 eV vs. NHE).27,48 On the other hand, the electrons accumulated in the CB of BiOBr and Bi2O3 would implement charge migration from the CB of BiOBr to the CB of Bi2O3 to prevent electronhole recombination. In contrast, the holes in the VB of BiOBr (EVB = +3.03 eV vs. NHE)17 and VB of Bi2O3 (EVB = +3.14 eV vs. NHE)47 might oxidize H2O/OH to form OH radicals (eqn (18) and (19)) because the standard redox potentials of OH/OH and H2O/OH are +2.40 eV vs. NHE and +2.72 eV vs. NHE, respectively,27 and they participate simultaneously in oxidizing the pollutant molecule. The results showed that OH radicals have no apparent influence on the degradation process because these generated OH radicals were consumed by a reaction with to produce singlet oxygen 1O2 (eqn (20)).42 The oxidizing agent of partially participated in a series of reactions (eqn (21)(23)) to generate singlet oxygen 1O2. Furthermore, the holes on the VB of AgBr could not oxidize H2O to generate OH radicals. These main oxidative substances, including 1O2, hVB+, and , were involved in the photocatalytic decomposition of the organic pollutants to CO2, H2O, and intermediates (eqn (24)),42 in which 1O2 was the predominant oxidizing agent in this photocatalytic system. The introduction of Ag in the photocatalytic system promoted the rapid migration of charges in the catalyst system owing to its excellent conductive properties and enhanced visible-light response due to SPR contribution,49 generating more photons absorbed by the catalyst. Therefore, more photogenerated electrons and holes are produced for the degradation of pollutants.15

The Z-scheme mechanism and band-to-band charge transfer were facilitated due to the conjugation of semiconductors (AgBr, BiOBr, and Bi2O3) with Ag species (a sink for photoinduced charges49 and a Z-scheme bridge15). The enhanced charge transfer and rapid electronhole separation at the semiconductor's interfaces prolonged the lifetime of the charges. This efficient separation provided many free electrons and holes on the catalyst surface to generate highly oxidizing radicals for the photodegradation of contaminants.

In practical applications, the photostability of the catalyst plays a vital role in catalyst selection for wastewater treatment.50 The morphology and phase components before and after recycling were also examined (Fig. S8). The chemical state of the catalysts was measured by XPS (Fig. S9) for fresh and 9-cycle-used Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3 catalysts. These data showed no noticeable differences in the crystalline structure and the binding energy between the fresh and used catalysts. The XPS data of the fresh and used samples showed that the catalyst kept all elements (Ag, Bi, Br, and O) at the same proportion after nine cycles (Fig. S9). The shifts of Ag 3d, Bi 4f, and Br 3d peaks of the used catalyst to the higher binding energy were due to the interaction between the intermediate products and catalyst. The intensity of the oxygen anion peak increased after use because of the water absorption. These data confirm the high stability and long cycle life of the Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3 catalyst. These results highlight the excellent stability of the catalyst under OHP light because of the presence of metallic Ag. Moreover, the rapid charge transfer of the catalyst system inhibited the decomposition of AgBr under visible light.15,16,49 The high degradation efficiency and good photostability of the catalyst highlight promising applications of Z-scheme-based photocatalysts in wastewater treatment.

4. Conclusions

A highly efficient Ag/AgBr@BiOBr/Bi2O3 photocatalyst was synthesized using a rapid one-step microwave approach. The conjugation among the semiconductors BiOBr, Bi2O3, and AgBr with the electron mediator Ag enhanced the light absorption due to the SPR effect. This also facilitated rapid charge transfer and efficient separation of electrons and holes via two charge transfer processes: band-to-band transfer and Z-scheme mechanism. The catalyst showed excellent photocatalytic performance toward the degradation of various pollutants (TCH (93.7%), MO (96.6%), and RhB (100%)) under visible light irradiation (400 W OHP light, pollutant concentration of 10 mg L1, catalyst loading of 0.3 g L1, 60 min for reaction time). The radical trapping experiment confirmed that 1O2, hVB+, and species were involved in the photocatalytic reactions, in which 1O2 played a predominant role in the degradation process. The excellent photostability of the catalyst (97% efficiency removal after nine cycles) highlighted its potential applications in industry. The Z-scheme mechanism has advantages in obtaining high degradation efficiency and good catalyst stability. This catalyst with the Z-scheme mechanism could be a promising photocatalyst to address various environmental pollution issues using diverse visible light sources and has potential for designing materials in the future.
