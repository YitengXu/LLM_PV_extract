Aqueous redox flow batteries: How ‘green’ are the redox active materials?

Zachary Deller , Lathe A. Jones and Subashani Maniam *
School of Science, STEM College, RMIT University, 124 La Trobe Street, Melbourne, VIC 3001, Australia. E-mail: subashani.maniam@rmit.edu.au

Received 16th April 2021 , Accepted 18th June 2021

First published on 18th June 2021

Abstract

Energy storage using aqueous organic redox flow batteries (ORFBs) is gaining momentum in recent years parallel with the renewable energy industry, principally to store energy from solar, wind and hydro generation. The safety and environmental effects of active materials utilised in aqueous ORFB systems are vital to stay true to the ultimate aim of producing a ‘green’ technology. In this review, we evaluate the ‘greenness’ of the synthetic methodology employed to prepare the anolyte or catholyte active materials which have been developed for aqueous ORBFs using the 12 principles of green chemistry. Some of the most common synthetic strategies that will be discussed involve acid catalysis, catalyst free procedures, and oxidative polymerisation. Additionally, the ‘greenness’ of several key commercial materials with limited synthetic procedures published will be considered.

1. Introduction

The energy sector has continued to evolve rapidly over the last 50 years as consumer needs have increased significantly.1 As the need for electricity increased, more fossil fuel plants were brought online to meet the demand, and the relative percentages of renewables and energy storage decreased.1 While fossil fuel energy sources, and even hydro and geothermal sources can provide stable energy production, renewable energy production from solar, tidal and wind sources are inconsistent in their energy production.2 They can produce excess energy at some times, and then none at others.3

To combat this, energy storage systems have been employed to reduce total load on the grid when more energy is being produced than is needed and then to supplement energy release when the demand is high.4–6 A range of alternative energy storage techniques have been used on and off grid, such as thermal, mechanical, chemical and electrochemical.5 Among these systems, electrochemical, specifically battery technology, is of much interest largely due to ease of use and flexibility when compared to other prevalent methods such as pumped hydroelectric, compressed air and thermal energy storage systems, which can require large infrastructure to be constructed.2,5

These systems primarily include lead acid, lithium ion and redox flow batteries (RFB).7 The first of these, the lead acid battery, has seen large design improvements and wide application in many industries since its inception over 150 years ago.8 Historically there have been many lead-acid batteries used to help provide consistent energy supply.9 However, lead-acid batteries are not able to meet the needs of the energy sector due to low energy densities and insufficient recharge cycles, leading to a short service life.8 Lithium ion batteries are a more recent product which can achieve significantly higher energy densities and cycles than lead-acid. While these batteries are prolific in small electronics, they are subject to economic constraints due to manufacturing costs and specifcations.9 An alternative to these two systems is RFBs. This system was first conceptualized in the late 1970′s10 while the first system was developed in 1981 at the NASA-Lewis Research Centre.10,11 Flow batteries differ somewhat from these other forms of batteries in that they consist of two redox solutions, the catholyte and the analyte solutions, which are stored in separate tanks that are pumped into a cell that contains an ion exchange membrane, which allows the movement of the balancing electrolyte but not the active species (Fig. 1).4


Fig. 1 A schematic representation of redox flow battery.

The RFB developed by NASA-Lewis Research Centre was based on an iron catholyte and a chromium anolyte system.11 During discharge, iron is reduced (Fe3+ to Fe2+) at the positive electrode and chromium is oxidized (Cr2+ to Cr3+) at the negative electrode, leading to electron flow through the circuit. Some of the early RFBs used Zn/Cl and Zn/Br systems, however the only significant commercially established RFB has been vanadium-based. This system was first developed in 19874 and since then has seen further research and improvements.12,13 As research in this area increases, the possibility of organic redox flow batteries (ORFB) using organic- or aqueous-based electrolyte has emerged as an exciting opportunity to expand upon and increase commercial viability.14–17 The operation of the systems is principally the same as seen in the inorganic based systems, however the metal species is replaced by redox active organic compounds.18 ORFBs seek to address some of the drawbacks of current inorganic systems, such as the cost, stability and resource limited nature of active materials such as vanadium19 compared to organic compounds that could be produced from renewable feedstocks.20 Additionally, formation of complexes and dendritic structures of the metal species reduce the capacity of these systems.19 The stability of the active species is still a concern in ORFBs, however the highly customizable nature of organic substances allows more options to successfully managing this problem.18,21–23 In summary, ORFBs can be based on cost-effective, accessible active species and materials, with customization and higher stability possible when compared to metal-based systems by careful design and use of appropriate synthetic procedures.

A consideration when developing ORFBs is the safety and potential environmental impact of the active material. As more research goes toward the development of storage systems, it can be expected that a deeper life-cycle analysis of the environmental impacts will be sought by industry and governments.24 This makes it important to improve chemical processes and applications by bringing them in line with the 12 principles of green chemistry, even at the research and development stages. The 12 green principles of chemistry (Fig. 2 and Table 1), first introduced formally in 1998,25 provide a framework for how chemical processes and substance can be formulated to be safe, sustainable and environmentally friendly. This method also allows greenness evaluation to be done without conducting the experiment if enough details of the substance and protocol are obtained. The construction of the metric begins with the evaluation of a reaction based on each principle in the scale of 1 (minimum) to 3 (maximum) of greenness. This requires the examination of the reaction protocol and making an inventory of all the substances used such as feedstock, products, by-products, auxiliaries including catalyst, solvents, additives etc. and waste. Then, the risks to human, environment and the potential hazard of each substance are analysed. Additionally, information of renewable substance and the degradation products are also collected. Each substance is then classified in a scale of 1 to 3 based on the 12 principles of green chemistry. Methods and substances are commonly referred to as ‘green/greener’ when they meet these 12 principles with each category having a score of 3 whereas when the greenness is minimum, all the scores will be 1 (Table 1). The data can also be visually presented as a radar graph with a fuller version displaying a higher degree of greenness.

Organic active species for applications in ORFBs should seek to adhere to these principles given the large push for these systems in renewable energy sectors. It can be expected that these systems will be heavily scrutinised in relation to their environmental and human health impacts in a similar way as consumer batteries and solar panels have been analysed for their impact.24,27 Here, we have reviewed publications on aqueous ORFBs in the last 10 years, focusing on the core synthesis of the active materials, evaluating the ‘greenness’ of the methodologies using the 12 principles of green chemistry. Some specific reviews on solar RFBs,28 the role of supporting electrolytes,29 parameters influencing RFBs30 and a general summary on next generation of aqueous flow batteries31 have been previously reviewed by others. We will begin this review by evaluating the synthetic strategies to prepare active materials using acid catalysts, followed by catalyst free procedures, and oxidative polymerisation and commercially sourced materials, then finally provide a summary and outlook on this subject.

2. Synthesis strategies of redox active materials

2.1 Catalysts

Utilising catalysts in chemical reactions is seen as a green approach, as the reaction will proceed quickly, often at a lower temperature, increasing the energy efficiency of the reaction.32,33 Acid catalysts in particular play an important role in many organic reactions34–37 and this methodology has been applied in preparing redox active materials for RFBs. There are a variety of acids that can be used, comprising oxides and halides largely, and including different structural forms such as zeolites and ligands.4,5 Some of these are relatively benign while others are less so. A large area of research focuses on developing or improving the green chemistry aspects of these acids by using more abundant precursors, improving recyclability and the safety of the acid used.38,39

Hofmann et al.40 synthesised quinones using a Lewis acid. These included 2,3-diaza-9,10-anthracenedione (DAD), and its two derivatives, 2,3-diaza-5,8-dihydroxy-anthracene-9,10-dione (DADdi(OH)), and 2,3-diaza-6-methoxy-anthracene-9,10-dione (DAD(MeO)) (Scheme 1). The three compounds were synthesised using the same method. Briefly, 1,2,4,5-tetrazine and the accompanying naphthoquinone were added to a nitrogen filled flask along with the bidentate Lewis acid, 5,10-dimethyl-5,10-dihydroboranthrene and benzotrifluoride solvent. The reaction mixture was left at 110 °C overnight, solvents were removed under vacuum with a nitrogen filled cooling trap. DAD and DAD(MeO) were purified by via column chromatography using DCM/MeOH as the eluent. The synthesis for these compounds reports a yield of 76% for DAD and 18% for DADdi(OH), however for DAD(MeO) no yield was reported. The addition of the diaza group, along with functionalisation of the aromatic regions has shown to improve the energy density and stability of the molecules. The research provides a greater understanding of the customisability of quinones for use in aqueous redox flow batteries as a catholyte active material. Considering the green principles relative to the reaction schemes above, the one pot synthesis of the reaction eliminates the need for derivatization steps. Additionally, the high yields improve the atom economy of the reaction providing a chemically efficient reaction. The areas that warrant consideration under the green principles are the use of benzotrifluoride as a solvent and the Lewis acid that is used as a catalyst in this reaction. Both increase safety risks, particularly if the active materials are prepared in large quantities. The synthesised compounds can also pose environmental risks if not contained properly and will require treatment for disposal. Heating of the reaction up to 100 °C is energy intensive, reducing the overall energy efficiency of the reaction. Thus, these series of reactions score (1) for all the green chemistry principles except P2 and P8, both scoring (2), as shown in the radar graph in Scheme 1.

Further studies by Hofmann et al.41 involved the synthesis of hydroquinone, 5,8-dihydroxy-2,3-phthalazine (DHP), along with two of its derivatives, 5,8-dihydroxy-6-methoxy-2,3-phthalazine (DHP(MeO)) and 5,8-dihydroxy-6,7-dimethyl-2,3-phthalazine (DHP(Me)2) (Scheme 2), which were carried out using the similar method as mentioned above. The products were obtained using 1,2,4,5-tetrazine and benzoquinone, 2,3-dimethylbenzoquinone, or 2-methoxy-1,4-benzoquinone, in the presence of the bidentate Lewis acid. These were added to a nitrogen filled Schlenk flask, after which the solvent benzotrifluoride was added. This mixture was stirred at 55 °C, then the temperature was increased to 110 °C and left overnight. The solvent and tetrazine were removed under vacuum and the resulting solids were dissolved in 1:1 DCM/MeOH and purified using column chromatography. This method provided excellent yields for DHP (94%) and DHP(Me)2 (88%), while DHP(MeO) gave a poor yield of 23%. The dihydroxyphthalazine derivatives presented here did show increased performance as catholyte active materials in aqueous redox flow batteries relative to the base dihydroxyphthalazine structure. The authors sought derivatives that showed increased performance by the addition of functional groups that improved their chemical stability, redox potential and water solubility. Therefore, these reactions score (1) for all the green principles except for P9 with a score of (2) as displayed in the radar graph in Scheme 2.

Wang et al.42 synthesised naphthoquinone derivative, 2-hydroxy-3-carboxy-1,4-naphthoquinone (2,3-HCNQ) (Scheme 3), which showed greatly improved solubility in alkaline solutions, as well as further improved performance characteristics in redox kinetics and stability metrics. The product 2,3-HCNQ was synthesised by dissolving ethyl benzoylacetate and AlCl3 in dry nitromethane under nitrogen. After stirring, a mixture of oxalyl chloride in dry nitromethane was added dropwise. The resulting mixture was heated to 80 °C for 3 hours. Once cooled to room temperature an aqueous solution of oxalic acid was added. The resulting mixture was extracted with ethyl acetate, this extract was then treated with aqueous NaCl solution before being dried with anhydrous sodium sulfate. The ethyl acetate was distilled off and the residue was chromatographed to obtain the precursor, 3-hydroxy-1,4-dioxo-1,4-dihydronaphthalene-2-carboxylate. A solution of the precursor in aqueous KOH was left to stir overnight at 60 °C. Once cooled the mixture was acidified with 1 M HCl, the resulting precipitate was collected and filtered while washing with cold water. The collected product was dried to obtain 2,3-HCNQ in 70% yield. Atom economy and no derivatisation steps score good when considering reagent efficiency in this reaction. The energy efficiency scores well due to moderately low reaction temperature. The solvent and reagent choice lead to lower ratings in safety due to hazard ratings. Thus, a score of (3) is given for P2 and P8, however this reaction scores (2) for P6 and P9, and all the other principles score only (1), as shown in the radar graph in Scheme 3.


Scheme 3 Lewis acid catalysed reaction to yield naphthoquinone reported by Wang et al. and the corresponding scores for the principles of green chemistry as a radar graph.

Another quinone-based material was developed by Schlemmer et al.43 through the synthesis of the redox-active quinone 2-methoxy-1,4-hydroquinone (MHQ) (Scheme 4). The material showed promise as an affordable and renewable product synthesised using vanillin from lignin feedstocks. The researchers optimized this reaction by altering peroxide and NaOH concentration, as well as THF and water ratios. The synthesis was carried out in a scintillation vial in which vanillin was dissolved in THF followed by the addition of peroxide and NaOH aqueous solutions. This mixture was then agitated using an ultra-sonic cleaner. After this conversion, the mixture was quenched via acidification to pH 3 using HCl. The solvent, THF, was removed using a nitrogen stream and the remaining aqueous phase was extracted with ethyl acetate and dried yielding 75% MHQ. The reaction was conducted at room temperature which scores well in energy efficiency and the use of renewable feedstocks for vanillin from lignin improved the reactions overall renewability rating. Additionally, the reaction used water as the solvent where possible, however the score for safety is lowered using THF. The reagents used are not notably hazardous and show minimal environmental risks, further improving the safety of the reaction. Therefore, a score of (3) is given for P2, P6, P8 and P9, and a score of (2) is given for P3, P4 and P7, with the other principles scoring (1) as shown in the radar graph.

Pang et al.44 have investigated the synthesis of amino acid-functionalised phenazine (AFP) compounds as anolyte active materials in aqueous RFBs, with 3,3′-(phenazine-1,6-diylbis(azanediyl))dipropionic acid (1,6-DPAP), as the most promising derivative. The synthesis of the AFPs first involves the bromination of phenazine at the appropriate positions before functionalisation with the amino acids. A generalised version of the synthesis for 2,7-dibromophenazine was used to synthesise both 2,7-, 1,8- and 1,6-dibromophenazine. The synthesis involved deprotonation of 4-bromoaniline using tBuOK in THF at −78 °C. This was followed by the addition of 1-bromo-4-nitrobenzene in THF. The reaction mixture was held at −78 °C before quenching with saturated ammonium chloride. The product was isolated using liquid–liquid extraction. The resulting residue was purified via flash chromatography using 0 to 20% ethyl acetate in hexanes gradient. The resulting crude product was added to a high-pressure flask with N,O-bis(trimethylsilyl)acetamide in acetonitrile and heated at 100 °C for 16 hours. The reaction mixture was then gradually cooled to room temperature and the precipitate was collected via filtration and recrystallised to produce the pure 2,7-dibromophenazine in 35% yield.

Following this general reaction, the AFPs were prepared by adding dibromophenazine and an amino acid to a high-pressure flask with tBuOK, Pd2(dba)3, Brettphos and t-butanol, and heated to 100 °C for 16 hours (Scheme 5). The mixture was then gradually cooled to room temperature before the precipitate was collected via filtration. The solid was then dissolved in water and HCl was used to adjust the pH to pH 2. The resulting precipitate was collected, then purified using reversed phase column chromatography using 5–10% MeOH in H2O. The AFP products were obtained in yields between 84% to 98%. The use of both low and high temperatures, below 0 °C and up to 100 °C, in these reactions lower the overall energy efficiency of the synthesis. Both the reagents, solvents and auxiliary substances pose human and environmental hazards, this reduces the safety rating of the synthesis. The final product is produced in high yields allowing for less waste to be produced and provide a reliable synthesis. As shown in the radar graph, this synthesis scored (3) only for P9 and (2) for P2, P6 and P8 with the other principles only scoring (1).

Zhu et al.45 synthesised and tested a highly water-soluble artificial bipolar molecule containing a cathode-active ferrocene moiety and an anode-active bipyridinium moiety that allowed for a single compound to be used as the active material in both the catholyte and anolyte of an aqueous redox flow system. The synthesis of 1-(4-ferrocenyl-n-butyl)-1′-[3-(trimethylammonio)propyl]-4,4′-bipyridinium trichloride (Fc-bipy3+ trichloride salt) consists of 4 reactions and an anion exchange step. Firstly, to synthesise (4-bromobutanoyl)ferrocene, AlCl3 was added to a solution of ferrocene in DCM and the mixture was stirred at room temperature for 30 minutes. 4-Bromobutyryl chloride in DCM was added slowly to the solution while the reaction temperature was maintained at 0 °C. After an overnight reaction time, water was added, and the subsequent ethyl acetate extract was washed with brine. The worked-up reaction mixture was purified by column chromatography using hexane:ethyl acetate, 100:1. This yielded 70% of (4-bromobutanoyl)ferrocene.

To synthesise (4-bromobutyl)ferrocene, LiAlH4 and AlCl3 in diethyl ether were placed under nitrogen. A solution of (4-bromobutanoyl)ferrocene in diethyl ether was added dropwise while the reaction temperature was maintained at 0 °C, this was stirred for 30 minutes and more, and AlCl3 was added before heating at reflux. Once cooled to room temperature, the solution was poured into ice water and the pH was adjusted to 7 with H2SO4. The product was extracted with diethyl ether and dried with Na2SO4 before purification via column chromatography with hexane as the eluent. This yielded 95% of (4-bromobutyl)ferrocene. To synthesise 1-(4-ferrocenyl-n-butyl)-4-(4-pyridyl)-pyridinium bromide the researchers began by combining (4-bromobutyl)ferrocene and 4,4′-bipyridine in toluene and heating at reflux for 48 hours under nitrogen. The resulting precipitate was collected by centrifuge and washed with hexane and toluene before being dried to give the product in 90% yield.

To synthesise the desired product, 1-(4-ferrocenyl-n-butyl)-1′-[3-(trimethylammonio)propyl]-4,4′-bipyridinium tribromide (Fc-bipy3+ tribromide salt), 1-(4-ferrocenyl-n-butyl)-4-(4-pyridyl)-pyridinium bromide, (3-bromopropyl)trimethylammonium bromide and N,N-dimethylformamide were stirred under nitrogen for 72 hours at 100 °C (Scheme 6). The resulting precipitate was collected by filtration and washed with acetonitrile and DCM, and the product was obtained in 85% yield after drying. Finally, the bromide ions were replaced with chloride by Amberlite® IRA-900 chloride using anion exchange resin to give Fc-bipy3+ trichloride salt in 100% yield. To achieve the range of moieties in this synthesis, there are multiple reactions that reduce atom economy. Although the reaction does deliver high yields, and while a catalyst is used, it is not benign. In these reactions the use of organic solvents and reducing agents diminish the overall safety of the preparation methodology. The energy efficiency is also reduced by using elevated temperatures. Thus, this multiple step synthesis score (1) for all the green chemistry principles expect for P2 and P6 with a score of (2) as shown in Scheme 6.

2.2 Catalyst free

While catalysts can improve the efficiency of a reaction, it also lowers the atom economy of the reaction. Introducing catalysts can present other hazards that are associated with the safety of the catalyst, or the energy required to recycle the catalyst.46 The development of green and safe synthetic methods in organic chemistry has become an important area of research, and catalyst free reactions are certainly an alternative.47 Many reactions that are carried out with catalysts can be altered to proceed in their absence, although there are limitations and obstacles in such reactions.48 Alterations to the reaction components such as solvents and the reactants used, or methods such as using microwave, mechanochemical reactions or on-water reactions can all allow dismissal of catalysts, resulting in a cleaner and more efficient reaction.48–50

There are several research groups involved in developing ferrocene and viologen as redox active materials in ORFBs.51–59 For example, Kim et al.55 synthesised dimethylethylferrocenylmethylammonium bromide (Fc1N112-Br) as a catholyte active material for aqueous ORFBs. The investigated product showed improved solubility in aqueous solutions compared to ferrocene. The synthesis of Fc1N112-Br involved dropwise addition of bromoethane to a solution of (dimethylaminomethyl)ferrocene in acetonitrile, which was stirred at room temperature for 24 hours (Scheme 7). The solution was then filtered and washed with diethyl ether to collect the Fc1N112-Br as a precipitate, which was dried at room temperature under vacuum to yield the final product. The reaction has a high energy efficiency as it is conducted at room temperature and, the lack of derivatisation steps leads to good scores in both regards. The reagents and solvents are both hazardous, thus reduce the ratings in the safety aspects of green chemistry. Therefore, the radar graph for this reaction gives a score of (3) for P2 and P6, and (2) for P8 with the other principles scoring only (1).

Another ferrocene-based redox active materials was developed by Zhao et al.56 They investigated the electrochemical properties of the catholyte active material, 1,1′-bis(sulfonate)ferrocene dianion disodium salt (1,1′-FcDS). When paired with commercially available anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonic acid disodium salt as the anolyte active material, the catholyte 1,1′-FcDS showed high solubility in aqueous solution then ferrocene. The synthesis of 1,1′-FcDS was carried out by dissolving ferrocene bis-sulfonic acid in ethanol, followed by the addition of concentrated NaOH which resulting in precipitation, which was collected via filtration and air dried to give 1,1′-FcDS in 96% yield (Scheme 8). The reaction produces low levels of waste and, as it is not heated or cooled, the energy consumption is good, leading to good ratings for each category. The choice of solvents improves safety ratings providing good scores. The synthesis however lacks the consideration of reagent degradation and renewable feedstocks. Therefore, a score of (3) is given to all the principles, except for P5 scoring (2) and P7, P10 and P11 scoring (1), as shown in the radar graph in Scheme 8.

Works by DeBruler et al.54,57 examined sulfonate-functionalized viologen molecules as anolyte active materials in ORFBs. Firstly, 1,3-propanesultone in toluene was added to a nitrogen purged Schlenk flask and brought to reflux. To this, a solution of 4,4-bipyridine in toluene was added dropwise and left to stir for 3 hours at 110 °C. The flask was then cooled, and the precipitate was collected and washed with acetonitrile. Upon drying under vacuum, 98% of the product was obtained (Scheme 9). The reaction is quick and produces a high yield however, the high temperature used reduces the energy efficiency of the reaction.

Referring to the green principles, the reactants pose safety risks related to handling and waste. Temperatures are not excessive, but they are still above 100 °C for multiple hours lowering the efficiency of the reaction. The atom economy and one pot synthesis of this reaction do appreciably increase overall efficiency of the reaction, and good yields were obtained. Another extension of viologen derivative from this group59 was the synthesis of a 4,4′-(thiazolo[5,4-d]thiazole-2,5-diyl)bis(1-(3-(trimethylammonio)propyl)pyridin-1-ium) tetrachloride [(NPr)2TTz]Cl4 as the anolyte active material (Scheme 10). The synthesis can be detailed in 2 steps. Firstly, a solution of rubeanic acid and 4-pyridinecarboxaldehyde in DMF was heated at reflux overnight. However, the synthesis requires long reaction times at higher temperatures which lowers the overall energy efficiency of the reactions. The choice of reagents and solvents used reduce the scoring in safety as well as waste production. Janoschka et al.60 presented the synthesis of anode and cathode materials, these were N,N′-dimethyl-4,4′-bipyridinium dichloride (MV) and N,N,N,2,2,6,6-heptamethylpiperidinyloxy-4-ammonium chloride (TEMPTMA), respectively. The researchers observed high capacities and energy densities while employing these materials in aqueous ORFBs. The synthesis for MV was reported as follows. 4,4-Bipyridine and chloroacetic acid were added to DMF and stirred at 140 °C for 24 hours. The resulting precipitate was collected and washed with DMF and DCM in succession to give the product in 94% yield (Scheme 11a). 2.3 Polymerisation

Another synthetic strategy to obtain redox active materials is to use polymerisation. Most materials become less soluble in the form of a polymer compared to a small molecule, however retain the redox activity and energy density of the system.83,84 Additionally, polymerisation can proceed in a green synthesis using water or alkaline water as the reaction solvent.85–88 However, not all polymerisation reaction can proceed via these solvent systems and here we have highlighted some recent polymers developed for RFBs.

Wang et al.89 synthesised a quinoxaline polymer derivative, poly(6-(1H-pyrrol-1-yl) quinoxaline (PPyQX) as the anode material. The polymer, PPyQX showed good electrochemical cyclability in an alkaline environment. This work provided a positive foundation for the use of polymers in aqueous redox flow systems. The 6-(1H-pyrrol-1-yl)quinoxaline (6-PyQX) monomer was first synthesised by combining 2,5-dimethoxytetrahydrofuran, 6-aminoquinoxaline and iodine with a mixture of DMF and water. This was stirred at room temperature for 2 hours before heating under reflux (130 °C) for 5 hours. After cooling, water was added, and the precipitate was collected to give 6-PyQX in 50% yield.

The polymer, poly(6-(1H-pyrrol-1-yl)quinoxaline) (PPyQX) was synthesised by dissolving 6-PyQX into DMF, followed by dropwise addition of bromine while maintaining the reaction at 60 °C (Scheme 28). After stirring for 5 hours, water was added, and the resulting precipitate was collected to yield 96% of PPyQX. The reaction scheme presented by the authors has middling to good ratings for green principles. The reactions use high and moderate temperatures, and moderate reaction times providing good yield of the polymer. Reagents are not used in excess and there are no derivatisation steps which would reduce atom economy. Therefore, this polymerisation reaction scores (1) only for P6 and P11 and has a high score of (3) for P8, P9 and P10, with the remaining principles scoring (2).

Cao et al.90 prepared poly(N-anthraquinoyl pyrrole) (PyAQPys) as part of a PyAQPys/graphene composite as the anode polymer material. This composite lead to excellent redox capacity in acidic aqueous mediums and this property was attributed to π-π stacking. To prepare PyAQPy (Scheme 29), firstly the monomer, 2-PyAQ, was synthesised by the addition of 2,5-dimethoxytetrahydrofuran, iodine and 2-aminoanthraquinone to DMF and water mixture, and stirred for 3 hours at 120 °C. Upon cooling to room temperature, water was added to initiate a precipitate which was collected. To prepare the polymer PyAQPy, 2-PyAQ was dissolved in DMSO, bromine was added, and the mixture was stirred for 5 hours at 60 °C. Then, water was added to the reaction mixture and the resulting precipitate yielded 96% of PyAQPys.

Finally, to prepare the PyAQPys/graphene, firstly a graphene dispersion was made by sonicating commercial graphene nanoparticles in DMSO for 30 minutes. Then, a solution of PyAQPys in DMSO was added and the resulting mixture was further sonicated for 30 minutes. Water was then added, and solid was collected to give a 2:1 PyAQPys/graphene composite which was dried under vacuum at 60 °C. This synthesis shows good ratings throughout the green principles, notably showing good energy efficiency and good atom economy with low wastage. Thus, this polymerisation reaction has a score of (2) for all the green chemistry principles except for P6, P7, P10 and P11 with a score of (1) and P9 with a score of (3).

Another example in this section is the synthesis by Zhou et al.91 of a polyimide from N,N’-diamino-1,4,5,8-naphthalenetetracarboxylic bisimide (DANTCBI) using dianhydride and diamine starting materials. The reported polyimide acts to slow redox-targeting reactions of the anolyte active materials leading to high-capacity in pH neutral electrolytes, reducing the need for highly acidic electrolyte solution. The synthesis was done by first preparing DANTCBI by treating 1,4,5,8-naphthalenetetracarboxylic dianhydride (NTCDA) and hydrazine in 1:1.2 proportions, this was then heated under reflux in N-methylpyrrolidone at 300 °C for 6 hours, the product (DANTCBI) was filtered and washed with ethanol and dried. Then, equimolar proportions of DABTCBI and NTCDA were treated using the same reaction conditions as above to yield the polyimide (Scheme 30). The high temperatures used in the reaction lower the overall energy efficiency of the reaction based on the green chemistry rating table. Additionally, the solvents used also increase the hazard ratings. The reaction lacks derivatisation steps and uses a minimal excess of reagents improving atom economy and allowing an easily scalable synthesis. This reaction has a score of (1) for all the green chemistry principles except for P2 with a score of (2) and, P8 and P9 with a score of (3).

2.4 Commercial source

Compounds that are already available commercially can be an affordable and accessible material for RFBs. However, evaluating the ‘greenness’ of the synthetic method is a difficult task as information may not be available in the published literature. Thus, here, we have classified the commercial materials and, evaluated their cost and hazard to the health and environment based on the SDS from the manufacturer.

Zhang et al.18 compared methylene blue (MB) against a range of phenothiazine based compounds (Fig. 3a), azure A, promethazine (PMZ) and chlorpromazine (CPZ), that were commercially available as redox active material in aqueous RFBs. This work provides detailed analysis on the potential, solubility, capacity and cyclability of a range of these materials in ORFBs. Given these materials are commercial reagents and no synthesis was performed, the safety of each compound and cost can be considered in relation to the green principles. Consulting the SDS, MB shows acute oral toxicity, and this is a low risk of harm if handled appropriately with health and safety guidelines. Azure A is not classified as an environmental hazard whereas PMZ and CPZ are classified as chronic aquatic hazards. These environmental issues should be considered especially when the materials are used on a large scale and if there are possibilities that the compounds may ultimately be released into the environment.


Fig. 3 Some commercially available redox active materials (a)–(h) used in aqueous ORFBs. Their chemical classification, cost per gram and manufactures are indicated below the structure.

Cao et al.92 investigated a range of commercially available hydroxyanthraquinone isomers (Fig. 3b), and determined their properties relevant to ORFBs. The detailed analysis provided a good demonstration of the effect of functional group alterations on the electrochemical characteristics and stability of a molecule in a redox cycle. Bauer and co-workers93 also studied anthraquinones however they were equipped with sulfonate salt functional groups, 2-AQS, 2,6-AQDS and 2,7-AQDS (Fig. 3c). These redox active materials showed electrochemical stability and no crossover phenomena. Also utilising 2,7-AQDS, Lee et al.94 discovered that this active material can form hydrogen bonding with ethylene glycol, which is used as the additive, thus increasing its aqueous solubility. This resulted in a high columbic efficiency and device capacity, 98% at 40 mA cm−2 and 17.8 A h L−1, respectively, with low capacity delay rate of more than 99% until the 100th cycle. All the anthraquinone compounds in these studies pose some level of harm to humans who handle the materials without proper safety equipment. Most notably, their toxicity is concerning as large-scale spills or leaks could lead to ecological damage and/or human health impacts. Closely related of quinones, cyclohexanedione isomers (Fig. 3d) were investigated by Leung and co-workers95 as the negative electrode in ORFB. 1,3-Cyclohexanedione recorded the most negative electrode potential (−0.4 V vs NHE) amongst the isomers, with a wide operating pH range (pH 1–5) and reversible redox reactions. Similar to anthraquinones, all cyclohexanediones pose some harm to human and the environment when used in large quantities.

Two quinone isomers, 1,2-naphthoquinone-4-sulfonate sodium salt (NQ-S) and 2-hydroxy-1,4-naphthoquinone (NQ-SO) (Fig. 3e) were evaluated as the negative active species with high solubility in KOH electrolyte.96 However, full-cell RFB performance was analysed only using 0.6 M and 1.2 M NQ-SO with the redox couple 0.4 M ferrocyanide (FeCN), recording 1.01 V as the cell voltage. The device performances (charge efficiency, discharge capacity, state of charge and power density) using NQ-SO were superior at higher concentrations (1.2 M), however, improved cycle stability was achieved at lower concentration (0.6 M) due to suppression of NQ-SO dimerization. Similar to other quinones, these compounds are not categorised as a dangerous good, however, their toxicity is concerning as they may cause irritation to eyes, skin and the respiratory tract resulting in chronic effects from short- and long-term exposure.

The next two publications used the same redox active materials in their ORFB investigations. Liu et al.97 and Huang et al.98 used methyl viologen (MV) and 4-hydroxy-2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-1-oxyl (4-HO-TEMPO) (Fig. 3f) as the anolyte and catholyte active materials, respectively, with NaCl as the supporting electrolyte. The anolyte and catholyte active materials were purchased from commercial sources with no further synthesis. Liu et al. aimed to provide insight on a cost effective and safe aqueous redox flow battery by drawing comparisons between vanadium and other organic RFBs. Huang et al. on the other hand showed the use of aqueous imidazolium chlorides as supporting electrolytes to significantly improve the properties of redox active materials in aqueous RFBs. Orita et al.99 also utilised 4-OH-TEMPO with Zn-based anolyte. They have shown factors such as pH and atmospheric gases influence side reactions relevant to 4-OH-TEMPO. Five TEMPO derivatives (Fig. 3h), 4-OH-TEMPO, 4OT, 4AT, 4CyT and 4CaT, as the positive electrode was recently extensively studied suggesting that redox potential, diffusion co-efficient, electron transfer rate constant and solubility are influenced by the supporting electrolyte and the functional groups on the TEMPO. Some of these compounds are highly unlikely to be used in large quantities such as that required for ORFBs, as they are relatively expensive.100 Hu and co-workers101 utilised MV as the active material coupled with an organometallic ferrocene-based anolyte material. They studied the effects of ion membrane and supporting electrolyte on the resistance and redox performance of ORFBs, revealing that thin membrane (90 μm) with low resistance (1.07 Ω cm−2) and 2 M KCl as the supporting electrolyte, give the highest battery performance for this redox system.

Molecular design of azobenzene (Fig. 3g) was adapted by Zu and co-workers where they utilised the redox activity of the azo group (NN).102 They used the active material, AADA with hydrophilic groups to increase its aqueous solubility exhibiting ORFB performance of long cycling stability while retaining capacity of 99.95% per cycle over 500 cycles. The azobenzene is again a non-dangerous good however, in large quantities it will be harmful to humans and the environment if it enters the ground water or the drainage system.

3. Conclusions and outlook

Tremendous efforts in the past decade has seen aqueous ORFBs develop to become a potential leader in energy storage. However, the field still encounters issues in utilising ‘green’ active materials for the anolytes or catholytes. Learning from the 12 principles of green chemistry, more control over the synthetic methodology and material processing can be advocated into the field of aqueous RFBs as outlined in this review. The green chemistry principles introduce the key concepts required to understand ways to minimise our impact on human health and the environment whilst gaining experimental progress. While many researchers consider aqueous RFBs as a green technology, they fail to consider these principles in their material synthesis. It thus provides an illusion of the ‘greenness’ of the RFB system as a whole. This review would delineate a true and multidimensional picture of the impact on the environment of these organic syntheses in developing active materials for aqueous RFBs.

We must recognise that the existing information for some active materials is sparse, particularly commercially obtained compounds, as their synthetic methods are absent in the literature, causing a ‘black hole’ in our understanding of the ‘greenness’ of these reactions. However, solid information is readily available in the literature for materials that are synthesised by research groups, allowing a transparent evaluation of their synthetic methodology in producing the active materials. Most reactions are not able to stringently obey all the 12 principles of green chemistry, however the majority of them are heading towards a ‘green’ label with requirement of some adjustment in the synthesis (Table 2), especially in the aspects of using renewable feedstocks (P7), real-time analysis for pollution prevention (P11) and using inherently safer chemistry for accident prevention (P12). Several methodologies including using a microwave reactor and utilising catalysts lead to energy efficient reactions. For some reactions, reducing the use of toxic solvents and avoiding unnecessary chemical transformation are considered as they are important to prevent threat to human health and the environment. In some reactions, maximum incorporation of all the precursors to produce the desired material is an atom economy strategy. While some syntheses have proven to be ‘greener’ than others, many more syntheses to come need to consider the green chemistry principles to truly harness the clean and renewable concept in aqueous RFBs and would also allow a more meaningful comparison with RFBs based on inorganic materials. Such understanding and implementation should become a practical demonstration in future research.
