Photophysical properties of metal complexes

Nathan J. Patmore *
Department of Chemistry, University of Sheffield, Sheffield, UK S3 7HF. E-mail: n.patmore@sheffield.ac.uk

First published on 2nd May 2007

Abstract

This review summarises the literature reported in 2006 on the photophysical properties of metal complexes and their polynuclear and supramolecular assemblies. The popularity of this field means that the review has been constrained to only cover literature from the major high-impact journals.

Highlights

Highlights for the year include a giant porphyrin wheel containing 24 Zn(II)-porphyrins that displays efficient excitation energy transfer around the wheel, and the direct observation of a 1MLCT excited-state in [Mo2(O2C-9-anthracene)4] by transient absorption spectroscopy. Application of metal complexes as dyes for solar cells, phosphorescent emitters in OLED devices and as fluorescent sensors and bioprobes continue to be areas that attract strong interest.

Review articles

A number of reviews have appeared on the topic of solar energy conversion, including; the design of metal complexes and their optimisation for use as dyes in dye-sensitized solar cells,1,2 and construction of artificial light harvesting systems using porphyrin, phthalocyanine, perylenebisimide and polypyridyl based metal complexes.3 Synthetic strategies towards lanthanide ion complexes for application as luminescent chemosensors4 and the photophysical properties of Cm(III) in crystals and solutions5 have been reviewed. The design and photophysical properties of a variety of Os(II) and Ru(II) complexes for utilisation in OLED fabrication has been reviewed6 along with light-emitting molecular devices that incorporate Ni(II), Cu(II) and Zn(II) metal complexes.7 The photophysical properties of metal-mediated assemblies of porphyrins have been summarised.8 Applications of bis-cyclometallated Ir(III) complexes and methods for the synthetic tailoring of their excited state properties have been reviewed.9 The photophysics and applications of rigid-rod di-, oligo- and polymetallaynes has also been discussed.10

An entire issue of Coordination Chemistry Reviews (2006, 250, issue 13–14) was dedicated to papers based on oral presentations at the 16th International Symposium on the Photochemistry and Photophysics of Coordination Compounds (which took place in California during July 2005).

Main group compounds

The properties of [Alq3] (q = quinolin-8-olate) derivatives that are blue emitting have been tuned for potential application in OLED devices in a number of studies. Al(III) complexes incorporating quinolin-8-olate ligands fluorinated at the C5 position exhibit a red-shift (λem = 407 nm) and decrease in emission intensity in CHCl3 solutions by comparison to [Alq3] (λem = 387 nm). However, fluorination at the C6 position of q causes a blue-shift (λem = 375 nm) and significant increase in emission intensity.11 [Alq2(OPh)] complexes containing a variety of electron poor aryl substituents in the C5 position of q display strong blue emission between 455 and 504 nm.12 The use of substituted phenolates in [Alq2(OPh)] compounds does not result in a significant change of the emission energy, but can affect the shape and intensity of the emission band.13 Attachment of oligofluorene Pt(II) porphyrin acceptors to the [Alq3] core (see I) results in donor–bridge–acceptor triads. OLED devices incorporating these species showed pure red emission from the Pt porphyrin, with improved output for the longer donor–acceptor distance (n = 4) because of a better alignment of the donor–bridge–acceptor triplet energy levels.14

Aluminium complexes containing a series of chelating anilido-imine ligands, [ortho-C6H4(NAr′)(CHNAr″)AlCl2] (Ar′, Ar″ = 2,6-iPr2C6H3, 2,6-Me2C6H3 or p-MeC6H4), display bright fluorescence in both solution and the solid state, with the emission colour in solution (λem = 510–554 nm) tuned by the ortho substituents on the rotatable aryl rings of the ligand.15 Complexes of Ga(III) and In(III) with 5-mesityldipyrrin (mesdpm) emit green light upon excitation with UV light at room temperature with quantum yields of 2.4%, [Ga(mesdpm)3], and 7.4%, [In(mesdpm)3].16

Biarsenical dyes with fluoro-substituted versions of fluorescein show increased photostability, lower pH sensitivity, strong absorbance and high quantum yields, which improves their use as tetracysteine probes.17 The closely related lead coordination polymers {[Pb4(OH)2(SIP)2(H2O)](H2O)5}n and [Pb3(SIP)2(H2O)5]n (SIP = 5-sulfoisophthalate) display fluorescent and phosphorescent solid-state emission, respectively, due to differences in their solid-state structures.18 A fluorescent sensor for detecting lead in living cells, combining a fluorescein-type scaffold and dicarboxylate pseudocrown receptor, exhibits a selective turn-on fluorescence emission at 514 nm in response to Pb(II) ions in aqueous solutions.19

The organic-inorganic hybrid material (HL)6[Ge6(OH)6(hedp)6]·2(L)·nH2O (Hhedp = 1-hydroxyethylidenediphosphonic acid) displays emission from the lowest energy triplet-state at 320 nm from 14 K to room temperature when L = phen. In contrast, when L = Hq, triplet emission (530 nm) is only detected at low temperature because of thermally activated non-radiative mechanisms.20

Copper, silver and gold complexes

The coordination of Cu(II) to anthrancenyl and dansyl based fluorophore ligands significantly quenches the fluorescence of the ligands. When exposed to NO(g) in pH 7.0 or 9.0 buffered aqueous solutions, a fluorescence increase is observed from NO-triggered Cu(II) reduction.21 The use of Cu(II) complexes as fluorescent turn-on sensors for the detection of nitric oxide has been extended to π-conjugated polymers containing bipyridyl or terpyridyl substituents in the π-conjugated backbone, or at side chain positions. Coordination of Cu(II) to the polymers results in fluorescence quenching of the polymer emission, but upon addition of NO there is up to a 3-fold enhancement of the metallopolymer emission due to reduction of Cu(II).22,23 A derivative of the dual fluorescent charge transfer fluoroionophore 2-methoxybenzaldehyde acylhydrazone acts as a sensor for the selective detection of Cu2+ with up to 170-fold fluorescence enhancement in the presence of Cu(II) ions.24 A dipyrromethene boron difluoride fluorescent reporter, attached to a thioether receptor, also acts as a turn-on fluorescent sensor for Cu2+ in living cells, with a 10-fold increase in fluorescent response and good selectivity over other biologically relevant metal ions.25

Mononuclear Cu(I) complexes, containing 2-(2′-pyridylbenzimidazolyl)benzene and a variety of phosphine based ancillary ligands, display MLCT emissions with long decay lifetimes (>200 μs) at 77 K when doped into PMMA polymer films.26 Sterically encumbered phenanthroline ligands containing 3,5-di-tert-butyl-4-methoxyphenyl and 2,4,6-trimethylphenyl substituents in the 2,9-positions were used to generate a [Cu(phen)2]+ complex. This complex exhibits unusually strong emission at 77 K due to the sterically protected coordination environment that prevents the formation of non-emissive pentacoordinated excited complexes.27 In a similar fashion, the 3MLCT lifetime and emission intensity of [Cu(2,9-Me-phen)(PP)]+ (PP = diphosphine ligand) complexes increases as the bulkiness of the diphosphine ligand is increased.28 DFT calculations on the luminescent tetranuclear Cu(I) complexes [Cu4(μ-dppm)4(μ4-E)]2+ (E = PPh and S) reveal that the emissive state in both complexes is derived from an admixture of 3LMCT (E → Cu) and 3MCC (3d → 4p) (MCC = metal-cluster-centred) characters.29 Self-assembly of nanometer scale [Cu24I10L12]14+ (L = triazole based N-heterocyclic ligand) cages with Keggin anions, [PMoV2MoVI10O40]5−, results in the formation of a 3D framework that exhibits photoluminescence at ambient temperature. The two prominent emission peaks at 436 and 462 nm are attributed to a mixture of I → Cu charge transfer and d–s transitions due to Cu–Cu interactions.30

The halogeno(cyano)argentite [Ag5(CN)Br6]2+ displays strong blue fluorescent emission at 450 nm (τ = 3.7 ps) upon photo-excitation at 397 nm, but [Ag2(CN)2Cl]+ exhibits a relatively weak band at 390 nm (τ = 7.0 ps) with photo-excitation at 307 nm.31 Strong emission is observed in the solid-state from silver cyano coordination polymers [Ag5(CN)5(bpy)2]n and [Ag5(SCN)4(CN)(bpy)2]n originating from an MLCT transition (Ag → bpy), and in [(Me4N)Ag3(CN)4]n from a coupled MLCT (Ag → CN−) and metal centred transition.32 The Schiff-base ligands L1 and L2 display very weak luminescence in solution but strong luminescence when coordinated to Ag(I) centres; attributed to the conformational rigidity of the ligands in the metal complex that reduces nonradiative decay of the intraligand 1(π–π*) excited state.33


Luminescence studies were performed to determine the intracellular distribution of a dinuclear Au(I) complex, containing a bidentate N-heterocyclic carbene ligand that supports Au⋯Au interactions.34 Cyclic Au(I) triazolate trimers, [Au(Tz)]3 (Tz = 3,5-diisopropyl-1,2,4-triazolate), form fully overlapping aurophilically bonded dimer-of-trimers that give multiple phosphorescence bands in both the solid state and solution at 280 K.35 A series of Au(I) thiolates, [(P)Au{SC(OMe)NC6H4-4-NO2}] (Pphosphine ligand) and [(PP){AuSC(OMe)NC6H4-4-NO2}2] (PP = bridging diphosphine ligand), were structurally characterised; the presence or absence of Au⋯Au interactions do not impose significant differences on the emission energy and excitation maxima.36 Thiolate (2-SC6H4NH2) complexes of Au(I) with mono- or di-phosphine ligands display intense photo-luminescence when cooled to 77 K. Reaction of the thiolate complexes with Au(C6F5)3 resulted in the formation of non-emissive Au(I)–Au(III) compounds.37

The coinage metal clusters [Au2M2(C6F5)4(NCCH3)2]n (M = Ag, Cu) display intense luminescence in the solid state at room temperature, but in donor solvents the emission is quenched for M = Cu due to solvent coordination.38

Zinc, cadmium and mercury complexes

Zn(II) is an abundant element in natural biological systems and a number of fluorescent dyes have been developed for use as turn-on fluorescence sensors in biological systems. These sensors incorporate derivatives of tris(2-pyridylmethyl)amine,39 tetra-azacyclododecane,40,41 bisimidazole,42 pyridyl,43,44 2,2′-dipicolylamine45 and quinoline46 as Zn(II) ion receptors. A phenyl-bridged bis(pyrrol-2-yl)methyleneamine derivative (BPBA) is a sensor for Zn(II) ions based on chelation-enhanced fluorescence. In turn, the BPBA-Zn(II) complex acts as a Hg(II) ion sensor due to replacement of central metal ion induced chelation-enhanced fluorescence quenching.47 Dinuclear Zn(II) complexes with a terpyridine bridge and phenanthroline based ancillary ligands can display fluorescence quenching in the presence of Hg(II) ions, but fluorescent enhancement in the presence of Pb(II) ions.48

The slight differences in the rates of electron energy transfer (EET) between two isomeric forms of a Zn(II) tetraaza macrocycle appended by a naphthalene donor and anthracene acceptor was determined [kEET = (0.92 ± 0.02) × 109 and (1.31 ± 0.01) × 109 s−1], and are in accord with the expectations of the Förster equation.49

The cadmium cluster [Cd10S4(SR)12] [R = an oligo(ethyleneglycol) unit] acts as a photoluminescence sensor with a turn-on response to Cu(II) ions in solution.50 The emission energy of a 3D porous metal-organic framework based on [Cd3L12] [L = 2,6-di(4-triazolyl)pyridine] clusters can be tuned between the UV and visible wavelengths by controlling the number of guest molecules in the framework.51 A series of 4-substituted triazole Cd(II) materials with zero-, one-, two- and three-dimensional frameworks exhibit strong blue fluorescence emission bands in the solid state at room temperature, that blue-shift in aqueous solutions.52

Signal ratio amplification via modulation of resonance energy transfer was demonstrated using a dithiazacrown-substituted benzaldehyde cation receptor appended to a boradiazaindacene dyad that displayed a 35-fold change in emission ratios upon addition of Hg(II) ions.53 Pentafluorophenylmercury chloride interacts with phenanthrene in the solid state and displays only fluorescent phenanthrene based emission at room temperature, but both fluorescent and phosphorescent emission is exhibited at 77 K.54 Fluorescein based fluorescent sensors appended with pyridyl-amine-thiol groups that selectively bind Hg(II) ions can detect mid-nanomolar to low-micromolar concentrations of Hg(II) in aqueous solution.55

Miscellaneous transition metal complexes

Subphthalocyanine chromophores act as electron-accepting units in subphthalocyanine-ferrocene dyads that, upon charge separation from singlet excited states, form long-lived (τ = 231 μs) charge-separated states.56 Efficient PET is observed in dendrimers of C60 with two and four ferrocene units on their periphery. Lifetimes of the [(C60)˙−-(dendron)˙+] charge-separated state vary between tens of nanoseconds in nonpolar solvents, to hundreds of nanoseconds in polar solvents, with higher values observed for the dendron containing four ferrocene units.57 Fluorescence quenching of a C60 acceptor having covalently linked ferrocene and N,N-dimethylaniline donors indicates that charge-separation takes place via the singlet excited state of the C60 moiety in both polar and non-polar solvents.58

The 1MLCT excited-state of [Mo2(O2C-9-anthracene)4] has an unusually long lifetime (∼10 ps), allowing the 1MLCT absorption spectrum to be recorded using ultrafast transient absorption spectroscopy. The 3MLCT excited-state also has a long lifetime (τ = 76 μs), and the longer excited-state lifetimes of MM quadruply bonded complexes containing aryl ligands, relative to mononuclear complexes, is thought to be a general phenomenon.59 Spectroscopic studies on a series of tetra-substituted thiol oxotitanium phthalocyanines revealed low fluorescence yields (0.05–0.14), relatively long triplet lifetimes (150–210 μs) and triplet yields of 0.31–0.82.60 The absorption spectra of the complexes trans-(N2)2W(R2PCH2CH2PR2)2 (R = Ph, Et, 4-CF3-Ph, 4-CH3O-Ph) display peaks assigned to 3MLCT and 3(d → d) transitions that become allowed due to spin–orbit coupling. Total luminescence from the R = 4-CF3-Ph complex at 5 K is thought to be composed of simultaneous emission from near-degenerate 3MLCT and 3(d → d) terms.61

A DNA based building block, pyridobenzimidazole nucleoside, acts as a fluorescent sensor for a wide variety of metal ions with different emission quenching, enhancement and energy responses observed for the metal ions.62 A fluorescein based thioester (SR) was employed as a light harvesting antenna that increases the rate of photogenerated NO production from the Roussin red salt ester [Fe2(NO)4(μ-SR)2].63

Rhenium complexes

The photophysical properties of a series of Re(CO)3(L) complexes, where L is a tridentate ligand derived from quinoline, benzimidazole, or tryptophan, were studied and show emission from MLCT or LC excited states.64 A series of tricarbonyl Re(I) phenanthroline estradiol compounds exhibit long-lived 3MLCT emission that is enhanced upon binding of estrogen receptor α.65 Luminescent rhenium(I) tricarbonyl amidodipyridoquinoxaline biotin complexes display a profound enhancement of the 3MLCT emission intensity upon binding to the avidin protein.66 The 3MLCT excited states of rectangular Re(I) compounds, [{Re(CO)3(μ-4,4′-bpy)Br}{Re(CO)3(μ-L)Br}] (L = a series 4,4′-dipyridylalkynes) are quenched by electron transfer reactions from aromatic amines more quickly than monomeric Re(I) complexes.67 Re(I) compounds of formula [(bpy)(CO)3Re(PCA)]+, [(bpy)(CO)3Re(PCA)Re(CO)3(bpy)]2+ and [(bpy)(CO)3Re(PCA)Ru(NH3)5]3+ (PCA = 4-pyridinecarboxaldehydeazine), do not emit at room temperature in acetonitrile but, in aqueous solutions, a decrease in pH induces luminescence in all of them from MLCT excited states.68 DFT has been used to investigate the role of MLCT excited states in the trans–cis isomerisation of fac-[Re(CO)3(bpy)(stpy)]+ (stpy = trans-4-styrylpyridine). It is concluded that photoisomerisation of the stpy ligand under visible light operates by a triplet intraligand mechanism via the 3(πstpy → π*stpy) state, that is populated by intramolecular energy transfer from the Re(I) 3MLCT state.69 The Re(III) metallocarborane [7,8,9-ReC2B7], containing a {Re(CO)(PPh3)2} fragment, displays turquoise blue dual fluorescence accredited to metal-perturbed 1LC states.70

The fluorescent complexes [Re(X)(diimine)(CO)3] (X = OH2, SCN− or CN−) have sufficiently long-lived excited state lifetimes to act as catalysts for the photocatalytic reduction of CO2.71 Ultrafast time-resolved emission and nanosecond transient absorption spectroscopies were used to monitor the kinetics involved in the photogeneration of fluorotyrosyl radicals from a Re(I) tricarbonyl polypyridyl complex.72 A multicomponent complex containing a Re(I) dppz emissive chromophore, an electron transporting 1,3,4-oxadiazole, and a hole transporting thiophene, was evaluated for application as a dopant in OLED devices.73 A series of mixed ligand bpy and phen Re(I) dicarbonyl complexes, [Re(CO)2(P–P)(N–N)]+ (P–P = chelating diphosphene, N–N = chelating polypyridine ligand), were synthesised and display a broad range of exited state lifetimes (25–1147 ns) and quantum yields (0.002–0.11).74 A methanofullerene, bearing a pyridine or bpy ligand separated from the fullerene through an oxyethylene macrocyclic spacer, was complexed with {Re(bpy)(CO)3}+ and {Ru(bpy)2}2+ fragments. Steady-state and time-resolved spectroscopy revealed luminescence quenching of the MLCT state, and triplet–triplet energy transfer from the metal centres to the fullerene.75

Ruthenium and osmium complexes

Mononuclear ruthenium complexes

Broadband femtosecond fluorescence spectroscopy was used to capture the early relaxation processes leading to the steady-state emission of the 3MLCT state of [Ru(bpy)3]2+. The dissipation of energy within the manifold electronic and vibrational levels of the 3MLCT state was proposed to occur via ultrafast intramolecular vibrational-energy redistributions.76 Slight modification of L to L′ in the complex [Ru(bpy)2(L)]2+ (L = diacetyl dihydrazone, L′ = the 1:2 condensate of L and acetone) induces a change from Ru(II) → L charge transfer to Ru(II) → bpy charge transfer as the lowest excited state.77 Perdeuteration of the bpy ligand in [Ru(NH3)6−2n(bpy)n]2+ or [Ru(en)3−n(bpy)n]2+ results in much larger blue shifts of the emission maxima when n = 1 than when n = 3, but perdeuteration of the ligands in [Ru(pyridine)4(bpy)]2+ does not result in any measurable shifts in the emission spectra.78 DFT calculations were used to probe the origin of the very long-lived (30 μs) excited state for [Ru(bpy)2(L3)]2+. The calculations provided evidence of excited states lower in energy than the MLCT states, originating from intraligand charge transfer from the phenolic rings to the phenanthroline part of L3.79 The complex [Ru(bqp)2]2+ [bqp = 2,6-bis(8′-quinolinyl)pyridine] has a remarkably long-lived room temperature 3MLCT excited-state lifetime (τ = 3.0 μs), rationalised by the near octahedral geometry of the Ru(II) core that raises the energy of the short-lived triplet metal-centred state. The MLCT excited states of [Ru(bpy)2(deeb)]2+ (deeb = 4,4′-(CO2CH2CH3)2-bpy) in dichloromethane are efficiently quenched by iodine via an electron transfer mechanism that results in the transient formation of I2˙− in high yield (Φ = 0.25).80 The synthetic application of photochemical upconversion was demonstrated using selective excitation of [Ru(dmb)3]2+ at a variety of visible wavelengths to efficiently drive bimolecular [4 + 4] cycloaddition between two anthracene molecules.81 A series of complexes of the type [Ru(NN)(CN)4] (NN = bpy, phen, terpy, dmb, bpym, L4, L5) showed the presence of 3MLCT-based emission with lifetimes of between 0.25 and 471 ns that were determined from luminescence studies or, for compounds with very weak luminescence, transient IR spectroscopy.82 Ruthenium bpy-based complexes, containing a dppz ligand with alkyl and aryl groups substituted at the 11 and 12 position, exhibit prolonged excited state lifetimes for the aryl substituted dppz ligands.83 A series of compounds of formula [Ru(bpy)2(L6)]2+ display electrochemiluminescence (ECL), with the most basic benzimidazole (X = NCH3) displaying the highest quantum yield for luminescence, and highest ECL efficiency.84 Variations in the nonchromophoric ligands of [Ru(L4)(bpy)] (L4 = four mono-, two bi- or one tetra-dentate amine ligands) result in large changes in emission band shapes, even when the emission energies are similar, due to different vibronic contributions and excited state distortions.85 Time-resolved luminescence and transient absorption studies of [Ru(bpy)3]2+ assembled in various alkali-metal exchanged zeolites reveals fluorescence quenching of the 3MLCT from PET to the alkali metal cations.86

The emission intensity of the complex rac-[Ru(dmp)3]2+ (dmp = 5,6-dimethyl-phen) is dramatically enhanced on binding with DNA, to a higher degree than the unsubstituted phen analogue.87 The MLCT luminescence intensity of a heteronuclear Ru(II)Pt(II) dimer, containing a [Ru(bpy)3]2+ derivative with pendant N-(3-ammoniopropyl)carbamoyl arms attached to a cis-PtCl2 unit, is enhanced by complexation with calf thymus DNA. The Ru(II)Pt(II) dimer also exhibits a relatively high efficiency towards the visible-light-induced strand scission of DNA, thought to proceed via an electron transfer process between the photogenerated Ru(III) centre and a guanine base bound to a Pt(II) centre.88

Covalent attachment of a [Ru(bpy)3]2+ photosensitiser to a diiron electron acceptor, which is a model of the iron hydrogenase active site, does not result in electron transfer. Instead the light-induced MLCT excited state is reductively quenched by the diiron site.89

Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes containing bpy and terpy subunits with pyrromethene-BF2 ‘bodipy’ chromophores have long-lived (μs) non-emissive excited states at room temperature. However, at 77 K the complexes exhibit bodipy based fluorescence or phosphorescence.90 A [Ru(bpy3)]2+ compound with a covalently attached methylviologen (MV2+) electron acceptor forms a stable 1:1 inclusion complex with cucurbit[8]uril (CB[8]). When this system is irradiated with visible light a PET process occurs, and a long-lived (τ = 2 μs) charge-separated state (Ru3+-MV+˙-CB[8]) is observed in aqueous solution.91 The heteroleptic complex [Ru(terpy-COOH)(terpy-NH2)], to which a ferrocene unit is attached at the N-terminal group, is significantly more luminescent than the analogous complexes with C-terminal attached ferrocene or both N- and C-terminal attached ferrocene. This is attributed to quenching of the ruthenium based 3MLCT state by greater electron transfer from the more efficiently coupled C-terminal ferrocene group.92 Efficient energy transfer from ruthenium to iridium occurs in heteroleptic bimetallic systems having one metal centre (Ru or Ir) coordinated to two terpy ligands, a second metal centre coordinated to three bpy ligands (Ir or Ru), and an aryl linker.93 Emission from CdSe nanocrystal quantum dots (NQDs) is quenched upon binding to the surface of [Ru(bpy)3−n(mcb)n]2+ (mcb = 4-carboxy-4′-methyl-bpy, n = 0, 1, 2) complexes. The NQD functions as a light sensitiser that, upon photoexcitation, oxidises the attached Ru(II) complexes via efficient hole transfer.94

Steady-state fluorescence and time-resolved flash photolysis measurements on a Ru(II) fullerene complex, [Ru(bpy)2(bpy-C60)]+, showed a rapid intramolecular quenching of the Ru(II) 3MLCT excited state by transduction of the triplet excited state energy to the fullerene.95 Photoinduced charge separation in ruthenocene-C60 dyads is more efficient with a 2-pyrazoline donor–acceptor bridge compared to a pyrrolidine linker, due to the higher electron donating ability of the pyraziline ring.96 Intramolecular triplet energy transfer between a [Ru(bpy)3]2+ based donor and C60 acceptor bridged by a short ethylene spacer is quantitative at all temperatures. The energy-transfer mechanism is dominated by super-exchange interactions at low temperature, and a through-space mechanism at higher temperatures as a result of a linker distortion.97 Photophysical studies on [M(η5-C60R5)(η5-C5H5)] (M = Fe and Ru, R = Me and Ph) results in a rapid charge separation for ‘bucky ferrocenes’, with radical ion pair lifetimes of between 27 and 39 ps. No charge separation was found in the corresponding ‘bucky ruthenocene’, due to faster excited state-deactivation from the heavier ruthenium centre.98

A dual signal responding fluoride sensor comprising of a Ru(II) bpy fluorophore and a 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazone chromophore displayed a new intense absorption band at 580 nm and a significant enhancement of the emission intensity upon titration with F− ions.99 Luminescent [Ru(bpy)2(L7)]2+ complexes act as fluorescence sensors for Cu(II) ions, with complete quenching of the luminescence observed upon binding of Cu(II) to the bis(pyridinamide) coordination site in the μM concentration range.100 Addition of Ba2+ ions to [Ru(phen)3]2+ complexes, with azacrown ethers attached to the 4,7 positions of the phenanthrolines, results in shifts of up to 87 nm in the emission spectra, and shifts of up to 370 mV in the redox potentials.101

Efficient energy transfer to 3,6-diphenyl-1,2,4,5-tetrazine from the excited state of [Ru(bpy)3]2+ produces the triplet excited state of tetrazine that has a much longer lifetime (τ = 28.4 μs) and higher oxidising ability (Ered* = 1.09 V vs. SCE) than the 3MLCT state of [Ru(bpy)3]2+.102 The photo-reduction of the Dawson polyoxometalate anions [S2M18O62]4− in the visible region was sensitised by [Ru(bpy)3]2+ cations.103 A single-component photo-hydrogen-evolving device comprising of a [Ru(bpy)3]2+ derivative as a photosensitisor, covalently attached to a Pt(II) H2-evolving catalyst, is successful in promoting visible-light-induced EDTA-reduction of water into molecular hydrogen.104

Visible excitation of the heteroleptic rotaxane [Ru(terpy)(L8)]2+ complex causes efficient electron transfer to the 4,4′-bipyridinium unit; a process that is slowed by a factor of two to three when a crown ether surrounds the cationic axle and it is totally blocked in a rigid matrix at 77 K.105 The complex [Ru(bpy)2(L9)]2+ displays room-temperature emission in the far-red region of the visible spectrum, from an excited state based on the imine ligand. However, the complex [Ru(terpy)(L9)Cl]1+ does not emit at room temperature, indicating that the energy of the 3MLCT excited state responsible for deactivation of emission in Ru(II) terpy complexes is unaltered.106


The complex [Ru(dcb)(L10)(NCS)2] has a superhigh light-harvesting capacity and is an effective photosensitisor for DSSCs, with a power-conversion efficiency 10% greater than that of [Ru(dcb)2(NCS)2] under the same conditions.107 Heteroleptic [Ru(dcb)(L11)(NCS)2] compounds have high molar extinction coefficients and, correspondingly, are highly effective sensitisers for DSSCs, with an overall conversion efficiency of 9% for the para-methoxystyryl derivative.108 The related complex [Ru(dcb)(L12)(NCS)2] also performs well as a TiO2-bound sensitisor in DSSCs, with a high overall conversion efficiency (9.4%). Calculations reveal that, importantly, excitation directs charge onto the dcb ligand bound to the TiO2 surface.109 The efficiency of a DSSC incorporating the sensitisor [Ru(dcb)(dppz)(NCS)2] was lower compared to that of [Ru(dcb)(phen)(NCS)2], attributed by DFT calculations to a smaller contribution from the dcb ligand to the character of the MLCT transitions.110 A series of DSSCs using [Ru(L13)2(NCS)2] sensitisors showed greater photon-to-current efficiencies than [Ru(dcb)2(NCS)2] in the region beyond 625 nm.111 The electron injection quantum yield of [Ru(bpy)2(11-PO{OEt}2-dppz)]2+ complexes anchored to anatase TiO2 thin films in 0.1 M LiClO4/MeCN solutions was reduced from 80% to 30% upon addition of large concentrations of water.112 Efficient-excited state dynamic quenching by I3− of [Ru(bpy)2(deeb)] [deeb = 4,4′-(CO2CH2CH3)2-bpy] anchored to mesoporous nanocrystalline TiO2 thin films was observed in CH2Cl2 and MeCN solutions. This suggests that for weak sensitisor–semiconductor electronic coupling, sensitised quenching by I3− may compete with electron injection, reducing the efficiency of DSSCs.113 Long-lived charge separated states that store energy in excess of the equilibrated excited state were formed in mesoporous TiO2 thin films functionalised with [M(bpy)2(deebq)]2+, [Ru(bq)2(deeb)]2+, [Ru(deebq)2(bpy)]2+ and [Ru(bpy)(deebq)(NCS)2] (M = Ru, Os; bq = 2,2′-biquinoline; deeb and deebq = 4,4′-diethylester derivatives of bpy and bq).114


The complex [Ru(dpphen)3]2+ (dpphen = 4,7-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline) shows promising electroluminescent behaviour in a solid-state light-emitting electrochemical cell (LEC), reaching a high power efficiency (1.9 Lum/W) at a luminous brightness of 390 cm/m2. Importantly, it is a lot more stable than [Ru(bpy)3]2+-containing LEC devices due to the increased hydrophobic nature preventing the formation of quenching H2O containing complexes.115 A chemiluminescent (CL) sensor based on a Ru(II) bpy derivative covalently immobilised on a silica surface has a number of advantages over sensors in which the CL reagent is immobilised by adsorption or trapping; these include no leaching, and a greater sensor lifetime and reproducibility.116

The complexes [Ru(CO)2−n(P)n(BQ)] and [Ru(CO)2−n(P)n(DBQ)] (n = 0 or 1, BQ = benzo[h]quinoline, DBQ = dibenzo[f,h]quinoline, P = PPh2Me or PPhMe2) display long-lived and weak phosphorescence when n = 0, but shorter-lived and more intense phosphorescence when n = 1.117 Ruthenium halide complexes bearing hemilabile phosphine pyrenyl ether ligands exhibit emission from pyrene excimers, with emission intensities dependent on the extent of nonradiative energy transfer to metal-based states.118 Phosphorescent emission (τ = 0.77 ms) at 77 K is unexpectedly observed from the metallocarborane complex [3-CO-3,3{κ2-Me2N(CH2)2NMe2}-closo-3,1,2-RuC2B9H11.119

Mononuclear osmium complexes

A series of photosensitisor-acceptor dyads of formula [Os(4′-tolyl-terpy)(L14)]3+ display conformational gating of photoinduced processes, with competing energy-transfer to a 3LC state or electron-transfer to a charge-separated state processes responsible for the quenching of the photoinduced 3MLCT state.120 DFT calculations on these systems provided insight to the processes involved; confirming the existence of a 3LC state that essentially involves the nitro substituents and that the energy of the 3LC state and, therefore, contribution to excited state behaviour, is dependent on the xylyl spacer tilt angle.121

The photophysical properties of the blue-emitting phosphorescent complexes [Os(CO)2(N∧NH)2] (N∧NH = 3-CF3-5-{2-pyridyl}pyrazole or 3-CF3-5-{2-pyridyl}triazole) and blue- and green-emitting isomeric forms of [Os(CO)(H)(PPh2Me)2(N∧NH)] have been studied. The subtle differences in the photophysical properties of the isomeric species were attributed to the π-accepting character of CO, confirmed by DFT and time-dependent DFT calculations.122 The lowest excited triplet state of a spiropyran substituted [Os(bpy)3]2+ complex in its open merocyanine form (L15) is higher in energy than the 3MLCT state, resulting in spiropyran-to-metal energy transfer upon excitation at 600 nm. By comparison, the 3MLCT of the Ru(II) bpy derivative is higher in energy than the spiropyran triplet excited state resulting in metal-to-spyropyran energy transfer instead.123

A [Os(terpy)2]2+ derivative attached to a cyclam receptor unit acts as a fluorogenic sensor, with quenching of emission from the Os(II) complex in the presence of Cu(II) and Ni(II) ions in MeCN/H2O solutions.124


Complexes containing two or more Ru or Os chromophores

The near IR-emitting dinuclear complex [(terpy)Ru(L16)Ru(terpy)]4+ has an unusually long-lived excited lifetime (τ = 3.2 μs at 77 K) and high quantum yield, attributed to the planar nature of the bridging allowing extensive delocalisation of the promoted electron.125 A similar multichromophore approach with dinuclear Ru(II) complexes, containing anthryl-terpy derivatives as ancillary ligands and pyrimidine-terpy derivatives as bridging ligands, afforded long-lived luminescence at room temperature. Multiple luminescence lifetimes were obtained as a result of a fast equilibrium between the rapidly generated 3MLCT state and anthryl triplet state.126 Control of the biphenyl dihedral angle in [(terpy)Ru(L17)Ru(terpy)] reveals that the minimum rate of nonradiative decay from the 3MLCT state occurs when the dihedral angle is 90°, and the maximum nonradiative decay rate when the dihedral angle is about 45°.127 A detailed study of the ultrafast excited-state excitation dynamics in [{Ru(NN)2}2(L18)] (NN = 4,4′-di-tert-butyl-bpy) reveals that the Ru-1MLCT state formed upon photoexcitation undergoes a rapid (τ ≤ 200 fs) inter-system crossing to the 3MLCT (τ = 2.5 ps) state, followed by charge transfer to the Pd moiety (τ = 50 ps).128

The Ru2Re2 metallomacrocycle, [(bpy)2Ru(L19)Ru(bpy)2][PF6]4, can function as a luminescent anion sensor; the emission intensity from a Ru(II)-based 3MLCT is enhanced upon titration of BF4− anions.129 Photoexcitation of a dinuclear assembly of Ru(II) phthalocyanines bridged by a perylenediimide derivative is followed by rapid intraensemble charge separation to form a long-lived (115 ns) radical ion pair.130 Luminescence from the [Ru(bpy)3] unit in II is quenched by rapid energy transfer to the cyclometallated Ru(II) unit by the Dexter mechanism.131 PEnT in the Ru(II)–Ru(II)-acceptor triad III, is followed by electron transfer to form a Ru(II)–Ru(III)+-acceptor˙− charge-separated state.132 The emitting states of homonuclear (M = M′ = Ru or Os) complexes of [(bpy)2M(L20)M′(bpy)2]4+ are 3MLCT in nature, and largely delocalised over the oligomeric ligand. In the heterodinuclear complex (M = Ru, M′ = Os), efficient Ru → Os energy-transfer occurs via a double electron exchange (Dexter) mechanism mediated by the oligomeric bridge.133 A supramolecular assembly consisting of a Ru(II) bpy-cyclodextrin based junction with anthracene and Os(II) terpy guest molecules has been synthesised; excitation of the anthracene moiety initiates an energy transfer cascade process ultimately resulting in the formation of a Os-based triplet excited state.134

Red-light emitting devices of di- and tri-nuclear Ru(II) complexes based on linear and star shaped 2-(2′-pyridyl)benzimidazolyl derivative ligands have been achieved with maximum luminance being 133 cd m−2.135 The photophysical properties of a family of mono- and di-nuclear Ru(II) complexes containing 5-aryltetrazolate ligands have been investigated, and exceptionally high electrochemiluminescence efficiency (150% greater than [Ru(bpy)3]2+) was observed for [{Ru(terpy)(bpy)}2(L21)].136

Rhodium and iridium complexes

Ir(III) complexes have an increased ligand-field stabilisation energy in comparison to Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes and, as a consequence, a less thermally accessible non-emissive triplet metal-centred state. This results in long-lived and intense emission for Ir(III) complexes containing polypyridyl (N,N-donor) or cyclometallating (N,C-donor) ligands, that can be tuned over the whole visible region by varying ligand substituents. This makes them particularly attractive as candidates for application in LEDs,137–142 luminescent sensors,143,144 and as photosensitisers in DSSCs.145 The emission wavelength of mononuclear Ir(III) complexes has been tuned by incorporating quinoline-based N,N-donor ligands,146 quinoline-based N,C-donor ligands,147 and a variety of benzimidazoyl substituted terpy-derivatives having N,C,N and N,N,N-donors.148

X-ray excited optical luminescence and time-resolved X-ray excited optical luminescence techniques were used to probe the excited states of neat [Ir(ppy)3] films. The excited state lifetimes of the films are shorter than observed in solution.149 Simultaneous two-photon excited fluorescence and one-photon excited phosphorescence was observed from thin films of [Ir(ppy)3] dispersed in poly(methyl methacrylate) upon excitation at 438 nm.150 Selective excitation of [Ir(ppy)3] results in upconverted singlet fluorescence from pyrene or 3,8-di-tert-butylpyrene by triplet–triplet annihilation.151

A rapid combinatorial synthesis and screening methodology using resin bound leads was successfully applied for the discovery of Ir(III) complexes displaying red emission in electroluminescence devices.152


The rhodium centred mixed-metal supramolecular complex [{(bpy)2Ru(L22)}2RhCl2]5+ undergoes photoinitiated electron collection at the rhodium centre. Photoreduction of the complex occurs in the presence of an electron donor to generate the two-electron reduced form [{(bpy)2Ru(L22)}2Rh]5+.153 A dirhodium carboxylate bound to a methyl methacrylate/methacrylic acid copolymer having axially coordinated fluorophores (N-dansylpiperazine) acts as a turn-on fluorescent sensor in the presence of NO under biologically relevant conditions.154

Platinum complexes

A series of luminescent cyclometallated Pt(II) complexes of formula [Pt(ppy-4-styryl-R)(OCCR′CHCR′CO)] (R′ = CH3, tert-butyl; R = H, OMe, NEt2, NO2), are weakly emissive in solution due to competitive quenching via E–Z photoisomerisation of the styryl ligand.155 Diimine square-planar [Pt(L23)(X)] (X = 1,2-dithiooxalate, Cl2, terminal alkyne) complexes emit at wavelengths between 777 and 800 nm, with DFT calculations indicating that the excited-state responsible for emission is best described as “mixed metal–ligand-to-ligand charge transfer”.156 The photophysical properties of a series of structurally similar diimine Pt(II) complexes could be significantly tuned using bulky bis- or di-coordinated thiolate and phenolate ligands.157 Planarisation and rigidity introduced by the cyclic diacetylide in [Pt(dbbpy)(L24)] leads to enhanced photophysical properties relative to the analogous complex [Pt(dbbpy)(CCPh)2].158 Dinuclear [Pt(CCPh-nBu)2(L25)] complexes, with a long enough flexible linker, undergo intramolecular self-association at low temperatures due to Pt⋯Pt and π−π interactions, and display a corresponding increase in the emission intensity from a triplet metal–metal-to-ligand charge transfer state.159

Phosphorescent emission from the pincer complexes IV and V originates from 3MLCT and 3(π*π) excited states, respectively.160 Luminescence from the trimetallic complex [{Mn(NCS)(bpy)2(μ-SCN)}2Pt(SCN)2] originates from a Pt(II) d–d transition; the band maximum shows a red shift of −99 cm−1 kbar−1 under external pressure of up to 32 kbar at room temperature.161

One-dimensional micro- and nano-metre-scale luminescent wires formed from [Pt(CNtBu)2(CN)2] molecules with weak Pt⋯Pt interactions display intense green 3(5dσ*, 6pσ) emission.162 Similar excited-states are responsible for the luminescence observed in organometallic polymers comprising of the bimetallic fragments [M2(dppm)2(CN–R–NC)] (M = Pt, Pd; R = aryl or alkyl), with excited-state lifetimes of 3–6 ns for the Pd, species and 3–4 μs for Pt.163 Electronic spectral studies of oligomeric Pt(II) alkynyls [(HCCRCC){Pt(dppe)(CCRCC)}nH] (R = phenyl, diphenyl; n = 1, 2, 3) indicate that, upon increasing the chain length, the optical band gap decreases, but the triplet-state emission intensity increases.164 The complexes [Pt(C6H5)2(L26)] each have two polymorphs that display distinct electronic absorption and emission spectra, due to different intermolecular π–π and Pt⋯H interactions.165


Heteronuclear metallopolyynes of formula [M(CCRCC)M′(CCRCC)]n (M = Hg, M′ = {Pt(PBu3)2}, R = 9,9′-dioctylfluorene) show substantial optical-power limiting responses with good optical transparency in the visible light region.166 Tweezer-type Pt(II)–Cd(II) complexes [{cis-Pt(C6F5)2(CCR)2}CdCl2]2− and [{cis-Pt(C6F5)2(CCR)2}2Cd]2− (R = tBu, Ph, tolyl) exhibit dual emission from alkynyl 3LC and Pt/alkynyl 3MLCT states for R = tolyl.167 Dinuclear [{PtTl2(CCR)4}2] (R = 4-CH3-C6H4, 1-naphthyl) and polymeric [{PtTl2(CC-C6H4-4-CF3)4}2] complexes display blue emission in solution and orange emission in the solid-state, attributed to aggregation through Tl⋯π(alkynyl) interactions in the solid-state.168 The one-dimensional polymer [{Pt(C6F5)4}2Tl]−n and the corresponding [(NBu4)(PF6)] adduct display luminescence thermochromism in frozen solutions, and emissions that are strongly dependent on concentration, solvent and excitation wavelength.169 The complex [Pt(L)(NHCOtBu)2] (L = 1,2-diaminocyclohexane) forms intensely luminescent polymers upon reaction with Tl(I) ions, that have emission maxima ranging between 467 and 520 nm at room temperature depending on the anion used.170

The luminescent bimetallic species [{Pt(NC)(C6X5)2}Ag(PPh3)] (NC = 7,8-benzoquinolate) exhibit two, close, distinct, structured emissions with short and long lifetimes (X = F, τ = 12.4 and 278 μs; X = Cl, τ = 21.2 and 206 μs), attributed to 3MLCT and 3LC emissive states.171 A series of PtM, PtM2 and Pt2M3 (M = Cu, Ag, Au) complexes formed by reaction of [Pt(CCR)4]2− (R = alkyl or aryl) and [M2(μ-dppm)2]2+ display decreasing emission energies with increasing donor ability of R, implying that the emissive states originate from ligand-to-cluster [RCC → PtM] LMCT transitions.172

A series of phosphorescent Pt(II) complexes containing alkyl substituted 2-thienylpyridine cyclometallating ligands act as dark-field turn-on optical chemosensors. Cyanogen halides oxidatively add to the Pt(II) complexes to give Pt(IV) products that exhibit significantly blue-shifted emission spectra.173 The imidoylamidinate Pt(II) compounds [Pt{NHC(R)NC(Ph)NPh}2] (R=CH2Ph, p-Cl–C6H4, Ph) have microsecond emission lifetimes and exhibit pH-dependent room temperature luminescence, with emission quenching observed at low pH.174

[Pt(CCPh)(4′-p-tolyl-terpy)]+ displays a broad MLCT transition between 375 and 420 nm and strong photoluminescence in the range 500–800 nm. The Pt(II) complex acts as a chromophore in the photocatalytic generation of hydrogen from water in the presence of an electron transfer donor (triethanolamine), electron transfer acceptor (methyl viologen), and colloidal Pt catalyst.175 Blue-violet absorbing thienyl-acetylide Pt(II) polymers [Pt(PBu3)2(CC–SC4H2–CC)]n, blended with fullerene, can serve as the active material in photovoltaic devices with a reasonable overall power conversion efficiency of up to 0.27%. Photoinduced charge separation of the blends was thought to occur via the triplet excited state of the organometallic polymer.176 A series of luminescent Pt(II) azolate (N∧N) complexes of formula [Pt(N∧N)2] are suitable phosphorescent dopants for OLED applications. The best OLED device performance was achieved with the 3-trifluoromethyl-5-(2-pyridyl) pyrazole Pt(II) complex with a 20% dopant concentration, having an external quantum efficiency of 6.0%, and a luminous efficiency of 19.7 cd/A at 20 mA/cm2.177 OLED devices incorporating phosphorescent complexes VI exhibit good device performance and tuneable colour emission by varying the aryl ring substituents. Single dopant white-light electroluminescence was also achieved through variation of the dopant concentration and the driving voltage.178

Metalloporphyrin complexes

Meso-to-meso ethyne-bridged (porphinato)Zn(II) oligomers are low band gap materials with intense, tunable excited state absorptivity and large NIR fluorescence quantum yields that have potential application in a range of photonic applications.179 The photophysical properties of the fluorescent S2 and S1 excited states in a range of Zn(II) porphyrin complexes were investigated using steady-state absorption and fluorescence spectrometry, with time-dependent DFT calculations employed to help interpret the results.180 Fluorescence from Zn-imidazolyl-porphyrin dimers, functionalised in the meso position with ferrocene, is quenched according to the degree of electronic coupling between the ferrocene and porphyrin.181 A bis(Zn-porphyrin) tweezer, with anthracene components as the apex and side-arms, acts as a host for a series of dipyridylporphyrin guests. PEnT from the host to the guest is nearly quantitative (95–98%) with rates of transfer consistent with a Förster mechanism having a reduced orientation factor.182 Time-resolved transient absorption and fluorescence anisotropy decays were examined for various hexaarylbenzene anchored polyester Zn(II) porphyrin dendrimers. The more densely packed dendrimers enhanced the efficiency of PEnT processes to give effective light-harvesting systems.183 Singlet–singlet and triplet–triplet PEnT was observed in a nonameric porphyrin assembly from eight peripheral zinc porphyrins to a central free base that is connected by nucleosidic linkers.184 Macrocyclic arrays of m-bis(ethylene)phenylene-linked Zn(II) imidazolylporphyrins display shorter excitation energy hopping times for the hexagonal array (12.8 ps) compared to the pentagonal array (21 ps) due to a better orientation of transition dipoles.185 Butterfly-type molecular construct VII displays a PEnT process from the central free base to the Zn-porphyrin. Metallation of the central terpy ligand (VIII) results in a change of the molecular conformation from “W” to “U”, and strong quenching of the Zn wing luminescence due to heavy atom effects and fast electron-transfer to the ground state of the “wing”.186 The photophysical properties of ethyne-bridged (porphyrin)Zn(II)–(porphyrin)Fe(III) complexes have been studied in detail, and unusually long excited-state lifetimes were observed for Fe-porphyrin pentacoordinate complexes.187

PEnT in oligothiophene linked Zn(II) porphyrins changes from a Förster mechanism mediating the singlet–singlet energy transfer to a Dexter mechanism, if the bridge has sufficiently low lying energy levels.188 Experimental and theoretical methods showed that singlet energy transfer in Zn(II) porphyrin (donor), p-phenyleneethynylene (bridge), free porphyrin (acceptor) systems occurred by both Förster and Dexter mechanisms.189 PET and PEnT processes in directly linked Zn(II) porphyrin–Zn(II) phthalocyanine heterodyads were studied. Charge-separated states that were formed with a 93% efficiency have high free energy and long lifetimes, that make them good candidates for use in photovoltaic cell applications.190 A giant 24-mer porphyrin wheel comprising of six meso–meso-linked tetra-Zn(II)-porphyrins, with a diameter of approximately 7 nm, exhibits an efficient excitation energy hopping rate of (35.1 ps)−1 around the wheel.191

A molecular triad comprising of two free-base porphyrins linked by a meso-phenylene [Ru(terpy)2]2+ bridge displays PEnT from the Ru-3MLCT state to the lowest energy π–π* triplet state localised on the porphyrins. Selective illumination also results in PEnT from the lowest energy singlet excited state on the porphyrin to the Ru-3MLCT state.192 The 3MLCT excited state of [Ru(terpy)(bpy)]+ fragments attached to tetra(pyridyl)porphyrin is effectively quenched by electron transfer to the porphyrin at room temperature, but electron transfer is not efficient at low temperature and emission from the Ru-3MLCT is observed.193

Sn(IV) complexes of N-confused porphyrins and oxoporphyrins bind halide ions at NH sites and metal axial positions, respectively, with pronounced fluorescence enhancement that means the complexes can be regarded as turn-on fluorescent halide sensors.194 The expanded porphyrin, meso-pentafluorophenyl[26]hexaphyrin-(1.1.1.1.1.0), acts as a highly sensitive near-IR-fluorescent chemodosimeter selective for the detection of Hg(II) ions.195 Luminescent alkyl-carbazole substituted platinum porphyrin complexes act as highly sensitive and fast-responding oxygen sensing materials, with luminescence intensity quenching of up to 86% in the presence of a 0.1% oxygen concentration.196 A photoanode composed of a Co(II) phthalocyanine/organic p-type semiconductor bilayer acts as a stable photocatalyst over a wide range of wavelengths for O2 formation from aqueous alkaline solutions.197

A quadruply azulene fused Ni(II) porphyrin complex has a very effective π-electron conjugation pathway throughout the porphyrin moiety, with an intense near-IR absorption and a large two-photon absorption cross-section.198 Electropolymerised thin-films of Fe(III) and Mn(III) porphyrin derivatives have second hyperpolarisability and two-photon absorption cross-sections that are enhanced by at least an order of magnitude in comparison to those of the free base porphyrin.199

The spectroscopic characteristics of the radical form of a tetraarylporphycene, and its Pd(II) complex, follow the π-radical pattern found in alkyl substituted porphycene; both the radical anions and the radical cations absorb at longer wavelengths than the triplet states.200 Ultrafast charge separation in Mg and Zn porphyrins, covalently linked via an amide bond to [Re(bpy)(CO)3] units, occurs upon excitation of the porphyrin, with charge-separated state IR bands appearing on a time scale of less than 1 ps (Mg) and ∼5 ps (Zn).201

Photophysical studies on a fullerene derivative containing a Zn-porphyrin and a second generation liquid-crystalline dendrimer, revealed quenching of the Zn-porphyrin fluorescence due to PET and formation of a radical ion pair.202 Efficient (ϕ = 0.97) photoinduced charge separation in a donor–acceptor dyad consisting of a Zn(II) N-confused porphyrin donor, and fullerene acceptor, was shown using DFT calculations and nanosecond transient absorption spectra measurements.203 PEnT is responsible for fluorescence quenching of Zn(II)-porphyrins attached to pyridyl isoxazolino functionalised single-wall carbon nanotubes, and emission from the carbon nanotube is observed upon excitation of the Zn(II)-porphyrin. Steady-state fluorescence and nanosecond transient spectroscopy measurements provide no evidence for electron transfer, in contrast to the PET behaviour observed in related Zn(II)-porphyrin/C60 dyads.204

Lanthanide complexes

Lanthanide complexes containing derivatives of the ‘cyclen’-based macrocylic ligand ‘DO3A’ (L27) continue to attract attention. Nd(III) and Yb(III) complexes of asymmetrically substituted DO3A derivatives having an antenna (rhodamine B) and quencher (nucleoside) attached to the cyclen scaffold have been prepared. Chemical switching of the lanthanide-based NIR emission is observed upon addition of a complementary base (adenosine or uridine) to the nucleoside.205 The interaction between lanthanide DO3A complexes, and chromophore bearing phosphonates or carboxylates, was used to probe the effect of sensitising chromophore choice on NIR emission.206 Sensitised NIR emission was observed in Yb(III) or Nd(III) tetraamide-functionalised cyclen complexes, bearing a single 1,10-phenanthroline moiety coordinated to a {Ru(bpy)2} fragment, upon excitation of either the ligand or MLCT transition.207 A Eu(III) DO3A complex functionalised with a tris-pyridyl-amine binding site was used as a sensor for Zn(II) ions, with signalling observed from changes in the emission intensity and lifetimes.208 A Tb(III) DO3A complex containing a tuftsin target for macrophage cells shows promise as a luminescent probe for biological assaying.209 The coordinatively unsaturated dinuclear Tb(III) complex of a p-xylene-bridged DO3A derivative exhibits enhancement of the Tb(III) emission, upon titration of mono- and di-carboxylate anions.210

The sensitisation mechanism for luminescent heterocyclic β-diketonate europium complexes, [Eu(L28)3·Ln] (L = H2O, EtOH), involves the transfer of energy from a ligand triplet state to the Eu(III) ion. The substitution of the solvent molecules by bidentate NN ligands (NN = bpy, 4,4′-dimethoxy-bpy, phen, 4,7-diphenyl-phen),211 ureasil212 or tri-n-octylphosphine oxide,213 enhances the metal-centred luminescence lifetimes and quantum yields. The β-diketonate complexes [Ln(L29)4]− (Ln = Nd, Er) produce sensitised NIR luminescence, via energy transfer from the singlet excited state of anthracene to the lanthanide ion, with lifetimes in the microsecond range. Intramolecular energy transfer to the Ln(III) ion was less efficient for Er, thought to be due to a larger energy gap between the singlet anthracene state and emitting level of the Er(III) ion.214 A series of NIR emitting Nd(III) complexes containing β-diketonate ligands with various electron withdrawing and donating groups attached were characterised, and the relationship between the energy of the ligand triplet state and photophysical properties discussed.215 The stable and highly luminescent Eu(III) complex [Eu(L30)2]− has a very intense 5D0 → 7F2 hypersensitive transition and consequently an exceptional quantum yield (ϕ = 0.215 ± 0.03) with almost pure red (λem = 612 nm) and long-lived (τ = 727 μs) luminescence was observed in aqueous solutions due to protection of the metal from deactivation by solvent.216 Sulfur bridged binaphtholate chromophores in MS[Ln(L31)(OO)2] and MS2[Ln(L31)2(OO)] (OO = di-tert-butyl β-diketonate; M = K, Li; S = solvent) sensitise emission for the Eu(III) and Tb(III) complexes, with relatively high luminescent quantum yields.217 Luminescence sensitisation of Eu(III) pyridine-2,6-dicarboxylate complexes was found to increase upon substitution at the pyridine 4 position in the order Cl > H > OH.218


The p-tert-butylsulfonylcalix[4]arene ligand acts as a good antenna chromophore for UV and near-UV light. The sensitised emission from Eu(III) and Tb(III) complexes is dependent on the energy of the ligand singlet and triplet excited states, which can be modified by slight conformational changes of the calixarene.219 Calix[4]azacrowns capped with aminpolyamide bridges also act as organic antenna chromophores for energy-transfer sensitisation of luminescent excited states of Ln(III) ions.220

Luminescent [Pt2(μ-dppm)2(CC–NN)4] (NN = bpy, phen) chromophores gave a series of Pt2Ln2 and Pt2Ln4 arrays, in which the excitation of the d(Pt) → π*(RCC) MLCT absorption induces sensitisation of the lanthanide luminescence through efficient d → f energy transfer.221 Photophysical and computational studies on heterobimetallic complexes of general formula [Zn(μ-L32)Ln(NO)3(S)n] (S = H2O, EtOH; n = 1, 2) showed that sensitisation of lanthanide luminescence occurs via singlet energy transfer from the Schiff base 1LC excited state.222


The luminescent behaviour of lanthanide ion complexes has been exploited in a number of sensor applications and as potential biomedical probes. The detection sensitivity of luminescence quenching of a Tb(III) complex containing a carbostyril chromophore by 4-hydroxy-TEMPO was improved by using time-gated detection, a concept that can be applied to other luminescent sensors.223 Eu(III) complexes containing derivatives of highly fluorescent 2,6-bis(benzimidazolyl)pyridine ligands have been used as sensitive luminescent sensors for the detection of chemical-warfare-agent mimics.224 A Eu(III) luminescence probe containing terpy-based 9-methylanthracene ligand is almost nonluminescent due to 3terpy → 3anthracene PEnT. The anthracene excited state specifically reacts with 1O2 to form the endoperoxide, which stops intraligand PEnT, dramatically enhancing 3terpy → Eu sensitised luminescence. The utility of the probe for in vivo1O2 detection was shown using time-gated luminescence imaging to monitor time-dependent 1O2 generation in living cells.225 A picolinate ligand derived from 1,4,7-triazacyclononane efficiently sensitises the Tb(III) ion, leading to a long luminescence lifetime and high luminescence quantum yield. This, combined with water solubility and physiological stability, indicates that the Tb(III) complex is suitable for adaptation into luminescent labels for biomedical applications.226 Polydentate ligands having Hq subunits attached to a tetraamine-1,2-ethylenediamine framework form water stable complexes with a series of lanthanide ions (Nd, Yb, Eu, La, Er, Lu). The polydentate ligands are effective at sensitising NIR luminescence, making them promising candidates for the development of luminescent bioprobes.227

Dinuclear complexes of Eu(III) β-diketonates bridged by a dpym ligand, that also serves as a sensitiser, were successfully incorporated into electroluminescent devices that produce stable pure red emission from the Eu(III) ion.228 Interpenetration of a Nd(III) β-diketonates stoppered imidazole thread with a macrocycle containing a Re(I) bpy sensitiser to form a pseudorotaxane complex, is signalled via sensitised NIR lanthanide emission.229 Heterotrimetallic helicates [M–Ln–M] (M = Cr, Zn; Ln = Eu, Tb) were synthesised using segmental bidentate-tridentate-bidentate benzimidazoyl pyridine-based ligands. For M = Zn, only lanthanide-centred emission was observed, but Cr(III) complexes displayed evidence of Ln → Cr energy transfer.230 The cocrystalline complex [Yb0.5Er0.5(OO)3(OPPh3)2] (OO = hexafluoroacetylacetonate) exhibits sensitised NIR erbium emission with intermolecular energy transfer from the ytterbium complex modelled by the Förster mechanism.231


The host–guest assembly [Eu ⊂ Na6Eu8(L33)16] exhibits Eu(III) luminescence with a substantial quantum yield (32%), while a similar mixed Eu(III)–Yb(III) cluster exhibits dual Eu(III)- and Yb(III)-centred luminescence.232 Tb(III) complexes containing polyaryl dendrimers of β-diketonate ligands have overall quantum yields that rise from 0.1 to 2.26% as the dendritic generation increases from 0 to 3. An “energy-reservoir effect” was observed, that is as the generation increased the energy transfer from the dendritic ligand to the Tb(III) increased, whilst the metal-centred quantum yield was almost the same.233

The luminescent properties of lanthanide ions is being exploited in materials and metal-organic frameworks. Incorporation of Tb(III) and Eu(III) ions, along with benzophenone or 4-acetylbiphenyl sensitisers, into zeolites results in red, green or blue photoluminescence, with the ratio of the three components used to tune the emission colour.234 The rods formed from [Gd(BDC)1.5(H2O)2] (BDC = terephthalate) metal-organic frameworks could be tuned in length from ∼100 nm to ∼1 μm.
