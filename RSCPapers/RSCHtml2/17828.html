Abstract

Two step metal oxide thermochemical redox cycles have seen growing interest in the research community over the last two decades. In particular, they have often been studied as a means of converting heat from concentrated solar power to chemical energy, which can subsequently be used for a number of applications. In this work, we offer critical perspective and valuable insight on these research fields, from authors with a combined experience of 50+ years in metal oxide redox cycles. The current fundamental understanding of thermochemical redox materials, and the implications and limitations this has on the redox thermodynamics are discussed. The underlying fundamentals of the redox materials are then used to give insight into the theoretical limitations imposed on a number of applications including; solar thermochemical fuel production, solar energy storage for off sun power generation, thermochemical air separation, oxygen pumping, and thermochemical heat pumping. A number of recent novel process developments are also presented, which offer valuable motivation and direction for the respective fields. The analysis shows that oxides which undergo a stoichiometric phase change during reduction, such as ZnO or Co3O4, have much larger specific energy storage than materials undergoing partial reduction such as ceria and perovskites. The partial reduction materials generally have faster kinetics and better activity at low temperature, and the selection of materials for the various applications is often a compromise between the importance of high specific energy storage vs. fast kinetics and low temperature operation.

Such cycles can be combined with concentrated solar power (CSP), offering a means of converting solar energy to chemical energy, via a reduction reaction.

The stored energy can then be used for a number of applications, including; as heat storage for off sun electricity generation in CSP power plants, as a chemical potential for CO2 and H2O splitting providing fuel, and for air separation and oxygen pumping via the absorption of oxygen. The oxide oxygen affinity (E) and operational temperatures depend on the target application.

For a number of years these cycles have been investigated as a possible route to solar fuels, where the reduced metal oxide can be used to split H2O and CO2,15 which has been demonstrated to offer a route to renewable liquid hydrocarbon fuels.6 Another common area of research for these cycles is that of energy storage, where the reduced oxide offers a high specific energy storage, which could be used for off-sun power generation in CSP plants.79

Redox cycles have been used for many years in chemical looping combustion (CLC), where the oxide is chemically reduced using a fuel and then re-oxidised in air, which allows for a cleaner (no NOx) lower temperature fuel combustion. More recently these cycles have been proposed and investigated as an oxygen absorber for thermochemical air separation,1012 with the possibility of producing both oxygen and nitrogen. Oxygen production via this method has recently been applied in chemical-looping combustion with oxygen uncoupling (CLOU), where the redox oxide is thermally reduced releasing gas-phase oxygen to a solid fuel reactor,13,14 allowing for a pure oxygen burn and CO2 capture similiar to CLC. The production of nitrogen via a CSP powered air separation redox cycle, could also be an inherently sustainable alternative to the ThomsonLinde process,15 where air is separated by cryogenic distillation. Using this process as a nitrogen source would be particularly relevant for the production of ammonia (NH3) via the HaberBosch process, an industry which is attributed to at least 1% of worldwide primary energy consumption.16

A very recent publication has also highlighted the high potential of using such a cycle to pump oxygen to low partial pressures,17 which can be beneficial in a number of applications, including thermochemical fuel production and membrane reactors.18,19 This method of pumping could avoid much of the energy sapping losses of mechanical vacuum pumping (friction losses and leakage), and also has the potential for a much higher throughput than established oxygen selective electrochemical membrane pumps.20

A simple reverse of this oxygen pumping method allows the cycle to be used as a heat pump, which has recently been suggested by Strhle et al. as a means of upgrading the heat stored in thermochemical energy storage systems.21

All of the above applications rely on the choice of a suitable redox material for the cycle, which should be optimised for the given application. Although the best choice can be different in each case, they have commonalities in the type of redox thermodynamics and material properties that are beneficial, and thus the selection of the correct redox cycle in each case is subject to similar boundary conditions. The thermodynamics are the first and most critical point which should be considered when trying to optimise materials or select conditions for two-step thermochemical redox cycles. If the thermodynamics are promising, the application may still have problems such as kinetic restrictions, material degradation, etc. Conversely, if the thermodynamics are not promising then it is certain that the practical implementation will be bound by this restriction.

Here we aim to offer a broad view of what is known in terms of fundamental material thermodynamics, and how best to use this knowledge when selecting and developing new materials for a given application. In addition, we discuss some new breakthroughs and recent novelties proposed in the respective fields. Overall, this work should offer a very accessible roadmap for researchers to assess and further develop these promising technologies.

It should be noted that these materials can also undergo phase change reduction, such as the reduction of CeO2 to Ce2O3 under very strong reducing conditions,42,43 but here we focus on the regions of partial reduction.

Ceria can form solid solutions with other metal oxides, such as ZrO2, which also exhibit partial reduction properties.44,45 The perovskite structure on the other hand can be attained using a huge variety of A and M site ions.4650 This offers the ability to essentially tune the redox thermodynamics to suit a given application, which is illustrated in Fig. 4.

Applications

When trying to narrow down the redox materials for specific applications there are a number of critical points to be considered. A thermochemical redox cycle can be broken down into two steps, the reduction and the oxidation. The reduction step essentially stores energy which can then be utilised in the oxidation step. The amount of energy which is stored in the reduction step is therefore of utmost importance. Another key property for cycles which involve a temperature swing, is the thermal mass which must be cycled for a given energy storage. Both of these properties are illustrated in Fig. 7, where the most striking point, is that partial reduction materials have much lower energy storage (particularly pronounced when considering similar hO, and reduction conditions) compared to phase change reactions, as one might expect due to the small stoichiometric changes.

The thermal mass is considered to be approximately proportional to the moles of atoms, according to the Dulong Petit Law. For partial reduction oxides the storage is reported for certain stoichiometry changes, i.e. CeO2/1.9. There are other benefits to the partial reduction materials, such as fast kinetics and flexibility in the reaction conditions. The high ionic conductivity of these materials, and the lack of an overall phase change during the reaction, means that rapid kinetics are observed, even at low temperatures (<300 C).60,66,78,79 They also exhibit variable reduction extent over a large range of temperatures and pressures, which is convenient in terms of research output, as one almost always has a result, as opposed to phase change oxides which require more specific conditions for the reactions to proceed.

When trying to select materials for certain applications one should consider the temperatures and partial pressures that can be considered suitable, as well as the function of the material, i.e. are fast kinetics and the flexibility of operating conditions preferable to overall energy storage. In the following discussions we give a perspective on the rationale behind selecting redox cycles for a number of applications, with a focus on using the so far discussed properties of the many available redox materials, to narrow down the promising options for each application.

Energy storage

The simplest and most obvious application of a thermochemical cycle is to store energy in the reduced metal oxide. This is of particular interest for concentrated solar power plants, where they could be used to store heat for off sun operation. The state of the art for heat storage in these plants is to use solid to liquid phase change materials such as molten salts,80,81 which are limited to temperatures of around 600 C. A thermochemical redox cycle on the other hand can have higher specific energy storage, and store the heat at higher temperatures, allowing for more efficient electricity production.

In terms of theoretical limitations on this technology, all of the heat that is stored as sensible and chemical heat can be recovered, and the efficiency of such a system has no theoretical upper bound. In practice however, there would be heat losses, and exergy losses if the re-oxidation takes place at lower temperature than the reduction. If the heat is used to produce power, then the power production is of-course bounded by the Carnot efficiency. The important thermodynamic properties for this application are shown graphically in Fig. 3, 6 and 7.

Phase change redox materials are the obvious choice here as they have high specific energy storage, as can be seen in Fig. 7. However, the cycles which have very high specific energy storage (ZnO/Zn, NiO/Ni, Fe3O4/FeO etc.), require excessive temperatures for this application according to Fig. 3. The selection then becomes a compromise between specific energy storage and the temperatures required for reduction given by equation. In this case it would be more reasonable to store heat at temperatures in the region of 5001100 C and the reduction should ideally take place in air (pO2 = 0.2 bar). In this way the cycle can be driven using a hot air stream from a volumetric receiver like e.g. in the 1.5 MWel Solar Tower Jlich (STJ) plant in Germany.82,83 In the case of phase change redox materials the energy is stored at a specific temperature. For example in an air atmosphere Co3O4 will undergo reduction at approximately 870 C and can be re-oxidised at just a few degrees below that.7

Looking at Fig. 3 and 7, suggests that amongst the simple binary oxides Co3O4/CoO, BaO2/BaO (similar specific energy storage to Co3O4/CoO) and at higher temperatures CuO/Cu2O, are promising candidates. Indeed, these materials have all been investigated experimentally. The CuO/Cu2O system is limited by the fact that the reduction temperature is high enough to cause extensive sintering and shrinkage of the material during cycling.84,85 In the BaO2/BaO system, the reduced phase reacts even with traces of moisture and/or CO2 present in the surrounding atmosphere to hydrated barium hydroxides and barium carbonate respectively,8587 a fact severely limiting its potential implementation under realistic on-site conditions. On the other hand, the Co3O4/CoO system exhibits high energy density and undergoes stoichiometric, cyclic reductionoxidation under a variety of heating/cooling rates and within a narrow temperature range.88

Furthermore, the currently employed state-of-the-art, regenerative sensible heat storage employed at STJ,89 where excess solar energy during on-sun operation is stored as sensible heat in porous ceramic honeycombs that function as recuperators during off-sun operation,90,91 led to the idea of employing similar ceramic honeycombs and/or foams coated with,9294 or manufactured entirely from Co3O4,9598 for hybrid sensible-thermochemical storage of solar heat. A demonstration of this approach has been carried out at STJ, culminating in a 74 kW hth pilot-scale TCS unit composed of 90 kg of Co3O4 coated on cordierite honeycombs, which clearly showed temperature effects due to chemical reactions.99101 Preliminary techno-economic evaluations indicated that the concept is promising with the main challenge being to identify active materials with a lower cost than Co3O4, but at the same time comparable to it in terms of energy density, cyclability and redox kinetics. The most likely candidates to replace cobalt oxide are mixed oxide systems, such as the FeMn redox system.29

This energy storage concept can be further elaborated in using a cascade of redox materials for combined sensible and chemical heat storage. In this case the redox materials can be arranged in order of their reduction temperatures, allowing more heat to be utilized from the hot air stream from a volumetric receiver.102 This idea is illustrated in Fig. 8, and has already been tested in lab-scale.85,94

Very recently, many researchers have also investigated perovskites for this application.103107 From Fig. 7, we can see that perovskite oxides have low specific chemical energy storage, which is a result of their partial reduction properties. Therefore, hope among researchers should be tempered, and the rationale for investigating these materials should be carefully considered. Perovskites do have reasonably high sensible heat storage,108,109 cycling stability,107 and they can be synthesised from abundant materials.46 As suggested in many of these studies they could then offer a combined sensible and chemical storage option where the reduction reaction offers a boost in the specific energy storage density. However, it remains to be seen if such a concept could be cost effective compared to a purely sensible storage system utilising a cheap storage media such as naturally occuring rocks.110

In the case of combined chemical and sensible storage using a cascade, as illustrated in Fig. 8, perovskites may also be of interest at lower temperatures, as they show redox activity down to 300400 C.52,60 Perovskites alone do not offer high specific chemical energy storage, but a cascade using both phase change reduction and perovskites at lower temperatures may have promising properties in terms of utilization of the hot air stream.

Having ruled out many cycles, we should next consider the specific and molar energy storage, the latter of which is inversely proportional to the energy required to heat the oxide during the cycle. From Fig. 7, it is clear that the ZnO/Zn cycle has by far the superior thermodynamics. It has very high specific and molar energy storage, and if both zinc and oxygen gas pressures are decreased, then the reduction temperature decreases rapidly. In reflection of this, there has been extensive research into this cycle. An excellent review of some of these efforts has been published by Koepf et al..112

However, the ZnO/Zn cycle has not yet been developed to a stage where it could be industrially applied. One of the main problems which hinders the practicality and the efficiency of this process, is that a sweep gas, such as argon, is used to carry away the products (also reducing pO2 and pZn, and thus the reduction temperature according to Fig. 3), which must then be quenched to avoid a back reaction. This quenching is then followed by a separation process to remove the oxygen and regenerate the sweep gas. This separation process is very energy intensive, and a reasonable techno-economic analysis predicted a hydrogen cost of 50$ per kg,112 which is not competitive.

Fe3O4/FeO and AxFeO1x ferrites are also promising candidates, with an excellent perspective on these cycles given by Miller et al.28 They show that partial reduction can be achieved at lower temperatures than those seen in Fig. 3 for Fe3O4/FeO, which can be explained by the formation of solution phases of the reactants and products. However, the reduction still takes place at temperatures above the melting point of FeO, introducing significant cycling challenges. As an alternative, ferrites have been investigated, most notably NiFe2O4, where the products have a higher melting point, and they allow partial reduction to take place at lower temperatures as seen in Fig. 6.113,114 The partial reduction of NiFe2O4 inevitably gives low specific energy storage as seen in Fig. 7 (NiFe2O4/3.8), and questions remain over the cyclability.28 Furthermore, this cycle requires low temperatures (<700 C) for a reasonable fuel conversion ratio (eqn (26)),28 but in practice the oxidation temperature is often set to values in the region of 1000 C for kinetic reasons, with very low experimental fuel conversion ratios, peaking at around .115 This low conversion means that a lot of energy must be used to produce excess H2O or CO2 for the oxidation reaction, and in the case of CO2 one must somehow separate a small amount of CO from the product stream.

With the challenges faced in the above phase change cycles, many researchers have focused their efforts on partial reduction materials, in particular ceria based oxides.1,6,42,116118 A quick look at the specific energy storage (Fig. 7) and the temperature and pressure conditions required to reach a reduction extent of CeO1.9 (Fig. 6), show that its thermodynamics are far from ideal. One must heat ceria to high temperatures at low oxygen partial pressures, to achieve a specific energy storage comparable to a rechargeable Ni-MH battery. However, in terms of experimental investigation and practical implementation, the ceria cycle does have many benefits. The lack of a phase change means that it is suitable for both fixed bed and particle fed reactors, and it's partial reduction properties give flexibility in the operating conditions.

Extensive studies have been dedicated to improving ceria by adding other metal ions such as Zr and Hf into the lattice.119123 The goal of such studies is often to reduce the reduction temperature required, which can be achieved by either reducing the enthalpy of reduction or increasing the entropy. Increasing the entropy is not likely, as pure ceria has a very high configuration entropy term and the addition of other metal cations into the lattice only leads to preferential or fewer overall configurations, and thus reduces the change in entropy. The addition of Zr4+ into ceria for example, improves the reducibility, by decreasing the enthalpy of reduction, but also decreases the entropy of reduction.68,77

The critical point from these studies is that lower hrd saves on the reduction energy (and conditions) but one must pay for this in the oxidation step by heating excess steam, which is a thermodynamic limitation of this process.77,124126 This limitation should always be considered when searching for new materials, where materials with improved reducibility do not necessarily improve the process efficiency. Unfortunately, research efforts typically take the approach of searching for oxides which are more easily reduced, and usually by decreasing hrd. This often leads to claims of new improved materials, which are later disregarded, when not just the yield, but also the oxidant to fuel conversion ratio are considered.

Rather than decreasing the enthalpy of reduction hrd, a more interesting materials improvement method might be to try and increase srd. Unfortunately, this change in entropy is limited by the type of reaction taking place. Larger changes in entropy can be achieved with phase changes, such as the sublimation of ZnO to Zn and O2 gases, a case which can be considered an upper bound for the entropy change in such reactions.

More recently many researchers have also turned their attention to perovskites,127132 with a recent review on these efforts by Kubicek et al.133 Again the goal of this research is often improving the yield at lower temperature, where improved reduction is easily mistaken for improved performance, without fully considering the fuel conversion ratio. Perovskites, like ceria, have a low specific energy storage (Fig. 7), and the low entropy of reduction when compared to ceria (Fig. 5) combined with their higher specific heat capacity,108,109 means that they are unlikely to offer any significant improvement over ceria, which has been noted by Ezbiri et al. in an in-depth study of fuel production thermodynamics with perovskites.74

The current state of the art, is that CeO2 remains to be the most pragmatic solution, in that it has been proven to work, and is therefore suitable to demonstrate the feasibility of the cycle and key process components such as solar reactors. Researchers at ETH Zrich recently reported a 5.25% reactor efficiency with a peak fuel conversion ratio of 83% CO2 CO, using the CeO2 cycle in a lab scale reactor,134 which was a large increase in demonstrated efficiency for this technology. For comparison to other renewable fuel technologies, researchers at Stanford University recently demonstrated a solar energy to hydrogen fuel conversion efficiency of 30%, using multilayer PV cells and PEM electrolysis.135 This method would not have been suitable for CO2 splitting, and in the end the cost is more important than the efficiency, but it does illustrate the high development level of some competing technologies, and the need for innovation in the process implementation of thermochemical fuel production.

In general, these thermochemical fuel production cycles could be greatly improved by efficiently achieving low oxygen partial pressures. At present, this is achieved using a pump or a sweep gas, with current trends seeing research move more in the direction of pumping as it requires less energy and offers better kinetics.78,125,134 Although the theoretical energy required to reduce the oxygen partial pressure is not very large, standard vacuum pumps are inefficient at low pressures (pO2 < 102 bar),17 and innovations, such as the thermochemical oxygen pump discussed later, are needed to make this approach of improving the cycles more attractive.

Air separation

The idea of using such thermochemical cycle for temperature swing air separation was proposed by Moghtaderi in 2009 as a method of oxygen production.10 Note that this method differs from the well established processes of chemical looping combustion, where air is also separated, but the oxide is reduced chemically, by directly reacting it with a fuel. Some further research into the thermodynamics of oxygen production via this method have been conducted, with authors suggesting Pb3O4/PbO, Ag2O/Ag, and CrO2/Cr2O3 (very similar reduction conditions to Ag2O/Ag) as low temperature options, and at higher temperatures CuO/Cu2O and Co3O4/CoO.11,12 It has also recently been suggested as a method of solar powered nitrogen production,60 which could offer a renewable supply of N2 for ammonia production.

For oxygen production, an ideal material would have a large oxygen storage capacity, a low reduction energy requirement hrd, and be capable of removing most of the oxygen from air to minimise the amount of air which must be heated per mole of oxygen produced. For this, one would look to the phase change oxides due to their stoichiometric oxygen storage, and indeed it seems that in terms of thermodynamics, Pb2O3/PbO, Ag2O/Ag and CrO2/Cr2O3 meet the requirements (Fig. 3). The Ag2O/Ag cycle can undergo reduction but the reversibility is not spontaneous in air,52 and the reversibility of CrO2/Cr2O3 is also highly unlikely due to the very low temperatures required. The Pb3O4/PbO cycle on the other hand is reversible,137 but more detailed studies on stability, kinetics and thermodynamics of the cycle in practice would be required to determine it's suitability. It may also be beneficial to consider solid solutions with other oxides, such as in the PbOCuO or PbOCaO systems,138,139 which could improve the stability and kinetics.

One very recent breakthrough in the field of oxygen storage redox materials, may offer an excellent alternative option for oxygen production via thermochemical air separation. Aoki et al. have reported a very interesting study of yttrium chromium phosphates, YCr1xPxO4, which have shown mass changes of more than 1% due to reversible oxidation and reduction at remarkably low temperatures in the range 50450 C.140 Such low temperature reduction implies a very low enthalpy hrd, which they found experimentally to be in the region 20 kJ mol1, and also calculated a theoretical value of 14 kJ mol1 using DFT. This low temperature range would also allow for very effective heat exchangers and these yttrium chromium phosphates may offer a very competitive oxygen production process.

After the oxygen production cycle, one would ideally have a hot stream of air with oxygen depleted to roughly 1% or less. The purification of this air stream to low oxygen ppm suitable for the HaberBosch process, then requires an oxide with a stronger affinity to oxygen. From Fig. 3 Co3O4/CoO, CuO/Cu2O and BaO2/BaO, could be used to absorb oxygen at low partial pressures. It remains to be seen however, if they can maintain reasonable kinetic activities at the relatively low temperatures and partial pressures required. Perhaps a more promising option to achieve low partial pressures and avoid high reduction temperatures due to high hrd (eqn (28)) are perovskite oxides, many of which show kinetic activity down to temperatures as low as 300 C and in low oxygen partial pressures of 105 bar.60,66 In this case the low specific energy storage would not be a major issue, as only a small amount of oxygen needs to be absorbed at very low partial pressures, meaning the energy requirement is small, and so fast kinetics and lower operating temperatures would become the key selection criteria.

The process shown in Fig. 11, if realised together with renewable water splitting, could provide the feedstocks for sustainable fertilizer production. For example, one of the most common nitrogen fertilizers, ammonium nitrate NH4NO3, requires nitrogen, hydrogen, and oxygen as feedstocks, with N2 and H2 for ammonia synthesis, and O2 for further processing of the ammonia via the Ostwald process. This offers strong motivation for further study into such thermochemical air separation processes.

Oxygen pump

The idea that a two-step thermochemical redox cycle could be used as a means of pumping oxygen gas was mentioned in the literature by Kawabe et al. as early as 1997, using praseodymium oxides as the redox material (Pr9O16).141 The concept seems to have then turned dormant for over a decade, until authors began to look at these cycles for air separation, as described above, which is essentially selective oxygen pumping.

Such a pump is particularly interesting at low pressures, where mechanical pumps have very poor efficiency due to adiabatic pumping, friction and leakage losses.17 A thermochemical pump would consume oxygen in a chemical reaction, removing the need for mechanical pumping. Calculations performed by the authors show that for a pump operating using the Co3O4/CoO cycle, the pumping efficiency would be orders of magnitude greater than that of mechanical pumps at pressures bellow 103 bar.17

Similarly to air separation, pumping to low partial pressures can be achieved using materials with large hrd and/or by performing the oxidation at low temperatures, according to eqn (28). This concept has, as of the publication of this manuscript, not received an in-depth screening of suitable oxides. It is unclear if any of the phase change oxides, which show large oxygen storage such as Pb3O4/PbO or at high temperatures Co3O4/CoO, show the kinetic activity required to react with low partial pressure oxygen gas at low temperature. On the other hand perovskites may be a viable alternative which are known to show rapid kinetics even at low temperatures and partial pressures.60,66

An example application of such an oxygen pump, and a method which may make thermochemical fuel production more competitive is by coupling the cycle to a thermochemical oxygen pump, allowing oxygen to be efficiently and selectively pumped to low pressures, which could for example make the ZnO/Zn cycle viable without a sweep gas. The oxygen pump could be powered using the waste heat from the fuel production cycle as illustrated in Fig. 12(a).

Another interesting application of an oxygen pump is to use low oxygen partial pressures to create a chemical potential across an oxygen conducting membrane. In membrane reactors, partial oxidation of methane to syngas on one side of a membrane creates low oxygen partial pressures and enough oxygen chemical potential to allow H2O or CO2 to be reduced on the other side of the reactor, producing additional syngas.19,142,143 In high temperature electrolysis the chemical potential is provided using electricity. An interesting hybrid between the two ideas would be to use a thermochemical oxygen pump, to produce low pressure and an oxygen chemical potential, reducing the electrical voltage and thus electrical energy demand in high temperature electrolysis. This idea is illustrated in Fig. 12(b), where the heat supplied to the oxygen pump is re-released at lower temperature and then used to produce steam, which can be used to provide the heat to the electrolysis or indeed for electricity production.

There are sure to be many areas of the chemical production industry where achieving low oxygen partial pressures are desired and many potential applications for such an oxygen pumping technology.

Outlook

Two step thermochemical redox cycles offer some valuable options for energy storage and conversion, which if realised, could have a huge impact on power generation, and large scale chemical industries such as fertilizer and fuel production. Here we highlight the importance of considering the fundamental redox properties of the metal oxides, in order to make informed decisions in developing these technologies. It is shown that the partial reduction materials ceria and perovskites exhibit very low specific energy storage when compared to simple oxides which undergo a phase change during reduction. This has important implications for applications such as energy storage, where thermochemical cycles using only these partial reduction oxides do not have ideal thermodynamics and could struggle to match the performance of competing technologies. In the case of fuel production, there is a need for greater innovation. Perovskites can be engineered to have high redox activity at low temperatures, which should be beneficial in air-separation and oxygen pumping technologies. In this growing field of research the data and insight presented here should help researchers to set achievable targets and choose suitable directions for their research into two step metal oxide redox cycles.
