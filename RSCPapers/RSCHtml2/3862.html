A comprehensive review on the fabrication, modification and applications of Na3V2(PO4)2F3 cathodes

Abstract

Na-ion batteries (SIBs) have garnered tremendous interest due to their unique advantages of high safety, abundant Na resources, and low cost. Great research efforts of SIBs have been devoted to the exploitation and in-depth mechanism investigation of high-performance electrode materials. Among the various cathodes, Na3V2(PO4)2F3 (NVPF), a representative member of Na superionic conductor (NASICON) structured compounds, has been considered to be a promising candidate because of its superior structural stability, fast ion transport, high operating potential and so on. However, its electrochemical performance and future large-scale applications have been hindered by the relatively low electronic conductivity and high cost of NVPF. This review emphasizes the crystal structure and Na storage mechanisms together with synthetic methods of NVPF, and then summarizes various proposed strategies including carbon coating, element doping, size and morphology design, etc. to meliorate the electrochemical performance of NVPF. Additionally, the applications of the NVPF cathode in other battery systems are included. Finally, our perspectives on the subsequent research and optimization of NVPF are also shared. This review not only is a comprehensive summary of NVPF for the first time but also provides a good reference for the rational design of high-performance NVPF in the future.

1. Introduction

Great efforts have been focused on developing solar, wind, and other renewable energy technologies to overcome the pollution problems caused by traditional fossil fuels.1 However, the space-time restriction of new energy sources makes it inevitable to exploit efficient electrochemical energy storage (EES) technologies suitable for renewable energy.2,3 Among the various EES technologies, Li-ion batteries (LIBs) dominate the market due to their low self-discharge rate, high energy density and so forth,4 while LIBs confront severe bottlenecks of high cost and limited resource reserve.5 Naturally, Na-ion batteries (SIBs) with abundant resources and low cost have sparked research interest again.6,7 In addition, as Na is located in the same main group as Li (the alkali metal family), Na exhibits similar physicochemical properties to Li, allowing partial work and strategies that have been implemented for LIBs to be applied to SIBs. Another profitable advantage is that the anode current collector for SIBs can adopt cheap Al foil instead of Cu since Na will not form an alloy with Al, which further lowers the cost of SIBs.8,9 Nonetheless, the current SIBs have not yet attained parity with LIBs in terms of cycling stability and energy density, which require considerable scientific and engineering improvements to push forward the development of SIBs.

Similar to LIBs, SIBs follow the rocking-chair mechanism, which relies on the reversible insertion/extraction of Na+ between the cathode and anode during the charge/discharge process to realize energy conversion.10 Therefore, the electrochemical performances of SIBs are largely determined by the inherent chemistry of the electrode materials.1113 Cathode materials are the core factor that directly affects the overall performance of SIBs since cathode materials not only account for a large proportion of the mass but also determine the output voltage of SIBs.1418 To date, all sorts of cathode materials, including transition-metal oxides,19,20 polyanion-type compounds,21,22 Prussian blue analogues,23etc., have exhibited considerable Na storage capacities. However, the poor cycling durability, low redox potential and high moisture sensitivity cannot be ignored in transition-metal oxides.24 Prussian blue analogues are often affected by the issues of low coulombic efficiency and poor cycling performance caused by the presence of lattice defects and coordinated water.25,26 In comparison, polyanion-type compounds become one of the extensively researched electrode materials on account of their versatile structure, high safety and excellent electrochemical performance.

As a member of polyanion-type compounds, Na3V2(PO4)2F3 (NVPF) with the NASICON structure is a promising candidate by virtue of its high energy density, large interstitial spaces, excellent structural stability and so on.27 As reported, NVPF outputs a high working potential of 3.9 V due to the stronger ionicity of the FV bond than that of the OV one, exhibiting a high energy density of 500 W h kg1.28 All such advantages make it quite popular in SIB applications. In recent years, researchers have carried out some work on this material and made tremendous progress. Hence, it is necessary to summarize current research in detail, so as to provide a reference for subsequent design. To our knowledge, however, a specialized review focusing on NVPF has not yet been published. This brings about an immediate requirement to systematically investigate its development, which is the motivation behind this review.

In this review, a detailed introduction of the crystal structure and sodiation/desodiation mechanisms together with synthetic methods of NVPF reported till the date is presented. Some strategies for meliorating the electrochemical performance of NVPF from the perspective of enhancing electron conduction are mainly analyzed. Moreover, the applications of the NVPF cathode in full batteries, LIBs and other battery systems are also summarized. Finally, our perspectives about the remaining challenges and the future prospects of NVPF will also be discussed. We believe that this review will inform readers of a comprehensive understanding of NVPF and inspire further research on it.

2. Crystal structure and Na storage mechanism of NVPF

The first synthesis and characterization of NVPF can be traced back to 1999. Meins et al.29 firstly synthesized a series of fluorophosphate materials (Na3M2(PO4)2F3 (M = Al3+, V3+, Cr3+, Fe3+, Ga3+)) by solid-state or hydrothermal methods, and systematically studied their crystal structures. The characterization results showed that NVPF with the Amam space group belongs to an orthorhombic crystal system, which is composed of [V2O8F3] bi-octahedral and [PO4] tetrahedral units. And the bi-octahedra units are connected by F atoms, whereas the [PO4] units are linked by O atoms (Fig. 1a). In particular, this arrangement results in the formation of large channels to accommodate Na+via the free diffusion along the [110] and [10] directions.30 The charge exchange is usually accompanied by the redox of transition metal ions in the crystal structure during the charge/discharge process, so the diffusion paths of electrons depend on the interconnection between [V2O8F3] bi-octahedra for NVPF. The presence of [PO4] not only allows the rapid migration of Na+ in the framework structure but also stabilizes the structure during the metal redox process. However, the electron transmission is blocked by [PO4], which are equatorially connected with the [V2O8F3] bi-octahedra. Therefore, although there are many ion diffusion channels in NVPF, the lack of electron transmission paths leads to low electronic conductivity, which severely restricts its electrochemical performance.31,32

The Na storage mechanism in NVPF was studied in detail by various in situ measurements including X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS), X-ray diffraction (XRD), high-resolution synchrotron XRD, solid-state nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), and so on.3335 The 23Na NMR spectrum (Fig. 1b) of pristine NVPF verified the presence of two diverse Na sites with an occupancy ratio of 2:1; the fully occupied Na sites are denoted as Na1 and the other partially filled Na sites are named Na2.33 Note that the process of extracting Na+ from NVPF is accompanied by a series of very complex phase transitions.34 There are four distinct intermediate phases before the ultimate formation of NaV2(PO4)2F3, i.e. N2.4V2(PO4)2F3, N2.2V2(PO4)2F3, N2V2(PO4)2F3, and N1.81.3V2(PO4)2F3, among which, only N1.81.3V2(PO4)2F3 with the I4/mmm symmetry undergoes a solid solution process. And the Na (de)intercalation processes involving other intermediate phases show biphasic reactions. Surprisingly, the crystal structure of NVPF will change from the space group Amam to I4/mmm and finally to Cmc21 during the complete charge process. Fig. 1c further illustrates the occupation of Na+ inside the unit cell of different intermediate phases. In subsequent research, Song et al.35,36 explored the electrochemical performance and ion migration pathway for the NVPF cathode by combining theoretical calculations and experimental studies. Upon replacing one (PO4)3 anion in NVP with strongly electronegative F, NVPF exhibits higher redox potentials (3.3, 3.6 and 4.0 V vs. Na+/Na), which are related to the redox reaction of V3+/V4+ (i.e. Na3V2(PO4)F3 NaV2(PO4)2F3 + 2Na+ + 2e) (Fig. 1d).36 And Na+ at Na2 sites will be preferentially extracted due to the high chemical potential.35 Exactly, the first two redox couples at a relatively low potential (3.3 and 3.6 V) are due to two-step uptake/release of the Na2 ions in the NVPF crystal, whereas the third one (4.0 V) is caused by the second Na+ intercalation/deintercalation into/from the Na1 sites.37 Generally, it should be noted that only two Na+ participate in the reaction, while the third one in the structure of NVPF is electrochemically inaccessible. To trigger the electrochemical activity of the third Na+, a new disordered NVPF phase will form.38 As shown in Fig. 1e, this disordered NVPF can reversibly intercalate/deintercalate three Na+ in the potential window of 1.04.8 V, and the last Na+ will be extracted at 1.6 V. Therefore, the energy density of the full SIB using a carbon anode and this disordered NVPF cathode is expected to increase by 1520%.

3. Synthetic methods of NVPF

Compared to anode materials, the synthetic strategies of NVPF are relatively fewer. Previously used methods are mainly traditional solid-state reaction methods,3943 solgel methods,4446 hydrothermal/solvothermal methods47,48 and spray drying.49,50 Basically, high-purity NVPF can be directly prepared by solid-state reactions. In a recent study, NVPF was produced using NaF, NH4VO3, NH4H2PO4 and pitch as the raw materials by a single-step solid-state ball-milling method (Fig. 2a).43 Through the SEM images of NVPF/C composites (Fig. 2b), it can be found that the particle size is large, heterogeneous and agglomerated, which is not conducive to their electrochemical performance.35 Compared with the solid-state method, low-temperature synthetic routes without a tedious re-annealing treatment, such as the solgel method, are widely used. Generally, citric acid or oxalic acid is added as a chelating agent and carbon source during the synthesis (Fig. 2c). The particle size and distribution of NVPF@C prepared by this method are much better, leading to much improved electrochemical kinetics (Fig. 2d).51 However, the controlling of the morphology and structure cannot be easily realized via this method.28

In order to build NVPF with unique structures to achieve high performance, the solvothermal/hydrothermal method is generally adopted.47,48 To our knowledge, this is a very simple method to synthesize NVPF. All the raw materials are just mixed together, followed by a hydrothermal process and heat treatment (Fig. 2e). NVPF microcubes wrapped by a 3D rGO network (NVPF@rGO) (Fig. 2f) and hierarchical hollow microsphere structure can be easily prepared based on the hydrothermal method.52,53

Virtually, an elaborate architecture enables superior Na storage due to enhanced electronic conductivity and facilitated ionic migration.54 Therefore, to realize the rational design of NVPF, spray drying49,50 or electrostatic spinning55,56 is often used as an auxiliary technology in combination with the solid-state reaction. Spherical NVPF@C/CNTs with a high tap density was fabricated through the spray drying method, which is promising to achieve practical applications for high power and long lifespan SIBs (Fig. 2g and h).50 Predictably, the scalable spray drying method can not only produce materials in a time-efficient way but also accelerate the commercialization of SIBs. Electrostatic spinning is a typical technology to synthesize 1D nanostructured self-standing electrode materials.57,58 Through electrostatic spinning and carbonized processes (detailed experimental procedure can be seen in Fig. 2i), the nanoparticles are uniformly dispersed and confined in interconnecting 1D carbon nanofibers (Fig. 2j). This technology has been widely applied in the synthesis of cathode materials including Na3V2(PO4)359 and NaVPO4F/C,60 so it is also feasible to synthesize NVPF.61 Unfortunately, only one research study on the synthesis of NVPF by electrostatic spinning has been published so far, and considerable work remains to be done.

Synthesis methods and conditions have great influence on the properties of electrode materials and application prospects. Among the various methods, the solid-state reaction method is a simple synthesis route, but the prepared NVPF usually has problems like inhomogeneity, agglomeration, irregular morphology, etc., which limits its rate performance. Both the solgel method and hydrothermal synthesis have many merits over the traditional solid-state reaction, such as a good mixture of the reagents and small grain size of NVPF, which are considered as promising methods to well realize an elaborately designed structure and homogeneous element doping. Also, developing other auxiliary methods (e.g., microwave heating) is still required.

Although NVPF exhibits a lot of merits as a cathode for SIBs, the overall electrochemical performance is still seriously hindered by its low inherent electronic conductivity.62 Therefore, it is crucial to improve the electronic conductivity to achieve better electrochemical performance. For this purpose, researchers have made a lot of attempts and efforts towards material modification. At present, some modification strategies, including carbon coating, element doping, size and morphology design, etc., have been proposed. By employing one or more above strategies, the electrochemical properties of this material can be effectively improved. 4.1. NVPF/carbon composites

Carbon modifications have attracted widespread attention due to its advantages of high conductivity, low cost, and excellent chemical stability. Note that carbon additives such as amorphous carbon, graphite, and carbon fibers are combined with active materials, which not only form external conductive frameworks but also protect the internal material structure. More precisely, carbon modifications possess many merits:68 (i) the electron conductive carbon layer can realize a fast-synergic diffusion between electrons and ions; (ii) the carbon layer is highly effective in preventing the active materials from reacting with the electrolyte owing to the favorable corrosion stability; (iii) the carbon material can provide a buffer for the tiny volume changes that occur during the ion (de)intercalation processes. Although compositing with carbon materials has been widely used, the difficulty to graphitize the carbon materials obviously limits the improvement of electronic conductivity. In the meantime, it is not easy to obtain a uniform carbon layer due to its susceptibility to preparation conditions. Actually, the thickness and uniformity of the carbon coating layer outside of the active materials will affect the migration of Na+ between particles and the electrolyte, so it should be precisely controlled.
4.1.1. NVPF/amorphous carbon. The advantage of amorphous carbon coating should be attributed to the following aspects:63 first, the carbon coating layer will be beneficial to restricting the growth of NVPF particles, so the particle size of the material becomes smaller with the increase of the carbon content (Fig. 4ac). Second, the amorphous carbon will form a conductive network for electron transportation. Furthermore, the amorphous carbon wrapped structure can also lower the charge transfer resistance and improve the electrolyte wetting area during the charge and discharge process.

Carbon sources are always introduced into the precursor to achieve an in situ amorphous carbon layer on the surface of NVPF through thermal decomposition. Since the coated amorphous carbon layer brings about a lot of advantages, the NVPF/amorphous carbon composites proposed by various research groups generally deliver improved rate capacity and cycling ability compared with pristine NVPF. For example, utilizing citric acid as the carbon source, a NVPF@C composite with a dense carbon shell (around 7 nm) was synthesized. It delivered a high reversible capacity of 130 mA h g1 at 0.5 C and excellent cycling performance at a high rate of 30 C, with a capacity retention of 50% even after 3000 cycles.44 However, too much carbon would cause severe particle aggregation, which may hinder the migration of Na+ and decrease the utilization rate of carbon. Simultaneously, the excess introduction of the inactive carbon into the electrode material would decrease the energy density of the electrode. Wang et al.43 found that the NVPF/C sample with a carbon content of 12.14 wt% showed the most excellent reversible capacities and outstanding cycling performance. Therefore, a moderate amount of carbon in the NVPF/C sample is essential.

In addition, doping heteroatoms (N,64 P,65 S66 or B67) into the carbon coating layers is intensively investigated. Heteroatom doping will introduce abundant active sites and extrinsic defects into carbon frameworks, so as to achieve a faster electronic and ionic transfer rate compared to original coatings. Among various heteroatoms, N, as the most widely studied heteroatom, can create more active sites for electrochemical reactions and improve surface wettability.68 For example, using dopamine (DPA) as a carbon source, a NVPF/C-PDPA sample with a uniform N-doped carbon layer (about 34 nm in thickness) was synthesized.64 Owing to the improved Na+ migration and enhanced charge transfer constructed by the N-doped carbon coating layer, the composite showed superior cycling ability (with a capacity retention of 95.8% after 800 cycles at 10 C) and rate capacity to NVPF/C. Certainly, compared with single atom doping, dual atom doping can introduce more active sites and significantly improve the electron/ion migration, thereby greatly improving the electrochemical performance of electrode materials.65 Although dual atom doping has not been reported in the NVPF system, it can be considered in light of its widespread and successful applications in other materials.

4.1.2. NVPF/carbon nanotubes and NVPF/carbon nanofibers. Dispersing carbon with better electrical conductivity directly into the precursor for high-temperature sintering is a relatively simple strategy. Among them, compositing NVPF with carbon nanotubes (CNTs) has attracted great attention from researchers to fabricate high-performance cathode materials because of the high electrical conductivity and exceptional thermal stability of CNTs.69 Eshraghi et al.49 prepared an NVPF/CNT sample, where CNTs were well dispersed inside the NVPF particles with an irregular morphology (as seen in Fig. 4d). Benefiting from the porosity that avails the penetration of liquid electrolytes and the bridging network of uniformly distributed CNTs, the NVPF/CNT material showed favorable electrochemical performance. In addition, a designed double carbon-wrapped structure is verified to be capable of offering a stable framework for fast transmission of Na+ and excellent electronic conductivity. For instance, the NVPF@C/CNT composite synthesized by a hydrothermal route without extra heat treatment showed higher rate capability and superior cycling performance than NVPF/CNT owing to the highly conductive 3D structure which was caused by the embedded CNTs and homogeneous carbon covering layer (about 5 nm) for NVPF (Fig. 4e).70

Single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) can contribute to the formation of a pure phase and serve as a seed crystal to increase the crystallinity of the material. Fig. 4f displays the morphology of NVPF-SWCNT; the nanocube-like NVPF particles are wrapped and connected with each other by an SWCNT network, which would account for the amelioration of electronic conductivity. Due to the addition of SWCNTs, this material demonstrated a high reversible capacity of 117 mA h g1 at 0.5 C, which was notably higher than that of NVPF.71 Favorable electrochemical performance can also be obtained from the cathode material after incorporating carbon nanofibers (CNFs). A CNF framework constructed in the NVPF electrode can not only serve as a conductive network for the realization of high-rate capacity but also act as a buffer for the variation of volume in the crystal during the charge/discharge process to enable the structural stability of the electrode. Zhao et al.51 obtained a NVPF@C@CNF composite (Fig. 4g) prepared by a facile CVD method that showed long-term cycling stability (86.3% capacity retention after 5000 cycles at 20 C) and high rate capability (84.3 mA h g1 at 50 C).

4.1.3. NVPF/graphene. The introduction of graphene into electrode materials is one of the most effective ways to enhance their electrochemical performance. Compositing with graphene can not only enhance the electronic transmission of the electrode, but can also increase the electrode/electrolyte contact area.72 The NVPF@GN composite was fabricated by a low-temperature hydrothermal method without any post-heat treatment. And dimethylformamide (DMF) was employed as a solvent to confine the growth of NVPF particles.73 In this composite, the cube-like NVPF nanoparticles are homogeneously embedded in a 3D graphene network (Fig. 4h and i). Such a structure could effectively reduce the Na migration time in the crystals and facilitate the charge transfer. Thereafter, Cai et al.52 synthesized NVPF@rGO microcubes by a similar hydrothermal strategy without adding DMF. The as-prepared micrometer-sized material can attain higher volumetric energy density than the nanoscale particles that have severe adverse surface side reactions. Accordingly, NVPF@rGO demonstrated superior long-term cycling stability with a high capacity retention of 98% after 2000 cycles at 20 C, and was more stable than NVPF@GN (75% capacity retention after 1000 cycles at 10 C).

Based on the above discussions, compositing with conductive carbon is a promising strategy to improve the electronic conductivity of NVPF. When 3D frameworks or double carbon-wrapped structures are constructed by the introduced carbon materials, the electron conductivity along with the Na+ transport kinetics in the NVPF electrode can be significantly increased. Furthermore, with proper heteroatom doping in the coated carbon layer, the electrochemical performance of the electrode can be further promoted. However, it should be pointed out that the carbon coating layer can merely ameliorate the surface electron conductivity, and it is helpless to the conductivity of inner particles. Additionally, the conductive materials currently studied in NVPF systems seem to be limited to carbonaceous materials. Other conductive materials, including conductive polymers, oxides/fluorides, metals, etc., which have achieved remarkable effects in other material systems, can be further investigated.28

4.2. Element doping

As is well known, NVPF often suffers from inferior intrinsic electronic conductivity caused by the insulating properties of the [PO4] tetrahedra. Element doping is a significant and effective method since it endows the researchers with great freedom to engineer intrinsic electronic conductivity, defects, crystal structures, redox platforms and so on. At present, plenty of cations such as K+,74 Li+,75 Al3+,76,77 Y3+,78 Cr3+,79 Mn2+,53 Ti4+,40 Ga3+,80etc., have been employed as dopants in NVPF cathodes. The doping elements can be classified into three types based on the substitution sites: Na sites, V sites, and anion sites.
4.2.1. Doping in Na sites. Generally, element doping with a larger ionic radius in the Na sites of NVPF can induce the expansion of lattice spacing, leading to a broad ion diffusion path.81 Moreover, after introducing these elements into the lattice, the VF and VO bonds will be shortened, which will enhance the structural stability of NVPF. For instance, K+ (r = 1.38 ) was introduced into the Na sites in NVPF (Fig. 5a),74 and the Na+ diffusion coefficient of this K+ doped sample was at least one order of magnitude higher than that of the pristine one. As a result, a high reversible capacity of 120 mA h g1 was obtained at 1C (Fig. 6b). After nearly 6000 cycles at 50 C, the capacity retention of NKVPF@CNT was still close to 90%.

On the other hand, element doping with a smaller ionic radius in Na sites has also been studied. Kosova et al.75 used Li+ (r = 0.76 ) to replace partial Na+ (r = 1.02 ) and they prepared a series of Na3xLixV2(PO4)2F3 samples (x = 0, 0.1, 0.4 and 0.6) by a two-step solid-state method. In this work, the authors found that in addition to the main phase Na3xLixV2(PO4)2F3, several impurities (e.g., V2O3) were also formed on the surface. These V2O3 impurities and the carbon particles formed a conductive network that could significantly increase the overall electronic conductivity of the Na3xLixV2(PO4)2F3 electrode, and decreased the activation energy, subsequently improving their rate capacity (Fig. 6c).

4.2.2. Doping in V sites. Compared to doping in Na sites, the substitutions of V with other earth-abundant and low-cost elements are more common. The doping elements can be divided into two categories according to their electrochemical activity. When inactive elements that would not participate in electrochemical reactions (such as Mg2+, Al3+, Y3+, etc.) are doped into NVPF, they can mainly act as a stabilizer for the crystal structure as well as generating defects and extra charge transfer paths in the NVPF crystals. On the other hand, active elements such as Mn2+, Cr3+, and Ti3+ can usually produce new active redox pairs during electrode reactions so as to increase the specific capacity and output voltage of the material significantly.

Among various inactive metals, Al has attracted great attention owing to its low cost, eco-friendliness, and beneficial function in the stabilization of materials during charge/discharge cycling. The NVPF with only 5% Al doping (Na3V1.95Al0.05(PO4)2F3) delivered higher reversible capacity and better cycling stability than the pristine one.76 Olchowka et al. successfully synthesized Na3V2zAlz(PO4)2F3 (z = 0.5) with an increased Al content by a solgel process. The theoretical gravimetric capacity of Na3V1.5Al0.5(PO4)2F3 was about 3% higher than that of NVPF for the reversible insertion/extraction of 2 Na+, meaning that the third Na+ ion may be activated.77 When Y3+ with a larger ionic radius (r = 0.9 ) partially replaces the V in the NVPF crystal (Fig. 5b), it provides larger interstitial spaces for a fast Na+ diffusion rate. And the weaker YO bonds compared to the VO bonds will facilitate intrinsic electronic conductivity. Moreover, the doped Y3+ serves as an upholder in the NVPF, which can provide a buffer for the deformation of crystals during the charge/discharge process. Hence, the optimum Na3V1.9Y0.1(PO4)2F3/C delivered an excellent capacity of 100 mA h g1 at 10 C and 80 mA h g1 at 50 C (Fig. 6e).78

For the active elements, the redox potentials of common electrochemically active elements are shown in Fig. 6a. Like the current research on Na4MnCr(PO4)3,82,83 Na4MnV(PO4)3,54,84 and other materials,85 it can not only solve the problems of toxicity and high cost of V but can also provide a higher working potential. However, because the amount of doping in the NVPF system is relatively low, a new voltage platform is not observed. The active element doping will still play a positive role in improving the performance. For example, the incorporation of Cr3+ (r = 0.615 ) into NVPF at low levels can reduce the internal resistance and cell polarization, leading to a significantly increased overall capacity. The rate performance of the Na3V2xCrx(PO4)2F3@C (0 x 0.1) samples is exhibited in Fig. 6d.79 By Cr3+ doping, the capacity increased obviously, especially for NVPF-Cr0.01 and NVPF-Cr0.03. Doping the NVPF particles with Mn2+ is also an effective way to enhance the electrochemical properties. Owing to the higher radius of Mn2+ (r = 0.67 ) than V3+ (r = 0.64 ), moderate Mn2+ doping into NVPF does increase the crystal cell volume effectively, which is beneficial to the transport of Na+. The synergistic effect of the Mn2+ doping as well as a thin carbon layer enabled a Na3V1.95Mn0.05(PO4)2F3@C composite with an initial discharge capacity of 122.9 mA h g1 and rate performance of 60.7 mA h g1 at 10 C (Fig. 6f).53

Ti doping was proved to have a positive impact on improving the intrinsic electronic/ionic conductivity of NVPF based on previously published work.86 A moderate amount of Ti doping can effectively restrain the growth of crystals and control morphology formation. Thus, Yi et al.40 selected Tix+ with different valence states to synthesize NVPF-Tiyx+ (x = 2, 3, 4; y = 0, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2) materials and compared the effects of VSC-doping (the valence state changes during the synthesis) and VSU-doping (the valence state of the doping ion remains unchanged) on NVPF. They verified that Ti doping in one-eighth V sites of the NVPF crystal (Fig. 5c) would lead to a reduced band gap for the Ti-doped NVPF, which could facilitate the electron motion from the valence band to the conduction band. As for the effects of the two different doping processes, they found that the VSC-doping samples (x = 2, 3) possessed a smaller particle size than the VSU-doping samples (x = 4), which obviously improved the electron conductivity and Na+ diffusivity. Finally, the NVPF-Ti0.12+ prepared via the VSC-doping method with the smallest particle size distribution (about 40 nm) showed a supreme reversible capacity of 125 mA h g1 at 0.2 C (Fig. 6g).

As seen from the above discussions, ion doping is indeed a valid strategy to enhance the electrochemical performance of NVPF, mainly through enhancing the intrinsic electricity and stabilizing the crystal structure during cycling. Moreover, doping with cheaper metals can reduce the cost of material fabrication to some extent. However, not all metal ion doping is feasible in ameliorating the electrochemical performance of NVPF. For example, the introduction of ions with a too large radius into the crystal may seriously affect the structural stability and even damage the crystal structure. Furthermore, an appropriate amount and homogeneous distribution of doping ions are also important. Excess doping could hinder the crystallization of NVPF or destroy the stability of the crystal structure and result in poor electrochemical performance. Therefore, to realize an efficient element doping, various parameters like the type, amount, distribution, doping site and cost of the choice element should be elaborately considered.

4.2.3. Doping in anion sites. As anions are generally the key building blocks of crystals, partial substitution in the anion composition in the crystal usually leads to the formation of undesirable secondary phases or impurities. At the same time, anionic substitution was often accompanied by a corresponding change in the valence of cations, which will make it difficult for the synthesis of the pure phase. However, the oxygen substituted samples, namely Na3V2(PO4)F3yOy (0 y 2, also named Na3V2O2y(PO4)2F32y and Na3(VO1yPO4)2F1+2y where y varies from 0 to 1), can be obtained in a similar crystal framework with large Na+ transport tunnels during (de)intercalation reactions.87 In addition, it has been proved that a mixed-valence of V (V3+/4+) caused by the partial doping of oxygen in Na3V2(PO4)F3yOy (0 y 2) can enhance the electrochemical properties of the electrodes because of more convenient Na+ migration and intrinsically better electronic conductivity.88,89 In order to investigate the structure and electrochemical properties of the oxygen substituted compounds with different oxygen contents, Park et al.87 synthesized a series of Na3(VO1xPO4)2F1+2x (x = 0.0, 0.2, 0.5, 0.8 and 1.0) and found that all the samples displayed a similar average potential (3.83.9 V) and discharge capacity (120130 mA h g1) regardless of the oxygen content, but these isostructural compounds indeed possessed distinct potential-composition curves as well as Na+ insertion/extraction mechanisms due to the different redox pairs (V3+/V4+ or V4+/V5+) and F/O distribution.

It has been further confirmed by Broux et al.90 that there are some differences in the structural and electrochemical features of the as-prepared Na3V2(PO4)F3yOy (0 y 0.5). As the y value increases (i.e. as the oxygen content increases), the c cell parameter decreases continuously and the Na+ distribution becomes more disordered. Moreover, when y increases to 0.5, the electrochemical signature of Na3V2(PO4)F3yOy changes from two voltage plateaus (y = 0) to an S-shape for the two potential domains. Most importantly, with the increase of the oxygen content, a noteworthy decrease in hysteresis between charge and discharge can be observed, which can be attributed to the enhanced electronic and ionic transport properties favored by the mixed oxidation state V3+/4+. The excellent electrochemical properties of the mixed-valence polyanion compounds have attracted great interest of researchers to exploit high-performance cathodes for SIBs. For example, Kumar et al.91 reported a Na3V2O2x(PO4)2F32x/MWCNT compound as a high-voltage insertion cathode material. Na3V2O2x(PO4)2F32x is actually a mixed phase of NVPF and Na3V2O2(PO4)2F with an average oxidation state of V3.46+ (Na3V2O0.92(PO4)2F2.08). Benefiting from the mixed-valence and additive MWCNTs, the electrode showed superior cycling durability and high rate capacity in both aqueous electrolytes and non-aqueous electrolytes. Among a series of Na3(VO1xPO4)2F1+2x (0 x 1) materials prepared by Qi et al.,92 Na3(VOPO4)2F exhibited the best Na storage properties with a specific capacity of 112 mA h g1 at 0.2 C and excellent cycling stability with 90% capacity retention after 1200 cycles at 2 C. This report further confirmed the effectiveness of oxygen substitution in the enhancement of the NVPF electrode. Through theoretical calculation, Goodenough et al.93 predicted that the substitution of Cl for O in Na3V2O2(PO4)2F was a feasible strategy to fabricate the Na3V2Cl2(PO4)2F compound which could realize the reversible insertion/extraction of the third Na+ and display an ultra-high theoretical energy density of 758 W h kg1. However, this conclusion has not been verified by experiments yet.

4.3. Size and morphology design

As mentioned above, both surface carbon modification and element doping are feasible strategies to enhance the electronic conductivity of materials. Generally, the adverse effect of poor electron conductivity can be relieved by downsizing the particles of the active material. Therefore, great efforts have been focused on synthesizing NVPF with a special morphology and structure, such as nanostructures, microstructures and micronano hierarchical structures.

It is well known that nanostructures with a short diameter and large specific surface area can decrease the diffusion distance of electrons and ions and improve the utilization of active materials, thus enhancing the rate capability. In addition, the small particle size can have favorable accommodation of the strain during Na+ (de)intercalation, improving the cycling life of the electrode. Based on these superiorities, Park et al.94 embedded phase-pure NVPF in a carbon matrix with the aid of polyol. Since polyol acts not only as a solvent but also as a reducing agent and stabilizer in the synthesis, which suppresses particle growth and realizes in situ carbon coating. As shown in Fig. 7a and b, the NVPF presents the morphology of spherical nanoparticles, which is conducive to achieving specific discharge capacities of 108.4 mA h g1 at a high current density of 30 C and retention capacity of 88% even after 1500 cycles.

However, due to the high specific surface area and high surface energy, it is difficult to disperse the nanoparticles homogeneously in the conductive agents and binders, leading to high contact resistance and severe particle aggregation during charge/discharge cycling.95,96 Moreover, the nanoparticles usually show low tap density, which will lead to low gravimetric and volumetric capacity. In contrast, the microstructures generally provide high packing density as well as high tap density, which can lead to a thinner slurry coating of the electrode even at a high mass loading. Hence, a well-designed spherical NVPF@C/CNT composite, composed of carbon-coated microsized primary NVPF particles embedded in the nanotubes network (Fig. 2h), has been synthesized.50 This homogeneous spherical morphology and appropriate size distribution of secondary particles (215 m) play an important role in the achievement of high-tap-density cathode materials.

To solve the problems of nanoparticles while keeping the high rate performance, a micronano hierarchical structure, in which the microsized secondary particles are assembled by the nanosized primary particles, has been reported. Qi et al.97 designed a nanoflower microstructure through a green synthetic route. Each nanoflower assembled by a number of nanoplatelets (about 10 nm in thickness) displayed a persimmon-like morphology with a diameter of around 8 m and a thickness of around 4 m (Fig. 7g and h). Compared with general nanoparticles, the ordered nanoflowers showed superior electrochemical capacity, owning to the easier penetration of the electrolyte and a shorter ion/electron diffusion path in the special microstructure of the material. This nanoflower exhibited an initial coulombic efficiency of 82.6% and a capacity retention of 94.5% after 500 cycles.97 Similarly, a hierarchical hollow structure of NVPF microspheres (about 1.7 m in size) assembled from nanoparticles (about 40 nm in diameter) and nanoplates (with a length of 130 nm and a width of 120 nm) was successfully synthesized using tetraglycol (TEG) as both a structure-directing agent and a reducing agent.53 As shown in Fig. 7c and d, the unique architecture of hierarchical hollow microspheres is beneficial to offering enough reactive sites, reducing the ionic diffusion barrier and alleviating volume changes, which account for the improved electrochemical performance for the material. In addition, a simple and effective approach that introduces active materials into a highly ordered mesoporous carbon (CMK-3) framework has been proposed to design core/shell hierarchical architecture materials. As expected, Liu et al.46 successfully synthesized an integrated NVPF@CD material (Fig. 7e and f), which also exhibited excellent performance.

To briefly summarize this section, size and morphology design is an important research topic in the field of SIBs. In comparison with anode materials, controlling the size and morphology is relatively difficult for cathode materials because of the complex components, especially for NVPF that contains 5 elements. Thus, theoretical calculations and other auxiliary means should be developed to achieve NVPF with different structures. Furthermore, the structural diversification of NVPF has a direct effect on the electrochemical performance. Nevertheless, the consequent issue is that reducing the particle size may lead to low volumetric energy density, which is not conducive to its application. Therefore, we should take all factors into consideration when designing the size and morphology of NVPF.

5.1. Na-ion full batteries

Various Na-ion full batteries based on NVPF cathodes have been constructed to reveal the application potential of NVPF. Considering that NVPF possesses the redox couple of V3+/V2+ with a potential plateau at 1.4 V, it can also be employed as an anode for SIBs (Fig. 8a). Yao et al.101 assembled a symmetric full battery of NVPF@C@rGO//1M NaClO4 in (EC:PC = 1:1)//NVPF@C@rGO, which exhibited a high discharge capacity of 107 mA h g1 and 53 mA h g1 (based on the mass of NVPF) at 1 C and 20 C, respectively. Even at 10 C, 75.7% of the initial capacity was maintained after 400 cycles (Fig. 8b and c). A pseudo-symmetric full battery using the NVP anode and NVPF-Ti0.12+ cathode was also constructed, which demonstrated a discharge capacity of 99.6 mA h g1 (calculated by the cathode mass) at 0.2 C with two output voltage platforms (2.1 and 2.6 V). Also, this full battery exhibited a decent rate performance (60% of initial discharge capacity at 40 C) and a relatively high energy density of ca. 224 W h kg1 (Fig. 8d and e).40

In brief, the investigation of full batteries aims to lay a solid foundation for fabricating practical batteries. It is, however, noted that there are obvious differences between them in many aspects. For the full batteries, the amount of Na+ is fixed and anodes generally suffer from intrinsic low initial coulombic efficiency. In order to measure the performance of NVPF, researchers often apply excess anode or pre-sodiated anode to compensate for the irreversible Na+ loss.121 But such an operation will undoubtedly reduce the specific gravimetric energy and increase the cost of the full battery, which is in contrast to the goal pursued by the battery in practical applications. Therefore, other Na compensation strategies, such as adding appropriate additives to NVPF or the electrolyte, which have the advantage of easy operation, universality and low cost, are a better choice. Nevertheless, the research on these strategies is still in its infancy, and more obstacles should be surmounted in the future.

5.2. Li-ion batteries

The investigation of NVPF as a cathode for LIBs was carried out earlier than that for SIBs since the exceptional thermal stability and decent electrochemical properties make it a possible candidate to replace LiCoO2 and LiMn2O4.115 Most researchers employed NVPF in conjunction with a Li metal foil anode and LiPF6-based electrolyte to fabricate LIBs. The Na-based cathode material has a complicated insertion/extraction mechanism in the hybrid-ion system (Fig. 11a). Thus, a series of studies were conducted to investigate the electrochemical performance and the ion migration mechanism of NVPF in LIBs. Jiang et al.98 found that the diffusion coefficient slightly increased in the initial cycles, indicating the gradual shift of the insertion mechanism from Na+ insertion to Li+ insertion because Li+ has a smaller ionic radius and mass than Na+. The reversible insertion/extraction of Li+/Na+ dual ions is accompanied by structural reorganization, resulting in the formation of mixed NaLi composites.117,127 Note that there is a competitive relationship between Li+ and Na+, and the intercalation ratio into NVPF rests with the relative concentration of these two ions in the electrolyte.128 However, Li+ is metastable in NVPF so that the insertion of Li+ will bring about a loss of the capacity to some extent. For example, as seen in Fig. 11b and c, NVPF@C@PTHF (a doubled coated NVPF compound with carbon and polytetrahydrofuran) in a hybrid electrolyte consisting of 1 mol L1 LiClO4 and 0.2 mol L1 NaClO4 delivered a capacity of 122 mA h g1 at 1 C with a capacity retention of 92.1% after 100 cycles, which was superior to that of NVPF@C@PTHF in other hybrid electrolytes (<0.2 mol L1 NaClO4).128

5.3. Zn-ion batteries

In addition to Li+/Na+ dual-ion batteries, the application of NVPF in aqueous Zn-ion batteries (ZIBs) was also explored. In comparison with nonaqueous batteries, aqueous batteries using safe and cheap aqueous electrolytes have wider scope in areas where cost is more important than energy density. In this regard, NVPF with characteristics like being rich in resources, easy to synthesize, and stable is suitable for aqueous batteries. Li et al.129 constructed an aqueous ZIB based on an NVPF@C cathode and 2 M Zn(CF3SO3)2 electrolyte along with a carbon film functionalizing Zn anode, which could show a high voltage of 1.62 V, and excellent capacity retention rate of 95% over 4000 cycles (Fig. 11df). The remarkable performance also proved the availability of NVPF in the field of aqueous ZIBs. Furthermore, Li et al.130 studied the electrochemical performance of NVPF in aqueous hybrid NaZn batteries with a highly concentrated electrolyte (8 M NaClO4 + 0.4 M Zn(CF3SO3)2). As seen in Fig. 11g, the soft package Zn@C/NVPF@C battery could successfully light up an LED lamp easily even under different bending conditions. The operating voltage of this battery was 1.57 V and the energy density was 71 W h kg1 based on the total weight of Zn@C and NVPF@C. From the perspective of the insertion/extraction mechanism, however, there are many of the details that remain to be researched. In addition, it is necessary to explore and develop the potential of NVPF in the field of high-temperature batteries and low-temperature batteries, which is conducive to breaking the geographical restrictions.

6. Summary and outlook

As a promising alternative to LIBs in the application of large-scale energy storage systems, SIBs have witnessed rapid development due to the merits of low cost and abundant resources. Also, the exploration of cathode materials that determine the cost and energy density has become a current research hotspot. Over the last decade, polyanionic compounds represented by NVPF are favored by scientists from various countries because of their stable structures and large ion diffusion channels. However, the electronic conductivity of NVPF is relatively poor, resulting in lower than expected both rate performance and long-term cycling stability.

In this review, we have introduced the research progress related to the crystal structure and Na storage mechanisms together with synthetic methods of NVPF, and in-depth analyzed the modification methods of NVPF toward higher electrochemical performance from the viewpoint of enhancing electron conductivity. Moreover, we have also summarized the applications of NVPF in other systems. What we want to mention is that although significant improvement has been achieved in the development of NVPF, there are still many remaining challenges that need to be overcome. Herein, we propose some perspectives and suggestions for the future direction in optimization strategies toward high-performance NVPF cathodes:

(1) Designing and selecting suitable electrolytes. The electrolyte is one of the important components, which is closely related to the electrochemical performance and security capability of batteries.137,138 Current commonly used electrolytes for NVPF are organic electrolytes e.g., 1 M NaClO4 in propylene carbonate (PC) with 5% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC).73 Considering that linear carbonates such as PC, dimethyl carbonate (DMC), ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC), and diethyl carbonate (DEC) have different effects on the electrochemical performance of materials, and FEC also plays an active role in carbonate solutions containing Na salts, the investigation the influence of of the electrolyte composition, concentration, additive type, etc. on the coulombic efficiency, cycling stability and rate performance of NVPF is indispensable.

(2) Reducing the content of V in NVPF. Considering the practical application, selected transition metal elements for cathodes should look into factors such as cost, chemical stability and safety hazards. It is well known that the toxicity and high cost of V will hinder the large-scale production and use of NVPF. For the NVP system, the lower cost and earth-abundant active redox elements, including Mn, Fe, Ni, Cr, etc., have been introduced to substitute half of V and fabricate a series of NASICON-structured NaxMV(PO4)3.85,134136 And it has been proved that these NaxMV(PO4)3 exhibit many advantages from the point of view of production cost and energy density. If the amount of V in NVPF can be further reduced on the premise of maintaining the NASICON structure and capacity, it is of great scientific significance for the development of promising high energy cathode materials. It is expected that the high throughput screening method will greatly facilitate the finding of new structures.

(3) Rational architecture design enables NVPF with conducive electronic/ionic diffusion routes, which significantly affects the electrochemical performance of NVPF for SIBs. In general, constructing nanostructured NVPF can improve the Na+ diffusion kinetics and increase the available active material/electrolyte interfaces. Micronano hierarchical structured NVPF can combine the advantages of micro- and nano-structures, i.e. keeping the good ionic/electronic conductivity and tap density at the same time. However, up to now, the reported morphologies of NVPF are limited. The knowledge of morphology control on Na3V2(PO4)3 can provide inspiration for NVPF design, which will lead to rapid development in the field of NVPF. For example, employing different approaches such as the template method and Ostwald ripening may be a good idea.

In addition, the optimization of the energy and power density is another important aspect for the practical applications of NVPF cathodes. Developing free-standing NVPF cathodes that can observably increase energy and power density along with avoiding side reactions caused by the presence of conductive additives and polymeric binders is of importance. This review can provide a reference for the researchers engaged in the development of NVPF cathodes for SIBs. This is an opening remark than a concluding remark. We also believe that more NVPF cathode materials with superior electrochemical performances will emerge in the near future.
