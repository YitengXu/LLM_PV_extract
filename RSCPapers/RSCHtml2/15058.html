Abstract

In this study, a fast and simple technique (2 min) has been developed to modify the pores of a novel micro-porous, amide-functionalized Co(II)-based MOF [Co(oba)2(bpfb)4](DMF)2, TMU-61, (H2oba = 4,4â€²-oxybis(benzoic acid) and bpfb = N,Nâ€²-bis-(4-pyridylformamide)-1,4-benzenediamine). The material produced through sulfurization of TMU-61, at ambient temperatures, can be used as an appropriate electrode material for supercapacitors (SCs). The highest specific capacitance of a system with three electrodes is 636.6 F gâˆ’1 at a current density of 5 A gâˆ’1, and the specific capacitance retention is nearly 94% after 6000 cycles, demonstrating an impressive long-term cycling stability. The asymmetric SC (ASC) based on S-TMU-61 showed the highest energy density (25.73 W h kgâˆ’1) and power density (2549.3 W kgâˆ’1). Additionally, it is observed that two S-TMU-61 ASCs connected in series are enough to supply energy for lighting blue, yellow, green and red light emitting diodes (LED) for 22 min and driving a mini rotating motor, demonstrating the excellent potential of the new material for a variety of applications. To the best of the authorsâ€™ knowledge, this is the first study to use post synthetic modification of a MOF with a suitable pore size to optimize the material for use in SCs.

1. Introduction

The development of advanced energy storage/conversion materials and strategies has become essential in this time of environmental and energy crises on a global scale.1,2 With everyday items such as mobile phones, portable computers and car electronics becoming more and more energy demanding as their functions improve, there is a desperate need for solutions.3,4 Supercapacitors (SCs), as one of the energy storage systems with a large power density, good reversibility and long cycle life, have attracted a large share of attention in the quest for improved energy storage and conversion materials.5â€“7 An outstanding electrode material must be selected to meet the requirements for enhancing energy density without sacrificing power density and cycle life.8,9

So far, there have been several different types of electrode materials under development for SCs, which can be divided into three general groups:10â€“12 (1) carbonaceous materials with a large surface area, (2) conducting polymers, and (3) metal oxides and their derivatives, like CuO, IrO2, MnO2, Co3O4, ZnCo2O4, MnMoO4, CoV2O6etc. The contact resistance between carbon particles of carbonaceous materials causes an internal series resistance.13 Energy storage for the latter two groups typically involves quick and reversible surface redox (faradaic) reactions while they are used as electrode materials, a process also referred to as pseudocapacitance. Unfortunately for the conducting polymers, they distend and contract during the charging/discharging procedure, which frequently causes their mechanical degradation, decreasing their electrochemical efficiencies.14,15 We propose to add, in earnest, a fourth horse to the race: metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) with their high surface areas, adaptable pore size/shape and combined redox metal centers have good theoretical potential to be applied as electrode materials in SCs.16â€“18 After the first report of Co8-MOF-5 for SCs,19 development of methods for applying original MOFs as electrode materials has been widely studied. Lee et al.20 synthesised a Co-MOF-71 film with a specific capacitance of 206.76 F gâˆ’1 at 0.6 A gâˆ’1 in a LiOH electrolyte, with about 98.5% of its initial capacitance being retained after 1000 electrochemical cycles. Lin et al.21 prepared two MOFs with redox-active ligands for SCs; however their specific capacitances were only 23 and 22 F gâˆ’1, respectively. Their overall outcomes demonstrated poor conductivity, and it seems that the microporous nature and poor stability are an issue for these materials. Efforts to overcome these issues have been sought more fervently in recent years.

One approach is applying a conductive binder forming â€œbridgesâ€ to facilitate a â€œMOF-to-conducting binder-to-MOFâ€ conducting mechanism: the Yaghi group reported this concept for the first time.22 In their study, they first physically combined graphene, a 2D carbon material, with original MOF particles. In the obtained materials, graphene sheets not only played a role as â€œbridgesâ€ between MOF particles, facilitating a â€œMOF-to-graphene-to-MOFâ€ conducting mechanism, but also aided in separating the MOF particles.

Most MOFs have been restricted as precursors/templates for synthesizing porous carbons, which typically need the application of harsh pre-treatment reagents, like alkalis, and high carbonization temperatures above 800 Â°C.23,24 In general, MOFs can be used in three ways: (i) original MOFs; (ii) destruction of MOFs to achieve metal-oxides; and (iii) pyrolysis of MOFs to provide porous carbons. Pure MOFs have very low conductivity, which is not at all useful for SCs, whereas the second and third processes require high energy consumption and time-consuming processes, which do not get past the research lab. Also, with pyrolysis procedures, it is very difficult to control what structure is obtained.25,26 Moreover, the weak electrical conductivity of MOFs delays electron transfer between a MOF based electrode and current collectors.27,28 All this goes towards explaining why MOFs have seldom been used as electrode materials in SCs.

Besides low conductivity, the nearly exclusively microporous (<2 nm) MOFs inhibit the diffusion of electrolyte ions,29 significantly restraining their use as electrode materials for SCs, particularly at a large current density. Several investigations have indicated that a hierarchical porous structure could improve the capacitive efficiency, rate capability and cycle life of porous materials in SCs through shortening the ion diffusion distance and minimizing the diffusion resistance to transporting mass at the electrode and electrolyte interface.30 One of the reasons MOFs are attractive for many applications is that loading of a guest species can modify their properties (e.g. electrical conductivity,31 ion absorption capacity,32 and optical features33) and structure significantly. Several important uses are electric/electronic devices,34 data storage,35 electrochemistry,36 heterogeneous catalysis,37etc. Consequently, their porosity and metal cations provide an ideal environment for electrolytes and redox active sites, respectively.38â€“41 Metal sulfides have attracted attention in a large number of research areas, such as solar cells, Li-ion batteries, photocatalysis, and particularly SCs.42,43 In recent years, transition metal sulfides, having increased intrinsic electroconductivity, chemical resistance against alkaline electrolytes, low cost, and ecological sustainability, have been considered as building blocks in the design of energy storage/conversion materials.44,45 Metal sulfides are not stable thermodynamically in aqueous media and strongly oxidizing media used for reactions, and can be simply oxidized to the relevant metal oxides/hydroxides.46â€“48 Moreover, their specific capacitance is decreased and its progress to a higher level is extremely restricted as a result of their small specific surface area and electrical conductivity.49 It is proposed that introducing sulfides into MOF pores can increase the electrical conductivity and stability significantly. Therefore, it is a practical way to produce low-cost MOFs with excellent electrical conductivities to improve their electrochemical efficiencies. The presence of pores and functional groups of ligands in the MOFs can provide conditions for MOF sulfurization. Moreover, the presence of functional groups inside the MOF cavities can provide sites for molecules such as Na2S to bind as a source of sulfur, which ultimately aids diffusion of electrons through the MOF cavities.

In this work, we synthesize a new three-dimensional Co(II)-based MOF [Co(oba)2(bpfb)4](DMF)2, TMU-61, and then perform a fast in situ pore sulfurization (2 min) of TMU-61 on Ni foam at ambient temperature in a Na2S solution. Based on the results obtained, we can propose the application of S-TMU-61 as an appropriate electrode material for SCs. To the best of the authors' knowledge, related investigations have not been reported on any other MOF. This is the first report using a Na2S-loaded MOF as an electrode material for application in SCs for enhancing their effectiveness. Following pore sulfurization, the S-TMU-61 electrode demonstrates great specific capacitance, better rate capability and a longer cycling lifetime when compared with TMU-61. A maximum specific capacitance of 636.6 F gâˆ’1 was achieved for this MOF, which far exceeds that of all previous MOFs. This production approach is ecologically benign and economically feasible.

Here, the question is about the mechanism for storing charges in the electrode material. Energy storage in SCs takes place through various mechanisms. According to these mechanisms, three SC categories are introduced. Electrical double layer capacitors are superconductors where charge storage takes place through adsorption of electrolyte ions on the surface of electrode materials and redox reactions do not occur. These properties are detected using rectangular cyclic voltammograms and the linear dependence of potential changes on time at a constant current. In the second category of SCs where battery-like electrodes save the energy, faradaic peaks are observed in cyclic voltammetric diagrams in which the potential difference between the oxidation peaks and reduction peaks is about 0.1 to 0.2 V, and two phases are observed in their galvanostatic chargeâ€“discharge diagrams. In the third group, pseudocapacitors, redox peaks are also found in the cyclic voltammetric diagrams, indicating a lower potential difference between the oxidation and reduction peaks.79,80 Recently, oxides, carbides, and nitrides have been considered as the electrode materials in this category. Based on the provided description and the results obtained in the present work, the electrode material in this work is categorized as a battery-like class with the faradaic charge storage mechanism.

4. Conclusions

This study on S-TMU-61 presents a facile, fast and efficient pore sulfurization approach to prepare a high-performance MOF for SCs. In a three electrode system, the S-TMU-61 electrode material shows more specific capacitance and better cycling stability in 2 M KOH electrolyte than any other MOF to date. A specific capacitance of 636.6 F gâˆ’1 was obtained for the S-TMU-61 electrode at 5 A gâˆ’1 in 2 M KOH. Moreover, 94% of the initial capacitance is retained after 6000 cycles. A new S-TMU-61//AC ASC, fabricated using the S-TMU-61 electrode as the cathode and an activated carbon electrode as the anode, shows a high cell voltage of 1.7 V, a high energy density of 25.73 W h kgâˆ’1 and a power density of 2549.3 W kgâˆ’1. Two cells of the S-TMU-61//AC ASC were used to light blue, green, yellow and red LED indicators for 22 min and drive a mini rotating motor indicating its potential for a myriad of practical applications. Consequently, the outcomes show that introduction of S2âˆ’ ions into the microporous TMU-61 successfully changes the specific capacitance of this material, and can resolve the issue of lower electrical conductivity of MOFs. The large efficiency and electrical activity of S-TMU-61 are assigned to its overall beneficial structural and electronic properties, resulting from pore sulfurization: the resulting structure is extremely conductive, exhibits little Na2S release under reaction conditions, can be consistently dispersed on Ni foam, and shows increased reactivity at the active sites. Thus, S-TMU-61 is a potential electrode material for SCs, because of its good electrochemical efficiency. This study opens a new path for developing advanced electrode materials for use in SCs with no pyrolysis and calcination required. Additional studies in the field of MOFs as electrode materials are ongoing in our laboratory.
