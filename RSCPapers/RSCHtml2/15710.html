Research progress of ABX3-type lead-free perovskites for optoelectronic applications: materials and devices

Abstract

In recent years, lead halide perovskite materials have attracted great interest and are widely used in solar cells and light-emitting devices due to their high photoelectronic quantum yield, high color purity, high defect tolerance, long diffusion length, high carrier mobility, and bandgap tunability. However, the application of lead halide perovskites is limited due to the presence of Pb, making lead-free perovskites an important substitute due to their same crystal structure and similar properties. Although some reports have been made on lead-free perovskite materials, there are still great challenges to realize their application due to their poor stability, easy phase transition, and low photoelectric conversion efficiency. Here, we mainly summarize the development and application of ABX3-type lead-free halide perovskite materials, especially in optoelectronic devices. The article first introduces the lattice and energy band structure, the optoelectronic properties of lead-free perovskites, including the research method of lead-free perovskites, and then analyzes the reasons for the low luminous efficiency and poor stability of lead-free perovskite materials. Second, the development history and current situation of lead-free perovskites in different optoelectronic device applications are summarized. Finally, we present the challenges and prospects for the future development of lead-free perovskites.

Introduction

Perovskites are a class of materials with a crystal structure similar to calcium titanate, and they have attracted the attention from many researchers due to their excellent optoelectronic properties, such as high color purity, bandgap tunability, high photoluminescence quantum yield (PLQY), and high carrier mobility.17 Especially in the past two decades, the properties of perovskites and their applications in solar cells,8,9 light-emitting diodes,10,11 and other devices have been greatly developed, which can be seen from the global and continental research reports on perovskite materials (Fig. 1(a)) published in the past ten years.

Perovskite materials were used in solar cells for the first time in 2009 with a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of only 3.8%,12 which has since exceeded 25% over ten years of development,1316 higher than that of commercial silicon solar cells, as shown in Fig. 1(b). However, high-performance perovskites all contain Pb ions. During the entire life cycle of the product, Pb2+ will continuously dissociate into the environment, causing pollution to the ecological environment and endangering life and health.17 What's more, Pb is also strictly controlled by countries around the world, which seriously restricts the commercial application of lead perovskite materials. Therefore, research on lead-free perovskites with excellent properties has gradually attracted researchers attention, and scientists have made great efforts toward it in the last decade (Fig. 1(c) and (d)).

At present, the synthesis of lead-free perovskites is mainly to replace the B ions in ABX3 with other nontoxic metal ions with similar properties to Pb,18,19 such as germanium, tin, copper, silver, and bismuth, and a lot of progress has been made in theoretical calculations and experiments. However, there are still some problems in the research and development of lead-free perovskites, such as poor stability20 and unsatisfactory luminescence properties,5,20 which result in challenges to obtaining lead-free perovskite materials with excellent performance.

Preparation and important properties of perovskites

Preparation of perovskite crystals

There are generally four preparation methods for perovskites, which are solution-based methods, the high-temperature Bridgeman method, high-temperature vapor phase epitaxy method and space-limited crystallization strategy. Solution-based methods are preferred for their facile processability, and they make it possible to obtain a material with the desired properties.21 The solubility of perovskite precursors in the corresponding solvents can be modulated by changing the growth temperature and concentrations, resulting in a controllable growth of crystals. The reduction in precursor solubility plays a driving role throughout the solution-based approach, which makes precise control of precursor solubility important for the synthesis of high-quality single crystals.22 The high-temperature Bridgeman method is a classic melted crystal growth method which was developed in the last century. The crude materials sealed in a quartz ampoule in a vacuum are first melted at their melting temperature, followed by nucleation occurring at the tip of the quartz ampoule at a definite temperature, and gradually expanding to the entire molten materials. Because of the sequent nucleation of the molten materials, the obtained single crystals exhibit rodlike or columnar sharps with large sizes.22 The high-temperature vapor phase epitaxy method is a classic method used to prepare single crystals with kinetically controlled growth. A mismatch between substrates and epitaxial crystals plays an important role in the morphology and quality of single crystals, which make it sensitive to the lattice mismatch between the substrate and the perovskite crystal.22 As for the space-limited crystallization strategy, it is more appropriate for vertical structured optoelectronic devices.22

Crystal structure

ABX3 perovskites have a cubic crystal structure, their space group is Pmm, and they have a unit cell containing 5 atoms. The positions of A, B and X atoms in the unit cell are A (0.00, 0.00, 0.00), B (0.50, 0.50, 0.50), and X (0.50, 0.50, 0.00), respectively. B2+ and its coordinated X form an octahedral space structure, with B2+ the center of the octahedral structure and X the vertex of the octahedron. The unit cell structure is shown in Fig. 2(a).

In the synthesis of lead-free perovskites, the Pb2+ at the B site is usually replaced by a nontoxic metal ion. However, due to the difference in ion radii, the stable lattice structure will be distorted when the element is replaced, or even the crystal structure will be destroyed, resulting in a phase transition. Therefore, to form the perovskite crystal structure, it is necessary to take into account both the tolerance factor t and the octahedral factor (rA, rB, and rX represent the ionic radius of elements A, B, and X, respectively). This indicates that a new type of perovskite structure can exist stably when t is between 0.8 and 1.0 and is between 0.414 and 0.732.18 A bulk perovskite structure cannot be formed with a small t, but a low-dimensional perovskite structure can be formed with a larger t. A simple strategy to ensure a stable perovskite structure is to select an element with a similar ionic radius and electronic structure to Pb. There are also some elements with quite different electronic structures and ionic radii with Pb2+ that can still satisfy the tolerance factor and octahedral factor by forming double perovskites. Band structure, optical and electronic properties

Perovskites have a direct bandgap, with the valence band maximum (VBM) and the conduction band minimum (CBM) located at the R (0.5, 0.5, 0.5) position of the k Brillouin zone.23,24 In perovskites, the B and X elements have a relatively larger influence on the bandgap structure,18,24 because most of the density of states near the VBM and CBM are generated by them, while A hardly contributes to the density of states. The dependence of the valence band and conduction band of ABX3 materials on the BX bonding strength decreases according to the order of halogen electronegativity Cl < Br < I, which makes the emission and absorption characteristics of perovskite adjustable by changing the halogen ion, as shown in Fig. 2(c).25,26

Bandgaps of various ABX3-type perovskites have been systematically studied with first-principles calculations. In 2017, Jiang et al. obtained ten perovskite structures with non-zero band gaps using first-principles calculations (Fig. 3(b)),27 considering three crystalline phases and 27 divalent metal elements in the periodic table. Of these, five perovskites (MAXI3: X = Pb, Sn, Ge, Cd, Be) have suitable band gaps as light-absorbing materials for solar cells. Mao et al. performed first-principles calculations on the band gaps of 260 possible ABX3-type all-inorganic halide perovskites for potential photovoltaic applications.2 They selected five elements, Li, Na, K, Rb, and Cs, as A-site elements, three elements, Pb, Sn, and Ge, as B-site elements, and four elements, F, Cl, Br, and I, as X-site elements. The three most common crystalline phases were selected, including the cubic phase, tetragonal phase, and two orthogonal phases, and a trigonal phase was also selected for building the Ge-based chalcogenide model. The obtained band gap distribution is shown in Fig. 3(c). Considering the multiple factors of perovskite formation, they report three all-inorganic lead-free halide perovskites, including cubic KSnCl3, cubic RbSnCl3, and triangular NaGeBr3, as materials with desirable band gaps (1.24 to 1.44 eV) for photovoltaic applications.28

Lead halide perovskites have both a pp transition and a direct bandgap. This can explain well the excellent absorption performance of lead halide as absorber layers for solar cells. The pp transitions can only be possible for compound semiconductors containing cation elements that exhibit lone pairs of electrons, such as Ge2+, Sn2+, Pb2+, Sb3+, and Bi3+, which is also a major factor for us adopting these elements in the preparation of lead-free perovskites.

The numerical differences in the band gaps of ABX3 perovskites with a single type of halide ions will be obvious. Taking CsPbX3 as an example, the band gap values are 3.0 eV (X = Cl),32 2.39 eV (X = Br)33 and 1.73 eV (X = I).34 Different band gaps correspond to different wavelength absorption, and the absorption and emission can be adjusted with multiple halide ions. Cs3Bi2(ClxBryI1xy)9 (0 x y 1) has shown absorption and emission continuously adjustable in the full visible range.35

Halide perovskites have moderate refractive indices in the visible range compared to conventional semiconductor materials. The real part of the refractive index of perovskites is significantly larger than that for SiO2 or most polymers, making perovskites a good material for resonant nanostructures, due to a high enough optical contrast. On the other hand, it is much lower than those for Si and GaAs, providing high optical contrast with these materials in advanced hybrid structures.3638 The refractive index is strongly dependent on the crystal morphology. For example, MAPbI3 has a refractive index of 2.45 at 800 nm as a single crystal, while the number is only 1.95 when as a thin film.39

Hybrid halide perovskites are strong candidates for applications in nonlinear optics. For instance, MAPbI3 shows a large third-order nonlinear response in the near-infrared transmission window of thin films and single crystals.4043 Silicon is considered to be an important material for nonlinear optics, and MAPbI3 has shown Kerr optical nonlinearity several orders of magnitude larger than that of silicon, showing great potential. The source of third-order optical nonlinearity and the consequent refractive index changes in most materials is the Kerr effect,44 which is usually weak in thin films, where excitation of electrons through direct single- or multi-photon absorption processes can bring about much larger refractive index changes than the Kerr effect. Kalanoor et al. pointed out that the nonlinear change of refractive index in perovskites is realized by the generation of free carriers,40 and interband free carrier absorption by single-photon excitation is the main mechanism leading to nonlinearity for resonant excitation. In the non-resonant region, bound carriers dominate at lower pump intensities, while free carriers and two-photon absorption mechanisms dominate at higher pump intensities. The large carrier-induced refractive index change would suggest hybrid perovskites have significant consequences for optical switching and lasing applications, both based on optical pumping and eventual electrical excitation or modulation. However, many reports indicate that hybrid halide perovskites have low laser-induced damage thresholds, which may hinder their use in nonlinear applications that typically require high optical intensities. Second-order processes include the generation of second harmonics and are limited to materials with non-centrosymmetric crystal structures. Since most perovskite materials have a centrosymmetric structure, even if with an asymmetric organic molecule in the A site (such as MA = CH3NH3), it will not produce more second-order nonlinearity,39 which makes it not ideal in this regard.

Carrier transport properties

The performance of optoelectronic devices is highly correlated with the carrier dynamics of semiconductors, and the effective mass of a carrier has a great influence on its mobility. The mobility will decrease with the increase of effective mass, leading to the accumulation of electrons and holes on opposite sides, which will intensify Auger recombination.45 Conversely, a smaller carrier effective mass results in a higher carrier mobility, longer carrier diffusion lengths and less nonradiative recombination. Band edges derived from p orbitals generally exhibit larger effective masses than those derived from more delocalized s orbitals,46 fortunately, in perovskites, the p-orbital-derived CBM from B exhibits a very large splitting due to the strong SOC which reduces the effective mass of the carriers.4648 Furthermore, the strong sp coupling at the R point enhances the dispersion of the upper VB and results in a small effective mass for holes.31 The importance of the SOC effect in halide perovskites was first highlighted by Even et al.49 Considering the influence of the SOC effect, the Pb 6p-derived CBM exhibits a very large splitting and the band gap is reduced from 1.2 eV to 0.1 eV. The influence of SOC on te effective mass of carriers in the MAPbI3 was studied by Umari.50 The average me and mh are 0.73 and 0.36 m0 (me and mh are the effective masses of electrons and holes, respectively, and m0 is the free electron mass) with the standard generalized gradient approximation (GGA) functional calculation, while the GGA + SOC calculations showed significantly decreased me and mh of 0.17 and 0.28 m0, which are close to the values (0.19 and 0.25 m0) calculated from the more advanced many-body perturbation (GW) theory with the inclusion of SOC.

Surface defects were found to be one of the main sources of trapped charge carriers. Therefore, surface treatment with ligands or passivators is considered an effective means to reduce the trap density in perovskite nanocrystals,51,52 and the corresponding means will be stated in the Defects section. These surface states may also lead to the formation of the trion which refers to the bound complex of two electrons (holes) and one hole (electron).53 The Auger recombination of trion is considered to be responsible for the decreased PL efficiency and PL blinking for single nanocrystal emission.5456 Makarov et al. found that triple ion recombination can be effectively suppressed by stirring.56 Recently, it has also been found that the problem of triple ions can be solved by surface passivation.57

The carrier mobility in nanomaterials is also affected by nano-effects. Compared with polycrystalline films, the carrier mobility in perovskite nanocrystals is reduced by more than an order of magnitude, which has limited the progress of nanocrystal-based solar energy.58 The dynamics of charge carriers in nanocrystal films are susceptible to the nanocrystal size due to the carrier localization effect. As the nanocrystal size decreases, the carrier mobility decreases significantly and the carrier lifetime shortens significantly. The carrier transport in nanocrystals is mainly contributed by interdot hopping due to the coupled wavefunctions of electrons and holes between adjacent nanocrystals before recombination. Therefore, enhancing the coupling between nanocrystals by surface chemistry can lead to a higher mobility and longer lifetime. Sanehira et al. improved the carrier mobility and lifetime by treating perovskite nanocrystals with Pb(NO3)2 and A-site cationic halide salts.59

Defects

The defect properties of halide perovskites play important roles in the optoelectronic device performance, for their inevitable existence in the active layers. In optoelectronic devices, deep-level defects mainly play the role of recombination centers, and shorten the lifetime of minority carriers, while shallow level defects are basically donors or acceptors, which mainly provide carriers.60,61 The ideal lattice of a perovskite and three typical point defects are shown in Fig. 4(a)(d). For lead halide perovskites, as shown in Fig. 4(e), most defects (Ii, MAPb, VMA, VPb, MAi, PbMA, VI and MAI) are shallow-level ones, and their formation energies are relatively low, while for a few deep-level defects (IMA, IPb, Pbi and PbI), the formation energy is relatively higher, which means most of the defects are in shallow energy levels, and brought them a good performance.31 Fortunately, Sn-based perovskites have similar defect properties (Fig. 4(f)),62 and it's believed that the excellent defect performance of halide perovskites is due to the strong sp coupling at the VBM.

Except for the point defects, interfacial defects are also critical in the device performance, which mainly appears at grain boundaries with a much larger density in the grains. With a larger grain size and fewer grain boundaries, a lower density of defect will be formed in the perovskite film, which can reduce carrier recombination and enhance the device's PCE. Studies have shown that the addition of dimethyl sulfoxide in the precursor solution is beneficial to the preparation of dense perovskite films with a large grain size, and the addition of C60 to the precursor solution can tune the perovskite crystallization and passivate grain boundaries, thereby reducing the trap density.63 There are also possibilities of defect migration at the interface between a perovskite and carrier transport layers affecting the efficiency of carrier extraction in turn.64,65

For the deep-energy-based defects and interfacial defects, some methods have been used to passivate them to improve the material properties. (1) Materials engineering. Composition engineering in forming mixed-cation (MA, FA and Cs) and mixed-anion (Cl, Br and I) perovskites has been reported to be an efficient method to improve the performance and stability of perovskite-based devices. The characteristics of high-efficiency solar cells containing such mixed perovskites have shown lower defect densities and also have a high defect tolerance.66,67 On the other hand, for uncoordinated B ions, this can be solved by introducing more halide ions in the preparation of materials. Zhang et al. reported the passivation of uncoordinated lead ions and iodine vacancies in an all-inorganic perovskite through additional anionic dopants like chloride, which reduced the defect density and also improved the morphology of perovskite crystals.68 Similarly, Wang et al. reported that antisolvent-washing treatment with excess iodine can be beneficial to enhance the PCE of solar cells, because this kind of treatment promotes better crystallization and defect passivation of undercoordinated Pb2+ defects and dangling bonds.69 (2) Extrinsic doping. Passivation of halide vacancies through immobilization or vacancy filling processes can prevent the migration of halide ions and reduce the currentvoltage hysteresis of solar cell devices. K+ has been found to be the most suitable candidate, for it can easily accommodate itself in the interstitial position instead of substituting the lead ions. In addition, it can prevent iodine migration and reduce the hysteresis of the currentvoltage characteristics of solar cell devices.70 (3) Lewis acidbase reaction passivation. The insufficiently coordinated B and X ions and dangling bonds on the perovskite surface reduce the stability of the material and the efficiency of perovskite-based devices, which can be passivated by derivatives of amines, carboxyl, hydroxyl, carbonyl, and phosphine oxides with a lone pair of electrons can. The lone pair of electrons on Lewis bases passivates the undercoordinated lead ions by forming coordination with them and thereby improving the optical and electronic properties of such passivated perovskites.7173 (4) Interfacial passivation. Introducing a passivation layer between the perovskite absorber layer and the charge transport layer is a typical technique to reduce defect density. Most commonly, the introduction of MABr and FABr between the perovskite layer and the hole transport layer (HTL),74,75 or the introduction of fullerene between the perovskite layer and the electron transport layer (ETL), can be used as effective interfacial passivation layers.76 (5) Physical passivation methods. In addition to chemical modification of the perovskite structure, physical treatment of perovskite films can also reduce the defect density. The corresponding methods include: light-induced passivation,77 annealing at a suitable temperature,78 hot casting,79 vacuum and solvent-assisted annealing,80 and anti-solvent-assisted crystallization.81 (6) Passivation of 3D perovskites with 2D perovskites. 2D perovskites not only protect perovskite layers from thermal degradation, but also prevent ion migration,82 and the reaction of long-chain organic spacers with perovskites has been found to be effective.83 Wang et al. introduced BA into 3D perovskites to form (BA)x(FA0.83Cs0.17)1xPb(I0.6Br0.4)3, and observed that the 2D phase contributes to the enhancement of crystallinity by reducing crystal defects and suppressing nonradiative recombination pathways.83

Doping

Doping perovskite ABX3 with targeted ions could tune the crystalline structure, electronic structure, luminescence properties, radiative recombination dynamics, and electrical features, which play important roles in the improvement of materials and enhancement of device performances.

A-site doping indeed has revealed a feasible strategy for improving the stability of phase structure. The appropriate ion radius of doped A is an aspect to improve stability. Take FAPbI3 as an example, a too large FA cation causes a highly anisotropic strain of the cubic phase lattice, leading to the deviation from the equilibrium interplanar distances, whereas the lattice strain could be relaxed by alloying smaller MA cations.84 In a similar way, double or triple cations of (Cs, FA)PbI3 or (Cs, MA, FA)PbI3 show the same achievements in better stability.8588 Generally, A-site doping will slightly increase or decrease the bandgaps depending on the size of the introduced cations. Taking CsPbX3 as the host, incorporation of larger impurities (such as FA or MA) will decrease the bandgap, whereas smaller (such as Rb or K) dopants will increase the bandgap,89 and further influence the PL spectrum.87,9092 Changing the halogen can also expand the luminescence range of perovskites.9396

In contrast to the A-site, B-site doping faces more challenges, which also play a big role in phase stability. These ions could be divided into two categories based on valence: divalent and trivalent. Divalent ions do not change the phase structure of perovskite, but may induce the lattice contraction/expansion or change the size of crystal particles,97,98 and furthermore a change of thermal stability. Stam et al. doped Sn2+, Cd2+ and Zn2+ into the CsPbBr3 system respectively.98 Compared with the PbBr bond, the newly generated BBr bond has a shorter length, which enhances the interaction between the B and X orbitals, resulting in a wider band gap while causing lattice shrinkage and improved stability. Changes in the band gap also bring about a red or blue shift in the PL spectrum. Ions with similar effects are Sr2+ and Ni2+, which do not produce new radiation centers or trap states for the light emission of perovskites.99,100 While for trivalent ions, like Bi3+ and rare earth (RE) ions (Ce3+, Sm3+, Eu3+, Yb3+, Er3+, Dy3+, Tb3+, Tm3+), there are abundant energy levels in the band structure, which provide new transfer channels for the excited electrons and induce luminescence decay.1,101 In particular, although Mn is a divalent ion, it can also exhibit the same characteristics. Cai et al. prepared Mn2+/Yb3+ co-doped CsPbCl3 nanocrystals by altering the stoichiometry of the Mn2+, Yb3+ and Pb2+ precursors, and two emission peaks appear except for the intrinsic emission of CsPbCl3, and an optimized PLQY of 125.3% was obtained.101 The exciton energy transfer process is shown in Fig. 4(g).

Defect trapping is the main cause of nonradiative transitions and the low quantum yield, and appropriate doping or passivation can effectively reduce nonradiative transitions and tune photoluminescence, thereby improving the PLQY, prolonging PL lifetime and enhancing device properties.103 Surface defects account for the vast majority of overall defects in perovskites,104 and the use of A-site, B-site and X-site doping can effectively passivate surface defects and reduce nonradiative states.89 Shi et al. doped CsPbI3 quantum dots with Yb3+, which can replace Pb2+ while reducing the defect concentration of Cs+ vacancies and I vacancies, reducing the recombination of electronhole pairs and indirectly improving the carrier transport capability.105

Stability

Currently, stability is an important issue limiting lead-free perovskites. For instance, Sn2+ can be easily oxidized to Sn4+, resulting in Sn2+ vacancies.106 There are similar problems with Ge-based perovskites, which also cause the instability of Ge-based perovskites. On the other hand, as mentioned above, defects can also destabilize perovskites. There are different strategies to improve the stability of lead-free perovskites. Adding substances with reducing properties to the precursor solution during the synthesis can largely slow down the oxidation of Sn2+ and Ge2+,107 doping smaller A-site cations (e.g. Cs+) to narrow the lattice seems to slow down the oxidation reaction as well.106 The solution to the stability problem caused by defects has been introduced in the section on defects. Passivation of defects by various means can achieve this goal.

Development of lead-free perovskites and optoelectronic applications

Lead-free perovskite solar cells

Perovskite materials have attracted many researchers attention in the study of solar cell applications, for the excellent properties compared with the traditional materials.108,109 Excellent light absorption capacity is the most important characteristic of perovskite in solar cells. As mentioned above, Si has a pp orbital which is favorable for electronic transitions in its energy band, but also has an indirect band gap. On the other hand, GaAs has the characteristics of a direct band gap but lacks pp orbitals that are conducive to electronic transitions. Perovskite integrates the direct band gap and pp orbitals, resulting in an unexpectedly excellent absorption ability. It can be observed from Fig. 5(a) and (b), that the JDOS of CH3NH3PbI3 is significantly higher than that of GaAs, and the absorption coefficient in the visible region also has the same trend. With an excellent light absorption ability, the energy conversion efficiency of Pb-based perovskite solar cells has exceeded 25%. Meanwhile, perovskites have a higher carrier mobility than typical traditional semiconductor materials. As mentioned above, in perovskites, the p-orbital-derived CBM from B exhibits very large splitting due to a strong SOC and the strong sp coupling at the R point, which enhances the dispersion on the upper VB, and thus reduces the carrier effective mass. The lower effective carrier mass directly brings a higher carrier mobility. Besides, perovskites have high defect tolerance capabilities. The donors in some traditional semiconductor materials such as CIS and CZTS usually have much higher formation energies than the acceptors, which makes the acceptors dominate the electrical conductivity.110 In perovskites, however, the formation energies of major donor and acceptor defects are comparable,60 and there are few deep-level defects, which enables more balanced carrier transport and higher efficiency in perovskite solar cells. The lead-free perovskites usually have a similar crystal and band structure to Pb-based perovskites, especially ABX3-typed ones, which give them the same excellent characteristics of absorption, mobility and defect tolerance, and the advantages for solar cell applications compared to traditional materials.

In recent years, the PCE of lead-free perovskite solar cells has been steadily improved,9,112,113 however, poor stability is still a major reason hindering the development of lead-free perovskite solar cells. Compared with Pb-based perovskite solar cells whose lifetime has increased from the first few seconds114 to nearly one year,115 the lifetime of lead-free perovskite solar cells is far from this level.8 Therefore, at present, the main problems focus on PCE and stability for lead-free perovskite solar cells. The effects of micro-strain and lattice mismatches between the layers cannot be ignored in the preparation of solar cells.139,140 Research shows that the resistivity of certain perovskite polycrystals will increase dramatically at low temperatures due to the large lattice mismatch and the strain relaxation. Meanwhile, higher lattice mismatch and strain relaxation will lead to higher paramagneticferromagnetic phase transition temperatures and metalinsulator transition temperatures of perovskite films.141 The lattice strain increase from the lattice mismatch between the substrate and films has a great influence on film quality and carrier mobility. It has been reported that the mismatch and strain decreases with increasing film thickness on a quartz substrate,142 and the lattice strain can be almost relaxed for a 20 m thick film. Unfortunately, this relaxation process could also produce some kinds of disorders, such as stacking faults, dislocations, etc., which induce the low value of Curie temperature and metalinsulator transition temperature in films.141,142

Crystallite size and grain size of perovskite will also have a greater impact on the device. As mentioned above, grain boundaries and the density of defects formed in perovskite films will decrease with larger grain size, which in turn reduces carrier recombination and improves the PCE of the device. If the grain size of the film is large, the longitudinal direction of the film will be composed of fewer grains. This superior structure can ensure that most of the photogenerated charges can encounter fewer grain boundaries in the process of reaching the electrodes, reducing the charge load at the grain boundaries, thus enabling solar cell devices with high photoelectric conversion efficiency.143 The grain size is especially important for perovskite thin films when used in magneto-resistive devices.144 The electrical conductivity and magnetic properties of these thin films demonstrate a grain size effect relatively at low temperatures, where the inter-particle conduction and magnetic properties become dependent on the grain size and the distance between the grains.145

There are three basic solar cell structures, which are mesoscopic, planar, and inverted architectures. More interest has been aroused toward planar and inverted architectures, because they do not require perovskite films to be deposited on mesoporous scaffolds. A nonporous homogenous perovskite film is important for the device's performance. Xiao et al. reported a one-step, solvent-induced, fast crystallization-deposition method and obtained flat, highly uniform CH3NH3PbI3 thin films. This simple approach involves the spincoating of a DMF(N,N-dimethylformamide, 153 C) solution of CH3NH3PbI3 on a substrate, followed immediately by exposure of the wet film to a second solvent, such as chlorobenzene, to induce crystallization, which can fulfill the film formation within 1 min, and easily control the thickness of the perovskite film by changing the concentration of the solution.146 In the spin-coating process, antisolvent can be used to ensure good perovskite morphology.146 By contrast, in a two-step method, a PbI2 film is prepared on the substrate and subsequently reacts with CH3NH3I, followed by an annealing treatment.14,147,148 The two-step method may require more preparation time, however, both of them can produce high-quality films.

The annealing temperature during the preparation also plays an important role in the film quality and material properties. The results of Goktas et al. showed that as the annealing temperature increases, the lattice constant decreases and the grain size increases.145 In 2018, Gao et al. adopted the anti-solvent dropping technology to deposit high quality Cs2AgBiBr6 films with ultra-smooth morphology.149 The grain size of Cs2AgBiBr6 gradually increases as the post-annealing temperature increases, and a device with a PCE of 2.23% was prepared when taking an annealing temperature of 300 C. Zhu et al. replaced 10 mol% of Pb in perovskite films with Sn to construct an inverted PSC based on MASn0.1Pb0.9I3, and found that the DMSO vapor-assisted annealing treatment was beneficial to the preparation of dense SnPb hybrid perovskite films with large and smooth grains, and the PCE of the device reached 10.25%.150

Sn-based perovskite solar cells. Sn is considered to be the most promising element to replace Pb since Sn and Pb are in the same main group and have very similar ionic radii,151 which makes the tolerance factor and octahedral factor of Sn-based perovskites well fulfilled. Research work on Sn-based solar cells has also been carried out relatively early. In 2014, Hao et al. fabricated the first solar cell device using CH3NH3SnI3xBrx material as the photoabsorption layer.126 Devices with CH3NH3SnI3 perovskite showed a notable absorption onset up to 950 nm due to the ideal optical bandgap of 1.3 eV, which is significantly redshifted compared with its benchmark CH3NH3PbI3 counterpart (1.55 eV). Modulation of the bandgap can be achieved by adjusting the ratio of I to Br in CH3NH3SnI3xBrx materials to achieve absorption at wavelengths from 954 nm to 577 nm. On this basis, Hao et al. investigated solvent effects on the crystallization of the lead-free CH3NH3SnI3 perovskite films in a solution growth process and obtained highly uniform, pinhole-free perovskite films from a DMSO solution via a transitional SnI23DMSO intermediate phase. This high-quality perovskite film enables the realization of heterojunction depleted solar cells based on a mesoporous TiO2 layer with an unprecedented photocurrent up to 21 mA cm2 in the absence of any hole-transporting material (Fig. 6(a)).152 Meanwhile, the CH3NH3SnI3 device can operate continuously for 300 s (Fig. 6(b)), possessing a lifetime comparable to that of the CH3NH3PbI3 device.

The currentvoltage and efficiency of Sn-based perovskite solar cells are still largely lower than those of Pb-based perovskite solar cells,113 and the Sn2+ in the bulk material are easily oxidized to Sn4+, which can lead to severe p-type self-doping, resulting in some Sn vacancies and forming defects in the perovskite film.153156 Considering the instability caused by the oxidation from Sn2+ to Sn4+, it can be significantly improved by adding a reducing agent to inhibit the oxidation of Sn2+ in the process of preparing the material.107 SnF2 is a strong reducing agent, which can improve the photovoltaic performance of Sn-based perovskite solar cells to a certain extent. As early as 2015, Koh's team tried to add SnF2 to the precursor to synthesize FASnI3 crystals. The final devices exhibited a PCE of 2.10%, significantly improved compared to the performance of FASnI3 without SnF2 on photovoltaic devices in photocurrent density, photoelectric voltage and PCE (less than 0.02% to 2.10%).157 A year later, Liao et al. further explored the effect of SnF2 doping in the precursor when synthesizing FASnI3 crystals.158 They found that the degree of surface coverage of FASnI3 films on poly(3,4-ethylene dioxythiophene):poly(styrene sulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS) was significantly dependent on the amount of SnF2 added in the precursor. Flower-like structures and many pinholes are observed on the surface of pure FASnI3 films grown on PEDOT:PSS. With a 5 mol% SnF2 additive, the flower-like structures disappear and the FASnI3 films become more uniform in appearance. However, pinhole formation within the film still occurs, albeit at a lower density. When the SnF2 concentration is increased to 10, 15 and 20 mol% (Fig. 7(c)(e)), highly uniform and compact FASnI3 films are obtained without any pinholes. However, rod-like bright regions on the surface of FASnI3 films start to appear for 15 mol% SnF2 additives (Fig. 7(d)), and become prominent for the films with 20 mol% SnF2 additives (Fig. 7(e)), which were found to be SnF2 residues. FASnI3 crystals synthesized by adding 10 mol% SnF2 were adopted as the absorbing layer, and the corresponding devices obtained a breakthrough PCE of 6.22%. In addition, using low-dimensional materials is an effective way to improve the stability of chalcogenide films.159162 Wang's team prepared a 2D-quasi-2D3D Sn perovskite film using removable pseudohalogen NH4SCN as a structure regulator,9 which remarkably enhances its air stability resulting from the parallel growth of 2D PEA2SnI4 as the surface layer. Together with the increase of carrier mobility and reduced background carrier density, the photocurrent and fill factor (FF) of the device are much improved, and a record efficiency of 9.41% is achieved for lead-free perovskite solar cells. Its stability was also significantly enhanced, maintaining 90% of the initial energy conversion efficiency for 600 hours. Mitzi et al. demonstrated that the metallic conductivity of conventional 3D organotin halide perovskites can be suppressed by reducing the crystal dimensions, and when increasing the 2D features of the perovskite structure, the material can be transformed from a metal to a semiconductor.163 Therefore, 2D Sn-based perovskites provide a direction to overcome the p-type self-doping in bulk materials.

The choice of electron transport material has a great influence on the performance of solar cells. The good electron-selective contact between the ETL and the perovskite material can reduce the potential barrier for electron transfer while blocking hole transport to minimize the recombination of carriers at the interface.15 Recently, Jayan et al. systematically studied the PCE of solar cells based on MASnI3 perovskites under different electron transport layers and hole transport materials with the SCAPS 1D software package. The device structure is shown in Fig. 6(c).112 According to their simulation results, the solar cell using the Glass/FTO/PCBM/MASnI3/CuI/Au structure achieved the highest PCE of 25.05%. This is because taking such a structure makes the CBM of the electron transport layer lower than that of the perovskite layer, and the electrons in the perovskite CBM can easily transfer to the ETL. Likewise, the VBM of the HTL is slightly higher than that of the perovskite, so the holes can be easily transferred, as shown in Fig. 6(d).

In a study by Jiang's team, indene-C60 bisadduct with a higher energy level was utilized as an electron transporting material for tin perovskite solar cells in 2020.113 It suppresses the increase of carrier concentration caused by remote doping, which significantly reduces interface carrier recombination and increases the device output voltage. Zhu et al. fabricated solar cells with about 20 nm SnO2 thin films as the ETL for the good electron transport properties of SnO2 nanocrystals, and achieved a PCE of 18.8% at 70% environment humidity, and about 17% after exposure in the environment for 30 days.164

In addition to the ETL, the HTL also has a great influence on the device's performance. It's good for the HTL to lower the transport barrier of the hole while preventing electron transport between the perovskite and the electrode, to reduce the carrier recombination and improve device efficiency.15 A 1020 nm thick PEDOT:PSS layer is the most popular HTL in such a device structure. Self-assembled PEDOT:PSS monolayers were prepared on an indium tin oxide (ITO) surface through a facile water rinsing process, and an improved PCE from 13.4% to 18.0% was obtained.123 This is mainly because the ultrathin layer of PEDOT:PSS after water rinsing can be firmly attached to ITO through the InOS chemical bonds between PSS chains and ITO, resulting in a bilayer structure between PEDOT and PSS due to Coulomb interaction, which induced a directional electric field from the positively charged PEDOT to the negatively charged PSS accelerating the hole extraction process.

Ge-based perovskite solar cells. Germanium is also a suitable substitute for Pb. Ge has a similar outer electronic structure with Pb as they are both in the main group IV in the periodic table of elements.

There are also many studies on Ge-based perovskites. Qian et al. gained insight into the relationship between the structure of Ge-based halide perovskites and their photovoltaic performance with the first-principles calculations method.165 Ge-based perovskites have similar absorption spectra to Pb-based perovskites (Fig. 8(a)), and the effective masses for electrons and holes of CsGeI3 (0.14 and 0.19) are approximate with those of Pb-based ones, which makes them good absorption layers in solar cells. Kanoun et al. also studied the device performance of germanium methylammonium halide (CH3NH3GeI3) perovskite with the method of simulation calculation.117 With various organic and inorganic hole transport materials, the VBMs of the selected hole transport layers are all slightly smaller than those of the perovskite layer, ensuring the effective hole transport (Fig. 8(b)). They also studied the thicknesses of the perovskite, hole transport layer, defect density, hole mobility, and metal electrode work function on the charge collection. With an optimal thickness of around 600 nm, a defect density of about 1015 cm3, and HTL of Cu2O and DPTTT-14, a PCE of 21% was obtained.

Sn-based perovskite luminescence and light emitting diodes. In 2016, Jellicoe et al. of Cavendish Laboratory successfully synthesized CsSnX3 (X = Cl, Cl0.5Br0.5, Br, Br0.5I0.5, I) quantum dots by mixing SnX2 with a precursor and using the same thermal injection method as the lead halide perovskite, taking the first step toward the luminescence of lead-free perovskite quantum dots. Lead-free perovskite detectors

The optoelectronic properties of perovskite single crystals, especially their high absorption coefficients, enable their successful application in photodetectors. The research on perovskite detectors is not limited to the ultraviolet-visible spectrum, and there are a lot of reports on X-ray detectors. In 2015, Yakunin et al. fabricated an X-ray detector based on organicinorganic hybrid MAPbX3 (X = Br or I) and brought the detection spectrum of the perovskite detector from the visible-UV to the X-ray for the first time.194 Lead-free perovskite photodetectors have also made some progress with the development of materials.
Sn-based perovskites photodetectors. In 2017, Xu et al. fabricated a photodetector with MA0.5FA0.5Pb0.5Sn0.5I3 material by replacing part of Pb with Sn, expanding the detection range from visible and X-ray to the near-infrared region.195 Ascorbic acid was added to retard the formation of an Sn vacancy caused by the Sn oxidation and also facilitate the film formation by modulating the perovskite crystallization kinetics. The photodetector demonstrated high responsivity with a detection of over 1012 Jones between 800 and 970 nm, rivaling the performance of the conventional inorganic photodetectors. In 2020, Cao et al. prepared a relatively stable CsSnI3 perovskite without serious p-type self-doping using a spin coating method with ascorbic acid as a reducing agent. A stable environmentally-friendly self-powered UV-Vis-NIR photodetector was fabricated with the CsSnI3 film, and a broad spectral response range (from 350 to 1000 nm), fast response speed (0.35/1.6 ms), high responsivity (0.257 A W1), and good stability were achieved.196 Zhu et al. prepared high-quality MA0.7FA0.3SnBr3 perovskite films using a simple solution process with graphite in the precursor. The surface topography of the perovskite film was improved and the pin-holes that existed in the film were reduced greatly with graphite due to the large amount of graphite play as the growth center of perovskite, and the photodetectors have a detectivity of 6.7 1010 Jones.197
Development of double perovskite X-ray detectors. ABX3 lead-free perovskites are not a good candidate for X-ray detectors, because they cannot meet the needs of high-energy X-ray detection due to their inherent instability, defects and halide migration.198 Double perovskites, however, are more stable and can easily be prepared thicker, which makes them a better choice for high radiation energy detection. Pan et al. first applied Cs2AgBiBr6 single crystals to fabricate low detection limit X-ray detectors in 2015.199 The optimized device exhibited a sensitivity of 105 C Gyair1 cm2 and a low detection limit of 59.7 n Gyair s1 at an external bias of 5 V and shows long-term operational stability. In 2019, Zhang et al. also applied Cs2AgBiBr6 to prepare an X-ray detector, and the device achieved a detection sensitivity of up to 316 C Gyair1 cm2 at a bias voltage of 18 V.200 Zhang et al. fabricated a Cs2AgBiBr6 thin-film X-ray detector with a photoconductive planar structure for the first time in 2020, achieving a minimum detectable dose of 145.2 nGyair s1.201 The sensitivity of the detector they fabricated was as high as 1.8 104 C Gyair1 cm2, which is about a thousand times higher than the sensitivity of commercial a-Se X-ray detectors. In 2021, Guo et al. fabricated a flexible X-ray detector using the Cs2TeI6 film synthesized on polyimide substrate.202 The stability of the flexible detector remained for up to 100 bending cycles at a bending radius of 10 mm, and furthermore the detector was preserved in the air for 6 months, exhibiting better stability than detectors based on rigid SnO2:F glass substrates. In addition, the detector achieved an X-ray sensitivity of 76.27 C Gyair1 cm2 and a detection limit of 0.17 Gyair s1.

Other optoelectronic applications based on lead-free perovskites

Sensors based on lead-free perovskites. Perovskites are good candidates for micro-nano sensors. In 2016, Gu et al. fabricated humidity sensors with monoclinic NaNbO3 piezoelectric nanofibers synthesized using a far-field electrospinning method. The sensor exhibits an excellent piezoelectric capture performance with an output voltage of up to 2 V during vibration and a negative correlation with ambient humidity in the range of 5% to 80%, and flexible active humidity sensors can be obtained by peeling off the polydimethylsiloxane substrate layer and electrode deposition coated on the nanofibers.203
Lasers based on lead-free perovskite. Since the invention of semiconductor solid-state lasers in the mid-20th century, lasers have become a ubiquitous part of our daily lives. Solution-processed gain media, such as organic polymers and colloidal quantum dots, have the advantages of low cost, sustainable production, and suitability for flexible and wearable optoelectronic devices,204 among which halide perovskites have emerged as an important part in this field. Xing et al. reported their research on Sn-based perovskite (CsSnX3, X = Br, I) and near-infrared lasers in 2016.205 Gains of 200 10 for 20% SnF2-treated CsSnI3 films were achieved, comparable with the leaded CH3NH3PbI3 film (40 10 cm1)206 and CsPbI3 nanocrystal film (450 10 cm1),207 and the threshold pump fluence (6 1 J cm2) was also comparable to that of CH3NH3PbI3 (about 12 J cm2),206 which is one of the lowest reported in solution-processed organic or inorganic thin films over the same period. Due to the constraint from the band structure, the gain bandwidth of lead perovskite is limited to about 800 nm, which can be expanded to 1 m by Sn-based perovskites. These results suggested that SnF2-treated CsSnI3 lead-free perovskite is a promising near-infrared gain medium.

Conclusion and outlook

In recent years, great progress has been made in developing lead-free perovskites. In this paper, we summarized the development and application of ABX3-type lead-free halide perovskite materials, especially in optoelectronic devices. Because of the similar crystal structure and band structure, lead-free perovskites have the same excellent characteristics as lead perovskite, such as high absorption efficiency, high photoelectronic quantum yield, high color purity, high defect tolerance, long diffusion length, high carrier mobility, and bandgap tunability, which create all the advantages of lead-free perovskites as optoelectronic materials. Different low-toxic or nontoxic elements (Sn, Ge, Cu, and Ag) are considered to be B-site substitutes due to their similar electronic structures and comparable ionic radius, among which, Sn2+ is the most popular and has achieved the best results. Materials like FASnI2Br and PEA0.15FA0.85SnI3 are prepared by using solution-based methods, and the Sn-based solar cell prepared by a one-step method has obtained an energy conversion efficiency of 13%.113,127 In terms of LEDs, the ITO/PEDOT/CsSnI3B3PyMPM/LiF/Al structure prepared by the one step method can obtain an external quantum efficiency of 5.4%, in which CsSnI3 is prepared by solution-based methods. For applications in other fields, such as lasers and detectors, more research results are needed. As for some other materials, like Ge-based perovskites, there have not been available devices due to the defect property and instability. The grain size, surface morphology, preparation method, annealing temperature, lattice mismatch, and transport layer selection are all important factors for device performance, and are discussed in the paper.

Despite the great progress, lead-free perovskite materials still face some challenges. First, the preparation routes should be improved based on the many existing synthesis methods. The preparation method plays a crucial role in the quality, size, and optoelectronic properties of the material and device. The solution method is favoured when preparing single crystals due to its simple operation process and reproducibility, however, the size and growth direction of the synthesized single crystals are difficult to control. In addition, the use of large amounts of solvents may cause serious environmental problems. Some other methods, such as the Bridgeman and high-temperature vapor phase epitaxy methods, can provide better materials, but the required high temperature, high vacuum and complex technology greatly increase the complexity and cost. Therefore, further research is needed to focus on exploring more controllable and efficient growth methods, and to obtain large-scale, high-quality lead-free perovskite materials.

Second, a clearer understanding of the structure and properties of a possible family of lead-free perovskite materials should be established. Studies have shown that A, B and X ions all contribute to the crystal structure, band structure, carrier mobility, and stability of the material, so a systematic investigation on the composition is indispensable. However, the existing database is far from enough, not only because of an insufficient workload, but also because many materials are difficult to prepare and exist stably until the preparation conditions and methods are more mature, and preparation with random A, B and X ions and testing the property is not a wise idea in terms of research. Compared to the traditional approach, advanced algorithms such as deep learning may be able to replace some of the experimental studies and predict material properties, providing new methods for research.

Third, the stability of lead-free perovskite materials and devices is still an urgent problem to be solved, especially in high temperature, high humidity, and oxygen-rich environments. Despite the different radii, an unstable valence state is the factor most affecting stability, which may cause self-doping or even metallic properties. In the fabrication process, precursors or surface ligands with reducing properties can be used to avoid rapid oxidation and failure of the material, or a shell structure can be adapted to encapsulate the perovskite nanocrystals in the shell structure, which can also enhance the material stability.

Finally, the kinetics of dopants in the crystal structure is still a difficult problem. The optoelectronic property, defect property and carrier mobility can be tuned by doping ions. However, the process and state of the doping in the crystal are not fully understood, and unexpected results usually occur. To solve this problem, it is necessary to deeply study the kinetics of the material doping process, and to control the amount of dopant, preparation temperature and other conditions to achieve controllable doping and crystal structure through theoretical guidance in practice.

In addition to preparing stable and excellent materials, improving the device structure is also an important aspect. For example, an ETL and HTL with good electron/hole selective contacts with the perovskite material can reduce the potential barriers for electron/hole transfer while blocking the hole/electron transport accordingly.

Nevertheless, lead-free perovskites are still one of the most promising materials for high performance optoelectronic devices, and undoubtedly, materials with better stability and properties will be available shortly with the efforts of scientists.
