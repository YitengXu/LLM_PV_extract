Materials chemistry and engineering in metal halide perovskite lasers

Abstract

The invention and development of the laser have revolutionized science, technology, and industry. Metal halide perovskites are an emerging class of semiconductors holding promising potential in further advancing the laser technology. In this Review, we provide a comprehensive overview of metal halide perovskite lasers from the viewpoint of materials chemistry and engineering. After an introduction to the materials chemistry and physics of metal halide perovskites, we present diverse optical cavities for perovskite lasers. We then comprehensively discuss various perovskite lasers with particular functionalities, including tunable lasers, multicolor lasers, continuous-wave lasers, single-mode lasers, subwavelength lasers, random lasers, polariton lasers, and laser arrays. Following this a description of the strategies for improving the stability and reducing the toxicity of metal halide perovskite lasers is provided. Finally, future research directions and challenges toward practical technology applications of perovskite lasers are provided to give an outlook on this emerging field.

1. Introduction

Laser technology has become ubiquitous in every section of modern society, such as scientific research, manufacturing, communication, medicine, and so on.1 A laser is a strong coherent light source consisting of a gain medium, a pump source, and an optical cavity.2,3 The gain medium is a material capable of amplifying light through stimulated emission. The development and innovative use of optical gain materials play important roles in laser performance improvement and functionality extension, which contribute to both the growth of existing industries and the emergence of new industries.1 Hence, new optical gain materials have always been pursued with the expectation of triggering the next revolution in the laser and related fields.

Metal halide perovskites have rapidly emerged as an outstanding class of semiconductors for optoelectronics, such as photovoltaics,4–18 photodetectors,19–23 light-emitting diodes,4,24–32 and lasers,24,25,33–43 because they combine the facile fabrication with excellent optoelectronic properties. The low-temperature solution-processability makes metal halide perovskites promising materials for low-cost and large-area optoelectronic applications. The high carrier mobility and low trap state density facilitate both carrier extraction and injection, and therefore realization of high-efficiency optoelectronic devices. The metal halide perovskites gained worldwide attention initially because of their unprecedented success in photovoltaics. The power conversion efficiencies have increased up to 24% in just ten years after the first perovskite solar cell.44–53 Soon the metal halide perovskites were proven to be good light-emitting materials with direct bandgap and high luminescence efficiency. The external quantum efficiencies of perovskite light-emitting diodes have exceeded 20% over a four-year period of academic research.54–60

As direct-bandgap semiconductors, the metal halide perovskites are an excellent class of optical gain media for lasers. The high optical gain coefficients of metal halide perovskites are comparable to that of the industry reference materials, such as gallium arsenide.61 One of the most attractive features of metal halide perovskites as gain media is their widely tailorable bandgaps, supporting tunable lasing from visible to near-infrared spectral regions. Due to their excellent processability, the metal halide perovskites can not only be fabricated into diverse optical microcavities but can also be integrated with various commercial optical cavities to construct lasers. More importantly, the metal halide perovskites hold great promise for electrically pumped laser devices because of their high and balanced charge carrier mobility.62

As early as 1998, amplified spontaneous emission (ASE) was demonstrated in two-dimensional (2D) perovskite films,63 but didn’t receive much attention due to cryogenic operation. It was not until 2014 that Xing et al. reported the room-temperature ASE from three-dimensional (3D) organic–inorganic hybrid perovskite films,64 and since then extensive research has been directed towards exploring perovskite materials for laser applications. Soon after, the lasing action was realized by embedding mixed-halide perovskites between a dielectric mirror and a gold mirror.65 In the same year, the first perovskite nanolaser was achieved based on chemical-vapor-deposited hybrid perovskite nanoplatelets.66 In 2015, solution-phase grown perovskite micro/nanocrystal lasers were demonstrated.67,68 In 2016, the perovskite based distributed feedback (DFB) laser and distributed Bragg reflector (DBR) laser were reported.69,70 Since then, the field of metal halide perovskite lasers has been experiencing an explosive growth. Great progress has been made in improving the performances and expanding the capabilities of perovskite lasers through materials engineering. For example, replacement of methylammonium cations with formamidinium and cesium cations significantly increased the stability of perovskite lasers.71,72 Composition-graded perovskite nanowires gave rise to the tunable dual-color lasing.73 Coupled perovskite microdisk cavities supported single-mode lasing.74 The dynamical phase mixture of the perovskite enabled continuous-wave (c.w.) laser operation.75 This is a significant step towards electrically pumped lasers that is expected to unlock a wide range of laser applications in information technology, manufacturing and sensing.76

In this Review, we aim to provide a comprehensive overview of metal halide perovskite lasers from the viewpoint of materials chemistry and engineering. We start with an introduction to the materials chemistry and physics of metal halide perovskites in Section 2, including crystal structure, material fabrication and processing, and electronic band structure and photophysics. The lasing mechanism of the metal halide perovskites is discussed in detail. Following this is a presentation of diverse optical cavities for perovskite lasers in Section 3, including intrinsic and extrinsic cavities. The growth mechanism of perovskite microcrystals is revealed based on a kinetically controlled crystal growth model. We then comprehensively discuss various perovskite lasers with particular functionalities in Section 4, that is, tunable lasers, multicolor lasers, continuous-wave lasers, single-mode lasers, subwavelength lasers, random lasers, polariton lasers, and laser arrays. Special emphasis is put on the laser function/performance-oriented composition and structure engineering of metal halide perovskites. In Section 5, we describe the strategies for improving the stability and reducing the toxicity of metal halide perovskite lasers. Finally, we give the conclusion as well as the prospect of future research directions in this promising field.

2. Material chemistry and physics

2.1. Crystal structure

Metal halide perovskites are a class of crystalline semiconducting materials with the general chemical structure of ABX3 (Fig. 1A and B), where A is a monovalent cation (typically CH3NH3+ (MA+), NH2CHNH2+ (FA+) or Cs+), B is a divalent metal cation (typically Pb2+ or Sn2+), and X is a halide anion (Cl−, Br−, I−).77–79 According to the A-site cation, the metal halide perovskites can be divided into two types, organic–inorganic hybrid and all-inorganic perovskites. The perovskite crystal structure is characterized by a three-dimensional (3D) network of corner- sharing [BX6]4− octahedra, where the [BX6]4− octahedron is formed through the six-coordination of the divalent metal cation with the halide anion, and the cuboctahedral void surrounded by eight octahedra is occupied by the monovalent cation. The perovskite usually adopts a high-symmetry cubic or tetragonal crystal structure at high temperature, while it may convert into a low-symmetry tetragonal or orthorhombic phase by the distortion of the [BX6]4− octahedra at low temperature.80,81
Fig. 1 Perovskite crystal structure. (A and B) Schematic diagram of the perovskite unit cell and crystal packing. (C) Illustration of 2D RP perovskites with different numbers of perovskite layers (n).

2D Ruddlesden–Popper (RP) perovskites are formed when the small monovalent cations are replaced partially or completely by large monovalent or divalent organic ammonium cations (Fig. 1C).14,82–89 The 2D metal halide perovskite has the chemical structure of (LA)m(A)n−1BnX3n+1, where LA is the large ammonium cation, m = 2 for monovalent LA or m = 1 for divalent LA, and n is an integer. The crystal structure consists of 2D sheets of corner-sharing [BX6]4− octahedra separated by the large LA cation layers. The 2D perovskite with alternating double layers naturally forms a multiple-quantum-well structure.

2.2. Material fabrication and processing

In general, the metal halide perovskites are synthesized through a simple chemical reaction between the organic ammonium/cesium halide and lead/tin halide:
 
AX + BX2 → ABX3(2.1)
This reaction can be conducted not only in gas-, liquid- and solid-phases but also at liquid–solid and gas–solid interfaces. The low-temperature solution-processing is certainly the most attractive fabrication technique of metal halide perovskites because of its great potential in scalable and commercial optoelectronic applications. Such versatile fabrication makes it possible to obtain desired perovskite materials for researchers in almost all fields, including physics, chemistry, and materials science.

The metal halide perovskites usually exist in two types of morphologies, crystalline films and single crystals. The fabrication methods of perovskite films include spin-coating, blade-coating, spray-coating, vacuum thermal evaporation, atomic layer deposition, pulsed laser deposition, and so on (Fig. 2A).17,18,90 Considering that many reviews have covered the fabrication of perovskite films, we won’t go into the details of all these methods. Nevertheless, it is necessary to give a brief introduction to the spin-coating technique of metal halide perovskites, which is the simplest and most commonly used method for fabricating perovskite films for laser applications. The spin-coating of perovskites is usually accompanied by an antisolvent dripping and a thermal annealing process. The dripping of antisolvent accelerates nucleation through inducing supersaturation of the precursor solution.91 The high nucleation density yields flat and uniform perovskite films. The thermal annealing removes residual solvent, assists in perovskite nucleation and growth, and coarsens the grains.

For laser applications, the metal halide perovskite crystals can be classified into three categories according to crystal size: bulk crystals, microcrystals (three dimensions larger than λ/2n), and nanocrystals (at least one dimension less than λ/2n). The difference between microcrystals and nanocrystals is that the microcrystals can support optical modes, while the nanocrystals with dimensions below the diffraction limit cannot. Thus, the perovskite microcrystals naturally constitute optical microcavities with well-defined crystal surfaces serving as reflecting mirrors. The nanocrystals need to be incorporated into external cavities to achieve lasing oscillations. The bulk crystals are not utilized directly for laser applications because intense multimode competition and high self-absorption loss originating from the overlarge optical cavity structures are detrimental for laser generation. For the syntheses of bulk crystals and nanocrystals, we refer the interested readers to several recent reviews.92–97 Here, we focus our discussion on the syntheses of microcrystals of metal halide perovskites.

The metal halide perovskite microcrystals can be synthesized in gas and liquid phases, and at gas– and liquid–solid interfaces. The liquid-phase method denotes the self-assembly of metal halide perovskites in solution (Fig. 2Bi).68 The driving force of self-assembly is supersaturation induced by solvent evaporation or antisolvent introduction. In the solution with increasing supersaturation, the organic ammonium/cesium halide rapidly intercalates into the lead halide, resulting in the direct formation of perovskites. The liquid–solid interface approach involves a solution-phase recrystallization growth process.98,99 When the lead halide/acetate film is dipped into the solution of organic ammonium/cesium halide, the lead halide/acetate first reacts with the halide anion to produce a soluble intermediate at the liquid–solid interface. The intermediate further reacts with the ammonium/cesium cation to generate the insoluble perovskite.

 
PbY2(s) + 4X−(l) → [PbX4]2−(l) + 2Y−(l)(2.2)
 
[PbX4]2−(l) + A+(l) → APbX3(s) + X−(l)(2.3)
where Y is the halide or acetate anion, and s and l stand for the solid and liquid phases, respectively. The slow generation and release of the [PbI4]2− precursor helps to maintain a low supersaturation condition and produce high-quality perovskite microcrystals.

The gas-phase method denotes the chemical vapor deposition (CVD) process (Fig. 2Bii).100–103 Unlike traditional semiconductors requiring ultrahigh growth temperature, the metal halide perovskites with lower melting points can be synthesized through a relatively low-temperature CVD method. The vapor-phase grown metal halide perovskites have superior crystalline quality and lower defect densities than those grown by solution methods. However, the direct vapor-phase growth of organic–inorganic hybrid perovskites is challenging because thermal decomposition occurs before evaporation. Fortunately, the ion migration behavior enables a two-step vapor-phase method for synthesizing hybrid perovskites.66,104 The lead halide microcrystals are first prepared through a physical vapor deposition method, and then exposed to gaseous methylammonium halide. The intercalation and diffusion of methylammonium halide into the lead halide microcrystals result in the formation of organic–inorganic hybrid perovskite microcrystals. The fast ion migration behavior ensures the complete conversion of lead halide into hybrid perovskite microcrystals.

The metal halide perovskites possess soft lattices, providing a possibility of designing and controlling the material composition and structure through reprocessing the as-prepared perovskites. For example, the halide anion exhibits high mobility in the relatively stable cationic sublattice of perovskites (Fig. 2Ci). Therefore, the existing halide anions in perovskites can be partially or completely replaced by another type of halides through ion-exchange reactions in vapor, liquid and solid phases.72,105–110

 
ABX3 + yX′− → ABX3−yX′y + yX−(2.4)
Such composition control leads to bandgap and luminescence tunability of metal halide perovskites. Besides the chemically tailorable composition, the as-prepared metal halide perovskites can be reprocessed into specific structures through the microfabrication technology. For instance, the distributed feedback structure can be patterned onto the perovskite film directly by using a nanoimprint technique because of the soft nature of perovskites (Fig. 2Cii).111 In addition, both the perovskite films and crystals can be processed into nearly any shape through top-down techniques, such as typical electron beam lithography and direct laser ablation (Fig. 2Ciii).112,113

2.3. Electronic structure and photophysics

2.3.1. Electronic band structure. Electronic band structure not only rationalizes the unique photonic and optoelectronic properties of perovskites, including carrier recombination, mobility, and lifetime, but also guides the design of novel perovskite materials and devices. To provide an insight into the electronic band structure of metal halide perovskites, we begin our discussion by introducing the bonding mechanism of the zero-dimensional (0D) [BX6]4− octahedron, as its electronic character will be largely retained in the ABX3. For [PbI6]4−, the Pb 6s–I 5p σ-antibonding orbital is the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO), while Pb 6p–I 5p π-antibonding and Pb 6p–I 5s σ-antibonding orbitals constitute the lowest unoccupied molecular orbitals (LUMOs), as shown in Fig. 3A.114 A large number of HOMOs and LUMOs form the valence band maximum (VBM) and conductance valence minimum (CBM), respectively. Fig. 3B shows the electronic band structure of cubic-phase MAPbI3 calculated with the many-body perturbation theory in the GW approximation.115 The VBM consists of 70% I 5p and 25% Pb 6s orbits, while the CBM consists of almost entirely the Pb 6p orbit. The elemental contributions to each band can be demonstrated more clearly by projecting the total density of states of MAPbI3 into lead, iodine, carbon, nitrogen, and hydrogen atoms (Fig. 3C).116 The [PbI6]4− octahedron gives rise to the CBM and VBM, whereas CH3NH3+ contributes little to the frontier orbits.

The band structure indicates that the bandgap of MAPbI3 perovskite is direct, but shifted slightly away from the R point owing to the spin–orbit coupling. The direct bandgap is a key ingredient for efficient luminescence and high optical gain. The measured value of the optical gain in MAPbI3 perovskites is as high as 3200,61 which is comparable to that of the industry reference materials, such as single-crystal gallium arsenide. Such high optical gain facilitates the low-threshold lasing in metal halide perovskites.

Because the electronic band structure is mostly composed of frontier orbits of divalent metal and halide ions, the bandgap (Eg) of perovskites is essentially controlled by the divalent metal and halide ions. With the X-site halogen changing from chlorine to iodine, the VBM of perovskites moves upwards with the halide p orbits, while the CBM shows little variation. As a result, the bandgap of the metal halide perovskite follows the trend of EgCl > EgBr > EgI. The emission wavelength of MAPbX3 can be continuously tailored from 390 to 790 nm through the halide anion mixing strategy.64 When substituting lead with tin at the B-site, the CBM of perovskites decreases with the divalent metal p orbit more significantly than the VBM with the divalent metal s orbit. Therefore, the tin perovskite or tin–lead mixed perovskites have narrower bandgap than the lead perovskite. The emission wavelength of mixed-halide tin perovskites ranges from 700 to 950 nm.117

The atom-projected density of states shows that the electronic contributions of the A-site cation locate several electronvolts below the VBM, indicating that the A-site cation doesn’t directly dictate the optoelectronic and photonic properties of metal halide perovskites. Accordingly, the homologous perovskites with different A-site cations usually exhibit very similar luminescence properties.118 The A-site component principally serves as a counterion for charge balance and structure stability of perovskites. The A-site cation can indirectly affect the electronic structure of perovskites through interacting with the [PbI6]4− octahedron, which ultimately expands and contracts the perovskite lattice. With the decrease of the A-site cation radius, the perovskite lattice contracts, leading to an increase of the bandgap. Nevertheless, the bandgap variation caused by the A-site cation is much less compared with the B-site metal cation and the X-site halide anion.

2.3.2. Photophysical process. A detailed understanding of the outstanding optoelectronic and photonic properties exhibited by metal halide perovskites comes from a meaningful analysis of the dynamics of photoexcited states, including carrier generation, thermalization, cooling, scattering and recombination.119–123 Here, we mainly discuss the recombination mechanism of photoexcited states, which directly determines the perovskite laser performances. Generally speaking, metal halide perovskites show modest exciton binding energies, 50–120, 20–100, and 2–80 meV for ABCl3, ABBr3, and ABI3, respectively.123 The large variation in experimentally determined values of exciton binding energies for each kind of perovskites results from the differences in material quality and measurement methods and inaccurate assumptions in theoretical models and calculations. Today, values in the lower range are more accepted. The modest exciton binding energies indicate that the excitons and free charge carriers coexist in perovskites at room temperature. The Mott density of MAPbI3 is estimated to be ∼4 × 1017 cm−3.127,140 As shown in Fig. 4A, the free carriers dominate in MAPbI3 even below the Mott density due to small exciton binding energy (only a few millielectronvolts). Thus, we can argue that the lasing originates from bimolecular electron–hole recombination from a theoretical point of view, although the exact lasing mechanism needs further experimental investigations. The situation becomes complicated for CsPbBr3, for which the Mott density is ∼2 × 1017 cm−3.99,100 The reported threshold carrier densities of CsPbBr3 lasers just locate in the vicinity of the Mott density ranging from 0.7 × 1017 to 6 × 1017 cm−3,99,100,129,141 where the excitons and free carriers share similar population as a result of a modest exciton binding energy. It is difficult to attribute the lasing to a certain recombination mechanism according to the population distribution between the exciton and free carrier. Note that a blue-shift of the lasing modes with excitation density is ubiquitous in perovskite lasers.72,99–101,105,142 The mode blue-shift is a typical characteristic of lasing in the electron–hole plasma regime. Therefore, the electron–hole plasma recombination is more likely to be responsible for the stimulated emission in metal halide perovskites.

Very recently, Schlaus et al. provided a clear picture of the lasing mechanism in CsPbBr3 nanowires.141 The laser dynamics in CsPbBr3 nanowires was probed using ultrafast spectroscopy (Fig. 4B). Upon excitation, a short-lived broad lasing spectrum emerges first, which can be attributed to hot carrier recombination. Then spectral narrowing and bathochromic-shift occur owing to the hot carrier cooling process. As the carrier density decreases significantly, the optical gain profile blue-shifts towards the peak energy of spontaneous emission. This signifies that the stimulated emission of electron–hole plasma involves the emission of a plasmon quanta. The plasmon quanta is a collective oscillation of electron–hole plasma with frequency proportional to the square root of carrier density. In a word, the lasing in CsPbBr3 nanowires originates from the stimulated emission of electron–hole plasma coupled with plasmon emission (Fig. 4C).

With larger exciton binding energies, the chloride perovskites probably support excitonic lasing, but this remains to be verified experimentally. The low-dimensional perovskite materials, such as perovskite quantum dots (QDs) and 2D RP perovskites, have large exciton binding energies due to the quantum confinement.94 Size-dependent bandgaps manifested the quantum confinement effect in perovskite nanocrystals.143–146 The lasing actions have been reported in many kinds of perovskite nanocrystal films.109,147–149 Different gain mechanisms were put forward to account for the lasing actions in perovskite nanocrystals, including exciton, biexciton and trion gains.150–157 Note that so far the lasing actions are mainly realized in perovskite nanocrystals with weak quantum confinement, which would not significantly increase the binding energies of the exciton, biexciton and trion. The exact lasing mechanism in perovskite nanocrystal films remains open to question. The 2D RP perovskites have multiple quantum well structures, exhibiting obvious perovskite layer number-dependent exciton absorption and emission. In such RP perovskites, the excitonic lasing has been demonstrated unambiguously.158–162

The charge carriers in metal halide perovskites exist as large polarons.163 A polaron is a quasiparticle consisting of an electron or hole dressed by its self-induced lattice polarization in polar semiconductors, as illustrated in Fig. 4D.164 There are two types of polarons, i.e. small and large polarons, formed through two fundamental interaction potentials, i.e. long-range Coulomb potential and short-range deformation potential, respectively.165 The polarons in metal halide perovskites were proved as large polarons formed predominantly from the deformation of lead halide sublattice [PbX6]4− frameworks.166 A direct consequence of large polaron formation is the efficient screening of the Coulomb potential. All events requiring the Coulomb potential, such as scattering of a charge carrier with charged defects or other charge carriers, are inhibited. Accordingly, the trap-assisted nonradiative recombination and many-body Auger recombination are suppressed effectively in metal halide perovskites. The protection of charge carriers as large polarons results in the excellent optoelectronic and photonic properties of metal halide perovskites, such as ambipolar carrier transport and high-efficiency luminescence.

3. Optical cavities

An optical cavity (or resonator) is required to provide optical feedback for laser oscillation. There are mainly two kinds of optical microcavities for metal halide perovskite lasers, intrinsic and extrinsic cavities. The intrinsic cavity is composed of the gain medium itself with a particular structure capable of providing the optical feedback. The extrinsic cavity denotes an additional geometry that induces the optical feedback. The intrinsic optical cavities for perovskite lasers can be further divided into two categories, self-organized and processed ones. The self-organized intrinsic cavity denotes the naturally formed optical feedback structure from the gain medium. The processed intrinsic cavity corresponds to the externally induced optical feedback structure on the gain medium.

3.1. Intrinsic optical cavities

3.1.1. Self-organized intrinsic cavities. Metal halide perovskites are a typical class of crystalline semiconductors. Any crystalline material has a tendency to spontaneously form a convex polyhedron in the crystallization process. It is well-known that the appropriate crystals can function as optical cavities with crystal surfaces as reflecting mirrors. Fig. 5A presents several typical kinds of metal halide perovskite micro/nanocrystals, including one-dimensional (1D) nanowires,102 2D triangular, tetragonal, hexagonal, and octagonal microdisks,66,68,167 and 3D microspheres,168 synthesized through bottom-up methods, including liquid-phase assembly and vapor-phase growth. The single-crystal perovskite microresonators possess both large optical gains and high quality (Q) factors due to their low trap state densities and atomically flat surfaces. The perovskite nanowire crystals usually constitute Fabry–Pérot (F–P) cavities with two end-facets as mirrors, while other perovskite crystals constitute whispering–gallery–mode (WGM) cavities with crystal lateral surfaces as mirrors. Noteworthily, under special conditions, especially when using focused laser excitation, the perovskite microwires with rectangular cross section can support WGM lasing with four lateral facets as mirrors,169–171 and likewise the rectangular perovskite microplates can support F–P lasing with two parallel lateral faces as mirrors.172 The F–P cavities exhibit efficiently directional output, which is beneficial for on-chip photonic integrations, but suffers from relatively low Q factors. The WGM cavities possess high Q factors, and therefore support low-threshold lasing and ultrasensitive sensing, but are subjected to the limitation of isotropic output.173,174

In the early stage, the researchers encountered a problem in the growth processes of perovskite microcrystals in that the nanowires and tetragonal microdisks always grew concurrently. Zhang et al. proposed a surfactant assisted liquid-phase self-assembly strategy to grow MAPbBr3 nanowires and square microplates separately.175 Two different organic ammonium compounds, octadecylammonium bromide (OTDB) and dodecylammonium bromide (DODB), were synthesized as surfactants to modulate the kinetic growth process of MAPbBr3. The OTDB and DODB surfactants selectively bind to and impede the growth of (110) and (001) crystal facets, respectively. Consequently, the MAPbBr3 crystals evolve into nanowires and microplates, respectively. In the same year, Zhou et al. reported the controllable fabrication of CsPbBr3 nanorods and microplates via a CVD method.102 The cubic-phase microplates were formed at high growth temperature, while monoclinic-phase nanorods were formed at low growth temperature.

Although many approaches have been developed to synthesize various perovskite microcrystals, there is still a lack of understanding on the microcrystal growth mechanism, especially the microcrystal growth kinetics of metal halide perovskites. Here, we attempt to elucidate the growth mechanism of perovskite microcrystals to some extent based on a kinetically controlled crystal growth model. All metal halide perovskites with different compositions and crystal phases share very similar crystal structures. Similar crystal growth mechanisms are expected for diverse perovskites. We selected the most widely studied cubic-phase CsPbBr3 as the research model. Fig. 7 displays the theoretical morphology of the CsPbBr3 crystal calculated based on the Bravais–Friedel–Donnay–Harker method.176 Very similar theoretical morphologies can be obtained for almost all metal halide perovskites. According to the theoretical prediction, the perovskites tend to grow into cubic structures with six identical {100} crystal faces dominating the crystal morphology. On the one hand, such cubic structure is the growth morphology of perovskite crystal because the six identical {100} crystal faces have the same growth velocity. On the other hand, this regular hexahedron is also the equilibrium morphology of perovskite crystal because it has the smallest surface free energy per unit volume compared with other convex polyhedra, such as nanowires and microplates. This was proven experimentally by the structural transformation of CsPbBr3 crystals from 1D nanowires with high surface energy to 3D hexahedra with low surface energy.99

In practice, the ideal 3D cubic equilibrium shape is rarely achieved. The crystal growth is a kinetic process which is highly dependent on the growth environment.177 Influenced by solvent, impurity, substrate, and even environment fluctuation, the crystals are prone to deviate from the equilibrium morphology. The achievement of ideal equilibrium morphology requires that the crystal has an infinite self-adjusting ability to the morphology deviation. It means that the crystal can adjust its shape back to equilibrium morphology through addition or removal of an infinite amount of components,176 which is difficult to be achieved in practical experiments. Therefore, the large-scale preparation of cubic perovskite microcrystals is challenging. The most common morphologies of perovskite microcrystals are irregular hexahedra, such as nanowires and microplates. For the same reason, the ideal 2D square morphology and the ideal 1D wire morphology with square cross section are rarely obtained as well. In general, without a strong external intervention, perovskite microcrystal products are usually a mixture of different structures, including 1D nanowires, 2D microplates, and 3D microhexahedra due to the random environment fluctuations.

Through a comprehensive analysis of a large number of experimental results, we found that there are certain rules underlying the perovskite microcrystal growth. The perovskite microcrystal products are dominated by 2D microplates for the liquid-phase self-assembly,68,74,178,179 1D nanowires for the liquid–solid interface growth,71,72,98,99,180,181 and 2D microplates and 1D nanowires for relatively high- and low temperature CVD.102 Various perovskite microcrystal morphologies arise from the growth inhibition of particular crystal faces. As shown in Fig. 7, the perovskite microcrystal growth can be divided into eight kinds: growth inhibition of (1) zero crystal face; (2) one crystal face; (3) two opposite crystal faces; (4) two adjacent crystal faces; (5) three adjacent crystal faces surrounding one vertex; (6) four lateral crystal faces; (7) four adjacent crystal faces surrounding one edge; (8) five crystal faces. The consequent morphology is (1) and (5) 3D hexahedron; (2), (3), and (7) 2D plate; (4) 1D wire with triangular cross section; and (6) and (8) 1D wire with rectangular cross section.

In the liquid-phase self-assembly process, the perovskite nucleates and grows on the substrate with one crystal face parallel to the substrate.68,74,178,179 With one crystal face protected by the substrate, the growth along other five crystal faces generates dominant perovskite microplates. As an extreme case of the 2D microplate, namely, the microplate with a large length/width ratio, a relatively small number of nanowires are bound to emerge in this situation. In the surfactant assisted self-assembly process, the rod-like OTDB micelles selectively inhibit the growth of four {110} crystal faces, while the laminar DODB micelles selectively inhibit the growth of (001) and (00−1) crystal faces.175 As a result, the MAPbBr3 crystals evolve into nanowires and microplates, respectively. Because of the strong growth control induced by the surfactants, the as-prepared nanowires and microplates are very close to the ideal 1D wire and 2D plate morphologies, respectively (Fig. 5B and C).

In the CVD process, the perovskite crystals grow with (100) and (011) crystal faces parallel to the substrate at relatively high and low growth temperatures, respectively,102 even though the mechanism is still unclear. At high growth temperature, perovskite microplates are formed due to the growth inhibition of the (100) crystal face (Fig. 6E). At low growth temperature, the direct contact of the (011) crystal face with the substrate suppresses the growth of both (010) and (001) faces, leading to the formation of nanowires with triangular cross section (Fig. 6F).

In the liquid–solid interface growth process, a dense polycrystalline perovskite thin film is first formed on the surface of the lead halide/acetate film.71,72,98,99,180,181 This polycrystalline perovskite film serves as a seed to initiate the crystal growth. The growth of all faces of the perovskite crystallites within and below the plane of the polycrystalline film is inhibited spatially. The perovskite crystals can only grow upwards along the exposed faces of seed crystallites. In the polycrystalline film, there are three kinds of seed crystallites with one, two and three faces exposed. The exposure of one crystal face supports only 1D wire growth, while the exposure of two adjacent crystal faces supports both 1D wire and 2D plate growth, and the exposure of three adjacent crystal faces surrounding one vertex supports all 1D wire, 2D plate, and 3D hexahedron growth. Such a growth mechanism is well consistent with the experimental results that the 1D nanowires dominate the perovskite microcrystal products and that all perovskite microcrystals are free-standing with one crystal face, edge, and vertex in contact with the polycrystalline film or substrate for nanowires, microplates, and microhexahedra, respectively.

3.1.2. Processed intrinsic cavities. The metal halide perovskites can be readily processed into cavity geometries through microfabrication technology owing to their structural softness. The perovskite microfabrication techniques mainly include lithography and molding. These microfabrication techniques exhibit high controllability and reproducibility in the preparation of perovskite cavities. The top-down lithography is a standard process in the fields of microelectronics and on-chip photonics. Hence, it is of crucial importance to develop top-down lithography techniques for fabricating perovskite lasers towards practical applications. Nowadays, both DFB and DBR cavities are standard devices for semiconductor lasers.198–200 As the name implies, the optical feedback necessary for laser oscillation in the DFB and DBR lasers is not localized at the cavity facets but is distributed throughout the cavity length. The DFB cavity is composed of a periodically structured element, i.e. photonic crystal (Fig. 10A), from which the diffracted wave feeds backwards. According to the dimension of the photonic crystal, the DFB cavities can be classified into three categories, 1D (Fig. 8J and 10B), 2D (Fig. 10D), and 3D (Fig. 8L) DFB cavities. The most commonly used DFB cavities are the 1D Bragg diffraction gratings. The resonant wavelength in the DFB cavity is given by Bragg conditions expressed as

 
2neffΛ = mλBragg(3.1)
where neff is the effective refractive index, Λ is the periodicity of the grating, m is the diffraction order, and λBragg is the Bragg wavelength. The first- and second-order DFB cavities lead to edge- and surface-emitting lasers, respectively. The DBR is a dielectric mirror formed from multiple layers of alternating materials with different refractive indices (Fig. 10F). Each layer boundary causes a partial reflection of an optical wave. For waves with wavelength close to four times the optical thickness of the layers, the many reflections combine with constructive interference, leading to high reflection. The range of wavelengths that are reflected is called the photonic stopband. A pair of DBRs parallel to each other form the DBR cavity (Fig. 10F and G). Besides, a DBR together with a gold mirror can constitute a hybrid cavity architecture, as illustrated in Fig. 10I.65

The metal halide perovskites can be easily combined with the DFB and DBR cavities through but not limited to spin-coating. Compared with those on-chip optical microcavity lasers, the DFB and DBR perovskite lasers have many advantages. The first one is the high reproducibility in device fabrication, which enables the pre-set and tuning of lasing wavelength. The second one is the single-mode operations. The DFB cavities support single-mode lasing because only the wave satisfying the Bragg condition can obtain optical feedback. The single-mode lasing from DBR cavities is attributed to very short cavity lengths, corresponding to mode spacing larger than the gain profile or cavity bandwidth. The third one is the well-defined spatially coherent laser output with small divergence angles. The 1D second-order DFB perovskite lasers exhibit a stripe-shaped (double-lobed or fan-like) far-field beam profile (Fig. 10C),75,191 while the 2D second-order DFB and DBR perovskite lasers exhibit a Gaussian beam profile in far field (Fig. 10E and H).69,70,154,156,196

4. Materials engineering in perovskite lasers

4.1. Composition engineering

4.1.1. Tunable lasers. A major advantage of metal halide perovskites for laser application is the broad wavelength tunability based on controllable stoichiometry. As discussed in Section 2.3.1, the perovskite bandgap highly depends on its material composition, that is, A-site monovalent cation, B-site divalent cation, and X-site halide anion. Accordingly, the lasing wavelength from the perovskite can be easily modified by changing its material composition (Fig. 11). Halide anion mixing is the most effective strategy for tailoring the output wavelength of the perovskite lasers. As shown in Fig. 11A–D, for all kinds of metal halide perovskites, including organic–inorganic hybrid and all-inorganic perovskites, the lasing wavelength can be tuned over ∼300 nm by simply changing the stoichiometric ratios of different halide anions.

The wide-wavelength tunability of perovskite lasers was initially demonstrated in single-crystal nanowires of methylammonium lead halide (MAPbX3) perovskites (Fig. 11A).67 The laser output covers the whole spectral region from 500 to 790 nm except for a gap from 580 to 670 nm, corresponding to MAPbBryI3−y with 1.6 < y < 2.8. The failure of the mixed-halide MAPbBryI3−y perovskites to lase is caused by photo-induced phase segregation between Br-rich and Br-poor phases via ion-migration, which shifts the emission wavelength gradually. The formamidinium lead halide (FAPbX3) perovskites have better phase stability because strong hydrogen bonding between the FA cations and halide ions suppresses ion migration. Therefore, the FA-based lead halide perovskite alloys, (FA,MA)Pb(I,Br)3, fill in the gap of lasing wavelength unavailable with MA-based perovskites (Fig. 11B).71 In addition, with narrower bandgap, the FA-based perovskite alloys broaden the wavelength tuning range of perovskite lasers towards the near-infrared spectral region up to 824 nm. Noteworthily, the blue spectral region (<500 nm) cannot be accessed at room temperature for organic–inorganic hybrid perovskite lasers. The blue emission is available from Cl-rich perovskites, but suffers from low luminescence efficiency due to high defect density. The full substitution of the organic ammonium cation with cesium cation significantly enhances the luminescence of perovskites. Hence, lasing actions spanning the entire visible spectrum from 420 to 710 can be realized in all-inorganic CsPbX3 perovskites (Fig. 11C).72 An approach to further broaden the wavelength tuning range of perovskite lasers is by substituting lead with tin, which largely reduces the bandgap of perovskites. The lasing wavelength of tin-based perovskites locates in the near-infrared spectral region from 700 to 950 nm (Fig. 11D).117 All in all, the output wavelength of metal halide perovskites covers the whole visible region and part of the near-infrared region ranging from 420 to 950 nm. The green perovskite lasers are particularly important because they may address the ‘green gap’ issue encountered in III–V semiconductor lasers.201

4.1.2. Multicolor lasers. With wide wavelength tunability, metal halide perovskites hold great potential for monolithic multicolor lasers that are useful in a wide range of applications, such as chemical and biological sensing, laser display, and on-chip optical communication.202,203 Several attempts have been made to create perovskite heterojunctions based on post-treatment of synthesized homogeneous perovskite nanowires, such as local ion exchange and induced phase segregation.110,204–208 Yet lasing action has never been demonstrated in these perovskite nanowire heterojunctions, which might be attributed to the introduction of defects in the post-treatment processes. Hence, the direct synthesis of perovskite heterojunctions is essential for multicolor lasing, but this poses a great challenge because the rapid ion exchange and migration leads to the formation of homogeneous alloys. Recently, Huang et al. reported a direct synthesis of composition-graded CsPbBrxI3−x nanowires through a vapor-phase epitaxial growth method (Fig. 12A).73 The relatively high phase stability of all-inorganic CsPbX3 perovskite provides the possibility for obtaining stable heterojunctions. Precise control of growth temperature and time in the epitaxial process is key for the fabrication of composition-graded CsPbBrxI3−x nanowires because either high temperature or long time facilitates the formation of homogeneous alloys. The as-prepared CsPbBrxI3−x nanowire heterojunctions exhibit a gradually decreased bandgap and therefore blue-shifted emission from center to end (Fig. 12B and C). As a result, the composition-graded CsPbBrxI3−x nanowires support a position-dependent lasing action, namely, short-wavelength lasing at nanowire ends, long-wavelength lasing at the nanowire center, and dual-wavelength lasing at the intermediate position between nanowire center and edge (Fig. 12D).

4.1.3. Continuous-wave lasers. The development of an electrically injected laser is a long-standing and elusive goal in solution-processed optoelectronics. The c.w. lasing is widely regarded as a key stepping stone on the path to a laser diode. Leading contenders for such lasers include organic semiconductors, inorganic colloidal nanocrystals and metal halide perovskites. So far, c.w. lasing has never been demonstrated in organic semiconductors due to triplet absorption.209 The triplet absorption is less of an issue for conventional inorganic semiconductors and metal halide perovskites because strong spin–orbit coupling and small exciton binding energies frustrate spin-conservation. Recently, c.w. lasing was realized in inorganic colloidal nanocrystals after two decades of investigations.210–214 Nevertheless, further progress towards electrically injected lasing is highly challenging for inorganic colloidal nanocrystals because of their limited charge carrier mobility.

Metal halide perovskites are promising for solution-processed electrically injected lasers because they combine large optical gains with high and balanced charge carrier mobility. The initial attempt to realize the c.w. perovskite laser was made by Jia et al. based on a second-order DFB laser architecture consisting of a silicon grating coated successively with a gold layer, an alumina layer, and a MAPbI3 film (Fig. 13A).191 The gold layer is used on the one hand to reduce cavity mode overlap with the light-absorbing silicon and on the other hand to improve thermal conductivity. The alumina layer suppresses the surface recombination of perovskites. Quasi-c.w. lasing was achieved in this DFB laser device under the excitation of an InGaN diode laser with 25 ns pulse duration and 2 MHz repetition rate (Fig. 13B). Unfortunately, true c.w. lasing was not possible, as the lasing action ceases ∼25 ns after pump turn-on (Fig. 13C). The origin of this lasing death phenomenon remains unclear at present, although thermal runaway and Auger recombination have been ruled out.

Subsequently, they successfully realized c.w. lasing in the same MAPbI3 perovskite below the phase-transition temperature (T = 160 K) from tetragonal to orthorhombic (Fig. 13D–F).75 The c.w. optical gain originates from a small amount of photogenerated tetragonal-phase inclusions in a predominantly orthorhombic-phase MAPbI3. With temperature cooling down to ∼100 K, the MAPbI3 perovskite reaches the orthorhombic phase. Upon c.w. excitation above a threshold, a tetragonal phase forms inside the orthorhombic lattice dynamically (Fig. 13E). These narrow-bandgap tetragonal-phase inclusions act as carrier sinks within the wide-bandgap orthorhombic-phase host matrix, facilitating population inversion in a fashion analogous to organic host–guest materials and inorganic quantum wells. This mixed-phase MAPbI3 perovskite can sustain continuous lasing for over an hour. This finding represents a significant step towards the ultimate goal of a perovskite laser diode.

Despite this impressive achievement, the pure-phase perovskites seem more practically useful for electrically injected lasers. Very recently, Brenner et al. reported c.w. ASE in phase-stable lead halide perovskites (Fig. 13G–I).215 To achieve phase stability, they employed a triple-cation perovskite, Cs0.1(MA0.17FA0.83)0.9Pb0.84(I0.84Br0.16)2.68, as the gain medium. 4.2. Structure engineering

4.2.1. Single-mode lasers. The controllable output of laser modes is of high interest in laser physics and applications. Single-mode lasers are particularly important for application in sensing, optical communication and spectroscopy. The single-mode operation can be readily obtained in macroscopic laser devices by using the DFB and DBR cavities, as discussed in Section 3.2. In contrast, the micro/nanocrystal cavities usually support multimode oscillations resulting from their wide bandwidths. The simplest approach to achieve single-mode miniaturized lasers is by reducing the cavity size to enlarge the mode spacing. As shown in Fig. 14A–C, the single-mode lasing was successfully realized in CsPbBr3 nanocrystals.216 The nanocuboids have a typical size of ∼400 nm, which results in a mode spacing (estimated to be ∼100 nm) much larger than the gain profile of CsPbBr3 perovskites. However, the single-mode lasing is realized at the expense of laser performance. The lasing threshold rises because the decrease of cavity size reduces the Q factors and optical gains of microcavities. This issue can be mitigated to some extent by fabricating high-Q microcavities. Tang et al. synthesized the submicron CsPbBr3 spheres via the CVD method, which function as ultrahigh-Q WGM cavities.168 The Q factors of these CsPbBr3 spherical cavities are the highest (up to 1.5 × 104) among all perovskite micro/nanocrystal cavities.217 As a result, low-threshold single-mode lasing was obtained from these CsPbBr3 spherical cavities.

Unfortunately, these perovskite microdisks are subjected to the reduction of Q factors because of the roughness induced optical scattering. The coupled cavity provides an ideal platform for fulfilling single-mode operation of miniaturized lasers without sacrificing laser performances.218 Within the coupled cavity system, one component cavity functions as a filter to the resonant mode of the other, leading to mode spacing enlargement and consequently single-mode lasing. Liu et al. constructed a perovskite coupled cavity by integrating two MAPbBr3 microplates via micromanipulation (Fig. 14G).74 The constructed perovskite coupled cavity exhibited stable single-mode lasing (Fig. 14H and I).

Laser mode tuning is required for directly manipulating communication channels in ultrafast optical networks.219–221 The coupled cavity system provides a possibility for laser mode tuning by introducing a tunable optical microcavity. Zhao et al. constructed a hybrid coupled cavity comprising a perovskite nanowire and a stimuli-responsive polystyrene microdisk (Fig. 15A).222 The MAPbBr3 nanowire serves as a laser cavity, while the polystyrene microdisk serves as a filter cavity. The laser modes in the MAPbBr3 nanowire can be selectively filtered out through adjusting the resonance modes of the polystyrene microdisk (Fig. 15B). Here, the resonance modes were adjusted by introducing acetone vapor to change the effective radius of the polystyrene microdisk. As a result, a reversibly switchable single-mode lasing behavior was demonstrated from the hybrid coupled cavity (Fig. 15C). Very recently, Yang et al. reported piezoelectric-induced dynamic modulation of single-mode perovskite microwire lasers (Fig. 15D–G).170 The selected CsPbBr3 microwire exhibits single-mode lasing with the four lateral facets constituting a small-size WGM cavity. By applying tensile and compressive strains onto the CsPbBr3 microwire, the piezoelectric polarization effect induced the variation of the refractive index (Fig. 15F and G). The tensile and compressive strain leads to red- and blue-shift of the lasing mode, respectively. This enables an effective tuning of the lasing mode position up to 8.5 nm for the CsPbBr3 microwire with an externally applied strain ranging between −0.96% and 1.06%.

4.2.2. Subwavelength lasers. From inception to present, the miniaturization of lasers has always been pursued because the ever-smaller lasers have opened up a plenty of new application areas.174,223–225 The metal halide perovskites have shown the potential for miniaturized lasers as various perovskite microcrystals function as optical gain media and microcavities simultaneously. However, such dielectric lasers are restricted by the diffraction limit (half the wavelength of light in medium) in both physical device dimension and optical mode volume. Surface plasmon polaritons (SPPs), the collective oscillations of electrons on the surface of a metal, can tightly confine light far below the optical diffraction limit.226 The hybrid metal–dielectric geometry provides a platform for creating subwavelength lasers.227,228 A facile approach to achieve subwavelength coherent light sources is by integrating the dielectric laser with the subwavelength plasmonic waveguide (Fig. 16A).172 Li et al. developed an epitaxial growth technique of perovskites on subwavelength silver nanowires to construct the hybrid metal–perovskite heterostructures (Fig. 16B and C). The excellent laser performance of the perovskites is retained in such heterostructures (Fig. 16D and E). In addition, the embedded heterostructures have a good connection between the perovskite crystal and the Ag nanowire, facilitating effective photon–plasmon coupling. From the perovskite microcrystal, the WGM laser modes were outcoupled into the subwavelength Ag nanowire as SPP modes (Fig. 16F and G). The intrinsic laser characteristics (e.g. wavelength and polarization) of the perovskite microcrystal are maintained in the subwavelength plasmonic waveguide.

Although providing subwavelength coherent light sources, the metal–dielectric heterostructures have overall device sizes larger than half the wavelength because the dielectric laser part is restricted by the diffraction limit. A way of addressing this issue is to construct plasmonic lasers, where the SPPs are directly amplified by the stimulated emission of gain medium.229,230 The principal challenge in achieving a plasmonic laser is to overcome the intrinsic Ohmic loss of the metal.231 Metal halide perovskites are promising candidates for plasmonic lasers because of their high optical gains. A hybrid plasmonic waveguide cavity geometry was adopted by inserting a nanoscale insulating dielectric layer between the metal and the perovskite nanowire to minimize this loss (Fig. 16H).232–235 The coupling between the plasmonic and waveguide modes enables the optical confinement in the non-metal gap region (Fig. 16I), thereby effectively reducing the Ohmic loss. Unlike the photonic lasers constrained by the diffraction limit, the plasmonic lasers do not experience mode cutoff with the decrease of physical device dimension (Fig. 16J). This hybrid metal–dielectric cavity geometry enables the realization of true subwavelength perovskite lasers (Fig. 16K). Furthermore, the perovskite plasmonic lasers possess a strictly longitudinal polarization. These perovskite-based subwavelength coherent light sources not only significantly increase the integration level of photonic devices and circuits, but also benefit the optoelectronic interconnects and hybrid optoelectronic integration due to the similar dimension with electronic elements.

4.2.3. Random lasers. A random laser is a special type of laser where the optical feedback is provided via multiple scattering, as illustrated in Fig. 17A.236,237 Like the conventional lasers, the random lasers exhibit high energy conversion efficiencies. Nevertheless, they show low spatial coherence because the random scattering produces many uncorrelated optical modes emitting in all directions. Therefore, the random laser is an advanced lighting source for laser illumination and speckle-free imaging.238,239 Another unique advantage of random lasers lies in their low-cost and simple processing technology as they can be realized by coating gain media on the surfaces of arbitrary shapes. The crystalline nature makes the metal halide perovskites a promising candidate for random lasing because the grains in perovskite films can serve as scattering centers for the optical feedback.240–242 The first perovskite random lasing was demonstrated in MAPbI3 microcrystal networks in 2014.243

Recently, flexible perovskite random lasing was demonstrated in MAPbBr3 films on a polyimide substrate (Fig. 17F–J).245 Such perovskite films can support random lasing under both flat and curved conditions (Fig. 17F and G). The compact and smooth perovskite film with well-interconnected grain boundaries facilitates high emission efficiency (Fig. 17H). The optical feedback originates from the multiple scattering at the internal grain boundaries and surface cracks. Bending the polyimide substrate makes the cracks more abundant and pronounced, leading to stronger scattering (Fig. 17H–J). Therefore, the laser threshold decreases with the bending radii. Besides the intrinsic perovskite grains, other scattering particles can provide optical feedback for random lasing. For example, a light wave that passes through a silica/polystyrene sphere film will undergo strong multiple scattering.246 Li et al. directly synthesized CsPbBr3 QDs on monodispersed silica spheres through an amination-mediated nucleation and growth process (Fig. 17K–M).247 The anchoring and separation of CsPbBr3 QDs on the silica spheres significantly improved the stability of CsPbBr3 QDs because of the inhibition of photoinduced regrowth and deterioration. As a result, the CsPbBr3 QDs/silica sphere composite film exhibited highly stable random lasing with the silica spheres serving as scattering centers (Fig. 17N).

4.2.4. Polariton lasers. Polaritons are bosonic quasiparticles formed from the strong coupling of elementary excitations (e.g. phonon and exciton) with electromagnetic waves. When the de Broglie wavelength becomes comparable to particle distance above a critical excitation density (or equivalently below a critical temperature), a large fraction of bosons condenses into the lowest quantum state, resulting in a spontaneous macroscopic coherence. This process is called as Bose–Einstein condensation (BEC). Polariton lasers are a type of coherent light sources that exploit the coherent nature of Bose–Einstein condensates of cavity exciton-polaritons (Fig. 18A).248 A major attraction of polariton lasers is the potential to achieve much lower thresholds since no population inversion is required.249 An ideal material for BEC has a large exciton binding energy to support high polariton density and a strong Coulomb interaction to drive efficient polariton condensation. However, there is a trade-off between the exciton binding energy and Coulomb interaction. The Wannier–Mott excitons in traditional inorganic semiconductors have small exciton binding energies (∼10 meV) and strong Coulomb interactions. The strong Coulomb interactions contribute to polariton–polariton interactions, leading to effective relaxation to the ground state.250–253 Due to small exciton binding energies, the polariton lasing from traditional inorganic semiconductors has mostly been observed at cryogenic temperatures and/or complex quantum structures. In contrast, the Frenkel excitons in organic semiconductors have large binding energies (≥100 meV) and weak Coulomb interactions. The large exciton binding energies enable room-temperature polariton lasing.254–256 Nevertheless, the weak Coulomb interactions result in high polariton lasing threshold and weak nonlinear behavior of the polaritons.

The metal halide perovskites are ideal alternatives to traditional inorganic and organic semiconductors for BEC because of their modest exciton binding energies and Coulomb interactions. The first perovskite polariton lasing was reported by Su et al. in a perovskite embedded DBR cavity at room temperature (Fig. 18B–F).257 The CVD synthesized CsPbCl3 nanoplatelet was selected as the excitonic material owing to its large exciton binding energy (∼72 meV) and high crystal quality.100 The emission of the CsPbCl3 nanoplatelet is centered at ∼2.99 eV with a fwhm of 80 meV, while the polariton emission from the CsPbCl3 nanoplatelet embedded DBR microcavity shifts to ∼2.9 eV with the fwhm narrowing to 9.7 meV (Fig. 18C). At low pump fluence, the polaritons exhibit a broad emission distribution in both energy and momentum (Fig. 18D). Upon reaching a threshold, the polaritons exhibit a much stronger emission near the zero-momentum (k‖ = 0) ground state, indicating the onset of polariton condensation. At higher pump fluence, polaritons macroscopically occupy the ground state. This nonlinear behavior is caused by the stimulated scattering of polaritons into the lowest quantum state. Recently, the polariton condensation was demonstrated in CsPbBr3 perovskites embedded in planar cavities.258,259 More interesting polariton condensation phenomena have been realized in these CsPbBr3 cavities, such as long-range coherent exciton polariton condensate flow and Rydberg exciton polariton condensation.

The polariton condensations have been mainly demonstrated in the planar DBR cavities, which suffer from sophisticated fabrication techniques. With easy accessibility and integrability, the nanowire cavities have attracted much research interest for the exciton–photon coupling and polariton condensation (Fig. 18G).260–263 Park et al. first discovered the strong exciton–photon interaction in perovskite nanowire lasers.101 The F–P mode spacing of the perovskite nanowire lasers dramatically decreases with the wavelength. Such unequal mode spacings correspond to a strong dependency of light propagation speed on the photon frequency, which is a typical characteristic of polaritons. The coupling strength between the exciton and photon determines the Rabi splitting energy (Ω) between the lower and upper polariton branches, which is a function of the exciton oscillator strength (f) and cavity mode volume (Vm) as Ω ∝ (fVm)1/2.264 The exciton oscillator strength has a positive correlation with exciton binding energy.265,266 The relationship between the exciton–photon coupling strength and exciton binding energy was demonstrated by Wang et al. in experiment.267 With similar cavity geometries, the exciton–photon coupling strengths in all-inorganic perovskite nanowires follow the same trend as exciton binding energies (Fig. 18H–J). Another advantage of the nanowire cavity for exciton–polariton coupling is the small mode volume, which significantly enhances light–matter interactions. Zhang, Liu and coworkers reported that the coupling strengths between the exciton and photon in perovskite nanowires increase with the decrease of nanowire dimension (Fig. 18K–N).268,269 More importantly, the enhanced exciton–photon coupling strength results in a significant reduction of the lasing threshold. Recently, Evans et al. realized c.w. lasing from the CsPbBr3 nanowire, which originates from polariton modes.181 Although the exciton polaritons play an important role in perovskite nanowire lasers, no conclusive evidence of polariton condensation has been demonstrated so far. One possibility is that polariton condensation occurred but was not detected due to the lack of measurement methods for directly obtaining angle/momentum-resolved spectra of nanowire cavities. Another possibility is that polariton condensation did not occur, which might be attributed to low cavity Q factors resulting from the low end-facet reflectivity of the nanowires. The rate of polaritons leaking out of the cavity as photons is much faster than that of condensation. More research effort is needed to explore the polariton condensation in perovskite nanowire cavities, including the development of measurement methods and the optimization of material systems.

4.3. Laser arrays

The laser array concept was initially proposed for power scaling of semiconductor lasers.199 From then on, the laser arrays have enabled many new technologies, such as laser printing, medical therapy, on-chip optical interconnects, laser imaging detection and ranging, among others.200 Due to the great potential in various applications, the monolithic integration of metal halide perovskite lasers has captured much research interest. Actually, the perovskite arrays have been widely explored for optoelectronic and photonic applications, including solar cells, photodetectors, light-emitting diodes, and lasers.270–273 Here, we focus our discussion on the controllable fabrication of perovskite laser arrays. So far, many fabrication techniques have been developed to obtain perovskite laser arrays based on liquid-phase self-assembly, vapor-phase growth, and top-down fabrication.

The key of the liquid-phase method for fabricating perovskite laser arrays is the regular splitting and positioning of perovskite precursor solution. Feng et al. developed a type of wettability-mediated micropillar-structured silicon templates to regularly split perovskite precursor solution and locate the solution microdomains (Fig. 19A and B).273 The regularly arranged micropillars have asymmetric wettability, lyophilic tops and lyophobic sidewalls. When in contact with the micropillars, the perovskite precursor solution wets the tops but is blocked by the sidewalls. Because the liquid cohesion is smaller than the adhesion between the liquid and pillar tops, a concave meniscus forms above the micropillars, which splits the perovskite precursor solution into regular microdomains. The nucleation and growth of perovskites are confined in the microdomains, leading to the formation of single-crystal perovskite microlaser arrays (Fig. 19C and D).

Afterwards, a more robust and flexible strategy was proposed to fabricate perovskite laser arrays by using a soft PDMS template to split and confine the perovskite precursor solution.105 The PDMS templates have regular cylindrical hole arrays. When pressing the soft PDMS template onto the perovskite precursor solution spread on a substrate, the solution is directly split by and confined in the cylindrical holes of the template. A large-scale perovskite microdisk laser array forms after the complete evaporation of the solvent. This space-confined strategy has a great controllability on the dimension, position, density, and especially morphology of the perovskite crystals. For example, the growth of perovskite nanowire laser arrays can be realized by adjusting the PDMS templates.170,274

Another strategy to split the perovskite precursor solution relies on periodically patterned substrates.275 First, silica microdisk arrays were fabricated on silicon substrates through standard photolithography. Then, MAPbBr3 solution was spin-coated onto the patterned substrates. During the solvent evaporation, surface tension drove the precursor solution on the top of each silica microdisk to assemble into a polycrystal microdisk. The obtained MAPbBr3 microdisk array exhibited uniform WGM lasing with peak positions nearly the same.

Inkjet printing is the most direct approach to pattern the liquid droplets. Gu et al. demonstrated the inkjet printing technique for fabricating perovskite laser arrays (Fig. 19E).178 The perovskite precursor solution as ink was programmatically printed onto a lyophobic substrate. The lyophobic effect induces the formation of semispherical microdroplets of perovskite precursor solution. The perovskite self-assembly confined in the well-patterned microdroplets produces single-crystal perovskite laser arrays (Fig. 19F and G). One major superiority of the inkjet printing in fabricating laser arrays is the multicolor laser integration.276 Full-color perovskite laser arrays can be easily obtained by successively patterning different perovskite inks.

The key of the vapor-phase method for fabricating perovskite laser arrays is the periodically patterned nucleation centers for heteroepitaxy. Liu et al. developed a template-assisted vapor deposition method to fabricate large-scale perovskite laser arrays, as illustrated in Fig. 19H.277 Monolayer boron nitride films were transferred onto SiO2/Si substrates and processed into regularly arranged arrays. The well patterned boron nitrides serve as heterogeneous nucleation centers of metal halide perovskites, and lead to the vapor-phase heteroepitaxial growth of perovskite laser arrays (Fig. 19I).

The top-down lithography techniques can be used to process the perovskite films into laser arrays. For example, large-scale perovskite microlaser arrays were successfully fabricated by using a direct laser ablation method (Fig. 19J).113 The detailed discussion on the laser ablation technique can be found in Section 3.1.2. The laser ablation exhibits high processing speed, reproducibility, and addressability, making it possible to fabricate centimeter-sized arrays of perovskite microdisk lasers. Similarly, the perovskite QD laser arrays can be fabricated through an orthogonal lithography approach.278 As a sacrificial layer, a fluorinated polymer was patterned on the substrates via photolithography. Then, CsPbBr3 QDs were spin-coated onto the patterned substrates. Finally, the CsPbBr3 QDs attached to the photoresist/fluorinated polymer bilayer were lifted off by dissolving the fluorinated polymer. As a result, large-area perovskite QD laser arrays were obtained.

5. Stability and toxicity of perovskite lasers

5.1. Stability

An important aspect of lasers relevant to practical application is the device lifetime. Although exhibiting excellent laser performances, the metal halide perovskites suffer from poor stability.279,280 The metal halide perovskites degrade when exposed to moisture, oxygen, light, and heat. We first briefly discuss the degradation mechanism of MAPbI3 perovskites, which guides the development of stable perovskite lasers. Water reacts with MAPbI3, producing volatile methylamine and hydrogen iodine. Oxygen penetrates the perovskite through iodide vacancies and traps electron forming the highly reactive superoxide anion. The superoxide anion decomposes MAPbI3 into lead iodide, neutral iodine, and water. Similarly, the photogenerated holes decompose MAPbI3 into neutral lead and iodine, and methylammonium iodide. The presence of both oxygen and light dramatically accelerates the degradation of MAPbI3. The thermal degradation of MAPbI3 proceeds through chemical decomposition, followed by the sublimation of methylamine and hydrogen iodide.

Various strategies have been developed to improve the stability of metal halide perovskite lasers. The first one is to synthesize perovskite single crystals for stable lasing because the low trap density can inhibit the oxygen- and light-induced degradation of perovskite structures. As shown in Table 1, the monocrystal perovskite lasers have much longer device lifetimes than their polycrystal counterparts. The second strategy is based on the composition engineering of metal halide perovskites. From the degradation mechanism of perovskites, it can be concluded that the A-site cation has the largest effect on the stability of perovskites. A-site cation substitution provides the most effective way for improving the stability of perovskite lasers. Compared with the methylammonium cation, the formamidinium cation has low acidity, large size, and strong hydrogen bonding with the [BX6]4− framework,281 which suppress hydrolysis reaction and decomposition of the metal halide perovskites. As a result, the formamidinium perovskite lasers are more stable than the methylammonium ones (Table 1).71,282 The partial substitution of methylammonium and formamidinium cations with even larger organic ammonium cations further improves the stability of perovskites because the large organic ammonium cations not only suppress the decomposition of perovskites but also inhibit the penetration of water molecules into perovskite structures due to their hydrophobicity. Therefore, 2D hybrid perovskite lasers exhibit better stability than their 3D counterparts (Table 1).158–161 Furthermore, the complete substitution of the organic ammonium cation with the cesium cation produces all-inorganic perovskites, which exhibit the highest stability for laser applications because of the much lower reactivity and volatility of the cesium cation.72,99 The third strategy for improving perovskite stability is packaging,31,283 which avoids the direct exposure to oxygen and moisture, preventing the irreversible loss of photo- and heat-generated volatile species. As shown in Table 1, the perovskite lasers show much better stability under the protection of polymers, boron nitride films, and DBR cavities. The fourth approach for improving the stability of perovskite lasers is by increasing heat dissipation to reduce the thermal degradation of perovskites. The high-thermal-conductivity sapphire substrates can be used to assist heat dissipation.75,191 In addition, the encapsulation of perovskite lasers with high-thermal-conductivity boron nitride films can accelerate the thermal dissipation as well.284

5.2. Toxicity

The toxicity of lead is a major obstacle limiting the practical application of perovskites. The device encapsulation is the simplest approach to avoid human exposure to the toxic lead. Accordingly, the recycling technology of lead perovskites is required for the prevention of environmental pollution. Another way to solve this problem is by substituting lead with nontoxic elements.288,289 A successful case is tin halide perovskites, from which near-infrared lasing was demonstrated. However, the tin perovskites suffer from poor stability and optoelectronic properties compared with lead perovskites. Fortunately, the chemical diversity of metal halide perovskites provides a promising material platform for exploring lead-free perovskites for laser applications.290

6. Summary and outlook

Over the past five years, we have witnessed explosive progress in the field of metal halide perovskite lasers. Numerous studies focused on the materials engineering of metal halide perovskites for high-performance lasers. A set of controllable fabrication and processing techniques were developed for obtaining perovskite lasers. By engineering the composition and structure of metal halide perovskites, great progress has been made in improving the performance and expanding the functionalities of perovskite lasers, such as lowering the lasing threshold down to hundreds of nJ cm−2, broadening the wavelength tuning range over ∼530 nm, enabling multiwavelength output and continuous-wave operation, improving the spectral purity (single-mode output) and the temporal coherence (linewidth narrowing to ∼0.09), reducing the device dimension down to subwavelength scales, and prolonging device lifetime up to dozens of hours in continuous operation. This Review comprehensively discussed the metal halide perovskite lasers from the viewpoint of materials chemistry and engineering. Special emphasis is put on gaining insight into the structure–property relationship in perovskite lasers, which we hope could promote and guide the research and exploration of new type of perovskite lasers. Although great progress has been made in exploring the metal halide perovskite lasers, difficult challenges remain on many fronts.

The lasing mechanism of metal halide perovskites is not entirely clear yet. Central to the debate on the lasing mechanisms is whether the stimulated emission of perovskites originates from free charge carriers or excitons. A major reason for the debate is the lack of accurate physical parameters of perovskites, such as exciton binding energy, exciton Bohr radius, Mott density, among others. The exciton binding energy determines the population distribution between excitons and free charge carriers. However, the accurate determination of exciton binding energy of perovskites remains a tough challenge. The large variation in experimentally determined values of exciton binding energies for each kind of perovskites results from the differences in material quality and measurement methods and inaccurate assumptions in theoretical models and calculations. The improvement of existing measurement methods and development of new characterization approaches are highly desired for obtaining accurate exciton binding energies of metal halide perovskites. The Mott density is another parameter affecting the population distribution between excitons and free charge carriers. It is a function of exciton Bohr radius and binding energy. Besides the lack of accurate exciton binding energies, the determination of the exciton Bohr radius of perovskites is rarely reported, even though it is important for revealing the lasing mechanism. More research effort should be devoted to determining the exciton Bohr radius of metal halide perovskites. The investigation of photoexcited state dynamics by using ultrafast spectroscopy offers a way to discovering the lasing mechanism in metal halide perovskites. However, such investigations mainly focused on two kinds of perovskites currently, that is, all-inorganic CsPbBr3 and organic–inorganic hybrid 2D RP perovskites. More laser dynamics investigations should be performed on different perovskite materials and devices to provide a detailed understanding of the lasing mechanism in metal halide perovskites, which is helpful for guiding the design of perovskite lasers towards practical applications.

The electrically pumped perovskite laser is a next frontier because electrical injection is almost a necessity for all semiconductor laser applications. With high optical gains and balanced carrier mobility, the metal halide perovskites are promising for electrically pumped lasers. The recent realization of c.w. lasing further demonstrated the great potential of perovskites for electrically pumped lasers. However, there are many challenges ahead of us in reaching electrically pumped perovskite lasers. The first one is that the c.w. lasing in perovskites only operates at cryogenic temperature so far. The origin of c.w. lasing death in perovskites at room temperature remains unclear. Both theoretical and experimental investigations should be performed to uncover the c.w. lasing death mechanism to provide a guidance for the design of perovskites towards room-temperature c.w. lasing. The second challenge is the heat management in perovskite laser devices. Due to diffusive phonon transport, metal halide perovskites have low thermal conductivity ranging from 10−1 to 100 W m−1 K−1,291–294 which is one to two orders of magnitude lower than that in conventional inorganic semiconductors.295 Hence, the optimization of the device architecture is imperative for mitigating heat accumulation in the perovskite active layer towards electrically injected perovskite lasers. For example, the thin perovskite layer and high-thermal-conductivity substrate and carrier transport layer might be helpful for increasing heat dissipation. The third challenge is about lasing threshold of metal halide perovskites. The current density required for electrically injected lasing can be estimated based on the threshold pump fluence of optically pumped laser. Most of optically pumped perovskite lasers exhibited threshold pump fluences in the order of 103 nJ cm−2. The corresponding threshold current density is about 103 A cm−2,38,296 which is far beyond the capabilities of current perovskite electroluminescence devices. For one thing, the lasing thresholds of perovskites need to be further reduced. Without careful control of composition and structure, the metal halide perovskites usually exhibit limited luminescence efficiencies. Nevertheless, this problem has long been overlooked in the field of perovskite lasers. Actually, a number of strategies have been developed to enhance the luminescence of perovskites that make use of quantum effect, ion doping, and surface passivation.30,94,297,298 We hope to see that similar or other new strategies will be applied to increase the optical gains of perovskites for low-threshold lasing. For another thing, the carrier injection efficiency needs to be improved, which can be realized by engineering interfacial layers. For the interfacial layer engineering in perovskite electroluminescence devices, we refer the interested readers to several specialized reviews.30,299,300

The design and development of new metal halide perovskites has been and continues to be a research focus for laser applications. The chemical and structural diversity of metal halide perovskites provides a promising material platform for exploring various new perovskites for high-performance and multifunctional lasers. Lead-free perovskites are particularly important for solving the toxicity problem. There are plenty of metal ions that have potential to replace the toxic lead ions for perovskite lasers, such as tin, germanium, and silver/bismuth double-metal, among others.288–290 In addition, the synthesis of composite perovskite materials by integrating perovskites with other materials, such as traditional organic and inorganic semiconductors, is a promising research direction because they not only combine the advantages of each component, but also generate new functionalities based on the interactions between the component materials.301,302

The demonstration of applications of metal halide perovskite lasers is another research frontier. With outstanding performances, perovskite lasers have great potential in a wide range of applications, such as laser illumination, display, imaging, biological and chemical sensing, on-chip photonic integration, and so on. However, the application demonstration of perovskite lasers remains very limited so far. More research efforts should be directed to implementing perovskite lasers into various photonic applications.

In summary, the metal halide perovskites exhibited many characteristics that an ideal optical gain medium should have, such as high optical gains and balanced carrier mobility. In addition, the low-temperature solution-processability makes the perovskites promising materials for low-cost and large-area laser applications. The chemically tailorable bandgap of perovskites enables tunable lasing from visible to near-infrared spectral regions. Meanwhile, there are several drawbacks of metal halide perovskites for laser applications, including poor stability, toxicity and low thermal conductivity. Fortunately, these problems have been becoming less serious gradually. For example, the stability of perovskite lasers has been improved significantly through composition engineering and the encapsulation strategy. It is anticipated that more researchers will be drawn to this emerging and interesting field of perovskite lasers. We believe all problems encountered in perovskite lasers will be settled finally with the collective efforts of researchers from different academic communities of chemistry, materials science, and physics. The perovskite lasers will find many applications in the near future.
