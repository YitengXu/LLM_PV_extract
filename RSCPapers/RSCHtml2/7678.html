A theoretical study of perovskites related to CH3NH3PbX3 (X = F, Cl, Br, I)†

Ibon Alkorta * and José Elguero
Instituto de Química Médica (CSIC), Juan de la Cierva, 3, E-28006 Madrid, Spain. E-mail: ibon@iqm.csic.es

Received 18th April 2018 , Accepted 9th July 2018

First published on 13th July 2018

Abstract

The bond dissociation energies of MAPI (CH3NH3PbI3) and related perovskites (with F, Cl, Br instead of I and with bases other than methylamine) have been calculated using a simplified model consisting of a corner of the perovskite (PbX3−) and the methyl ammonium (CH3NH3+) and other protonated bases. The values obtained show that besides the size (related to the tolerance factor), the energy of the interaction should be considered. Using relativistic corrections (ADF), the 1H, 13C, 15N and 207Pb absolute shieldings were calculated and transformed into chemical shifts by empirical equations established here. The 13C and 15N light nuclei were well reproduced by the “corner” model but the 207Pb chemical shifts need a large correction factor owing to the fact that lead in the perovskites is surrounded by six iodine atoms instead of the “corner's” three atoms.

The importance of perovskites leads us to seek a simple model that allows carrying out fast calculations on some of their properties that periodic models cannot. These properties are related to some topics that we will report in more detail.

1.1. Theoretical models: C+PbX3− corner vs. periodic models

To study perovskites theoretically (but the following is the same for MOFs, COFs, ZIFs,…), two approaches are possible: either to use methods based on periodic properties13,21–26,55 or to carry out classical calculations of isolated molecules or molecular complexes, but both approaches are very different.

When classical calculations are carried out on salts, like [(CH3)NH3+PbI3−], it is necessary to ensure that the neutrality and the number of valence charges are calculated. If one uses a cube, besides the Pb cations being discoordinated, the charge is +4 instead of −1. If one surrounds the central cube by 26 cubelets (see Table S1 (ESI†) on stacked congruent cubes) to form a 3 × 3 × 3 grid (n = 3), the charge of each cubelet is −0.59, still far from the real situation. For n = 10 (1000 cubelets), the charge per cubelet is −0.97, which is acceptable but computationally out of reach.

There is another possibility: to study [(CH3)NH3+PbI3−] in the position they occupy in MAPI perovskite, i.e. in one of the eight equivalent corners. Some authors have used this approach that we will call the “corner model” (see Fig. 1b).13,27,28


Fig. 1 The N–H⋯I hydrogen bonds in MAPI: (a) the unit cell and (b) the corner.

If successful in reproducing some of the properties of perovskites, this approach is considerably faster than periodic calculations.

One aspect that has been much discussed is the Goldschmidt tolerance factor (t or TF). Introduced in 1927,38 it has been used to determine the size of the cations for a given ZX3 anion that are appropriate to act as a template for the synthesis of perovskites. This depends on the radii of A (the cation, for instance MA), B (the metal, for instance Pb) and X (the halogen anion, F, Cl, Br, and I):

TF = (rA + rX)/√2[rB + rX]

According to the literature, it must range between 0.8 and 1.0.29,39,40 Further studies have improved the criteria,41–43 but this remains a pure steric one without considering hydrogen bonds and other directional interactions.

1.3. Orientational disorder and hydrogen bonds

Since PbI3 is an anion and MA is a cation, it is normal that strong hydrogen bonds (HBs) will be established in response to the electrostatic interaction. Besides, both being of C3v symmetry, three HBs are the expected result. It is surprising that several publications contain H3C–NH3 structures without assigning the C and N atoms when the N–H groups form HBs much better than the C–H groups.

A molecule like MAPbI3 (named MAPI) of Oh symmetry has 12 iodine atoms, thus resulting in four PbI3 corners on the cube (the I–Pb–I angles have values identical or close to 90°), therefore a staggered MA can move between four positions inside the perovskite cavity (a cube octahedron). For instance, in the tetragonal phase, MA is disordered on the Wyckoff44 4(b) site.45–48

The disorder is related to the phase: fully ordered, orthorhombic phase; partially ordered, tetragonal phase; fully disordered, cubic phase.45 The number of publications discussing the disorder in perovskites is very large.9,46,49–62 The dynamics of MA cations inside MAPI perovskite has been studied by Wasylischen et al. in the solid state by using NMR spectroscopy (2H, 14N, and 207Pb).62

Reports on N–H⋯X HBs (mainly X = I) are also abundant;46,51,53,56,63–71 note that in the case of the hydroxylammonium cation, the HB implies the preference of OH over NH of the NH3+ group.72

Experimental data on the HBs, generally obtained by neutron diffraction, depend on the phase, but it is clear that in the case of MAPI, N–H of the H3N+ group is bonded to iodine atoms linked to three different Pb atoms, with one of them being axial and the other two being equatorial66 (also named α- and β-modes) (Fig. 1a).71 The HBs of MAPBr depend on the phase but always belong to the iodine atoms linked to different lead atoms.59 These authors (using Raman spectroscopy) also found C–H⋯I HBs.

The equatorial HBs are stronger and shorter than the axial ones.63–66,71 Since our model has three identical HBs, we averaged the literature data to compare with our calculations. A number of structures have been determined by neutron diffraction (powder, single crystal, both conventional and synchrotron sources, from 11 to 350 K). 1.4. Template vs. ferroelectric effects

The cation inside the cage not only serves as a template but also determines the ferroelectric effects depending on its orientation (it can change from ferroelectric to antiferroelectric).68,76–78 This aspect will not be covered in the present work but due to its great importance, it has been commented here.

1.5. 207Pb NMR data

207Pb has I = 1/2 and medium sensitivity (natural abundance 22.1%); the reference is tetramethyllead, Me4Pb, although Pb(NO3)2 is often used in solid-state NMR spectroscopy. The absolute shielding of 207Pb in Me4Pb has been determined by Jaszunski et al., leading to a value of 10.790 ppm for the isolated molecule in the gas phase.79 Other values used for the discussion will be reported in Section 3.3. 207Pb NMR.

2. Computational details

The geometry of the system has been optimized using the hybrid DFT/HF B3LYP method80,81 and the aug-cc-pVDZ basis set82 for the light atoms (H, C, N, O, F and Cl) while the effective core potential (ECP) aug-cc-pVDZ-PP basis set83 has been used for the heaviest atoms (Br, I and Pb). Frequency calculations have been carried out at the same computational level to confirm that the structures obtained correspond to energetic minima. In some selected cases, the systems have been reoptimized at the PBE0//aug-cc-pVTZ/aug-cc-pVTZ-PP level.84 All these calculations have been carried out using the Gaussian-16 program.85

NMR chemical shieldings have been obtained using the full electron QZ4P basis,86 the PB86 functional87–89 and the relativistic ZORA spin–orbit Hamiltonian.90 These calculations have been performed using the ADF-2017 program.91

4. Conclusions

We have studied the geometries, energies and NMR chemical shifts of the complexes formed by six anions (PbI3−, PbBr3−, PbCl3−, PbF3−, PbI2Br−, and PbIBr2−) and six cations ([NH4]+, [(CH3)NH3]+, [(CF3)NH3]+, [(H2N)NH3]+, [(HO)NH3]+, and [OH3]+). These complexes are the corners of the corresponding perovskites.

The calculations carried out at the PBE0//aug-cc-pVTZ/aug-cc-pVTZ-PP level and for the NMR properties at the full electron QZ4P basis, the PB86 functional and the relativistic ZORA spin–orbit Hamiltonian yield good results.

The geometries are similar when the “corners” are compared with the perovskite experimental geometries obtained, in general, by neutron diffraction except for Pb–X distances and N–H⋯X angles where the PbX3− anion differs from the perovskite unit cell.

The hydrogen bonds are equivalent for ammonium salts and PbX3− anions; for the hydroxylammonium cation, OH forms a stronger HB than that of NH, although these belong to an ammonium cation. In the mixed anions, I is a better HB acceptor than Br.

The binding energies afford a comprehensive view of the complexes that can be partitioned into different contributions using Free–Wilson models.

To discuss the 1H, 13C, 15N and 207Pb NMR chemical shifts, we have calculated, at the same level, a few simple molecules to establish empirical relationships between absolute shieldings and chemical shifts. Then, we have compared the experimental chemical shifts of perovskites with the calculated ones, which are in good agreement except for 207Pb where a dummy variable is necessary to compare the simple molecules and the “corner” complexes to perovskites.

Concerning the strengths and weaknesses of the presented model, the main strengths are its simplicity and good performance; on the other side, its main weakness is that it cannot be transformed step by step unto a continuous model.

Finally, we conclude that the corner model is a useful approximation to perovskites because of its simplicity. It can be used to explore future structures of perovskites before their synthesis and be applied in a similar way to other structures like MOFs and ZIFs.
