Photothermally powered conductive films for absorber-free solar thermoelectric harvesting

Department of Chemical and Biomolecular Engineering, Yonsei University, 50 Yonsei-ro, Seodaemun-gu, Seoul 03722, South Korea. E-mail: eunkim@yonsei.ac.kr

First published on 18th December 2018

Abstract

The photothermoelectric (PTE) effect in conductive polymers is significant because it combines high photothermal (PT) and thermoelectric (TE) effects. Using poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) (PEDOT) films with various conductivities and crystallinities, the PTE effect in thin films can be established. The PT temperature increase (TPT) under light exposure and the thermal voltage induced by TPT for a series of PEDOT films indicated an unparalleled sensitivity to the carrier mobility in PT and TE conversions. The PTE Seebeck coefficients (SPTE) and power factors (PFPTE) for the PEDOT films correlated well with those in the dark state, indicating that the PTE effect in PEDOT can be translated into the TE effect induced by heating upon light exposure. The 180 nm-thick pristine PEDOT film shows high PFPTE values of 620 and 590 W m1 K2 for a near-infrared laser and sunlight, respectively. The thin-film solar TE harvester with four PEDOT legs generates a power density of 38 mW m2 and an energy density of 420 W h g1, demonstrating the first and highest absorber-free organic solar TE output yet reported. Thus, the PT conversion mechanisms in CP films can be extended to thin-film solar energy harvesters.

Introduction

When semiconductors absorb electromagnetic radiation, they generate hot electrons that produce temperature gradients.1o Such photothermal (PT) effects have shown great potential in applications including photoacoustic spectroscopy, photodynamic therapy, photodetection, printing, and cell sheet engineering.210 Since PT materials generate temperature increases (TPT) upon light exposure at the interface between two materials having different thermoelectric (TE) powers, a photothermoelectric (PTE) response is measurable even at room temperature.11 In addition, most PT materials absorb near-infrared (NIR) light and can be used as absorbers for photovoltaic panels.12 However, the PT and PTE conversion mechanisms of conjugated polymer films are not yet completely understood and have been rarely used for energy harvesting applications.

PTE materials reported to date have been limited to graphene1315 and inorganic materials16,17 such as InAs, InP, and MoS2, which require precisely controlled deposition techniques for synthesis. Furthermore, the PTE effect has only been measured under light generated by an accurately focused laser beam, in order to maximize light power. Finally, PTE effects from these materials have mostly been examined under external voltages supplied under biased conditions because of their low PTE efficiencies.

Recently, conductive polymers (CPs) have shown high PT conversion efficiencies (PT, >40%) and large thermoelectric power factors (PFTE, >1270 W K2 m1),1820 indicating that they are strong candidate PTE materials for flexible and large-area devices. In addition, CPs have high absorption coefficients in the NIR region in the doped state and low thermal conductivities (0.37 W m1 K1), suggesting that CP films may show preferable carrier-gradient to lattice-gradient PTE effects. Thus, much higher PTE effects could be achieved with CPs because heat conduction in the lattice does not contribute significantly to temperature equilibrium. In this sense, CPs also have significant advantages over conventional PTE materials, where phonon-driven heat flow is a serious limitation.

Nevertheless, the reported PTE power factors (PFPTE) of CPs remain low, e.g., 354.7 W m1 K2 for hexyl poly(3,4-ethylenedioxyselenophene)s,21 mainly because of their low electrical conductivities and instability upon air exposure. However, poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene)s (PEDOTs) show a high PT and large PFTE,18,2224 and could therefore be explored as PTE film devices once the PT energy conversion mechanisms in PEDOT films are understood.

Herein, we report the PTE effects of PEDOT films, which benefit from high PT and solution processability. The compositions and doping levels of the PEDOT films can be tailored to obtain high temperature increases and PTE effects, which largely originate from the controlled structural order of polymers and their carrier mobilities. Taking advantage of the high achieved PTE effect, a PEDOT film was applied as a thin-film solar energy harvester, demonstrating the first example of a CP-based solar TE harvester under bias-free conditions. This research provides general guidelines regarding the PTE mechanism and the potential applicability of CPs for bias-free and absorber-free thin-film solar TE harvesters.

Experimental section

Materials

Iron(III) p-toluenesulfonic acid monohydrate, iron(III) p-toluenesulfonate hexahydrate, poly(ethylene glycol)-block-poly(propylene glycol)-block-poly(ethylene glycol) (PEPG, average molecular weight 2800), 3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene (EDOT), pyridine, ferrocene, anhydrous n-butanol, isopropyl alcohol, tetrabutylammonium perchlorate, and anhydrous propylene carbonate were purchased from Aldrich Chemicals. All other materials were used without further purification.

Preparation of the P4 films

P4 films were prepared using solution casting polymerization (SCP). PEPG (0.20 g) was added to the oxidative solution (1.0 g, 40 wt% of iron(III) p-toluenesulfonate in n-butanol) and stirred for 2 h. Pyridine (11.6 mg) was added to the solution and stirred for 2 h. Then, a homogeneous red solution was obtained by filtration using a hydrophilic syringe filter (0.45 m) and EDOT (37.8 mg) was added prior to use. The molar composition of the mixture was monomer:iron(III) p-toluenesulfonate:pyridine = 1:2.64:0.55. Large PET films or glass slides (2.5 3 to 5 7 cm2) were treated with O2 plasma for 3 min with a flow rate of 40.0 sccm to increase the uniformity of the polymer film. The monomeric solution was spin coated onto the substrates using various spin speeds (7002500 rpm) to control the thickness of the final polymer films and heated at 6080 C and 35% RH for 2 h as shown in Table S1 (ESI). Then, the polymer films were washed with ethanol to remove the catalyst, residual oxidant, and impurities. Finally, the polymer films were dried under N2 flow and annealed at 60 C for 5 min. The polymerization mechanism is shown in Scheme S1 (ESI).

Measurement of thermoelectric and photothermoelectric properties

The Seebeck voltage and the temperature gradient were measured using a homemade shielded setup and an Agilent 34410A Multimeter and Agilent 34970A, respectively. The parallel Au electrodes were thermally evaporated at a rate of 1 s1 on a polymer film with a size of 2 0.2 cm2 and a thickness of 100 nm at a pressure of 5 106 mbar using a stainless steel shadow mask.22 The doping level of the film was precisely controlled by cyclic voltammetry, and the cyclic voltammetry was stopped at the desired potential in the conductive electrode-free polymer film used as a self-electrode. In general, two Peltier devices were fixed on an aluminum heat sink using thermal paste (PK-3, Prolimatech), and a temperature gradient was generated between the two Peltier devices (4 mm gap) (Scheme S2, ESI). The temperature gradient was controlled using a Keithley 2400 Multimeter by changing the input current to the two Peltier devices simultaneously. The temperature gradient between the two Peltier devices was measured using T-type thermocouples, which were placed in contact with the two Au electrodes using a z-direction controllable stage. Eleven points for T and V were obtained 3 times by changing the source current for each sample and linearly plotted to calculate the Seebeck coefficient. All of the thermoelectric measurements were performed at room temperature. For the temperature dependent experiment, a probe station (Probe station, M5VCMF, MS Tech Inc.) was used with the same setting as above.

The photothermoelectrically generated temperature gradients, voltages, and currents were measured using a similar procedure to that for the thermoelectric properties. A polymer film on the PET film was laminated using a thermal coater. The temperature gradient was controlled by the intensity of the NIR laser, which irradiated one side of the polymer film from 2.33 to 0 W cm2 in the probe station at 30 mTorr or at atmospheric pressure (760 Torr). An LED was controlled by the current from the source meter and irradiated on one side of the films with a diameter of 3 mm. The colors of the LED light were ultraviolet, blue, green, and red and had maximum intensity peaks at 400, 460, 532, and 630 nm, respectively. Five points for T, V, and resistance were obtained 3 times by changing the source current for each sample and linearly plotted to calculate the photothermal voltage. All of the photothermoelectric measurements were performed at room temperature. A halogen lamp source was controlled using a fiber source measurement system and irradiated one side of the films with a diameter of 2 mm. The generated voltages or currents were measured using a Keithley 2636 System SourceMeter and TRX-50-1 triaxial cable (Trompeter electronics).

Characterization

Electrochemical studies were performed using a CHI624B (CH Instruments, Inc.) potentiostat with a PEDOT film as the working electrode (self-electrode), a platinum plate as the counter electrode, and a Ag/AgCl wire as the reference electrode; 0.1 M TBAPC/PC was the supporting electrolyte. The E1/2 = (Epa + Epc)/2 of the Fc/Fc+ couple was 0.38 V against Ag/AgCl. The resistance (R) of the conductive polymer films was measured using a potentiostat and a four-point probe (Macor probe, 1.00 mm probe spacing, 200 m tip radius, 100 g loads, Jandel, UK). The thickness (t) of the conductive polymer films was measured using an Alpha step profilometer (Tencor Instruments, Alpha-step IQ) with an accuracy of 1 nm. The electrical conductivity () was calculated using the equation = ln2/Rt. The charge carrier concentration, carrier mobility, and Hall coefficient were measured using an HMS-5300 Hall measurement system (Ecopia) with a magnetic field of 0.53 T at room temperature. Polymer films on PET films were cut into areas of 1 1 cm2, and the edge was soldered with In 95% Sn 5%. The polymer films were peeled off and weighed using a microbalance (Sartorius CPA2P, resolution of 0.001 mg). The heat capacity of the conductive polymer was measured using a differential scanning calorimeter (DSC) 200 F3, Netzsch in a temperature range from 10 C to 50 C at a heating rate of 10 C min1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. Particle size measurements were carried by dynamic light scattering (DLS) using a Zetasizer nano ZS90 system (Malvern Instruments). DLS experiments showed that the solution of 40 wt% of Fe(Tos)3 in n-butanol containing PEPG forms micelles of 1520 nm size that remain stable for 3 days (Fig. S4, ESI). Upon the addition of the monomer, the solution underwent polymerization to yield strongly colored large particles (70300 nm) within 3 min, and then formed microscale particles within 1 h at 25 C. At 60 C, the polymerization occurred much faster than at 25 C, affording microparticles within 5 min. The samples were exposed to an NIR laser (1.6 W cm2) through an X-ray protective window at an angle of 45. High resolution transmission electron microscopy (HR-TEM) was performed on a JEOL JEM-F200 at 120 kV. Grazing-incidence wide-angle X-ray scattering (GIWAXS) spectra were collected at the 3C beamline in the Pohang Accelerator Laboratory (PAL) using a monochromatized 10.25 eV ( = 0.121 nm) X-ray irradiation source with a two-dimensional charge-coupled device detector (Mar165 CCD). The scattering vector (q) was calculated from the equation: q = 4sin()/.

The absorbance of the film is well matched to the thickness of the PEDOT film (Fig. S1c and d, ESI), following the BeerLambert law with slopes (absorption coefficients) of 42000 and 52000 cm1 and high correlation constants R2 = 0.995 and 0.992 for A808 and A1064, respectively. As the absorption in the NIR region is strongly related to the PT temperature increase, the PT effect is conveniently tuned by manipulating the solution composition, electrochemical doping, and film thickness.

Subsequently, to gain fundamental insight into the ordering of the polymer chains in the PEDOT films, the degree of crystallinity (C) of the polymers was determined by grazing-incidence wide-angle X-ray scattering (GIWAXS) measured at beamline 3C of the Pohang Acceleration Laboratory, as well as by X-ray diffraction (XRD) (Fig. 1c, d, S2, and S3). The GIWAXS spectrum for P3 shows a high C (37 2%) in the z-direction, in which the PEDOT chains are more likely stacked as a sheet (Fig. 1d, inset).19,27,28 The polymer sheets are oriented parallel to the substrate surface (a axis), which may favor carrier transport through the in-plane direction. The interchain edge-to-edge distances d100 are 13.9 and 12.3 for P3 and P2, respectively, as determined from the sharp (100) peaks at the scattering vector along the z-direction of qz at 0.45 and 0.51 1, respectively (Table S1, ESI). These d100 values are similar to the reported value (14.0 ).19 For in-plane directional diffraction, both the x- and y-directions show similar scattering patterns of 1.80 1 (d010 = 3.5 ) for P4 (Fig. S3, ESI), implying that the ordering in the b and c axis directions may be averaged out in the bulk. Importantly, the C values determined from the (100) peaks for the PEDOT films correlate well with the of the films (Fig. 1b, black diamond) and A1064 (blue dot), demonstrating that the polymerization solution composition and doping control the structural order of the bipolaronic states, which directly affect the film conductivity.

The differences in crystallinity for different PEDOTs can be attributed to the formation of PEPG-induced micellar structures that generate high crystallinity in PEDOT films.27,29 Indeed, dynamic light scattering (DLS) experiments showed that the formation of micellar structures containing an oxidant could favor the ordering of PEDOT units (Fig. S4, ESI), because polymerization occurs near the oxidant molecules that are captured at hydrophilic PEG shells (see ESI). Thus, the P10P11 films showed weak (100) peaks and the lowest C values, mainly because no PEPG was used in their synthesis.27,30 P7P9 films showed smaller (100) peaks with a lower C than those in the P1P6 films, because the absence of PY facilitates polymerization by scavenging protons. Schematics of the mechanism for the polymerization process are shown in Fig. S4 and Scheme S1 (ESI).

PTE effect of PEDOT under bias-free conditions

A thin film-type harvester was prepared with PEDOT films on a flexible PET film with no top electrode (Fig. 3a). Bottom Au electrodes of 100 nm-thickness were deposited to collect the harvested PTE output. The gap between the two Au electrodes (d) was fixed at 5 mm, at which thermal diffusion after PT heating is minimized (Fig. S10, ESI). The PEDOT films were coated on the PET film with the Au electrodes. Half of the film was covered by Al foil to avoid light exposure (dark side) and thus to achieve a temperature gradient between the electrodes under light irradiation. The dark side of the device was connected to a heat sink kept at room temperature (LTE1).

Because of its high PT, the PTE output of the P9 film was examined first. Upon exposure to a NIR source (808 nm, 60 mW), the P9 device generated a TPT of 120 K and a large PTE voltage (VPTE) of 9.5 mV (Fig. 3b). As a reference, a 100 nm-thick Au film was exposed to the same laser under bias-free conditions (Fig. S11a, ESI) and showed undetectable VPTE generation. The VPTE from P9 was obtained reproducibly during repeated cycling of irradiation. The P9 film showed higher TPT and thus higher VPTE than the other films, as compared in Fig. 3c. However, the PTE Seebeck coefficient (SPTE) and power factor (PFPTE) of the P9 device were lower than those of devices using P3 and P4, because of the poor of P9. Meanwhile, the P3- and P4-based devices showed the highest PFPTE and SPTE, respectively (Fig. 3d, Table 1).

Interestingly, the PTE effects (SPTE and PFPTE) for most of the PEDOT films correlated well with those in the dark state (STE and PFTE) with a 1:1 match (black dashed line) as shown in Fig. 3d. The SPTE of P2 and P5 was 1520% lower than STE. With these differences, the PFPTE of P2 and P5 was higher than the PFTE. This difference between the TE and PTE properties of PEDOT films may arise from several factors, including the difference in T of 5 and 86127 K, respectively. Nonetheless, the obvious matching of the TE and PTE properties of the PEDOT films suggests that the PTE effect can be translated into a TE effect, induced by the PT heating of PEDOT that generates TPT as a function of A and PT (eqn (1)).

Therefore, the mechanism for the PTE effect of PEDOT could be described as four physical processes (Fig. 3e), similar to that of the semiconductor.4446 (i) Photoexcitation of CP films causes rapid (<1 ps) nonequilibrium heating that generates hot carriers, (ii) which undergo vibrational relaxation to the lowest excited singlet state (S1). (iii) Then, the charge carriers (holes for PEDOTs) are relaxed to the ground state (S0) through non-radiative relaxation pathways, with evolution of heat at the light-exposed area. During this process, TPT is established between the illuminated and dark regions as the hot carriers are coupled to lattice phonons, followed by phononphonon coupling. (iv) Because of the TPT development, the carriers flow from the illuminated hot area to the dark room-temperature area, thus generating a VPTE by the TE effect. The direct photo-Seebeck effect is, in general, negligible for organic materials. Indeed, it was much smaller than the PTE effect, as shown in Fig. S11b (ESI).

The IPTE of the films was measured directly from the PTE device (LTE1), revealing that the IPTE of P4 was comparable to that of P3 and higher than those of P1 and P9 for NIR powers of <1 to 60 mW (Fig. 3f, Table S5, ESI). The power density from P4, structured as LTE1, was 0.13 mW m2, which is the highest value among the PEDOT films, possibly because of the large IPTE generation of P4. Because the SPTE of the PEDOT films correlated well with the dark STE values, which were increased by increasing the crystallinity of the film (Fig. 2d), the PPTE was increased by increasing C, as shown in Fig. S12 (ESI). Therefore, increasing the C of the PEDOT film, which can be detected by the increase in A1064, promotes both the conductivity and PTE power density.

Interestingly, the current generation of the P3 and P4 films in response to the NIR laser was very high compared to that of previously reported PT materials including graphene, MoS2, and poly[Cux(Cu-ett)] (Fig. 3f and Table S5, ESI). The slope in Fig. 3f indicates the NIR sensitivity under bias-free conditions for different samples; it shows that P4 affords the highest sensitivity among the reported NIR-sensitive materials to date, including single-layer13,48 and multilayer graphenes,11 pn junction graphenes,47 and inorganic materials.15,17,49

PTE films as solar heat harvesters

From the above results, P4 was explored as a thin-film solar TE harvester. The solar PTE device (STE1-4, Fig. S13, ESI) was similar to the above NIR-light TE harvester (LTE1) but the electrode separation (d) was fixed as 11 mm (Fig. 4a) because of the diffusive nature of sunlight. P4 was coated on a PET film with Au electrodes; half of the sample was covered by Al foil to block sunlight and achieve TPT upon sunlight exposure (STE1). With one sun exposure (1 S, 100 mW m2), the P4 film shows a TPT of 6.9 K and a solar VPTE output (VSTE) of 0.54 mV at room temperature. The SPTE from sunlight exposure (SSTE) was 78 V K1, which was well matched to the SPTE from NIR exposure (79 V K1). The IPTE from the sunlight exposure (ISTE) was 20 A, affording a power density of 50 W m2 under 1 S conditions.

By exploiting the simple preparation process for P4, the solar TE output could be enhanced by increasing either the thickness or the surface area of the film. Other methods include increasing the sunlight input by using a Fresnel lens and integration of the STE1 devices into multiple legs. The VSTE of the STE1 device was more than doubled by increasing the P4 thickness from 180 to 540 nm under 1 S conditions, mainly because of the large increase of TPT. While SSTE was almost equal for the thin (180 nm) and thick (540 nm) P4 films, the ISTE of the thick film was more than doubled, yielding a power output of 5 that of the thin film (Table S6, ESI). Although the sunlight absorption of the P4 layer was limited when the P4 film was thicker than 540 nm and thus the VSTE increased little, ISTE and PPTE from sunlight exposure (PSTE) were increased further because the P4 film was thicker. In this way, a high power density of 480 W m2 was obtained from a 2.23 m-thick P4 film under 1 S conditions (Table S6 and Fig. S14, ESI).

Sunlight was concentrated and the input power was doubled (two-Sun condition, 2 S) by placing a Fresnel lens in the solar simulator (STE2). As the TPT of P4 was increased by approximately 5, both the VSTE and ISTE of the STE2 devices with different P4 thicknesses were increased by 45 under the 2 S condition, as summarized in Table S6 (ESI). Concurrently, the TPT reached 82 K and the power density was 10 mW m2 with the 2.23 m-thick P4 film (P4d2230) from the 2 S condition.

Because the thin-film PTE devices here use thin PEDOT films in which PEDOT is both a light absorber and the PTE layer, the PTE devices in this work can be described as absorber-free solar cells. They have very simple cell structures compared to device structures with absorbers (Fig. 4c). Thus, the PTE-based devices could be integrated as a module with multiple legs of PEDOT films. To demonstrate, four P4d2230 films are connected in series or parallel (Fig. 4b and d). For the series connection (STE3), the VSTE was increased to 26 mV, 4 that of the single leg under the 2 S condition. However, a 13% loss occurs in ISTE, possibly because of the connection resistance in the integrated device. In the parallel connection (STE4), the ISTE was increased to 1.1 mA, 4 that of the single leg under the 2 S condition. Interestingly, the voltage drop with the parallel connection was <1%, and the power density from the four-leg module with parallel connections reached 38 mW m2. This high power density from sunlight should confirm the PTE effect of CPs as a promising method for solar TE harvesting. The surface power density of STE4 corresponds to an energy density of 420 W h g1, which was the first and highest absorber-free organic solar TE output reported so far (Table S7, ESI).

This PTE device exhibits good storage stability (>10000 h with 2% variation) under ambient conditions (Fig. 4e). From these unique features of light energy conversion, the PTE device could be exploited as a thin-film energy-harvesting device and a film-type module for the solar spectrum. The device is environmentally friendly, uses low-cost materials, and is easily applicable as a thin film-type wearable power source or in wireless NIR sensor network devices.

Conclusions

PTE energy conversion under solar light at room temperature was demonstrated by exploiting the PT and TE properties of PEDOT films. To reach the highest PTE energy conversion, PEDOT films were optimized by controlling the polymer conductivity though varying the chemical composition and electrochemical doping. The PT for the PEDOT films varied from 20 to 73% for an 808 nm laser depending on and changing linearly with the carrier mobility of the PEDOT films. Among the PEDOT films, the highly ordered P4 film was very efficient in PTE power generation. The SPTE of the PEDOT films correlated well with the dark STE values, which were increased by increasing the C of the films. Importantly, the increasing C, which can be detected by the increase in A1064, promotes not only the conductivity but also the PTE power density. In addition, these PEDOT films showed the highest sensitivity of known PTE materials to NIR light under bias-free conditions. The PTE of PEDOT was extended further into solar energy harvesting, generating a high power density of 38 mW m2 under 2 S conditions and showing stability for more than 10000 h under ambient conditions. These results indicate the light sensing and future application potential of low-cost conductive polymer-based materials in thin film-type wearable solar power sources, among many other devices.
