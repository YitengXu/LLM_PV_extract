Abstract

Singlet fission (SF) is a photophysical downconversion pathway, in which a singlet excitation transforms into two triplet excited states. As such, it constitutes an exciton multiplication generation process, which is currently at the focal point for future integration into solar energy conversion devices. Beyond this, various other exciting applications were proposed, including quantum cryptography or organic light emitting diodes. Also, the mechanistic understanding evolved rapidly during the last year. Unfortunately, the number of suitable SF-chromophores is still limited. This is per se problematic, considering the wide range of envisaged applicability. With that in mind, we emphasize uncommon SF-scaffolds and outline requirements as well as strategies to expand the chromophore pool of SF-materials.

Introduction – motivation and applications

The construction of efficient light harvesting optoelectronic devices, which convert sunlight as one of the most promising renewable energy sources into utilizable electric energy, remains one of the grand challenges in materials science.1–4 A central issue for conventional semiconductors is that every photon exceeding the bandgap will be subject to rapid intra-band relaxation towards the edge of the conduction band.5 As such, the excess energy is lost in the form of dissipating heat. This aspect amongst others limits the maximum attainable thermodynamic power conversion efficiency for a photovoltaic device to around 33%, better known as the Shockley–Queisser limit.6 To push the performance of single junction solar cells beyond their thermodynamic efficiency limit, the strategy of implementing semiconducting materials, which are capable of multiple exciton generation, has attracted tremendous scientific interest.7–11 It is, in essence, one of the potential ways of engineering the next generation photovoltaic devices.12 Singlet fission (SF), the molecular analogue of the multiple exciton generation process, has received a lot of attention ever since the path-breaking report in 2006 by Hanna and Nozik in which the impact of carrier multiplication on the efficiency of solar cells was revisited.13,14 In the simplest description, the SF process may be denoted as the following two consecutive steps:15 First, an initially formed singlet excited state descends towards a correlated triplet pair 1(T1T1) intermediate. The latter state represents two triplets, which are coherently coupled with overall spin-zero angular momentum. In the second step, decorrelation into two separate and independent triplet states (T1 + T1) may be accomplished. As a result, two excitons are formed upon photoexcitation by only one photon.

In order to exceed the efficiency limit of a conventional photovoltaic device, a SF-material could be implemented as a charge carrier multiplier together with, for example, conventional organic semiconductors to reduce thermal loss effects.16 By means of this concept, it is conceivable to surpass the Shockley–Queisser limit and, hence, to achieve a theoretical thermodynamic efficiency of up to 42%.13,17,18 Accordingly, chemical stability, high absorptivity, long triplet lifetimes, good triplet as well as charge carrier transport properties and maximized triplet quantum yields near 200% constitute the general preconditions for optimal device performance.19,20 In general, quantitative 1(T1T1) pair dissociation into long-lived independent triplet monomers T1 + T1 is desired. Furthermore, to be suitable for light harvesting devices, sufficiently high triplet energies of the corresponding singlet fission chromophores are required for efficient charge carrier injection into a semiconducting material. In the case of silicon, the triplet energy should exceed an energy of 1.11 eV.21–23 Beyond the integration of SF-chromophores in photovoltaic devices, various other potential application areas were invoked during the last few years. While efficient triplet separation is crucial for implementation in solar energy conversion devices, the opposite is true for the recently discussed applications in quantum information technology.24–26 Kinetic stabilization of the 1(T1T1) quantum entangled state renders SF-molecules exciting candidates for quantum computing even at room temperature.27

In addition, organic SF-materials are utilizable as non-linear optical materials in, for example, ultrafast optical switching or data storage.28–30 It was outlined theoretically and, recently, experimentally confirmed that SF enhances third-order non-linear responses by several orders of magnitude.31–34 Thus, SF-induced non-linear response materials have the potential to replace conventional non-linear optical materials in the future. Moreover, SF might revolutionize the field of organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs).35–37 In analogy to photovoltaic devices, SF based OLEDs could break the efficiency barrier of 100%.38 Here, a formal maximum efficiency of 200% under electrical excitation could be achieved by combination of SF chromophores with thermally activated delayed fluorescence emitters and phosphorescence emitters. Consequently, near-infrared light emitting devices with high intensity could be constructed, which would be of use in photonics, in general, and in vivo imaging, in particular. Last, but not least, it was demonstrated that SF has tremendous potential for developing photo-detectors with hitherto unrivaled efficiency.39,40

Initially, the incentive for highly efficient solar cells reignited the interest in SF, which was considered, at the time, an exotic photophysical deactivation process. Such a specialized interest transcended, in the last decade, into a wealth of intriguing mechanistic insights. Despite the remarkable progress in the elucidation of the underlying mechanism, the pool of SF-chromophores is still quite limited and most SF-materials are built around the molecular platform of linear acenes, namely tetracene and pentacene.41–46 By virtue of the numerous and diverse areas of applications outlined above, it is of utmost importance to broaden the pool of SF-materials to also address the consequently largely varying molecular and photophysical requirements. Eventually, the experimental investigation of novel SF-chromophores will assist in gathering new mechanistic insights into SF, which is still not completely understood.

Indeed, from what we know today, it is fair to say that the interconversion in SF is highly complex. A full mechanistic description of SF by means of elucidating every facet in detail is, therefore, far beyond the scope of this review. Instead, it is mostly dedicated to recent trends and “unconventional” SF-materials. We will, therefore, start with a brief overview of the mechanistic background in Section 1 to provide a general understanding of the discussed photophysical processes. We will then elaborate in Section 2 on computational strategies for the identification of “novel” SF-materials and round it off with Section 3, which focuses on “unconventional”, emerging SF-materials and molecules.

1. Mechanistic overview

We consider SF as four consecutive steps:
 
S0 + hν ⇌ S1(1)
 
S1 ⇌ 1(T1T1)(2)
 
1(T1T1) ⇌ 2S+1M(T1T1)(3)
 
2S+1M(T1T1) ⇌ T1 + T1(4)
In the simplest case, SF is operative between two interacting equivalent chromophores, i.e. homofission. Light–matter interaction ensures photon absorption by the ground (S0) state and affords the first singlet excited (S1) state (eqn (1)). According to Kasha's rule, the excitation into higher excited singlet (Sn) states leads usually to the rapid internal conversion towards S1.47 However, in rare cases, SF may also proceed from Sn rather than S1.48 Once formed, the interconversion from S1 to the spin-entangled, correlated triplet pair 1(T1T1) state occurs (eqn (2)).49–55 By virtue of its singlet nature, no spin–orbit coupling is required in the spin conserving 1(T1T1) population.56 As a result, 1(T1T1) is typically formed on ultrafast timescales, that is, from sub-picosecond to picosecond domains, with formal triplet quantum yields of up to 200%.54,57–59 The 1(T1T1) state stands in equilibrium with the corresponding states of higher multiplicity (eqn (3)), which ultimately may give two independent excited triplet states (eqn (4)).

This description is, however, a vast oversimplification of the complexity behind the second SF-step and neglects several important aspects. Discussions regarding intrinsic rate equation calculations based on non-adiabatic transitions serve as a good starting point.

1.2 Vibronic quantum coherent mechanism

In eqn (2), the oversimplified description neglects any potential “quantum coherence mechanism”.102–104 Photoexcitation creates a coherent quantum superposition of 1(T1T1) and S1. In some cases, additional coherent mixing to CT states occurs, as demonstrated by Mandal et al.105 Coherent quantum superposition between excited electronic states is enabled by strong electronic coupling typically mediated by CT states and/or substantial vibronic coupling. It is, however, incoherent when the population of the 1(T1T1) potential energy surface is either adiabatic or non-adiabatic. In such cases, a state transition is imagined as a step-wise “hopping” from S1 to 1(T1T1). In turn, it is much slower than the coherent 1(T1T1) formation. Unlike coherent pathways, it is promoted by thermal activation. The coherent mechanism was first invoked to explain the simultaneous rise of S1 and 1(T1T1) in the time-resolved two-photon photoemission (2PPE) spectra of tetracene and pentacene studied by Chan et al. (Fig. 3).50 This mechanism together with an entropic driving force provides a rationale for an ultrafast, barrierless SF in, for example, tetracene.107 At this point, the formation rates cannot anymore be modeled by conventional incoherent rate equation approaches. We like to emphasize the importance of quantum dynamical approaches, which contributed significantly to today's mechanistic understanding of SF. Not only were quantum master equation calculations successful in describing the ultrafast dynamics, but these approaches also provided fundamental mechanistic insights: the role of CT states, vibronic coupling, and the importance of energy resonance conditions.77,87,108–113 We focus herein on the identification of novel SF-materials and not on the underlying mechanism per se. In turn, we will not go further into details and refer the interested reader to recently published theoretical reviews, extensively discussing dynamic exciton theories and their application to SF.114,115

In early work, the coherent mechanism was postulated based on pure electronic couplings.50 The coherent mechanism was already postulated in early work based on pure electronic coupling. Albeit a matter of debate,81,96 it is now referred to as the vibronic quantum coherent mechanism. First experimental evidence for vibrational modes came from ultrafast impulsive vibrational pump–probe as well as pump–dump–probe experiments on triisopropylsilyl (TIPS) decorated pentacene performed by Musser et al.116 Their results revealed a vibrational coherence transfer from the potential energy surfaces of S1 to 1(T1T1) through a conical intersection.

Shortly afterwards, Bakulin et al. followed with an ultrafast two-dimensional electronic photon-echo spectroscopy study on pentacene and two derivatives.117 Here, high frequency vibrational modes meet the energy resonance condition and enable ultrafast SF in agreement with time-resolved 2PPE experiments. This happened despite rather weak electronic couplings. The vibronic quantum coherent mechanism was further corroborated by Monahan et al., who performed both transient absorption and time-resolved 2PPE experiments on hexacene single crystals, supported by quantum dynamical simulations.68 The authors came to the conclusion that both coherent and incoherent channels are active. On the one hand, the initial delocalized S1 transforms vibronically in a quantum superposition with 1(T1T1) with a time constant of less than 50 fs. On the other hand, in a parallel deactivation channel, the vibrationally relaxed S1 deactivates via an incoherent process to afford 1(T1T1) with a lifetime of around 180 fs. Co-existence of coherent and incoherent SF pathways was found in rubrene crystals by Miyata et al. by impulsive vibrational pump–probe spectroscopy and later confirmed by Breen et al. using two-dimensional electronic spectroscopy.118,119

In 2017, Stern et al. applied transient absorption spectroscopy together with time-resolved photoluminescence spectroscopy to study the deactivation of disordered and polycrystalline thin-films of TIPS-tetracene with time delays ranging from 10 fs to 100 μs.120 The quantum superposition does not evolve instantaneously after photoexcitation. Instead, it is driven by vibrational modes from the initial Franck–Condon position at the S1 potential energy surface towards the 1(T1T1) potential energy surface on the sub-300 fs timescale. Ultrafast 1(T1T1) population mediated by vibronic coupling is temperature independent, whereas 1(T1T1) dissociation was found thermally activated. In 2019, Schnedermann et al. constructed a molecular movie for ultrafast vibronically coherent SF.121 They used a directly connected orthogonal DP-Mes pentacene dimer and combined impulsive vibrational pump–probe spectroscopy and tree-tensor network state simulations.122 Such a powerful approach allowed disclosing the time-dependent interplay of both tuning and coupling vibrational modes.

Moreover, coherent and incoherent channels from a “real” CT state en-route towards 1(T1T1) may even be traced as was demonstrated by Alvertis et al. for an orthogonally positioned tetracene dimer through the change of solvent polarity by means of symmetry breaking interactions.123 It was demonstrated by both experiment and calculations that intramolecular motion around the dihedral angle Φ (low frequency vibrational motion of 39.6 cm−1) enables the mixing of CT and 1(T1T1) adiabatic potential energy surfaces in dependence of solvent polarity. In a solvent of low polarity and at room temperature, 1(T1T1) does not mix with CT states in the orthogonal position and, thus, remains pure. Incoherent SF is made possible by the large dihedral distortion. Here, crossing is avoided if enough excess energy is taken up. Intermediate-polarity solvents destabilize CT states relative to 1(T1T1). Consequently, mixing between both potential energy surfaces sets in. It is maximized by small inter-chromophore angles Φ. The latter allows for a vibronic coherent SF transition between CT and 1(T1T1) state. By further increasing the solvent polarity, the energy of the CT state rises above that of 1(T1T1). Now, mixing between both adiabatic potential energy surfaces is reduced to a minimum. Once again, SF proceeds incoherently. This solvent dependency was reflected in the experimentally observed SF-rate constants (Fig. 4).

1.3 Correlated pair states

So far, our discussion missed the nature of the correlated triplet pair state.124–128 Consider a dimer model composed of two equivalent chromophores A and B within the minimal 2 × 2 active space, that is, four electrons in four orbitals plus HOMO and LUMO are on each molecule. In the absence of any significant spin–orbit coupling, the entire adiabatic wave-function of the coupled pair system is given by the following product ansatz:
 
|ΨI〉 = A|ΦI〉|XI〉(22)
where |ϕI〉 is the spatial wave-function, |XI〉 the total spin function, and A the antisymmetrizing operator that permutes spatial and spin functions. The spatial wave-function term for the correlated pair state is a combination of diabatic electronic configurations including the primary configuration together with configurations representing CT states.129 Here, we will treat the correlated pair state wave-function purely diabatically as
 
|ψi〉 = A|ϕA(1,2)〉|ϕB(3,4)〉|Xi(1,2,3,4)〉(23)
with the corresponding primary electronic configuration, where each of the four electrons occupies one of the HOMOs and LUMOs, respectively.130 It should be noted that this assumption is only an approximate. As shown by Feng et al., the contribution of other diabatic configurations to the overall adiabatic wave-function of the correlated triplet pair state can be in the range of 10%.93 For a four electron system, 16 different products of single electron spin functions |α〉 and |β〉 corresponding to the 16 possible distributions |αααα〉, |αααβ〉, …, |ββββ〉 exist.131 The resulting 16 primitive electron spin functions are not eigenstates of the total spin angular momentum operator S2. By linear combination of the primitive electron spin functions, the total spin angular momentum matrix S2 is diagonalized. As a result, 16 pure spin eigenstates |Xi(1,2,3,4)〉 are obtained, which are combined with the spatial part of the wave-function |ϕA(1,2)〉|ϕB(3,4)〉. For the correlated pair states, two states with singlet multiplicity (S = 0, MS = 0), three times three with triplet multiplicity (S = 1, MS = 0, ±1) and one times five quintet states (S = 2, MS = 0, ±1, ±2) are overall obtained (Fig. 5).124,127 According to Scholes, the correlated pair state with singlet multiplicity, which is lowest in energy, corresponds to the correlated triplet pair 1(T1T1) state. The other correlated pair state, which is higher in energy, represents a correlated singlet pair 1(S1S1) state. It should be noted that this description neglects potential mixing due to configuration interactions or magnetic dipole–dipole couplings.132,1331(T1T1) is the key intermediate of SF, whereas 1(S1S1) was proposed by Casanova as an intermediate state in the bright fission process.134 Bright fission corresponds to the photophysical down-conversion of a single S2 into two S1 states. Notably, this intriguing down-conversion remains basically unexplored so far. The only experimental evidence was published by Klein and Voltz in 1975 when probing anthracene crystals.135 Ever since, no follow-up studies have been, to the best of our knowledge, carried out lacking any reproduction or contradiction of these results.

Two of the three triplet correlated pair states 3(T1T1) are of mixed triplet and singlet–triplet pair character, whereas the third correlated pair state with an overall triplet spin quantum number represents a correlated singlet–triplet pair state 3(T1S1). Lastly, the quintet state is described as the correlated triplet pair 5(T1T1) state and is degenerate with 1(T1T1).

In a more simplified description, the electron spin properties of the correlated triplet pair state are understood as the linear combination of the spin angular momenta of both isolated triplet entities, yielding overall one singlet, three triplet, and nine quintet spin eigenstates 2S+1(T1T1).15,136,137 Here, in the absence of any interaction, the antisymmetrizer permutes on electrons only within a single isolated chromophore and all energy levels of different spin multiplicities remain degenerate. Per se, the correlated triplet pair constitutes a coherent superposition of all spin sub-levels with entangled spin. The “nine spin state” approach derived by Merrifield in 1968 works excellent in cases of mixed spin states without significant inter-triplet interactions.136 This is found, for example, in the absence of substantial electronic coupling between the two triplets that evolved from SF and lost their electronic inter-triplet interaction (e.g. in the case of tetracene); the same is true for the microscopic reversal, namely triplet–triplet annihilation.133,138–142 In stark contrast, the “nine spin state” model fails when inter-triplet interactions are important, which is the case for strong spin dipolar coupling and/or substantial orbital overlap.132,133,143 Now, inter-triplet interactions cannot be neglected anymore and the “sixteen spin state” representation, here within a 2 × 2 active space, affords a much better description.126,127,144,145

In principle, two different correlated triplet pair states play a role: one, in which inter-triplet electronic coupling (orbital overlap) is present, and one, in which the correlated triplet pair is free of electronic inter-triplet interactions and the wave-functions do not overlap considerably any more. Consequently, Scholes proposed a notation, where 1(T1T1) is described as a triplet pair with electronic inter-triplet interaction as well as spin coherence. 1(T1⋯T1), on the other hand, has lost its inter-triplet electronic coupling, but still maintains its spin coherence.124 In this context, the correlated triplet pair with inter-triplet interaction 1(T1T1) may be formed via either a coherent or an incoherent pathway (Fig. 6).146 In the case of a coherent mechanism, dephasing of the quantum coherent superposition induces a population transfer towards 1(T1T1) within the SF-rate expression given in eqn (18).99 In a subsequent step, electronic inter-triplet interactions may incoherently be lost. Decisive is the coupling to electronic and/or vibrational degrees of freedom of the environment.

1.4 Spectroscopic properties of the correlated triplet pair states

Time-resolved 2PPE spectroscopy (Fig. 3) assisted in corroborating the existence of 1(T1T1) and 1(T1⋯T1) in pentacene single crystals.50,1021(T1T1) was confirmed by a high-energy feature, which relaxes by 0.11 eV with a time constant of 260 ± 50 fs to yield 1(T1⋯T1). Assignment of the conversion from 1(T1T1) to 1(T1⋯T1) was forwarded by Miyata et al. Independent evidence for the existence of two intermediate correlated triplet pair states came from by Pensack et al.147 They studied solid-state samples of pentacene derivatives by time-resolved transient absorption in the visible and near-infrared (near-IR) spectral regions. Here, the correlated triplet pair state 1(T1T1) revealed a featureless transient absorption plateau in the near-IR region of the electromagnetic spectrum. The latter was absent for the non-interacting 1(T1⋯T1) (Fig. 7). In short, distinct differences in the vibronic progression between both correlated triplet pair states suggest changes in the excitonic interactions. Subsequent time-resolved transient IR experiments revealed that the triplet vibrational dynamics coincide with the observed evolution of the correlated triplet pair state 1(T1T1) to 1(T1⋯T1) affording two distinct electronic intermediate states.148,149

In the weak coupling regime, the direct incoherent formation of a non-interacting or weakly interacting spin-entangled 1(T1⋯T1) is yet another possibility. Such an incoherent population of 1(T1⋯T1) was found for rubrene and hexacene crystals, where coherent as well incoherent mechanisms were the modus operandi.68,150

To this date, the loss of electronic coupling remains not well explored, being experimentally difficult to study and hence not well understood.41,151–154 Accordingly, it is often impossible to distinguish the spectroscopic signatures of 1(T1T1) and 1(T1⋯T1) (as well as independent triplets). In most cases, it remains unclear if inter-triplet interactions are preserved during the transitions or eliminated. In the following, we will not differentiate between these two intermediate correlated triplet pair states. Instead, we denote the correlated triplet pair as 1(T1T1), independent of the electronic inter-triplet interactions.

Likewise, it is often challenging to differentiate the transient absorption characteristics of 1(T1T1) from those of a pair of independent triplets (T1 + T1). Sometimes, distinct differences from that of free triplets, which are generated by, for example, triplet–triplet sensitization experiments, are discernible.155–157 Crucial is again, in this context, the degree of inter-triplet couplings and configuration interaction contributions. Accordingly, 1(T1T1) absorption maxima may be considerably shifted in reference to those of T1 or may exhibit additional markers in the near-IR spectral region.44,158–160 A prominent example is end-connected pentacene dimers with varying spacer length, for which different transient absorption characteristics were demonstrated by Trinh et al.155 The spectrum of the directly connected dimer was unambiguously different from the sensitized triplet spectrum. In contrast, the 1(T1T1) transient absorption spectrum of the dimer linked by two phenyl groups revealed almost identical transient absorption properties. A rationale is based on weaker inter-triplet interactions in 1(T1T1) as the inter-pentacene distance increases. Of great value is also the examination of the mid-IR regime. It assists in elucidating the excited state dynamics of 1(T1T1) by means of tracking specific vibrational frequencies and their underlying shifts.148,161 In the case of weakly interacting 1(T1⋯T1), these transient absorption features are absent and, thus, indifferent from independent triplet states.

Beyond that, 1(T1T1) is an electronic dark state with zero oscillator strength.49 It is inaccessible by direct one-photon excitation as well as indirect energy transfer and is typically non-emissive. Nevertheless, it may acquire a weak oscillator strength via the Herzberg–Teller mechanism and, thus, exhibit fluorescence.120,157,163 In such cases, vibronic coupling to the optically bright S1 induces symmetry breaking and the radiative recovery of the electronic ground state is activated.164 The same mechanism is of course viable under opposite conditions and leads to a weakly allowed ground state transition to 1(T1T1) as demonstrated by two-dimensional electronic spectroscopy.117 The course of 1(T1T1) formation and decay is then tractable in time-resolved photoluminescence experiments. Fluorescence due to excimer formation was unambiguously excluded due to the well-resolved vibronic progression of the 1(T1T1) emission. Emission maxima were always found to be slightly smaller in energy than 2 × E(T1). In other words, 1(T1T1) is bound with respect to the independent triplets T1.

An indirect way to corroborate SF and to probe the 1(T1T1) dynamics is based on delayed S1 fluorescence (biexponential decay).133,162,165,166 In particular, triplet–triplet annihilation must afford the bright S1 state, if SF is endothermic or isoenergetic. On a similar note, photoluminescence quantum beating due to delayed S1 emission is notable in, for example, tetracene single crystals (see Fig. 8a).54,55,88,144,167 These oscillations in the time-delayed fluorescence signal stem from the relative quantum phase acquisition, leading to constructive and destructive interference during the triplet fusion process.138 The obtained quantum beating frequency relates to the energy spacing between the spin sublevels and, further, indicates the coherent quantum superposition of the correlated triplet pair spin states (Fig. 8b). This observation provides clear evidence for the presence of the 1(T1T1) state generated upon singlet fission. Furthermore, this points at the admixture of the 1(T1T1) state in the presence of a small singlet–quintet energy gap (vide infra).

By considering the exchange coupling J, which is connected to the orbital overlap, 1(T1T1) as well as 3(T1T1) are stabilized with respect to 5(T1T1).127,133,168 Therefore, the three correlated triplet pair states, which are degenerate in the Merrifield “nine-spin state” model, are separated in energy by 2J and 4J, respectively (Fig. 9, top). If, in fact, the exchange couplings are substantial and, moreover, dominate over the spin dipole–dipole couplings, admixing is prevented. Under these conditions, the correlated triplet pair spin states remain pure.140,169 The exchange coupling term J is distance dependent and decays exponentially with increasing separation. In turn, the correlated triplet pair states approach once again degeneracy at larger and larger distances. Spin-pure 1(T1T1)s were corroborated by means of magnetic field dependent emission experiments.168,170–172 For example, the dependence of the delayed fluorescence intensity of 1,6-diphenyl-1,3,5-hexatriene crystals in a varying magnetic field was investigated (Fig. 9, bottom). Initially, the delayed fluorescence intensity decreased until the applied magnetic field reached the range of spin dipole–dipole interactions. Then, the intensity increased again until saturation was reached at around 1 T. Magnetic field effects at <1 T are explained within the standard Merrifield model for triplet–triplet annihilation.52,137,141,173 Going beyond 1 T, three distinct local minima in the fluorescence intensity are observable. They correspond to the three level crossings, where the exchange coupling gap is canceled due to Zeeman splitting of the correlated triplet pair spin states. Thus, mixing between the corresponding spin states is allowed and the fluorescence intensity is reduced. Overall, the probability of fluorescence relates directly to the singlet contribution of the overall wave-function. Time-resolved electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) studies suggested that the initially spin-pure 1(T1T1)s transform into strongly exchange-coupled 5(T1T1)s.174–177

Eqn (3) implies that 1(T1T1) might convert into various 2S+1(T1T1) states. Decisive is the spin dipole–dipole term of the spin dependent part of the interaction Hamiltonian. The role and emergence of pure 3(T1T1)/5(T1T1) in SF are under controversial debate.178–182 It remains unclear if 5(T1T1) is a crucial step towards decorrelation. Strong spin dipole–dipole interactions are generally recognized as SF-preconditions. But, it is poorly understood which structural parameters promote 5(T1T1) formation and which factors determine its lifetime.

The application of SF-materials in quantum information science was invoked by the potential formation of spin pure 5(T1T1).27,157 Several desirable features are obvious: First, SF-materials are tolerant to the “tyranny of low temperatures”; spin entanglement is preserved over a wide range of time- and temperature-scales. Second, entangled triplet pair states need to be pure spin states. By virtue of optical HOMO–LUMO gaps, which are much larger than the thermal energy kBT, thermal fluctuations will not mix any triplet pair states with the electronic ground states. In short, quantum data gate operations of the optical pumped spin polarized system seem feasible by magnetic resonance, as state selective interconversion between 1(T1T1) and 5(T1T1) occurs.183

Decreasing the distance between the SF-monomers causes minimization of the exchange couplings and admixture of the triplet pair states of higher spin multiplicities to 1(T1T1). The latter is rationalized by the spin dependent Breit-Pauli part of the interaction Hamiltonian. At the same time, mixing with other configurations is prevented. The probability for spontaneous spin-flips, which relates to spin–orbit coupling, leads to decoherence of the correlated triplet pair state. Interactions with the environment such as coupling to the phonon bath, nuclear spins, temperature, or energetic disorder promote the spin-flips. Eventually, the spontaneous spin transitions lead to independent triplets and spin coherence cancels as indicated by off-diagonal elements of each zero. As spin mixing proceeds, all diagonal elements of the density matrix approach 1/9. In turn, spin coherence disappears, and mixed states of independent triplet pairs are left behind.

The two triplets in 1(T1T1) were corroborated as bound relative to (T1 + T1) with the help of optical spectroscopy.46,157,163,174,190,191 Thus, the 1(T1T1) binding energy (EB) is given by92

 
EB = 2·E(T1) − E(1(T1T1))(26)
Considering that the energy gap between 1(T1T1) and 5(T1T1) relates directly to the triplet–triplet separation step (“inter-triplet binding energy”) and that 5(T1T1) is not further stabilized, the energy of 5(T1T1) is twice that of the monomer triplet excited state T1.114 Here, admixing of 1(T1T1) to 3(T1T1) is typically neglected, since mixing only occurs by breaking the particle exchange symmetry.171 An immediate consequence is that the inter-triplet binding energy can be approximated with
 
EB ≈ E(5(T1T1) − E(1(T1T1))(27)
In thin films of SF-materials, the triplets are likely to diffuse apart via triplet energy transfer between neighboring entities, i.e. triplet hopping.154,157,192 As demonstrated, the triplet hopping rate is modeled within the framework of the Marcus theory.154,193 This, in turn, decreases the electronic coupling of the triplet pair state and simultaneously decreases the 1(T1T1)–5(T1T1) energy gap. Thereby, the inter-triplet binding energy is reduced until, finally, full triplet decorrelation is realized, at which the binding energy approaches zero. As a matter of fact, triplet separation is observed as a rather slow and thermally activated process.120,154,157 It is important to emphasize that the spatial separation between triplets is not necessarily linked to spin decoherence. Triplet energy transfer does not change the total spin wave-function of the correlated triplet pair state.124 But, it affects the magnitude of the exchange coupling term J. Importantly, spin coherence is maintained during the spatial triplet pair separation. This stands in contrast to the spatial part of the correlated triplet pair wave-function, which changes as a function of separation. Accordingly, the rate determining step for triplet decorrelation is either the ability of triplet hopping or the binding energy of 1(T1T1) itself.194 Thermal activation will be governed by one or both, depending on which one is the more limiting factor for a certain molecular SF system. In summary, the triplet pair state is able to retain its spin coherence over a large spatial distance and triplet energy transfer reduces the binding energy of 1(T1T1), which is approximated by the energy gap between 1(T1T1) and 5(T1T1).46,174,176,191

It is rather challenging to investigate the decorrelation step from either a theoretical or an experimental point of view. Marcus and Barford published a pioneering theoretical study on a uniform Heisenberg spin chain to model decoherence of the correlated triplet pair state.195 For the dephasing interaction with the environment they used the Lindblad master equation, considering the effects of spin–orbit coupling. They defined spin decoherence in SF as the decay of both spin entanglement and coherence and connected these quantum effects to the magnetization of the system and total spin as experimental observables.

The above outlined SF-mechanism is just a scratch on the surface of what is known today about this intriguing photophysical deactivation. Several aspects were not even touched on, such as inter alia the central role of excimers or the impact of delocalization effects.81,196–200 For a more detailed discussion, the interested reader is referred to several excellent reviews on SF, in general,15,19,115,200,201 as well as the nature of the correlated triplet pair state or theoretical simulation of SF,101,114,146,194,202 in particular.

2. The search for singlet fission chromophores

Hand-in-hand with the elucidation of the mechanism that drives SF, the discovery of new SF-materials is a very active field of current research.115,203 The number of unconventional SF-materials remains, nevertheless, rather limited. But how can we identify novel SF active molecules? This chapter presents general design guidelines and strategies towards the discovery of new SF-building blocks. The search for SF-materials is largely driven by computational approaches, which are based on the so-called energy matching conditions.

Such energy matching conditions were established as the basic requirements for SF-chromophores by Michl and co-workers.15,201,204 In particular, the energy of S1 should be at least equal to twice the energy of T1 in order to be able to subsequently generate two triplets:

 
E(S1) ≥ 2·E(T1)(28)
This requirement is often referred to as the first energy level matching condition. It is necessary to enable the exothermic population of the triplet pair state, on the one hand, and to suppress the undesired re-fusion of two (T1)s back to a singlet (S1) by triplet–triplet annihilation.

Some important points should be made here. First, SF is also seen to proceed at slightly endothermic conditions, since entropic contributions are likely compensating the slightly positive enthalpy.59,102,205,206 Second, the two triplets in 1(T1T1) are found to be bound with respect to the free triplets in (T1 + T1) and, thus, stabilized and lower in energy (vide supra).120,157,163 Moreover, electronic coupling is likely to shift the energetic landscape considerably.45,201 For example, the energy of S1 of a dimer might be significantly lower in energy than anticipated by just considering the excited state energies of the monomers. The same is applicable for true vibronic coupling, as it modulates the energies of all excited states relevant to SF. Lastly, the energy resonance condition can be fulfilled also due to electronic band formation.

In order to prevent triplet–triplet recombination into the second triplet excited state (T2), the second energy level matching condition is important:

 
E(T2) > 2·E(T1)(29)
These two energy-matching conditions relate only to thermodynamic considerations and neglect of course kinetic considerations. For efficient deactivation via SF, any other photophysical process which evolves from (S1) needs to be evidently kinetically outcompeted. Most notable undesired deactivation pathways are arguably the radiative as well as non-radiative populations of (S0) or (T1) by, for example, intersystem crossing.

In addition to these thermodynamic and kinetic aspects, high charge carrier mobility, large extinction coefficients, and chemical-, photo- as well as thermal stability constitute important factors for device engineering amongst others.19 Going beyond these considerations, cost-effective and non-toxic materials are desired.

The energy matching conditions may be evaluated by directly computing the energies of the relevant states (Fig. 10). Per se, this remains the most common strategy, despite the considerable computational demand. Consequently, other approximate methodologies have been developed, which allow one to estimate the energy matching conditions.207–210 Arguably the most important is the calculation of the ground state diradical character as diradical and tetraradical indices proved to be very helpful.211,212 A high diradical character indicates low-lying singlet- and triplet excited states.213 Also, the aromaticity of the ground- and excited states is helpful.214 An aromatic molecule with a high diradical index will feature a comparably low degree of aromaticity in the ground state and as well an energetically low-lying triplet excited state. Calculations of the energies of the frontier orbitals are another approximate for the vertical transitions into the low-energy excited states.215 Surprisingly, this straight-forward approach has not yet found widespread application.

In terms of computational methodology (Fig. 11), density-functional theory (DFT) allows one to predict the energies of the S0 and T1 states, whereas time-dependent DFT (TD-DFT) may be used to estimate the vertical energies of the excited singlet and triplet states. DFT methods are comparably fast, especially if combined with geometry optimization at the semi-empirical or molecular mechanics level and if only looking at the energies of monomers. It is very important to note that energies of diradicaloid states as obtained from unrestricted calculations are unreliable and heavily depend on the degree of exact exchange admixture.216 A routine tool, which at times improves the agreement with experiments, is spin-decontamination procedures.217–220 That said, TD-DFT calculations using local hybrid functionals may afford also an accuracy of 0.2 eV for the calculation of excited state energies and also mixed-reference spin–flip time-dependent density functional theory gives reliable results.221,222 Spin–flip approaches have also proven to be useful to obtain fairly low-cost, yet accurate ground-state and excitation energies with wave-function based methods.223,224 Conversely, computationally more demanding multi-configurational methods are arguably better to treat the strongly correlated systems in SF. This is particularly true for doubly excited states and dimers, which are not well treated at the classic DFT level. 2.2 Diradical character

Nakano and co-workers pointed out that the above discussed thermodynamic requirements (eqn (5) and (6)) correlate with the multiple diradical characters y0 and y1.211,212 Here, y0 and y1 correspond to the occupation numbers of the lowest unoccupied natural orbital (LUNO) and the second lowest unoccupied natural orbital (LUNO+1), respectively.235–238 These indices are directly connected to the effective bond order as well as aromaticity of the electronic ground state.239,240 They describe the degree of electron correlation within a molecular system. In the case of a pure closed-shell system, the diradical character y0 as well as the tetraradical character y1 is zero. The other limiting scenario constitutes open-shell systems. There, a pure diradical is indicated by y0 ≈ 1 and y1 ≈ 0, while for a pure tetraradical both indices are one. An imperfect diradical, namely a diradicaloid, stems from weak interactions of both unpaired electrons.241,242 For such intermediate open-shell systems, the diradical character y0 scales from zero to one.

Minami et al. used a minimum four hydrogen atom chain model with D∞h symmetry representing a “4 electrons in 4 orbitals” case to tune the nature of the electronic ground state by varying the interatomic distances as shown in Fig. 15a.211 As a consequence, all scenarios, that is starting from a pure closed-shell up to a pure open-shell (diradical as well as tetraradical), were established. With this at hand, the excitation energies were calculated and related to the two multiple diradical indices y0 and y1. Based on their computational results, the authors formulated important guidelines for the discovery of SF-materials. First, a slight diradical character is required for any material to undergo SF. A purely closed-shell system with y0 ≈ 0 will fail to satisfy the first energy matching condition of eqn (28). With increasing diradical index y0 a larger exothermicity of SF impacts the energy conversion efficiency due to the loss of excess energy in the form of heat. Notable is that for tetraradicaloids, where y0 ≈ y1, the first thermodynamic requirement is not fulfilled. In general, the tetraradical index should therefore be kept small. Further, a high tetraradical index also dissatisfies the second energy matching condition according to eqn (29). In short, in the simplest formulation, every singlet fission chromophore is a diradicaloid.

Besides the aforementioned, the multiple diradical indices provide information regarding the classification of SF-materials. Such classifications were proposed earlier by Michl and Smith by reviewing the electronic nature of (S1).15,201 Most SF-materials such as tetracene and pentacene fall into the category Class I. Here, the excited state configuration is understood as a classic Kasha-allowed HOMO → LUMO transition. In contrast, S1s of Class II are better described as a linear combination of HOMO → LUMO+1 and HOMO–1 → LUMO excitations. Accordingly, (S1) excitations are one-photon forbidden. Their population is based on, for example, internal conversion starting from higher singlet excited states. Although theoretical predictions for Class II exist, little is known about this class and the role of the corresponding excited state configurations in the SF-mechanism remains to be elucidated.253,254 Class III molecules resemble Class II, with the exception that (S1) is mainly characterized by a HOMO,HOMO → LUMO, LUMO doubly excited electronic configuration. Likewise, in this case, the direct one-photon excitation to afford the doubly excited state with Ag symmetry is typically forbidden. Access to this state is made possible by fast internal conversion from a bright π–π* Bu transition. Its existence was already invoked by calculations on trans-butadiene and applied to explain the unusual absorption and fluorescence of polyenes.255,256 In short, the 21Ag dark state is best described as a superposition of singly excited configurations as well as two triplets localized on each ethylene sub-unit. Accordingly, the underlying two-electron excited state can be interpreted as a 1(T1T1) and SF may proceed intramolecularly within a single chromophore. Here, due to the spatial proximity within the conjugated π-system, 1(T1T1) is strongly bound with respect to each other. Examples of known, experimentally investigated SF-materials of Class III are extended polyenes such as carotenoids and conjugated polymers.257–260

We would like to point out that the multiple diradical indices allow for differentiating between Class I, Class II, and Class III chromophores. Increasing y1 favors the stabilization of the 21Ag dark state until it finally becomes the lowest singlet excited state. Additionally, the introduction of a tetraradicaloid character may transform a congener of Class I to Class III and/or even to Class II.

The experimental determination of the T1 energy level is tedious. Likewise, the experimental determination of y0 for a molecule is far from trivial,261 which hence explains why the search for new singlet fission chromophores is largely dominated by quantum chemical calculations.114,202 In this context, diradical stabilization inspired numerous studies, where small SF-materials are at the forefront by virtue of reasonable computational costs. In addition, small molecules ensure in principle high exciton densities in optoelectronic devices. Eventually, they could be integrated as core structures in SF-monomers and, in this way, further thermodynamically and kinetically stabilized. By computing the diradical indices for several rylenes, Minami et al. discovered terrylenes and quaterrylenes as potential SF-platforms (see Fig. 15b).262,263 Rylene derivatives undergo SF, a fact that was proven in subsequent experimental studies.89,178,264–269 Today, the family of rylenes is arguably the most well-established representative for SF-materials next to acenes.

Beyond that, Nakano and co-workers revisited isobenzofulvene (2).212 Moreover, they identified in further studies non-Kekulè heteroacenes (8) as core structures for SF (Fig. 16).254 As demonstrated recently by Li et al., higher non-Kekulè heteroacenes of this type incorporating four nitrogen atoms (9) could act as potential SF switches upon hydrogen migration.270 Typically, closed-shell structures serve as starting points for the search of SF-diradicaloids. Another strategy is the use of two interacting radicals as shown for dimers of the phenalenyl radical (10).271 These so-called pancake-bonded systems (the minimal SF-system constitutes phenalenyl tetramers) are predicted to have high SF-rates as well as high charge carrier mobility, which is an often-neglected material property important for later construction of SF-based devices. Accordingly, these molecular systems could tackle the trade-off between SF-efficiency and charge carrier mobility, usually observed for conventional SF-molecules. In another study, it was showcased that the strong open-shell character of bisanthene (11) can be weakened by twisting the planarity or by attaching aromatic rings and, in turn, SF is isoergic rather than strongly exothermic.272 The same approach was used in an experimental work on perylene diimide model systems.273 Additional computational works with a focus on diradical indices were performed by Sandoval-Salinas et al. on spiroconjugated dimers (12) and López-Carballeira et al., where 10 SF-candidates, including 13, with intermediate y0 values were identified out of 206 quinone and methylene derivatives.252,274 An interesting strategy for the stabilization of open-shell centers is the captodative effect which is the appropriate balance between electron-withdrawing and electron-donating groups.253,275 Here, 14 and 15 are lead examples. This successful concept was later extended to azaborines (16).276–278

2.3 Aromaticity

An increase in y0 in conjugated molecules is typically associated with a decrease of aromatic stabilization and vice versa.207,210 Hence, tuning the ground state aromaticity is yet another strategy in the search for novel SF-materials. Aromaticity of molecular building blocks is conveniently assessed by computational methods. Most popular (beyond structural considerations such as alternating bond lengths) is the calculation of the nucleus-independent chemical shift (NICS).279–282 It refers to the NMR-shift of a dummy atom positioned in the π-system of a planar, conjugated ring, thereby assessing the magnetic shielding due to the aromatic ring current. Alternatively, aromaticity may be estimated by topological wave-function analysis. Prominent examples include the analysis of the electron density by the Quantum Theory in Molecules (QTAIM),283 the Electron Localization Function (ELF),284–287 as well as the Anisotropy of Current Induced Density (ACID) methods.288,289

Anti-aromatic systems such as antiaromatic condensed-ring polycyclic hydrocarbons with 4n π-electrons serve as starting points.290 Amongst the investigated antiaromatic systems, 17 is one that satisfies the two energy level matching conditions given by eqn (5) and (6) (Fig. 17).

Fallon and co-workers applied the concept of aromaticity to triplet excited states.291 Hückel's rule predicts stability of (S0) for cyclic systems with 4n + 2 π-electrons. The situation changes in the excited state, where cyclic systems with 4n π-electrons undergo stabilization.292–294 This is known as Baird's rule of excited state aromaticity. In their work, they tuned the (T1) aromaticity of Cibalackrot derivatives by substituting them with different chemical functionalities. It should be noted here that the indigoid structure still follows the idea of diradical stabilization. This goal is achieved upon planarization by ring fusion towards a strongly stabilized diradicaloid. With that strategy at hand, the authors demonstrated that the energy of (T1) is reduced as its aromaticity increases, while the energy of (S1) remains unaffected. They also identified out of 9960 theoretically constructed Cibalackrot-based structures 2616 SF-candidates. In subsequent work, El Bakouri et al. utilized both Hückel and Baird aromaticity rules.214

By plotting, for instance, the energies of (S1), (T1) and (T2) of a compound family substituted with different functionalities versus an aromatic or anti-aromatic index, shown in Fig. 18, they were able to determine an aromaticity threshold. First, it is only applicable if the (S1)s and (T1)s are described by singly HOMO → LUMO excitation configuration like in Class I.

2.4 Frontier molecular orbitals

Blaskovits et al. investigated the relation between the adiabatic and vertical energy landscape and the energy of the frontier molecular orbitals.215 This study was inspired by the search for an efficient computational screening protocol to identify electron donor-acceptor-type co-polymers suitable for intramolecular SF. The CT character was found to provide a cost-effective screening protocol. Out of a library of overall 81 copolymers, the authors identified four promising candidates, two of which had already been experimentally validated. Accordingly, it was predicted that this approach should also be successful in the high-throughput screening of much larger databases.

2.5 Electronic coupling and crystal packing

As discussed in Section 1, SF-rate constants depend on electronic couplings (eqn (5)). It is very important to consider in this regard that vibrational degrees of freedom (viz. vibronic coupling or non-adiabatic coupling) may have a dramatic influence on the energetics and the couplings. Electron–phonon coupling is very important in the solid state and affects the excited state electronic energies dynamically, resulting in fluctuations of the energy gaps between S1 and 1(T1T1) and higher intermediate states.303,304 Accordingly, vibronic coupling of course also causes dynamic fluctuations in electronic couplings in solution.305,306 An impressive demonstration is SF in rubrene crystals, where an antisymmetric symmetry breaking vibrational mode is responsible for a non-zero electronic coupling that enables SF via a vibronic quantum coherent mechanism.118 Incoherent SF may occur on longer time scales if the thermal activation is accompanied with such a symmetry breaking vibrational mode. For instance, Feng calculated the electronic structure of tetracene dimers with various distances and found that simple diabatization procedures are not applicable to understand the underlying electronic changes such as the charge transfer character.93 Vallett reported a DFT study on a series of bridged tetracene dimers and investigated the influence of the bridge on the inter-fragment electronic coupling.307 Thereby, the authors suggested understanding the Davydov splitting as a proxy for the interchromophore electronic coupling between the two tetracene subunits. Another important aspect is delocalization, resulting in electronic band formation.81 Consequently, a simple dimer model is only a crude approximation for a crystalline system.

3. Experimental studies on unconventional singlet fission chromophores

In recent years, the pool of potential SF-candidates has increased considerably. Still, acenes and acene derivatives are the most studied family of SF-materials. Linear acenes were tuned by several strategies to expand the structural and functional diversity. First, at least two linear acenes are linked to each other (homo- as well as hetero-fission chromophores) with different spacers to evaluate intramolecular SF.186,203,308–311 Second, heteroatom incorporation and electron-withdrawing addition are applied to, inter alia, change the excited state energetics and enhance their stability by lowering HOMO and LUMO levels.41,157,312,313 Besides linear acenes, also rylenes,187,265,314–318 diketopyrrolopyrroles,319–322 polyenes/carotenoids, and conjugated polymers have been well-established as SF-materials.257,323–328 In the following, we examine what is known beyond these established SF-families and explore experimental studies on “unconventional” SF-diradicaloids.

3.1 1,3-Diphenylisobenzofuran derivatives

SF was incidentally discovered in 1965 when investigating anthracene crystals.329 It took until 2010 for the first experimental proof of a previously theoretically proposed SF-candidate, that is, 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran as a Class I chromophore (19, Fig. 20).330,331 Inspecting the structure of 19, it is evident that it is derived from the diradical parent o-quinodimethane 3. It is stabilized by ring closure towards an oxygen-containing, five-membered heterocycle and two additional phenyl groups. 19 was found to be unstable in oxygen-saturated solutions.332 For 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran, the triplet formation stemmed from an isoergic SF due to the fast interconversion, the ground state bleaching recovery, and the sum of triplet and fluorescence quantum yields exceeding 100%.331 In the solid state at 77 K, a remarkable triplet quantum yield of formally 200 ± 30% was determined. In subsequent work, two different crystal morphologies, referred to as α and β, were identified for 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran.333,334 The α- and β-polymorphs differ strongly in terms of their SF-behavior. Efficient SF with a triplet quantum yield of around 140% was recorded for the α-polymorph. The more stable and compact β-polymorph showed only a moderate yield of ≈10%. It is the long-range order not the slip-stacked arrangement that differs in the two polymorphs. The authors concluded that SF is inhibited by a fast excimer formation and trapping in accordance with the fluorescence behavior seen for both polymorphs. In the α-modification the time constant of SF is in the range of 10 to 30 ps and independent of thermal activation. Singlet–singlet annihilation turned out to be the dominant loss channel. Still, trapping was found to be significant above 180 K. Also, SF from higher excited singlet states (Sn) is faster than from (S1). The authors concluded that even small variations in the π–π stacking interactions or changes in the long-range order influence the electronic coupling and, in turn, the SF-efficiency. A later study, where different sterically demanding alkyl groups were introduced in 20 to 22, further corroborated this conclusion.335 Bulky substituents systematically disturb the slip-stacked arrangement and lead to highly amorphous films. All of the aforementioned factors have of course a crucial impact on the triplet quantum yields. One methyl group at the phenyl ring evokes a triplet quantum yield of 75%, while the bulkier tert-butyl substituents reduced the triplet quantum yield to 55%. Following this trend, the addition of a second tert-butyl group at the opposing phenyl group decreased the triplet quantum yield further to 35%. Dron et al. inferred that with weaker electronic coupling, excimer formation as a competitive deactivation pathway becomes more and more favored. To test the impact of weak perturbations, three fluorinated derivatives of 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran (23–25), in which one, five, or ten fluorine atoms were placed onto the phenyl rings, were investigated.336 In a comparison of theory and experiment, the authors concluded that dimer based calculation models may not be sufficient to elucidate the effect of chromophore packing in the solid state. Instead, Buchanan et al. proposed higher oligomers as model systems to be applied. Besides side group engineered 1,3-diphenylisobenzofurans, three covalently coupled molecular dimers (26–28) were reported.184 All of them exhibited low triplet quantum yields, which was rationalized by weak electronic coupling. For the directly connected dimer 26, it was shown that the localized and delocalized (S1) excitation co-exists and the coexistence is heavily influenced by the solvent environment.337 Here, the interconversion to 1(T1T1) is supported by localized excitations, whereas singlet delocalization is detrimental. Ultimately, 19 as well as 29 with an aliphatic carboxylic anchor was successfully implemented into a dye-sensitized solar cell.338 As a semiconductor, mesoporous TiO2 coated with ZrO2 was used and for 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran, internal quantum efficiencies of around 70% together with power conversion efficiencies of 1.1% were demonstrated.

3.2 Tetracyanoquinodimethane

1,3-Diphenylisobenzofuran is a derivative of the o-quinodimethane diradical. Tetracyanoquinodimethane (TCNQ, 30) is formally a diradicaloid derived from the parent p-quinodimethane and constitutes another example for Class I chromophores (4) (Fig. 21). TCNQ is also a prominent electron acceptor, which has been integrated into charge transfer co-crystals. The first example of SF in charge transfer co-crystals was found in phenazine and fluorene TCNQ 1:1 co-crystals in 1991 by Frankenvich et al., followed by work by Gundel et al.339,340 Magnetic-field dependent fluorescence measurements as a function of temperature and excitation flux proved very useful in these studies. Shortly afterwards, deactivation via SF in the phenazine TCNQ 1:1 complex was further underscored by time-resolved EPR measurements focusing on the initial optical spin polarization.341 Two years later, Agostini et al. contributed work on substituting the earlier utilized electron donors with biphenyl and, in addition to conventional TCNQ, they turned to tetrafluorotetracyanoquinodimethane (F4-TCNQ, 31) as well.342 Again, they could unambiguously verify triplet population through thermally activated SF by zero-field optically detected magnetic resonance experiments. Recently, Ni and co-workers investigated binary co-crystals composed of perylene and TCNQ (3:1 stoichiometry) by time resolved photoluminescence and transient absorption spectroscopy.343 Upon excitation with high energy photons in higher excited states SN (4.96 eV), they observed a branching deactivation pathway between SF and internal conversion towards charge separation. Importantly, TCNQ remains passive here and SF occurs between two stacked perylene units. In contrast, for direct excitation with lower energies into CT states, no SF was detected. Instead, instant charge separation occurred, and ion pair recombination resulted in triplet population via spin–orbit CT enhanced intersystem crossing.

3.3 Quinoidal thiophenes

Structurally related with TCNQ is tetracyanoquinodimethane bithiophene (32), where a polyene backbone is embedded into the overall quinoidal thiophene (Fig. 22). Important is the presence of the electron-withdrawing, cyano-substituted termini, which should, on the one hand, assist in localization of the two triplet excitations and, on the other hand, add CT character to the optically bright state.344,345 Quinoidal oligothiophenes exhibit substantial diradicaloid character and high photostability, which drew attention to 32 as a potential SF-material (Fig. 22).346–348 Indeed, it was demonstrated by Varnavski et al. that, after photoexcitation, tetracyanoquinodimethane bithiophene deactivates ultrafast on the sub-picosecond timescale to interconvert the bright 11Bu to the 21Ag state through a conical intersection near the Franck–Condon region.349,350 This is in line with predictions by Di Motta et al., who suggested a low-lying doubly excited state for 32.351

Accordingly, 21Ag constitutes an intramolecularly formed, strongly electronically coupled 1(T1T1). According to the classification of Michl et al. discussed in Section 2, this family of SF-molecules belong to Class III type of SF-systems.15 The interconversion efficiency was quantified with a triplet quantum yield as high as 180%. Appreciable (T1) formation by, for example, intersystem crossing was excluded due to negligible spin–orbit coupling. The 57 μs lifetime for the doubly excited 21Ag state is remarkable. Internal conversion paths towards the electronic ground state are suppressed by the rigidity of the polyene backbone. Note that in conventional, flexible polyenes the decay proceeds within a few picoseconds via conical intersections located at nearby nuclear geometries.260,352 Limiting and associated with the rigidity of this system is the substantial energy barrier of 1.76 eV to reduce the 0.43 eV triplet binding energy. Although triplet separation could be in principle feasible, an enormous energy barrier (i.e. 1.76 eV) needs to be overcome in order to reduce the triplet binding energy (i.e. 0.43 eV) by geometrical distortion and, thus, to ensure triplet dissociation. Consequently, both (T1)s of the intramolecularly formed 1(T1T1) state remain bound with respect to each other. Kim et al. demonstrated in a subsequent study a remarkable electron transfer efficiency of 83–103% originating from the strongly coupled 1(T1T1) of tetracyanoquinodimethane bithiophene (32) to a two-electron accepting anthraquinone.353 The electron transfer mechanism is based predominantly on a two-electron pathway, which could open a new approach for efficient light harvesting in photovoltaic devices.

In 2016, Kawata et al. used three different quinoidal thiophenes (33–35) in bilayered, solar energy conversion devices containing electron accepting C60 or N,N′-1H,1H-perfluorobutyl dicyanoperylenecarboxydiimide.354 SF was unambiguously proven through the magnetic field dependency of the photocurrent. Unfortunately, the power conversion efficiency was only <1.1%. With alkylbithiophene substituted 9,9’-bifluorenylidene (36) and [6,6]-phenyl-C71-butyric acid methyl ester, the power conversion efficiency increased to 1.3%.355

3.4 Zethrenes

In 2017, Lukman and co-workers explored SF in seven different zethrenes, namely 37–43 (Fig. 23).163 Taking inspiration from diradicaloid stabilization, networks of fused six-membered ring-systems varying from six (hexazethrene) to eight (octazethrene) and, beyond that, phenalenofluorene (38) with an incorporated five-membered ring were probed. Variable side groups, namely phenylethynyl, triisopropylsilylethynyl, mesityl or fluorinated mesityl, were added to ensure chemical stability as well as to control the molecular stacking. Triisopropylsilylethynyl revealed to be the least sterically demanding, thus, allowing for a more compact packing arrangement in the solid state and, in turn, affording stronger electronic coupling. The biradical indices y0 of these compounds span a large range from y0 = 0.05 in 38 to 0.43 in 43, which leads to optical band gaps between 1.4 and 1.9 eV. In cases of strong open-shell character, such as in 41–43, a low-lying doubly excited state, close in energy to the one-photon allowed excitation, was theoretically predicted. The presence of this doubly excited state was then experimentally corroborated by weak absorption onsets in the red regions of the electromagnetic spectra. According to Negri et al., this is a common feature observed for these molecules with low-lying dark states.356 Time resolved spectroscopic measurements failed, however, to testify direct transitions into this forbidden doubly excited state. In consequence, the investigated zethrenes represent examples for Class I members. 37–47 are subject to temperature-independent spin-entangled 1(T1T1) formation. In contrast, triplet decorrelation requires temperature exceeding 50 K due to strong inter-triplet bindings, which needs to be overcome by sufficient activation energy. Nevertheless, the experimentally derived values deviated significantly from the calculated energies in opposition to what had been found for TIPS-pentacene or F2-TES ADT.157,357 Triplet hopping turned out to be the limiting factor for the successful triplet separation. Interestingly, a Herzberg–Teller mechanism allows the generally dark 1(T1T1) to decay radiatively via fluorescence. The two fluorescent species, which were found in time resolved fluorescence experiments, were assigned to (S1) and 1(T1T1). The latter fluoresces red-shifted relative to the former. Moreover, the 1(T1T1) fluorescence decay is overlaid by three distinct quantum beating frequencies. Considering that triplet–triplet annihilation is inoperative in the (S1) repopulation, the relative quantum phase acquisition of 1(T1T1) modulates the singlet character of the triplet pair periodically over time. Spectroscopic analyses prompted to 1(T1T1)s that are bound with respect to free (T1) as well as (S1). As such, even endothermic SF is enabled in 37, 38, and 41. Furthermore, the emissive 1(T1T1) state together with the observed phosphorescence (Fig. 24), determined in sensitization experiments with platinum octaethylporphyrin, allowed for the calculation of the inter-triplet binding energy EB = 2E(T1) − E(1(T1T1)). 3.5 Baird/anti aromaticity and indigo based singlet fission materials

In 2017, a SF-material based on an antiaromatic core structure was experimentally investigated by Wu et al.359 Following the strategy of Ito et al.,290 a dibenzopentalene center with 4n π-electrons was utilized with attached styryl substituents to provide an extension of the π-conjugated system (Fig. 25, 44). Although Ito et al. predicted, in non-compliance with eqn (28), SF to be unfeasible for dibenzopentalene, effective side group engineering rendered SF feasible for 44.

In any case, the SF-generality for this SF-family, which belongs to Class III, was successfully confirmed within this study. Like for TCNQ bithiophenes, antiaromatic 44 decays in diluted solutions to an optical dark doubly excited (S1),349 which relates to a strong electronic coupled 1(T1T1) state, via internal conversion from the initially populated bright S2 state. A lifetime of 15.8 ± 0.6 ps was determined for the intramolecularly formed 1(T1T1). Geminate triplet–triplet recombination was corroborated to be one of the major deactivation paths. Turning to vapor-deposited polycrystalline thin-films, 14.8 ps- and 15.6 μs-lived 1(T1T1) and (T1) states, respectively, were detected for 44. The modus operandi is exciton diffusion of 1(T1T1) to disfavor triplet–triplet recombination and to favor triplet dissociation into long-lived, independent (T1)s. A triplet quantum yield of 142 ± 10% was achieved in thin films. A SF mechanism, which switched from intra- to intermolecular, was earlier invoked in a study by Trinh et al.360 Here, a series of diphenyl-dicyano-oligoenes were investigated in solution as well as thin films. Overall, the observations were in line with those made with antiaromatic 44. While in solution an intramolecular 1(T1T1) pair was populated, intermolecular interaction in the solid state paved the way for intermolecular triplet transfer yielding two long-lived (T1)s on adjacent singlet fission monomers. In 2019, Liu et al. extended the chromophore pool of SF active dibenzopentalene chromophores by three more derivatives, where dibenzopentalene was substituted by phenylethynyl groups (45, 46 and 47).361 Again, an intra- to intermolecular SF-mechanism was found for all three SF systems. The introduction of functional groups to the phenyl rings led to different intermolecular distances, variable dihedral angles as well as changes in the longitudinal and transversal displacements. Interestingly, differences in the intermolecular molecular arrangements did not influence the decorrelation of the intramolecularly formed correlated triplet pair state. In fact, a separation yield near unity was shown for all chromophores.

The exploitation of Baird's aromaticity rule has attracted considerable attention within the SF-community. In the work by Fallon et al., Cibalackrot indigoids with varying H-, F-, Cl-, Br- and CN-substituents (Fig. 26, 48–53) were characterized.291 The various substitution patterns allowed one to tune the absorption from 602 to 650 nm and to improve the photostability. For example, 80% of CN-substituted Cibalackrot (53) could be recovered after one month under ambient conditions. Although the concept of triplet stabilization comes at the expense of ground state aromaticity, the overall photostability of these SF-materials is remarkable. Overall, this corroborates the indubitable power of a design approach based on (T1) anti-aromaticity towards highly stable SF chromophores. To investigate the deactivation of these compounds, transient absorption and time resolved EPR experiments were performed. Their photophysical investigations point towards spectroscopic characteristics known for Class I chromophores. For 52 and 53, a spectral triplet feature was detected throughout the μs time regime. All other materials exhibited triplet population with lifetimes in the sub-μs regime, except for H-substituted Cibalackrot 48, which lacked any triplet formation at all. For thin films of 52 and 53, the spin polarization pattern demonstrated the presence of 1(T1T1) and the exclusion of intersystem crossing. This together with the 2 to 3 ps rise of the triplet excited state absorption substantiated SF in 49–53. Interestingly, the following trend of the relative triplet exciton yield was found: 48 (H-) < 49 (F-) < 50 (Cl-) ∼ 52 (CNBr-) < 51 (Br-) < 53 (CN-). At first glance, this appears counterintuitive as the triplet quantum yield increases with increasing SF-exothermicity.362 However, the trend seems to be due to the degree of intermolecular electronic coupling, which is in accordance with the absorption shifts of the thin films.

Shortly after this study, Ryerson et al. reported on parent Cibalackrot (54), igniting a controversial debate on SF within this family.363 Cibalackrot (54) is a long known industrial dye, which was already proposed in 2013 as a potential SF-material. The authors identified two crystalline polymorphs, designated as α and β, as well as an amorphous phase. This rendered, however, the photophysical characterization challenging. It was shown that SF based on a direct or super-exchange mechanism competes with a fast excimer and CT population. Hence, direct or super-exchange SF-mechanisms are unlikely or, if present, minor in triplet contribution. Accordingly, only a two-step SF-mechanism with a real CT intermediate seems reasonable as the main SF-channel. Because the triplet quantum yield was limited to only 60%, the ultimate SF-proof, that is a triplet quantum yield exceeding 100%, remains to be reported. Increasing the CT character led to higher triplet quantum yields, suggestive of an intermediate CT governing the triplet population. It is not entirely proven that triplet formation occurs via SF rather than intersystem crossing with or without a CT intermediate. It was outlined that the same spin polarization pattern, as seen in the time-resolved EPR spectrum by Fallon et al., may also stem from intersystem crossing between a CT and (T1).291 In addition to this work of Ryerson et al., different packing geometries were calculated. These shed light on whether SF could be enhanced or not. Indeed, they found promising stacking motifs, which could be achieved by suitable functionalization of Cibalackrot.

Recently, Wang et al. adopted the design strategy of (T1) stabilization using Baird's aromaticity and applied it to dipyrolonaphthyridinediones (55).364 Like for Cibalackrots, the adaptive aromaticity guarantees outstanding chemical stability. In a stability test under air and light exposure over 50 days, 91% of the initial absorbance was maintained for thin films of 55. With the help of steady state fluorescence and phosphorescence of 55, (S1) and (T1) energies of around 2.2 and 1.2 eV were determined.

Only in the solid state, a rapidly formed and long-lived triplet excited state absorption emerged and, in turn, corroborated the endothermic SF. The (T1) spectral features decayed multi-exponentially with lifetimes of 87 ± 3 ns and 1.566 ± 0.065 μs. More importantly, a triplet quantum yield of 173 ± 30% was determined by the singlet depletion method. This study strikingly underscores the power of the design concept utilizing both Hückel and Baird aromaticity to identify novel and chemically stable SF-materials beyond the Cibalackrot scaffold.

In 2020, Wang et al. examined the photophysics of isoindigos 56 and 57.364 Previously, the isoindigo scaffold was integrated as part of a SF-active co-polymer. Although isoindigos are subject to ultrafast non-radiative internal conversions, 56 and 57 revealed efficient intramolecular SF. Again, an intra- to intermolecular SF-mechanism was observed in thin films. In solution, no long-lived species associated with independent triplets was found and the dominant deactivation proceeded via triplet–triplet annihilation. In the solid state, long-lived triplet absorption was detected. Here, the intramolecularly formed 1(T1T1) is allowed to diffuse apart and to dissociate into free triplets. Fluence dependent measurements revealed non-geminate recombinations between free colliding T1s as a major deactivation path in thin films of 56 and 57. The quantum yield of independent triplets was calculated as 10% and 181% for 56 and 57, respectively. This large discrepancy regarding the triplet quantum yield between the non-functionalized and thiophene-decorated isoindigo was rationalized by different crystal packing structure, that is, face-to-face versus slip-stacked configuration, as well as the shorter 1(T1T1) lifetimes in 56.

In 2018, Hansmann et al. synthesized diradicaloids with exceptional strong open-shell character and high stability by utilization of terminal and super-bulky cyclic (alkyl)(amino)carbene (CAAC) end groups (Fig. 27).365,366

Changing the distance between the two open-shell centers by integrating different spacers led to varying diradical characters y0. Inspired by this work and further synthetic achievements in the area of carbene stabilized diradicaloids,367,368 Messelberger et al. theoretically investigated two series of carbene derived diradicaloids with a focus on the electronic ground state multiple diradical characters.250 As a result, they identified several new potential SF-molecules. Beyond that, for most structures a significant tetraradical character y1 was recognized, indicating potential Class III SF chromophores with an accessible low-lying doubly excited state. Following their calculations, bisCAAC diradicaloids with incorporated phenyl-(58), naphthalene-(59) and biphenyl-(60) molecular bridges and y0's ranging from 0.28 for 58 to 0.79 for 60 were investigated by Ullrich et al.369 At room temperature, transient absorption measurements revealed for 58–60 a transition from the initially populated S2 towards the optical dark state (S1). This is in sound agreement with studies on heteroacenes and hexacene, which postulated that the SF-efficiency decreases with increasing exoergicity.58,362 In contrast to cryogenic temperatures, no changes and consequently no SF were found at room temperature and at higher concentrations. Phenylene bridged diradicaloid (58) did not show any concentration dependency at all, even at low temperatures. The self-assembly of 59 as well as 60 at cryogenic temperatures is a likely rationale, whereas the sterically demanding carbene groups in 58 prohibit such π-stacking. Due to steric hindrance, the interaction via the spacers is limited to 59 and 60. A direct and exclusive excitation of 60 was possible with an excitation wavelength of 850 nm that matches the absorption of the self-assembled ground state complex. Again, the transient absorption spectrum altered dramatically (see Fig. 28E and F). Now, S2 decays, 1(T1T1) rises, and the near-IR fluorescence pattern was quenched. Moreover, a ground state recovery became visible. Inspection of the ground state bleaching revealed Davydov splitting and no shift of the minima in the visible spectral region, which is in contrast to the transient absorption spectrum obtained upon 670 nm photoexcitation. Here, a shift and subsequent broadening go hand in hand with the S2 decay. The only reasonable explanation is the simultaneous photoexcitation of interacting and non-interacting SF-materials. Interestingly, although theoretically possible for Class III, no evidence for an intra- to intermolecular SF-mechanism like in 58 was gathered even at high concentrations.359,360 Arguably, this might be due to the absence of S1 at cryogenic temperatures (Fig. 28A and B, vide supra). Accordingly, 1(T1T1) is initially formed between two monomers. On longer timescales, 1(T1T1) deactivates multi-exponentially. Here, the lifetimes are substantially longer for 59 than for 60, which is in line with the energy gap law of non-radiative decay. The adherence of the energy gap law for SF-materials had been demonstrated previously by Sanders et al. on a series of acene dimers.370 Last but not least, further stabilization of these structures could be achieved by the captodative effect via e.g. fluorination, as pointed out by Japahuge et al.221

3.7 Miscellaneous singlet fission chromophores

Beyond the above discussed experimental studies on non-traditional SF-materials, SF was also detected in earlier studies on thin films of benzophenone (61),371 tris-(8-hydroxyquinoline)aluminium (62),372 and p-hexaphenyl (63).373 All of them (Fig. 29) share that SF was initialized from higher excited singlet states (SN), while belonging to Class I SF materials. In 2018, Montero et al. studied the photophysics of a directly connected orthogonal boron dipyrromethene (BODIPY) dimer (64) by femto- and nanosecond transient absorption spectroscopy.374 A strong dependency on solvent polarity was found for 64, which led to the interpretation of a CT-mediated SF. Triplet formation was absent in non-polar cyclohexane. Quantum yields for 1(T1T1) were, however, up to 132% (140%) and 160% (199%) in more polar chloroform and acetonitrile, respectively.
Fig. 29 Experimentally investigated compounds, which do not belong to a particular SF-chromophore family discussed above.

Values well beyond 100% together with fast 1(T1T1) formation prompt to SF in this Class I molecular dimer. Interestingly, triplet quantum yields determined by singlet oxygen sensitization experiments revealed high T1 yields in chloroform and much lower ones in acetonitrile. Accordingly, branching between triplet decorrelation and ground state deactivation is solvent dependent. A subsequent time-resolved EPR study inferred that the spin–orbit CT intersystem crossing mechanism acts as a competitive triplet formation channel.375 The co-existence of triplet states on one of the subunits in 64 was confirmed by temperature dependent measurements. It was found that the triplet energy for the horizontal sub-unit is slightly higher than that for the vertical sub-unit. The spin polarization pattern excluded radical pair intersystem crossing as well as spin–orbit induced intersystem crossing. Spin–orbit CT intersystem crossing rather than SF was corroborated at temperatures as low as 20 K. Very recently, ab initio molecular dynamics studies with 64 questioned this conclusion.376 SF is controlled by the thermodynamic driving force and the kinetic barrier. Even if 64 formally fails to meet the energy level matching criterion, structural/vibrational fluctuations in combination with solvent interactions enable SF. In addition, the synergy of vibrational dynamics and solvent governs the CT energy as a means to remove the kinetic barrier and to meet the kinetic criterion ΔE(CT – S1) ≤ 0. Both criteria are only fulfilled in polar solvents. This is in sound agreement with studies on cyclohexane. It is important to note that not all structural changes are suitable for SF. It was found that only deformations along the connecting C–C bond and/or resonant structural changes within the B–N bond, which are assisted by dihedral twisting of the BODIPYs, govern SF.

In 2019, Bae et al. examined the photophysics of anthanthrone (65) and two anthanthrenes (66 and 67) as potential Class I SF systems.377 Side-group functionalization altered singlet and triplet energies as well as their photophysical deactivation behavior. 66 (E(S1) = 2.51 eV, E(T1) = 1.30 eV) and 67 (E(S1) = 2.46 eV, E(T1) = 1.23 eV) meet the first energy level matching conditions. 65 exhibits a far higher triplet energy (E(S1) = 2.25 eV, E(T1) = 2.01). Fluorescence quantum yields of 65 ± 5% and 55 ± 4% were found for 66 and 67, respectively. Contrarily, only a yield of 9 ± 2% was determined for 60. The opposite trend was found for the triplet quantum yields, measured in singlet oxygen emission measurements: 65 revealed the highest triplet quantum yields of 50 ± 8%, followed by 66 with 20 ± 4% and 67 with 14 ± 3%. The higher triplet and lower fluorescence quantum yields of 65 were attributed to a faster intersystem crossing. Responsible are the n–π* character of the S1 excitation and the smaller singlet–triplet energy gap. By time-resolved EPR experiments, the mechanism of triplet formation was elucidated. Spin polarization patterns revealed that T1 population proceeds via spin–orbit induced intersystem crossing. At 85 K and at high concentrations of 67, the triplet EPR spectrum featured significant contributions from a non-Boltzmann population of the ms = 0 spin sublevel. This is, however, indicative for SF. The best fit was obtained by a linear combination of SF intersystem crossing (47%) and spin–orbit induced intersystem crossing (53%). In contrast, at a high concentration of 66 the triplet EPR spectrum was fit exclusively using the spin–orbit induced intersystem crossing mechanism. The authors concluded that 66 may not form any small aggregates favoring SF under these conditions, while 67 can do. Eventually, we would like to highlight that singlet diradicaloid electronic structures are a well-established area of research in inorganic p-block chemistry (Fig. 30).378–387 In particular four- (68–72), but as well to some extent five-membered ring structures with very high diradicaloid character have received a lot of attention from the synthetic community. Nevertheless, none of these systems seems to have been linked with SF or even explored in this context according to our knowledge.

4. Conclusions

Until now, the research of SF is largely dominated by investigations with acenes, rylenes, diketopyrrolopyrroles and conjugated polyenes. Only few studies were dedicated to other SF-materials as outlined in Section 3. This is, however, surprising considering the numerous breakthroughs already achieved based on computational predictions and the large number of unexplored, computationally identified candidates (Section 2). We conclude that the experimental research of SF has only scratched the surface of a large field and much awaits to be unraveled.

Although mechanistic studies on “conventional” SF-platforms (i.e. acenes and their analogues) gave tangible insights into the generally encountered SF-dynamics, more experimental and computational effort will be necessary to unravel the concrete SF-mechanism as well as the interplay of participating electronic states for a particular SF-system. Here, the identification of novel SF chromophores is often just the first step towards a full understanding of the underlying electronic structure and SF mechanism.

Synthetic accessibility and experimental scrutiny of new classes of SF-materials constitute the major driving force for upcoming innovation in the field: first, an extension to hitherto unknown SF-materials will expand our basic understanding of the SF-mechanism. Second, so far unknown SF-materials will likely outperform existing ones in solar energy conversion. Third, SF is also relevant for other optoelectronic devices: quantum computing technology, photodetectors, OLEDs or applications associated with third order non-linear optical behavior. Any of the aforementioned applications are likely to require various families of SF-materials and the limited choice of SF-molecules constitutes a central bottleneck here. This status quo needs to be changed in our point of view.

Eventually, we would like to emphasize practical aspects in the synthesis of SF-materials. Can those SF-materials, which were theoretically proposed, also be synthesized? What modification does it take to make typically reactive diradicaloids air-stable to be incorporated into photovoltaic devices with a reasonable lifetime? Here, the presented dyes 48 to 57 exploiting Baird and Hückel aromaticity with their outstanding photostability and concurrently high T1 energy are definitively interesting starting points. Also, much progress has been made in recent decades to advance synthetic methodologies. Transient intermediates were, for example, isolated by kinetic stabilization with sterically encumbering substituents, the elegant exploitation of the captodative effect, or harnessing heteroatom effects. Here, the study of carbene-derived diradicaloids, which feature exceptional high diradical character, yet are entirely photo-stable under inert conditions, might give guidelines for future work. Crucial is, in this context, the interplay between steric protection, on the one hand, and sufficient intermolecular electronic couplings, on the other hand, to gain control over the deactivation via SF. Equally important is tuning of SF-materials by means of covalent dimerization, heteroatom substitution, or probing various packing structures by chemical decoration. In summary, SF is a dynamic and truly exciting area of research and the discovery of new SF-materials will be crucial for moving towards the various envisioned applications.
