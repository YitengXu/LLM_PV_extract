Abstract

Solar cells involving organic small molecules and polymers have attracted intense attention from chemists, physicists and materials scientists in the past decade. Efforts in materials synthesis and device processing have led to significant improvement of the power conversion efficiency, approaching 10%. In organic solar cells (OSCs), the morphology and the interface of the donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) heterojunctions play a critical role in determining the device efficiency. In this article, we highlight recent progress on both materials synthesis and self-assembly and lithography techniques toward ordered nanostructures and well-defined D/A interfaces that are expected to enhance the performance of OSCs.

1. Introduction

Organic solar cells (OSCs)1â€“14 based on semiconducting small molecules and polymers offer a promising option to meet the increasing demand of our society for clean, renewable and affordable energy sources. Compared to commercialized silicon solar cells, OSCs exhibit the advantages of solution processability to enable large-scale roll-to-roll fabrication, mechanical flexibility, potential low cost, and synthetic variety.

In OSCs, the active layer sandwiched between two electrodes normally consists of two distinct components: an electron donor (D) and an electron acceptor (A). The morphology of this active layer plays a critical role in determining the device efficiency. The concept of donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) bilayer planar heterojunction was first applied by Tang15 to OSCs, and a power-conversion efficiency (PCE) of 1% was achieved using copper phthalocyanine and a perylene tetracarboxylic derivative. The major limitation of a planar bilayer heterojunction, however, is the relatively small Dâ€“A interface area and the requirement of long charge-carrier lifetime to ensure that electrons and holes reach their respective electrodes.

An elegant approach to this problem lies in the discovery of interpenetrating Dâ€“A networks, termed bulk heterojunctions (BHJs).1,2 The active layer of most BHJs is composed of a conjugated polymer1,2,6,12 or small molecule9,10,16â€“22 as the electron donor, randomly blended with an electron acceptor1,2,23â€“29 such as fullerene derivatives. It is widely believed that, upon light irradiation, excitons mainly produced in the donor phase migrate to the Dâ€“A interface where the excitons are dissociated into charge carriers that then migrate in opposite directions to the electrodes30â€“34 (Fig. 1A). The PCE of polymerâ€“fullerene BHJ solar cells has been significantly improved in the past decade, thanks to the rapid advances in both materials synthesis, particularly the exploration of new low-bandgap polymers, and processing optimization for device fabrication.35â€“45 Very recently, Yang and co-workers reported a PCE of 8.62%,40 which has been further improved to 10.6%,11 in tandem polymerâ€“fullerene BHJ solar cells featuring a spectrally matched low-bandgap conjugated polymer, poly{2,6â€²-4,8-di(5-ethylhexylthienyl)benzo[1,2-b;3,4-b]dithiophene-alt-5-dibutyloctyl-3,6-bis(5-bromothiophen-2-yl)pyrrolo[3,4-c]pyrrole-1,4-dione} (PBDTT-DPP). Several challenges, nevertheless, still remain to be addressed in order to make OSCs competitive compared to their inorganic counterparts. For example, current device fabrication of BHJs via solution processing commonly results in irregular morphologies that strongly depend on the processing conditions and molecular structures.35,41,46â€“50 As a consequence, intensive optimization of device fabrication is needed for each newly developed material to maximize the potential for high power conversion efficiency.

This ideal phase separation is expected to provide transport â€œhighwaysâ€ for both p-type and n-type charge carriers to electrodes, without sacrificing the Dâ€“A interfacial contact.

In this article, we summarize recent progress on both â€œtop-downâ€ and â€œbottom-upâ€ approaches towards organized BHJs in order to improve the efficiency of OSCs. The first part includes the â€œbottom-upâ€ strategy of molecular design, synthesis and self-assembly of linked Dâ€“A molecules and block copolymers which serve as a single material for OSCs. The second part overviews recent top-down approaches toward long-range ordered Dâ€“A heterojunctions. 2. Bottom-up self-assembly of Dâ€“A small molecules and block copolymers

Different from physical blends of Dâ€“A molecules, covalently or noncovalently linked Dâ€“A molecules are expected to ease the control of the Dâ€“A interface and the phase separation into ideally long-range ordered heterojunctions. To date, a myriad of Dâ€“A molecules has been synthesized and the photoinduced charge/energy transferred has been intensively studied.54,55 In this section, we highlight the recent advances in molecular design, synthesis and assembly of Dâ€“A molecules and block copolymers. We focus on the strategies for molecular design that lead to long-range ordered segregated packing of Dâ€“A moieties and how such self-assembly leads to photoinduced energy/charge transfer that is favorable for improving the efficiency of OSCs.

2.1.1 Linear Dâ€“A dyads. The simplest structure of Dâ€“A molecules is a dyad in which an aromatic donor is linked to an aromatic acceptor mostly through a covalent bond. Fullerenes and their derivatives, due to their unique optoelectronic properties such as high electron affinity, small reorganization energy and superior ability for electron transport, have been extensively used in OSCs.3,4,11,24,35,40â€“43,48,66â€“69 To date, 6,6-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PC61BM)23 and its C70 analogue PC71BM are the most widely used acceptors for state-of-the-art OSCs. 3. Top-down approaches to long-range ordered Dâ€“A heterojunctions of molecules and polymers

The advent of new nanostructure-fabrication techniques such as e-beam lithography,113 nanoimprint lithography,110,114,115 block copolymer lithography89,116,117 and edge lithography118 has pushed the limit of dimensional control from the micro- to the nanoscale. A variety of nanostructures with arbitrary shapes and structural characteristics can be fabricated using these techniques. In this section, we highlight the progress of using these top-down nanostructure-fabrication techniques to create long-range ordered Dâ€“A heterojunctions in polymer solar cells.

3.1 Nanoimprint lithography

Nanoimprint lithography (NIL) is an embossing method by which large areas and high densities of nanostructures can be replicated in polymers from pre-patterned moulds. He et al.119 reported a double nanoimprinting process in which P3HT was patterned with high density arrays of nanostructures by solvent-assisted NIL using a prepatterned silicon mold, followed by a second NIL process of stamping the resulting P3HT nanostructures into a heated film of poly((9,9-dioctylfluorene)-2,7-diyl-alt-[4,7-bis(3-hexylthien-5-yl)-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole]-2â€²,2â€²â€²-diyl) (F8TBT) as the acceptor (Fig. 18). Importantly, a gradual increase of the PCE of the photovoltaic devices involving these nanopatterned Dâ€“A arrays was observed when the feature size-width decreased from 200 to 25 nm. The device with 25 nm features showed a PCE as high as 1.85%, which is 50% higher than that of the blend control device without patterning.

4. Summary and perspective

Rapid advances in both materials synthesis and device processing for OSCs have led to improved power conversion efficiency up to 10%. In state-of-the-art OSCs, the active layer, termed â€œbulk heterojunctionsâ€, is primarily composed of physically blended light-absorbing Ï€-conjugated molecules/polymers as donors and fullerene derivatives (mainly PCBM) as acceptors. It is expected that the device efficiency will be improved further by long-range ordering of the Dâ€“A heterojunction with domain sizes comparable to the exciton diffusion lengths (âˆ¼10 nm for Ï€-conjugated polymers). In an effort to achieve such â€œidealâ€ Dâ€“A heterojunctions, one approach is through bottom-up self-assembly of covalently linked Dâ€“A molecules and block copolymers, and the other is through top-down lithography using prepatterned solid molds.

Several factors need to be considered in the design, synthesis and assembly of Dâ€“A molecules and block copolymers: (1) Dâ€“A charge transfer interactions should be minimized while segregated Dâ€“D/Aâ€“A stacking needs to be maximized. This can be achieved, for example, by asymmetrical functionalization of Dâ€“A molecules with soluble groups which are incompatible with each other. (2) In most of the present self-assembling systems of Dâ€“A molecules and block copolymers, the orientation of Dâ€“D and Aâ€“A stacking tend to be parallel to the substrates or electrodes, which limits the vertical charge carrier transport to the electrodes in solar cell devices. (3) The donor segment of existing Dâ€“A block copolymers has been limited to P3HT, largely due to the synthetic advances of chain-growth polymerization.125,126 The development of polymer chemistry for low-band-gap polymers with well-defined chain lengths and chain-end groups is needed for new Dâ€“A block copolymers with broadened absorption of light and other desired properties for OSCs.

It remains a challenge in supramolecular self-assembling systems to control both nanoscale phase-separated morphology and vertically orientated Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking relative to electrodes. Although nanoimprint lithography provides an alternative way to achieving this goal, it is difficult to push the patterning size feature to the limit of exciton diffusion length which is typically below 10 nm for most Ï€-conjugated polymers.

To date, efforts for obtaining ordered Dâ€“A organic/polymeric heterojunctions by bottom-up assembly of molecular/polymeric building blocks have not resulted in photovoltaic devices competitive with the state-of-the-art polymer/fullerene BHJ devices (Table 1). The top-down lithography approaches, with the exception of that of Chang et al.,122 have also not been competitive with the state-of-the-art polymer/fullerene BHJ devices. Nevertheless, long-range ordered nanostructures and well-defined D/A interfaces are important to understand the correlation between macroscale device performance and nano/micro-scale photophysics at defined D/A interfaces, thus providing insights for materials design and morphology optimization to improve the device performance. In the future, a combination of top-down lithography and bottom-up assembly, which has been successfully used to achieve large-area ordered assembly of conventional coilâ€“coil block copolymers,89,116,127 but rarely for Ï€-conjugated molecules and block copolymers, will bring new opportunities to address the still open question: what is the ideal morphology for OSCs?
