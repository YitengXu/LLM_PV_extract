Abstract

Linear small molecules have been proved to be promising donor materials for high performance small molecular solar cells (SMSCs). In this paper, a series of narrow-band gap, Ï€-conjugated small molecules (SMs) with a D(Aâ€“Ar)2 linear framework, denoted as FBT(PyDPP-T)2, FBT(IID-T)2 and FBT(TDPP-T)2, based on fluorinated 2,2â€²-bithiophene (FBT) as an electron-donating (D) central core, pyridine-flanked diketopyrrolopyrrole (PyDPP), isoindigo (IID) or thiophene-flanked diketopyrrolopyrrole (TDPP) as electron-accepting (A) units, and 2-octylthiophene (Ar) units as end-capping units, have been designed and synthesized for application as donor materials in solution-processed SM organic solar cells (OSCs). The impacts of different acceptor units and fluorinated central cores on bulk properties, such as the optoelectronic properties, carrier mobility, the HOMO/LUMO energy level, and the morphologies of blend films, as well as optimization on device performance via appropriate solvent vapor annealing (SVA) are systematically investigated. The as-cast devices based on FBT(PyDPP-T)2, FBT(IID-T)2 and FBT(TDPP-T)2 exhibit PCEs of 5.04%, 5.68% and 6.86%, respectively. Encouragingly, after a SVA treatment with carbon disulfide (CS2) for 20 s, an impressively improved power conversion efficiency (PCE) from 6.86% to 9.00% with an increase of 35% was obtained in the FBT(TDPP-T)2/PC71BM (w/w, 1:1) based cells due to an enhanced short circuit current density (Jsc) of 16.14 mA cmâˆ’2 and fill factor (FF) of 73.52%, which is among the highest reported for D(Aâ€“Ar)2-type SM-based solar cells with a PCE up to 9.0% so far. Our research results reported here clearly demonstrate that highly efficient SM-OSCs can be obtained via rational design of their molecular structure and optimization of the phase-separated morphology through an appropriate solvent-vapor annealing process.

1. Introduction

As a promising next generation energy source technology, bulk heterojunction organic solar cells (BHJ-OSCs) based on polymers or small molecules (SMs) as donors and fullerene derivatives (e.g., PC71BM) as acceptors have received an extensive amount of attention in the past few decades due to their remarkable advantages, such as light weight, low cost, flexibility and large-scale fabrication.1â€“7 So far, the power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) of single junction BHJ-OSCs have rapidly increased to over 13% and 11% for polymers and SMs as donors, respectively.8,9 Especially over the past two years, as a result of rapid improvements in both innovative materials and device architectures, high PCEs exceeding 12.0% have been achieved for SM BHJ-OSCs.10,11 Particularly, since the narrow bandgap polymer donor PTB7-TH combined with a SM-nonfullerene acceptor showed a high PCE of 6.31%,12 an outstanding achievement has already been made in polymer solar cells (PSCs) based on SM-nonfullerene acceptors, and record PCEs of 14.08% and 14.9% have been achieved for ternary and tandem PSCs,13,14 respectively. Unquestionably, these successes might highlight their advantages in the future commercialization of OSCs.

Though great progress has been made recently in PSCs,5â€“7,12â€“14 some unsolved problems, such as purification, reproducibility and the undefined relationship of the structure, are still arduous challenges to enhance photovoltaic performance.15â€“18 Nevertheless, compared to their polymeric counterparts, SMs have many prominent merits, such as easy purification and high reproducibility ascribed to the defined structure and thus less batch to batch variation,16â€“18 which can provide a deeper understanding of how to design efficient photovoltaic materials and thus ensure greater reproducibility in the device performance. Therefore, solution-processed, SM-based OSCs have attracted dramatically increasing attention over the past few years, approaching the records of the best PSCs reported so far.11,19â€“25 For example, solution processed SM-OSCs have demonstrated PCEs exceeding 10% recently.11,22 However, the relationship of the molecular structure and properties, and the design method of SMs with high photovoltaic performance still remain unclear. Therefore, in SM OSCs, donor material innovation is regarded as the key point to achieve high-performance devices.

To obtain high PCE in SM OSCs, numerous narrow-band gap SMs composed of electron-rich donor (D) and electron-deficient acceptor (A) units have been extensively synthesized and applied in OSCs, due to their easily tunable energy levels, planarity and solubility.22â€“32 BHJ OSCs based on Aâ€“Dâ€“A or Aâ€“Ï€â€“Dâ€“Ï€â€“A structured SMs as donors and fullerene derivatives as acceptors have shown the highest PCEs of 12.7% and 11.3%, respectively.10,22 Recently, another family of SMs with a linear D(Aâ€“Ar)2 skeleton has also made significant progress, which exhibited maximum PCEs from 2.90 to 8.30%.33â€“45 Particularly, the diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP) unit is often used as the A unit, mainly because of its relatively rigid molecular structure, strong electron withdrawing ability, high carrier mobility, strong light absorption and good planarity, and some SMs with a D(Aâ€“Ar)2 skeleton containing DPP units as acceptor arms have been reported,38â€“45 which lead to a high short-circuit current (Jsc) and fill factor (FF) in SM-OSCs and thus enhance the photovoltaic properties. For example, OSCs based on a SM named BDTTâ€“(DPP)2â€“CF3 with DPP acceptor arms coupled with alkylthienyl-substituted benzodithiophene (BDTT) donors showed a PCE value up to 8.30%.42 Recently, OSCs based on DFPh(DPP-Py)2 with DPP as acceptor units coupled with 1,4-difluorobenzene (DFPh) rigid donor cores exhibited a splendid PCE of 7.54% as reported by our group.45 Meanwhile, thiophene-flanked diketopyrrolopyrrole (TDPP) and pyridine-flanked diketopyrrolopyrrole (PyDPP) were selected to make a comparison as acceptor units, in which both of them not only possess good coplanarity, but also show totally different energy levels.46 Furthermore, isoindigo (IID) units have been widely used in the dye industry because of their following advantages: strong electron-withdrawing ability, broad absorption spectra, high extinction coefficients, low HOMO levels and better planar architectures.47,48 Clearly, significant progress in the D(Aâ€“Ar)2 skeleton system could be comparable to that achieved in PSCs, indicating its promising future in practical applications.

Fluorine (F) atom substitution on polymers has been a powerful strategy to improve their PSC performances.49â€“57 For instance, a device based on fluorinated PNTz4TF2 as the donor exhibited a higher PCE of 10.50% than nonfluorinated PNTz4T as the donor with a PCE of 3.7%.56 Particularly, combining the fluorinated polymer PM6 with the fluorinated nonfullerene acceptor IT-4F, the corresponding PSCs achieved an impressive PCE of 13.5%, which is among the highest values in the literature for PSCs to date.57 Recently, various SMs containing F atoms as photovoltaic donor materials have been reported.46,55,60 For example, by changing the donor material of nonfluorinated TTH-D3TRh to the fluorinated TTF-D3TRh, the PCE of the corresponding OSCs was tremendously elevated from 5.89% to 7.14% with an increasing of 21%.55 This is mainly due to the intramolecular interactions between fluorine and sulfur atom (Fâ‹¯S) noncovalent bonds, which are beneficial for enhancing the planarity of the molecular backbone and crystallinity.46â€“56 As we all know, the fluorinated 2,2â€²-bithiophene (FBT) unit has been used as a donor unit in SMs and polymers for photovoltaic applications.44,51,58â€“60 The FBT moiety was employed as the central core mainly owing to its good rigid skeleton structure, which can reduce the torsion angle between conjugated units and lead to the improvement of molecular co-planarity. Therefore, the corresponding SMs and polymers exhibit an improved Ï€â€“Ï€ packing with enhanced backbone aggregations, which would also induce an enhanced charge carrier mobility.44,51 For example, DFDT(DPP)2 exhibited a PCE value as high as 7.87%.63 Apparently, the development of innovative donor materials plays a key scientific roll in achieving high-performance devices.

To systematically explore the influence of the different A arm units and molecular framework on the photovoltaic performance of SMs with a D(Aâ€“Ar)2 framework, in this work, a series of spatially symmetrical narrow-band gap SMs with a D(Aâ€“Ar)2 skeleton, named FBT(PyDPP-T)2, FBT(IID-T)2 and FBT(TDPP-T)2, containing FBT as the central D core and a 2-octylthiophene terminal as the Ar unit coupled with different A units, such as pyridine-flanked diketopyrrolopyrrole (PyDPP), isoindigo (IID) or thiophene-flanked diketopyrrolopyrrole (TDPP), were designed and synthesized, respectively (Chart 1). The strategy of combining fluorination on the core unit and different A units would provide a reasonably lower-lying HOMO energy level and higher carrier mobility to increase the Voc, Jsc, and FF of the related SM-OSCs.55,62 As expected, the devices with PC71BM as the acceptor exhibited the PCEs of 5.04%, 5.68% and 6.86% for FBT(PyDPP-T)2, FBT(IID-T)2 and FBT(TDPP-T)2, respectively. After an appropriate solvent vapor annealing (SVA) treatment with carbon disulfide (CS2), extensively improved PCEs of 6.40%, 7.78% and 9.00% were achieved for FBT(PyDPP-T)2, FBT(IID-T)2 and FBT(TDPP-T)2, respectively. Compared with FBT(PyDPP-T)2 and FBT(IID-T)2, FBT(PyDPP-T)2 shows a lower band gap, better planarity, higher carrier mobility and better film morphology. Ultimately, the BHJ OSCs based on FBT(TDPP-T)2/PC71BM present an outstanding PCE up to 9.00%, attributed to the enhanced Jsc of 16.14 mA cmâˆ’2 and an improved FF of 75.32% simultaneously.45,60 To the best of our knowledge, the PCE of 9.0% and Jsc of 16.14 mA cmâˆ’2 are the highest values that have been recorded so far for D(Aâ€“Ar)2-type SM-based BHJ-OSCs. The results reveal that high PCE in OSCs based on SMs can be noticeably enhanced through careful engineering of the molecular structure and optimization of the morphology of blend films by CS2-SVA treatment. As we all know, the treatment using different solvents also has important effects on other types of solar cell devices; for instance, solvent-treated hybrid perovskite-based solar cells have attracted more attention due to their high device performance.64,65 Meanwhile, organic Ï€-conjugated molecules have been applied in hybrid perovskite solar cells to realize efficient passivation and charge-extraction.66 This synthesized narrow band gap FBT(TDPP-T)2 may be applied in perovskite solar cells to achieve higher performance or to optimize some parameters due to its higher thermal stability, better crystallization and improved light-harvesting ability.

4. Conclusions

In conclusion, a family of narrow-bandgap Ï€-conjugated SMs with a D(Aâ€“Ar)2 linear framework based on FBT as the central core, coupled with various acceptor arms and 2-octylthiophene as terminal units, has been synthesized for BHJ-OSCs. The modification of axisymmetric electron-deficient acceptor groups furnished valuable parameters to tune charge transport, energy levels, and the morphology of the blend thin film, which had a critical influence on the device performance. The TDPP unit made FBT(TDPP-T)2 possess better molecular packing and higher mobility in comparison with those of FBT(PyDPP-T)2 and FBT(IID-T)2. The better PCEs, Jsc and FF values based on these SMs showed an apparent improvement upon SVA treatment in comparison with the values of the initial devices. Especially, an impressively improved PCE from 6.86% to 9.00% with an increase of 35% was obtained in the FBT(TDPP-T)2/PC71BM (w/w, 1:1) based devices after a SVA treatment for 20 s, which is about 1.16 and 1.40 times higher than those of FBT(PyDPP-T)2/PC71BM and FBT(IID-T)2/PC71BM based devices, respectively, which is the highest PCE and Jsc of solution-processed BHJ solar cells based on SMs with a D(Aâ€“Ar)2 framework reported so far. Obviously, this type of SM design strategy reported here indicates that the PCE of solar cells based on SMs can be dramatically enhanced via careful engineering of the molecular structure and optimization of the morphology of blend films.
