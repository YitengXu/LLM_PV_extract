Abstract

Considering the increasing heavy energy demands and severe environmental issues, researchers have been searching for an alternative renewable source of energy, aiming to achieve carbon neutralization. Biodiesel, which is deemed as an environment-friendly, sustainable, and renewable energy, has attracted extensive concerns worldwide with the merits of non-toxicity, biodegradability, and carbon-neutral nature. Generally, biodiesel is mainly produced via the transesterification of triglycerides and esterification of fatty acids with the assistance of highly effective catalytic material, which is determined to be a critical issue for biodiesel production. Benefiting from the superiorities of huge porosity, uniform pore size, controllable functional group, and excellent activity with stability, organic–inorganic hybrid porous coordination polymers (OIHPCPs) are considered to be promising catalysts for biodiesel production. However, limited reviews have concentrated on the specific application of OIHPCP-based catalytic materials for the production of biodiesel. With this, this study systematically reviews the current application of OIHPCPs in catalyzing the trans/esterification of liquid biomass for biodiesel production, including pristine metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), functionalized MOFs (i.e., acid, base, bifunctional, and enzymatic), MOF derivates, unconventional MOFs (UMOFs), and biomass derivate coordination polymers catalysts. Besides, the important impacts of trans/esterification parameters, such as the amount of catalyst, type and molar ratio of alcohol, reaction temperature and time, water and fatty acids content of the substrate, and auxiliary production technology of biodiesel are also comprehensively evaluated. Finally, the corresponding plausible reaction mechanisms and main conclusions together with future perspectives for OIHPCP applications in biodiesel production are further emphasized. This review is timely and conducive for advancing the bioenergy realms and achieving carbon neutrality.

1. Introduction

Nowadays, the rapid technological and economic development calls for an increase in the demand of fossil energy, while the non-renewable nature and huge demand of traditional fossil energy gradually exhaust it.1–4 Hence, looking for a kind of new, green, and renewable alternative energy has attracted extensive attention among relevant researchers all over the world.5–8 In addition, for the proposal of the development goal of “peaking carbon dioxide emissions by 2030 and achieving carbon neutrality by 2060”, it will further promote the shift in China's fossil energy utilization to new green energy, which will continue to strengthen the trend of low-carbon and the decarbonization of energy.9–11

There are many kinds of renewable energy sources that can support energy demand, which mainly including wind energy, solar energy, hydro energy, biomass energy, and geothermal energy.12–15 They are all important components of China's energy supply system and are of great significance for improving the energy structure, protecting the ecological environment, coping with climate change, and realizing sustainable economic and social development.16–19 Biodiesel, a mixture of long-chain fatty acid methyl ester (FAME) of great importance is regarded as a representative kind of green energy that is derived from renewable and sustainable biomass.20–22 Due to its pollution-free and renewable characteristics and suitable properties, it is expected to meet the world's energy demand and to be widely considered a promising alternative to fossil energy.23–26 Compared with conventional fossil energy, biodiesel has similar primary properties, which can be directly used for utilization in diesel engines.27,28 Furthermore, it exhibits significant benefits such as high combustion efficiency, ash point, with cetane number, lower CO2 emission, lower sulfur content, and better lubrication, implying its great application potential in terms of better combustion characteristics and lower emissions. More importantly, it is easier and safer for utilizing, storing, and transporting throughout the relevant procedure.29–32 The comparison of the main physicochemical properties between biodiesel and fossil diesel is shown in Table 1.33,34

Frequently, biodiesel (fatty acid methyl ester, FAME) is synthesized by transesterification or esterification in the presence or absence of catalysts,45,46 wherein catalysts can be divided into two categories, namely, homogeneous and heterogeneous.47 Despite the relatively low cost, short reaction time, and high yield, homogeneous catalysts usually suffer from some inevitable defects, such as the easy corrosion of production equipment, laborious separation and recycling, and severe environmental pollution. Hence, many efforts have been made to develop promising heterogeneous catalysts with outstanding activity, effortless separation, and good reusability.48–51 Organic–inorganic hybrid porous coordination polymers (OIHPCPs) are important types of hybrid materials connected by covalent bonds between metal atoms and organic ligands, which mainly include metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) and some other peculiar porous coordination polymers. OIHPCPs possess a series of very favorable properties that are beneficial for the preparation of biodiesel, such as high specific surface area, high porosity, easy functionalization, and more importantly, excellent catalytic activity and stability.52,53 The common synthesis methods of OIHPCPs mainly include the solvothermal method, hydrothermal method, volatilization method, and diffusion method. Moreover, some new synthesis methods have also been reported recently, such as electrochemical, microwave, and mechanical synthesis.54–56 In the last decades, much attention has been paid to the research on the production of biodiesel based on burgeoning hybrid porous coordination polymers as catalysts. 2. Trans/esterification mechanism

As shown in Fig. 3, biodiesel is usually obtained by the transesterification of triglycerides and methanol, or the esterification of FFAs and methanol.60
Fig. 3 Schematic illustration of biodiesel production through the transesterification of triglyceride and esterification of FFAs.

Generally, Brønsted bases exhibit outstanding activity for the transesterification of triglycerides, while Brønsted acids are regarded as highly-effective catalysts for the esterification of FFAs.46,61 The possible transesterification mechanism catalyzed by the Brønsted base is illustrated in Fig. 4(a), which is a key step for the Brønsted base to accept the protons of methanol to form methoxy anions with high chemical activity. Methoxyanion attacks the carbonyl carbon atom of the triglyceride to form a tetrahedral intermediate, which is then decomposed into a fatty acid methyl ester.62–64 In contrast, the reaction mechanism of Brønsted acid-catalyzed esterification is shown in Fig. 4(b). The protons first combine with the carbonyl group of FFAs to form carbon cation intermediates. Protophilic methanol combines with carbon cations to form a tetrahedral intermediate, which is then dehydrated to form FAME and generates protons to catalyze the next round of reaction.64–66


Fig. 4 Possible reaction pathway of the transesterification catalyzed by Brønsted base (a) and esterification catalyzed by Brønsted acid (b).

In addition to the Brønsted base, the Lewis acid and base are also capable of catalyzing the transesterification reaction. As shown in Fig. 5(a), bearing a resemblance to the Brønsted base catalytic process, the nucleophilic attack on the aliphatic carbonyl group was realized by activating methanol with the Lewis base site to form a tetrahedral structure. The generated alkoxy anion obtains the H atom, and the Lewis base generated at the same time continues to catalyze the next reaction.67,68 While the transesterification catalyzed by the Lewis acid site is completed by interacting with the catalytic site and ester oxygen, finally removing the alcohol and Lewis acid site, which could be used for consecutive catalysis (Fig. 5(b)), and as for the esterification reaction, just replacing the OR2 group with the hydroxyl group.68–70

3. Application of organic–inorganic hybrid porous coordination polymers in biodiesel production

Benefiting from the large specific surface area, high porosity, and easy functionalization, hybrid porous coordination polymers with high catalytic activity are considered promising catalysts for biodiesel production. This section mainly introduced the application of pristine MOFs catalysts, functionalized MOFs catalysts, MOF-derived catalysts, UMOFs catalysts, and biomass-derived coordination polymers catalysts in biodiesel synthesis.

3.1 Pristine MOFs catalysts

MOFs are special coordination polymers with a periodic network structure, formed by inorganic metal ions as connection units, organic ligands interconnected by self-assembly, and strictly regular nanopores and cavities are defined, which are similar to the structure of the zeolite (Table 3).71–73 The catalytic activity of Al2O3, MOF-808, MIL-125, and ZIF-8 for oleic acid esterification was compared by Zhou et al. Benefiting from the stable supported porous framework ensured by the strong interaction between the metal ions with organic ligands of MOF catalysts and more accessible Lewis acid sites of higher activity, ZIF-8 showed the highest catalytic activity under the conditions of 130 °C, 0.1 g oleic acid, 1 mL methanol, and 10 mg catalyst. Accordingly, the yield of methyl oleate was about 90%, proving that MOFs have a fine application prospect for biodiesel catalysis.74

Besides, Cu- and Ca-based MOF synthesized by solvothermal and hydrothermal processes was developed by Unza Jamil et al. to investigate biodiesel production from waste cooking oil. The characterization implied the successful synthesis of cubic MOF crystals with good thermal stability, large specific surface area, and unique mesoporous. Moreover, the coordination of more unsaturated active sites derived from the binding of Cu MOF + Ca MOF promoted the activation of methanol and triglycerides, providing a high yield of 85% after the process parameters were optimized via response surface methodology (RSM), which was better than single Cu-MOF (78.3%) or Ca-MOF (78%). In terms of the MOF stability, a slight decrease of 7% was found after 3 runs, which might be attributed to the leaching of Cu and Ca active sites and deposition of impurities on the catalyst surface.75 Considering the abundant FFA of raw Calophyllum inophyllum oil, T. M. M. Marso et al. adopted chromium(III) terephthalate (Cr-Tp) and cobalt(II) terephthalate (Co-Tp) as heterogeneous acid catalysts for pre-esterification, leading to a significant reduction (88%) in the acid value. Followed by the transesterification of pre-esterified oil with methanol in the presence of 1% KOH (by weight of oil), 93% yield of biodiesel with no saponification under mild conditions was reported. In this research, it was disadvantageous to directly use a homogeneous base catalyst for substrate oil with a high acid value. Pre-esterification before transesterification could effectively reduce the acid value, avoid saponification, and improve the yield under mild conditions.76 Alternatively, unlike T. M. M. Marso's two-step reaction, another MOF, Zn3(BTC)2 (BTC = 1,3,5-benzenetricaboxylic acid), was fabricated by Lunardi et al. to prepare biodiesel from degummed palm oil (DPO) via one-step trans/esterification, and the highest FAME yield was 89.89% under the optimized reaction conditions.77 It was reported that Zn3(BTC)2 emerged as a triclinic structure, possessing a large specific surface (1176 m2 g−1) and reasonable thermal stability (up to 300 °C), as well as a mass of macropores and mesopores, which made it available for macromolecular substrate contacting with the Lewis acid catalytic site (Zn2+) and promoted mass transport. The proposed mechanism for one-step trans/esterification is shown in Fig. 6; it could be seen that the proposed mechanism was consistent with the Lewis acid catalytic mechanism in Fig. 5, unsaturated Zn2+ center acted as Lewis acid for the coordination with oxygen, promoting the convergence and nucleophilic attack (no attack) between methanol and DPO, thereby facilitating the trans/esterification. It is worth mentioning that there was no obvious decrease in yield and leaching of the active sites after three consecutive cycles. In a similar way, Pangestu et al. synthesized rod-like MOF crystals by coordinating Cu2+ with BTC and applied them to the preparation of biodiesel from palm oil. Also, a high FAME conversion rate of 91% together with acceptable recyclability was obtained.78

In another precedent, MOF-5, an important member of the MOF family, was employed to prepare biodiesel from nonedible oil. Despite the fine conversation yield of 82.0% from waste cooking oil, a sharp decline in activity was observed, which was claimed to be relevant to the liberation of Zn, thus indicating the significance of a stable structure.79 Among various MOFs, the Zr-based UiO series exhibits outstanding stability in thermal, chemical, and mechanical properties. In addition to that, the Zhou research group claimed that the connection defects of UiO-66 played a major role in the catalytic activity as the absence of organic ligands led to more Zr active sites. In this regard, a series of UiO-66 with different defect amounts were successfully synthesized by changing the synthesis temperature or terephthalic acid/ZrCl4 ratio. The results demonstrated that UIO-66 synthesized at lower temperature and molar terephthalic acid/ZrCl4 ratio had more connection defects, which promoted the transesterification of tributyrin and soybean oil with methanol, and obtained an equivalent yield of other solid acid catalysts.80 Although UiO-66 has unsaturated metal active sites, its catalytic performance can be further improved by functional chemical groups. F. G. Cirujano reported that the content of the Lewis acid site of UiO-66-NH2 after amino group functionalization was about half that of UiO-66. However, UiO-66-NH2 showed better activity, benefiting from the synergistic action of Lewis acid and a basic amino group. To be specific, the connection between –NH2 and H improves the nucleophilicity of the alcohol, and the connection between Zr and O improves the electrophilicity of the carbonyl carbon (Fig. 7b), suggesting that the catalytic activity of MOFs could be improved by grafting chemically active groups.81

Impregnation is a relatively simple and effective method to load functional groups into MOF structures. By impregnation, p-toluenesulfonic acid and methanesulfonic acid were introduced into UiO-66(PTSA/UiO-66 and MSA/UiO-66, respectively). It was reported that the crystal structure of the prepared heterogeneous acid catalysts had not changed. With palmitic acid and methanol as the substrates, the catalytic activities of PTSA and MSA supported by UiO-66 were significantly higher than those supported by activated carbon, whose better catalytic performance of MOFs could be attributed to the significantly higher porosity and specific surface area (1058.9 m2 g−1, 1.79 nm). More importantly, the active metal Lewis acid sites (Zr4+) and Brønsted acid sites (–SO3H) in the MOF skeleton were determined as the crucial reasons for its outstanding activity. Accordingly, the yields were 97.1% and 97.0%, respectively, and the activity basically remained after five cycles, confirming that employing MOF as the carrier to load active sites is an effective strategy.85 Similarly, Liu et al. prepared MF-SO3 by functionalizing MIL-100 with dilute sulfonic acid. After sulfonation, the original MOF structure was almost unaltered, and thanks to the abundant Brønsted and Lewis acid sites of MF-SO3, a yield of 95.86% was obtained.86 Another sulfonic acid-functionalized MOF catalyst, MIL-101(Cr)-SO3H, prepared by one-step hydrothermal synthesis, exhibited excellent activity in oleic acid esterification and butanol vapor phase dehydration. After sulfonation, the specific surface area decreased significantly from 3015 m2 g−1 to 1801 m2 g−1, the pore volume decreased from 1.82 cm3 g−1 to 0.92 cm3 g−1, but the acid density increased to 1.62 mmol g−1. In particular, with the assistance of a microwave, the yield obtained in merely 20 minutes was equivalent to that obtained by conventional heating for 10 h.87

Polyoxometalates (POMs) are a vital category of metal–oxygen clusters formed by the connection between early transition metal ions and oxygen, possessing strong acid strength and unique redox properties,88,89 while the issues of high solubility and small specific surface area in polar solvents often lead to an unsatisfactory catalytic performance in biodiesel synthesis.90,91 Hence, the strategy of immobilizing/supporting POMs on porous MOF materials has attracted extensive interest. For example, 12-tungstophosphoric heterology acid (HPW) with a Keggin-type structure was successfully encapsulated in MIL-100 through the one-pot reaction of HPW ferric nitrate and trimellitic acid, and the as-prepared HPW@MIL-100(Fe) was used for the esterification of n-hexanol and acetic acid. The characterization techniques proved that HPW molecules with intact proton acidity were incorporated into the mesoporous cage of the MIL-100(Fe) framework as a non-coordination guest. Because the molecular size of POM was larger than that of the MOF framework, the issues of leaching could be dismissed. Also, a yield of 96.3% was obtained due to the synergistic effect between HPW and MIL-100(Fe).92 Similarly, by incorporating phosphomolybdic acid (PMA) into Fe-BTC, Zhang's research group fabricated PMA/Fe-BTC by a facile one-pot synthesis method. Due to the active species (PMA), large specific surface area, and good thermal stability, the yield was 72.3% in the reaction of methanol and oleic acid.93 Moreover, the esterification of oleic acid and methanol catalyzed by Zr-doped silicotungstic acid supported on Fe-BTC and UiO-66 (ZrSiW/Fe-BTC and ZrSiW/UiO-66) was investigated. The possible mechanism was depicted in Fig. 8; it could be seen that methanol attacked the carboxylic acid activated by ZrSiW/MOF and is finally dehydrated to synthesize biodiesel. According to the catalytic mechanism, acidity played an important role in the catalytic process. Due to the high acidity of ZrSiW and the stable structure of MOFs, the yields of ZrSiW/Fe-BTC and ZrSiW/UiO-66 still reached 79% and 88.9% after 6 cycles. The higher yield of ZrSiW/UiO-66 might be attributed to the more stable UiO-66 structure.94 Another example of UiO-66-based MOF-encapsulated POMs was reported by Zhang et al., wherein specifically, instead of direct assembly, they probed a two-step high-temperature formation of Zr6(OH)4O4(Ac)12 and low-temperature self-assembly of PW12@UIO-66(PW12 = H3PW12O40). The as-prepared PW12@UIO-66 exhibited outstandingly in terms of stability, the biodiesel conversion of the tenth cycle was equivalent to the fresh one, and the activity could even be maintained for 4 weeks.95 Magnetic materials can be effectively recovered by an external magnet, thus avoiding centrifugation or filtration operations. In this case, magnetic high-efficiency solid catalyst (H6PV3MoW8O40/Fe3O4/ZIF-8) was synthesized by adding ZiF-8 into Fe3O4 nanoparticles and then encapsulating vanadium-substituted heteropolyacid (H6PV3MoW8O40) (Fig. 9). Benefiting from the strong interaction between heteropolyacids and imidazole groups in ZIF-8, the active acid sites were stably supported, and the porous structure provided more accessible active centers and mass transfer. As a result, in addition to showing good magnetism, H6PV3MoW8O40/Fe3O4/ZIF-8 gave a yield of 92.6% and decreased slightly after 5 runs.96 Furthermore, Zhang et al. investigated a precedent of embedding Ce-BDC with immobilized HSiW into UiO-66(Zr). In addition to introducing favorable high acid sites, the average pore size of the Ce-BDC@HSiW@UiO-66 composite hybrid material was also optimized from 2.58 nm to 3.42 nm, thus facilitating the mass transfer of oleic acid, and an oleic acid conversion of 81.5% together with outstanding recyclability was reported.97

Furthermore, ionic liquids (ILs) with adjustable structures can be exchanged with the POMs anion to further modify POM-based MOF materials. In this regard, Xie et al. prepared two analogous, efficient, and green catalysts AILs/HPW/UiO-66-2COOH98 and AIL/HPMo/MIL-100(Fe),99 respectively. Therein, AILs/HPW/UiO-66-2COOH was prepared by functionalizing UiO-66-2COOH with POMs via in situ synthesis and then adding sulfonated acidic ionic liquid to the prepared POM/UiO-66-2COOH composites, and IL, POMs, and MOF composites were constructed by POMs anion exchange (Fig. 10). The as-prepared AILs/HPW/UiO-66-2COOH was reported to have both Brønsted sites from ILs and Lewis acid sites from POMs, and under the optimum reaction conditions, the yield of biodiesel reached 95.8%. It is worth mentioning that it could still maintain high activity even in low-quality substrates containing 9% FFA and 3% water.98 The other catalyst based on MOF, POMs, and ILs was prepared by Wan et al. and named as [SO3H-(CH2)3-HIM]3PW12O40@MIL-100; when utilized for the esterification of oleic acid and ethanol to prepare biodiesel, the yield was 94.6%.100 In addition, Chen et al. embedded HPW guest molecules into larger MIL-101(Cr) cages by simple impregnation, and then realized robust encapsulation by grafting sulfonic acid ionic liquid with HPW to generate a larger heteropoly ionic liquid, wherein the Brønsted acid came from the ILs, and Lewis acid in the MOFs was identified as the main active sites. At the same time, the uniform distribution of active sites and the retained mesoporous were also responsible for the effective esterification of oleic acid.101

In addition to utilizing POMs as connecting bridges to realize the heterogeneity of ILs, ILs can also be introduced into MOFs by connecting the unsaturated metal ions in MOFs. [(CH2COOH)2IM]HSO4@H-UiO-66, fabricated by approximate ligand substitution, exhibited excellent catalytic activity in the esterification of oleic acid with methanol. As depicted in Fig. 11, propionic acid etching was employed to remove partial ligands in MOF, resulting in more exposed defects, followed by bidentate coordination between the –COO− group and two portions of unsaturated Zr ion. Ultimately, [(CH2COOH)2IM]HSO4@H-UiO-66 was synthesized through the addition reaction of ionic liquid and sulfuric acid. Due to the similarity between the ligand of ILs and the original MOF ligand, stable Zr–O bonds were formed, and the generated Brønsted-Lewis acid sites endow it with satisfactory activity. The mechanism of Brønsted-Lewis acid synergistic catalysis is shown in Fig. 12; taking advantage of the nucleophilicity of methanol hydroxyl oxygen, the formation of tetrahedral intermediate was promoted with the assistance of Brønsted-Lewis acid sites, followed by dehydration to form FAME. Also, the reaction conditions were optimized by RSM, under the conditions of 10.39 molar ratio, 6.28% catalyst amount, and 5 h; the yield of 93.82% was obtained and can be reused six times without a significant loss of activity.102

Impregnation-reaction-encapsulation strategy was exploited by the Guan research group to encapsulate dicationic acid ionic liquids (DAILs, 1,4-bis[3-(propyl-3-sulfonate)imidazolium]butane hydrogen sulfate) into the MIL 100(Fe) framework. Due to its outstanding acidity (1.76 mmol g−1), spindle morphology, and superparamagnetism, 93.2% ethyl oleate yield could be obtained; besides, it showed no obvious loss of activity after six runs and could be separated under the action of magnetic force without obvious mass loss.104 Instead of modifying MOFs (MIL-101(Cr)) supporters with functional groups, Han et al. reported that electron-rich SH groups were capable of being introduced into the backbone of 2-mercaptobenzimidazole ILs to confine ILs through S–Cr coordination bonds. Due to the presence of abundant highly active ILs enriched on the surface of the MIL-101(Cr) framework, 91.0% conversion of oleic acid was attained under the optimum reaction conditions. Nevertheless, about 10% yield reduction was observed due to the leaching of ILs and the blockage of the MOF channel.105 As illustrated in Fig. 13, Peng et al. fabricated UiO-66 solid superacids grafted with different ILs via the quaternization of UiO-66 with 1,3-propane sulfonic acid and ion exchange with H2SO4 or HSO3CF3. The as-prepared UiO-66 solid superacids were qualified with outstanding acid concentrations (3.33 mmol g−1), super acidity, and unique microporous structure, which were demonstrated to be able to accurately activate methanol. In the process of transesterification, methanol was adsorbed in the internal pores of solid acids and activated by the acid sites, followed by being captured by the surface tripalmitate via diffusion. It was worth mentioning that this pattern of methanol transportation in the pores was claimed to be faster and more effective. As a result, UiO-66 solid superacids exhibited remarkable catalytic activity.106 Developing novel ILs with higher acidity and more sites is of great significance to endow MOFs with better activity. Accordingly, an interesting trisulfonic ILs was effectively confined in amine-functionalized UiO-66 via the intense acid–base interaction of –NH2 and –SO3H. However, the apparent reduction of specific surface area and pore blockage was observed, which further implied the connection of ILs, good wettability, and novel ILs with high acidity, which enabled a satisfactory oleic acid conversion of 96.86%.107

It can be concluded that the reaction conditions of MOF-based acid catalysts are normally more stringent, but their favorable catalyst structure, stable acidity, and wider scope of application have led to extensive investigations.

3.3 Base-functionalized MOF catalysts

Along with acid-functionalized MOFs, some active base-functionalized MOFs were also developed as catalysts for biodiesel production (Table 5). A typical strategy is to immobilize an alkaline reagent on MOF supporter. For instance, guanidine, a strong organic base, was bound on ZiF-90 via imine condensation reaction to develop a novel catalyst for the transesterification of soybean oil.112 Benefiting from the superior physicochemical properties of ZiF-90-Gua (i.e., high alkalinity, large specific surface area, and good stability), a conversion of 95.4% was reported under the optimal reaction conditions of 65 °C, 6.0 h, 15:1 methanol/oil molar ratio, and 1 wt% catalyst dosage. According to the catalytic mechanism proposed in the research (Fig. 14), imine as the active site improved the electrophilicity of carbonyl carbon, promoting the adsorption of methanol, thus facilitating the transesterification reaction, and the alkalinity was closely related to the catalytic performance.

3.4 Bifunctional MOF catalysts

Possessed with both alkaline and acidic active sites, bifunctional catalysts can play a synergistic role in the reaction steps for biodiesel production (Table 6).119 As reported, UiO-66-NH2,81 a typical bifunctional catalyst equipped with –NH2 and Zr, was used in the esterification of lauric acid and methanol, giving a yield of over 99%. Similarly, another bifunctional catalyst based on UiO-66 was fabricated by Ahmed S. et al. It was claimed that UiO-66(Zr)-NH2-green was prepared by a green method and exhibited a high conversion of 97.3% together with good reusability. Moreover, due to the presence of –NO2 with great electronegativity, which would increase the positive electricity of Zr, UiO-66(Zr)-NO2-green theoretically had better acidity than UiO-66(Zr)-NH2-green, while its poorer catalytic performance indicated that the synergistic effects from two diverse sites had positive impacts on oleic acid esterification.120 Unlike the commonly used impregnation121 or encapsulation methods,122 chemically-bonded catalysts normally exhibit better stability, while anchoring amino sites in organic MOF ligands can provide media for subsequent chemical grafting. As shown in Fig. 16, an interesting Schiff base Zr coordination catalyst was fabricated by the condensation of salicylaldehyde with amine groups anchored in organic ligands, followed by the tetradentate coordination of Zr with [O, N, N, O] (Zr-salen). Under the synergistic assistance of two Lewis acid sites (Zr, Cr) and Schiff base, 74.1% conversion of methyl oleate was observed; more importantly, this stable coordination pattern and robust skeleton maintained a yield of 73.6% after 6 cycles. In particular, limited literature on catalysis by bimetallic multi-sites has been reported, and this Zr(IV)-Sal Schiff base complex could be regarded as a feasible and highly promising direction.123

3.5 Enzymatic MOF catalysts

The transesterification for biodiesel can be conducted under mild conditions with better selectivity in the presence of enzymes (Table 7). Moreover, the purity of the obtained biodiesel is usually higher, and the process is green and ecofriendly.130,131 However, the poor stability and recyclability limit the application. To deal with these issues, the immobilization of enzymes on stable porous supporters has been widely investigated.132,133 From a comprehensive view, Tan and his co-workers reviewed the strategy of lipase immobilization and the application of lipase confined on different nanomaterials in biodiesel production, for example, nanoparticles, nanofibers, carbon nanotubes, graphene, and MOFs. It turned out that MOF-supported enzyme catalysts exhibited excellent characteristics due to their rigid skeleton and adjustable aperture.134 Adsorption, encapsulation, and covalent bonding are common immobilization strategies.135

Through the study on the relationship between reaction time and FAME yield, the yield of traditional ANL/ZIF-8 was higher in a short time due to a large number of enzymes adsorbed on the surface, but with the extension of time, soybean oil, and methanol molecules could enter the macropores and contact with the active center. The results showed that magnetic modification did not inhibit the loading and activity of the enzyme, and could significantly improve the reusability of the catalyst.142 This relatively facile and convenient adsorption method could avoid damaging the overall structure of the enzyme, but maintaining the stable immobilization of the enzyme is a long-term challenge.

3.6 MOF-derived catalysts

In addition to the hard template method,150 high-temperature calcination and carbonization are also capable of optimizing the pore size limitation of MOFs (Table 8). However, the insufficient tolerance of MOFs to an exorbitant temperature usually makes them unable to maintain their initial structure, but their calcined derivative still inherits the porous structure and molecular level adjustable characteristics of MOFs; moreover, the metal ions in MOFs could be transformed into active metal oxides.151 Among diverse MOFs, MIL-Fe(100) was extensively employed as a precursor due to the satisfactory magnetism of the calcined derivative; incidentally, magnetic material produced by this in situ calcination decreases the agglomeration formed by dipole mutual attraction. Considering this, Li's research group reported a series of examples of carbonized MIL-Fe(100)-loaded metal oxides, one was that carbonized MIL-Fe(100) (Fe@C) supported active SrO heterogeneous alkaline catalyst, and after carbonization, the Fe@C was endowed with magnetism and a larger pore size, which was more conducive for the access of macromolecular oil substrate. The effects of SrO loading and calcination temperature on the activity were investigated; under the saturated 30 wt% SrO loading condition, the activity calcined at exorbitant temperature decreased due to crystal sintering and massive agglomeration. The best basicity of Fe@Ce–Sr achieved was 7.94 mmol g−1, and the conversion of biodiesel was 98.12% in the transesterification of palm oil and methanol.152 In addition, Li's research group also probed the catalytic performance of (NH4)2SO4-impregnated carbonized MIL-Fe(100) in the esterification of oleic acid and methanol, and the as-prepared CIS demonstrated an exciting 95.68% conversion of methyl oleate without a significant loss of the circulation activity.153 Unlike the carbonization of MIL-Fe(100) and then supporting SrO, SrCO3 was firstly immobilized on MIL-Fe via mechanical mixing and in situ titration, and then calcined in N2 atmosphere to fabricate MM-SrO, and ST-SrO. The obtained catalyst was used in the transesterification of palm oil and methanol with a high yield of 96.19%.154 Compared with SrO, CaO possessed a wide range of sources and lower cost; in line with this, Li's research group proposed that calcium acetate and MIL-Fe(100) were utilized as precursors, followed by calcination at 750 °C nitrogen atmosphere (CAM 750). The characterization technique confirmed the two-step decomposition process from calcium acetate to calcium carbonate and then to calcium oxide, which further combined with Fe3O4 and Fe2O3 to form the main active center Ca2Fe2O5 and CaFe3O5, leading to the total basicity of 2.66 mmol g−1. Similarly, the expansion of micropores to mesopores after calcination was also conducive to increase mass transfer, and the saturation magnetization of 112 emu g−1 allowed it to be easily recovered by external magnetism. Despite the 95.07% conversion of the fresh catalyst, a significant activity loss was found in the fourth operation that was subsequently proved to be induced by the accumulation of organic matter rather than the leaching of calcium.155

3.7 UMOF catalysts

As reported, MOFs are a kind of crystalline porous coordination polymer with periodic network structure (Table 9).72,73 Some specific coordination polymers, such as trivalent and tetravalent metal phosphates, are normally short of the highly ordered structure defined by MOFs and featured by precipitation in the form of layered hybrids with poor crystallinity.158 However, their good thermal stability and easy functionalization make them potential catalysts in biodiesel production. Representatively, an acid–base bifunctional organic–inorganic hybrid mesoporous zirconium phosphate (ZP) was manufactured by Pan et al. via a template-free method under solvothermal conditions. After post modification with sulfonic acid-functionalized polyionic liquid (P[SIH]), the final bifunctional catalyst was successfully fabricated (ZP-P [SIH]), as shown in Fig. 20. ZP-P [SIH] presented a coral blocky appearance with an obvious cavity and rich uniform mesoporous structure, which improved mass transfer and reaction efficiency. More importantly, through control experiments, it was found that evenly dispersed multi-active sites endowed it with excellent activity after the introduction of ionic liquid, and its benign hydrophobicity also lessened the adsorption toxicity of byproduct water to the catalytic sites. For biodiesel production by oleic acid and crude Euphorbia lathyris L. oil, esterification with methanol gave 96% and 93% yield, respectively.159 Similarly, another phosphate-based porous zinc sulfonated zinc phosphate (HZnPS-1) was reported by Bhaumik et al.160 The high activity sulfonic acid group introduced by sulfonation and abundant mesopores that may come from the intervoids between the flake particles endowed it with excellent activity in the esterification of long-chain fatty acids. At room temperature, 90% yield and acceptable reusability were observed.

Using soybean oil as a feedstock, the molar ratio of methanol to oil is 12:1 and the yield of biodiesel was 98% in the reaction time of 2 h with 2% catalyst at 180 °C, and there was no obvious desorption after 5 cycles of operation.161 With no template, a novel porous acid–base bifunctional catalyst (FDCA-Hf) was prepared by Li et al. through by the simple assembly of hafnium with 2,5-furan dicarboxylic acid (FDCA), one of the top-12 valuable molecules in lignocellulose listed by the U.S. Department of Energy. Furthermore, through the poisoned experiments, it indicated that the Brønsted acid sites in FDCA-Hf were the dominant part to obtain the 98% biodiesel yield.162 In addition, Li et al. also reported a series of mesoporous zirconium phosphonates that were synthesized by a simple and template-free assembly, and the prepared m-PhPZr showed remarkable performance in the transfer hydrogenation of biomass-derived compounds.163

Compared with CaO particles, the CaO microcapsule system assisted in improving the accumulation rate of FAME and impeded the dissolution of catalysts, impressively shortening the reaction time from 12 h to 6 h. Nevertheless, the modified capsule shell inevitably hindered the permeation rate of the reactants, leading to a relatively lower FAME yield (63%). Another interesting catalyst, PVA (polyvinyl alcohol)-alginate-sulfate beads immobilizing lipase, was reported by Matinja et al., giving the methyl oleate conversion of 65.06%.169

3.8.2 Other biomass-derived catalysts. Chitosan, an alkaline polysaccharide, is the product of the deacetylation of chitin that normally consists of the shell and skeletons of crustaceans and plant cell walls.176 Benefiting from the weak alkalinity, biocompatibility, non-toxicity, and easy availability, it has been widely used in medicine, environmental remediation, textiles, etc.177 For biodiesel production, a series of interesting and efficient chitosan and chitosan-derived catalysts were reported, including chitosan-cryogel beads,178 CaO/chitosan,179 adsorbed Cu(II) and Co(II) ionic chitosan.180 As shown in Fig. 24, the amino groups in chitosan were bonded with Ti4+ through coordination bonds. 4. Effects of the reaction conditions on biodiesel production

For the preparation of biodiesel, the optimization of reaction conditions including the amount of the catalyst, the type and molar ratio of alcohol, reaction temperature and time, water and FFA content of the substrate, and relevant auxiliaries of ultrasound and microwave, are of great significance to the final yield. Considering this, this section mainly investigates the influences of the above reaction conditions on the final yield and the optimal parameters. In the end, some auxiliary for biodiesel production technologies (ultrasound and microwave) were also introduced.

4.1. Alcohol type and molar ratio

Various alcohols with different carbon numbers have been adopted to synthesize biodiesel, for example, methanol, ethanol, propanol, isopropanol, and butanol.28,183 According to the mechanism of transesterification/esterification in section 2, the nucleophilicity and steric hindrance of different alcohols had an important influence on the reaction.184,185 The nucleophilic and spatial effects of different alkoxy anions are as follows.

Nucleophilicity: CH3O− > C2H5O− > C3H7O− > C4H9O−

Spatial effect: CH3O− < C2H5O− < C3H7O− < C4H9O−

Based on the above two factors, it could be concluded that with the growth of the carbon chain, the transesterification rate and equilibrium constant gradually decreased, which was consistent with the research of H. Sanli et al.186 H. Sanli reported a detailed comparison of biodiesel prepared from methanol, ethanol, 2-propanol, and 1-butanol as substrates. By comparing ester conversion and fuel characteristics, methanol was the most suitable alcohol for biodiesel production. Furthermore, the research group of Hanh et al. attributed the poor catalytic performance of secondary alcohol (2-propanol, 2-butanol, 2-hexanol, and 2-octanol) to its large steric hindrance.183 Considering the low price and abundant resources, methanol is generally the preferred reactant for the production of biodiesel.

In the light of Fig. 3, 3 mol methanol was required to synthesize 1 mol FAME. Owing to the reversibility of the transesterification reaction, excess methanol is conducive to the positive direction of the reaction. However, exaggerating the amount of methanol might dilute the catalyst concentration, leading to worse catalytic performance and lower conversion.187,188 It was further confirmed in Pangestu's optimization process that as the methanol:oil volume ratio increases from 5:1 to 10:1, the yield of FAME decreases slightly.78 In the presence of an enzyme catalyst, high alcohol concentration may even inactivate the enzyme.189 For instance, in a solvent-free medium catalyzed by immobilized Thermomyces lanuginosus lipase, the maximum initial reaction rate was 9.0 g l−1 min−1 for a 1:1 molar ratio of methanol to oil. When the molar equivalent of methanol exceeds 1.5, the inactivation of the enzyme was observed.190 In summary, the most suitable molar ratio was related to the type of the catalyst and was one of the most crucial parameters for optimizing the experiment.

4.2. Reaction temperature and time

According to the chemical reaction kinetics, the reaction speed can be accelerated by increasing the reaction temperature.191 Moreover, the trans/esterification reaction belongs to the endothermic reaction, which means that external heat is essential to promote the reaction. Generally speaking, the optimal temperature is controlled around the boiling point of substrate alcohol (methanol boiling point 64.7 °C), maintaining a slightly boiling state, which was in line with Zhang research group's optimization result (65 °C) of methanol as the reactant.192 The exorbitant temperature will lead to methanol evaporation, hindering the progress of transesterification and consuming abundant heat energy. For an enzyme-catalyzed system, the reaction conditions were normally milder, avoiding the inactivation of the enzymes due to high temperature. Accordingly, the reaction rate catalyzed by immobilized Thermomyces lanuginosus lipase (molar ratio of 1:1) increased significantly when the temperature increased from 30 °C to 40 °C, but no noticeable improvement was observed when the temperature further increased to 50 °C.190

In optimizing the reaction conditions, the reaction time is usually determined by the amount of catalyst and reaction temperature molar ratio. Appropriately prolonging the reaction time can improve the conversion rate. Continuing to prolong the reaction time will reduce the utilization rate of the equipment, increase the production cost, and promote side reactions as well.193,194 The optimum reaction time normally refers to the time when the reaction reaches equilibrium. In this respect, a novel method of non-catalytic supercritical methyl acetate method was reported by Saka et al. and the influence of the reaction time was further investigated.195 The results showed that the yield of FAME was the highest at 45 minutes, and the decrease in the yield when extended to 60 minutes may be attributed to the thermal decomposition of FAME compounds. The reaction time is also related to the quality of raw oil. Low-quality oils such as waste cooking oil generally require a longer reaction time.

4.3. Catalyst amount

The catalyst amount is another crucial parameter for optimizing the esterification and transesterification. Initially, the increase in the catalyst concentration leads to the formation of more reaction activation centers and the yield is enhanced until the equilibrium point is achieved. Further adding the catalyst may block the pore size and increase the mass transfer resistance, resulting in the decline of FAME conversion.196,197 Especially in an alkaline catalytic system, excess catalyst might promote the saponification reaction. Xie et al. compared the oil conversion with different ZiF-8-Gua loadings (0.4, 0.6, 0.8, 1.0, and 1.2 wt%). The oil conversion was boosted from 51.4% to 95.4% by increasing the catalyst loading from 0.4 to 1.0 wt%. Yet, the conversion was slightly decreased as catalyst loading was enhanced to 1.2 wt%.112 Similarly, Bedir et al. determined that 5% was the optimal amount of the catalyst from 1% to 7%.198

4.4. Free water and FFA content

The feed oil applied for producing biodiesel is theoretically expected to be anhydrous to avoid triglyceride hydrolysis and saponification. Particularly in an alkaline catalytic environment, fatty acid salts formed by saponification will adhere to the surface of the catalyst.199,200 Moreover, severe saponification leads to the emulsification of the whole reaction system, resulting in the inability of trans/esterification.201 In the presence of Amberlyst-15 or sulfuric acid as a catalyst, the effect of water on the esterification of high FFA oil was investigated by Park et al.202 The results showed that even if the initial water content was 1% (relative to oil), the esterification reaction was hindered and the reverse reaction was promoted. Also, water had a greater impact on Amberlyst-15 catalysis due to the toxicity of water to the acidic sites. As the initial water content further increased to 20%, the esterification rate decreased significantly. Nevertheless, a certain amount of water is essential for enzyme catalysis to improve the activity of lipase. Sara Rafiei et al. researched the influence of water concentration in the range of 5–20 wt% and determined that 10% is the optimal water content.146

FFA content is another parameter that requires exacting control for biodiesel production catalyzed by alkaline, thus avoiding the consumption of extra catalyst.187 The acidic catalyst H6PV3MoW8O40/Fe3O4/ZIF-8 was developed by Xie's research group and applied to catalyze the trans/esterification of soybean oil simultaneously. Correspondingly, Xie reported the reduced conversion with the increase in FFA content from 2% to 14%.96 For high FFA rubber seed oil, A. S. Ramadhas et al. developed a two-step transesterification process: acid-catalyzed esterification reduces FFA content to less than 2%, followed by base-catalyzed transesterification, indicating an effective production route.203 The enzyme is relatively insensitive to the substrate containing abundant FFA. In this regard, Kírian et al. reported biodiesel production from natural waste olive oil with high FFA content catalyzed by recombinant 1,3-positional selective Rhizopus oryzae lipase. Not only was the initial reaction rate improved but the enzyme was also claimed to obtain better stability due to the high FFA content.204

4.5. Ultrasound and microwave

Ultrasound refers to the mechanical waves with short wavelengths and high frequency (≥20 kHz).205 For an immiscible reaction system, cavitation will occur in the presence of ultrasound, resulting in abundant bubbles, expanding and exploding, to strengthen the contact between the reactants and promote trans/esterification.206,207 Many pieces of research on the ultrasonic-assisted synthesis of biodiesel have been reported. Amit Pal et al. took advantage of ultrasonic cavitation to synthesize biodiesel from WCO, which obtained a higher conversion rate in a shorter time compared with the mechanical stirring method.206 Similarly, Rathod et al. confirmed that the biodiesel synthesized by ultrasonic-assisted synthesis had a superior performance in comparison with the traditional stirring method, boosting the yield from 65.6% to 93.5%.208 In addition, the consistent result was also reported by Devarajan's research group.209

In contrast, microwave, an electromagnetic wave with a frequency of 300 MHz–300 GHz, generates heat through the interaction between polar molecules and microwave electromagnetic fields to realize uniform and rapid heating.210,211 With the assistance of microwave radiation, Lin reported a faster and more efficient preparation of biodiesel from Jatropha curcas seed oil, achieving 90% conversion in merely 10 s.212 Moreover, Yu et al. reported that microwave radiation also had positive effects on the enhancement of the enzyme activity in the ionic liquid medium, which might be attributed to the direct effect of microwaves on ionic liquids. Under the synergistic activation of microwave and ionic liquid, a remarkable yield of 92% together with excellent recyclability was obtained.213 Employing microwave crushing technology, Dai et al. investigated the preparation of biodiesel by microwave-assisted extraction of lipids in different solvents, and the best conversion obtained was 76.2%.214 In particular, Pravin et al. adopted conjoint ultrasound and microwave reactors to synthesize biodiesel. While significantly reducing the specific energy consumption, it suggested another potential associated and efficient method.215

Overall, the substrate and catalytic environment had great impacts on the parameters that needed to be optimized. For oil with high FFA and water content, efficient FAME conversion could be realized using acidic or enzymatic catalysts, while FAME synthesis can be completed in a short time under alkaline conditions. In addition, biodiesel production catalyzed by lipase can be carried out under milder conditions, but it usually takes a longer time, which can be shortened by ultrasound or microwave.

5. Conclusion and perspectives

As a significant kind of green and renewable biofuel, biodiesel has attracted the extensive attention of researchers. For industrial production, homogeneous alkali is widely used, benefited by its short reaction time and fast reaction speed. Nonetheless, the defects of complex post-treatment and pollution of homogeneous catalysts are forcing the transition toward heterogeneous ones that are convenient to reuse. Among them, OIHPCPs with high specific surface area and functionalized highly active sites have been widely investigated in the synthesis of biodiesel. Therefore, this review systematically emphasized the application of OIHPCPs in biodiesel production, including pristine MOF catalysts, functionalized (i.e., acid, base, bifunctional, and enzymatic) MOF catalysts, MOF derivate catalysts, UMOF catalysts, and biomass derivate coordination polymer catalysts. In addition, the mechanisms of esterification and transesterification reactions, factors affecting the yield of biodiesel, and auxiliary technologies (ultrasound and microwave) were also clearly introduced (Fig. 26). The main findings and future perspectives are summarized as follows.
Fig. 26 The main finding of functionalized organic–inorganic hybrid porous coordination polymers catalysts for biodiesel production.

I. MOFs, as typical kinds of organic–inorganic hybrid coordination polymers, can be effectively applied in the preparation of biodiesel. Furthermore, it is demonstrated that MOFs are also considered good functional matrixes, which will bring about better catalytic performance by immobilizing efficient active sites.

II. Among them, as concluded from this study, acid-functionalized MOFs by sulfonation, ILs, or HPA loading are the most widely investigated due to their wider applicability. However, higher temperature and higher alcohol-oil ratio make them unsuitable for the industrial production of biodiesel, which should be further investigated, optimized, and improved.

III. The effective immobilization of active sites is a great challenge for base functionalization. Although the alkaline environment can realize transesterification in a shorter time, the strict requirements for the acid value and water content of substrate oil limit its wide application. On the contrary, acid–base bifunctional catalysts are considered desirable catalysts for the industrial synthesis of biodiesel, in terms of their ability to catalyze trans/esterification with beneficial synergy and integrate catalytic conversion and product/catalyst recovery in a simple one-pot process.

IV. Biodiesel production catalyzed by ecofriendly enzymes can be carried out under mild conditions. To strengthen the stability, it is of great significance to understand the interaction mechanism between the MOF and enzymes at the molecular level, which is rarely reported in enzyme-based MOFs reference. On the other hand, looking for economic enzymes with high activity and fabricating novel MOFs with suitable pore sizes are long-term challenges for researchers, wherein MOF derivatives are prepared by calcination or carbonization, which seem to be an easy and effective pore size optimization method; in particular, the generated coordination defect is also motivating to the synthesis of biodiesel.

V. Importantly, developing UMOFs with diverse molecular structures is strongly recommended. Exploring more metal attachment centers and different organic ligands, especially biomass-derived species, such as FDCA-HF, should be deemed as a feasible and highly promising direction. From a practical standpoint, utilizing biodegradable biomass-derived coordination polymers with a wide range of sources to achieve a more efficient and mild catalytic process and to realize the green biodiesel synthesis indeed shows the prospect of industrialized applications.

VI. For various catalytic environments and substrate oils, reaction temperature, alcohol-oil molar ratio, and catalyst dosage are the principle optimization parameters affecting the final biodiesel yield. An appealing pattern of ultrasound and microwave can obviously promote trans/esterification. In addition, it was also found to play a positive role in improving the stability of the enzyme activity, for which this should be further developed in the future.

VII. Last but not least, hydrophobicity and magnetism also demonstrate a great influence on the activity and recoverability of the catalyst, which is also ought to be taken into account in the design philosophy fabrication of the catalyst. To successfully realize the application of heterogeneous catalysts in industrial production, powdered catalysts, which are the vast majority of synthetic catalysts, need to be further optimized in the future to adapt to the utilization and recovery of continuous fixed reactions.
