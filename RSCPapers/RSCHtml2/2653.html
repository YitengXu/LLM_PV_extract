Abstract

We report on two novel p-type small molecules with a donorâ€“acceptor molecular structure for application to green-light-selective organic photodiodes (OPDs). To achieve the requirement of high light selectivity and sensitivity, an electron-donating aryl amino moiety is combined with two respective electron-accepting heterocycles so that the molecules approach cyanine-like character, characterized by intense and sharp absorption. Molecular stacking is controlled by the addition of bulky aryl functional groups to the main backbone to further control the electrical charge transport properties. With this molecular design, a maximum external quantum efficiency close to 61% (Î»max = 550 nm) and a dark-current density below 1.6 nA cmâˆ’2 (or specific detectivity D* = 1.19 Ã— 1013 cm Hz1/2 Wâˆ’1) at an applied reverse bias of 3 V are obtained when mixed with fullerene (C60) in an inverted-structure bulk heterojunction OPD composed of two transparent electrodes. The potential construction of a full-color photodetector or an image sensor is demonstrated by combining the green-light-selective OPD with a silicon photodiode containing solely blue and red color filters in a stacked architecture.

Introduction

The field of organic electronics, driven by the successful commercialization of organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs), is now expanding toward other applications, such as organic solar cells (OSCs),1,2 organic photodiodes (OPDs),3,4 and organic photodetectors.5â€“15 For the molecular design of p-type semiconductor materials, the pushâ€“pull approach consisting of building molecules with alternating electron-donating and electron-accepting moieties has been widely investigated for OSCs.16â€“19 These materials potentially gave high external quantum efficiency (EQE), while the pushâ€“pull mechanism associated with the electron-donating and electron-accepting units led to a decrease in the band-gap and an extended absorption range. Because of the large absorption range, this approach would apparently be inappropriate for the OPDs that require an intense and sharp absorption for color selectivity and sensitivity. It was however shown that in the case of organic pushâ€“pull dyes for nonlinear optics (NLO) application, it is possible to design molecules with an intense and sharp absorption.20â€“22 In terms of the absorption properties of the pushâ€“pull dyes for the NLO, regardless of their electrical properties, the strength of the donor (D) (or push) and the acceptor (A) (or pull) parts should be well counter-balanced, so that the Dâ€“A molecule changes its character from a polyene-like molecule to a cyanine-like molecule. A cyanine limit was then defined, which corresponds to the minimization of the difference between the dipole in the excited state and the dipole in the ground state (Î”Î¼).23 In the cyanine limit, the electronic structure of the dyes is close to the resonance limit, characterized by an equal contribution of the neutral and zwitterionic resonance structures.24â€“26 Despite not strictly defined, a Î”Î¼ value approaching 2 or below would be ideal in terms of molecular conjugation to get a sharp and intense absorption.

In this study, we therefore apply the Dâ€“A (or pushâ€“pull) approach to the design of two novel p-type small molecules to use them as chromophores for green-light-selective OPDs. The strength of the D and A was carefully chosen to remain rather close to the cyanine limit while tuning the absorption to the green region of the visible spectrum. We show that the combination of electron-donating aryl amino group with heterocyclic acceptors was appropriate to get intense and sharp green absorption. The Dâ€“A small molecules with strong polarity are prone to extended stacking along their main axis, with their dipole aligned in the opposite direction.27 Such a stacking manner has a positive effect for the optoelectronic properties such as the OPD efficiency, because of the elimination of the single molecular electrical dipoles that increase entropic disorders and impede charge transport properties. Extensive stacking might however negatively affect the performance of the OPD by increasing the dark-current (DC) and the absorption width (full width at half maximum (FWHM)). We thus controlled the stacking properties by functionalizing the electron-donating aryl amino unit with phenyl and naphthyl groups that lies outside the plane of the main molecular axis. Thanks to the high performing intrinsic molecular properties and an optimized stacking arrangement of the molecules, an inverted-structure green-light-selective OPD composed of two transparent electrodes was developed by mixing the Dâ€“A molecules with fullerene (C60). The device exhibited a maximum EQE close to 61% (Î»max = 550 nm) and a DC density below 1.6 nA cmâˆ’2 (or specific detectivity D* = 1.19 Ã— 1013 cm Hz1/2 Wâˆ’1) at an applied reverse bias of 3 V, corresponding to our knowledge among the highest green-light-selective OPD performances reported so far.1â€“15

Results and discussion

The new Dâ€“A molecules 1a and 1b were synthesized via the synthetic routes shown in Fig. 1a with very high reaction yields, and their molecular structures were completely characterized via nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy, mass spectroscopy, and elemental analysis (see the Experimental section). To increase the strength of the D part, an electron-rich thiophene group was used as a linker between the donating aryl amino group and the two respective accepting heterocycles a and b used as the A units.28Fig. 1b represents the optimized molecular configuration of the two molecules at the B3LYP level of the DFT method with the 6-31G(d,p) basis set, calculated using Gaussian09.29 In the minimum energy configuration, the methine group is oriented so that the oxygen of one of the carboxyl groups is in the close vicinity of the sulfur of the thiophene group, presumably because of electrostatic interactions.27 The conjugation of the molecules stabilizes the main backbone in a planar configuration, while the phenyl and naphthyl groups lay outside the backbone plane. An experimental nuclear Overhauser effect spectroscopy (NOESY) revealed that one of the protons at the ortho-positions of the phenyl group showed an apparent NOE with a proton of the thiophene moiety through space correlation (see Fig. S1 in the ESIâ€  for the NOESY spectra). The out-of-plane orientation of the phenyl and naphthyl groups gives a 3-dimensional shape characteristic to the molecules. The lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) level is lower for 1b because of the stronger A strength of b as compared with that of a. The highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) level is as expected less impacted by the change in the A strength leading to a smaller band gap for 1b and to the observed red-shifted absorption as compared with that of 1a. The dimers were selected by taking the minimum energy configuration among those generated by using the module Blends in the Materials Studio package,30 as shown in Fig. 1e, after assigning electrostatic potential (ESP) charges to each molecule by using the module DMol3. The energetically favorable configurations were obtained by using a sampling technique based on the excluded-volume constraints method and the molecular Silverware algorithm.31,32 The polarity in the ground state is slightly higher for 1a and its Î”Î¼ is lower as compared with that of 1b. The two Dâ€“A molecules approach the cyanine limit arbitrary fixed at 2. In our molecular design, thiophene was chosen, in addition to strengthen the electron-donating character of D, because of its rather low aromatic stabilization favoring the zwitterionic structure through charge delocalization.25 The combination with the two respective A units in this study, necessary to tune the maximum absorption in the desired wavelength range, is however not perfect yet to get the compounds with a fully cyanine-like character, especially with b having a larger accepting strength.24 The Î”Î¼ values in Table 1 originates from a larger dipole moment in the excited state than in the ground state. This implies a positive first molecular hyperpolarizability and shows that the two molecules, while approaching the cyanine limit, still possess a rather neutral-like character than a zwitterion-like character.25 In addition, when comparing both molecules, the slightly smaller hole internal reorganization energy of 1a as compared with that of 1b is consistent with the smaller Î”Î¼ value of 1a (Î»i,h = 0.210 eV for 1a, Î»i,h = 0.216 eV for 1b). For the reorganization energy calculation, the geometry optimization of the neutral and cationic states were obtained using the B3LYP hybrid density functional and the 6-31G(d,p) basis set.33 Finally, the electrical dipole moment of the dimer in the x-direction, which approximately corresponds to the main axis of the molecule, nearly vanished for both molecules in the ground state. The magnitudes of the dimer dipole moment reported in Table 1 are similar to the z-component of the vector. The so-designed Dâ€“A molecules, despite not having an exact cyanine-like character, possess the basic characteristics to work as efficient absorbers, in the green in our case, with intense and sharp absorption, as confirmed by Fig. 1c and d (absorption coefficient Î± = 1.29 Ã— 105 cmâˆ’1 at Î»absfilmmax = 515 nm, FWHM = 83 nm for 1a and Î± = 1.38 Ã— 105 cmâˆ’1 at Î»absfilmmax = 547 nm, FWHM = 83 nm for 1b). The more intense absorption of 1b is thus likely induced by the extended conjugation of this molecule having a naphthyl group as an A unit, since its cyanine-like character was less pronounced than 1a. The two respective p-type Dâ€“A molecules were then mixed with C60 as the n-type material via thermal co-evaporation under high vacuum (<10âˆ’7 Torr) to form the bulk heterojunction (BHJ) active layers. C60 was selected as an n-type molecule in this study because of its fairly low absorbance in the visible light spectrum and its high charge carrier mobility in the thin-film state.37 The mixing properties between the respective p-type Dâ€“A molecules and the n-type C60 were then estimated by using the module Blends in the Materials Studio package. Blends is used to predict the thermodynamics of mixing based on the interaction energy of contacting pairs of molecules, which allows the calculation of the mixing energy and the interaction parameter Ï‡ based on an off-lattice generalization of the Floryâ€“Huggins theory.32,38 The Ï‡ parameters were, respectively, 2.23 for the 1a:C60 system and 8.92 for 1b:C60. The larger number for the 1b:C60 blend indicates a stronger predisposition of this system for de-mixing. The respective minimum energy Dâ€“A molecule:C60 configurations obtained after the calculation with the module Blends are represented in Fig. 2a, which shows a slightly more compact structure for 1a:C60. We do not here intend to simulate the representative dimers corresponding to the real morphology of the thin films, but simply to visualize the preferred interactions between the respective Dâ€“A molecules and C60. The morphologies of the blend thin films were further analyzed by atomic force microscopy (AFM) and the height images of 1a:C60 (root mean square (RMS) roughness: 0.45 nm) and 1b:C60 (RMS roughness: 0.60 nm) are represented in Fig. 2b. The morphology of 1b:C60 is coarser, which correlates with the larger Ï‡ parameter of this blend. Despite the inhomogeneous areas, it is not possible to observe phase-separated domains, for both blends. We additionally intended to highlight any possible crystalline areas by X-ray diffraction analysis, but both blends show an amorphous structure. Grazing incidence wide angle X-ray scattering (GIWAXS) analysis was therefore carried out to check the local molecular arrangement in the respective blend films. The GIWAXS spectra in Fig. 2c confirm the rather amorphous characteristics of both blends, but a degree of local arrangement is nonetheless expected, in particular for the BHJ of 1b:C60. A preferred alignment in a direction parallel to the surface is to be observed for this blend. By comparison with the C60 diffraction spectra and the respective single thin films (see Fig. S3 in the ESIâ€ ), it appears that this orientation is rather induced by C60 than by 1b itself. By combining the simulation results, AFM images, and GIWAXS data, the effect of the change of heterocycles a by b is to induce less favorable interactions with C60, leading to the eventual de-mixing with C60 (without phase separation) that can then re-arrange in a locally stacked area. It is likely that the rigid naphthyl moiety contained in the accepting heterocycles of 1b leads to a stronger intermolecular interaction between the neighboring 1b molecules.

The hole and electron-dominant charge-carrier mobilities of the two BHJ blend compositions were then measured (see the Experimental section).39 The results in Fig. 2d and Fig. S4a (see the ESIâ€ ) show higher hole and electron-dominant mobilities for 1b:C60, in particular at the lower electric field in the case of hole mobility. In an OPD made of a BHJ architecture, the efficiency properties depend on the number of charges generated at the interface of the grain boundary composed of p-type and n-type materials, which is defined as the charge separation efficiency (Î·cs), and then to their effective transport to the electrodes, which is defined as the charge collection efficiency (Î·cc). In the case of 1b:C60, a certain level of molecular de-mixing coupled to a better molecular stacking of segregated domains without extended phase separation certainly positively affects the charge-carrier mobilities leading to the higher Î·cs and Î·cc. Finally the absorption profiles for both BHJ blends are represented in Fig. 2e.

To characterize the actual optoelectronic properties of the BHJ blends, inverted-structure green-light-selective OPDs consisting of indium tin oxide (ITO)-coated glass substrates, BHJs of 1a or 1b and C60 (volume ratio, 1:1), a hole-extraction layer of molybdenum oxides (MoOx), and an ITO contact were fabricated (see the Experimental section and Fig. 3a inset).40 Notably, transparent ITO electrodes on both sides of the OPDs were used to evaluate the green-light-selectivity. Representative current densityâ€“voltage (Jâ€“V) curves of the OPDs with 85 nm-thick BHJs and a blend volume ratio of 1:1 under dark conditions are presented in Fig. 3a and the detailed device characteristics are reported in Table 2. The diodes exhibited fairly low DC density (Jd) of 2.77 nA cmâˆ’2 (173 e sâˆ’1 Î¼mâˆ’2) for 1a:C60 and 25.69 nA cmâˆ’2 (1603 e sâˆ’1 Î¼mâˆ’2) for 1b:C60 at a reverse bias of 3 V, which was the operating voltage used in this study. As shown in Fig. 3b, furthermore, the EQE curves of the OPDs driven at the 3 V bias exhibited remarkably high selectivity to the external green light, displaying FWHMs as narrow as 98 nm for 1a:C60 and 101 nm for 1b:C60, which is attributed to the narrow absorption profile of the BHJs formed by 1a or 1b and C60 (Fig. 2e), resulting in strong symbatic responses.41 Plots of the EQE as a function of applied reverse biases are presented in Fig. 3b (right-hand). Rather low EQEs of 14.13% for 1a:C60 and 16.28% for 1b:C60 were obtained without any external reverse bias (0 V), but a drastic increase in efficiency was observed at the reverse bias of 3 V, with the EQEs of 47.48% for 1a:C60 and 51.98% for 1b:C60. These enhanced EQE values upon applied bias are attributed to the electric-field assisted charge-carrier mobilities, which lead to the higher Î·cs and Î·cc, as a result of the minimization of charge recombination loss, as previously described (see Table 2 and Fig. S4b in the ESIâ€  for more details).15,42 Furthermore, the internal quantum efficiency (IQE) was estimated to be as high as 74.19% for 1a:C60 and 78.76% for 1b:C60 at the reverse bias of 3 V based on the calculated absorptance (Î·A) of 0.64 (1a:C60) and 0.66 (1b:C60) of the BHJs. In terms of these device characteristics, which can be described by the expression EQE = Î·A Ã— IQE = Î·A Ã— Î·cs Ã— Î·cc, the main difference between the two p-type Dâ€“A molecules is therefore the higher EQE obtained with 1b:C60 under the same conditions. This better EQE performance is mainly attributed to the higher Î·cs and Î·cc values thanks to the higher charge-carrier mobilities in the 1b:C60 blend, correlated with the larger IQE, along with a slightly higher blend coefficient of absorption (or Î·A). One sees that more pronounced local molecular stacking observed for 1b:C60 is effective in improving the charge-carrier mobilities, for the better IQE and improved final EQE of the device with the higher Î·A. It is also to be highlighted that a high Î”Î¼ value might be an indicator of good IQE in OPDs, as the degree of photo-induced intermolecular charge transfer is related to the difference between the ground and excited state dipole moment.43 This is well consistent with our data, with the higher Î·cs value for the p-type material with the larger Î”Î¼ value. In contrast, the DC increased for this 1b:C60 blend composition, even though the hole-injection barrier of the 1b:C60 blend from the cathode ITO is 0.1 eV higher than that of the 1a:C60,14 which is a negative effect of stacking on OPD properties. Molecular stacking has thus an opposite effect depending on which device property is considered. It is noteworthy that a trade-off would then exist between the IQE and the DC in the field of OPDs.

The device efficiency performance was first optimized by varying the volume ratios of 1b:C60 and the total thicknesses of the BHJ. The optimal structure was found to be a 150 nm-thick BHJ for a 1:1 volume ratio (see Fig. S5 in the ESIâ€  for the efficiency contour plots). As presented in Fig. 4a, the diode exhibited a very low Jd of 1.60 nA cmâˆ’2 (100 e sâˆ’1 Î¼mâˆ’2) at the reverse bias of 3 V and a high injection current of 2.71 Ã— 10âˆ’4 A cmâˆ’2 at a forward bias of âˆ’3 V, leading to a very high rectification ratio of 1.69 Ã— 105 at Â±3 V. The device additionally responded to the external green light (Î»max = 553 nm), and the response behavior of the photocurrent density (Jph) versus the light intensity (I) exhibited good linearity as illustrated in Fig. 4c (right-hand). Conclusion

Two newly designed and synthesized Dâ€“A molecules 1a and 1b approaching the cyanine limit were presented in this study satisfying the requirements as active materials in green-light-selective OPDs, characterized by high selectivities, very low DC densities, and high photo-conversion efficiencies, when mixed with C60. Considering the use of two transparent ITO electrodes on both sides of the OPD composed of the BHJ blend of 1b:C60, the maximum EQE value close to 61% (Î»max = 550 nm) and a DC density below 1.6 nA cmâˆ’2 (or D* = 1.19 Ã— 1013 cm Hz1/2 Wâˆ’1) at an applied reverse bias of 3 V, accompanied by a very distinct green-light-selectivity, an excellent thermal stability, and linear and rapid photo-response characteristics, are among the highest records reported in this field, to date. The green-light-detection ability of the OPD was demonstrated by constructing a hybrid stacked-type full-color photodetector composed of this green-light-selective OPD top layer and a bottom silicon photodiode for the additional detection of blue and red light. To achieve this level of performance, importantly, the first key point in the molecular design was a suitable combination of the D and A units to build a new Dâ€“A molecule approaching a cyanine-like character, characterized by sharp and intense absorption. The second key factor was the choice of the additional functional groups to control the stacking manner of the molecules in the molecular blends with C60, connected to the trade-off-like IQE and DC properties of the OPDs. It appears that in the development of high performing Dâ€“A materials for OPDs, consequently, the choice of the D and A strength combination should be done in parallel with the control of the 3-dimensional configuration of the molecules to optimize the OPD properties. This study gives thus important points to be considered in the design of new Dâ€“A molecules for optoelectronic applications. This study also validates the use of organic pushâ€“pull dye molecules to be incorporated into various optoelectronic devices with high efficiencies, and widens the scope of applications of p-type organic semiconductor materials.

Experimental section

Instrumentation

1H-NMR and 13C-NMR spectra were obtained using a Bruker Avance III 600 (600 MHz) instrument with methylene chloride-d2 (CD2Cl2) and chloroform-d (CDCl3) as the solvents. Mass spectra were recorded using a TOF LC/MS (Shimadzu) in ESI positive mode. Elemental analysis was performed using a PE 2400 Series II analyzer. UV-visible absorption spectra were acquired using a Varian Cary 500 Bio spectrophotometer. The HOMO levels of all organic thin films were measured using an AC-2 photoelectron spectrophotometer (Hitachi High Tech). The LUMO levels were determined from the optical band gap calculated from the edge of the absorption spectra. Absolute PL quantum yields excited at the wavelength of 365 nm were measured using a Quantaurus-QY spectrometer (Hamamatsu C11347). The current densityâ€“voltage (Jâ€“V) characteristics of the OPDs were measured using a Keithley K4200 parameter analyzer. The photocurrent characteristics were evaluated under illumination with a green laser diode (Î»max = 553 nm) at various light intensities in the range of 0.5â€“10 mW cmâˆ’2. EQEs were measured using an instrumental setup illuminated with monochromatic light generated by an ozone-free xenon lamp with a chopper frequency of 30 Hz. The monochromatic light intensity was calibrated using a silicon photodiode (Hamamatsu S1337). The corresponding R (A Wâˆ’1), defined as the ratio of the generated photocurrent (A) to the incident light power (W), was calculated from the EQE using the relation R = EQE/hÎ½, where hÎ½ is the incident photon energy in electron volts (eV). The frequency response was evaluated using a measurement setup consisting of an oscilloscope with a bandwidth of 500 MHz (LeCroy Wavejet 352A), an AFG-2125 function generator (GW Instek), and a green laser diode (Î»max = 553 nm, power = 10 mW cmâˆ’2).

Syntheses of 1a and 1b
