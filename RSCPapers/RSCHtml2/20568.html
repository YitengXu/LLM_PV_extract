Strategies to improve the photocatalytic activity of TiO2: 3D nanostructuring and heterostructuring with graphitic carbon nanomaterials

Abstract

TiO2-based photocatalysis has been considered to be one of the most promising avenues for environmental remediation including water purification. However, several technical issues such as the limited surface area of bulk TiO2, the large band gap of TiO2, and rapid charge recombination still limit the practical application of TiO2 photocatalysts. Therefore, here we focus on two structural design strategies: (i) monolithic three-dimensional (3D) nanostructuring, and (ii) heterostructuring with graphitic carbon nanomaterials. A monolithic 3D nanostructure enables maximal surface area in a given volume and efficient reuse of the photocatalyst without recollection. Heterostructuring with carbon nanomaterials helps achieve maximal utilization of the solar spectrum and charge separation and provides efficient TiO2 photocatalysts. In this review, recent progress on TiO2 photocatalysts toward the abovementioned strategies will be summarized. Further discussion and direction will provide insights into the rational design of highly efficient TiO2 photocatalysts, and help develop advanced photocatalyst models.

1. Introduction

The application of photocatalysts for water purification has received tremendous attention around the world because it potentially uses inexhaustible sunlight, and operates at room temperature without additional power sources. Among the various photocatalytic materials, titanium dioxide (TiO2) is known as the most competitive material due to its excellent photoactivity, high oxidation potential, physical and chemical stability, nontoxicity, and earth abundancy.1–3 In general, photoinduced pollutant decomposition is primarily related to redox reactions on the surface of TiO2. Photon absorption inside and at the surface of TiO2 generates excited electron–hole pairs, which migrate to the surface of the catalyst where they participate in a degradation reaction with the pollutant (eqn (1)). During the reaction, most of the electron–hole pairs undergo recombination within a few nanoseconds, and only a small portion of the charge carriers are involved in the redox reaction (∼10%).1 The generated hole (h+) reacts with the surface-trapped O2 or hydroxyl group (–OH) to produce a hydroxyl radical, which is a strong oxidizing agent for most organic/inorganic molecules, as indicated in the following equations (eqn (2), (3) and (6)). The generated electron (e−) participates in forming a superoxide anion radical, which is finally transformed to the hydroxyl radical (eqn (4) and (5)).4

A technical issue that is directly related to photocatalytic efficiency is increasing the surface area of TiO2. This is because the photodegradation of the pollutants entirely depends on the oxidation–reduction reaction occurring on the TiO2 surface, as described above. To increase the surface area, many researchers have developed various synthesis methods to produce TiO2-based nanoparticles,5 nanorods,6,7 and nanoflakes.8,9 These dispersible nanoparticulate TiO2 have shown high photocatalytic efficiency due to their large surface area, but they are difficult to recycle after use, which causes problems of secondary contamination and operational expense. TiO2 3D nanostructuring in a monolithic network can achieve both recyclability and high photocatalytic activity derived from porous network which ensures an effective mass transportation for various reactions and productions, a strong photon trapping ability, and an enlarging specific surface area. For example, Ahn's study reported that monolithic porous TiO2 structure shows almost 3.5 times higher photocatalytic performance than non-porous TiO2 thin film, which is originated from the merits of porous structure.33 E. Eftekhari et al. showed that well-designed high ordered porous structure can maximize light absorbance efficiency by slow photon effect, and it led high photocatalytic efficiency without chemical modification.38 However, the generation methods, such as electrospinning,10 3D printing,11,12 and Ti anodization,13,14 are limited in terms of reproducibility, high throughput processing, simplicity, and precise morphology control over a large area.

Another issue is that the generation of electron–hole pairs is only realized by absorption within the ultraviolet (UV) range (∼5% of the solar spectrum) due to the large band gap depending on the polymorphs of TiO2 (>3.0 eV for rutile, >3.2 eV for anatase, >3.13 eV for brookite),15,16 which means that much of the sunlight cannot be used for a photocatalytic reaction. Many researchers have solved this problem by lowering the band gap by doping pure TiO2 with an impurity element (N, S, Fe, etc.).17,18 Absorption from the additional energy states generated by doping can enhance the activity of TiO2via visible light, while intermediate energy states from the introduced atoms and defects can serve as traps for the electron–hole recombination site that degrades the photocatalytic efficiency.19–21 Instead of lowering the band gap of TiO2 through doping, a strategy to physically or chemically attach a heterogeneous material (called a photosensitizer) on TiO2 can enhance the absorption of visible light in the TiO2 matrix in a simple and more flexible way. For the sensitization of TiO2, typical photocatalytic materials such as CdS and WO3 have been mixed with to TiO2 to change the band gap and induce light activation via visible light or to transfer photoactivated electrons using a photosensitizer that absorbs visible light.22 However, these photosensitizers are limited in terms of their toxicity, water-solubility, and high cost.23,24 Therefore, an important issue in this strategy is the selection of inexpensive and nontoxic materials that can achieve efficient electron transfer between the photosensitizer and the TiO2 matrix without electron–hole recombination.

2.1. 3D anodic aluminum oxide (AAO) template

After H. Masuda developed two-step replication mold processes that provide highly ordered porous AAO sheets,49 AAO has become a very versatile template material hitherto. A large number of papers related to AAO-assisted nanostructured TiO2 have been published in a variety of research fields in the form of two-dimensional (2D) nanotube arrays.50–53 Researchers have struggled to extend the complexity of the structure from a 2D AAO array to a 3D anisotropic structure.54,55 A clever strategy to attain a 3D AAO template was proposed by D. Wang et al., which utilized microstructured Al foils.35 Highly pure Al foil was chemically etched in a mixed solution of CuCl2 and HCl to form microstructured Al (Fig. 2d), followed by a two-step anodization process, resulting in a 3D hierarchical nanoporous alumina template as shown in Fig. 2a. Microstructured Al foil shows unusual phenomena during pore generation. An SEM image (Fig. 2a(iii)) demonstrated that the nanopores formed vertically to all the exposed Al facets in stark contrast to the conventional AAO structure. Additionally, the growth of branched and bended nanopores was observed near the concave and convex edges, respectively (Fig. 2a(iv–v)). This unconventional pore growth is due to the abnormal electric field distribution and internal stresses of microstructured Al. An additional result of microstructuring is that the growing AAO pores undergo noticeably reduced breakdown failure which indicates the destruction of the AAO sheet due to increasing local current flow during the anodization process. Using the 3D AAO template, hierarchically ordered 3D TiO2 nanotube arrays were generated via atomic layer deposition (ALD), followed by etching and annealing processes (Fig. 2b). Fig. 2c verifies the superior photoinduced MB degradation performance of the as-prepared hierarchical 3D TiO2 compared to the 2D TiO2 nanotube arrays fabricated by a conventional AAO template under UV illumination. The excellent performance is definitely attributed to the hierarchical 3D nanostructure. This method makes it possible to reduce the Al sheet anodizing time and its breakdown, but the difficulty in controlling the pore arrangement and the partially non-anodized Al problem should be solved.

2.2. Macro/mesoporous self-assembled opal structure

Another method to obtain monolithic TiO2 3D nanostructures is the self-assembly of polymeric spheres. Compared to AAO, self-assemblies are a faster and more scalable method, and its high-ordered structure has already been lively studied in photonics,56,57 a battery,58 a catalyst,59 and other applications.60 In particular, in terms of photocatalysts, self-assemblies are favorable for producing macro/mesoporous inverse opal structures that show higher photocatalytic activity due to a large surface area from the mesoporosity and light trapping and mass accessibility from macroporosity.34,61–63 These multiporous nanostructures can be obtained easily by using a surfactant-dissolved sol–gel process. For instance, Y. Fu et al. infiltrated triblock copolymer (P123)-dispersed titanium precursor solution into the interstitial sites of an assembled polystyrene sphere opal structure to build an ordered macro/mesoporous TiO2 inverse opal structure and confirmed its high photocatalytic performance.64 A more advanced approach was introduced by L. N. Quan et al. (Fig. 3a).34 They utilized P123 not only as a molder for formation of a mesoporous structure but also as a carbon source, creating a visible light sensitive TiO2 photocatalyst with improved performance. Although the additional UV stabilization and carbonization steps (under Ar gas, 600 °C) led to very thin, shrunk inverse opal structure with a reduced surface area (mTiO2: 114.4 m2 g−1, mcTiO2: 75.1 m2 g−1, Fig. 3b), superior photocatalytic performance compared to surfactant-free, and noncarbonization inverse opal structures was obtained under either UV and visible light irradiation (Fig. 3d). They described that the carbonized polymeric soft template produces additional electronic states above the valence band (VB) of TiO2 which contribute to decreasing the band gap of TiO2 (Eg = 2.9 eV, Fig. 3c). Moreover, converted carbon moieties may act as a channel to extract electrons from the conduction band (CB) of TiO2, thereby decreasing the rate of recombination.

2.3. Interference lithography – proximity field nanopatterning

Two preparation methods for 3D nanostructured template have been introduced thus far. However, these techniques are unfavorable in terms of reproducibility and the formation of a uniform 3D nanostructure over a large region in a short time. Photolithography, is a good alternative for efficient preparation of 3D nano-scale templates. Lithographic processes can fabricate a well-defined template in a relatively short time (>100 min) regardless of the fabrication volume,33 which is better than that for other templates (AAO: 3–30 h, PS self-assembly: 4–6 h).34,35,65 For three-dimensional photolithography, several studies have been conducted with accompanying tools such as an elaborately calculated phase mask,66–69 truncated pyramidal prism,70 direct ink-writing,12 and two-photon lithography.71 In particular, a technique called proximity field nanopatterning (PnP) can acquire large-sized (>4 × 4 inch2) and highly ordered monolithic 3D nanostructures through a single exposure of light using an elastomeric phase mask. The PnP technique is fundamentally based on a self-imaging phenomenon called the Talbot effect. When the ordered patterns of the phase mask have a periodicity that corresponds to the wavelength of the incident light, diffraction beams are generated after light passes through the phase mask. As diffraction beams propagate, ordered interference patterns occur as well as formation of repeated self-images in the normal direction of the phase mask. The ordered interference patterns are transferred on a negative-tone photoresist (SU-8) film by irradiation with a planar laser beam through the phase mask, which is tightly contacted to the spin-coated photoresist film. The alternating light intensity of the interference patterns generates a highly ordered 3D continuous nanostructure after subsequent post-exposure baking and developing steps. The reproducibility and scalability of the PnP technique has already been applied for various applications, such as an energy device,72,73 a catalyst,74 a sensor,75,76 a photonic devices,76 and nanocomposites.77 More technical details of the PnP process can be found in previous studies.66
2.3.1. 3D TiO2 nanoshell structure. The 3D polymeric templates prepared by the PnP technique possess a distinctive body centered tetragonal (BCT) unit cell as described in Fig. 4a. This is a representative feature of the Talbot effect, where a phase-shifted self-image appears in the middle of two repeated self-images. C. Ahn et al. attempted to deposit titania nanoshells uniformly on this polymeric 3D structure (1 × 1 inch2) by ALD (Fig. 4) to obtain monolithically nanostructured 3D TiO2.33 The as-prepared polymeric 3D nanostructure is likely to lose its shape due to a collapse of the structure during TiO2 deposition at high temperatures (>100 °C). In this regard, deposition through ALD was conducted at 80 °C, and operated with at least 300 cycles (23 nm) to sustain the structure after the template removal (Fig. 4b). After TiO2 deposition by the ALD process, a two-step calcination process was performed. It consisted of a preannealing (350 °C, 5 h) step, which was essential for densification of the deposited titania to avoid structural collapse at a high temperature (500 °C), and a calcination (500 °C, 2 h) step, where the template removal and crystallization of the deposited TiO2 shell (to the anatase phase) occurred simultaneously. Fig. 4c represents the photocatalytic performance of the 3D TiO2 nanoshell structure. The result exhibited comparable performance with a film consisting of P25 nanoparticles, although the surface area of a P25 film might be higher than that of nanostructured TiO2 for the same film thickness. Well-arranged submicron pores enhance the accessibility of the reactants unlike the P25 film, whose random pores limit the accessibility of the reactants. Moreover, since the 3D TiO2 nanoshell had a monolithic form, recyclable use of the nanoshell was achieved without recollection process and performance degradation (Fig. 4d).

2.3.2. N-Doped 3D TiO2 nanoshell structure. The aforementioned monolithic 3D nanoshell structure ensures reusability and superior performance for photocatalysis in water. However, the intrinsic limitation of TiO2, which is only sensitive to UV light irradiation, remains a critical issue. Recently, S. Cho et al. proposed a new approach to make visible-light sensitive 3D structured N-doped TiO2 by ALD. The conversion of TiO2 into N-doped TiO2 was achieved through thermal treatment of TiO2 under a nitrogen-abundant atmosphere.78–80 However, the harsh conditions of toxic gases (e.g., NH3, NO, etc.) and high temperatures (<800 °C) to introduce N atoms into TiO278 are not industry-friendly, making preservation of the 3D nanostructure without collapse difficult. Therefore, direct conversion from TiN to an N-doped 3D TiO2 nanoshell was attempted in an atmospheric environment. Subsequent TiN deposition via ALD onto the 3D TiO2 nanoshell structure, which was prepared in the aforementioned manner, formed a sandwich shell structure with a total thickness of 40 nm (Fig. 5a). The conversion of the deposited TiN into N-doped TiO2 was carried out under an atmospheric environment at 550 °C.81 The fabricated N-doped 3D TiO2 nanoshell structure still maintained high porosity (∼90% air) without structural breakdown (Fig. 5b–d) and preserved its recyclability in photocatalysis. An optical investigation (Fig. 5e) revealed redshifted absorbance spectra derived from the introduced N 2p energy state, along with a reduced band gap (Eg = 2.75 eV). The MB decomposition performance of the 3D N-doped TiO2 nanostructure was 134% higher than that of an unstructured N-doped TiO2 film and 33% higher than that of an undoped 3D TiO2 nanostructure under solar irradiation (Fig. 5f and g). This result shows the improved photocatalytic performance through the synergetic effects of nanostructuring and doping.

Nanostructuring with a monolithic form assisted by a sacrificial template is an effective and practical way to obtain a scalable TiO2 photocatalyst. Besides its improved photoactivity due to a large surface area, it is also possible to overcome the limited visible light absorbance of TiO2 by doping of the nanostructure. Further, limitations such as control over the pore arrangement in AAO template, process complexity of a doped nanostructure, and hindered charge separation due to the introduction of intermediate energy sites from dopants should be solved for practical applications in real systems.

3. Heterostructuring with graphitic carbon nanomaterials

Constructing a heterostructure consisting of TiO2 and other photoactive materials can address the recombination issue of photoexcited charge carriers, which is the biggest problem associated with the visible light activation method by introducing dopant or defect states within the TiO2 band gap.20,21 A heterostructure with an optimal band structure can accommodate the photoinduced charge carriers of TiO2, and further visible light activation can also be achieved when using a material that absorbs visible light, resulting in the simultaneous enhancement of the photoactivities and charge carrier separation of TiO2.82,83

In recent years, the hybridization of TiO2 with graphitic carbon nanomaterials has been actively studied to solve the drawbacks of common sensitizers (high cost and toxicity) and to improve the photocatalytic reactivity of TiO2.84,85 Since graphitic carbon nanomaterials have numerous advantages, including great electrochemical properties, high electrical conductivity, simple synthetic methods, and earth abundancy (low cost), they have been widely used in various applications.86,87 Additionally, because graphitic carbon nanomaterials have various shapes and structures, excellent electrical properties and unique electronic structural characteristics, TiO2 hybridized with graphitic carbon nanomaterials is a desirable composite material capable of improving photocatalytic reactivity.88

Considering the basic mechanism of the photocatalytic reaction, the adsorption of the reaction material should occur first, and further light absorption and charge separation must occur to achieve efficient photocatalytic performance.89 Numerous studies have been reported on TiO2 photocatalysts with graphitic carbon nanomaterials, which can be broadly classified into three types along with their roles in the heterostructure: graphitic carbon nanomaterials as an electron acceptor (electron reservoir),90–97 a dopant (for activating visible absorption),91,98,99 and a visible light sensitizer.100–102 In this section, the role of various graphitic carbon nanomaterials, including carbon nanotubes (CNTs), graphene, and graphene quantum dots (GQDs), in enhancing photocatalytic performance is discussed with underlying mechanisms.

3.1. Carbon nanotubes (CNTs)

Among the various carbon nanomaterials, CNTs and graphene have advantages such as high specific surface area, excellent mechanical properties, efficient cost and excellent electrical properties, and simple surface chemical modification to form a chemical bonding or van der Waals interaction with TiO2 (Fig. 6a). CNTs, consisting of graphitic sp2 carbons, can have either metallic and semiconducting properties depending on their chirality.103 Therefore, they are available in the form of both sensitizers that participate in light absorption and electron acceptors (reservoirs) that accept the excited electrons of TiO2 when forming heterostructures with TiO2.

Woan et al. described three mechanisms for improving the photocatalytic mechanism of CNTs and TiO2 complexes based on the existing literature.98 Three mechanisms have been discussed to explain the improvement of the photocatalytic properties of conventional CNT–TiO2 composites. The first mechanism, involving metallic CNTs, was proposed by Hoffmann et al., where a high-energy excited photon excites electrons from the valence band into the conduction band of anatase TiO2.104 Photogenerated electrons in the space charge region are transferred to the CNTs, and holes remain in the TiO2 to participate in the redox reaction (Fig. 6b(i)). However, the application of metallic CNTs as an electron reservoir increases the efficiency of charge transfer but makes it difficult to efficiently utilize the irradiated light. The second mechanism introduced by Wang et al.105 suggested employing semiconducting CNTs as a sensitizer to transfers electrons to TiO2. Photogenerated electrons are injected into the conduction band of TiO2 to form a superoxide radical by adsorbed molecular oxygen. When this happens, positively charged nanotubes leave holes in the valence band of TiO2 by removing electrons. This positively charged TiO2 reacts with the adsorbed water to form hydroxyl radicals (Fig. 6b(ii)). Unlike these two mechanisms, the use of chemically bonded CNT–TiO2 to form nanocomposites involves a relatively more complex process.106 Ti–O–C bonding in the CNT–TiO2 complex forms carbon dopant states within the TiO2 band gap, which enables the absorption of longer wavelengths due to the formation of additional interband states of carbon, thereby improving the photocatalytic activity (Fig. 6b(iii)).

In 2011, Cong et al. reported multiple roles of CNTs in CNT/TiO2 heterostructures based on the potential of these mechanisms.91 TiO2-coated multiwalled CNTs (MWCNTs) were prepared by the oxidation of TiC-coated MWCNTs, and their photocatalytic properties were observed. First, the strong pollutant adsorption effect of MWCNTs without irradiation was confirmed by observing that a mixture of TiO2 nanofibers and MWCNTs had a much higher MB decoloration rate than pristine TiO2 nanofibers. Second, the carbon-doped TiO2 coating with MWCNTs exhibited a better decoloration rate than the simply mixed TiO2–MWCNTs, indicating that the chemical bonds between TiO2 and MWCNTs play an important role in improving the photocatalytic performance under visible light irradiation. Furthermore, MWCNTs with Fermi levels lower than the CB of TiO2 help to separate electrons by accepting the photogenerated electrons.107 As a result, CNTs can not only help to adsorb pollutants but also act as an electron acceptor and a dopant when CNTs are chemically bonded with TiO2 to form a heterostructure.

3.2. Graphene and graphene oxide (GO)

Similar to CNTs, graphene and graphene oxide have also been reported to increase the photocatalytic performance of TiO2. Unlike CNTs, which have different characteristics according to their chiral vectors, graphene is basically semimetallic but has a band gap in the case of graphene oxide.108 Therefore, graphene and graphene oxides (even reduced graphene oxide) can play a role similar to CNTs when they form heterostructures with TiO2.

In 2012, Kim et al. fabricated TiO2-attached graphene/carbon composite nanofibers (TiO2-CCNFs) and showed their photocatalytic degradation performance under visible light irradiation.92 For the fabrication of TiO2-CCNFs, CCNFs prepared via electrospinning using a homogeneous solution of PAN and graphene were immersed in a titanium n-butoxide (Ti(OnBu)4) solution, followed by heat treatment and carbonization. The carboxyl groups present in graphene enabled chemical bonding with TiO2 particles, allowing the TiO2 nanoparticles to be uniformly distributed without aggregation (Fig. 7a(i)). The TiO2 distributed in the as-prepared TiO2-CCNFs showed a mixed phase of anatase and rutile (Fig. 7a(ii)). The photocatalytic properties of the TiO2-CCNFs were confirmed by a simple dye degradation test, and the degradation performance of the TiO2-CCNFs was better (k = 0.16 min−1) than that of TiO2-carbon nanofibers (TiO2-CNFs, k = 0.012 min−1) without graphene. They explained that the origin of the improved photocatalytic performance is attributed to the graphene in TiO2-CCNFs serving as an electron acceptor (reservoir) for rutile, which promotes charge separation. On the other hand, in anatase TiO2, excited electrons generated by light absorption in graphene are injected into the CB of TiO2, indicating that graphene acts as a photosensitizer (Fig. 7b). Obviously, it has been considered to be predominant that graphene, GO, or reduced GO (rGO) act as an electron acceptor rather than a visible sensitizer when they form a heterostructure with TiO2, which has been experimentally proven by an electron paramagnetic study (EPR).95 However, there have also been a number of reports showing the contribution of the graphene-based materials to the visible light activation.

Lee et al. reported the importance of the interaction between Ti atoms and C atoms in the visible light activity of TiO2 by red-shifting (2.80 eV) the absorbance tail of GO-wrapped amorphous TiO2 through one-step hydrothermal treatment for simultaneous GO reduction and TiO2 crystallization (Fig. 7c and d). This is much higher band gap reduction than the case of GO-wrapping and reduction of the anatase TiO2 pre-crystallized by a two-step method (3.19 eV). During the one-step hydrothermal treatment, the Ti atoms are rearranged during the conversion of TiO2 to amorphous anatase, and at the same time the GO is reduced and unpaired electrons are formed. In this process, the favourable interaction of the unpaired electrons and Ti atoms decreases the conduction band level of TiO2 as like a doping effect, resulting in decrease of the band gap. As a result, the conduction band of TiO2, which is originally higher than the excited level of MB, is lowered, so that the excited electrons of MB flow efficiently through the graphene to the TiO2 conduction band (Fig. 7e).109

Similarly, Qiu et al. also introduced the importance of the interaction between TiO2 and GO in the visible light activity of TiO2 through the self doping effect of Ti3+. They prepared Ti3+-self-doped TiO2-graphene by one-step vacuum activation technology and found that visible light activation was enabled by the Ti3+ self-doping effect, the resulting surface bonds between TiO2 and graphene oxide (GO, simultaneous loading of TiO2 nanoparticles onto a graphene surface), and a reduction of GO.99 As shown in Fig. 7f, GO is cleaved into small-sized graphene during the vacuum activation process, and the decreased graphene size facilitates the interaction between the TiO2 nanoparticles and graphene, thereby improving the photocatalytic efficiency. The smaller the size of graphene is, the more edges that can bond with TiO2, and the photoexcited electrons generated from TiO2 are more easily transferred to graphene. Moreover, as GO is reduced to graphene, Ti–O–C bonding is formed, which forms self-doping of Ti3+, resulting in a shallow trap inside the band gap (Fig. 7g).110,111 From the formation of surface shallow defect states by the introduction of Ti–O–C bonding, the band gap energy of P25 (3.03 eV) slightly reduced to 2.98 eV. As a result, the visible light activity was improved by the dopant effect. Furthermore, they demonstrated that graphene plays a major role in the adsorption of contaminants by confirming that the amount of methyl orange (MO) adsorption increases with increasing graphene content.112 These results show that graphene contributes to the adsorption of pollutants and to charge acceptor and visible light activation, similar to CNTs. However, because graphene and CNTs have a very small or no band gap, their visible light activation is based on C doping rather than the direct absorption of visible light. Thus, their visible light activation mechanisms are limited by defect states that can adversely affect efficient charge separation. In addition to the aforementioned studies, it has been confirmed in various previous studies that the visible light activity of TiO2 photocatalysts increases when TiO2 has a heterogeneous structure with CNTs or graphene. However, since CNT and graphene do not have a distinct and wide band gap and because contribute to the performance improvement, it has been difficult to clearly identify the enhancement in visible light absorption by CNT and graphene.

3.3. Graphene quantum dots (GQDs) and carbon quantum dots (CQDs)

In contrast to various graphitic materials and large-sized graphene, carbon quantum dots (CQDs, or carbon nanodots (CNDs)) and graphene quantum dots (GQDs, or graphene nanodots (GNDs)) have a relatively large band gap100,113–115 that can improve the photocatalytic performance of TiO2 in a different way from the two main mechanisms (electron reservoir and doping) of graphene and CNTs.91,92,98 Due to a number of advantages of GQDs and CQDs including chemical, physical and optical tunability,116 abundant edge site,114 low cytotoxicity117,118 and chemical stability, they have been considered as desirable materials for various catalytic applications such as selective oxidation and reduction,119 water splitting, hydrogen and oxygen evolution, and pollutant degradation.120 Among various benefits which can be taken from GQDs and GQDs in photocatalytic reaction, the most efficient point is obviously the visible light activation by their band gap. The possible methods of improving the light absorption or activity with GQDs and CQDs can be described in two mechanisms: (i) sensitizing with heterogeneous materials such as dye or semiconductor quantum dots (QDs) with a narrower band gap121 and (ii) obtaining a new absorption band through interfacial charge transfer (IFCT) or ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) by forming a charge transfer complex with surface adsorbates.22 These two methods are mainly achieved by heterostructure formation through the hybridization of TiO2 with a heterogeneous material, and the mechanism of enhanced photoactivity changes depending on the properties of the heterogeneous material or binding geometry.22 Due to their relatively large band gap, GQDs and CQDs can enhance the photocatalytic activity of TiO2 by absorption alone, compared to semiconducting materials (CdS, CdSe, WO3, etc.)122–124 that have been commonly used as sensitizers of TiO2 photocatalysts. In addition, they mostly consist of sp2 carbon, unlike inorganic semiconducting QDs, and the formation of a charge transfer complex with TiO2 can also be obtained, which may result in more efficient absorption and charge separation.
3.3.1. High-oxidized CQDs and GQDs as sensitizers (charge injection). Recently, a number of studies on the application of GQDs and CQDs to catalysts have been reported.84,100,102,125 In the case of a GQD/TiO2 or CQD/TiO2 complex prepared by physical adsorption, the efficiency of photocatalytic decomposition increases mainly due to the efficient adsorption of organic pollutants onto the GQDs or CQDs. However, it has been reported that when there is strong chemical interaction between GQDs or CQDs and a semiconductor such as TiO2, the photocatalytic activity can be further enhanced by stabilizing the complex and making the charge transfer more efficient.126,127

Yu et al. tested the photocatalytic performance of a CQD/P25 complex under UV-visible and visible (>450 nm) light with a CQD/P25 complex of the “dyade” structure, where the P25 surface was modified with a CQD, and obtained improved light absorption and performance (Fig. 8a and b). The authors explained that this improved photocatalytic performance was achieved by the dual role of the CQD in TiO2. Under UV-visible light irradiation, the CQD acts as an electronic reservoir as in graphene and CNTs to improve the efficient separation of the photoinduced electron–hole pairs of P25. However, under visible light irradiation, CQDs act as a photosensitizer, such as an organic dye, forming a visible light responsive “dyade” structure of the CQD to P25 (Fig. 8c). After formation of the dyade structure, absorption, which was not observed in pristine P25, was observed in the range of 410–600 nm. This was due to the absorption of the CQD with a relatively small band gap (<2.5 eV). As a result, the excited electrons of the CQD formed by visible absorption became available for injection into P25 to enable sensitization.128

Similar to CQDs, GQDs have a band gap, can provide many active sites due to their small size and two-dimensional disc-like structure and can suppress electron–hole recombination, which is one of the strategies used to improve photocatalytic reactivity. Thus, GQDs are a candidate for improving both photoactivity and active sites.129,130 In 2016, Sudhagar et al. confirmed the optical carrier accumulation and recombination process after forming a heterostructure by surface modification of the GQDs (lateral size of 2 nm) with TiO2 hollow nanowires (HNWs, length 3 mm, diameter 100–250 nm) by electrostatic attraction (Fig. 8d).101 The light scattering of TiO2 increased by using the hollow structure of TiO2, and at the same time, the light utilization was improved by the adsorbing GQD (Fig. 8d). As shown in Fig. 8e, TiO2 HNWs exhibit enhanced absorption of UV and visible light after GQD adsorption due to the long tail of the absorption band of the GQD (∼600 nm), which emits yellow photoluminescence by its high oxidation state.131 In addition, the band alignment of TiO2 and the GQD in the TiO2 HNW-GQD heterostructure resulted in photogenerated holes in the GQD that could be injected into a solution for water oxidation, as well as in holes in TiO2, and photogenerated electrons of GQD that could be injected into TiO2 to enhance the photocatalytic reaction efficiency. This shows that a physically adsorbed GQD (or CQD), unlike the previously described chemically bonded CQD/P25, can also be used as a sensitizer for TiO2. In the case of graphitic materials such as GQDs and CQDs applied to TiO2 as shown above, the most common method of making GQDs and CQDs is based on a harsh reaction of strong acids and graphitic materials. Therefore, it is easy for GQDs or CQDs to possess defect or oxygen functional groups. Accordingly, the synthesized GQDs or CQDs have a relatively narrow band gap, and the increase in the visible light absorption of TiO2 is caused by a number of defective states generated during the synthesis of the GQDs (CQDs).100,125 Moreover, their absorption in the narrow band gap region due to their high oxidation degree generally corresponds to the band tail, so the increase in absorption increase is not noticeable.

3.3.2. Charge transfer complex with low-oxidized GQDs (direct charge transfer). Another way to increase the light absorption of TiO2 is to from a charge transfer complex. When a transition metal or transition metal oxide with a high oxidation state, such as TiO2, is chemically bonded with an organic molecule whose HOMO/LUMO levels are higher than the VB/CB of the transition metal or its oxide, direct charge transfer from the organic molecule to the metal region can be achieved. Therefore, additional absorption occurs, corresponding to the energy transferred from the HOMO level to the CB, resulting in additional absorption in the non-band gap region of each material. Because GQDs have the characteristics of dye molecules capable of absorbing and emitting light and can also be considered an organic material such as catechol or ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), which are representative adsorbates,22,132 it can be expected that both approaches (sensitization and direct charge transfer) would be possible by controlling the binding geometry between the GQD and TiO2.

Accordingly, if GQDs with broad and discrete band gaps with a HOMO level slightly higher than the VB of TiO2 are applied, it can be expected that additional absorption by charge transfer will occur in the UV-visible region.

Our group found that low-oxidized GQDs obtained from graphite intercalation compounds (GICs) undergo mild oxidation, unlike with conventional GQD fabrication methods using strong acids, which result in low defect levels and discrete band gaps.32,100,115 In addition, GQDs with such a discrete band gap unusually increase TiO2 light absorption by forming a GQD/TiO2 complex by chemically modifying TiO2 nanoparticles (NPs).32 A low-oxidized GQD was chemically bonded to the surface of TiO2 NPs through a hydrothermal process to facilitate direct charge transfer absorption of the GQD/TiO2 heterostructure (Fig. 9a and b). The chemically bonded GQD/TiO2 complex exhibited a remarkable absorption enhancement in both the UV and visible regions even with only a very small amount of GQDs (optical density ∼283% and extinction coefficient ∼243% at the absorption maximum). The large increase in the absorption of TiO2 was attributed to the fact that the photogenerated electron of the GQD was directly transferred to the CB of TiO2 by orbital coupling derived from the strong interaction between TiO2 and the low-oxidized GQD with a discrete band gap. Additionally, by comparing various oxidized graphitic materials, such as GO and GOQD, it was confirmed that only mildly oxidized GQDs with a discrete band gap satisfy the requirement for the direct charge transfer phenomenon. In addition to strong absorption, it is expected that the charge transfer absorption phenomenon could effectively separate the photoinduced e–h pairs of the GQD and TiO2 into holes toward the GQD and electrons toward TiO2. Both the high-oxidized GQD or CQD and low-oxidized GQD play roles in visible light activation and charge separation, but unlike the low absorption by the band tail in high-oxidized GQD or CQD, in the case of low-oxidized GQD, absorption by direct transfer from the GQD to TiO2 makes it possible to increase the photoactivity in the entire UV-visible region (250–600 nm) more efficiently. As a result, the practical applicability of GQDs as a photocatalytic material was proven by the pollutant adsorption capacity of a GQD attached to a TiO2 surface, with dramatically improved absorption in the entire UV-visible region (Fig. 9c–f).

4. Summary and outlook

In this review, we presented two practical and feasible strategies for overcoming drawbacks originating from the intrinsic properties of TiO2 and improving efficiency in a cost-effective and an eco-friendly manner. Template-assisted approaches for 3D nanostructuring are favorable routes for fabricating a highly ordered nanostructure in a monolithic network. The monolithic 3D TiO2 photocatalyst prepared by a 3D AAO template and the soft template incorporated opal structure show superior pollutant degradation performance compared to 2D AAO and the conventional inverse opal structure, respectively. The PnP technique makes it possible to fabricate a large-size uniform monolithic 3D nanostructure in a simpler and more rapid way. A monolithic 3D TiO2 nanostructure provides a large surface area and maintains its performance after several reuses without a recollection process. Additionally, a visible light sensitive photocatalyst has been achieved through a nitrogen-doped monolithic 3D TiO2 structure prepared by oxidizing the deposited TiN.

We also discussed TiO2 photocatalysts using various carbon materials (mainly graphene nanocarbons) including graphene, CNTs, and GQDs, and their underlying mechanisms and advantages as photocatalytic materials. Graphitic carbon nanomaterials have been physically or chemically decorated onto TiO2 or have been used to form a composites, and the photocatalytic performance has been improved by four mechanisms largely depending on their structure, geometry, and bonding nature. Graphitic carbon nanomaterials as electron reservoirs can efficiently collect the excited electrons of TiO2, however they cannot contribute to the visible light response. Chemically bonded graphitic carbon nanomaterials with TiO2 forming Ti–O–C bonds can contribute to the visible light response of TiO2 by introducing intermediate states within the TiO2 band gap, but the intermediate states are likely to act as charge recombination sites. However, when graphitic carbon nanomaterials with the band gap function as a photosensitizers in TiO2 or charge transfer complexes with TiO2, it is possible to improve the visible light reactivity and efficient charge separation simultaneously.

With the advantages of monolithic nanostructuring and heterostructuring based on graphitic carbon nanomaterials, a significant enhancement in photocatalytic performance can be achieved through a synergetic effect of the two main strategies. Nevertheless, the practical application of nanostructured and heterostructured TiO2 in actual industry still a challenge, with limitations associated with scale and mass production. However, the suggested novel approaches in this review are expected to provide a direction for the synthesis of advanced and practical TiO2 photocatalysts.
