Abstract

Two-dimensional layered materials have been universally acknowledged to be promising candidates for alternative precious metals in the field of catalysis. The crystal-facet effect is currently rare in the field of electrocatalysis and a deep understanding of the catalytic mechanism of bimetallic highly active crystal facets is critical for fabricating high-performance catalysts, but details are still lacking. Herein, we demonstrated a solvent-induced strategy to manufacture well-defined structured CoNi-layered double hydroxides (LHDs) for highly efficient water electrolysis, and gave a detailed mechanism of catalytic activity towards an enhanced OER. Such considerable improvement in catalytic activity was attributed not only to the synergistic catalytic effect of bimetallic hydroxides, but also to the crystal-facet effect, which is caused by the high density of unsaturated-coordination of surface metal atoms. Consequently, the adsorption energy was enhanced and the activation energy of the target reaction was lowered. These unsaturated coordination surface or edge metal atoms can be easily oxidized to form a great amount of active oxyhydroxides, such as Î²-NiOOH intermediates. In addition, the adjacent Co sites can also regulate the redox reaction kinetics of Ni(OH)2/NiOOH and synergistically catalyze the OER.

Introduction

Electrochemical water splitting to produce hydrogen fuel, powered by green energy sources such as wind, tide and solar, is one of the most practical alternatives for a clean energy cycle. Hence, the existing issues of the sluggish kinetics of the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) are being addressed by rational design and synthesis of highly efficient catalysts.1â€“3 Noble metal-based catalysts possess high intrinsic catalytic activity and can efficiently reduce the overpotential of water splitting; however, their scarcity with high cost limits their wide application. Hence, developing non-precious-metal-based catalysts is urgently needed.4

Layered double hydroxides (LDHs) are widely used in the fields of electrocatalysis5,6 and electrochemical energy storage,7,8 because of their advantages, such as a high specific surface area, feasibility of tailoring the electronic/physical structure and combination with other functional materials.9,10 Although a tremendous amount of protocols, such as engineering defects,11â€“13 generating vacancies,14â€“16 creating lattice strain,17â€“19 hybridizing a multi-metallic and/or nonmetallic interface,9,13â€“16etc., has been applied to further develop their intrinsic activity, engineering high-energy crystal facets to regulate the catalytic activity for water splitting is less investigated.20,21 Most of them showed an outstanding catalytic performance in the fields of photocatalysis,22â€“25 photoelectrocatalysis26â€“28 and traditional thermocatalysis.29,30 Regarding the electrocatalysis, Nakamura et al. demonstrated the correlation between the stability of the intermediate species and crystal facets by monitoring the surface intermediates of the OER on rutile MnO2 with different facet orientations.31 Carter et al. surveyed the OER activities of Î²-NiOOH with the crystallographic facets of {0001} and {100}, where six-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated and five-fold-lattice-oxygen-coordinated Ni sites were exposed, respectively, using hybrid density functional theory,32 and discovered that each crystallographic facet exhibited remarkably different electrocatalytic activities towards the OER. Therefore, tailoring highly-active crystal facets is another efficient protocol to optimize the electrocatalytic performance. In addition, most of these facet-engineering approaches are energy-extensive and uneconomical, as they use metal organic framework (MOF) materials as precursors,21,33 controlled atmosphere high temperature sintering,34â€“37 chemical vapor deposition (CVD),26,38etc. which make the realization of large-scale production difficult.

Herein, we demonstrated a facile ethanol-induced strategy to synthesize a micro-honeycomb-sphered CoNi-based LDH [Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-E] with high-index-exposed {003} facets for efficient overall water splitting, whereas CoNi LDHs consisting of nanorods obtained in water exhibit unsatisfactory catalytic ability. The Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-E presents abundant unsaturated coordination centers on both the surface and edge, promoting the formation of an active oxyhydroxide intermediate for the OER. In addition, a synergistic catalytic effect of Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-E was also observed, as the transformation from Ni(OH)2 to active NiOOH is enhanced with the help of Co, leading to the oxygen evolution reaction probably via the lattice oxygen mechanism pathway.

The enhancement of Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-E catalytic activities towards the OER and HER was analysed theoretically and confirmed experimentally. For bimetallic/multimetallic catalysts, the adsorption energy50 of all the involved species during the reaction, the dissociation energy51 of reactants, and the binding energy of intermediates52,53 were optimized through the regulated surface electronic structure formed by the modification of foreign metals, usually concerned about the changes in the d-band center54â€“56 and eg orbital occupancy.57,58 Indeed, all the catalytic enhancement effects, such as the coordination/ligand effect,59â€“61 the strain effect,62,63 the size effect,64 the alloying effect,65 the crystal-facet effect,21,32etc. can be ultimately attributed to the change in the surface electronic structure of catalysts, achieving the optimization of surface adsorption energy.66 However, to our knowledge, the spillover effect46,67â€“69 does not involve the changes in the surface electronic structure, but originates from the spilling of intermediates or products from their corresponding optimized adsorption sites to desorption sites, releasing more free active sites for further reactions. A hydrogen/oxygen spillover effect has been observed at the catalyst surface during the different catalytic reactions.68,69 In this work, the enhancement in the synergistic catalytic effects of bimetallic Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2 for the HER and OER was speculated to be attributed to both the optimized surface electronic structure and the hydrogen/oxygen spillover effect.

In addition, it was also mentioned that lattice oxygen spillover dynamically takes place during the electrocatalytic reaction.70 The lattice oxygen evolution mechanism was proposed for the OER at the NiCo-LDH surface, where NiOOâˆ’ is a precursor to oxygen.71 Hence, combining the above experimental results, bulk Co/Ni interfaces and Ni sites in CoNi-LDHs are active and evolve oxygen via a NiOOâˆ’ precursor, and the OER mechanism would most likely be passed via the lattice oxygen mediated mechanism, which was discussed in detail in the literature.72 On the Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2 surface, Co incorporation can not only enhance the kinetics and reversibility of the conversion process of Ni(OH)2 â‡„ NiOOH, but also change the nature of the active sites, and accordingly dramatically improve the electrocatalytic activities towards both the OER and HER. In particular, the metal sites exposed on the {003} facet after releasing lattice oxygen or with an unsaturated coordination environment have moderate surface adsorption energy of reactants and intermediates and desorption energy of products, and easier electron transfer cross the solidâ€“liquid/gas two-phase interface, thereby improving their catalytic activity.

The structure of Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2 was characterized in situ by Raman spectroscopy. Notably, the larger I525/I474 peak intensity ratio suggests that more Î²-NiOOH is generated and Î²-NiOOH is suggested to be a more active intermediate structure for the OER.32,73â€“75 For Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-E, the peak intensity ratio I525/I474 at 0.2 V and 0.6 V was determined to be 2.4 and 1.4, respectively, while for Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-W, the peak intensity ratio I528/I483 at 0.2 V and 0.6 V were evaluated to be 1.5 and 1, respectively. Furthermore, a slight shift in the Niâ€“OH stretching mode from 474 to 468 cmâˆ’1 on the Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-E surface appears to be characteristic of the transformation of Î±- to Î²-Ni(OH)2.76 These results are consistent with the electrochemical evaluation results as shown in Fig. 4g. For Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-W, the decline of I528/I483 peak intensity ratio indicates that Î²-NiOOH was probably transformed to disordered Ni(OH)2, as suggested by the peak appearing at 504 cmâˆ’1 with the increase in the applied potential, which was not the case for Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-E. In brief, compared with Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-W, more active Î²-NiOOH intermediate phases can be formed on the Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-E surface, and those can be easily formed at a relatively lower overpotential, which was also confirmed by the electrochemical measurement results shown in Fig. 5d, f and S10.â€  In addition, the conversion process of Ni(OH)2 â†” NiOOH appeared before the OER due to the unsaturated coordination environment of surface metal atoms on the highly-active {003} facets or at the edges, and its reversibility was higher. As we mentioned above, the adjacent Co sites can also promote the redox reaction kinetics of Ni(OH)2/NiOOH. Hence, Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-E exhibits excellent catalytic activity and stability towards the OER, and such higher enhancements of catalytic activity towards the OER of the CoNi-LDH surface are mainly attributed to the crystal-facet and synergistic catalytic effects of bimetallic catalysts.

Conclusions

Solvent-induced structure engineering of CoNi-LDHs was carried out via a facile approach that is easy to realize large-scale synthesis, and the obtained material exhibited outstanding catalytic activity towards both the HER and OER in alkaline media, especially, Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-E, which is synthesized in ethanol and has a micro-sphere-like structure consisting of nanosheets with highly-active exposed facets, demonstrated the highest catalytic activities. At the output current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2, the feedback potentials for the OER and HER are only 1.51 V and 0.23 V vs. RHE, respectively. On one hand, the synthetic solvent can induce the growth mode of materials and prompt orientated growth, thereby exposing highly-active facets, where the unsaturated coordination metal centers could be easily oxidized at relatively lower overpotentials to form more active *OOH intermediates. On the other hand, Co can enhance the kinetics of the Ni(OH)2/NiOOH redox process and improve its reversibility, thus exhibiting a strong synergistic catalytic effect. It is convincing that the enhanced activities of bimetallic Co0.67Ni0.33(OH)2-E are attributed to not only the crystal-facet effect, but also the synergistic catalytic effect. These findings provide insight into the design principle for engineering bimetallic catalysts with highly active exposed facets and a strong synergistic effect, as an effective approach to modulate the physicochemical properties of transition metal hydroxides, boosting their intrinsic catalytic activity.
