Nonlinear features of Fano resonance: a QM/EM study

Abstract

The nonlinear Fano effects on the absorption of hybrid systems composed of a silver nanosphere and an indoline dye molecule have been systematically investigated by the hybrid approach, which combines the quantum mechanics method (QM) with the computational electromagnetic method (EM). The absorption spectra of the dye molecule in the proximity of an Ag nanoparticle have been calculated by changing the incident field intensity, the phenomenological dephasing of molecular excitation, and the enhancement ratio of the near field. The contribution of molecular nonlinear response properties and the quantum interferences of the incident and scattered fields and of resonant plasmon–molecular excitations to the spectra has been identified. It is in no doubt that Fano resonance due to the plasmon–molecular interaction can appear in both the weak and strong field regimes; however, the Fano effect is more pronounced in the strong field regime where quantum interference leads to a nonlinear Fano effect controlled by a complex field-dependent Fano factor. When the incident field is strong enough, the resonance antisymmetry structure is spectrally resolved, and it changes with the change of the field intensity. As the field intensity varies from weak to strong, the Fano lineshape's asymmetry increases with increasing intensity in the beginning, and then decreases with a further increase of the field intensity attributed to the increase of the detuning energy induced by the integrated energy shift upon field dressing during the excitation. Decreasing the enhancement ratio of the near field or the dephasing of molecular excitation can also control the spectral lineshape transformation from an asymmetric profile to a symmetric Lorentzian lineshape. These findings are consistent with previous experimental and theoretical observations arisen by quantum interferences and are expected to stimulate further work toward exploring the plasmon–molecular interplay and the applications of Fano resonance in optical switching and sensing.

I. Introduction

For a nanosystem involving plasmonic metal NPs (MNPs) and organic molecules or semiconductors, its optical performance is influenced by many factors such as the excitation strengths, spatial separation, excited-state lifetimes, and the spectral overlapping of the constituent parts. Due to the complex interplay between the MNP's localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) and the molecule or semiconductor's excitation (exciton), a set of particular characteristics in absorption/scattering spectra such as excitonic splitting, asymmetric lineshapes, plasmon-induced absorption enhancement and transparencies have been observed in those nanosystems.1–8 Recently, resonant plasmonic structures have been considered promising candidates for enhancing nonlinear optical processes such as above-threshold ionization and high-order harmonic generation of nearby molecules9,10 due to their ability to greatly enhance the optical near field.

In the strong plasmon–exciton coupling regime where the plasmon–exciton coupling is much larger than the damping rates of plasmon resonance and excitons,11 the interaction of plasmons and excitons results in two new mixed states of light and matter separated energetically by a Rabi splitting that exhibits a characteristic anticrossing behavior of the LSP–exciton energy tuning.12–14 In the weak coupling regime where the plasmon–exciton coupling strength is much less than one of the dampings,11 the absorption spectra characterize an asymmetric Fano lineshape.15–20 The realistic lineshape has been found to be susceptible to the detuning energy,21–25 the external radiation loss and intrinsic losses of the molecule–plasmon system,22,23,26 and the laser power.27,28 This usual Fano resonance effect can be introduced for both the linear regime29 and the strongly nonlinear regime.27,28,30,37 Govorov et al. found that when a semiconductor quantum dot becomes strongly excited at a high intensity of light, a nonlinear Fano effect appears in hybrid metal–semiconductor nanostructures.27,28,30 The field-tunable nonlinear Fano effect appears at high intensities when the degree of coherence in the system becomes strongly increased, because in this case the Rabi frequency starts to exceed the exciton broadening. With their unique characteristic and unusual combination of classical and quantum structures, Fano resonances are quite attractive for applications in a wide range of fields, from telecommunications to ultrasensitive biosensing, medical instrumentation, data storage, optical switching and solar energy harvesting and conversion. For example, LSPR biochemical sensors with improved sensitivities31–36 have been built by exploiting the feature that plasmonic Fano resonances are extremely sensitive to their local dielectric environment.

Fano's pioneering work29 found that the interference of a discrete localized state with a continuum band of states gives rise to characteristically asymmetric peaks in excitation spectra. The Fano resonance lineshape manifested in the absorption spectrum, σ(E), was described by the famous Fano formula: where E is the energy, and Er and Γ are the energy and resonance width of the continuous band, respectively. The absorption lineshape is predominantly determined by the Fano asymmetry parameter q, being a cotangent of the phase shift δ between two modes. At |q| ≫ 1, |q| ≪ 1 and |q| ∼ 1, one may observe the characteristic resonance, antiresonance, and dispersion absorption lineshapes. This Fano formula is generally applicable to the weak field case. When the laser power in the nonlinear regime interacts with the molecule in the proximity of a MNP, the multiphoton absorption and emission processes can take place easily, the field dressing of the strongly driven excitation may induce energy and phase shifts of the molecular excited state relative to the field-free resonant transition energy,38 and quantum interference leads to the nonlinear Fano effect controlled by a complex field-tunable Fano factor.39 Additionally, for a molecule, the two-state model may not be suitable since a few molecular excited states may lie within the plasmon mode volume. In this regard, for a plasmon–molecular system under an intense field, it is difficult to derive an analytical formula for the Fano lineshape. A numerical ab initio calculation is required.

One faces challenges in theoretically describing the optical responses of plasmon–exciton hybrid systems because the elementary excitations of molecules or semiconductors and MNPs possess different properties, and there exists a large disparity in length scales in such systems. Furthermore, the interaction between the LSPR and the exciton needs to be suitably tackled because it endows hybrid superstructures with novel properties. Many theoretical approaches have been proposed to investigate plasmon–exciton systems. The first category of the most commonly used methods is the multiscale approaches40–46 wherein the plasmonic nanostructures are treated by the classical47–53 or cavity quantum electromagnetic methods (EM),43,54–57 and the molecules are described by quantum mechanics (QM) approaches such as time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) or TD Hartree–Fock (TDHF). The second category is based on the effective model Hamiltonian,23,28,30,58–65 where the molecule or semiconductor is identified by a two-level model and the resonant plasmonic system is identified by the plasmonic resonance modes. The third one is based on a pure classical electrodynamic framework such as the dipole–dipole coupling approximation model,5 and coupled harmonic oscillators.66,67 Detailed atomic distribution information of hybrid systems is missed by the latter two categories of treatment.

In the past few years, we investigated the linear and nonlinear optical properties of hybrid complexes assembled from molecules and MNPs using the mixed QM/EM approach, where the localized field produced by the LSPR, as usual, was obtained by the numerical solution of Maxwell's equations using the classical electrodynamics finite difference time domain (FDTD) technique,68 and the optical properties of the adsorbates were described by the real-time time-dependent density functional theory (RT-TDDFT)52,69 or RT-TDHF methods.70 In this work, we adopted this hybrid approach to study nonlinear properties of Fano lineshapes. The surface enhanced localized field obtained by FDTD will interact with the molecule described by RT-TDHF. We will investigate the factors which control the transformation from symmetric Lorentzian to nonlinear asymmetric Fano absorption lineshapes of a dye molecule–Ag NP hybrid system, and unveil how the absorption lineshape varies with the intensity of the incident laser field and the phenomenological dephasing of molecular excitation. The effects of the interference between the incident and scattered fields, and of the different approximations used for truncating the induced density matrix, on the absorption lineshapes will be demonstrated. The origin of the nonlinear Fano effect is thus unveiled.

The rest of the paper is arranged as follows. In Section II, we briefly describe the hybrid scheme for molecular optical absorption spectroscopy. The details of RT-TDHF coupled with the FDTD method to simulate optical absorption spectroscopy are shown. In Section III, the plasmon-enhanced nonlinear optical properties will be discussed. We investigate how the spectral lineshapes change with the incident field intensity, the interference behaviours between the incident and scattered fields, the absorption difference yielded by the approximations with and without a linear response, etc. The plasmon–exciton coupling is tuned by the incident field intensity and the enhancement ratio of the local near field. Besides, the influence of the damping ratio of molecular excitation on the spectral lineshape will also be shown. Finally, concluding remarks are given in Section IV.

II. Theoretical methods and computational details

In this work, the optical properties of a hybrid system composed of a silver nanosphere with a radius of 10 nm and an organic adsorbate, an indoline dye molecule (ID1)71 (see Fig. 1), are investigated by using the mixed quantum-classical approach, where the localized field produced by the plasmon resonance of noble metal NPs is obtained by the numerical solution of Maxwell's equations using the FDTD technique,68 and the optical properties of the adsorbates are described by the real time TDHF (RT-TDHF) approach. Indoline dyes are one class of organic photosensitizer dyes with high power conversion efficiencies for dye sensitized solar cells. A detailed description of this hybrid QM/EM method can be found in our previous work.52,70

III. Results and discussion

The plasmon resonance of an Ag NP with R = 10 nm appears around 3.5 eV (see Fig. 1(c)).

Compared with the pulse duration of the incident field, those of the scattered fields obtained by eqn (6) are much longer as Fig. 1(b) shows. The intense near field of the resonant plasmons behaves like a strong coherent optical excitation source, leading to increased population rates of excited states and new resonance-enhanced emission of the molecules. Since the off-diagonal terms of λ(r,ω) are nearly negligible compared with the diagonal terms, only λxx(r,ω) and λyy(r,ω) are involved in the spectral calculations. Two separate RT-TDHF simulations have been carried out by assuming that the incident electric field pulse is applied along the x- and the y-axis, respectively. The calculated spectral cross sections are obtained as σ(ω) = 1/2(σxx + σyy).

The dye molecule is optimized at the B3LYP/6-31G* level of theory. For the isolated ID1 molecule, three low-energy absorption peaks appear at around 2.77, 3.42 and 3.52 eV (see the inset of Fig. 2). As the damping factor γ increases, the two bands centered at 3.42 and 3.52 eV mix together to form a broad absorption band centered around 3.5 eV, at which the NP plasmon is resonant with the excitation frequency.

A. The effect of the incident optical intensity on the absorption spectra

At first, we investigate the influence of the optical intensity (I ∝ (E0max)2) of the incident laser on the absorption of both isolated and bound ID1 molecules. The corresponding absorption spectra are shown in Fig. 2 and 3, respectively. γ = 0.2 eV is set.
Fig. 3 The absorption spectra of the bound ID1 molecule varying with I in (a) W/LRA and (b) W/OLRA.

In the strong field regime, the relative intensities and locations of the absorption peaks are susceptible to I when RT-TDHF includes the response of the electron density beyond the linear response regime (without LRA (W/OLRA)). As Fig. 2 shows, the increase of the field intensity continuously decreases the on-resonance absorption of the two high-energy bands at around 2.77 and 3.5 eV, blue-shifting and red-shifting their peak locations, respectively. Besides, many new absorption bands appear in the low-energy range. These phenomena show that the fields couple with the molecule nonlinearly. The decrease of the on-resonance absorption and the integrated energy shift are attributed to the absorption saturation and the field dressing during the excitation, respectively, which are consistent with the experimental observation.38 The higher-power applied field strongly admixes the states of different energy levels and makes higher-order multiphoton processes become increasingly important.

Compared with the spectra of the isolated ID1 molecule, the impact of the scattered field on the molecular spectra is very pronounced. It not only further alters the absorption peak intensities and locations, but also gives rise to a characteristically asymmetric lineshape in the resonance energy region. For example, at I = 5 × 1011 W cm−2, the absorption peak at ∼2.77 eV nearly disappears, and a distinctive antisymmetric profile appears at around 3.5 eV. The antisymmetric profile becomes sharp at I ≥ 1 × 1011 W cm−2, and the negative part of the absorption reaches a maximum at I = 5 × 1011 W cm−2. However, further increasing I inhibits the Fano resonance effect.

In the weak field domain, the linear part is dominant, and σ is thus not sensitive to the optical intensity because all nonlinear high-order responses are negligibly small. For example, at I = 1.0 × 106 W cm−2, the spectra produced by W/LRA and W/OLRA are nearly the same. If the molecule is immobilized in a strong field, the contribution of the nonlinear parts to μ(t) is also significant. As I become larger and larger, the nonlinear parts in eqn (8) increase more obviously than the linear part since the dipole moment is proportional to the second and higher powers of the field intensity.

The induced dipole moment oscillates with the basis frequency ω of the induced field as well as with other frequencies: such as the second (2ω) and third (3ω) harmonics. However, because the phases of different orders of optical responses are different, μ(t)/Emax decreases more quickly with the increase of the propagation time as Fig. 4 shows. Besides, the equilibrium position of the induced dipole moment shifts away from zero because an oscillating electric field may result in a non-oscillating dipole moment related to the hyperpolarizabilities βi,j,k(0;ω,ω) and γi,j,k,l(0;0,ω,ω).75,76

When the incident field is strong enough, the highly excited molecule cannot absorb subsequent radiation, and the absorption saturates. The higher frequency signal modes begin to dominate the time-resolved dipole moment. So the on-resonance absorption around 2.77 eV decreases with the nonlinearity.77 Besides, in the nonlinear regime, the absorption around 2.77 eV is blue-shifted with the increase of the field intensity. It is because the highly displaced charge density experiences a nonlinear restoring force which is stronger than in the linear regime.77

Compared with the absorption in other regions, the absorption in the resonant energy region of about 3.47 eV is more complex due to the quantum interference between resonance plasmons and molecular excitations. In the linear regime, the plasmon–exciton coupling increases linearly with E0, and the Fano asymmetry parameter q doesn't change. Therefore, from I = 1.0 × 106 to I = 1.0 × 108 W cm−2, there are no changes for this peak in the normalized spectra as Fig. 3(b) shows. Quickly, nonlinear saturation effects make the excitation to the discrete state stop increasing. But the transition to the continuum still increases with the enhanced incident field, leading the Fano parameter q to decrease. Besides, when I increases to 1.0 × 1010 W cm−2, the absorption at 3.52 eV of the isolated ID1 molecule red shifts by 0.01 eV (see Fig. 2), and the plasmon–exciton coupling enhances because Δω = |ωm − ωp| decreases. The competition between the coupling and q makes the absorption spectra at the resonance energy increase a little when I = 1.0 × 1010 W cm−2, and decrease quickly as I increases continuously. As the field intensity increases, the negative absorption part becomes deeper and deeper. But the negative peak doesn't further enhance with a further increase of I. At I = 2 × 1012 W cm−2, the Fano interference effect becomes weak since this strong field red-shifts the molecular excitation slightly, leading to the increase of detuning energy Δω.

To unveil the nonlinear Fano effect clearly, we calculate the absorption spectra with the LRA. Fig. 3(a) shows that the absorption profile does't vary with I in W/LRA. There is no obvious difference in σ as I increases from 1.0 × 106 to 1.0 × 1012 W cm−2, and no negative absorption peak appears.

To show a more obvious demonstration of the nonlinear feature in the excitation, the absorption difference near the resonance regime produced using W/LRA and W/OLRA is plotted in Fig. 5. It is clear that the applied fields coupled to the molecule nonlinearly, and the nonlinear component is susceptible to I. For the dye–Ag NP hybrid system, the effect of the nonlinear response on the spectrum is mainly twofold. On the one hand, it induces a significant Fano effect in the resonance energy regime, and, on the other hand, it leads to a nonlinear change in the relative spectral intensities and peak positions. As Fig. 5 shows, at I = 1 × 1010 W cm−2, sharp antisymmetric Fano profiles appear at around 2.7 and 3.5 eV. As I increases to 2 × 1011 W cm−2, the Fano lineshape formed by the nonlinear response changes from asymmetric to a dip. As I continually increases, the dip deepens. At I = 2 × 1012 W cm−2, the Fano effect decreases in the W/OLRA absorption spectra because the detuning energy of Δω increases. However, the hyperpolarizability still prevents the absorption strength from growing linearly with E0. So there is also a shifted dip in Fig. 5 and the depth of the dip decreases. It is clear that at higher power a laser field strongly admixes the states and the physics becomes rich. A remarkable interplay of coherent nonlinear transitions takes place in the strong field regime.78

B. The interference effect of incident and scattered fields on the spectra

The nonlinear properties of surface-enhanced absorption spectra can also be studied from a different point of view. As we know, when two light beams recombine, they interfere and form a pattern that is dependent on the phase difference between them. For a dye in the proximity of NPs, light with an intensity

In order to investigate the contribution of the coherence term to the absorption spectra, we calculate σ(Esca) and the absorption difference defined by σ(Etotal) − σ(E0) − σ(Esca) and show the results in Fig. 6. Here σ(Etotal), σ(E0) and σ(Esca) are the absorption cross sections produced by the fields applied to the dye molecule with Etotal, E0 and Esca, respectively.


Fig. 6 (a) The absorption spectra produced by Esca varying with I. (b) The absorption difference of σ(Etotal) − σ(E0) − σ(Esca).

In the intense field regime, both E0 and Esca couple to the molecule nonlinearly. The Fano antisymmetric profile only appears in σ(Esca), demonstrating that it originates from the quantum interference between the plasmon and exciton. Compared with the absorption arisen by the incident field E0, the absorption arisen by the scattered field is more susceptible to I. As Fig. 6(a) shows, the sharp antisymmetric lineshape in σ(Esca) appears more quickly than that in σ(Etotal) as I increases.

As Fig. 6(b) shows, the antisymmetric profile near the resonant energy originating from the coherent term is very evident even when the field intensity is as low as I = 1.0 × 1010 W cm−2. As I increases, this phenomena become more pronounced. The antisymmetric profile induced by the coherence term of two fields varies from the style of ‘negative peak after positive peak’ to ‘positive peak after the negative peak’ as I increases from 1 × 1010 to 2 × 1012 W cm−2. As a result, the negative part of the absorption in σ(Etotal) disappears gradually at I ≥ 2 × 1012 W cm−2 as Fig. 3(b) shows.

C. Lineshape variation with the SRF and the damping ratio

Last, we investigated the variance of the absorption lineshape with the plasmon–exciton coupling and the damping ratio of molecular excitation. The plasmon–exciton coupling is tuned by the local near field intensity. Here we set I = 1 × 106 W cm−2, and tune the enhancement ratio of the near field by scaling the SRF, λ, with a factor of 0.6, 0.4, 0.2, and 0.1, respectively, and the reduction of the SRF can be achieved by increasing the distance between the molecule and Ag NPs. Then the corresponding absorption spectrum of ID1 in the proximity of the Ag NP is calculated. The results are shown in Fig. 7. We found that both the absorption intensity and profile near the resonance region are more sensitive to the scaling factor than those around 2.77 eV. Decreasing the scaling factor weakens the interaction between the exciton and plasmon, and the lineshape becomes broad and more symmetric.
Fig. 7 (a) The absorption spectra varying with the scaling factor of λ. (b) The lineshapes of the bound ID1 near the resonance energy. γ = 0.2 eV and I = 1.0 × 106 W cm−2.

In addition to the resonant plasmon–exciton coupling, the dampings of plasmons and molecular excitation can also affect the optical response of the hybrid system. Fig. 8 displays the electronic absorption spectra at different γ. As the half-width of the molecular absorption spectra increases, the asymmetric character of the lineshape becomes more obvious. An antisymmetric Fano lineshape starts to appear at γ = 0.3 eV. This is because, in the weak field regime, the damping ratio of the molecular excitation is larger than the plasmon–exciton coupling.

IV. Concluding remarks

We have calculated the surface enhanced absorption spectra of a dye molecule in the proximity of an Ag nanosphere of R = 10 nm by using the hybrid RT-TDHF/FDTD approach as a function of the incident optical intensity, the phenomenological dephasing of molecular excitation, the enhancement ratio of the near field, and different approximations adopted to truncate the induced electron density. The quantum interference effects between the incident and scattered fields as well as between the resonant plasmon and molecular excitations on the spectra have been unveiled. A result comparison is made with the isolated molecule. The main conclusions are summarized as follows:

(1) Our findings support the previous experimental and theoretical evidence that Fano resonance behaviour can appear at a field intensity in the linear and strongly nonlinear regimes. However, it is more pronounced in the nonlinear regime at high power due to a remarkable interplay of coherent nonlinear transitions.

(2) In the strong field regime, we clearly observe the absorbance decrease and the energy shift of the molecular on-resonance absorption bands with increasing intensity. A remarkable Fano asymmetric lineshape appears in the resonance energy region, mainly attributed to the molecular nonlinear optical response properties. The lineshape's asymmetry increases with increasing intensity at the beginning and decreases with further increasing intensity because the integrated energy shift upon field dressing during the excitation leads to the increase of the detuning energy.

(3) The nonlinear Fano effect can always be clearly observed in the absorption difference of σ(Etotal) − σ(E0) − σ(Esca), which corresponds to the interference effect between the incident field and scattered field. Around the resonant energy region, the antisymmetric lineshape in the absorption difference varies from the style of ‘negative peak after positive peak’ to ‘positive peak after the negative peak’ as I increases from 1 × 1010 to 2 × 1012 W cm−2. When two fields interact with a molecule, the first field E0(t) induces a dipole, μind = αE + βE2 + γE3 + …, the second field Esca interacts with both the molecular permeant dipole and μind, and multiphoton processes may take place even when the field intensity is in the linear regime.

(4) An asymmetric Fano lineshape can also appear in the weak field regime when the damping ratio of the molecular excitation is larger than the plasmon–exciton coupling. The larger the near field enhancement ratio, the more remarkable the asymmetric lineshape. Besides, in the resonance energy region, the molecular absorption doesn't respond to the plasmon–exciton coupling linearly, manifesting the nonlinear feature of the Fano effect.

Our study establishes the microscopic mechanisms underlying the appearance and variance of the Fano resonance phenomenon with the light intensity. It reveals the feature of the Fano resonance and the influence of quantum interferences on the absorption lineshape. The general and fundamental principles established by analysis of the simulation results are useful for understanding the nonlinear phenomena in surface-enhanced absorption spectra, the development of nonlinear plasmonic devices, and the design of plasmonic metal nanoparticles to tune the photochemical and photophysical properties of nearby molecules. Our findings are expected to stimulate further work toward exploring the plasmon–molecular interplay and exploiting the Fano resonance.
