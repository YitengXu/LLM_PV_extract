Design, synthesis, and structural characterization of the first dithienocyclopentacarbazole-based n-type organic semiconductor and its application in non-fullerene polymer solar cells

Abstract

Ladder-type dithienocyclopentacarbazole (DTCC) cores, which possess highly extended -conjugated backbones and versatile modular structures for derivatization, were widely used to develop high-performance p-type polymeric semiconductors. However, an n-type DTCC-based organic semiconductor has not been reported to date. In this study, the first DTCC-based n-type organic semiconductor (DTCCIC) with a well-defined ADA backbone was designed, synthesized, and characterized, in which a DTCC derivative substituted by four p-octyloxyphenyl groups was used as the electron-donating core and two strongly electron-withdrawing 3-(dicyanomethylene)indan-1-one moieties were used as the terminal acceptors. It was found that DTCCIC has strong light-capturing ability in the range of 500720 nm and exhibits an impressively high molar absorption coefficient of 2.24 105 M1 cm1 at 669 nm owing to effective intramolecular charge transfer and a strong DA effect. Cyclic voltammetry measurements indicated that the HOMO and LUMO energy levels of DTCCIC are 5.50 and 3.87 eV, respectively. More importantly, a high electron mobility of 2.17 103 cm2 V1 s1 was determined by the space-charge-limited current method; this electron mobility can be comparable to that of fullerene derivative acceptors (e 103 cm2 V1 s1). To investigate its application potential in non-fullerene solar cells, we fabricated organic solar cells (OSCs) by blending a DTCCIC acceptor with a PTB7-Th donor under various conditions. The results suggest that the optimized device exhibits a maximum power conversion efficiency (PCE) of up to 6% and a rational high VOC of 0.95 V. These findings demonstrate that the ladder-type DTCC core is a promising building block for the development of high-mobility n-type organic semiconductors for OSCs.

Introduction

Bulk heterojunction organic solar cells (OSCs), as promising candidates for renewable energy conversion devices, have gained significant attention owing to their advantages, which include low cost, mechanical flexibility, and light weight.15 Due to great efforts in innovative material design and device optimization, high power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) of over 10% have recently been achieved for several single-junction fullerene OSCs.612 However, further enhancements in the PCEs of fullerene-based OSCs might represent a great challenge in view of the intrinsic drawbacks of fullerene derivative acceptors, including weak light-capturing ability in the visible-light region, difficulties in the synthesis and purification procedures, and a strong tendency to molecular aggregation. To our satisfaction, non-fullerene acceptors have recently been demonstrated to be promising alternatives to fullerene derivative acceptors4,5 because they have great flexibility in synthesis for dealing with the abovementioned disadvantages of fullerene acceptors. Based on the strongly electron-deficient building blocks such as BN complexes,1316 perylene diimide,1719 naphthalene diimide,2024 and naphthodithiophene diimide moieties,25,26 a great number of n-type polymeric semiconductors were successfully prepared and applied in highly efficient non-fullerene OSCs. In addition, numerous aromatic diimide/imide-based n-type organic semiconductors (n-OSs),2735 as well as n-OSs with a well-defined ADA (acceptordonoracceptor) backbone,4,5,3646 have also been widely investigated as electron acceptors in non-fullerene OSCs.

Among reported non-fullerene acceptors, n-OSs with ADA backbone configurations have been demonstrated to be the most successful to date. Since Zhan et al. reported fused-ring-based ADA n-OSs (ITIC),36 great interest has been excited in the investigation of ITIC and its ADA derivatives as electron acceptors in non-fullerene OSCs because of their great advantages4,5,3646 such as a tunable HOMO/LUMO energy-level structure, strong light-capturing ability from 500 to 800 nm, and low-cost synthesis and purification, as well as appropriate aggregation and crystallinity, etc. Encouragingly, non-fullerene OSCs fabricated from ITIC derivatives as acceptors and conjugated polymers as donors exhibited impressive PCEs of above 10%,4,3650 which were even higher than those of their fullerene-based counterparts. As far as molecular design is concerned, current synthesis strategies for ideal n-OSs with ADA structures mainly concentrate on the modulation of -conjugated aromatic cores,4648 -bridges,49,50 and terminal groups,37,38,51 as well as solubilizing side-chain substituents.42 Among these, the modulation of -conjugated aromatic cores is regarded as the most versatile method for developing a large number of fascinating n-OSs with ADA structures. Therefore, the further development of novel aromatic cores for n-OSs with ADA structures is worthy of study.

Dithienocyclopentacarbazole (DTCC), as a well-known ladder-type conjugated framework, has been utilized to develop p-type polymeric semiconductors for fullerene-based OSCs and organic field-effect transistors (OFETs) (Fig. 1)5255 owing to its strong electron-donating ability, highly coplanar backbone, and versatile modular structure for the facile synthesis of polycyclic ring analogs. It has been widely demonstrated that the use of this ladder-type conjugated framework in organic/polymeric semiconductors can not only facilitate the crystallization and organization of thin films but can also improve intra- or intermolecular interactions, which would be helpful for carrier transport and PCEs.56 On the basis of ladder-type DTCC derivative donors, Wu et al. first reported the design, synthesis, and photovoltaic properties of p-type DTCC-based polymeric semiconductors52 and then developed a set of DTCC-like building blocks for constructing p-type polymeric semiconductors with hole mobilities of 0.0010.14 cm2 V1 s1.54 Traditionally, these have been used as electron donors in fullerene OSCs and afford relatively high photovoltaic performance, with PCEs of 3.74.6% (Fig. 1).52,53,55 Nevertheless, no n-type DTCC-based organic semiconductor has been reported to date that can be used in non-fullerene OSCs. In view of their great advantages mentioned above, it is believed that by incorporating two strongly electron-deficient terminal groups into the backbone of the DTCC unit ADA-type DTCC-based organic semiconductors might be transformed into electron-accepting n-OSs.

On the basis of the strategy mentioned above, we first reported the design and synthesis of a novel n-OS with an ADA structure bearing a ladder-type DTCC core, i.e., DTCCIC (Fig. 1), in which DTCC was used as an electron-donating building block; two strongly electron-deficient 3-(dicyanomethylene)indan-1-one (IC) moieties were used as the terminal acceptors; and four p-octyloxyphenyl side chains were used as solubilizing groups. Furthermore, we systematically studied the thermal and optical properties, energy-level structure, carrier mobility, photovoltaic performance, and film morphology of the DTCCIC acceptor. It was found that the DTCCIC acceptor had a strong, broad absorption band and appropriate HOMO/LUMO energy levels, as well as a high electron mobility of 2.17 103 cm2 V1 s1, so that it could match other components well to fabricate non-fullerene OSCs. With the use of the reported PTB7-Th as a polymeric donor and DTCCIC as an acceptor, non-fullerene OSCs were fabricated successfully. The best-performing device achieved the highest PCE value of 6%. It should be mentioned that this result is the best reported to date for OSC devices fabricated from DTCC-based organic/polymeric semiconductors.

Experimental section

Measurements

Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectra of samples were recorded with a Bruker Avance 400 instrument (400 MHz) using tetramethylsilane as the internal standard. Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time of flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF-MS) was performed using a Bruker Autoflex III instrument. UV-vis absorption spectra in a chloroform solution and a thin film coated onto quartz glass were recorded with a Perkin-Elmer Lambda 25 spectrometer. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) was performed using a Nicolet 6700 spectrometer. The energy-level structures of samples were determined by three-electrode cyclic voltammetry (CV) on an electrochemical workstation (CHI660A, Chenhua, Shanghai). The working electrode was a platinum stick coated with a layer of a thin film of the sample. Platinum wire and Ag/AgCl (Ag in 0.01 mol L1 KCl) were used as the counter electrode and reference electrode, respectively. Ferrocene/ferrocenium (Fc/Fc+) was used as an internal standard. The electrolyte was an anhydrous N2-saturated acetonitrile solution containing 0.1 M tetrabutylammonium hexafluorophosphate. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed using a Netzsch TG 209 analyzer at a heating rate of 10 C min1 under an inert atmosphere. Differential scanning calorimetric analysis (DSC) was performed using a TA DSCQ10 instrument at heating and cooling rates of 10 C min1 under an inert N2 atmosphere. Atomic force microscopy (AFM) measurements were carried out with a Bruker Multi-Mode 8 microscope in tapping mode. Detailed space-charge-limited current (SCLC) measurements can be found in our previous work.27

General procedures for fabrication of non-fullerene OSCs

OSCs were fabricated with a conventional device architecture of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/active layer/Ca/Al. The cleaning of ITO and the preparation of PEDOT:PSS were reported in our previous work.27 The PTB7-Th donor and DTCCIC acceptor were co-dissolved in chloroform with different weight ratios and CN concentrations. The overall concentration of PTB7-Th was fixed at 6.5 mg mL1. The blend solution was spin-cast onto the PEDOT:PSS layer at 2000 rpm for 30 s under a nitrogen atmosphere and were then treated by thermal annealing at 100 C for 5 min. Subsequently, a 20 nm Ca layer and a 100 nm Al layer were sequentially deposited onto the active layer at a vacuum pressure of 5 105 Pa. The active area of the devices was 4.50 mm2. The OSCs measurements can also be found in our previous work.27 External quantum efficiency (EQE) spectra were recorded using a QEX10 solar cell IPCE measurement system (PV Measurements, Inc.).

Materials

All the starting chemicals and reagents were purchased from Chem Greatwall and Alfa Aesar and were used as received. Dry tetrahydrofuran (THF) was distilled from a blue solution of sodium and benzophenone. Three key intermediate compounds, namely, 9-octyl-2,7-bis(4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-1,3,2-dioxaborolan-2-yl)-9H-carbazole (1),57 ethyl 2-bromothiophene-3-carboxylate,58 and 3-(dicyanomethylene)indan-1-one (IC),59 were prepared by following the reported procedures. 1H NMR and 13C NMR spectra for all the prepared compounds are provided in the ESI (Fig. S1S8).

Results and discussion

Synthesis, characterization, and thermal properties

All the intermediate products and the target ADA-type n-OS (DTCCIC) were readily prepared according to the procedures depicted in Scheme 1. The synthesis began via a conventional Suzuki coupling reaction by the combination of a 2,7-diboronic ester of carbazole (1) and ethyl 2-bromothiophene-3-carboxylate to give compound 2, and a subsequent double nucleophilic addition reaction between freshly prepared 4-(octyloxy)phenyllithium and the ester groups of compound 2 was performed to give a benzylic alcohol (3), which was then directly converted to the fused heptacyclic arene (DTCC) via an acid-mediated FriedelCrafts reaction. After that, DTCC was subjected to the Vilsmeier reaction to give the key intermediate DTCCCHO in a moderate yield (68%). Finally, DTCCIC was obtained by the Knoevenagel reaction between DTCCCHO and 3-(dicyanomethylene)indan-1-one in a high yield (73%). The chemical structures of all novel compounds were confirmed by 1H NMR, 13C NMR, and MALDI-TOF (Fig. S1S12). In order to further confirm the chemical structures of DTCCCHO and DTCCIC, the FTIR spectra of both samples were recorded. As shown in Fig. S13, a strong signal at 1656 cm1, which corresponded to the stretching band of CHO groups, was observed for DTCCCHO. For the DTCCIC sample, the strong signal that appeared at 1696 cm1 is attributable to the stretching band of CO groups, whereas a weak signal assigned to the stretching band of CN groups can be observed at 2216 cm1. Owing to the appropriate substitution of solubilizing alkyl chains, DTCCIC exhibits excellent solubility in most chlorinated solvents and common organic solvents (>20 mg mL1) at room temperature, which substantially affects processability and film quality in the corresponding non-fullerene OSC devices. Thermal analysis of DTCCIC was conducted by TGA and DSC at a temperature ramp rate of 10 C min1 (Fig. 2). As shown in Fig. 2a, DTCCIC exhibited a high decomposition temperature (5% weight loss) of up to 372 C, which was indicative of high thermal stability for the fabrication of OSC devices. Note that DTCCIC displayed distinct thermal transitions, which featured high melting/crystalline transformation temperatures (Tm/Tc) of 254/228 C, during the heating/cooling cycles, respectively.

Optical and electrochemical properties

The optical properties of both DTCC and DTCCIC were measured in chloroform solution and the thin-film state; the relevant data are provided in Table 1 and Fig. 3. Note that the absorption spectrum of the parent core (DTCC) in chloroform solution is mainly located in the ultraviolet region, together with a weak response in the visible region (Fig. 3a); this feature is consistent with the reported polycyclic fused arenes.60 By the incorporation of two strongly electron-deficient IC terminal groups, the ADA-type DTCC-based n-OS (DTCCIC) was developed, and its absorption spectrum in chloroform solution was significantly broadened in comparison to that of DTCC. Moreover, strong absorption at 500720 nm with an impressively high molar absorption coefficient ( = 2.24 105 M1 cm1, Fig. S14) at 669 nm was demonstrated, which mainly originated from strong intramolecular charge transfer (ICT), as well as a strong DA effect.61 In comparison with the absorption in solution, the DTCCIC thin film gave rise to an expected sign of J-type aggregation,62 namely, a typical absorption feature with a significantly red-shifted ICT peak approaching 731 nm. The results indicate that DTCCIC has a large tendency to crystallize and aggregate in solid-state films.61,62 The estimated optical band gap (Eopticalg) of DTCCIC film is 1.59 eV, as determined from the absorption edge of the thin film (778 nm, Fig. 3a).

To investigate the absorption spectra of blends, we further studied the absorption features of blends comprising a PTB7-Th donor and a DTCCIC acceptor under different conditions, including different D/A weight ratios (1:0, 0:1, and 1:1) and the addition of 1% chloronaphthalene (CN) as an additive. As shown in Fig. 3b, neat PTB7-Th film displayed stronger absorption at 500698 nm than that of DTCCIC, and had a certain overlap in absorption with DTCCIC. After the addition of 1% CN as an additive, the absorption spectrum of the blend (PTB7-Th/DTCCIC = 1:1) underwent a red shift of 24 nm in comparison to the equivalent blend without 1% CN as an additive, which was mainly caused by an enhancement in crystallinity and aggregation in the blended films.40 It should be noted that the strong, broad absorption features of DTCCIC and its blended films are helpful for the enhancement of light-capturing ability, short-circuit current density (JSC), and PCEs in OSC devices.

Fig. 4a shows typical CV curves of DTCCIC versus an Ag/AgCl electrode, and the relevant electrochemical data are listed in Table 1. Under the same measurement conditions, the oxidation onset potential of the Fc/Fc+ external standard was determined to be 0.38 V. As a result, the HOMO and LUMO energy levels of DTCCIC can be estimated from the following equations: HOMO = (Eonsetox + 4.42) eV; LUMO = (Eonsetred + 4.42) eV. As shown in Fig. 4a, the oxidation and reduction onset potentials are 1.08 and 0.55 V, respectively. Therefore, the calculated values of the HOMO and LUMO energy levels of DTCCIC are 5.50 and 3.87 eV, respectively (Table 1). As shown in Fig. 4b, the HOMO and LUMO energy levels of the well-known polymeric donor PTB7-Th are 5.20 and 3.56 eV, respectively. When non-fullerene OSC devices are fabricated from a PTB7-Th donor and a DTCCIC acceptor, a well-matched energy-level alignment between the donor and acceptor would be achieved, which ensures sufficient energy offsets of 0.31 eV (LUMOLUMO) for electron transfer and 0.30 eV (HOMOHOMO) for hole transfer (Fig. 4b). Therefore, this kind of active blend would provide sufficient driving force for the efficient dissociation of excitons in OSCs.19,63 Note that the LUMO energy level of DTCCIC is higher than those of PC61BM (3.91 eV)64 and other well-known non-fullerene acceptors such as ITIC-Th (3.93 eV),40 which should lead to an improvement in VOC.65

Density functional theory (DFT) calculations

To investigate the geometric and electronic properties of DTCCIC, we performed DFT calculations at the B3LYP/6-31G* level of theory. It should be mentioned that the four octyloxy groups in DTCCIC were replaced by methoxy groups in order to simplify the DFT calculations. As shown in Fig. 5, DTCCIC exhibits a highly coplanar backbone with very small torsion dihedral angles (<0.2) along the whole molecular backbone. In general, this highly coplanar feature would facilitate -electron delocalization along the whole -conjugated backbone and in turn lead to enhancements in ICT and crystallinity, as well as carrier transport.66 Note that the four aryl side groups are located outside the main backbone of DTCCIC, which would effectively suppress the strong backbone aggregation caused by the highly coplanar molecular backbone of DTCCIC.48 In addition, the extensive delocalization of the HOMO/LUMO electron orbitals over the whole fused ring backbone further demonstrates that DTCCIC has a highly coplanar backbone and thereby features strong ICT between the DTCC donor and the IC acceptor. All the calculated results strongly support the data from the absorption spectra discussed above.

Photovoltaic performance

To investigate the potential of the DTCCIC-based electron acceptor for employment in OSCs, non-fullerene OSC devices with a conventional device structure of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/PTB7-Th:DTCCIC/Ca/Al were fabricated, in which the well-known PTB7-Th was used as the electron donor and DTCCIC was used as the electron acceptor. Fig. 6a shows typical current densityvoltage (JV) curves of the best devices. The relevant photovoltaic parameters are listed in Table 2. To achieve optimized device performance, active blends with three different D/A weight ratios (1.3:1, 1:1, and 1:1.3, w/w) and with/without 1% CN as an additive were investigated systematically. The optimized ratio was determined to be 1:1; JSC and FF values were highly dependent on the D/A weight ratio and additive, but VOC values were not sensitive. Note that optimized OSCs based on blends with D/A = 1:1 exhibited a JSC of 10.74 mA cm2, a high VOC of 0.95 V, and an FF of 0.474, which resulted in a moderate PCE value of 4.84%. After the addition of 1% CN as an additive to the blends with D/A = 1:1, VOC remained at 0.95 V, whereas the PCE reached a maximum value of 6.0%, which mainly resulted from increases in JSC (11.23 mA cm2) and FF (0.562) in comparison to OSCs without additives. On further variations in the D/A ratio from 1:1 to 1.3:1 or 1:1.3, VOC values remained at 0.950.96 V, whereas JSC and FF values decreased markedly, which thereby led to a reduced PCE value of <5.04%.

To validate the JSC values measured above, we measured the EQE values of OSCs fabricated under different conditions. The corresponding JV curves and integral JSC values are provided in Fig. 6b and Table 2, respectively. Note that all the optimized blends displayed broad, strong photoresponse from 400 to 740 nm. Obviously, the strong photoresponse in the longer wavelength range of >700 nm should be mainly ascribed to the contribution of the DTCCIC acceptor; this EQE result is also highly consistent with the absorption spectra of the PTB7-Th/DTCCIC blend, as discussed above. Furthermore, we found that the intensity of photoresponse was markedly influenced by changing the D/A weight ratio, and the solvent additive CN could improve the EQE values. Among all the conditions, OSCs fabricated from blends (D/A = 1:1 or 1.3:1, w/w) with 1% CN achieved higher EQE intensity at 400740 nm, which thereby led to higher integral JSC values of 11.28 and 11.21 mA cm2 over the AM 1.5G spectrum, respectively, which closely match the JSC values calculated from the JV curves above (11.23 and 10.84 mA cm2). In addition, a polymer donor with a wide band gap, namely, PBT1-EH,67 was employed to match the DTCCIC acceptor. These exhibit highly complementary absorption that covers nearly the entire wavelength region of 300800 nm. However, OSCs exhibited a high VOC of 1.1 V, a JSC of 6.8 mA cm2, an FF of 0.415, and a corresponding PCE value of 3.1% (Fig. S15), which is lower than that of OSCs fabricated from PTB7-Th:DTCCIC blends.

Charge transport properties

To better understand the differences in the observed JSC and FF values, we measured the hole (h) and electron (e) mobilities of the blends using the conventional SCLC method. The device structures were ITO/MoOx/PTB7-Th or blend film/MoO3/Al for hole-only devices and ITO/Al/DTCCIC or blend film/Al for electron-only devices. Typical JV curves and detailed parameters of carrier mobility are provided in Fig. 7 and Table 3, respectively. The results demonstrated that owing to the incorporation of two strongly electron-deficient IC terminal groups the as-synthesized DTCC-based organic semiconductor (DTCCIC) was transformed into an electron-accepting n-OS. The observed electron mobility of DTCCIC was 2.17 103 cm2 V1 s1, which is comparable to that of fullerene derivative acceptors (e 103 cm2 V1 s1).4651 Such a high value of electron mobility indicates that DTCCIC is an excellent n-type small-molecule acceptor for non-fullerene OSCs. For neat PTB7-Th films, a rational hole mobility of 1.42 103 cm2 V1 s1 was observed, which closely matches the electron mobility of the DTCCIC acceptor. When PTB7-Th and DTCCIC were blended in the ratio of 1:1, the observed hole and electron mobilities of the blend remained at a high level (h = 6.76 104 cm2 V1 s1, e = 1.52 103 cm2 V1 s1, and the e/h ratio = 2.2) in comparison to those of the neat films. After the addition of 1% CN as an additive, the average h and e values of the PTB7-Th/DTCCIC (1:1, w/w) blends increased to 1.07 103 and 1.86 103 cm2 V1 s1, respectively. Therefore, the PTB7-Th/DTCCIC blend with 1% CN as an additive afforded a better balance between hole and electron transfer (e/h ratio = 1.7) than that (e/h ratio = 2.2) without CN as an additive. In general, higher h and e values, as well as a closer balance between hole and electron mobilities, will facilitate charge extraction and reduce charge recombination, which is helpful for achieving higher JSC and FF values of the corresponding OSC devices (Table 2).68

Film morphology analysis

Because the morphology of a film can be closely connected with its photovoltaic performance,7 we determined the surface morphological structures of neat DTCCIC and PTB7-Th/DTCCIC blend (1:1, w/w) films with/without 1% CN as an additive by AFM measurements. As shown in Fig. 8, AFM height and phase images indicate that the neat DTCCIC film displays large crystalline grains, which demonstrates an interconnected surface morphology with a root-mean-square (RMS) roughness of 5.0 nm. Such large crystalline grains and interconnected surface morphology, which mainly originate from strong intermolecular interactions, would provide highly efficient pathways for high-mobility electron transport.66,69 Interestingly, the grain size of the PTB7-Th/DTCCIC (1:1, w/w) blend films was markedly reduced after blending the DTCCIC acceptor with the PTB7-Th donor or the further addition of 1% CN as an additive. As a result, relatively homogeneous surface morphological structures were formed for the two blend films, which thereby afforded much lower values of RMS roughness of 1.562.09 nm in comparison to the neat DTCCIC films. Judging from the phase images in Fig. 8d and f, clear evidence reveals that on the addition of 1% CN as an additive the scale of phase separation was markedly reduced in comparison to that without CN as an additive. The fact is that a much smaller scale of phase separation between the donor and acceptor will improve the JSC, FF, and PCE values of OSC devices.68

Conclusions

In summary, we have demonstrated that the well-known ladder-like fused-ring structure DTCC can be used to design and synthesize a high-mobility n-type organic semiconductor (DTCCIC) by the incorporation of two strongly electron-withdrawing terminal groups. For the first time, a high electron mobility of 2.17 103 cm2 V1 s1 has been demonstrated for the as-synthesized DTCC-based n-OS. Moreover, its good comprehensive properties, such as strong light-capturing ability in the region of 500720 nm and rational energy level structure (HOMO/LUMO = 5.50/3.87 eV), make DTCCIC a promising non-fullerene acceptor for solar cells. By blending PTB7-Th as the donor and DTCC as the acceptor, non-fullerene OSCs were fabricated and optimized under different conditions. We found that the device performance and morphology of blend films could be fine-tuned by changing the PTB7-Th/DTCCIC weight ratio or adding CN as an additive. An optimized OSC device fabricated from PTB7-Th/DTCCIC (1:1, w/w) blend films afforded a moderate PCE value of 4.84%. After the addition of 1% CN as an additive to this blend, the hole and electron mobilities were enhanced and the scale of phase separation was further reduced, which thereby led to significant enhancements in JSC (11.23 mA cm2), FF (0.562), and PCE (6%). This research indicates that DTCCIC with the ADA structure is a promising electron acceptor for high-performance OSCs. Therefore, the further study of novel DTCC-based electron acceptors and the investigation of their structure-performance relationship are highly promising.
