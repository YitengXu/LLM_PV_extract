Photovoltaic characteristics and dye regeneration kinetics in D149-sensitized ZnO with varied dye loading and film thickness

Abstract

Porous ZnO electrodes on fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) were prepared by electrochemical deposition from an O2-saturated ZnCl2 solution in the presence of eosin Y as a structure directing agent (SDA). Sensitization was reached by desorption of the SDA and subsequent adsorption of the indoline dye D149. The influence of film thickness and dye concentration in the films on their photovoltaic characteristics, recombination, and dye regeneration kinetics was investigated. The recombination kinetics was analyzed by time-resolved photovoltage measurements. The dye regeneration by iodide ions in the electrolyte was investigated using scanning electrochemical microscopy (SECM) feedback mode approach curves. Analysis of a SECM kinetic model shows strongly different effective D149 regeneration rate constants for D149ZnO electrodes of systematically varied film thickness and dye loading. It was found that the short-circuit current density Jsc and correlated directly with the adsorbed dye concentration. was found to be independent of the dye loading but correlated strongly with the dye concentration in the film or inversely with the film thickness. Furthermore, we discussed the perspective of correlating macroscopic cell characteristics with SECM kinetics data.

1. Introduction

Photosensitization of semiconductors with dyes for solar energy conversion has already been studied since the 1960's.1 However, interest in this field started to grow considerably after O'Regan and Grtzel reported the development of a 7% efficient dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSC) consisting of a highly porous titanium dioxide electrode loaded with the ruthenium dye Ru(dcbpy)2(-(CN)Ru(CN)(bpy)2)2 in contact with an electrolyte containing the I/I3 redox couple.2 During the past two decades, DSSCs have been investigated intensively and have gained acceptance as an alternative concept to traditional pn junction photovoltaics, because they offer significantly reduced production costs and at the same time reasonable conversion efficiencies of up to 12.3%.3 Such state-of-the-art cells could still be improved by careful optimization of sensitizer regeneration kinetics.4

Apart from TiO2, other wide-bandgap semiconductors in combination with various photosensitizers have also been studied.57 Zinc oxide has particularly attracted interest as alternative semiconductor material for DSSCs because it offers good electrical properties (e.g. a high electron mobility).8,9 In many studies it was found that the electron collection efficiency in nanostructured ZnO is high enough to allow the construction of efficient DSSCs.10,11 Devices based on ZnO, however, do not reach the performance of TiO2 cells but are severely limited by poor injection and/or regeneration of the sensitizer.10 On the other hand, ZnO offers advantages in film preparation since it can be synthesized with high crystallinity under mild conditions, which allows its deposition on temperature-sensitive substrate materials like plastics or textiles.1214 For example, electrochemical deposition in the presence of a structure-directing agent (SDA) represents a low-temperature (70 C) and low-cost method for the preparation of porous yet highly crystalline ZnO electrodes that are well suited for application in dye-sensitized solar cells.12,1517 The deposition bath usually consists of an oxygen-saturated aqueous electrolyte containing zinc chloride and the xanthene dye eosin Y as SDA.17 Interaction of the SDA with the growing zinc oxide layer results in the formation of sponge-like, nanoporous ZnO crystals with eosin Y occupying the pores.12 Following electrodeposition, the SDA can be removed from the hybrid film by soft alkaline treatment in pH 10.5 aqueous KOH.16,18 Subsequent adsorption of suitable sensitizers onto the large inner surface of the ZnO films yields active photoelectrodes for dye-sensitized solar cells.18 Many different dyes were investigated as sensitizers for ZnO, including ruthenium complexes,19,20 substituted metallophthalocyanines2123 and various fully organic dyes.2426 The highest conversion efficiency reached so far for DSSC based on electrodeposited ZnO is 5.56% and was accomplished using the fully organic indoline dye D149.12,24

Both the dye content as well as the film thickness can significantly influence the photovoltaic performance of dye-sensitized solar cells.19,27 For instance, increasing the film thickness and thus the internal surface area while keeping the total dye content constant was found to decrease the open circuit photovoltage because of the presence of additional electron recombination pathways.27 However, thicker films can be loaded with more dye and hence absorb a larger fraction of incident light.28 In thinner films, an increased dye loading will increase the aggregation probability, which promotes radiationless decay and thereby decreases the probability of electron injection.29,30

SECM has been found to be a powerful technique for probing a wide range of interfacial kinetics.31,32 SECM feedback mode approach curves based on approaching an ultramicroelectrode (UME) to a substrate and following its current response as a function of the distance from the surface offer attractive advantages as a means of studying dye regeneration kinetics in dye sensitized photoelectrochemical cells.3338 The principle of the SECM measurement of photoelectrochemical redox kinetics in DSSC in a highly localized area has been introduced in our previous report33 and is shown schematically in Fig. 1. Briefly, the dye-sensitized ZnO electrode is operated as a substrate (sample) in the SECM setup with back illumination. A Pt wire connects the fluorine doped tin oxide (FTO) back contact to the I3 electrolyte. The surface area of the wire is large compared to the illuminated area at the sample and several millimetres separated from the DSSC. If the substrate is under illumination, iodide ions electrogenerated at the UME with radius rT in a distance d (d < 5rT) to the sample rapidly diffuse to the substrate and reduce the photo-oxidized dye molecules adsorbed onto the surface and form I3 which diffuses back to the UME, where it is reduced. Therefore, the photoelectrochemical redox reaction on the sample surface induces a change of the UME current as the UME approaches the interface. Our previous study33,34 could relate the current change with I3 mediator bulk concentration [I3]* and the wavelength and/or intensity of illumination Jh using a simplified kinetic model for dye regeneration.


Fig. 1 Basic arrangement for probing the heterogeneous reaction at the dye-sensitized semiconductor surface in the SECM feedback mode under short-circuit conditions. Tri-iodide is provided in the solution bulk. The schematic is not to scale.

In this study, ZnOD149 films of different film thickness lfilm and dye loading D were prepared by varying the ZnO deposition time as well as the residence time in the D149 adsorption solution. The dye loading of the films was measured using UVVis absorption spectroscopy. Basic photovoltaic parameters of the films (open-circuit photovoltage VOC and short-circuit photocurrent Jsc) were determined from currentvoltage characterization. Time-resolved photovoltage measurements were used to derive the effective electron lifetime n.39,40 The kinetics of D149 regeneration by iodide ions in the electrolyte was studied using a SECM feedback mode approach on the films under two different experimental conditions (varying I3 mediator bulk concentration and illumination intensity). The result was interpreted using a revised SECM kinetic model for dye regeneration. The perspective of correlating the SECM kinetic parameter to the macroscopic solar cell characteristics is discussed.

2. Experimental

2.1 Preparation of D149-sensitized ZnO thin films

Porous zinc oxideeosin Y hybrid films on FTO (fluorine-doped tin oxide) coated glass substrates (Asahi glass, sheet resistance <12 square1, effective transmittance >85%) were electrodeposited at 70 C in a thermostat-controlled cell using a three-electrode arrangement with FTO glass as a working electrode, a zinc rod (Riedel-de-Han) counter electrode and a RedRod (Radiometer) reference electrode (0.04 V vs. SCE). For the deposition, 110 mL of an aqueous, 0.1 M solution of KCl (Merck, 99.5%) were filled into the deposition cell and saturated with O2 (Air Liquide, technical grade) gas. The FTO-coated glass substrate was pre-treated by immersing it into 45% HNO3 (Roth) for 2 minutes. After that, the substrate was mounted on a rotating electrode holder, contacted by an electrically conductive tape (Chomerics CCK-36-101) and fixed by an insulating tape (neo-Lab) leaving uncovered a circular active electrode area of 2 cm in diameter. The substrate was immersed into the O2-saturated KCl solution and a rotating speed of 500 rpm (Radiometer CTV101) was set. Using a Wenking potentiostat LPG03 (Bank Elektronik), a constant potential of 1.1 V vs. SCE was applied. The substrate was then activated by electrolysis in the oxygen-saturated KCl solution for 5 minutes. After this, an aqueous ZnCl2 (Merck, 98%) solution was added to the electrolyte so that the concentration of zinc chloride in the electrolyte was adjusted to 5 mM. This triggered the growth of a compact zinc oxide film (blocking layer). After another 5 minutes, an aqueous eosin Y (Kanto, 85%) solution was added to adjust the concentration to 50 M eosin Y and to initiate the deposition of a ZnOeosin Y hybrid film, which was carried out for 10, 20 or 30 minutes. Following electrodeposition, the films were rinsed with water and dried in a nitrogen flow.

To remove eosin Y out of the pores, the samples were immersed in aqueous solution (adjusted to pH 10.5) of KOH (Roth, 85%) and were irradiated with the light of two fluorescent tubes, which accelerates the eosin Y desorption process. The indoline dye D149 (5-[[4-[4-(2,2-diphenylethenyl)phenyl]-1,2,3,3a,4,8b-hexahydrocyclopent[b]indol-7-yl]methylene]-2-(3-ethyl-4-oxo-2-thioxo-5-thiazolidinylidene)-4-oxo-3-thiazolidineacetic acid synthesized as reported earlier24) was adsorbed from a solution containing 0.5 mM D149 and 1 mM chenodeoxycholic acid (Fluka BioChemika, 98.0%) in a mixture of acetonitrile (Sigma-Aldrich, 98%, anhydrous) and tert-butanol (Roth, 99.5%) (1:1 by volume). The adsorption was carried out in the dark at room temperature. Adsorption times of 15 min, 30 min, 60 min, 90 min or 120 min were used. Samples were divided and pieces of identical samples were characterized by different techniques.

The total ZnOD149 film thickness was determined from confocal laser microscopy images of small pieces cut out of the ZnOD149 films recorded with a Keyence VK-9700K. At the cutting edges, the ZnOD149 films were partly scratched off the FTO-coated glass substrate to measure the step height between FTO and the ZnOD149 films. The thickness was determined at three different sites of each piece of film and the obtained values were averaged. This method was confirmed by profilometry (Tencor Instruments -step 10-00020) performed at the border of the films (defined during deposition by the insulating tape).

Atomic absorption spectrometry was used to determine the zinc content of small pieces of the ZnOD149 samples of known area. For this purpose, the films were dissolved in 40 mL of 0.2% HNO3 solution. Atomic absorption of the solutions at a wavelength of 213.9 nm (characteristic for zinc atoms) was measured by a PerkinElmer Analyst 300. A linear calibration curve using three standard solutions containing 0.2 mg L1, 0.6 mg L1 and 1.2 mg L1 zinc was obtained. The AAS measurements yielded the zinc concentration of the sample solutions, from which the amount of zinc oxide in the film was calculated. Porosities p (void volume over total volume of the porous part of the ZnO film) were calculated from the amount of ZnO in the porous part of the film and the volume of the porous part of the film determined by the thickness lporous of the porous layer. The blocking layer thickness and the amount of ZnO in the blocking layer were estimated from the amount of charge transferred during the blocking layer electrodeposition using Faraday's law and assuming a current efficiency of 90% for the electrodeposition of compact ZnO.41

UVVis absorption spectra of the D149-sensitized ZnO films were measured in transmission using a tec5 UVVis spectrometer system (consisting of an LS-CH device for illumination and an LOE-USB device for detection) or a Lambda900 spectrometer (PerkinElmer). For the determination of the D149 loading D [mol cm2], i.e. the amount of dye per geometric sample area of the ZnOD149 films, a small piece was cut from each of the samples and was immersed into 3 mL of dimethylformamide (DMF) to dissolve D149 out of the films. When the dye had been completely dissolved, UVVis absorption spectra of the obtained solutions were recorded using a Lambda900 spectrometer (PerkinElmer). This allowed determining the amount of D149 via the LambertBeer law using an extinction coefficient of 68000 L mol1 cm1 of D149 in DMF solution at 531 nm.42 The D149 loading was then calculated by dividing this amount by the area of the piece of film determined by pixel count analysis of photographic images of the pieces using image analysis software. The ratio D/(plporous), the dye concentration referred to the total pore volume in the film, is used as a rough measure for the amount of dye per internal surface area of the films. This is valid if the pore geometry and size are assumed to be constant for all films.

2.2 Photoelectrochemical measurements

In order to allow direct comparison with the SECM measurements (Section 2.3) the photoelectrochemical measurements were performed in a three-electrode arrangement instead of a sandwich-type two-electrode cell as it would be more appropriate to provide optimum comparability to other work on DSSCs. The dye-sensitized ZnO film was mounted as a working electrode, a Pt wire as a counter electrode and a Ag/AgNO3 reference electrode with acetonitrile as internal solvent. The electrolyte was composed of a mixture of acetonitrile and ethylene carbonate (1:4 by volume) dissolving 0.5 M tetra-N-butylammonium iodide (TBAI) and 0.05 M iodine. An IviumStat potentiostat/galvanostat was employed for all measurements. For the measurement of the time-resolved photovoltage at open circuit as well as the currentvoltage curves, the illumination was performed by a 1000 W Xenon arc lamp (LOT Oriel) equipped with a water filter. Neutral density filters were used to adjust the light intensity measured by a thermopile (Laserpoint) to 100 mW cm2 and the illumination time was controlled using a mechanical shutter (Prontor magnetic E/40m, response time of 12 ms).

2.3 SECM apparatus and procedure

The basic SECM apparatus used for photoelectrochemical experiments was described previously.34 A three-electrode cell made of Teflon base with small opening from back side was placed on a home-made 2-axis tilt platform, which allowed parallel adjustment of the sensitized ZnO electrode with respect to the lateral movement of the SECM tip. A custom made potentiostat P3 (M. Schramm, University of Dsseldorf, Germany) was used to control the potential of the UME, while the current was acquired using AD/DA (PCI DAS 1602/16, plug in Elektronik, Eichenau, Germany) data acquisition card through home developed SECMx software. A solution of 0.057 mM2.01 mM KI3 in acetonitriletetra-N-butylammonium trifluoromethanesulfonate (TBAS) was used as a redox mediator. The solution was prepared by mixing equimolar amounts of KI and I2 in acetonitrileTBAS and diluting the resulting stock solution to the required concentration. KI, iodine (Fluka) and acetonitrile (Spectrochem, HPLC grade) were purified before use as described previously.34

The dye-sensitized ZnO film was illuminated through glass/FTO by a green LED with maximum emission intensity at 529 nm wavelength. The LED intensity was controlled using an XPOT potentiostat (Zahner electric, Kronach, Germany). The LED was placed close to the cell and focused on the dye-sensitized film by an objective lens (10, Carl Zeiss, Jena, Germany) so that the photo-illuminated spot had a diameter of about 0.7 mm (1.54 102 mm2). The integrated light intensity at the position of the sample was measured using a laser power meter (FieldMaster, Coherent Inc., Santa Clara, USA).

3.2 Photoelectrochemical properties of D149-sensitized ZnO photoelectrodes

Currentvoltage characteristics. Fig. 3 and Table 1a show currentvoltage (IV) characteristics of the nanoporous D149ZnO films 1.3S11.2 to 0.4S62.9 in contact with an I/I3 redox electrolyte at an illumination intensity of 100 mW cm2. Changes are observed both in open-circuit voltage (Voc) and short-circuit current density (Jsc) for the films of different lporous and D. A correlation was computed among all the quantities investigated here (Table 1b). The Jsc increases with increasing D, characteristic for an increased absorption of light. Jsc also increases with D/(plporous), even more significantly. It is thereby indicated that the efficiency of electron injection is not limited by deactivation of excited states by dye aggregates which becomes more likely at increased proximity of dye molecules on the inner ZnO surface. Close proximity of indoline dye molecules on oxide semiconductor surfaces can on the other hand lead to direct hole hopping among the molecules.43 Such additional charge transport could lead to an increased photocurrent by facilitated dye regeneration as explicitly investigated below.
Fig. 3 Currentvoltage characteristics of ZnOD149 films of different thickness and different D149 loading with white light illumination intensity of 100 mW cm2 (close to AM 1.5 conditions). The film thickness and dye loading were given in Table 1a. The electrolyte consisted of 0.05 M I2 and 0.5 M TBAI dissolved in acetonitrile:ethylenecarbonate (1:4) was used.

As can be seen from Table 1a, the open-circuit photovoltages observed for the samples analyzed in this study range from 404 mV to 515 mV. No clear correlation between Voc and any of the structural characteristics is found (Table 1b). In the present experiments Voc seems to be mainly determined by recombination processes between the semiconductor and the electrolyte and is widely independent of either blocking of recombination by adsorbed dye molecules or dye-mediated recombination. It is reasonable to assume that the observed variation of Voc stems from changes in the density and energetic distribution of trap states in ZnO which are not explicitly analyzed in the present study.

The lifetime curves obtained for the samples of different film thickness and dye loading are shown in Fig. 4. In the high voltage range the curves reflect the recombination rate that is significant for device performance under realistic conditions. In this range, typically a linear relationship between log n and Voc is expected, reflecting recombination determined by detrapping of electrons from an exponential distribution of localized states in the porous semiconductor.45 However, in the present study the curves are all slightly non-linear, indicating a non-exponential trap distribution. To analyze possible correlations of the lifetime under operating conditions with the structural characteristics of the films, the values of n at the highest photovoltage reached by all samples (0.4 V) were inserted into the correlation matrix (Table 1b). No clear correlation could be found with either of the structural parameters (lporous, p, D, D/lporous or D/(plporous)). This means that the amount of dye molecules and their distribution on the porous ZnO surface are not decisive for the overall rate of recombination in the present set of samples. Most likely the differences in the lifetimes between the six samples are caused by differences in the density of traps that influence recombination between ZnO and the electrolyte.45

For lower voltages, the shape of the curves in Fig. 4 differs significantly for the six samples. This could again be caused by different densities and distributions of localized states in the porous ZnO, in this case those with energy levels far below the conduction band level. On the other hand, it is known that recombination via the FTO-coated substrate can lead to a strong decrease of the electron lifetime in the lower voltage range.46 In this sense, the results could indicate that insulation by the blocking layer is insufficient for some of the samples, e.g.1.3S11.2 and 2.3S22.6, while it is better for others, e.g.1.8S44.6 and 0.4S62.9.

3.3 SECM measurement of the dye regeneration rate

There is a serious lack of systematic studies on the effect of dye loading and film thickness on the rate of dye regeneration. A lot of studies about film thickness and dye loading refer to macroscopic characteristics of solar cells (e.g. Voc and Jsc) and not to reaction rate e.g. rate of dye regeneration. These quantities depend on the rate of regeneration but also on rates of other phenomena. SECM feedback measurement allowed analysis of dye regeneration kinetics at a microscopic sample.33,34,38 The extent of feedback at the dye-sensitized substrate could effectively be controlled by regulating tri-iodide bulk concentration [I3]*, photon flux Jhv, dye loading D and film thickness lfilm.

SECM feedback mode approach curves on D149ZnO film electrodes in the electrolyte solution containing I3 redox species were measured with an ultramicroelectrode (UME) biased at a tip potential of 0.7 V against the Pt quasi-reference electrode with an approach speed of 2 m s1. At this potential the reduction of I3 to I at the UME is diffusion-controlled. At first, the distance of the closest approach to the surface was estimated by recording approach curves above the non-illuminated D149ZnO film substrate and comparing it to the theoretical approach curve to an electrically insulating surface using the analytical approximation of Amphlett and Denuault47 (ESI). Then, the UME was withdrawn and a series of approach curves was measured on illuminated D149ZnO films with different I3 mediator bulk concentrations [I3]* and illumination intensities expressed in photon flux Jh. The iodide I produced at the UME tip reacts at the illuminated dye-sensitized film producing a flux of I3 back to the tip. This influences the tip current compared to that observed when the tip is far from the surface. The approach curves are displayed in normalized form, in which the UME current iT and distance d from the substrate were divided by the steady state current far from the substrate iT, and UME tip radius rT, respectively.

A common feature for all curves in Fig. 5 and Fig. S1 (ESI) is that they all lie in the finite kinetics regime, indicating that heterogeneous reaction at the substrate is slower than the mediator diffusion. It must be emphasized that the low [I3]* and the comparatively large radius of the UME (rT = 12.5 m) were intentionally selected to drive the reaction into a kinetically controlled regime that allows analysis of the substrate kinetics. The normalized approach curves depend very strongly on the thickness of the film and concentration of adsorbed dye. It is found that for the given Jhv and [I3]*, keff decreased with increasing film thickness and dye loading. For example, D149 regeneration at Jh = 9.1 109 mol cm2 s1 and [I3]* = 0.124 mM yields keff of 1.03 102 cm s1 with sample 1.3S11.2, while sample 2.3S22.6 under the same conditions produces only 8.22 103 cm s1, see Table S1a (ESI). For a given sample (characterized by D, lporous, and p) one expects a decrease of keff with increasing [I3]*. An increase of [I3]* increases [I] after diffusion controlled reduction at the UME. At sample surfaces with a limited number of reaction sites, increasing the available [I] does not lead to proportionally increased reaction currents at the sample. On the other hand iT, increases proportionally to [I3]*. The normalized current IT = iT/iT, thus decreases with increasing mediator concentration at such samples. Similar effects are common for the investigation of immobilized redox enzymes.49,50

Analysis of keff for dye regeneration rate. In order to relate the calculated curve to the more complicated case of dye regeneration, we followed an example of Liu et al.32 for relating the kinetics at ferrocene-terminated self-assembled monolayers to SECM feedback curves. As confirmed by various fitting procedures, a considerable difference is observed in values among the six D149ZnO electrodes of varied lporous and D. It is found that values are negatively correlated to the thickness of the ZnO layers but positively correlated to the dye loading D (Table 1b). Our very simplified model assumes uniform accessibility of the dye molecules with respect to illumination and I diffusion. The obtained values are therefore effective parameters. This would be the real value if all dye molecules would have uniform accessibility for light and mediator. The effective quantities provide consistent descriptions if one electrode is analyzed under different mediator conditions (within the validity range of the model, i.e. at low mediator concentrations (1 mM) and low Jh). However, when comparing different samples, the internal mass transport processes of I inside the films become more important with increasing lporous and the effective values tend to decrease with increasing electrode thickness as a consequence of the limitations by internal mass transport processes. We have seen an analogous behavior for the SECM investigations of immobilized enzymes in multilayer arrangements of intentionally reduced complexity.61,62 The situation in the DSSC is considerably more complex and it is remarkable that a similar conclusion could be drawn here. The other important parameter h came out as constant for all electrodes in our set. This is reasonable because it depends on the extinction coefficient of the dye which can be considered constant even at high packing densities of D149 since only very little spectral broadening was observed for D149 adsorbed on ZnO.

It was found that shows direct correlation with D/(plporous) which also correlated directly with Jsc. The quantity D/(plporous) is taken as a measure for the amount of dye molecules per internal surface area. This is justified if the pore geometry and pore size are assumed to be constant for all films. Correlation to this quantity would indicate effects caused by the lateral distance between dye molecules on the surface. This shows that the distance between the dye molecules is of importance for the dye regeneration. With increasing concentration of D149 molecules, increases, i.e. the regeneration of oxidized dye molecules is facilitated by hole conduction within adsorbed layers of D149, as also concluded from independent cyclic voltammetry at D149 adsorbed to TiO2.43 The short-circuit photocurrent shows the same increase with D/(plporous) indicating the dependence on dye regeneration rate. It is concluded that regeneration of the dye molecules rather than charge transport through the semiconductor matrix is limiting the photocurrent in the present system. Since no influence of the dye concentration on the open circuit photovoltage or the electron lifetime is seen, neither a blocking nor a facilitation of recombination by the dye molecules is detected in the present experiments.

4. Conclusions

The photovoltaic performance, electron recombination, and dye regeneration kinetics by the I/I3 redox couple, all relevant in dye-sensitized solar cells, were studied depending on film thickness and dye loading of D149-sensitized ZnO photoelectrodes. It was found that the photocurrent increased with the total dye content D and, even more clearly, with the dye concentration on the inner surface D/(plporous). Close proximity of the sensitizer molecules seems to assist the photoelectrochemical efficiency rather than decreasing it by radiationless decay in dye aggregates. The photovoltage and the electron lifetime, however, showed no clear correlation to neither D nor D/(plporous) suggesting that they are mainly determined by the electronic structure of the semiconductor. From SECM feedback approach curve analysis, considerably different effective heterogeneous first order rate constants for the D149 regeneration are observed among the photoelectrodes of systematically varied thickness and dye loading. It is found that does not depend on D but correlates very strongly with D/(plporous). This suggests that dye regeneration is heavily supported by close proximity of the sensitizer molecules. By systematic variation of film thickness and dye loading, non-uniform accessibility of dye molecules for I as well as unequal light intensity reaching dye molecules in different vertical distances from the back contact become more pronounced. Further refinement of the SECM model avoiding the assumption of a constant accessibility of the dye molecules by the redox electrolyte is necessary in order to incorporate structural variations within the pore system and to more directly address functional characteristics of dye-sensitized ZnO films from different characterization methods into predictive models. The aggregation effects of the dye could be important and the dye loading may influence the tendency for recombination. The strong scattering in the films makes a description of the local light intensity difficult. In addition it turned out to be challenging to prepare films in which only one parameter is changed while all other structural parameters are kept constant. Furthermore, gradients in dye concentration across the film thickness cannot be excluded because even after long adsorption times, the dye loading still seems to increase for a given material thickness. Furthermore, surface effects (ion absorption, Donnan exclusion) not considered in the model may influence the response. Adsorption or association of negatively charged ions within the pores in ZnO or at the dye molecules may hinder the access of negatively charged I into the pores. The extent of this effect will depend on the mediator concentration but also on the pore diameter that seems to vary between the preparations.
