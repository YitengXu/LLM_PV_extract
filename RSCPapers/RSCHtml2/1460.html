The state-of-the-art in the electroreduction of NOx for the production of ammonia in aqueous and nonaqueous media at ambient conditions: a review

Yuxian Fan , Xiang Xue , Lingyue Zhu , Yuwei Qin , Dandan Yuan , Di Gu and Baohui Wang *
College of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Northeast Petroleum University, Daqing 163318, P. R. China. E-mail: wangbh@nepu.edu.cn

Received 31st December 2022 , Accepted 26th February 2023

First published on 27th February 2023

Abstract

The synthesis of ammonia through the HaberBosch process has indirectly led to significant CO2 emissions. Electrochemical synthesis of ammonia from NOx under ambient conditions is key for achieving resource conservation and nitrogen cycle equilibrium. Herein, the state-of-the-art of the NOx reduction reaction to ammonia (NOxRR-to-NH3) is totally reviewed, with aspects on aqueous and nonaqueous electrolytes. In this context, the functional mechanism is briefly outlinedin aqueous and nonaqueous media, respectively, and the electrocatalyst design as well as the electrolyte modification in both solvents is summarized in detail. N2 reduction in ionic liquids is presented, which provides meaningful suggestions for the electrocatalytic NOxRR-to-NH3 process in such media. The principal challenges faced by this field were discussed in the review. The primary obstacles are the disfavourable chemical kinetics, which includes the inert dynamics of NOx and hydrogen evolution reaction during the reduction process. Secondly, developing cost-efficient and performance-superior electrocatalysts and electrolyte systems is urgent for addressing such issues. At last, the outlook for future investigation is proposed.

1 Introduction

As a vital commercial chemical,1 ammonia (NH3) is the only carbon-free energy carrier with a hydrogen content of 17.6 wt% and is considered an essential hydrogen storage medium in the future energy economy.2 There are 150 million tons of ammonia produced every year, 90% of which is attained through the HaberBosch process, which is energetically intensive and environmentally adverse as it suffers from harsh conditions (400500 C, 1525 MPa) and releases a vast bulk of greenhouse gas.3 The electrochemical method is regarded as a promising way to produce NH3 under mild conditions, while N2 used as the nitrogen source in the traditional way is restricted to its intense stability, nonpolar properties, low proton affinity, undesirable solubility in water (0.69 mM, 25 C),4 as well as the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) in an aqueous solution, leading to unexpected ammonia production rate (<10 mmol gcat1 h1). NOx has attracted interest due to its easily activated bond (204 kJ mol1) and relatively high solubility (1.9410.73 M, 25 C), even NO (1.94 mM) and N2O (0.38 M) with low solubility are still favourable than N2.5 NOx reduction reaction to ammonia (NOxRR-to-NH3) is an alternative way to convert NOx to valuable NH3.6

Great efforts have been made to reduce NO3 and NO2 in wastewater, with N2,7,8 HNO3,9 NH3,1012etc., as the products. NOx is health-threatening and there are several environmental issues caused such as ozone depletion and acid rain. Removing NOx from flue gases is equally important. The gaseous pollutant NOx is mainly produced from power plants, industrial processes, and stationary fuel combustions where nearly 5% NO2 and 95% NO are generated from fossil fuel combustion. NO2 and N2O will be reduced to NO and N2, respectively, after two protonated steps. In this perspective, we concentrate on the research of NOxRR-to-NH3 from NO. Post-combustion methods are adopted in the application by removing or destructing NOx from flue gas, such as selective catalytic reduction (SCR), selective noncatalytic reduction (SNCR), wet scrubbing method, adsorption, and nonthermal plasma. Though high efficiency and hazard-free disposal have made such techniques available, there exist major impediments due to which industrial techniques cannot make a good trade-off between convenient operation and economic efficiency, for example, catalyst waste for SCR/SNCR, liquid waste from wet scrubbing method, and stringent operation conditions for adsorption. Nevertheless, the existing commercial application in an electrochemical way is reducing NO to the useless N2.13 Such a method requires NO oxidized by an LSC oxidation catalyst to NO2 and then converted to N2 and O2via an electrochemical cell. Unlike other NOx reduction technologies, the electrochemical method does not require valuable reducing agents such as NH3 and H2, and the needed power can be provided by renewable energy. Furthermore, reducing NOx to high-value NH3 is conducive to a winwin situation of economic benefit and environment friendliness. The electroreduction of NOx for the production of ammonia is an ideal technique for NOx removal. Attaining ideal catalytic selectivity and activity simultaneously is the primary goal of the electrochemical synthesis of ammonia (ESA).14

There are three coping strategies to elevate the undesirable NH3 yields (YNH3) and faradaic efficiency (FE) of NOxRR-to-NH3 under mild conditions: (1) catalyst design, including interface engineering,15 construction optimization,16 defect engineering,17 heteroatomic doping,1824 and cooperative effect;2527 (2) electrolyte modification, which is comprised of aqueous environment28,29 and nonaqueous environment;30 (3) operating conditions, mainly about applied potentials and introduced NO concentrations. Since water can be a likely inhibitor of HER, ionic liquids (ILs) are often considered as a substitution for aqueous solutions, previous studies have paid attention to NH3 synthesis from N2 in ILs; however, NOxRR-to-NH3 in ILs is somehow few. Herein, we focus on the state-of-the-art of NOxRR-to-NH3 in both aqueous and nonaqueous solutions under ambient conditions. Combined with the role of ILs in HER suppression, nonpolar gas binding, and N2/NOx reduction, the feasibility of NOxRR-to-NH3 to produce ammonia in ILs was prospected.

2 Electrocatalytic NOxRR-to-NH3 in aqueous solutions

2.1 Mechanism studies

2.1.1 Reaction pathway. In contrast to N2 reduction reaction to ammonia (NRR-to-NH3),3133 NOxRR-to-NH3 is a favourable mechanism. Unlike N2, lone electrons exist only in the N of NOx, making NOx more nucleophilic. Metal catalysts possess electrically neutral surfaces, resulting in the further strengthening of the metalNO reaction. NH3 and H2O were obtained by NOx adsorption and hydrogenation in the electrochemical process. As shown in Fig. 1, NO2 and N2O will be reduced to NO and N2 after two protonation steps, followed by NORR-to-NH3 and NRR-to-NH3 processes, respectively. Wang et al.18 proved that the product selectivity is related to the adsorption mode of the catalyst-NO. O-end adsorption makes NN coupling (N2O) more available, and N-end adsorption drives NH3 generation. Fortunately, the latter case is more available.19 Such processes follow either associative or dissociative mechanisms. Both reaction methods are given below.

*NO + 3H+ + 3e *NH3 + *O(2)

There are two types of hydrogenation processes of NOxRR-to-NH3: the Tafel-type route and the Heyrovsky-type route, where the hydrogen source is H* and H+, respectively, and where H* is the adsorbed state formed by solvated protons on the catalyst surface. Hence, four types of NOxRR-to-NH3 mechanisms could be proposed associative-Tafel (A-T), association-Heyrovsky (A-H), dissociation-Tafel (D-T), and dissociation-Heyrovsky (D-H) mechanisms.16 When the dissociative mechanism is followed, *N and *O form on the catalyst surface after the NO bond is broken. Further protonation results in the formation of NH3 and H2O. The associative process that is mostly spontaneous becomes more favourable than the dissociative one, where the NO bond is activated instead of broken. The hydrogenation process, however, can either follow distal or alternative association. The former process involves the complete hydrogenation of the distally-associated atom (O or N), followed by the reduction of the other atom (N or O). However, the latter case is completed by the alternate hydrogenation of O and N. NH3 and H2O can then be released by the reduction of the intermediate HxNOHy. In addition, the potential determining step (PDS) of the NOxRR-to-NH3 process is likely related to the binding energy of the NO-catalyst.19,34

2.1.2 Theoretical simulations. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations were used in finding outstanding catalysts, which determine the relationship between the catalytic performance and surface adsorption energy. The calculated Gibbs free energy (G = nF), which is negative, facilitates the reaction to occur spontaneously, whereas the positive one renders reactions nonspontaneous. Therefore, the situation of G(*NO) < 0 and G(*H) > 0 is the most beneficial scenario for NORR-to-NH3. Mechanistically speaking, a group of metals (Au, Ag, Zn, and the p-block metals Pb and Bi) exhibits nonspontaneous intermediate (*NO and *NOH) adsorption in the range of 0.140.34 eV and 0.160.85 eV, respectively, with no clear dependence on G(*H)34 (as shown in Fig. 2a and b, the green shaded area indicates where G(*NO)/G(*NOH) < G(*H)). In addition to Au (just 0.05 eV positive than G(*H)), the adsorption of *NO by selected metals is more positive than that of H*. The theoretical calculation was improved by the experiment carried toward Au NP/C in acidic solutions (pH = 3.5), in which 30% FE (0.3 V vs. RHE) was obtained in NORR but there was no activity in NRR.35 It is proved that such transition d-metals could act as available active sites for NORR in aqueous solutions. The NORR profiles for Au, Ag, Zn, Pb, and Bi that are relatively inferior are summarized in Fig. 2c. Inspiringly, *NO binding in most metals all preferred over *H adsorption. Thermodynamic barriers indicate the rate-determining step (RDS) and NH3 desorption-free energies of NOxRR-to-NH3. In this view, the reduction potential was calculated at about 0.16 to 0.85 V vs. CHE (computational hydrogen electrode), indicating that the formation of NH2 is the RDS of NOxRR-to-NH3. Compared to the five-electron process of NORR-to-NH3, the reduction of NO2 and N2O, respectively, undergoes seven-electron and eight-electron processes. Thus, both processes are relatively kinetics disadvantaged. Bismuth has been simulated to be the efficient catalyst for NO2RR. The adsorption of NO2 on the metal surface is not stable but easily decomposed. From the perspective of N2O reduction, several metals (Ti, Mo, V) facilitate the reduction process but are less favourable than HER. Even though the free energy of many metals, such as Zn, Pd, and Bi, is relatively more negative than HER, the reduction is still nonspontaneous. Furthermore, the binding free energies of N2O are highly correlated to N2, whereas NRR-to-NH3 is more favourable in kinetics. As a primary greenhouse gas, N2O mitigation still has a long way to run.

2.2.1 Research in neutral conditions. A couple of previous studies on NOxRR-to-NH3 have paid enough attention to neutral medium. Efficient and stable catalysts are particularly essential for complex electrocatalytic processes. The multiproton-coupled electron transfer (PCET) process is considered the RDS of NOxRR-to-NH3. Owing to the proton donation capability and abundant active sites, transition metal-based catalysts usually exhibit excellent performance in mitigating the kinetic issues, which efficaciously overcomes the obstacles in NO activation and hydrogenation.37 Based on DFT calculations, Long et al.16 revealed that Cu exhibited excellent activity in the NOxRR-to-NH3 process (as shown in Fig. 3a and b), where the production of H2 (135.7 mol cm2 h1) was only 0.18 times that in Pt at 0.9 V (vs. RHE), Cu foam showed high activity toward FE and YNH3 reaching as high as 93.5% and 517.1 mol cm2 h1, respectively. In light of the problem of mass transport in the reaction process, the porous structure can offer efficient surface area and remarkable accessibility, promoting the contact with the reactant and the diffusion of intermediates, which is the reason that the NH3 rates (9234 mol g1 h1) surprisingly achieved the level of the HB process but operated in ambient conditions. Heteroatomic doping is popular in catalysts modified to decrease the Gibbs free energy of RDS, resulting in the improvement of NOxRR-to-NH3. Shi et al.39 added Ru into Cu, which induces the center of the d-band of C far away from the Fermi level and the RDS (*NO *NHO) prefers to occur (as shown in Fig. 3c). The cost of the gathering device was considered, and NO/Ar (1/4) was introduced as the nitrogen source. Ru0.05Cu0.95 exhibited a good NO conversion selectivity to NH3 (FE of 64.9% at 1.1 V vs. Ag/AgCl), but the excessive doping of Ru will give rise to HER. Heteroatom doping of metal oxide is able to effectively create oxygen vacancies (VO) as active sites promoting NO adsorption as well as lowering the reaction energy barriers.53 Inspired by this, Chen et al.25 decorated Cu on the surface of porous TiO2 and discovered that Cu/PTiO2 was catalytically more characteristic for NH3 generation (3520.80 g h1 mg1YNH3 and 86.49% FE at 0.3 V vs. RHE) due to the 3D structure enabling the exposure of more active sites and the achievement of better current density. Such a nanoscale system promoted the kinetics of the electrode reaction.38 Meanwhile, crystallographic tailoring at the atom-scale through that finely exposes specific facets is significant for enhancing the performance, which is beneficial from the crystal facets effect, resulting from the atomic arrangement on different exposed crystal facets.54 Lin et al.42 provides a facile route for achieving excellent NH3 yield (1194 g h1 mgcat.1) and FE (89.2%) at 0.5vs. RHE through Bi-based nanocatalysts (as shown in Fig. 6d and e). DFT calculations revealed that the binding and activation of NO were effectively conducted on the Bi(012) surfaces through a two-way charge transfer mode. Such engineering sheds light on the in-depth research on surface speciality-catalysts activity; however, attention to metal dopant sites by controlling the reaction pathways is unfortunately absent. From the viewpoint of construction optimization, multimetal catalysts reveal outstanding electrocatalytic properties in cooperative effect, electronic tuning, and accessible adsorption of intermediates superior to single or double metal catalysts. Such efforts have been blossoming toward NRR-to-NH3 but studies in NOxRR-to-NH3 are not paid enough attention.55

Combining electrocatalysts with hierarchical substrates, an interfacial engineering strategy has been demonstrated as a promising method for strongly coupled hybrid electrocatalyst design.56 In this context, He et al.26 evaluated the electron structure and adsorption characteristics for NO of the two-dimensional metal borides (MBenes). Acting as an electron transport channel, bridging bonds between multimetals are propitious to the reaction kinetics, which lower the overpotential of the reduction reaction. Therefore, MBenes can be used as an ideal catalyst for future NOxRR-to-NH3 research.

2.2.2 Research in acidic conditions. Many pioneering works have been devoted to proving that an acidic environment is conducive to NOxRR-to-NH3.57 Early in 1996, Otsuka and his co-worker58 demonstrated that the yield of hydroxylamine was nearly 100% under acidic conditions (H2SO4) instead of that in alkaline solutions (KOH). However, they did not further explain the relationship between electrolytes and reaction. It is worth mentioning that an acidic environment may intensify the influence of HER. Hence, tremendous efforts are required to develop efficient and preferable cheap catalysts, which can balance NH3 synthesis and other competitive reactions. Carro et al.34 analyzed the binding properties of the intermediates and H species on a series of transition metals (as shown in Fig. 2). They revealed that the adsorption of *NO was better than that of *H in all metals except for Au; even Au with poor performance still reached an FE of 30% (0.3 V vs. RHE) under acidic conditions (pH = 3.5), improving the competitive HER that was suppressed among all the metal surfaces.

Ascribed to the competitive HER, catalysts with outstanding catalytic activity are particularly important. There are two main perspectives for catalyst performance improvementdimension regulation and morphology adjustment. From the viewpoint of dimension regulation, nanosized catalysts, such as nanotubes, nanoparticles, and single-atom (SA), enlarge the scope of atom utilization, increase the unsaturated active sites, and possess distinct size quantum effects. Single-atom catalysts (SACs) supported on several carbon substrates are commonly introduced in the study of NOxRR-to-NH3 in acidic environments1820 (as shown in Fig. 4bd). SACs are small in size, with particles dispersed on the surface of the substrate to fully expose active sites, which reduces the cost of using precious metals and achieves nearly 100% atom utilization. In addition, the performance of SAC is determined by the atomic properties of the doping metal and the strong interaction between the metal and the substrate. Owing to the excellent conductivity and expected transport capacity, defective graphene has been applied as a traditional support. Attributed to the superior activity toward NO fixation, the investigation of SA loaded on N-containing supports, especially C with four pyrrole-N (g-C3N4), raises tremendous interest but the risks of contaminations from the support decomposition should be taken into consideration. However, the exact active sites of SACs that are obscured for most of the catalytic processes are proposed at the surrounding support atoms instead of the doped metal. The construction of SACs should also be paid more attention to, including SAs loading content and supported stability. Also, the relationship between the catalyst structure and catalytic mechanisms at the atom-scale are required for deeper exploration through theoretical calculations.

From the perspective of morphology adjustment, nanoscale multidimension catalysts possess unique physicochemical properties, which directly provide an efficient mass transfer pathway and facilitate the transportation of electrons, such as 1D nanotubes, 2D nanosheets,47 and 3D porous/hollow structure.45 The in situ array growth is one of the facile strategies for catalyst morphology modification, which intensifies the binding between the support and doped metals.47 Meanwhile, element doping or defect engineering provides more active sites, and increases the specific surface area and pore channels.59 Transition metal phosphides/sulfides are appropriate for inhibiting HER, which are frequently applied to NOxRR-to-NH3 and NRR-to-NH3 studies in acidic media. Mou et al.47 loaded Ni2P on carbon paper (Ni2P/CP) and found that 3 Ni atoms formed a threefold hollow site, and the Ni2P(111) surface offers a good coordination environment so that the adsorption and activation of NO are effectively improved via an acceptancedonation mechanism, thereby improving the selectivity of the product (as shown in Fig. 5a and c). Zhang et al.45 anchored MoS2 nanosheet to graphite felt (MoS2/GF) by the in situ growth method. They demonstrated that the electron accumulation of the antibonding orbital and the electrons depletion of the bonding orbital caused by the two-way mode for charge transfer on the MoS2(101) surface led to NO bond weakening, similar to the N2 activation mechanism (as shown in Fig. 5b and d), which further confirmed Mou's conclusion. In the fuel cell system, Kim et al.29 took Fe-SAC as the catalyst, and it was found that NH2OH and NH3 were limited by the multiproton coupled electron transfer (PCET) process, which proved that the generation of NH3 was pH-dependent and low pH was favourable, unlike NO removal from the anode.60 On the contrary, the formation of N2O was pH-independent but NN coupled under more negative potentials. This was because charge accumulates at high pH, and Fe-II-NH3 states containing antibonding singly occupied molecular orbitals were destabilized in such a situation. Xiong et al.28 also proved the correlation between NOxRR-to-NH3 and electrolyte pH. Although Au performed less well in the NOxRR-to-NH3 process, Au/rGO surprisingly got a higher FE of 98.3% under acidic conditions (pH = 1). Also, they inferred the possibility of NO-protonation preceding NO-adsorption.

2.2.3 Research in alkaline conditions. Rapid advances have been achieved in the attractive research field for NOxRR-to-NH3 in acidic electrolyte, and existing catalysts reveal superior performance toward NH3 selectivity improvement and suppression of by-product (N2, NH2OH) formation. Abundant hydrogen protons are also required for the reduction reaction. It has been proposed that NOxRR-to-NH3 process is pH-dependent, and acidic environment is more favourable, which will be discussed in Section 2.3.1. Intriguingly, the local acidic electrolyte is no longer acidic at high current densities (>200 mA cm2), similar to that in alkaline conditions. Unfortunately, there is hardly any study highlighting NOxRR-to-NH3 in alkaline media; hence, the exploration of a pH-universal catalyst for NOxRR-to-NH3 is expected. Studies of pH-universal catalyst have obtained significant attention in the NRR-to-NH3 procedure. Interestingly, it is proposed that the alkali metal ion K+ (KOH) is favourable for donating electrons.52,61

In terms of structural optimization, inspired by the multicatalyst design, Arif et al.51 introduced CoVP into NiFeV-LDHs nanosheets (CoVP@NiFeV-LDHs), and the hierarchical structure catalyst with hollow interior and ultrathin exterior were obtained (as shown in Fig. 6ac). Benefiting from the ultrathin feature, the catalyst reveals superior function in mass transfer, structural stability, and catalytic activity, promoting diffusion kinetics. The average NH3 yield and FE are 1.54 106 mol h1 cm2 and 12.9% in 1.0 M KOH, and 1.6 106 mol h1 cm2 and 13.8% in 0.05 M H2SO4 at 0.3 V vs. RHE, respectively. Ascribed to the cage effect, the confinement of reaction intermediates enhances the stability of pivotal intermediate concentration in RDS. This work makes the catalyst appropriate for NRR research at various pH; however, it is still challenging to prepare the catalyst with ultrafine hierarchical structure within the limitations of the existing technique.

Metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) are a class of metal-based nanomaterials obtained with controllable morphology. The POMOFs-derived strategies are a novel approach for morphology regulation, viz., the homogeneous distribution of polyoxometalates (POMs) in MOFs composite, which could generate uniform nanocrystallites and provide a benign cooperative environment. Considering that Fe and Mo are the natural active sites for azofixase in bionic perspectives, Feng et al.50 inserted iron and molybdenum disulfides in the reduced graphene oxide matrix to form FeS2MoS2-based bimetallic sulfide composites (FeS2MoS2@RGO), and they revealed that the synergistic effects on the heterointerface promote the NRR performance. DFT calculation shows that the strong FeN bond and moderate FeNH3 bond led to a pleasing situation for N2 adsorption and activation, which establish new possibilities to probe pH-universal catalysts in a wide pH range with a fast reaction rate.

Notably, Mukherjee et al.52 put forward that Fe doping likely blocks the N vacancy in the MOF-derived N-doped carbon catalyst, resulting in severe HER. Derived from the well-defined ZIF precursors (zinc-based zeolitic imidazolate frameworks) via one-step thermal activation, Mukherjee provided a new insight into metal-free carbon catalyst design. This metal-free catalyst is function-controllable and morphology-tailorable, achieved by heating tuning (7001100 C). Compared to the NRR process in 0.1 M KOH and NaOH (as shown in Fig. 7ac), they found that temperature is positively correlated with the yield because the graphitization extent and nitrogen doping are regulated to the heating operations in which the electronic and geometric structures of carbon are modified. The catalyst activity obtained in optimal conditions could sustain excellent stability after 18 hours. Yet, the result in the acidic medium deviates from pre-conclusion due to the serious competitive HER. pH-universal catalysts mentioned above consistently show excellent stability with increasing cycle numbers in various conditions (as shown in Fig. 7dg).

2.3 Electrolyte modifications

2.3.1 pH of solvents. The limited solubility of gases in aqueous solution would disturb pH as well as mass transfer.69 It is a promising approach to control proton concentration by adjusting the pH or adding counterions in aqueous solution. Several studies were carried out in a neutral environment and most of them focused on catalyst designing with excellent performance, promoting the occurrence of NOxRR-to-NH3 and suppressed the HER competitive reaction, which was ubiquitous in the aqueous solution. It is worth noting that NO behaves with poor solubility in aqueous solution; thus, electrolyte modification is an alternative. Fe-based catalysts have been proven to work favorably in electrochemical ammonia synthesis.32,7073 In alkaline conditions, using an MEA membrane battery, Kong et al.61 first demonstrated that -Fe2O3 can be used as NRR-to-NH3 reductant, but the yields of NH3 (58.9 nmol h1 cm2 at 0.4 V vs. RHE) and FE (1.96% at 0 V vs. RHE) were undesirable. The reaction process was limited by the solution pH and cannot be carried out under acidic conditions. The same issue also exists for the MEA membrane electrode, where decreasing pH affected the selectivity of NH3. Recently, Liang et al.40 took Fe2O3(104) as the NOxRR-to-NH3 catalyst in an airtight three-electrode system under a neutral environment. Fe2O3(104) worked excellently (YNH3 of 8.02 mol h1 cm2 at 0.6 V vs. RHE and FE of 86.73% at 0.4 V vs. RHE), even though the concentration of NO was low (10 vol% NO, 30 sccm). This was because the special electronic properties enabled NO to bind strongly to the Fe2O3(104) surface, and the 2* back-donation effect favored the superior selectivity and activity of NO-NH3. HER was able to be suppressed at lower potentials but only happened at 0.6 V, where the FE of H2 was only 1.07%. The measured power density of the ZnNO battery with Fe2O3/CP as the cathode material was 1.18 mW cm2. YNH3 and FE reached 145.28 g h1mgcat1 and 82.5%, respectively. Most interestingly, pH increased from 7 to 12 after electrolysis, which was caused by the accumulation of NH4+ and the generation of OH in the electrolyte. Such a case could not exist stably in alkaline medium, and so did N2H4. In acidic medium (pH 0.5), Ko et al.44 found Cu showed superior activity toward NH3. The NH3 yield rates reached as high as 1806 mol h1 cm2 (0 V vs. RHE) and the FE was nearly 100% (Fig. 9). However, the FE was lower than 80 under alkaline medium. The partial electrolyte was no longer acidic under high current density, which confirmed Liang's conclusions. Remarkably, the use of a ZnNO battery drove the NOxRR-to-NH3 for Zn dissolution. Zhang et al.45 investigated the performance of MoS2/GF in both saturated NOHCl and NOKOH. It was found that MoS2/GF was inactive under alkaline conditions, while in acid electrolyte, the peak values of YNH3 and FE were 99.6 mol h1 cm2 (0.7 V vs. RHE) and 76.6% (0.1 V vs. RHE), respectively, which were superior to NRR-to-NH375,76 and NOxRR-to-NH339 in other aqueous electrolytes. Under the same cases, the yield of NH3 in ZnNO battery with MoS2/GF cathode reached 411.8 g h1 mgcat1, and the battery power density 1.04 mW cm2 was superior to other metal N2 batteries.77,78 The positively charged Mo edge sites were conducive to the adsorption and activation of NO but not favourable to proton binding and NN coupling. Therefore, in a certain range of electrochemical windows (0.10.5 V vs. RHE), the performance of NOxRR-to-NH3 was better than HER, and the YNH3 was 1.13 and 7.54 times that of N2H4 and H2 at 0.3 V vs. RHE, respectively. In addition, the reaction of NH* + OH* and HER was exothermic, resulting in the inhibition of the formation of the by-product N2O, which was endothermic.

2.3.3 Applied potentials. Early in the last century, operating in acidic conditions, Colucci et al.81 discovered that NH3 formed under low potentials (<0.1 V), and the existence of oxides disturbed the metal catalyst surface to form other by-products such as HNO2 and NO2. However, recent studies elucidated that low potentials would raise serious HER issues. The chalcogen atoms are favourable to improving the H+ activation. Compared with other neutral liquids, ILs are unique in properties such as low volatility, high thermal stability, wide electrochemical windows, and large absorption capacity, which stabilize the electrochemical system and purify the products, further improving some issues in aqueous solvents such as evaporation and deposition.84 ILs enable catalysts to possess special properties, which include dispersion improvement and adsorption site enhancement.85 ILs brought the electrode/catalyst with superior inorganic/organic hierarchical lamellar micro/nanostructures. As described above, ILs can obtain desirable properties through the selection of appropriate anions and cations, which is an important aspect to distinguish them from other aqueous electrolytes. However, the explicit mechanism of its function in electrocatalytic reduction needs to be paid more attention by researchers. In situ characterization is one of the significant estimate methods for mechanism study and product analysis, which is particularly beneficial for the study of the multistep reaction process or that concluding various intermediates, such as in situ infrared (IR) spectroscopy, in situ Raman spectroscopy, in situ EPR spectroscopy, and operando SHINERS.

3.2.2 Research in LiMEAS. Ignoring water molecules, lithium acts as nonaqueous organic electrolytes, such as tetrahydrofuran (THF) or lithium perchlorate, which could behave well in NH3 selectivity and gain higher current efficiency.140 Along with that, lithium-mediated electrochemical ammonia synthesis (LiMEAS) has become a research hotspot.141 Solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) formed through cathodic reduction suppressed HER efficiently, inducing ammonia synthesis more favorably. However, the yield of NH3 was suppressed after 2 h because the products of NH3/NH4+ accumulated on the active site of the catalyst. Hence, efforts are in great need to develop efficient reaction systems. Gas diffusion electrodes (GDE) have received tremendous interest due to their excellent mobility. In the aqueous system, GDE was used to improve mass transport through efficient gas-liquid contact, while the application of GDE in a nonaqueous solution was restrained. This is because the hydrophobic electrolyte will combine with the carbon fiber structure and suppress the active site of the catalyst. Importantly, with the gas diffusion distance increased, it was difficult to obtain the desired current density. It is necessary to improve the hydrodynamics and reduce the thickness of the boundary layer to elevate the transport limit current density.142 Lazouski et al.142 designed a lithium-mediated approach with a stainless-steel substrate, which improved the application of nonaqueous electrolytes in GDE (as shown in Fig. 20a and b). The current density of NH3 was 8.8 1.4 mA cm2, and the FE reached 35 6%. Within this system, N diffused selectively to the electrode surface via proton donors. Notably, the proton source is a key factor determining the performance and lifespan of the whole system, but most of the proton donors (alcohols, H2) usually added are nonrenewable.145,146 As shown in Fig. 20c, Suryantol et al.137 introduced phosphorus salt proton shuttle, a substitution for a traditional proton source, to realize the regeneration and utilization of proton donors. Besides, [P6,6,6,14][eFAP] showed exceptional solubility of N and ionic conductivity, improving the electrodeposition of Li at the cathode. However, the reaction rate of phosphorus salt was low. Regrettably, all nonaqueous approaches investigated to date still face great challenges. On the one hand, it is difficult to reclaim and separate the organic solvent due to its low conductivity and volatility; on the other hand, there is a trade-off between the good performance of the ILs and their high cost as well as corrosive and polluting nature.

3.3 ILs for denitrogenation

3.3.1 Adsorption of NOx. NOx is kinetically inert to many common reactants, yet ILs have been proven to behave well in nitrogen-containing pollutants for fuel removal.73 On the active sites containing negatively-charged N atoms and O atoms on functional groups, ILs are favourable for gas capture, such as amine groups, phenolate anions, azolate anions, and imide anions.147,148 The commonly used ionic liquids for denitrogenation are shown in Fig. 21. In particular, the lone pair electrons contained in the N atom of neutral N-compounds can participate in the ring conjugation with strong electron density, resulting in stronger interaction with ionic liquid. To improve the NOx reduction, ionic liquids containing electron-absorbing substituents can be considered. Previous research elucidated that changing the electrolyte pKa was also accessible for the acidity positively related to the electron stretching ability.149 In all cases, imidazole ionic liquids are suitable for the absorption of NOx150 because the positive charge of imidazole avoids the electrophilic attack of NO2+ or similar substances. Kunov-Kruse et al.9 compared [BMIM][NO3] with the nitrate solution (NaNO3) of the same molarity (as shown in Fig. 22). They suggested that ILs were an attractive solution, and they combined them with water to form a high boiling point azeotrope with strong hydrogen bonding mode, which enabled it to remain stable at higher temperatures (240400 C). Considering water and oxygen in flue gas, the final product of the reaction was HNO3. The uncharged organic molecules or other impurities were completely oxidized and decomposed into CO2 and water, then heated to the regeneration temperature (120 C), realizing efficient self-cleaning and ionic liquid reclamation.

3.3.2 Reduction of NOx. Studies on NOx abatement in ILs primarily focused on absorption47,152 or gas sensors153,154 and were barely noticed in the field of electroreduction. On the other hand, the feasibility of transformation from NO/NO2 to ammonia has been proved by existing research via iron and cobalt porphyrin complexes.155157 The application of Fe(EOP)(NO) in molecular acid has been established by Ryan's group,158160 and ammonia is the final product for the reduction of [Fe(OEP)(NO)] in weak acids. They further discovered that less acidic protonated 1,8-diazabicyclo(5.4.0)undec-7-ene ([DBU-H]+),151 which could only be reduced at a relatively negative potential, has an excellent performance in NO reduction with molecular solvent (MS) THF through in situ analysis versus that in molecular acid. The structures of the anion of the molecular acid ([R3NH]+) and PIL([DBU-H]+) are shown in Fig. 23a. It revealed that PIL first only formed HB with NO complexes. The reaction pathway and cyclic voltammetry of PIL ([DBU-H]PF6) versus MS(R3NHOTf) are shown in Fig. 23b and c, respectively. Due to the nanodomains formed within PIL/RTIL-MS, which promoted the interaction of acid protons and anionic species entropically favorable, facilitated charge neutralization, and provided protons required by PCET reaction, the subsequent activation will be easier and the NH3 selectivity will be higher. Hydroxylamine will be re-oxidized in PIL theoretically. Interestingly, due to the existence of conjugated bases in the nanodomains, the proton transfer process of PIL was accelerated, resulting in the re-oxidation process being slower than the reduction reaction. To avoid the proton reduction being obscured by HER, approaches can be adopted to drive the reduction potential of the proton to a more negative one, such as increasing the pKa of PIL and using a working electrode with a higher overpotential of HER. In this context, the low concentration of PIL/RTIL improved various issues such as viscosity and the cost of ionic liquids. Such strategies can provide some useful suggestions for achieving significant NOxRR-to-NH3 in terms of yield, production rate, and faradaic efficiency.

The author paid sufficient attention to the performance of the catalyst and successfully enhanced the product selectivity. However, there is a lack of in-depth exploration in the field of the IL-ammonia system. Therefore, strategies should be considered, such as in situ analysis and theoretical study. A deeper understanding of the reaction mechanism on the double-layer interface in a small scale is significant, especially the following two points: the interaction function between the proton donors and IL as well as between the electrolyte bulk and electrode/catalyst, the reduction species that are generated in nonaqueous solution, and how they influence the selectivity of NH3 formation. Consequently, the revelation of the NOxRR-to-NH3 reduction mechanism in nonaqueous mechanism is imperative, and the exploration of ILammonia is expected to be more systematic and mature in the future.

4 Summary and outlook

Compared to the traditional NOx reduction technology, the electrochemical reduction of NOx to synthesize higher value ammonia with no CO2 emissions is a green and appealing chemical technology to replace the HaberBosch process, which uses N2 as a nitrogen source, there are still many challenges and problems in achieving industrial applications. On the one hand, the NOxRR-to-NH3 process is unfavorable kinetically, in which going through an 8-electron transfer process results in the generation of a serious unwanted by-products (e.g., NH2OH and N2H4), which could affect ammonia selectivity; on the other hand, the proton problem, i.e., hydrogen evolution reduction competes with the NOxRR-to-NH3 process, affecting the yield of ammonia. Along with that, strategies are proposed for achieving the widespread use of NOxRR-to-NH3 in the future.

(1) As the acidic environment is favourable for the NOxRR process, the competition of absorption between H+ and NO* requires serious consideration. Fortunately, a majority of metals or transition metals perform well in the adsorption of NO* rather than H+. The design of cost-efficient and performance-superior electrocatalysts is significant for the NOxRR-to-NH3 process, including interface modification, defect introduction, single-atom doping, and construction optimization. Inducing catalysts possess a stable structure and outstanding catalytic activity as well as excellent transfer capability in mass and electron. Furthermore, the IL-modified catalysts can effectively control the surface proton concentration and electron availability, thus inhibiting HER and achieving an efficient FEYNH3 equilibrium. Multidimensional and hierarchical catalysts with abundant active surface area that act as superior pH-universal catalysts are successfully applied for NRR-to-NH3 in alkaline conditions, which encourage catalyst engineering on NORR-to-NH3 in a wide range of pH.

(2) Operating conditions should be considered seriously, such as decreasing the NOx concentration to assure the proper active sites exposure, controlling the potentials to prevent NOxRR-to-NH3 from being affected by HER, and lowering the pH of the solvents to create an acidic environment that is inhospitable to some unwanted by-products such as hydroxylamine and hydrazine. The poor solubility of NOx in water and proton problems in acidic solvents should be valued carefully, in which electrolyte functionalization is a feasible strategy. Otherwise, introducing nonaqueous solvents as electrolytes is another accessible approach. Room temperature ionic liquids (RTILs) and protic ionic liquids (PIL) with excellent solubility and stability have proven the feasibility in nitrides and nitrogen oxide reduction. Imidazole ionic liquids or noncoordination solvents can be considered as the medium for NOx reduction. In view of the high viscosity of ionic liquid itself and competitive reactions caused by proton donors, the former can be enhanced through a gas diffusion cell or introducing a certain proportion of fluidity solvents such as molecular solvents (MS), and the latter can be restrained by changing the pKa of the proton donors or selecting an electrode with higher HER overpotential. Even in weak acid RTIL/PIL, the nanodomain formed between RTIL/PIL and MS is entropically beneficial for the reduction process, which is cost-efficient and process-beneficial, providing worthy strategies for NOxRR-to-NH3 in nonaqueous solvents.

(3) Systematic theory has been established in aqueous solutions. However, there are impediments of low current efficiency and low product yield in the field of IL-ammonia; efforts should be paid to the analysis of the electrode surface structure and the study of the reaction mechanism for IL-ammonia study. In situ position analysis or theory study is crucial to be extended for a deeper understanding of the reaction kinetics and mechanisms for the infancy research on NOxRR-to-NH3 in nonaqueous solvents, such as in situ infrared spectroscopy, in situ Raman spectrum, and operando X-ray absorption spectroscopy (operando XAS). In this context, there is extensive room for the exploration of catalysts with abundant active sites and superior properties such as electron transformation and gas diffusion are required.

(4) The development and utilization of renewable energy are prosperous in electrochemical reactions, especially solar energy. In the view of the NOxRR-to-NH3 process, photothermal coupling is conducive to promoting the reaction kinetic process, and the conversion energy required for the reaction could be provided by photoelectric coupling, while research was scarcely reported based on such comprehensive utilization. Our group focuses on the application of the STEP (Solar Thermal Electrochemical Process) theory, intending to improve the utilization of the full spectrum of solar energy. Solar thermal energy was creatively introduced into electrochemical reactions, thus achieving the efficient utilization of solar energy. Incorporating the STEP theory into the NOxRR-to-NH3 process is a promising avenue for ammonia production in an environment-friendly and cost-effective pathway.
