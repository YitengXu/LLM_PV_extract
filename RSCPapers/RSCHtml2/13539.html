Abstract

A flexible, lightweight and efficient nanohybrid of poly(vinylidene fluoride) and functionalized graphene oxide is prepared through an optimized electrospinning process for energy harvesting. Nanohybrids yield good quality fibers upon the incorporation of functionalized graphene oxide. The addition of nanofillers leads to a decrease in the fibre diameter arising from better interaction of the nanoparticle with the polymer matrix. An electroactive phase of ∼88% is achieved upon the addition of nanoparticles which leads to better piezoelectric materials for energy harvesting applications. The transformation of the electroactive phases from the non-piezoelectric part is confirmed through spectroscopic and thermal studies. The developed scaffold is fabricated into a nanogenerator device for electro-mechanical responses. The fabricated device from the nanohybrid of PVDF and functionalized graphene oxide produces a better electromechanical response of 62 V (peak-to-peak) and 48.3 μW cm−2 power generation using finger tapping as compared to 20 V and 23.4 μW cm−2 power generation from pristine PVDF fibres. These devices have the capability to generate a considerable amount of power from various human body movements like walking, foot tapping and bending. These devices have been used to power LEDs from various real life waste mechanical resources like opening and closing of drawers and walking over door mats.

1. Introduction

Graphene, a two-dimensional planar sheet with sp2-carbon atoms closely packed in the form of a honeycomb lattice, has high electrical conductivity, a high surface area, high aspect ratio and higher Young's modulus.1–3 It provides better electrical, thermal, mechanical and shielding effects4–7 which enables it to be used in various applications like solar cells, super capacitors, transistors and biomedical applications because of its diverse nature and versatile properties.8–11 Apart from its numerous advantages, it has certain limitations such as weak absorption of light and low capacitance values12 which restricts its use in many areas especially in the energy sector. To overcome these limitations, some functionalization is performed on graphite sheets like addition of functional groups, oxidation of the sheets or further reducing it to enhance its properties.13 Oxidation of graphite improves its dispersion in an aqueous medium, while surface functionalization e.g. sulphonation leads to better hydrophilic properties and improves adherence properties which facilitates the deposition of metal oxides on its surface.14 To further add to its merits, graphene oxide is reinforced with the polymer matrix to form nanohybrids which show enhanced mechanical and electrical properties suitable in the fields of energy harvesting, sensors, energy storage etc.15–17 Energy harvesting has attracted much attention due to its continuous progress and advancement as it provides a battery-less solution by scavenging energy from waste mechanical energy like vibrations, and human motion thereby producing useable electrical energy.18 Piezoelectric based energy harvesting has some profound importance because of its scalability, cost-effectiveness and time saving features.19 A piezoelectric material produces electrical polarization upon the application of stress. These materials have the potential to convert mechanical vibrations or body movements to useful electrical power. Hence, these materials are used to prepare piezoelectric nanogenerators.20,21 Polymer based nanohybrids have been the most efficient material for energy harvesting due to their flexibility, and improved mechanical and thermal properties.22,23 Poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF) is an important piezoelectric polymer which has efficiently been used for energy harvesting.24–26 It possesses three important crystalline phases e.g. α, β and γ of which the α-phase is a non-polar, and a non-piezo phase whereas β and γ are the prominent electroactive phases because of their polar nature.27,28 Certain stimuli like mechanical stretching or poling transform the α-phase into electroactive phases which are appropriate for piezoelectric energy harvesting.29 Electrospinning is a versatile technique where the non-polar phase gets transformed into polar, electroactive phases by the combined effect of stretching and poling under a high potential.30 To induce electroactive phases in the polymer matrix, certain fillers such as nanoclay,31,32 reduced graphene oxide,33,34 ZnO,35 KNN,36 doped PZT, and BaTiO3 (ref. 37 and 38) and bio-fillers like fish scales are also incorporated into the polymer matrix.39 Graphene oxide based polymer nanohybrids also show the transformation of the non-piezo phase into electroactive phases.40,41 Due to its versatile nature including high electrical conductivity and wide applications, graphene oxide is a much explored material in the field of energy but detailed quantification and the use of the functionalized part of graphene oxide and its application in energy harvesting is not explored, especially considering the alignment of two dimensional graphene under a high potential gradient. However, the quest for new materials for energy generation is at the forefront of research worldwide and graphene with its superior properties (both in terms of electrical and mechanical) is a natural choice of materials as fillers to make electroactive materials suitable for energy generation from waste mechanical resources.

In this work, we have performed the functionalization of graphene oxide with polar sulphonated groups and used it as a filler for the PVDF matrix to prepare highly aligned scaffolds through electrospinning. Functionalization of graphene oxide leads to better dispersion in solvents followed by superior electrical properties. The effects of functionalization and process technology have been used for energy harvesting applications from waste mechanical energy. A miniature device has been fabricated using hybrid materials and its electromechanical response has been measured utilizing different human motions like finger tapping, bending, walking, foot tapping etc. The device has been used to generate power and powering of LEDs from waste mechanical energy like opening/closing of drawers and walking over door mats. The mechanism of the greater electroactive phase and high energy conversion has also been revealed.

2. Experimental

Materials

Polymeric beads of commercial grade SOLEF 6008 poly(vinylidene fluoride) is used for the experiments and is kindly supplied by Ausimont, Italy. PVDF used has a molecular weight of 2.7 × 105, density ∼1.98 g cc−1 and a PDI of 2.1. Dimethyl formamide (DMF) and acetone of spectroscopic grade are used and purchased from Merck, India. Graphene oxide (GO) and functionalized graphene oxide (GS) were synthesized in the laboratory.

Graphene oxide synthesis

GO is synthesized as reported in previous studies.11 Graphite flakes (3.0 g, 1 wt. equivalent) are added to a mixture of concentrated H2SO4 and H3PO4 taken in a ratio of 9:1. KMnO4 (18 g, 6 wt. equivalent) is added slowly to the mixture. The reaction temperature is maintained at 50 °C with continuous stirring of the mixture overnight. 30% H2O2 is added to the mixture in the presence of ice water to cool the mixture followed by centrifugation at 4000 rpm. A mixture of ethanol and dilute HCl is used to wash off the product until the pH of the media becomes ∼7. The product is dried at 70 °C for 2 days under ambient pressure.

Functionalized graphene oxide synthesis

The functionalization of graphene oxide is carried out as stated in the previous work.14 The prepared graphene oxide is sulfonated in the presence of ammonium sulfate. Graphene oxide and ammonium sulfate are dispersed in deionized water in a mass ratio of 1:5. The mixture is then sonicated for 45 minutes followed by drying. The dried sample is heated at 245 °C for around 1 hour to allow ammonium sulphate to decompose and form a –SO3H moiety in the graphene sheet. Henceforth, it will be termed as GS.

Nanofiber preparation

Viscous solutions of PVDF and graphene oxide (GO or GS) are prepared from the solution route. The concentration of the filler used is 1 wt% with respect to the polymer weight. The nanohybrids of PVDF and graphene oxide are developed in a DMF and acetone mixture in a ratio of 2:8 (v/v). The polymer and the filler are taken separately and mixed or dispersed in DMF/acetone at 60 °C under constant stirring followed by ultrasonication. Then, both the sonicated portions are mixed together under the same conditions and stirred until a homogeneous solution is developed. This solution is used for electrospinning in fiber preparation. The solution is transferred into a syringe of 0.8 mm diameter and is placed into a chamber for electrospinning. The collector of the electrospinning set up is covered with aluminium foil acting as a substrate. Electrospinning is performed at a flow rate of 0.75 ml h−1, 20 kV potential and by maintaining a distance between the collector and spinneret of 15 cm after carrying out certain optimizations. After the electrospinning, the prepared scaffold is vacuum dried at 50 °C overnight to remove the residual traces of solvent. The prepared scaffold is abbreviated as P, P-GO and P-GS for pure PVDF, PVDF and graphene oxide and PVDF and functionalized graphene oxide fibers, respectively.

Device fabrication

The prepared scaffold is placed between two pieces of aluminium foil and then electroding is performed using copper wires. Then, the prepared device pre-form is encapsulated using PDMS in a mould after degassing and keeping at 50 °C overnight to obtain the final device which is used for electromechanical measurements.

3. Characterization

Spectroscopic studies

UV-visible analysis is performed in the range 200–800 nm in the reflectance mode in a Jasco V-650 spectrophotometer. FTIR studies are performed using a Nicolet iS5 in the range 400–4000 cm−1 with a resolution of 4 cm−1 at room temperature.

Morphological investigation

The obtained scaffold is analysed using polarized optical microscopy (POM) Leitz Biomed to observe the fibrous nature. This characterization is performed in reflectance mode. To observe the morphology of the scaffold more precisely, a scanning electron microscope (Supra 40, Zeiss) is used. The samples are gold coated through sputtering to impart conductivity to the material.

Structural analysis

The structural changes in the nanohybrids are analyzed using X-ray diffraction (Rigaku Miniflex 600 X-ray). Characterization is performed using a Cu-Kα source with a wavelength of 0.154 nm and 40 kV and 20 mA voltage and current are applied to the generator. Scaffolds are placed on a quartz holder and are subjected to a scanning rate of 3° min−1. XPS was performed using a Thermo Fisher Scientific (K-alpha) system at room temperature to understand the functionalization. A Renishaw InVia Raman Microscope was used to perform Raman spectroscopy at a laser power 5% of 100 mW using a solid state diode laser of wavelength 532 nm.

Thermal and mechanical studies

Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC), Mettler 832, is used to examine the melting temperature (Tm) and heat of fusion (ΔH). The system is calibrated using indium prior to experiment. The samples are analyzed between 25 and 250 °C at a rate of 10° min−1. The thermal degradation of the pristine polymer and its nanohybrid is analyzed using a thermogravimetric analyser (TGA) (Mettler Toledo) in the range of 30–600 °C at a heating rate of 20° per minute. Tensile testing was performed using an Instron universal testing machine at a strain rate of 5 mm min−1.

Electromechanical response

The prepared device is subjected to external pressure to measure the voltage obtained using a Tektronix TBS 1072B digital storage oscilloscope. Power was also calculated using a variable resistance box. Capacitance storage was measured in terms of voltage using a Tektronix TBS 1072B digital storage oscilloscope. Initially stress was applied to the sample (charging) and after attaining the maximum voltage stress was removed (discharging) from the sample. The piezoelectric coefficients of the samples are measured using a Piezotest PM200. Dielectric measurements were performed using a Keysight E4990A from 20 Hz to 10 MHz at room temperature.

4. Results and discussion

Evidence of functionalization and its effect on structure and morphology

The schematic representation of the preparation method of graphene oxide (GO) and sulphonated graphene oxides (GS) is shown in Fig. S1 (ESI†). FTIR peaks at 1719 and 1580 cm−1 are observed for GO which are the characteristic peaks of the carbonyl group (>CO) and CC group, respectively, and a broad peak at 3426 cm−1 is seen for the hydroxyl group (ESI Fig. S2†). The peaks of GO become attenuated for the functionalized graphene oxide and new peaks at 1162, 1096 and 600 cm−1 are observed. The peaks at 1162 and 1096 cm−1 are attributed to the stretching vibration for SO while the 600 cm−1 peak signifies the presence of the C–S bond. An UV-vis peak at 271 nm is observed for GO due to n → π* transition while the peak shifts to 267 nm for GS and supports the functionalization of graphene oxide (ESI Fig. S3†). However, the functionalization is evident from the spectroscopic studies.13,14,42,43 The evidence of functionalization is also supported by the XPS spectra (ESI Fig. S4†). In the case of GS, a new peak at 167.38 eV corresponds to the binding energy of the S 2p region of the sulphate group attached with a GO sheet which is categorically absent in the case of GO. However, FTIR, UV-vis and XPS studies confirm the functionalization of GO to GS.44

The quality of nanofibers was optimized after embedding the functionalized GO in the PVDF matrix by controlling electrospinning parameters like flow rate, voltage, concentration of the solution, solvent ratio and distance between the collector and spinneret. 20 kV potential, 0.75 ml h−1 flow rate, 10 wt% solution, 15 cm drum to spinneret distance and the solvent ratio of 2:8 of DMF and acetone are found to be the optimized parameters to obtain good quality fibres (ESI Fig. S5†). The concentration of the fillers (GO and GS) incorporated with respect to the polymer was 1% which was chosen after optimization at different concentrations as shown in the ESI (Fig. S6†). Fig. 1a shows the optical image of pure PVDF and its hybrid nanofibers which exhibit better quality (no bead formation) in the presence of GO and GS. Well dispersed two dimensional GO/GS layers easily get charged during electrospinning (under high potential) which results in finer nanofibers in the hybrid system vis-à-vis in pure PVDF. Higher magnification images through SEM clearly indicate thinner nanofibers in the presence of GO/GS (Fig. 1b) while the thinnest and very ordered nanofibers are generated using functionalized GO as it is evident from the comparative histogram of the fibres (Fig. 1c). The EDS mapping and spectrum also confirm that the prepared scaffold had GO and GS incorporated into it which led to better fibre quality, with the detection of various elements that appeared after functionalization e.g. sulphur in P-GS (ESI Fig. S7†). The fibre diameters of 260 ± 5, 190 ± 4 and 150 ± 4 nm for pure PVDF, P-GO and P-GS, respectively, are generated through electrospinning. Addition of GO leads to an increase in the charge density in solutions which thereby produces higher elongation force in jets and a better quality fibre is obtained.45 Upon addition of functionalized graphene (GS) as a filler to the polymer matrix, even better fibres are obtained presumably due to the polar –SO3H moiety in the graphene sheets which leads to better dispersion and greater charge values, eventually producing finer quality fibres. This is to mention that the polar sulphonate group enhances the interaction between GS and PVDF through a dipolar interaction which causes better dispersion.14 However, the incorporation of a filler into the PVDF matrix leads to better interaction with the polymer and also increases electrostatic repulsion and coulombic force on the spinneret Taylor cone which leads to a decrease in fibre dimensions.15


Fig. 1 (a) Optical images of the electrospun nanofibers indicated (P, P-GO and P-GS); (b) SEM micrographs of the prepared fibers; (c) fibre diameter distributions of P, P-GO and P-GS, obtained from SEM micrographs; and (d) UV-vis spectra of pristine PVDF and its nanohybrid fibres showing the absorption peak positions.

The interaction between the filler and the polymer is also studied through UV-vis spectroscopy (Fig. 1d). The pristine PVDF does not show any characteristic peak in the spectra46 but on addition of the filler (GO) to the PVDF matrix, there appears a new peak at 275 nm for P-GO while the peak has been shifted to 270 nm for P-GS using functionalized GO as the filler. UV-vis spectra of pure graphene oxide (GO) and sulphonated graphene oxides (GS) are shown in ESI Fig. S2† and the red shifts of both the hybrids, as compared to that of the pure fillers, indicate a strong interaction between PVDF and GO/GS. This is to mention that a stronger interaction in hybrid systems leads to finer fibre dimensions through electrospinning as observed in morphological studies stated above.39

Better quality fibers are developed using two-dimensional fillers after certain optimization of the process parameters. Now, it is important to understand the structural change and to visualize the effect of GO/GS in the hybrids. The XRD pattern of PVDF fibres shows the β-phase along with a certain α-phase while significant conversion to the electroactive β-phase has clearly been observed in P-GO and P-GS (Fig. 2a). It is worth mentioning that the pure PVDF film shows only the α-phase which possess characteristic peaks at 17.6 (100), 18.5 (020), and 19.9 (110).29 PVDF nanofibers, prepared through electrospinning, show two peaks at 2θ values of 18.6° (020) and 20.5° (200/110), where the first peak is for the α-phase and the major peak occurs at 20.5° indicative of the β-phase. On contrary, the peak at 18.6° almost disappeared for P-GO and P-GS fibres suggesting a greater β-phase in the presence of the filler. XRD peaks are deconvoluted and the estimation of various phase fractions clearly indicate the greater electroactive phase in the hybrid fibres (Fig. 2b). Moreover, the content of the electroactive phase is maximized in P-GS, around 88% in comparison to 59 and 76% for pure PVDF and P-GO, respectively. The α-phase peak of PVDF gets transformed into the piezoelectric active phase while the β-phase peak gets shifted to 20.54° in the case of P-GO and 20.6° (200/110) for P-GS. The shifting and transformation of the peaks and phases as observed here are also reported in some other studies.47,48

The appearance of 839, 1070, 1172 and 1274 cm−1 peaks in the FTIR spectrum clearly indicates the presence of the electroactive β-phase and the 1234 cm−1 peak corresponds to the γ-phase (Fig. 2c).49 Interestingly, the intensity of both the electroactive phases (β + γ) is higher in hybrid fibres and even greater in P-GS vis-à-vis that in P-GO, which clearly supports greater electroactive phases in the functionalized graphene hybrid. The peaks at 615, 762, 794 and 974 cm−1 represent the α-phase whose intensity is quite prominent in ‘P’ (pure PVDF fibre) while the said peaks almost disappear for hybrid fibres especially for P-GS, conversely indicating the conversion of α-peaks into electroactive phases in the presence of GO/GS. Further, a new FTIR peak appears at 601 cm−1 only for P-GS, suggesting the presence of the C–S bond due to sulphonation group attachment in GS and subsequent P-GS. Other characteristic peaks for sulphonation presented in ESI Fig. S2† are due to O–H stretching vibration from the sulphonate group.48,49 Raman spectral characterization is performed (Fig. S8 ESI†) and suggests the incorporation of GO and GS due to the presence of two peaks at 1320 and 1535 cm−1 which are the D- and G-band of graphene oxide, respectively, while no peak is observed for the pure PVDF fibre. The Raman shift in nanohybrids as compared to that in graphene oxide indicates the strong interaction between PVDF and GO/GS. This result is in accordance with the literature report.39 Differential scanning calorimetry is another probe to distinguish various phases of crystalline forms arising from their distinct melting temperatures. From the DSC thermograms (Fig. 2d), it is observed that the pristine PVDF fibres show a broad melting peak at 178 °C which is a combined melting behavior of the α- and β-phase. On the other hand, P-GO shows two melting peaks at 168 and 173 °C for the β- and α-phase, respectively, whereas P-GS exhibits melting temperatures of 170 and 181 °C predominantly due to the melting behavior of β and γ- phases, respectively. The appearance of the γ-phase in P-GS, as evident from XRD studies, is also confirmed from thermal measurements. This is to mention that the melting temperatures follow the order of β < α < γ.31,45 However, sulphonated graphene oxide exclusively nucleates the electroactive phases in the PVDF matrix through electrospinning as opposed to pure PVDF and P-GO nanofibers and this phenomenon is supported through XRD, FTIR and DSC measurements. The greater content of the electroactive phase in P-GS is due to better interaction between the filler and the polymer matrix arising from the higher dipole in sulphonated graphene oxide in the presence of the polar –SO3H moiety in graphene layers. The piezoelectric coefficient (d33) values for P, P-GO and P-GS are −2 pC N−1, −5 pC N−1 and −9 pC N−1, respectively, measured at room temperature and the higher value of nanohybrids indicate their greater piezo responses. Thermal stability of materials is an important factor to be considered for the use of any device. Fig. 2e shows the thermal analysis of electrospun PVDF and its hybrid nanofibers. The thermal stability is measured under heat treatment and the temperature corresponding to 5% weight loss is considered as the degradation temperature. The degradation temperature for P is nearly 490 °C, whereas degradation temperatures of 430 and 410 °C are observed for P-GO and P-GS, respectively.50 The stress–strain curves support the enhanced mechanical properties (Young's modulus of 3.1, 30.2 and 86.1 MPa for P, P-GO and P-GS, respectively) with the addition of sulphonated graphene oxide leading to better fibre quality (ESI Fig. S9†). The comparative mechanical properties are presented in ESI Table S1† which corroborates the above observation as well. From the above discussion it is evident that the addition of a filler to the polymer matrix leads to better fiber quality, having reduced fibre dimensions. Based on the greater interaction between functionalized graphene and PVDF, a schematic has been presented in Fig. 2f showing thinner and strong fibres of P-GS as compared to pure PVDF and P-GO fibres. Much better alignment of fibres with a decrease in fibre dimensions occurs upon the embedment of functionalized graphene oxide in PVDF, which in turn, is suppose to enhance the piezoelectricity in nanohybrids.

Nanofiller induced piezoelectricity and its applications: the effect of functionalization

From the morphological, structural and thermal analysis, it is evident that the hybrid material prepared from PVDF and functionalized graphene oxide is suitable for piezoelectric energy harvesting. The incorporation of a filler, especially the sulphonated graphene oxide into the PVDF matrix enhances the electroactive phases while non-polar phases (α- and amorphous) get diminished to a higher level.

Now, the rise in electroactive phases provides a way for its application in the energy harvesting arena. A schematic representation of the fabricated device utilizing pure PVDF and hybrid scaffolds is shown in Fig. 3a. The devices consist of four parts; a polymer scaffold, aluminum foil as the substrate, copper wire as the electroding material and poly(dimethyl siloxane) (PDMS) as the encapsulating material. PDMS encapsulation was performed as it provides better stability and uniformity to the device. The device is subjected to external pressure using different modes to measure its electromechanical responses. Fig. 3b shows the electromechanical response of the devices when subjected to external pressure via finger tapping. The amount of pressure applied on the device through finger tapping is ∼40 kPa which is calculated as in previous articles51 and is presented in the ESI† of this article. The output voltages (peak-to-peak) are 20.5, 45 and 62 V for the devices made of the P, P-GO and P-GS scaffold, respectively, as obtained from the finger tapping method. The nanohybrid of PVDF and sulphonated graphene oxide provides the highest output voltage as compared to P and P-GO. The output current is measured using a digital multimeter and the maximum current values obtained are 0.055, 0.86 and 2.1 μA for P, P-GO and P-GS, respectively. However, P-GO also produces a considerable amount of higher voltage as compared to the pristine PVDF scaffold while the better fibre formation is in P-GS, arising from the presence of the –SO3H moiety on graphene oxide which leads to better dispersion and greater interaction with the polymer matrix, resulting in the enhancement of the electromechanical response in terms of voltage. A piezoelectric material has the potential to convert mechanical energy to electrical response. The device undergoes deformation under the application of pressure which leads to the flow of charges back and forth between the electrodes at the top and bottom and, thus, electrical signal is produced in the external circuit. As a control, devices are fabricated out of electrospun poly(lactic acid) (PLA; non-piezoelectric) and pristine PDMS which do not exhibit any voltage generation under similar mechanical stress and thereby, eliminate the possibility of the triboelectric effect (ESI Fig. S10†). This in turn suggests the efficacy of hybrid PVDF scaffolds as a superior energy harvester. Electric polarization must be induced in the material under pressure which creates a potential difference between the electrodes. This leads to the generation of a positive voltage signal in the circuit. Further, a negative voltage signal is obtained when the stress is released and the charges move back to the electrodes. This is to mention that the in-built potential and the net charges generated diminish to zero under constant stress and the free charges are being balanced by the piezoelectric bound charges.18,52–54 A switching polarity test is carried out to confirm the output signal due to the piezoelectric properties of the fabricated device using reverse connection. Fig. S11 (ESI†) presents the polarity test being carried out at room temperature under similar conditions. It is obvious that the magnitude of the output voltage is similar with a change in polarity which substantiates that the output voltage generated is from the fabricated devices.55


Fig. 3 (a) Schematic view of the fabricated device using electrospun nanofibers; (b) output voltage response from the fabricated device using scaffolds and finger tapping mode; (c) power densities of the P, P-GO and P-GS measured as a function of applied load (resistance); (d) comparison of response obtained from instantaneous load on the prepared unimorph through a single hit, showing much better performance in the device made of P-GS; and (e) charging and discharging phenomenon of the device made of the P-GS scaffold.

The power densities (Pd) for the devices are measured using the formula:

Pd = V2/(RL × A)
where, V is the output voltage generated on repeated hitting, RL is the load resistance across the device and A is the area of the device over which periodic stress is applied. The values for the power densities correspond to the data obtained for the output voltage across a variable load resistance (Fig. 3c). The maximum power density is found to be 48.3 μW cm−2 for the device made of the P-GS scaffold as compared to 33.7 and 23.4 μW cm−2 for P-GO and P, respectively. The power density and output voltage are higher than literature reported values. Alluri et al.19 reported a maximum output voltage and power density of 37.8 V and 6.3 μW cm−2 using a PVDF-activated carbon composite film. 6.8 μW cm−2 (11 V) and 4.41 μW cm−2 (4 V) were the output from the PVDF composite with a graphene + Ce3+ system and a composite bilayer film of PVDF-TrFE–GO through electrospinning, respectively.15,56 Much higher output power from the device using the functionalized graphene–PVDF hybrid is understood from the greater electroactive phase during electrospinning presumably due to better fibres arising from intense dipolar interactions. To get a clear view of the stability of the device, a unimorph has been prepared to compare the output voltage generated on application of instantaneous load through a single hit (Fig. 3d). From the response obtained from the unimorph, it can be observed that P-GS fibres produce a better output voltage (∼5 V peak-to-peak) on application of impulsive load as compared to pristine polymer fibres (∼1.8 V peak-to-peak). Importantly, the response time measured through the time after which the signal decays out is of major concern. For P-GS, the signal lasted around 75 ms as compared to pure PVDF, which is around 35 ms. This suggests that the prepared hybrid device is suitable for energy harvesting applications because of its better piezoelectric responses. Variation of the dielectric constant with applied frequency is measured at room temperature (Fig. S12†). The obtained dielectric constants are observed at 7.3, 16.2 and 21.3 for P, P-GO and P-GS, respectively. There is a substantial increase in dielectric values on incorporation of GO and GS due to the better electronic properties of GO and GS than pure PVDF.57 After obtaining a high output voltage and power density, the fabricated device is used for charging–discharging of a capacitor for storage purpose (Fig. 3e).There is substantial storage of charge under repeated application of pressure on the device which is followed by it reaching a saturation limit. The device was connected to a capacitor of capacitance of 1 μF attached to a rectifier circuit on a bread board. The maximum charge achieved in a 1 μF capacitor is ∼1.05 μC with repeated hitting using finger tapping and the time required to gain the maximum storage was around 30 seconds while the discharging phenomena occur in 35 seconds showing moderately high restoration of charge generated by the device. To confirm the storage capacity of the device it is subjected to higher capacitance as shown in the ESI (Fig. S13†). The output charges are found to be 2.09, 4.23 and 8.6 μC when the devices are associated with higher capacitances of 2.2, 4.7 and 10 μF, respectively, which in turn supports that the device is able to store charge substantially with variable capacitors. The device is quite stable mechanically under different force applications as seen from the cross sectional view of the device analyzed closely before and after force application without any rupture (Fig. S14†). The durability test of the device is also carried for 1000 seconds which suggests that the sample is stable in spite of repeated hitting for a longer period of time (ESI Fig. S15†).58

From these observations, it is evident that the hybrid scaffold especially the one using functionalized GO is appropriate for charge storage and can be a functional tool as an energy storage device. Apart from the finger tapping method, some other human movements are utilized to measure the electromechanical response of the device like foot tapping, and walking. Fig. 4a–c present the images and the corresponding output voltages and power densities from foot tapping, walking and bending phenomena, respectively. The output voltages (peak-to-peak) for foot tapping, walking and bending are ∼12, 8.2 and 6.5 V, respectively. The power densities are also calculated from these different modes across the load and are found to be 6.9, 5.2 and 3.6 μW cm−2 for foot tapping, walking and bending, respectively. Under the bending process, the amount of charge generated in the material is less as compared to that in foot tapping and walking methods because of the load applied onto the material which varies with the nature of human movements. A video has been provided in support of the observations (ESI Video SV1).†

At this juncture, it is confirmed that the addition of the functionalized nanoparticle to the PVDF leads to an increase in the amount of the electroactive phases which in turn enhances the electromechanical responses from the device. Some applications of the developed device have been examined using day to day activities, where there is waste mechanical energy. Fig. 5a–c show the application of the fabricated device in finger tapping, drawer pushing and walking over the door mat, respectively. Images of the different techniques used to power LEDs are provided for better understanding of various application processes to generate energy from waste mechanical energy. Finger tapping leads to glowing of 5 LEDs with bright illumination whereas drawer pushing and walking over door mats lead to glowing of a single LED with sufficient intensity (ESI Video SV2).† These observations open up a path to use these materials in real life applications where these devices are very useful in conserving the conventional source of energy and help, even in a minute quantity, to meet the severe energy crisis of modern days along with powering the biological implants.

5. Conclusion

Electrospun nanohybrids of PVDF, graphene oxide and sulphonated graphene oxide have been processed through electrospinning after optimizing process parameters to obtain the best quality nanofibers. The consolidated nanofibers are achieved using functionalized graphene oxide as a two-dimensional filler. The electroactive phases have been found to be very high (88%) in functionalized nanofibers against meager values obtained for pure PVDF/P-GO scaffolds (59%). The appearance of electroactive phases is confirmed through a spectroscopic technique (FTIR) and two distinct melting temperatures of the β- and γ-phases in DSC thermograms. The thinner fibers for functionalized hybrids are revealed through SEM images and a model of the consolidated fibers is shown through a schematic diagram arising from greater interaction between sulphonated GO and polar PVDF chains through dipolar interactions. High piezoelectric phases of the scaffold from functionalized nanohybrids are utilized to fabricate energy harvesting devices with appropriate design to measure the electromechanical response from different human motions like finger tapping, foot tapping, walking and bending. The results show that the P-GS produced a significantly higher response (62 V and 48.3 μW cm−2 power density) in comparison with the pure PVDF scaffolds (20 V and 23.4 μW cm−2) and P-GO (45 V and 33.7 μW cm−2) arising from its better interaction with the polymer matrix. The generated power output is demonstrated to power 5 LEDs from finger tapping with high illumination intensity. Some of the common daily life applications, e.g. pulling drawers or walking over door mats, are also demonstrated to power LEDs utilizing waste mechanical energy and thereby exhibit tremendous potential for energy harvesting devices based on hybrid scaffolds from waste mechanical sources which become an effective tool for generating energy especially powering biological implants. The developed nanohybrid device with its high efficiency demonstrates its superior energy harvesting capability by utilizing waste mechanical resources.
