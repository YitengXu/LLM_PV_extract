Abstract

MnO2 nanoparticles were uniformly loaded on the surface of zeolitic imidazolate frameworks (ZIF-67 and ZIF-8) with ligand modification via a facile method with no other impurities introduced, with an average diameter of less than 10 nm. The MnO2/ZIF-COOH composites with stable structures can effectively alleviate expansion tension and provide a fast ion transport channel during electrochemical performance tests. The introduction of carboxyl (â€“COOH) increases the specific capacity due to Li insertion, enhances the conductivity with ionization, and improves stability through the formation of H-bonds. When used as an anode for lithium ion batteries, the MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH composite exhibits an excellent rate performance (1208 mA h gâˆ’1 at 50 mA gâˆ’1, 931 mA h gâˆ’1 at 100 mA gâˆ’1, 828 mA h gâˆ’1 at 200 mA gâˆ’1, 684 mA h gâˆ’1 at 500 mA gâˆ’1, 566 mA h gâˆ’1 at 1000 mA gâˆ’1, 431 mA h gâˆ’1 at 2000 mA gâˆ’1, and 242 mA h gâˆ’1 at 5000 mA gâˆ’1) and high cycle stability even at high rates (a high capacity of 664 mA h gâˆ’1 is achieved at 1000 mA gâˆ’1 and is maintained at approximately 100% after 100 cycles).

1. Introduction

With the growing consumption of energy, energy storage is a challenge faced by the general public. Moreover, sustainable and renewable energy resources such as wind power and solar power are unreliable.1 Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs), with effective energy storage, have been applied in various fields, such as electric vehicles (EVs), mobile phones, aerospace, and so on.

In order to improve the energy density of LIBs, great efforts have been made to explore various novel materials, and transition-metal oxides (MOx) are among them.2 Manganese dioxide (MnO2) is a promising material with high theoretical specific capacity (âˆ¼1230 mA h gâˆ’1) and low cost. However, pure MnO2 is not an ideal anode material due to its low electrical conductivity and rapid capacity drop. This can be ascribed to the expansion and aggregation of MnO2 during the discharge/charge process.3 Therefore, there have been numerous studies concentrating on MnO2 composites in LIBs to address this issue. Carbon nanohorn-supported MnO2 anodes were developed by Huang et al., and a specific capacity of 565 mA h gâˆ’1 at 450 mA gâˆ’1 after 60 cycles (42 wt% MnO2) was obtained.4 Xia et al. used nanoflakey MnO2/carbon nanotube nanocomposites as anode materials, which obtained a specific capacity of 620 mA h gâˆ’1 at 200 mA gâˆ’1 after 50 cycles.5 Recently, MnO2 nanoscrolls/reduced graphene oxide (RGO) nanocomposites were reported by Liu et al.,2 with a specific capacity of 528 mA h gâˆ’1 at a current density of 100 mA h gâˆ’1. Based on the previous literature, it seems that MnO2 composite materials as anode materials are superior to pure MnO2, however the energy and power density of these composite materials are too low for any practical applications.

MOFs are porous materials with a high pore volume, high specific area and inorganicâ€“organic hybrid properties with an ordered microporous structure, and are favorably superior as an ideal matrix for metals,10,11 oxides12,13 and hydrides.1 Zeolitic imidazolate frameworks (ZIFs) are one subclass of metalâ€“organic framework (MOF) made by the linking of metal ions and organic ligands.6â€“8 ZIF-67 and ZIF-8 have been extensively studied due to their great chemical and thermal stability as well as their adjustable pore sizes, unique adsorption and catalytic properties.1,9 Based on the fact that the metal ions in MOFs can serve as redox active sites in electrochemical processes, MOFs can be used as electrode materials in LIBs. However, MOF materials usually have low electrical conductivity which greatly affects their electrochemical performance in LIBs. Therefore, we focused on MnO2/MOF composites, which may have excellent electrochemical properties due to the splendid MOF structure.

In this paper, a novel method was applied to prepare MnO2/ZIF-COOH composites. This method can not only uniformly load nanoparticles of MnO2 on ZIF-67 and ZIF-8, but can also increase the length of the ligand, transforming methyl (â€“CH3) to carboxyl (â€“COOH), which can also change the characteristics of a material. Under the conditions of an ice bath, smaller particles were produced with a low speed. The nanoparticles can improve the electrochemical performance of MnO2. It is worth mentioning that no impurity was observed. The MOFs (ZIF-67 and ZIF-8) not only alleviate the expansion tension of the material, but also offer solid frameworks for fast electron transport.16 Furthermore, the stability of the MOFs can be promoted due to the formation of carboxyl groups (â€“COOH), which can build H-bonds through interaction. 2. Experimental section

2.1 Chemicals

All chemicals were of analytical grade and were used as purchased without further purification.
Synthesis of ZIF-67 and ZIF-8. ZIF-67 and ZIF-8 were prepared through a solvothermal method developed by the Yaghi group.17 10 mmol nitrites (cobalt nitrate or zinc nitrate) and 100 mmol 2-methylimidazole were dissolved in 20 ml N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) in a Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave (50 ml). Solvothermal synthesis was carried out at 90 Â°C for 72 h. The powder product was collected by centrifugation (6 min at 5000 rpm) and washed thoroughly using DMF and methanol. The obtained ZIF-67 (purple) and ZIF-8 (white) powders were dried at 100 Â°C for 12 h before further experiment.
Synthesis of MnO2 nanoparticles. 2-Methylimidazole (0.03 g) and KMnO4 (0.02 g) were dissolved in 30 ml deionized water in a flask (100 ml). With continuous magnetic stirring in an ice bath, the mixture was allowed to react for 12 h. The black product was collected by centrifugation (6 min at 5000 rpm) and washed thoroughly using deionized water. The obtained MnO2 powders were dried at 100 Â°C for 12 h before further experiment.
Preparation of the MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH composite. ZIF-67 (0.05 g) was dispersed in 10 ml deionized water with stirring in an ice bath (designated as solution A). KMnO4 (0.01 g) was dissolved in 10 ml deionized water and was introduced dropwise into solution A with vigorous stirring. After 30 min, brown precipitate was collected by centrifugation (12000 rpm, 10 min) and cleaned thoroughly by DI water washing. Then, the obtained powder was dried (under vacuum at 100 Â°C for 12 h).
Preparation of the MnO2/ZIF-8-COOH composite. The method was almost the same as the method for the MnO2/ZIF67-COOH composite, except ZIF-8 was used rather than ZIF-67.

2.3 Electrochemical measurements

CR 2032 coin cells were used to study the electrochemical properties of the as-prepared materials in lithium-ion cells. The active materials were mixed with acetylene black (AB) and poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF), with a weight ratio of 70:20:10. The mixtures were ground for 3 h after adding a moderate solvent, N-methyl-pyrrolidone (NMP), dropwise. The resultant slurries were uniformly cast onto a copper foil substrate. After drying under vacuum at 90 Â°C for 12 h, the electrodes were assembled into cells in a glove box filled with argon. The mass loadings of the MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH composite and the MnO2/ZIF-8-COOH composite were 1.19 and 1.12 mg, respectively. The anode was Li, and the electrolyte was 1 M LiPF6 in 1:1 v/v ethylene carbonate (EC)/dimethyl carbonate (DMC). The electrochemical measurements of cyclic voltammetry (CV) and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) were carried out with a CHI660D electrochemical workstation, both of which were measured before the tests. Charging/discharging curves were recorded on a NEARE CT-3008 with the voltage range of 0.01â€“3.0 V (vs. Li/Li+) at the rate range from 0.05 C to 5 C (C = 1000 mA gâˆ’1) at room temperature. Furthermore, the cycle performance of the materials was recorded after the rate tests.

3. Results and discussion

The synthesis route for the MnO2/ZIF-COOH composites is presented in Scheme 1. An ice bath was chosen to maintain the structures of the MOFs with tiny MnO2 particles formed at a relatively low speed. The content of MnO2 can be regulated by the introduction of KMnO4, and a series of tests were conducted to explore the optimum recipe of KMnO4 and the MOFs. However, it was found from the XRD patterns that the MOFs are prone to collapse with an increase in KMnO4, so a proportion was chosen in the synthetic method. The reaction time was also explored, and 30 min was a period of time that ensured experimental repeatability. Moreover, the chemical compositions of MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH and MnO2/ZIF-8-COOH were measured by ICP, and the mass ratios of MnO2 were 0.19 and 0.18, respectively. To verify the purity of the product, the XRD patterns of ZIF-67 and ZIF-8 were collected and compared in Fig. 1(a) and 2(a), respectively. It was observed that the XRD pattern of ZIF-67 matches well with that of the simulated ZIF-67. The difference in peak intensities can be attributed to the crystal morphology.18 Several characteristic peaks of ZIF-67 can be observed in the XRD pattern of the MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH composite which indicated the presence of ZIF-67 after the redox reaction. It should be noted that the peak intensities of ZIF-67 in the MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH composite were much lower than those of pure ZIF-67, which can be attributed to the partial amorphization of ZIF-67 during the redox reaction and the formation of MnO2. The two broad humps marked by black hearts in the XRD pattern of the MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH composite match well with those of MnO2 (JCPDS no. 72-1982). Moreover, some peaks of MnO2 overlapped with the peaks of ZIF-67. From Fig. 2(a), it can be seen that there is no obvious difference before and after loading MnO2 on ZIF-8, other than that the intensity of the MnO2/ZIF-8-COOH composite at around 35Â° is higher than that of pure ZIF-8. This is mainly attributed to the introduction of amorphous MnO2, which is demonstrated by the TEM and mapping images of the MnO2/ZIF-8-COOH composite. The conversion of methyl groups in the MOFs (ZIF-67 and ZIF-8) to â€“COOH groups can be observed in the IR spectra (Fig. 1(b) and 2(b)). The most obvious distinction between the original ZIF-67 and MnO2 supported on ZIF-67-COOH is an extra peak at 1634 cmâˆ’1 in the MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH composites (1621 cmâˆ’1 in the MnO2/ZIF-8-COOH composites), which is characteristic of carboxylate groups (â€“COOâˆ’).19 Specifically, the broad band at 3451 cmâˆ’1 can be attributed to the stretching vibration of Oâ€“H. The distinct peak at 3132 cmâˆ’1 corresponds to the Nâ€“H stretching vibration of imidazole. The stretching vibration of saturated Câ€“H is exhibited at 2932 cmâˆ’1. The peak at 1577 cmâˆ’1 is ascribed to the CC bond of imidazole. The existence of Câ€“H testifies that KMnO4 can oxidize the methyl groups on the external surface of the ZIF crystals (ZIF-67 and ZIF-8).

Since the ligands transformed from â€“CH3 to â€“COOH in this synthesis method, the reaction mechanism of MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH shifted, intuitively seen from the CV curves of ZIF-67 and MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH. Most notably, although ZIF-67 and ZIF-8 are isostructural, there are significant differences with regards to their electrochemical performance. This result can be traced back to the property differences of ZIF-67 and ZIF-8. The metal cation of ZIF-67 is Co, different from the Zn of ZIF-8. From the original report of ZIF-67 and ZIF-8,8 it is known that their density of metal atoms per unit volume (T/V(T/nm3)) is different, as the T/V of ZIF-67 is larger than that of ZIF-8. Moreover, from the EIS patterns shown in Fig. 6(e), the charge transfer resistance of the MnO2/ZIF-8-COOH composite (1016 Î©) is significantly larger than that of the MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH composite (44 Î©). The charge transfer resistances were obtained from the equivalent circuit using the ZSimWin software. Since the charge transfer between the Li ions and these samples might be the major factor in the electrochemical tests (shown by the absence of the semicircle in the high frequency region), it is not a surprise that a poor electrochemical performance was obtained for the MnO2/ZIF-8-COOH composite, compared with the MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH composite.

Since the electrochemical performance of the MnO2/ZIF-COOH composites is superior to that of the pure MOFs, it can be speculated that the electrochemical performance of MOFs can be improved by loading MnO2 on the surface of MOF crystals. The electrochemical performance of the MnO2/ZIF-COOH composites is superior to that of pure MnO2, demonstrating that MOFs, to a certain extent, can prevent the aggregation of MnO2 particles and improve electrical conductivity. 4. Conclusions

In summary, the simultaneous conversion of the surface methyl groups of ZIF-67 and ZIF-8 crystals to carboxylate groups and the uniform deposition of MnO2 nanoparticles on the crystal surface were realized by a simple facile post-modification method. The obtained MnO2/ZIF-COOH composites, as anode materials in lithium ion batteries, exhibited a superior electrochemical performance over pure MnO2 and ZIFs. This improvement can be attributed to the synergy of the following factors. ZIF crystals offered solid frameworks which alleviated the volume expansion of the anode material. The uniform deposition of MnO2 particles provided a high specific capacity. The presence of â€“COOH groups enhanced the interaction with Li ions, which significantly improved conductivity compared with pure ZIFs. The outstanding stability of ZIF materials also contributed to the long term stable performance.

Compared with the MnO2-ZIF-67-COOH composite, the MnO2-ZIF-8-COOH composite exhibited a substantially lower performance due to its low charge transfer resistance, which came from the low density of metal atoms per unit volume of ZIF-8. The best result was obtained with the MnO2/ZIF-67-COOH composite as an anode material for LIBs, which had a high specific capacity of 664 mA h gâˆ’1 after 100 cycles at 1000 mA gâˆ’1. The post-treatment method can be extended to the synthesis of other metal oxide/ZIF-COOH materials, which should have great potential as anode materials for LIBs.
