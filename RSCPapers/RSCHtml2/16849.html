Abstract

Phenazine derivatives have made significant progress owing to their favorable photophysical and chemical properties in recent years. Nevertheless, only a few overviews on phenazine derivatives are reported in the optoelectronic area. Herein, we comprehensively summarize and discuss the tremendous progress on the innovative design strategies, excellent properties and promising applications of novel functionalized phenazine derivatives in optical sensing and electrochemical fields. We anticipate that this review will facilitate new researchers to expand their horizons on the molecular design and development of advanced materials in the future.

1. Introduction

Phenazine is a significant class of aza-polycyclic compound. Compared with other nitrogen heterocyclic compounds, phenazine systems possess irreplaceable advantages, such as strong and stable fluorescence emission, good biocompatibility, excellent antifungal and antimicrobial activities. Recently, more than 6000 compounds based on the phenazine skeleton have been synthesized and recognized, including natural products and synthetic phenazine derivatives. Among them, natural products have antibacterial activity, greatly participating in significant biological processes.1,2 Similarly, various synthetic phenazine derivatives are well-known for use in biology and medicine. Moreover, with the successful development of natural products, enormous enthusiasm has been attracted among researchers to explore their remarkable properties and widespread applications. So far, phenazine derivatives have been employed in a wide variety of applications ranging from medicine to electronics owing to their stable optical and chemical properties.3–10

Phenazine is a rigid planar, redox-active and electron-deficient aromatic compound, which could be easily modified with functional groups or combined with some ligands to create novel functional materials.11–25 In the design of photoelectric functional materials, it becomes one of the most attractive and appropriate structures.26–40 Typically, the functionalization of nitrogen atoms on the phenazine skeleton leads to the formation of a series of phenazine derivatives with variable absorption and fluorescence properties. From then on, researchers have functionalized phenazine in a variety of ways, including alkyl, thiophene, aryl, cyanide and metal bridging, resulting in obvious colored, excellent conductive functional materials with photophysical properties that differ dramatically from those of other unmodified materials.41–50 Most importantly, by functionalizing phenazine derivatives to construct smart molecular materials, the unique properties of the phenazine framework and the merits of the introduced groups are integrated together, which further promotes the development of phenazine derivatives and broadens the types and applications of smart molecular materials.51–55 At present, intelligent materials based on novel functionalized phenazine derivatives have been applied to optical sensing, anti-cancer drugs, cell imaging, and biological antibacterial and other fields due to their unique optical properties and good biocompatibility, presenting huge potential for research.55–62

Up to now, previous reviews on phenazine derivatives have mainly focused on a summary of synthetic methods and their application in the detection of heavy metal ions, and in antineoplastic and antimicrobial activity.63–66 Herein, the present review is designed to provide a more comprehensive and up-to-date overview of novel functionalized phenazine derivatives in recent years. Moreover, we mainly emphasize the design of innovative and exceptional strategies to create functional materials based on novel phenazine derivatives, such as multicolor-tunable dihydrophenazine, thermally activated delayed fluorescent dibenzo[a,c]phenazine, mechanochromic luminescent dibenzo[a,j]phenazine and stable organic frameworks. Simultaneously, it explores the new design thought, excellent properties and promising applications in optical sensing and electrochemistry. We expect that this review will attract particular attention from the researchers working in this field but also serve as a useful starting point to new researchers in the future.

2. N,N′-Dihydrophenazine

Synthetic methodologies for multicolor-tunable molecules involved complicated design and rigorous processes in previous decades. Since then, researchers have already made tremendous progress in exploring methods for generating functionalized phenazine derivatives that might also conveniently and economically exploit excellent photophysical characteristics and potential application. Surprisingly, N,N′-dihydrophenazine and its derivatives demonstrate a distinctive vibration-induced emission (VIE) behavior that facilitates multicolor emission owing to excited-state configuration transformations from bending to planar.67–73 Most notably, the photophysical properties of dihydrophenazine derivatives might generate extraordinary stimuli responsiveness to external environmental factors, such as temperature, humidity, pH, solvent polarity and viscosity.74–80 As a consequence, through altering the environmental factors, such special VIE-active molecules might achieve a significant change in fluorescence color from blue to orange-red and even white. These interesting molecules have been extensively investigated in the fields of sensors, white-light-emitting materials and other multicolor smart materials.81–90

Tang and Li et al.91 designed and synthesized a series of N,N′-dihydrophenazine derivatives 1–5 to give a white-light-emitting, aqueous temperature sensor in 2020. It was observed that such VIE-active fluorophore 5 was considerably encapsulated in F127 micelles to create a multicolor emission by altering the temperature, successfully avoiding the use of organic solvents and the weak solubility of the fluorophore. Firstly, as shown in Scheme 1, this strategy proceeds via the Vilsmeier–Haack formylation at the p-position of dihydrophenazine 1 was required to afford molecule 2. Subsequently, intermediate 2 underwent reduction to give bi-functionalized compound 3. Moreover, the chlorinated dihydrophenazine 4 could be derived from the nucleophilic substitution reaction of compound 3 and SOCl2. In the functionalization strategy, the desired molecule 5 was furnished in moderate yield through phenolic modification of compound 4 and propanoic acid under optimal conditions. Due to the amphiphilic nature of F127 micelles, they could facilitate hydrophobic molecule 5 to be greatly solubilized in an aqueous system. Hence, in F127 micelles, the fluorescence properties of VIE-active molecule 5 were illustrated (Fig. 1). As the temperature increased, the microviscosity change of the F127 micelles could availably affect the intramolecular vibrations of molecule 5 according to the mechanism of the VIE effect, which further achieved multicolor fluorescence from blue to orange, including nearly the pure white-light emission. Meanwhile, the fluorescence of molecule 5 in the F127 solution could be observably quenched after addition of Fe3+. Interestingly, when adenosine 5-triphosphate (ATP) was added, the quenched fluorescence of 5 was dramatically recovered. This was due to the competition of the combination with Fe3+ between molecule 5 and ATP, resulting in the fluorescence quenching or recovery. In light of these fluorescence properties, this aqueous system could be applied in anti-counterfeiting fluorescent ink and white-light-emitting materials with temperature-sensitive response.

The same year, Zhang and Su et al.92 employed a similar strategy to furnish a white-light-emitting system based on crown-ether functionalized dihydrophenazine (6) by supramolecular assembly. Herein, as shown in Scheme 2, this approach involved introducing two benzo-15-crown-5 units into the intermediate followed by ester formation to obtain the desired product 6. The dihydrophenazine unit could act as a fluorescence signal and the crown ethers combined with K+. It was worth noting that crown ethers could possess hydrophobic and hydrophilic properties. They were regarded as phase transfer catalysts and strongly combine appropriate metal ions to form stable complexes. In view of this, the fluorescence properties of molecule 6 were illustrated (Fig. 2). With the addition of K+, the supramolecular interaction between molecule 6 and K+ could result in furnishing supramolecular aggregates, achieving blue emission. Nevertheless, disaggregation of the supramolecular assembly occurred when further 18-crown-6 was added, obtaining orange-red emission. Very interestingly, water solution also had the same effect as 18-crown-6. Utilizing the supramolecular assembly successfully gave tunable multicolor emission from blue to orange-red and even to white-light emission, which provided forceful connection between the VIE effect and supramolecular chemistry.

Considering their sustainability and affordability, non-volatile molecular liquids as soft materials have attracted considerable attention for organic methodologies. As shown in Fig. 3, these molecular liquids could mainly emit blue fluorescence at 20 °C. Nevertheless, they exhibited orange-red fluorescence at 85 °C. Moreover, these luminous liquids without solvents could be applied to multicolor printing, anti-counterfeiting and information encryption (Fig. 4).

Meanwhile, Su et al.94 developed and synthesized two new compounds (11 and 12) that combined the VIE effect and aggregation-induced emission (AIE) to accomplish single-molecular white-light emission with a high degree of control over solution viscosity and aggregation extent. To create a white-light material, the authors used VIE-active dihydrobenzo[a,c]phenazine and AIE-active tetraphenylethene or triphenylethene (Scheme 4). As shown in Fig. 5, in the mixture of toluene and polytetrahydrofuran, molecule 11 demonstrated a dynamic fluorescence shift ranging from red to green, including white-light emission. Moreover, molecule 12 had tunable transitions from red to blue, including white light, depending on the degree of aggregation in THF–water solution. Due to the competition between the VIE and AIE effects, as well as the restriction of intramolecular rotation, this technique might achieve single-molecular white-light emission.

Interestingly, Zhang et al.95 combined two colorless compounds (13 and 14) to create an excellent panchromatic fluorescent system by changing the mixture proportion and solvent polarity in order to obtain a full-color luminescent material. This unusual combination worked for a variety of reasons. First and foremost, 13 and 14 had nearly identical absorption in the near-UV region, which favored simultaneous convenient excitation. Furthermore, there was no association between the donor and recipient, resulting in avoidance of any interference from electron and energy transfer. Surprisingly, 13 with the excellent VIE effects was polarity-insensitive and acted as the red primary color. 14 possessed sensitive polarity and a dynamic fluorescence color ranging from blue to green. It was easy to achieve the entire luminescent system and form an RGB color triangle by adjusting the mixing ratio and solvent polarity of two fluorophores (Fig. 6). The full-color reproduction strategy was first reported by combining two colorless compounds. Moreover, this two-component tunable full-color material had potential applications in special fluorescent crayons and multicolor printing. Furthermore, the single molecule 15 was successfully synthesized (Scheme 5), exhibiting similar fluorescence emission, even including white light. Such an interesting molecule could be used as a white-light material.

As the typical example above, the sensors based on N,N-dihydrophenazine could achieve the detection and recognition for specific anions. in sharp contrast, they111 also developed a ratiometric fluorescent sensor 17 for a noxious metal ion (Hg2+). The synthetic route shown in Scheme 7 introduces a thymine into the dihydrophenazine core allowing it to combine with Hg2+, causing a configurational change. With the addition of Hg2+, the fluorescence intensity of sensor 17 gradually increased at 470 nm, and the emission at 601 nm vastly decreased. Moreover, the fluorescence color of sensor 17 could dramatically change from orange-red to blue (Fig. 8). It was worth noting that the stimulus response of sensor 17 for Hg2+ obtained within 2 min. Such distinct changes were not observed when adding other metal ions, including Ag+, Al3+, Ca2+, Cd2+, Co2+, Cr3+, Cu2+, Fe3+, K+, Mn2+, Na+, Ni2+, Pb2+, and Zn2+, which exhibited high sensitivity and selectivity for Hg2+. The result was attributed to the combination between sensor 17 and Hg2+, leading to a configuration transition from planar to bent shape by the mechanism of the VIE effect.

The brilliant properties and applications of such molecules stimulate us to explore more about the VIE mechanism. In 2017, Chou and co-workers126 devised a series of various macrocycles 25–32 (n = 1–8), which were greatly exploited in snapshotting the excited-state planarization. In this new strategy, the bi-functional alcohol intermediate was used as the main skeleton, in which the p-positions of 9,14-diphenyl were completely enclosed by phenyl ester or ether with different alkyl lengths (Scheme 13). Moreover, its synthetic approach was similar to the open-chain synthesis. The difference was that the key precursor could be further cyclized with carboxylic acids bearing various alkyl lengths to the corresponding macrocycles. It was observed that this could inflict different levels of constraints to prevent the structural deformation through their chain length, which could enable us to deeply understand the coupling of excited-state electronic processes with conformational motions. As shown in Fig. 15, we could see clearly that these molecules 25–32 exhibited obvious changes from blue, green to orange (n = 1–8) in toluene. Subsequently, by the time-resolved emission experiment and computational approaches three types of emission (R* → I* → P* process) could be observed. Therefore, this process constrained the specific structural changes and thus various fluorescence emissions were observed in excited states. Furthermore, by cutting alkyl-ester linkages the structural freedom could be released, resulting in prominent changes of the emission.

3. Dibenzo[a,c]phenazine

The dibenzo[a,c]phenazine unit has a highly conjugated skeleton and molecule rigidity. It is such a distinct structural feature that it facilitates prominent intermolecular interactions when combined with donor units. Impressively, dibenzo[a,c]phenazine employed a deeper LUMO level in the D–A molecule, which is used as a novel electron accepting moiety. Notably, such multi-ring fused phenazine might be used as an emitter for organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs). In light of this, Lee and co-workers in 2020134 developed a novel strategy to obtain two highly efficient yellow or red TADF emitters 45 and 46, which led to the fabrication of OLEDs with high external quantum efficiencies (EQE). As shown in Scheme 17, one fluorine atom of intermediates were selectively replated with an acridine unit, which were subsequently reacted with copper cyanide to acquire 45–46 in moderate yields. Among them, introducing cyano groups into the molecules 45 and 46 was beneficial to increase electron-accepting capacity and conjugated length. Fluorine acted as a subsidiary acceptor to fortify the HOMO–LUMO overlap, improving device performances. The experimental result showed that owing to the planar structure of emitter 46, the HOMO–LUMO gap decreased to 0.3 eV and the emission peak red-shifted 58 nm in comparison to the other emitter 46. Furthermore, the two emitters 45 and 46 possessed high EQEs, 27.6% and 23.8%, respectively (Fig. 20).

Adachi and co-workers135 also reported a novel TADF emitter 47, which possessed highly efficient electrofluorescence and applied in near-infrared organic light-emitting diodes. As shown in Scheme 18, the synthetic strategy involved the condensation reaction between tetraphenyl tetraamine and dibromo-9,10-dione to obtain the target molecule 47 with strong rigidity in the presence of CuCN, where dibenzo[a,c]phenazine and the tetracarbonitrile unit on 47 possessed high molecular rigidity, which could act as an electron acceptor. Two triphenylamine units as electron-donating moieties greatly provided efficient NIR-TADF behavior. More importantly, molecule 47 could availably act as an energy sensitizer for NIR fluorophores. The experimental result indicated that emitter 47 showed triplet-harvesting capacity. Moreover, it exhibited longer device lifetime during high current operation. Most importantly, the near-IR emitter 47 exhibited high PLDY than 40% at 729 nm and showed an EQE of 13.4% at 734 nm, which could be nonfrequent for near-IR OLEDs in this spectral range.

5. Organic framework materials

2D-conjugated polymers with specific repeating units have aroused extreme interest due to their precise and predictable structures, satisfying different particular requirements through ingenious and innovative designs.171–175 In particular, stable 2D-conjugated polymer frameworks also provide extensive applications in materials.176–182 So far, the construction of 2D-conjugated polymer frameworks has mainly required covalent organic frameworks (COFs), π-conjugated metal organic frameworks (MOFs), hydrogen-bonded organic frameworks (HOFs) and supramolecular organic frameworks (SOFs). As early as 2016, Feng and co-workers183 revealed the first construction of a 2D sp2-carbon-linked conjugated polymer framework. Similarly, Jiang et al.184 found that this 2D sp2-carbon-linked conjugated polymer framework showed high chemical stability and enhanced delocalized electronic structures. Recently, 2D conjugated polymers have gained much importance in photoelectrochemical (PEC) applications, and might be used as photocathodes for water reduction. This study enriched the applicability of PEC based on 2D conjugated polymers and also provided a valuable organic material as a photocathode for water reduction.

Multipurpose, switchable molecules are intriguing and promising for use as molecular sensors. Weber and co-workers208 developed four nickel(II) complexes with phenazine derivatives 95–98, achieving highly sensitive detection of the coordination-induced spin state switch (CISSS) by means of fluorescence spectroscopy. This method involved two steps (Scheme 41). Firstly, the condensation reaction between 2,3-diaminophenazine and the corresponding keto-enol ether gave intermediates as ligands. Afterwards, coordination of nickel(II) with the ligands afforded nickel(II) complexes. Most importantly, the result indicated that CISSS could greatly connect with the basicity of the axial ligands and the substitution-dependent acidity of the nickel(II) coordination position (Fig. 41). Remarkably, the fluorescence quenching after adding ligands displayed a “black or white” color, achieving digital sensing of spin state alteration.

Hua and Liu et al.209 developed near-infrared organic dyes 99 and 100 based on phenazine-cyanine, which could be used for photoacoustic imaging and photothermal therapy. Firstly, the n-butyl group connected with the nitrogen atoms of phenazine under alkaline conditions. Afterwards, the desired target molecules 99 and 100 were furnished in lower yields by the reaction of both iodide salt and the phenazine aldehyde (Scheme 42). Observably, the phenazine group could change from electron-deficient to electron-rich through connection between n-butyl groups and nitrogen atoms. In light of this, the optical properties of molecules 99 and 100 were studied and the two dyes could be self-assembled with human serum albumin (HSA) to form nanoparticles. Moreover, the two types of nanoparticles exhibited certain stabilities in both weakly basic and weakly acidic environments (Fig. 42). Furthermore, they could absorb light and convert light energy into heat energy. Meanwhile, using a nude mouse experiment photoacoustic imaging and photothermal effects of the nanoparticles were successfully verified.

Most surprisingly, phenazine might be employed as a photosensitizer. Ooyama et al.210 introduced hydroxyl groups and thiophene rings into the phenazine structure to obtain new molecules 101 and 102 as photosensitizers (Scheme 43). The phenazine structure generally contains an aromatic ring system and is replaced by two nitrogen atoms at the 9,10-positions, and has potential as a next-generation photosensitizer. The appropriate modification of multiple functional groups greatly facilitates their photophysical characteristics and photosensitizing ability. The addition of a nitro group could broaden the photoabsorption range into the near-infrared (NIR) region, whereas the quick nonradiative decay of excited states prevented photosensitized 1O2 production. Interestingly, the modification of the formyl group made phenazine derivatives acquire excellent photosensitizing ability (Fig. 43). Such favorable photosensitizers based on phenazine derivatives had a wide range of applications, including photocatalytic synthesis and water purification.

Besides that, Sergeeva and co-workers212 reported a photoactive dye 104 to obtain a novel 3D functional graphene nanophase material through covalent modification under white light. In this strategy, firstly, neutral red, as a non-toxic available dye, underwent diazotization to furnish a phenazine diazonium salt. Subsequently, phenazine radicals were generated from the phenazine diazonium salt by white light activation, achieving covalent modification of graphene. This strategy could be not only first successful and facile technology, but also an environmentally friendly photochemical approach. Most importantly, this 3D graphene hybrid material by modification enhanced its solubility and exhibited an optical band gap of 1.95 eV. Solar energy and wind energy are the renewable resources applied in many fields. Redox flow batteries and water splitting cell are innovative and promising technologies, which exhibit cost-effective and long-duration performances. Wang and Ji et al.213 designed and synthesized a series of amino acid functionalized phenazine derivatives 105–113, which could be applied in aqueous organic redox flow batteries (AORFBs) in almost neutral environments. In this method, the coupling reaction between brominated phenazine derivatives and various amino acids occurred to furnish the target molecules with good to excellent yields in Pd as catalyst (Scheme 45). This synthesis method possessed a wide substrate range. Due to their lower redox potentials and ability to adapt to double electron transfers, the phenazine derivatives have excellent potential for use as negolyts in AORFBs. Previous literature reports on AORFBs based on phenazine higlighted some drawbacks, such as weak solubility in aqueous solution and short working life. In light of this, the effects of the electrochemical redox characteristics and battery performance by various functional groups at different substituent positions of 105–113 were studied. The batteries based on amino acid functionalized phenazine derivatives 105–113 displayed open-circuit voltages of 1 V and excellent circulation performance, which achieved super high stability (Fig. 46).

Triptycene possesses a particular molecular structure comprising of polyhedral π-conjugated planes. According to the relevant literature, its derivatives could form honeycomb lattices owing to the intermolecular face-to-face overlaps of π places. It is noteworthy that this unique characteristic could greatly lead to the sorption and separation of gas molecules. In 2020, Awaga and co-workers214 reported a phenazine derivative based on triptycene acceptor 114. The synthetic strategy involved the condensation reaction between 2,3,6,7,14,15-triptycenehexaone and o-phenylenediamine to furnish the desired molecule with 31% yield (Scheme 46). Meanwhile, through the vapor diffusion method orange crystals of acceptor 114 were obtained in THF solution. Moreover, the experimental results demonstrated that 114 formed a honeycomb network owing to the π–π overlaps of the intermolecules, and that the ability of phenazine parties to accept hydrogen bonds enabled this honeycomb network to form a wide 1D channel with a diameter of 10 A. Very surprisingly, these honeycomb crystals of acceptor 114 greatly showed a strong reversible host–guest charge-transfer interaction with tetrathiafulvalene as an organic donor, achieving the adsorption/desorption of tetrathiafulvalene on/from these honeycomb lattice surfaces. Meanwhile, this phenomenon also appeared/disappeared with the host–guest charge-transfer interaction (Fig. 47).

7. Conclusions and perspective

This review summarizes the research progress of functionalized phenazine derivatives from synthetic strategies, to property studies, and application explorations in recent years. Meanwhile, we showed that particular groups of these molecules greatly affected their properties and applications owing to their diverse photophysical and chemical characteristics. In spite of achieving significant progress in these various fields, there is still a huge challenge of practical application in pure water and biological cells due to their toxicity. Moreover, it could be difficult to use some acquired materials for practical application in a large scale and highly efficient method. Furthermore, few materials could even lack long-term stability and durability in electrochemistry, limiting their valid value in real application owing to the poor solubility of large nitrogenous heterocyclic compounds. Therefore, there is imperious demand to solve these drawbacks. For all this, the exploration of these smart materials based on functionalized phenazine derivatives has still many opportunities owing to their excellent properties, which could need tremendous efforts to explore. Furthermore, phenazine derivatives possess excellent structural flexibility and redox-active activity, and we anticipate that phenazine derivatives will have a bright future in chiral catalysis, supramolecular catalysis and organic adsorption.
