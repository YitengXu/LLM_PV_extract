Abstract

Photocatalysis with stable, efficient and inexpensive metal-free catalysts is one of the most promising options for non-polluting energy production. This review article covers the state-of-the-art development of various effective metal-free polymeric photocatalysts with large Ï€-conjugated units for chemical transformations including water splitting, CO2 and N2 reduction, organic synthesis and monomer polymerisation. The article starts with the catalytic mechanisms of metal-free photocatalysts. Then a particular focus is on the rational manipulation of Ï€-conjugation enlargement, charge separation, electronic structures and band structures in the design of metal-free polymeric photocatalysts. Following the design principles, the selection and construction of functional units are discussed, as well as the connecting bonds and dimensions of Ï€-conjugated polymeric photocatalysts. Finally the hot and emerging applications of metal-free polymeric photocatalysts for photocatalytic chemical transformations are summarized. The strategies provide potential avenues to address the challenges of catalyst activity, selectivity and stability in the further development of highly effective metal-free polymeric photocatalysts.

1 Introduction

Very recently, there has been increasing attention on photocatalytic solar energy conversion via organic transformations in order to develop new environment friendly methodologies for chemical transformations at a lower cost and from renewable resources (water, CO2, N2, etc.).1â€“3 Regarding photoredox catalysts (PCs) for material conversion,4 concerns have been raised regarding the sustainability of precious metals (ruthenium, palladium, platinum, iridium, etc.) and trace metal (copper, iron, zinc, etc.) contamination, which may impede the use of the resulting materials in optical waveguides, microelectronics and biomaterials, for example.5 Consequently, the development of inexpensive metal-free photocatalysts is of considerable interest. Additionally, due to the variability of organic compounds, the optical and redox properties of metal-free polymeric catalysts can be effectively tuned through molecular design.

To make full use of solar energy as a renewable energy source, significant research endeavours have focused on the exploration of molecular photoelectric devices and photochemical systems for energy conversion. In the construction of solar energy conversion systems with metal-free polymeric semiconductors, a large conjugated structure is a prerequisite of solar light active materials covering the full spectrum of UV-vis to near IR light.6 Polymeric materials that are conjugated are made of large conjugated Ï€-electron systems, and they represent a brand new generation of photocatalysts featuring homogeneity. They display fundamental features of photocatalytic processes, that is, visible light activity, non-toxicity and robustness. Therefore, they are good candidates for renewable resources.7

Graphitic carbon nitride8,9 is a conjugated polymer, and it creates a new hotspot for research, and attracts attention from different disciplines. It can be used as a visible light responsive and metal-free photocatalyst for solar energy conversion and environmental remediation due to its appealing electronic band structure, high physicochemical stability, and earth-abundance. The utilisation of g-C3N4 in heterogeneous catalysis started in 200610 and the first application of g-C3N4 as a metal-free conjugated semiconductor photocatalyst was by Wang in 2009.11 These reports shifted research exploration from hard-to-tune inorganic catalysts to physicochemically-stable polymeric conjugated semiconductor photocatalysts.

However, C3N4 compounds still suffer from insufficient light-absorption and intrinsic charge separation, although many methods including doping, copolymerisation, coordination, covalent modification, non-covalent modification and defect engineering have been developed to solve these shortcomings.12,13 Compared with C3N4 conjugated compounds, other Ï€-conjugated metal-free catalysts have the potential for superior molecular design because the abundance of organic compounds leads to easy modulation of their photophysical and photochemical properties. Therefore, in recent years research into metal-free polymeric catalysts has emerged as a hot topic in photocatalytic chemical conversion. With the significant development of organic photovoltaic applications, the employment of various donor- and acceptor-based molecules with increasingly enhanced performance will provide a variety of options for the development of polymeric metal-free conjugated materials for chemical transformations.

In 2016, Vilela14 summarised the newly reported conjugated microporous polymers (CMPs) along with a view to their application as heterogeneous photocatalysts for chemical transformations including hydrogen production, organic synthesis and photopolymerisation. To provide a greater depth and breadth of information, this review presents many more state-of-the-art reports concerning Ï€-conjugated metal-free polymeric semiconductors used for photocatalytic chemical transformations. Beginning with the fundamentals of photocatalysis, we focus on the design principles and construction strategies for metal-free polymeric photocatalysts with large conjugated units as an intriguing platform for the development of various highly efficient and recyclable heterogeneous catalysts in chemical transformations. Additionally, metal-free non-polymeric conjugated catalysts (NPCCs) with a large degree of Ï€-conjugation are also summarised (Table 1). This review also describes a range of potential applications for metal-free polymeric photocatalysts, with special attention paid to water splitting, CO2 and N2 reduction, and organic reactions. Finally, an outlook on the directions in which metal-free photocatalyst research might lead is presented, as well as identification of some issues that remain to be solved.

2 Catalytic mechanisms of Ï€-conjugated metal-free polymeric photocatalysts

Generally speaking, there are two specific catalytic mechanisms, i.e. the semiconductor mechanism and the molecular mechanism, that have been reported to explain the photocatalytic process with metal-free photocatalysts (Fig. 1). As polymeric catalysts are constructed from small molecules or monomers, and the demarcation between metal-free semiconductors and small organic catalysts in photocatalytic reaction systems is non-deterministic, it is of great importance to discuss the two catalytic mechanisms separately herein. Moreover, this discussion will bring valuable inspiration for developing metal-free polymeric photocatalysts.
Fig. 1 Fundamentals of metal-free polymeric photocatalysts in the semiconductor and molecular mechanisms16â€“20 (CB, conduction band; VB, valence band; LUMO, lowest unoccupied molecular orbital; HOMO, highest occupied molecular orbital; CT, charge transfer; ET, energy transfer; PT, proton transfer; S1, excited singlet; T1, excited triplet; S0, ground state; Esub, electron potential of the substrates; RA, reactant A and B).

Basically, the key factors determining the catalytic availability are the excited-state properties of the photocatalyst and the subsequently-generated active species or direct reaction pathways with substrates. The roles of various active species, such as trapped electrons (eâˆ’) and holes (h+), superoxide radicals (O2Ë™âˆ’), hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), hydroxyl radicals (OHË™) and singlet molecular oxygen (1O2), have been reported during catalysis via the semiconductor mechanism. All kinds of techniques, such as transient absorption spectroscopy, the transient-state measurement of the surface photovoltage, and the time-resolved method of microwave conductivity (TRMC), are performed for characterization of the charge separation generated by photons during visible light active catalysis.15 Both computational and theoretical models have been adopted to reveal the electronic density of states and the semiconductor band structure so that we can develop applicable photocatalysts. Because the accuracy is high and the computational costs have been reduced, computational screening with high throughput is used to accelerate the process of discovering photocatalytic reactions.

In the photocatalytic molecular mechanism, the reaction process is dominated by the transfer of energy and electrons from the excited states to the substrate. As far as the transfer of electrons is concerned, reductive and oxidative quenching become possible, and each of them would result in quite different products. As far as the transfer of energy is concerned, under light irradiation, the photocatalyst's excited state returns to the ground state when the deactivation is completed; meanwhile, the transfer of electrons leads to the reduction or oxidation of the photocatalyst. Thus, the key factors in understanding and designing a photocatalyst are to match its excited state (singlet and triplet) and redox properties with the redox potentials of the substrate.

In the photocatalytic semiconductor mechanism, the photocatalysts act as heterogeneous catalysts, while in the molecular mechanism the photocatalysts are homogeneous. The mechanistic bridge that combines the catalytic fundamentals is found in frontier orbital theory. Obviously, it is challenging to understand the link between homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysis, as well as to design and build efficient catalytic systems at the molecular level for metal-free polymeric semiconductors for photocatalysis. Therefore, beyond the detailed discussion of metal-free polymeric semiconductors in this review, the subject of metal-free molecular photocatalysts will also be elaborated on in the Organic reaction section. Metal-free non-polymeric conjugated catalysts are thoroughly summarized to give some direction at the molecular level for designing much more efficient metal-free polymeric semiconductors dedicated to photocatalysis.

3 Design of metal-free polymeric photocatalysts with large Ï€-conjugation

In order to ensure an efficient photocatalytic event, an ideal photocatalyst should exhibit multiple optical, chemical and electrical properties. These include high stability to photocorrosion, a significant overlap of absorption with the sunlight emission spectrum (as a consequence of the bandgap involved), low cost, satisfactory efficiency in charge carrier separation, and a high quantum yield of the photoreaction. But, fundamentally, what will determine the photocatalytic availability and efficiency of a photocatalyst are the intrinsic principles of the electronic and band structures and charge separation. Following the presentation of the design fundamentals, this section discusses different functional units, connecting bonds, and dimensional extensions used in constructing metal-free polymeric semiconductor photocatalysts at the molecular level.

3.1 Modulation of the electronic and band structures

The optical spectrum coverage and catalytic activity of a photocatalyst mainly arise from its electronic and band structures, which also determine its redox properties for chemical transformations. Therefore, it is of great importance in molecular design to pay careful attention to the tuning of the electronic and band structures for specific chemical reactions. One of the effective ways to carry out electronic and band structure modulation is to introduce different donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) or donorâ€“Ï€â€“acceptor (Dâ€“Ï€â€“A) structures within the conjugated backbone.

For organic frameworks (POFs, Fig. 2) which are porous and Ï€-conjugated, a simple method used to adjust their redox potential was developed by Zhang et al.21 The obtained carbazolic copolymers (CzCPs) displayed a wide range of redox potentials due to the special co-monomers of electron donors and acceptors, and they are similar to those of general transition-metal complexes. Co-polymeric semiconductors display high efficiency in the stepwise degradation of lignin Î²-O-4 models.

In addition to the strategies of donorâ€“acceptor selection and arrangement, and Ï€-conjugation degree adjustment, introducing different electron-donating or withdrawing substituents is also an effective way to perturb the electronic structures of frontier orbitals by decreasing or increasing the highest occupied molecular orbital/lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (HOMO/LUMO) alignment.25 These methods enable utilisation of the electronic and band structures to regulate the ground-state absorptivity of low optical bandgap Ï€-conjugated polymers and molecules, and can also be exploited as a powerful means to modulate their excited-state relaxation pathways. Finally, the designed metal-free photocatalysts can be adjusted within a wide range to match the redox properties of certain chemical reactions.

3.2 Charge-separation promotion

The charge separation is designed so that the photogenerated carriers can spread spatially, and it is important for the enhancement of the photocatalytic activity. The reason is that the speed of the process of charge recombination (on the nanosecond scale) is significantly higher than that of the process of surface redox (10âˆ’8 to 10âˆ’1 s).26 Therefore, enhancing photo-generated carrier separation to suppress the recombination rate, as well as extending light harvesting to longer wavelengths, can generate more available electrons and holes for redox reactions. Generally, effective light harvesting and a suitable band structure are two of the factors guaranteeing the thermodynamic photo-generation of charge pairs. Generally, the charge migration from the internal bulk to the surface and subsequent effective contact with redox-active substrates both affect the overall kinetic efficiency of solar-to-fuel conversion.

Photocatalytic reactions, triggered by highly reactive charge carriers, start from the generation of photo-excited electronâ€“hole pairs. Then the electrons and holes are separated and transfer to the surface of the semiconductor photocatalyst. In molecular catalysis, the excited-state charge separation of photocatalysts is also a key factor in catalytic availability and efficiency.27â€“29

The chemical composition of most CMPs is nothing but carbon. As a result, with the transfer of charge, it is not enough to avoid the electronâ€“hole pairs induced by photons recombining spontaneously;30 efficient photocatalysis based on CMPs has great dependence on the migration of electrons to a specific co-catalyst, for example, platinum. There is another strategy that can be adopted to separate the charge effectively; different domains or point defects can be introduced to improve the probability of electronâ€“hole separation. Therefore, the photocatalytic activity can be improved. The results are confirmed for azine-based covalent organic frameworks.31

Within the molecular structure of the catalyst, only donor acceptor dyads hold the key to preventing undesired electronâ€“hole recombination via efficient charge separation. Building on the incorporation of electron-donating, sulphur-containing electron-rich linkers and electron-deficient triazine (C3N3) units into porous polymers, Bojdys32 reported the construction of several visible-light active, triazine-based polymers (Fig. 6) with a high ratio of S and N heteroatoms. The polymeric semiconductors photocatalytically generated H2 from H2O: up to 915 mmol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1 with a Pt co-catalyst, and 200 mmol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1 without, which is one of the highest reported values to date. The highly modular Sonogashiraâ€“Hagihara cross-coupling reaction was employed, enabling the systematic study of mixed (S, N, C) and (N, C)-only polymer systems.

Another external strategy for promoting charge separation is construction of direct Z-scheme systems. In these, the interface contact (i.e., boundary) is of great importance because photo-induced electrons can be injected directly into the VB of semiconductor I from the CB of semiconductor II or through an intermediate with a more conductive feature, and such catalysts are known as all-solid-state Z-scheme catalyst systems.33 Taking the example of CO2 reduction, the oxidation of redox mediators that are reduced and the reduction to solar fuels take place at one photocatalyst; at the same time, the oxidation of H2O to O2 or the reduction of redox mediators that are oxidised occurs at the other one, so as to simulate the photosynthesis process of plants in nature.34 Considering the intrinsic compatibility of organic semiconductors, this strategy is highly suitable for the construction of Z-scheme photocatalytic systems using different metal-free polymeric semiconductors.35

3.3 Functional units

Until now, many kinds of conjugated groups including pyrene, bipyridyl, benzothiadiazole, triazine, porphyrin, carbazole, perylene, sulfone, thienothiophene and truxene have been frequently used as the functional units of metal-free polymeric photocatalysts (Table 1). Many 3-D building blocks, such as the tetrahedral monomers used in covalent organic frameworks (COFs), cannot be used to synthesise CMPs because the tetrahedral node (e.g., saturated carbon atom) breaks the widely spread Ï€-conjugation. However, one of the reported strategies to avoid this is the use of spirobifluorene linkers.87,88 Novel functional segments and the corresponding materials are still to be discovered in the search for much more effective organic semiconductor photocatalysts for chemical transformations.

Benzothiadiazole-containing and benzoxadiazole-containing CMPs were synthesised by Zhang et al. through a Sonogashiraâ€“Hagihara coupling reaction.89,90 Subsequently, the research group presented a simple structural design principle for CMPs, and it is a heterogeneous system of photocatalysis which is purely organic, to realize the fine-tuning of the photoredox potential.16,90,91 If the positions of substitutions at the central unit of phenyl are changed, then the increased activities of catalysts can be regulated. It serves as a 3D centre of a number of poly(benzoxadiazole) networks (Fig. 7). For polymers, the positions of the CB and VB can be best aligned to include the needed oxidative and reductive potentials of the catalytic reactions, and there is no need to alter the moieties of the electron acceptor and donor in the backbone of the polymer network.

3.5 Dimensional design

Linear (1-D) conjugated polymers (CPs) have superiorities in molecular design, material synthesis and processing due to the relatively easy modification of two-functional-group monomers. Furthermore, 1-D CPs set the foundation of metal-free polymeric materials with the development of synthetic methods and functional-unit effects at the molecular level. However, with combined features of high porosity, light-weight elements and strong covalently bonded structures, dimensional extension of metal-free materials from linear structures (1D) to 2D or 3D results in superior exciton dissociation and yields to efficiently generate free charges and abundant reactive sites. The strategy has been proven to facilitate photocatalytic redox reaction processes.97,98
Linear (1-D) conjugated polymers. In 1985, Yanagida et al. published pioneering work on conjugated photocatalysts [linear poly(p-phenylene)s] for the evolution of H2. However, due to the poor stability and activity, there are only a few studies on conjugated polymeric semiconductors which are organic and linear for the photocatalytic evolution of H2.99,100 Although the AQY (0.015% at Î» = 405 nm, with a ruthenium co-catalyst) was poor in comparison with inorganic counterparts, this result opened up a new avenue for the design of efficient and low-cost organic semiconductors for photocatalytic H2 evolution from H2O under solar light irradiation. Allowing for different conjugated polymers and great diversity of their structure and composition, the research brings possibilities for the development of materials that absorb light for artificial photosynthesis.

Nevertheless, polymeric photocatalysts are still challenging. There is a limitation for most linear polymer semiconductors, because the binding energy of excitons is high, which ranges between 0.5 and 1 eV. If the crystal semiconductors are inorganic, such as ZnO, the binding energy is no more than 60 MeV. It is thus of pivotal importance to design new conjugated polymers with extended dimensions to promote exciton splitting and active sites, and ultimately improve the photocatalytic activity for chemical reactions.

Since 2009, as a distinctive kind of 2-D material, graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4) has gained wide attention due to the considerable activity for photocatalytic chemical transformations because of its multiple properties including light weight, metal free nature, stability, low cost and visible light activity. Polymerisation of planar and rigid monomers with more than two functional groups will give polymers with a 2D chemical structure. Homocoupling of trifunctional or polyfunctional monomers can result in 3D porous networks. 4 Photocatalytic chemical transformations

In the last decades, photocatalysis has been demonstrated to be one of the most promising approaches for environmental protection, solar energy conversion, and chemical transformations in the sustainable production of fuels. Visible light active photocatalysts such as metal-complexes, metal oxides, plasmonic photocatalysts and polymeric carbon nitride have been utilised for selective redox organic transformations.114,115 However, visible light induced photocatalysis with metal-free, highly conjugated polymeric catalysts is a relatively new area of materials science compared to the abovementioned categories. The current status of research on metal-free polymeric materials has been highlighted for photocatalytic energy conversion and utilisation including photocatalytic water splitting and CO2 reduction, and other photo-assisted selective conversion of organic compounds, N2 reduction, pollutant degradation and biological disinfection. This review will not discuss the applications of pollutant degradation and biological disinfection.

4.1 Water splitting

Particulate semiconductors are utilized to photocatalytically split water, and this is one of the most convenient ways in which photosynthesis can be imitated so that solar energy can be turned into renewable and clean H2 fuel that can actually be exploited and stored from the natural resource of water.116â€“118 So far, among the photocatalysts that have been developed, most of them are inorganic (oxy)nitrides, (oxy)sulfides and inorganic metal oxides, which are generally constructed on the basis of metal cations with electronic configuration features of d10 and d0. However, in most cases, their light-response properties have certain limitations. By contrast, due to the Ï€-electron system which is delocalized, visible light can be absorbed by conjugated materials. Because of the relatively low efficiency (AQY = 0.006% for H2 production under UV-light irradiation, Î» > 366 nm), there has been substantial interest in the design and synthesis of conjugated polymers for photocatalytic H2 evolution beyond C3N4 materials, which have been well reviewed.8,12,13,103,119â€“122

For photocatalytic water splitting, the electron-efficient dibenzo[b,d]thiophene 5,5-dioxide group is also effective in the construction of photoactive CMPs. Chen74 reported three conjugated microporous poly(dibenzo[b,d]thiophene 5,5-dioxide)s (B-DTs, Fig. 25) consisting of alternating electron-rich and electron-deficient units with ethynyl linkers. The range of the optical bandgap is between 2.29 eV and 2.79 eV, and if the CC unit is adjusted through the linkage to the central benzene in the three dimensional structure, this range can be well tuned. Allowing for its intrinsic advantages, such as the large BET surface area, the easily tunable bandgap etc., it is found that the as-obtained CMP (B-DT) is an ideal photocatalyst for the photocatalytic removal of NO. Later, it was demonstrated by Cooper71 and Jiang72 that crystalline COFs (Fig. 26 and 27) which are constructed on the basis of the benzo-bis(benzothiophene sulfone) moiety displayed greater activities for the photochemical evolution of hydrogen compared with their semicrystalline and amorphous counterparts. Computations suggested that the geometry of the polymer skeleton and the fine detail of the eclipsed conjugated unit layered stacking in the FS-COF might determine its prospects for thermodynamic proton reduction and water oxidation, and finally enabled a considerable improvement in the efficiency of photocatalytic hydrogen evolution.

4.3 Organic reactions

Organic synthesis. Recently, polymeric semiconducting photocatalysts for organic synthesis represent a more sustainable, easy post-treatment and separation, and environment-friendly alternative to traditional metallic photocatalysts because of their absorption in the visible range, non-metal and non-toxic nature, and highly tunable optical and electronical properties.42,56,128 Clearly, photoredox catalysis for relevant chemical reactions have emerged as an important application of conjugated polymeric semiconductors.

According to the definition made before, organic synthesis which is realized through a redox pathway can be categorised as reduction reactions and oxidation reactions in terms of the substrate. Commonly, oxidation reactions include oxidative coupling for the formation of carbonâ€“heteroatom bonds and carbonâ€“carbon bonds and oxidative degradation of organic contaminants. Reduction reactions include the hydrogenation of carbonâ€“heteroatom bonds or reductive bond formation. For photocatalytic oxidation reactions, molecular oxygen is often observed in the catalytic cycle. Generally, the active oxygen species singlet oxygen (1O2) and superoxide radicals (O2Ë™âˆ’) can be generated through the pathway of energy and electron transfer from the photocatalyst to molecular oxygen, respectively. 4.4 Nitrogen reduction

Conversion of naturally abundant dinitrogen (N2), which is found in excess of 70% in the earth's atmosphere, to ammonia (NH3), is one of the most attractive and challenging topics in chemistry: the very high kinetic and thermodynamic stability of triple-bonded N2 make it difficult to use as a substrate for chemical reactions.148 For practical use, one of the prerequisites for N2 fixation from solar energy and atmospheric air is a solar-to-NH3 (STA) efficiency of 0.1%. Although this high obstacle hinders the screening of effective catalysts as a great challenge for researchers, photocatalytic N2 reduction is still a promising alternative to the existing Haberâ€“Bosch technique, especially for metal-free photocatalysts. Consequently, metal-free and solar-driven photocatalysts have been rarely explored, with current studies mainly focusing on the electrocatalytic N2 reduction reaction (NRR) or N2 fixation using B-modified or Fe-doped g-C3N4 in both experimental and theoretical studies. Other metal-free photocatalysts for N2 for N2 fixation under solar light have been seldom reported.64,149,150

In principle, the pioneering development of transition metal-based catalysts originates from the complicated electronic configuration of empty and occupied d orbitals existing in one atom, which matches well and simultaneously with the lone pair electrons and anti-bonding orbitals of N2 to weaken the strong NN triple bond (Fig. 51).151,152 The activated N2 bond then cleaves, eventually producing ammonia after then incorporation of protons and electrons from external sources. Therefore, metal-free catalysts for photocatalytic reduction of N2 using B atoms incorporated into g-C3N4 are effective if B atoms are introduced at the atom-level molecular plane. It is a tough process in ensuring an empty sp3 orbital and a half-occupied sp3 orbital of B atoms. One of the techniques is that each distinct B atom interacts with two N atoms to form two Bâ€“N bonds in C3N4 compounds. Therefore, the incorporated B atoms will have strong binding energy with N2 molecules and produce the possibility of photocatalytic activity of N2.65

To overcome this limitation, multielectron processes are more preferable to achieve lower reduction potentials during photocatalytic N2 reduction.155 The breakthrough should also involve the ultra-slow surface reaction concerning 6eâˆ’/6H+ dynamics, which finally determines the rate-limiting reductive activation of N2.156 Consequently, it is predicted that photocatalytic chemical N2 reduction or transformation with metal-free catalysts will be an area of great challenge and uncertainty for a long period in the future.

5 Conclusions, perspective, and outlook

Photocatalytic chemical transformation for solar fuel production using large Ï€-conjugated metal-free semiconductors or organic catalysts can not only retard the problem of the greenhouse effect from CO2 emissions in the atmosphere, but also produce CO2-based chemicals from sustainable solar energy in principle. This review highlights the catalytic mechanism, design principles and structural construction of metal-free polymeric photocatalysts bearing large Ï€-conjugated units, dedicated to photocatalytic chemical transformations including organic synthesis, monomer polymerization, water splitting and CO2 and nitrogen reduction. There are a growing number of studies in this popular field, and this research is performed with an aim to adjust catalystsâ€™ intrinsic properties, such as the reactant mass transport on the nano scale, rapid interfacial reaction kinetics, rapid charge collection, transport and separation and exceptional light absorption.

Especially, due to the unsatisfactory light harvesting, low conversion efficiency, lack of selectivity to specified products, and complicated photocatalytic mechanisms, there is still a long road to pave for solving the scientific and technological obstructions to promote this artificial photosynthesis for potential use with abundant solar sources.

Despite the significant advances in the last 10 years in the field of metal-free photoredox catalysis, there remain many opportunities for further exploration. First, the most important task is to find a more selective and active photocatalyst with which the recombination of charge can be reduced efficiently, and the entire solar spectrum can be captured to increase the photocatalytic efficiency to be comparable with inorganic counterparts. The frequently reported non-polymeric conjugated compounds available for certain chemical reactions will provide considerable inspiration for the design and screening of effective metal-free, highly-conjugated polymeric catalysts for large-conjugation in photocatalytic chemical transformations.

There is another challenge, that is, the adsorption on the surface of photocatalysts should be optimised, as the capacity and state of adsorption can impose an influence on the substrate conversion in terms of its selectivity and activity both thermodynamically and kinetically.

Finally, a deeper understanding of photocatalytic mechanisms is required to accelerate the screening of specific catalysts for traditional chemical reactions. So far, there are only a few studies on the explanations and theories behind the collective heterostructure phenomena that have been observed. If there are more theoretical studies, the band structure can be improved in a rational way, and photocatalytic materials can be designed morphologically so that new materials can be discovered.

Ultimately, all results indicate that the photocatalytic conversion of chemicals into valuable energy-bearing fuels is still in its infancy, and that breakthroughs for bringing this technology to reality can occur when all these problems and strategies above have been taken into consideration, enabling a well-designed photocatalytic system.
