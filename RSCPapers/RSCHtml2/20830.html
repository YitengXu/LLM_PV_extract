State of the art and prospectives of heterogeneous photocatalysts based on metal–organic frameworks (MOFs): design, modification strategies, and their applications and mechanisms in photodegradation, water splitting, and CO2 reduction†

Abstract

Metal organic frameworks (MOFs) have sparked a wave of research in the field of photocatalysis due to their ultrahigh specific surface area, porous sparse structure, and tunable topology and composition brought about by numerous metal centers/organic ligands. In this study, we first summarize and refine the basic design strategies of MOFs as photocatalysts and their modification strategies in enhancing photocatalytic performance from the perspective of heterogeneous photocatalysts. Subsequently, we review the recent research progress on the specific applications of MOFs in heterogeneous photocatalysis, i.e., photocatalytic degradation of water pollutants, photocatalytic water separation, and photocatalytic reduction of CO2, including the selection of design strategies and modification strategies for MOF-based photocatalysts, photocatalytic mechanisms, and parameters affecting the photocatalytic performance. In addition, this study highlights the mechanistic studies of MOF-based heterogeneous photocatalysts from different perspectives according to their specific applications and compares and refines the photocatalytic mechanistic studies in different fields through different techniques such as in situ characterization and DFT as well as the commonalities and connections between different mechanistic studies. Finally, the disadvantages and advantages of heterogeneous photocatalysts based on MOFs, possible ending directions for existing problems, and possible key research directions for the future, are discussed.

1 Introduction

1.1 Backgrounds

In the past few decades, a variety of advanced porous materials consisting of inorganic and organic components, such as zeolite,1 porous organic polymer,2 covalent organic frameworks (COFs),3 and metallic organic frameworks (MOFs),4 has gained popularity in scientific and technical domains.5,6 MOFs, a brand-new and rapidly expanding class of ordered porous materials, have been the focus of intense scientific attention since their discovery by the Yaghi group7 in 1995. In MOFs, organic connectives serve as secondary building units (SBUs),8,9 which self-assemble from transition metal ions. These periodically networked crystalline porous inorganic–organic compounds exhibit flexible design,17 excellent surface area,18 obvious high thermal stability, and diversity of topological structures,19 making them attractive for a wide range of applications including gas adsorption10 and separation,11 catalysis,12 sensing,13 drug precursor development,14,15 and energy storage.16

i) Yaghi et al.7 (1995) pioneered the development of isoreticular metal–organic frameworks (IRMOFs), which are octahedral open-bridged coordination polymers consisting of [Zn4O6]+ clusters and organic carboxylic acid ligands. This breakthrough enabled the transition from crystalline microporous materials to crystalline mesoporous materials, a significant advancement in the field of porous materials.

ii) Zeolitic imidazolate frameworks (ZIFs) are composed of imidazoles acting as double-fork bridging ligands and transition metal ions such as Zn or Co, which assemble from N atoms to form pores resembling those of a silica–aluminum molecular sieve.

iii) Materials of Institute Lavoisier (MILs) are a class of porous materials that typically exhibit a very large specific surface area. There are two subgroups of MILs: one is composed of transition metals, dicarboxylic acids, and lanthanides, while the other is synthesized using terephthalic acid or caramel acid and trivalent chromium, iron, aluminum, or vanadium.

iv) Porous coordination networks (PCNs) are primarily synthesized by reacting Cu with ligands such as pyranoic acid, H3TATB (4,4′,4′′-triazine-2,4,6-tribenzoate), or HTB (s-heptane tribenzoate). PCNs have a cage and channel connected by a tiny diameter window and a three-dimensional orthogonal channel structure, resembling a bag (Fig. 1).

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have garnered considerable attention due to their high porosity, rich redox chemistry, high surface area, and various functional groups on the frames.15 MOFs consist of organic ligand molecules that collaborate with metal ions or clusters to create a unique frame with a porous topology, uniform structure, and high surface area. Metal ions in MOFs include Ni(II),26 Cu(II),27 Fe(II),28 Mn(II),29 Cr(III),30 Zn(II),31 and Co(II).32 By combining different metal ions with organic ligands, an infinite number of MOFs can be created with the requisite structural, magnetic, electrical, optical, electrochemical, and catalytic capabilities. MOFs have been formed in over 20000 species and are classified into 1D,23 2D,24 and 3D25 structures based on organic and inorganic units. 1.2 Energy storage equipment

Rapid global population growth and technological advances have resulted in an increased demand for fossil fuels, leading researchers to explore numerous renewable energy sources,35 such as hydrogen, geothermal, wind, and solar energy, to replace traditional energy sources with environment-friendly options. However, renewable energy efficiency also depends on effective energy storage devices.36 Electrochemical energy storage devices, including lithium-ion batteries and supercapacitors, play a crucial role in modern electronics and flexible wearable devices. Effective energy storage is essential to optimize renewable energy efficiency. Developing a rapid charge–discharge system with high specific volume is crucial. Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) and their derivatives possess high surface area, tunable pores, structure, and composition, as well as the functional characteristics of both an organic ligand guest and a metal ion host. These properties offer significant benefits and prospects for applications in energy storage and conversion. MOFs can be used directly or through composite derivations in certain structural materials.37
1.2.1 Lithium-ion battery. Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) have achieved remarkable commercial success in powering portable electronic devices. During charging and discharging, lithium ions are intercalated and deintercalated between the cathode and anode of lithium-ion batteries, also known as “rocking chair batteries”. The large capacity of lithium-ion batteries is limited by the use of graphite in the positive electrode, which has a theoretical capacity of only 372 mA h g−1.38

Graphite-based anode materials usually require fluoridation or oxidation, coating, doping, surface treatment, and mechanical grinding. Although it can meet the energy requirements of some modern devices, developing new electrode materials with high specific energy to enhance the energy density and cycle life is a feasible alternative to replace traditional lithium-ion batteries. Porous carbon or metal compounds with low oxidation–reduction reaction potential, such as lithium cobalt oxide and lithium iron phosphate, are often used as lithium-ion battery cathode materials. These cathode materials are typically composed of lithium-rich oxides with a high oxidation–reduction potential. The electrochemical properties and structure of graphene have been improved through oxidation modification, coating modification, and etching modification to address its shortcomings. However, the use of transition metal compounds in Li-ion batteries is limited by drawbacks such as poor electrical conductivity and a significant volume change of lithium ions during deintercalation.39

MOFs and their derivatives, carbon materials, and metal compounds with distinctive nanostructures created by the template method of MOF materials offer an attractive platform for addressing the current concerns associated with organic cathodes.40 For example, Hai-Jun Peng et al.41 reported a sacrificial precursor of MOF constructed with Mn(II) particles and ligand H2PBA (5-(4-pyridin-3-yl-benzoylamino)-isophthalic acid). The mesoporous Mn3O4/C composites formed after MOF pyrolysis at 500 °C exhibited a remarkable capacity of 1032 mA g−1 after 500 cycles. Alternatively, Yuzhen Han et al.42 used a MOF coating approach (MOF-SC), casting a thin MOF film on the silicon layer's surface and sandwiching it between the active silicon and the diaphragm to increase the area capacity of low-cost silicon from 265 to 1700 μAh cm−2, which remained at 850700 μAh cm−2 after 50 cycles.

In the realm of solid electrolyte (SSE),43 MOFs have wide-ranging applications in lithium-ion electron electrode materials. MOFs have been developed as nanofillers in polymer matrices to construct SSEs. These MOFs serve as lithium-ion conductors by absorbing lithium-containing chemicals and transferring lithium ions through the nanopores. However, they also disrupt the chain-arrangement of polymer molecules and reduce the crystallinity of PEO to achieve high ion conductivity. For instance, Guolong Lu et al.44 fabricated a novel composite solid electrolyte by synthesizing the bifunctional MOF (MOF-2) from two distinct functionalized UiO-66 materials with carboxyl and amine groups (PEO-MOF-2). This electrolyte achieved higher ion conductivity of 5.20 × 10−4 S cm−1 and lower battery loss, exhibiting a capacity retention rate of 98.45% after 100 cycles at 1.0 °C (Fig. 3).

1.2.2 Supercapacitors. Supercapacitors have the potential to be widely employed in electronics and hybrid electric cars in the future of energy storage.46 Due to their ability to deliver a significant amount of power instantly as well as their high charge–discharge rate, high power density, safe operation, and ease of maintenance, which have a special significance in the application of hybrid electric vehicles,47 supercapacitors are also able to provide immediate power assistance for rechargeable batteries. On the other hand, supercapacitors cannot attain an energy density that is equal to or greater than the batteries, which drastically restricts the practical uses for them. As a result, the increase in supercapacitor energy density while retaining capacitance and power density has become a new essential condition.48

The energy density of supercapacitors is largely dependent on the materials used for the electrodes. They were classified as either fake capacitors49 or electric double layer capacitors (EDLCs) based on the charge storage technique and the material used as the electrode. In the case of EDLCs, a double layer of ions is often formed at the electrode/electrolyte interface, where the charge of a supercapacitor is normally stored.50 The energy density attained by EDLCs is often lower because of the poor physical buildup of charge in the water-based electrolyte. A false capacitor, on the other hand, stores charge through a reversible Faraday reaction. Typical electrode materials are transition metal oxide,51 metal sulfide,52 etc. Supercapacitors achieve higher capacitance and energy density than carbon-based materials.

Supercapacitors hold great promise for use in electronics and hybrid electric vehicles as a future energy storage solution.46 Due to their ability to deliver a large amount of power instantaneously, as well as their high charge–discharge rate, high power density, safe operation, and ease of maintenance, supercapacitors have a special significance in the field of hybrid electric vehicles.47 In addition, supercapacitors can provide immediate power assistance for rechargeable batteries. However, they cannot achieve an energy density that is equal to or greater than batteries, which significantly limits their practical applications. Therefore, increasing the energy density of supercapacitors while maintaining their capacitance and power density has become a new essential condition.48 The electrode materials used in supercapacitors largely determine their energy density. Based on the charge storage technique and the electrode material used, supercapacitors can be classified as either pseudocapacitors49 or electric double layer capacitors (EDLCs). In the case of EDLCs, a double layer of ions is formed at the electrode/electrolyte interface, where the charge of a supercapacitor is typically stored.50 The energy density achieved by EDLCs is often lower due to the poor physical buildup of charge in the water-based electrolyte. On the other hand, pseudocapacitors store charge through a reversible Faraday reaction and commonly use transition metal oxides,51 metal sulfides,52 and other materials as electrode materials. Compared to carbon-based materials, pseudocapacitors can achieve higher capacitance and energy density.

MOFs have received significant attention in the research of supercapacitors due to their unique characteristics, such as adjustable porous structure, rich redox chemistry, and high surface area. To serve as an electrode material, MOFs must possess high conductivity, specific surface area, redox activity, large pore structure, and chemical stability.53 MOFs can be utilized as the active material or as the substance for the active electrode, or indirectly by serving as the template for the sacrifice.54

MOF-derived carbon compounds have a distinct advantage over other carbon materials such as graphene and carbon nanotubes because of their tunable porous structure, high porosity, and high specific surface area. The heat treatment of MOF precursors has been effectively used to produce carbon compounds in various forms. The calcination of MOFs in the air leads to the production of metal oxides with a structure similar to the MOFs template and a higher surface area compared to equivalent metal oxides produced using traditional methods. MOFs also play a role in the creation of a range of mixed metal oxides and metal oxide composites. Carbon materials such as graphene, reduced graphene oxide, and composites of carbon nanotubes can be used as supercapacitor electrodes. Metal oxides derived from MOFs can also be used, such as hexagonal layered nanostructures of NiCo2S4 formed by calcining MOFs on reduced graphene oxide sheets for supercapacitor electrode materials reported by Subalakshmi Kumar et al.49 In the future, supercapacitors may use the exceptional properties of MOF-derived metal oxides in combination with highly conductive carbon materials (Fig. 4).

1.3 Physical adsorption and separation of gas

Due to the combination of metal ions and organic ligands, MOFs and their derivatives show promising applications as porous coordination polymer materials for gas adsorption and separation.56 As a low-cost porous crystal, MOFs composites can be sintered to increase the hollow area of the material and the active adsorption sites on the material surface, thus increasing the adsorption capacity of gas molecules.57 They can then be further applied to the separation and absorption of toxic gases.

Researchers can design nanoporous material MOFs to realize the preferential binding of different gas molecules due to the regulability of the target metal ions and organic ligands with different functional groups, making it possible to use MOFs materials to treat air pollutants (ppm level). For example, Susan E. Henkelis58 sintered large amounts of Mg-MOF-74 to separate SOx from water vapor. The SO peak in the FTIR spectra confirmed the MOF selective absorption of the SOx, and as a kind of hydrophilic material, Mg-MOF-74 in the lab before two days to maintain the integrity and the three-dimensional structure of temperature.

Alternatively, we can directly deploy MOFs materials in factories to study the adsorption capacity of energy and chemical applications.

For instance, natural gas treatment concentrates on eliminating both added and naturally existing odor-causing components, such as sulphur compounds. While Lomig Hamon et al.9 focused on evaluating the H2S adsorption capacities of different MOFs, the natural gas industry has developed absorption procedures employing amine treatments to remove the majority of these sulfur compounds. Measurements were conducted to determine the sulfur weight isotherms of MOFs MIL-53(Al, Cr, Fe), MIL-47(V), MIL-100(Cr), and MIL-101(Crhydrogen). To lower the final sulphur level and thus fulfil the environmental criteria, porous substances are also being taken into consideration (Fig. 5).

A range of chemicals, including H2S gas, can be treated using the physical features of MOF materials. Using MOF materials, modern industry can also purify methane from light hydrocarbons without distillation. A new 3D microporous MOF [Co2(5,4-PMIA)2(TPOM)0.5] with superior C2Hn/CH4 and CO2/CH4 separation capabilities was developed by Jingui Duan et al.59 In addition, a successful submicron-based microsolid phase extraction (SPE) approach in conjunction with liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry has been developed for the accurate and sensitive detection of trace quantities of monohydroxy polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons in complicated human urine. By coupling the ligand 5-(4-pyridinyl)-methoxy-m-benzoic acid (5,4-PMia) with Co(II) ions in the presence of the flexible linker tetris (4-pyridinoxymethyl) methane, the new 3D micropore MOF [Co2(5,4-PMIA)2(TPOM)0.5]-x solvent was effectively realized (TPOM). The mild recognition sites of oxygen atoms and benzene rings from the 5,4-PMIA2-molecule, as well as the flexible TPOM connectomes inside the channel of 1, are designed to offer a pore environment with moderate contact to the guest to enable gas storage and separation with extraction selectivity for OH-PAHs via a synergistic effect.

Taking advantage of this, we can even go a step further and use MOF materials as gas detectors (ppb level) with higher precision requirements by combining them with integrated circuits. For example, Hairui Fang60 designed a CuO/NiO composite material derived from MOFs that has a strong response to H2S gas via hydrothermal sintering of low-temperature precursors. A 106% response rate to 50 ppb H2S was shown by this material-based sensor, which also showed remarkable H2S gas selectivity. The MOFs material may be used to assess the freshness of meat in cold storage settings because hydrogen sulphide (H2S) gas is one of the hallmark gases for determining the freshness of meat and detecting meat spoilage.61

1.4 Effects of catalysis

Catalysts play a crucial role in the production of more than 10000 products, which are worth over $10 trillion per year, accounting for 15% of the world's gross domestic product (GDP).62 It is widely believed that catalysis will play a key role in the development of new energy, chemical manufacturing, and pollution control technologies that will help societies break their reliance on fossil fuels and move toward a more sustainable future. This belief is based largely on the accomplishments of the 20th century and is supported by the strong international science and technology base currently engaged in catalysis research.63 The development of more efficient catalytic materials and processes is a rapidly evolving and exciting field of study, and MOFs offer an excellent opportunity for the rational design of new catalytic solids.
1.4.1 Electrocatalysis based on MOFs. Among the various hydrogen production methods, water electrolysis stands out as an efficient and pollution-free approach. Currently, alkaline electrolytic cells are widely used for industrial electric-driven water reduction hydrogen production. However, the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) has been observed to suffer from significant kinetic retardation due to the high activation barrier of the H–O bond of water molecules. Therefore, the development of high-performance and stable HER electrocatalysts is of utmost importance. Platinum (Pt) catalyst is an excellent electrocatalyst due to its low overpotential, fast reaction kinetics, and excellent molecular adsorption and desorption properties.64 However, the high cost and poor stability of platinum catalysts have led to a search for nonprecious metal electrocatalysts. Metal oxides, metal nitrides, and metal borides have been extensively studied as potential HER electrocatalysts with significant activity.65

Metal organic frameworks, which possess dispersed planar metal nodes and distinctive 2D π-conjugated structures, have been identified as promising electrocatalysts for HER.66 MOFs can serve as direct electrocatalysts or as sacrificial precursors and templates to create other electrocatalysts.67 In addition, due to their excellent and stable electrocatalytic activity,68 MOFs can even be used for precise electrochemical signal induction applications (see Fig. 6).

For example, Kun Rui et al.69 reported an effective strategy for constructing noble metal/two-dimensional MOF heterostructures using uncoordinated MOF ligand surface oxygen sites. This method regulates the electronic structure of highly dispersed precious metal nanoparticles such as Pt and Pd on the MOF surface, without the use of surfactants. As a proof-of-concept, 2D Ni-MOF@Pt hybrids with good interfaces were applied to promote electrochemical hydrogen evolutionary reactions (HER) and provide good electrocatalytic activity under acidic and alkaline conditions. Subsequent electrochemical characterization showed that at 10 mA cm−2, the overpotential of Ni-MOF@Pt loaded with 20 wt% Pt decreased by 477 and 528 mV, respectively, which verified the improvement of electron transport dynamics in Ni-MOF@Pt, as shown in Fig. 6. 1.4.2 Thermal catalysis based on MOFs. Since the industrial revolution, the excessive use of fossil fuels has led to various environmental problems, including global warming and acid rain caused by high nitrogen oxide emissions. The traditional NH3 selective catalytic reduction (SCR) method has been widely used in industrial settings to eliminate nitrogen oxides, using classical SCR catalysts such as V2O5, WO3, and MoO3. However, the high operation temperature range of 300–400 °C and the limited availability of SCR catalysts due to their expensive nature, poor sulfur resistance, and low catalytic activity pose significant challenges. MOFs have recently garnered significant interest in the field of SCR thermal catalysis due to their large surface area, considerable porosity, and adjustable chemical activity.70 For instance, Haoxi Jiang et al.71 discovered that Cu-MOF-74 exhibited excellent water resistance and achieved 100% N2 selectivity and 97.8% NO conversion at 230 °C. In another study, Jiang72 employed the hydrothermal approach to produce Mn-MOF-74 with a hollow spherical structure and Co-MOF-74 with a petal-like form. The two MOF catalysts showed 99% and 70% catalytic activity for low-temperature denitrification at 220 °C and 210 °C, respectively, in the presence of NH3. These promising results suggest that MOFs may play a significant role in the development of efficient and cost-effective SCR catalysts for reducing nitrogen oxide emissions in various industrial applications.

MOFs materials can be assembled by organic connectors and transition metal ions. The doping of transition metal ions is a reliable method to regulate the low-temperature denitrification performance of MOFs materials. Shangzhi Xie et al.73 used the solubilization approach to create mono and bimetallic MOF-74-M (M = different metals) catalysts for NH3-SCR. The researchers tested the catalysts through various characterization techniques and found that the effectively synthesized MOF-74-M catalyst had a nearly hexagonal shape, high specific surface area, and uniform distribution of metal components. Monometal catalysts using Mn were found to increase activity due to the element's variety of valence states and excellent oxidation reducibility. Water experiments for catalytic activity and sulphur resistance showed that the addition of a proper quantity of SO2 boosted the catalytic activity of MOF-74-M due to an increase in the acidic sites. In situ DRIFTS data revealed that the E–R mechanism dominates the low temperature ranges of MOF-74-MnCo and MOF-74-Mn, while the L–H mechanism dominates the high temperature range (Fig. 7).

1.4.3 Photocatalysis based on MOFs. Since the first industrial revolution, human reliance on fossil fuels (such as coal, oil, and natural gas) has driven the modern industrial era74 and rapid economic and social growth.75 However, the excessive burning of fossil fuels has significantly increased carbon emissions (such as CO2, CO, and CH4) into the atmosphere, causing severe environmental pollution and drawing high attention from governments worldwide.76,77 Unfortunately, only half of the 2.13 billion tons of CO2 emitted into the atmosphere each year are taken up by natural processes, leaving a net increase of 10.65 billion tons of CO2 in the atmosphere annually.78 Atmospheric CO2 concentration has increased steadily, reaching 419.05 ppm in April 2021, the highest level on Earth since the advent of humans, according to the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration's monitoring data. Thus, one of the most significant challenges facing humanity is climate change, which is caused by greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide absorbing long-wave radiation from the Earth's surface and warming the atmosphere.79 To address this issue, finding a renewable energy source that is clean, cheap, safe, viable, and environment friendly is crucial to meet future energy needs.80

Solar energy is an abundant and renewable energy source that has the potential to address the worldwide energy problem and pollution.81 Photocatalysis, as a solar-mediated catalysis, has attracted increasing attention from researchers interested in harnessing solar energy through the interaction of light and matter.82,83 The most commonly used photocatalysts are composed of transition metal complexes modified with organic ligands. However, conventional heterogeneous photocatalysts are generally limited by their active sites,84 which constrain the spectrum of photocatalytic processes. Novel materials that can combine the advantages of homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysis, such as MOFs, are being researched, as are procedures for depositing photocatalysts onto solid substrates. MOFs offer excellent solid-state support due to their ability to be synthesized into materials with controlled porosity, chemical functionalities, and customizable porous structures, making them useful in establishing the structure–activity relationship of photocatalysis.85 Molecules with high light absorption capacity can even be used as assembly materials to design MOFs with photocatalytic activity.86

Metal energy sites, ligands that bring dopants to bind to MOFs either through a preparation process or by post-synthetic modification, and changes to organic linkers are all factors that affect how effective MOF-based catalysts are in real-world applications. Organic linkers have a variety of uses, including as supports for encapsulating or excluding biomolecules, catalytic complex separators, and homogeneous catalysts. As a result, materials based on MOFs are frequently used as oxidation, epoxidation, and photocatalyst catalysts. To produce a wide variety of functional nanomaterials utilizing various processing procedures, MOFs can be employed as supports to load active nanomaterials for diverse catalytic applications or as sacrificial templates.

In practice, the performance of MOF-based catalysts normally depends on

(i) Metal energy sites.

(ii) Ligands that introduce dopants to bind to MOFs either during the preparation process or after the fact.

(iii) Organic linker modification.

Organic linkers can be used as homogeneous catalysts to separate catalytic complexes and as supports for biomolecule capture or exclusion.

Therefore, MOF-based materials are often utilized as catalysts for oxidation, epoxidation, and photocatalysis due to their versatility. MOFs can act as supports for active nanomaterials in various catalytic applications or as sacrificial templates to produce functional nanomaterials using diverse processing methods. MOFs possess exceptional features such as long-range ordering, high porosity with diverse organic structures, and facile degradation to metal oxides through a simple self-assembly mechanism with organic ligands and metal ions, making them well-suited for heterogeneous catalysis. Co-doped MOFs and mixed metal oxides can be synthesized using metal frameworks with diverse compositions, and these heterogeneous frameworks exhibit superior catalytic activity due to the synergistic effects of various metals. Complex materials such as metal/metal oxides, metal/carbon, metal oxides/carbon, and metal/metal oxides/carbon can be developed to achieve high photocatalytic performance. MOFs also have several advantages over other inorganic materials. For example, photoactive organic linkers can provide MOFs with semiconductor properties. Upon exposure to light, organic linkers absorb it by transferring electrons from the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO).87 The metals in the MOF network then receive the electrons produced by the light via the organic connector-to-cluster charge transfer mechanism (LCCT), allowing them to participate in photoredox reactions such as the reduction of carbon dioxide,88 photocatalytic water splitting, or photodegradation of contaminants in water bodies. By selecting appropriate metal centers and organic linkers and controlling the synthesis process, MOFs can efficiently serve as heterogeneous photocatalysts.

To advance the future development of photoassisted heterocatalysis, this review introduces recent advances in heterogeneous catalysts based on MOFs and summarizes the basic design strategies and modification approaches to enhance the photocatalytic activity of MOFs materials. Subsequently, we discuss the photocatalytic applications of MOFs materials in the degradation of water pollutants, water splitting, and carbon dioxide reduction from various perspectives, including design concepts, synthesis techniques, and essential photocatalytic parameters. The focus of this paper is on understanding the photocatalytic mechanism of MOFs materials in various applications to better comprehend the factors that promote their photocatalytic activity, such as light collection, charge transfer path, and key intermediates. We anticipate that future researchers will conduct more comprehensive studies in this area. We conclude by briefly outlining the challenges facing MOF photocatalysis today and in the future as well as providing recommendations for its advancement (Fig. 8).

2 Design strategies and modification methods of MOFs

2.1 Design strategies of MOFs

2.1.1 Transition metal ion center. The kind of metal center, coordinate number, and oxidation state all affect the structural stability of MOFs. Excellent-oxidation metals can establish potent coordination bonds with carboxylate O donors in the ligand,13 imparting high stability on the resultant MOFs. High-priced metal cations often have richer coordination environments than low-priced metal cations. As the core metal ions grow more inert, the chemical stability of the identical MOFs in acids, bases, and water rises. On the other hand, the strength of the metal–oxygen bonds in common oxides controls their thermal stability.

Joong Kang et al.,90 for example, purified MOFs of the same kind without coordinating organic connections, including Al-BDC (aluminum benzoate of MIL-53-Al), Cr-BDC (MIL-53-Cr), and V-BDC (MIL-47-V). We compared the thermal and chemical stabilities. The chemical stability for bases, acids, and water decreases in the order Cr-BDC > Al-BDC > V-BDC, suggesting that the stability rises as the core metal ion's inertness increases. On the other hand, the strength of the metal–oxygen bonds in common oxides such as Al2O3, Cr2O3, and V2O5 may explain why thermal stability declines in the order Al-BDC > Cr-BDC > V-BDC.

The exchange of transition metal ion centers in MOFs can also strengthen the bonds between the metal and ligand. For example, Huanhuan Li et al.91 prepared a nickel-doped MOF-5 by the solubilization method, which has significantly larger Langmuir specific surface area, larger pores, and stronger hydrothermal stability compared with untreated MOF-5.

2.1.2 Ligand. Numerous stable MOFs are built using different nitrogen-based ligands or by attaching highly charged metal ions to carboxylate-based ligands. The HSAB (hard/soft acid/base) concept can be used to support this tactic. On the one hand, functionalizing organic ligands that are hydrophobic can make the framework more stable in water. On the other hand, the ligand's basicity will make the metal coordination link stronger. Metals are mostly composed of Lewis acids, which may form stronger bonds with basic ligands.

For example, Lv et al.92 produced three series of ligand rotations with comparable connectivity but different degrees of flexibility. 2.1.3 Band structure adjustment. The entire MOFs structure is composed of central interfaces of organic ligands and transition metal ions. Therefore, the band structure of MOFs is strongly related to the rearrangement of interfacial energy levels at these interfaces. This unique feature allows us to artificially exchange organic ligands and transition metal ion centers to adjust the band structure of MOFs.95 The appropriate VB is a necessary condition for photocatalytic water splitting and the reduction of CO2. The appropriate bandgap displacement of MOFs has a nonnegligible impact on effective charge separation at heterogeneous junctions. Therefore, the bandgap engineering of MOFs has attracted the attention of many researchers. Simply put, the exchange of transition metal ion centers or the binding of functionalized organic ligands are currently the most effective means of energy band adjustment in MOF materials.

For example, for the first time, Chi-Kai Lin et al.96 demonstrated that increasing the size of zinc-based metal clusters led to a red shift in the bandgap of MOFs. Further studies showed that the π-conjugation produced by organic ligands in Zn-based MOFs also has a significant effect on its bandgap; reducing the conjugation of carboxylate organic connectors can expand the bandgap of MOFs. Hung Q. Pham et al.97 studied the band structure of a series of halogen-doped IRMOFs by density functional theory. The authors demonstrate that halogens can adjust the bandgap of MOFs, and halogens I are particularly good at increasing the valence band maximum (VBM). Rushie Mae Cedeno et al.98 demonstrated that the construction of mixed organic connectors formed a mid-gap state in zirconium-based MOFs, leading to a reduction of the bandgap. Moreover, in zirconium MOF with π–π packing, the change in the bandgap is more obvious, which is directly related to the interaction energy between organic ligands in MOFs.

In addition, Terence Musho et al.99 confirmed that amino groups may lower the bandgap energy of Zr-UiO-66 using DFT calculations and UV-vis studies. Contrarily, the nitro group somewhat lowers the bandgap energy of Zr-UiO-66, which is a result of the bonds that the functional group nitrogen and aromatic carbon have. To change the valence band maximum (BDC) of MOFs, Christopher H. Hendon et al.100 functionalized organic ligands, such as terephthalates, with NH2. It has been done to reduce the bandgap from 3.76 eV to 2.75 eV. The movement of N 2p electrons in the direction of the aromatic junction unit is what causes this VB redshift. The authors further show that while the electrical tuning of the aromatic pattern is confined, modifying the VB directly alters the optical characteristics.

According to Sizhuo Yang et al.,12 a molecular Pt-catalyst (Pt(dcbpy)Cl2) and Ru-photosensitizer ([Ru(dcbpy)(bpy)2]2+) were effectively integrated into Zr-MOF, and the resulting catalyst demonstrated good activity and stability in light-driven H2 generation from water. The authors show that charge separation occurs in this mixed MOF by electron transfer (ET) from Ru-photosensitizer to Pt-catalyst using optical instantaneous absorption (OTA) spectroscopy. The Pt L3 XTA spectra may reveal the reduced Pt site in the center, conclusively demonstrating the charge separation state produced by the ET of the Ru-photosensitizer.

2.1.6 MOFs activation. The most popular technique for activating MOFs includes exchanging them with low-boiling solvents, including acetone, methanol, and methylene chloride, before vacuuming at high temperatures. This is due to the fact that most MOFs are created using solvents, especially those that are very flammable, such as N-dimethylformamide (DMF), N-diethylformamide (DEF), and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO). Although solvent exchange and vacuum processing of the created MOFs are also required, this process, called “thermal activation”, has been extensively used for conventional porous materials such as porous carbon and zeolite.

Despite the fact that the majority of MOFs have low stability, an increasing number of them have been shown to have good thermal and chemical stability. This makes it possible for them to be instantly activated using typical thermal activation. But most MOF crystallizes in a combination of one or more very flammable solvents, including acids, DMF, DMSO, water, and DEF. If the M–O bonds in these MOFs are weak and their thermal stability is limited, the structures of these MOFs may partially dissolve after heat activation. 2.2 Modification strategies of MOFs

2.2.1 Semiconductor and cocatalyst load. In the specific applications of MOF, the researchers found that the photoexcited charge carrier easily recombines. The modification of surface forces in MOFs with a cocatalyst is an option to solve the problem.106

In general, there are two strategies to improve the photocatalytic activity of MOFs using cocatalysts: one is to construct an interface/barrier between the catalyst and MOF to facilitate charge separation and transfer, and the other is to use the loaded cocatalyst as a reaction site to consume the separated charges. These two ideas show us the importance of interface design in the tube catalysis discourse. This is very reasonable because, on the one hand, the bulk of molecular-level redox processes take place on its surface, and on the other, the interface or barrier we build provides a crucial conduit for charge carrier transfer and separation and is crucial in avoiding electron and hole recombination. To actualize the flow of electrons from the MOF to the cocatalyst, the bandgap energy of the cocatalyst should typically be lower than that of MOF. As an illustration, when noble metal ions are loaded, their low Ea enables them to not only create Schottky junctions with MOF to trap electrons but also to offer a useful clean return point. In the photocatalysis of MOFs, conducting semiconductors such as graphene and metal oxides can also have a comparable effect as noble metals.

Taking the surface oxygen sites of uncoordinated MOF ligands into consideration, for instance, Kun Rui et al.69 reported an efficient method for building noble metal/2D MOF heterostructures. Without the use of surfactants, the electronic structure of highly dispersed precious metal nanoparticles such as Pt and Pd is regulated on the surface of MOF. 2D Ni-MOF@Pt hybrids with well-defined interfaces were used as a proof of concept to promote electrochemical hydrogen evolutionary reactions (HER) and provide excellent electrocatalytic activity in acidic and alkaline conditions.

Jinfeng Chen et al.107 reported a TiO2/MIL-101(Cr) photocatalyst. The characterization technique revealed that on the TiO2-MIL-101(T-MCr) photocatalyst, two types of heterojunctions, type II heterojunctions and surface heterojunctions, were formed simultaneously, and their synergistic effects effectively promoted the separation and transfer of photogenerated charges and holes. The photocatalytic activity of MCr in degrading tetracycline under visible light irradiation was significantly increased after loading TiO2 particles on its surface (the degradation efficiency was 94.85% after 45 min of visible light irradiation).

Alternatively, we can more accurately embed active metal ligands with high photocatalytic activity into the MOF backbone or photosensitive transition organics, thus optimizing the catalyst to achieve a more precise tuning of the performance of photocatalysis.

For instance, Deshan Zhang et al.108 successfully embedded the H3TCA ligand (triphenylamine-based photosensitive ligand 4,4′,4′′-nitrotribenzoic acid) into a highly porous and stable MOF, and the obtained MOF photocatalyst(Zr-TCA) showed an excellent photocatalytic activity (Fig. 13).

2.2.2 LSPR (localized surface plasmon resonance). A significant amount of free electrons is present in the noble metal, according to the isoplason effect. These electrons can be thought of as electron gas travelling against a homogeneous backdrop of positive charge. The local mobility of the electrons inside the metal causes the entire electronic system to move collectively as a result of the Coulomb force at work. When exposed to light, isomeric plasmon nanoparticles generate an oscillating conductive electron set or isomeric plasmon. Similar to how light (optical oscillations) is comprised of photons, plasma oscillations are built of plasmons. Plasmons are the quanta of plasma oscillations. The other decays to generate high-energy heat carriers (hot electron–hot hole pairs), which may be efficiently retrieved from the isometric plasmon nanoparticles and drive several unique chemical processes. Some of the light quantum energy is stored in the isometric plasmons and re-emitted as light. The isoionization plasmons have a big impact on things such as increasing sunlight's ability to absorb energy, widening the titration spectrum response range, encouraging photogenerative electrons to separate into holes, boosting the internal energy of photogenerative carriers, and acting as the catalyst for catalytic reactions. The isoplasmon approach is frequently used to apply MOFs materials for photocatalysis, and the load of mono or even polymetallic particles is frequently expressed (Fig. 14).109
Fig. 14 Schematic diagram of isoplasmon resonance effect on local surface of metal nanoparticles. This figure has been reproduced from ref. 109 with permission from Ahmed, S. A., copyright 2019.

The interaction of electromagnetic waves with free electrons on the metal surface results in a near-field electromagnetic wave that moves along the metal surface, which is what causes the surface plasmon effect. Electromagnetic waves that hit the metal-dielectric interaction cause the free electrons on the metal surface to collectively oscillate. Resonance will happen if the electron's oscillation frequency matches the incident light wave's frequency. In the resonance state, the free electrons on the metal surface efficiently transfer the electromagnetic field's energy into their entire vibrational energy, creating a novel electromagnetic mode in which the electromagnetic field is amplified and restricted to a limited region around the metal surface.

When free electrons and photons interact at the surface of a metal, surface isopionons, which are electromagnetic oscillations, are produced. Surface ionization displays a number of peculiar properties as a result of the interaction between surface charge oscillation and the electromagnetic field of a light wave.

Metal nanoparticles' plasmon resonance cannot be sustained for a very long time due to the damping effect and will eventually relax. Surface plasmon relaxation can occur in one of two ways.

(1) The radiation relaxation of the released photon increases the strength of the electromagnetic field close to the metal nanoparticles, increasing the signal of molecules adhering to their surfaces.

(2) The nonradiative relaxation of high-energy hot carriers is produced. The energy of the photon is transformed into surface isometric plasmon energy when the metal nanoparticles exhibit the LSPR phenomenon. The surface isometric plasmon attenuates in a nonradiative form as a result of the damping effect. A nonequilibrium electron distribution is created when the hot electron that results gains the energy of a photon and makes an energy level leap. Higher-energy electrons can transmit more energy to lower-energy electrons (hot electrons). Adsorbed molecules will undergo chemical reactions as a result of the metal-molecule charge transfer that takes place when the energy level of the molecules that are adsorbed on the surface of metal nanoparticles matches the high-energy carrier (hot electron/hot hole pair). Warm electrons contribute energy to lattice vibration by electron–phonon scattering and subsequently release energy into the surrounding space via phonon–phonon scattering in the thermal form.

For instance, Kulamani Parida et al.110,111 produced a collection of Zr-based MOFs, UiO-66, UIO-66-NH2, and Au/UIO-66-NH2 and assessed their effectiveness for H2 evolution. The outcomes demonstrated that Au/photocatalytic UiO-66-NH2's efficiency has increased. Au/UIO-66-NH2 > UIO-66-NH2 > UiO-66 is the order in which the reported H2 evolution rates are seen. This is explained by the potent LSPR impact of the priceless metal Au and the linker's improved ability to absorb visible light as a result of the presence of the –NH2 group. The first recombination of electron–hole pairs is prevented by the role of gold nanoparticles as electron collectors from the photoexcited UiO-66-NH2 conduction band (see Fig. S2†). A buildup of photogenerated electrons on the surface of Au can easily encourage the reduction of H2O molecules to produce H2.

2.2.3 Z-type heterojunction modification. Given the wide bandgap and high electron–hole pair recombination rate of MOFs, we often use other semiconductor materials with the same photocatalytic activity together with MOF to construct a composite nanocatalyst possessing a heterojunction structure for promoting photocatalytic activity. The traditional heterojunction structures112–115 were mainly three types of heterojunctions, type I, type II, and type III (Fig. 15(b)–(d)). In type I heterojunctions, the bandgap of one semiconductor (SC-I) is completely covered by the bandgap of the other semiconductor (SC-II), which makes the photogenerated carriers and holes all gather on the same semiconductor (SC-I) after separation, which brings an extremely high electron–hole complexation rate and is not conducive to the utilization of photogenerated carriers. In the type-II heterojunction, the bandgaps of both are staggered and lined up, avoiding too high electron–hole complexation. However, the redox reactions all occur on the lower side of the point (oxidation: SC-I, reduction SC-II), still limiting the further enhancement of the photocatalytic activity. The gap structure of the type III heterojunction even leads to no photogenerated carrier separation with the separation of holes. The researchers fully absorbed the advantages and disadvantages of the three traditional heterojunctions and developed the Z-type heterogeneous photocatalyst with the most excellent photocatalytic performance by optimizing the bandgap structure.

The Z-type heterojunction system reduces the bandgap of the semiconductor116–119 by bringing more positive VB edges and more negative CB edges compared to the traditional heterojunction system.

The photogenerated electron–hole pair (EHP) separation follows a distinct path in Z-scheme photocatalysis. The photogenerated VB hole of SC-I and the electrons in the CB of SC-II join when exposed to visible light. The outcome is the formation of sufficiently strong oxidizing VB holes in SC-II and reducing electrons in SC-CB. This significantly increases EHP separation while significantly reducing broad electron–hole pair recombination, which is the basis of our photocatalysis. A Z-type heterojunction photocatalyst's excellent separation efficiency of photogenerated carriers with high redox capacity is hence its most notable characteristic. Direct and indirect Z-type heterojunctions are the two forms of Z-type heterojunction. The fundamental difference between the two is whether or not a carrier transfer intermediate is present. A direct Z-type heterojunction's internal electric field drives carrier transfer, but an indirect Z-type heterojunction needs electrical conductors (such as Ag and graphene) to support charge-carrier transfer.120

For example, Ya Li et al.122 developed a Z-scheme Fe2O3@Ag–ZnO@C heterostructure that was employed to photodegrade tetracycline and methylene blue pollutants in wastewater (see Fig. S3(a)†). According to EIS measurements, the Z-scheme structure created from MOFs considerably increases the lifetime of photogenerated electrons, accelerating charge separation in photocatalysis. The interface effect and carbon layer, which effectively separate electron transfer via the heterojunction and electron transfer through the carbon layer, are to blame for the longer lifespan. Because Ag–ZnO has a higher negative Fermi level than Fe2O3, the bands of Ag–ZnO and Fe2O3 are bent into a Z-shaped structure when they join to form a heterojunction. This can significantly enhance charge carrier separation.

At the interface between Fe2O3 and Ag–ZnO, photogenerated electrons from the CB of Fe2O3 may be effectively transported to the VB of ZnO, where they can recombine with holes. The photogenerated electrons on ZnO CB and the holes on VB of Fe2O3 can thus remain stable in redox reactions. Ag is a possible electron acceptor. The photogenerated charge generated on ZnO CB can be transferred to Ag to enhance the separation of the photogenerated charge carrier. It is noteworthy to notice that both the efficiency of charge transfer during the photocatalytic process and the capacity to absorb light are increased by the carbon layer created by the MOF coating on the catalyst.

Liu and Jiang et al.123 also reported a series of novel Z-type photocatalysts, which were prepared by loading Ag nanoparticles on the Ti-MOF/NiFeLDH surface (see Fig. S3(b†)). The obtained Ti-MOF/Ag/NiFeLDH Z-wake composite photocatalysts achieved 95% rhodamine B (RhB) degradation (50 min) and 92% levofloxacin (LVX) degradation (70 min) under visible light.

2.2.4 Functional groups-amine-functionalized. Due to the highly controllable nature of MOFs, it is feasible to generate visible light active catalysts by modifying the type of organic ligands.124 However, the catalytic active sites located on these MOFs produce relatively low activity.125 We also know that adding different functional junctions to a framework will affect its catalytic performance; thus, the inclusion of functional junctions into MOFs is called the trend.126,127 Among them, the achievement of amine group functionalization is the most remarkable.

Meicheng Wen et al.,128 for example, reported an amine-group functionalized H2 evolutionary system using Pt NPs as a catalyst, rhodamine B as chlorophyll to collect solar energy, and TEOA as a sacrificial reagent. At low temperature carbonization (300 °C), Qiong Zhu et al.129 synthesized a carbon frame decorated Cu2O (NH2–C@Cu2O) photocatalyst modified with –NH2 by metal–organic framework (NH2–Cu-MOF). The organic ligand converts to amorphous carbon during the low temperature annealing process, which can promote photogenic electron transfer and inhibit Cu2O photocorrosion. In situ FTIR analysis revealed that changing the amino group could significantly increase the chemical adsorption of CO2 due to the strong acid–base interaction (see Fig. S4†). Furthermore, NH2–C@Cu2O preferentially forms HCOO−-intermediates over others, improving CO2 reduction selectivity to HCOOH. At the same time, because of its electron acceptance properties, amino functionalization can further inhibit carrier recombination. The yield of HCOOH increased threefold when compared to pure Cu2O. Tian Wei Goh et al.130 explored in detail the effect of different functionalized linkers on photocatalysis based on MOFs. Goh prepared a series of Zr-MOFs using NH2-BDC (2-amino-1,4-benzoate ester) as the main connector and X-BDC (X = H, F, Cl, Br) as the auxiliary connector. 2.2.5 Construction of mixed linkers MOFs. Most MOF-based photocatalysts are characterized by sacrificial agent dependence or poor thermal stability in photocatalytic performance evaluation. An effective solution is to introduce multiple linkers with different topologies or functions into the original MOFs to construct a mixed linker MOFs, which exhibit the following advantages over single-linker MOFs.

(1) The increase in the number of linkers can bring higher stability to the mixed linker MOFs.

(2) The variety of linkers that can be introduced makes the resulting hybrid linker MOFs more tunable than traditional MOFs, with more ordered pores and multiple functional properties from the linkers.

MOFs, for example, can incorporate a large number of different functions in the manner of mixed connectors on the linking groups, rather than forming separate domains, according to Yaghi et al.127 Yaghi synthesized complex MOFs from 1,4-phthalate esters and their derivatives –NH2, –Br, –(Cl)2, –NO2, –(CH3)2, –C4H4, –(OC3H5)2, and –(OC7H7)2. Eighteen multivariable MOF-5 structures with eight different functions of zinc oxide and methylene units in these structures provide an orderly backbone, but the distribution of functional groups is disorganized. Properties that are not straightforward linear summations of pure components may be produced as a result of the intricate arrangement of numerous functional groups in the pore. For example, MTV-MOF-5-EHI, when compared to its best congener, has a selectivity for carbon dioxide that is 400% greater than that for carbon monoxide.

Yanxin Gao et al.132 successfully prepared hybrid linker Zr metal-centered MOFs PCN-134 for the photocatalytic degradation of DF (diclofenac). 2.2.6 Construction of missing linkers MOFs. In three-dimensional (3D) metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), the presence and properties of structural defects are considered a key factor affecting the physical and chemical behavior of the material. The presence of missing linker defects has been shown to have a significant impact on the gas adsorption capacity,133 catalytic activity,134 and stability135 of MOFs.

Hui Wu et al.136 first demonstrated that genuine UiO-66 materials contain a large number of missing linker defects. Through high-resolution neutron power diffraction measurements on a deuterated UiO-66 sample, Hui Wu found that on average, approximately one out of 12 linkers is missing in UiO-66, effectively reducing the network connectivity from 12 to 11, and the UiO-66 structure has better tolerance to missing linkers. The experiment also demonstrated that the presence of missing linker defects significantly improves the porosity of the actual material (from 0.44 to 1.0 cm3 g−1), and the metal center has a preferential adsorption for gases.

Rui Zhang et al.137 assembled UiO-66 crystals with controlled size and missing linker defects into Fabry–Perot thin film sensors. Due to the well-controlled micro and mesostructure features of MOF crystals with missing linker defects, the mesoporous characteristics of the sensing element improved the sensitivity (increased by ∼24.6% for saturated ethanol vapor), response rate (increased by ∼42.9%), and recovery rate (increased by ∼59.7%).

3 Photocatalytic degradation of water pollutants based on MOFs

The water environment all over the world has deteriorated dramatically due to pollution caused by unregulated human factory sewage treatment and the burning of huge amounts of fossil fuels,139 and the project to treat the natural water environment deserves our attention.140 Biomedical development is a culprit. With numerous reports of widespread detection of drug residues in surface and groundwater, they can also have a very negative impact on human health.93,106,130 To remove these pollutants, scientists have used a variety of procedures, including biological, physical adsorption,141 and various electrochemical methods.142 However, the complexity of operation, high cost, and inefficiency limit their further development. Therefore, there is an urgent need to update our pollutant removal methods to protect the environment as well as save the costs.

The degradation of water pollutants by photocatalysis is a very promising method,142 which requires us to develop a photocatalyst with excellent photocatalytic properties. This imposes the following requirements.

(1) A broad spectrum of light absorption; (2) a bandgap that is small enough to allow visible light stimulation; (3) effective charge separation; (4) a high level of stability and redox capacity; (5) recycling; and (6) a reasonable price. For the efficient degradation of different EOC,143 heterogeneous photocatalysts such as TiO2, ZnO, Fe2O3, CdS, GaP, and ZnS have been extensively utilized. However, several of them exhibit common flaws when put in a working environment. Because only about 5% of the solar spectrum is in the ultraviolet region, the largest bandgap of the most common TiO2 photocatalyst prevents it from being used in practical applications.144 There is a practical motivation to create stable and effective photocatalysts that function in the visible range to improve solar energy usage as about 43% of the solar spectrum resides in this area. Finding narrow bandgap-stabilized photocatalysts that can effectively degrade water pollutants by generating OH and O2 in visible light has proven to be difficult though.101

Since the first report on the use of MOF for the catalytic degradation of industrial products (phenol) in water in 2007,146 many researchers have carried out a lot of work in the field of photocatalytic degradation of water pollutants147 by MOFs due to its good photoelectric and photocatalytic performance, high chemical stability, and unique structural characteristics.148

3.1 Design of traditional MOFs

Jianchuan He et al.148 designed a NH2-MIL-88B with Fe as the metal ion center through group functionalization. He studied in detail the degradation of methylene blue (MB) in water at different pH environments. The SEM images showed that the NH2-MIL-88B(Fe) worked as a needle with a length of about 800 nm and a diameter of 300 nm. Characteristic peaks of symmetry and asymmetry due to amine functionalization were observed in FTIR at about 3490 and 3370 cm−1. NH2-MIL-88B(Fe) showed good MB removal and hydrothermal stability in the pH range of 3.0–11.0 in the presence of visible light.

Li Shi et al.149 thoroughly investigated the photocatalytic reduction of Fe-based MOFs NH2-MIL-88B(Fe), NH2-MIL-53(Fe), and NH2-MIL-101(Fe) Cr(VI). When NH2-MIL-88B(Fe) was used to photocatalyze the reduction of Cr(VI) in the presence of visible light; its photocatalytic activity was much higher than that of unfunctionalized MIL-88B(Fe).

3.2 Metallic heterogeneous photocatalyst materials based on MOFs

Because of the tunable structure and composition of MOFs, their synergistic catalysis with nanometals and other cocatalysts has also been extensively studied. Mahendra Yadav and Qiang Xu150 synthesized a MIL-101with highly dispersed metal nanoparticles fixed in for the catalytic reduction of Cr(VI) to Cr(III) (see Fig. S5†). We know that because the electrons and holes of Cr(VI) have a high recombination efficiency and a low adsorption activity, its photocatalysis employs a two-solvent method to highly fix dispersed nanometal particles in. The composites formed during the reduction of Cr(VI) to Cr(III) with formic acid were investigated. The results showed that among all the metal nanoparticles, Pt-MOF performed the best.

Composites incorporating single ionic metals alone still suffer from inadequate interactions, clogging of active sites, weak exciton separation, etc. So, to solve this problem, the researchers explored several mixed-metal MOFs.

The sol–gel integration of Cu, Pd nanoparticles on ZIF-8, which was also employed for the photoreduction of Cr(VI) in water, was accomplished by Yifan Zhang et al.151 In addition, it has been carefully examined, utilizing a number of characterization methods. The obtained CuPd@ZIF-8 catalysts significantly outperformed the original ZIF-8 catalyst, according to the performance evaluation results, achieving a photocatalytic reduction of 89% Cr(VI) (20 ppm) in aqueous Cr(VI) for 60 min and maintaining a stability of more than 90% of the photocatalytic activity after four cycles. Suraj Prakash Tripathy et al.152 further developed a mixed-valence bimetallic Ce/Zr MOF comprising Ce3+ and Ce4+ ions for photocatalytic hydrogen generation and degradation of ciprofloxacin (CIP) by a one-step pyrolysis technique since the Ce3+ and Ce4+ oxidation states are cheap and readily interchangeable. Due to the exchangeable Ce3+/Ce4+ ion pairs, this bimetallic Ce/Zr MOF exhibits a smaller bandgap, improved exciton separation, and superior ligand–metal charge transfer (LMCT).

The insertion of Ce ions brings about a higher electron density around the Zr4+ ions and a large number of oxygen vacancies (OV), resulting in better photocatalytic activity. The Ce/Zr 1:1 MOF in the photocatalytic performance evaluation exhibited a 4-fold enhancement in hydrogen production efficiency (468.30 μmol h−1 (ACE = 3.51%)) and 90.8% enhancement in degradation efficiency of the CIP. A further intriguing finding from the PL and EIS analyses is that the prepared bimetallic MOFs have improved exciton anti-conjugation. 3.3 Nonmetallic heterogeneous photocatalyst materials based on MOFs

Reduced graphene oxide (rGO) is widely used for the photocatalytic degradation of pollutants in water due to its excellent photoinduced excitation ability and environmental friendliness (nonmetallic material).154

Using bioinspired polydopamine nanoparticles (PDA NPs) as starting templates, Nuray Celebi et al.155 developed zirconium-based MOFs (IOO-66, PDA@MOF) in suitable core/shell heterogeneous topologies for the photocatalytic destruction of MB. The nanostructures created can aid in the reduction of oxygen to superoxide anion (O2) and the formation of HO−, which results in visible LED light. The PDA shells used have a regular spherical morphology and a size of 350 nm. The band properties of the PDA@MOF (ECB = −1.10 eV and EVB = 1.85 eV) compared to a normal hydrogen electrode (NHE) also show a high surface area and good bandgap provided to the MOF by Celebi's core/shell architecture, permitting 99% MB degradation in 80 min under visible LED illumination while this was happening (see Fig. S8†).

Feiping Zhao et al.157 developed a MIL-101(Fe)/g-C3N4 heterojunction as a bifunctional photocatalyst to simultaneously reduce Cr(VI) and degrade BPA under visible light. The improved photocatalytic performance of the MIL-101(Fe)/g-C3N4 heterojunction is attributed to the formation of direct Z-type heterojunctions between MIL-101(Fe) and g-C3N4 with appropriate band alignment, which enhances light absorption and effective charge carrier separation. The presence of higher O2 concentrations is crucial for the photocatalytic degradation of BPA on the MIL-101(Fe)/g-C3N4, and electron spin resonance and radical trapping studies have confirmed that light-generated electrons are responsible for oxygen-induced Cr(VI) reduction and BPA degradation. Charge transfer at the interface stage can occur via a straight Z-scheme method or a dual transfer mechanism, with the former being the most effective in explaining the improved photocatalytic reduction of Cr(VI) in MIL-101(Fe)/g-C3N4. The excellent separation of photoinduced e− and h+ couples results in reduced charge recombination of each semiconductor through the direct Z-type process, which accounts for the exceptional photocatalytic performance. Overall, the MIL-101(Fe)/g-C3N4 heterojunction photocatalyst holds great promise for the simultaneous reduction of Cr(VI) and degradation of BPA under visible light.

3.4 MOF-derived nanocomposite photocatalyst

Metal sulphides,158 such as In2S3, ZnIn2S4, and CdIn2S4, are excellent candidates for photocatalysis159 because of their distinct electronic structure (bandgap and redox potential). Because of photocorrosion, delayed electron/hole separation, and low charge carrier transfer kinetics, single metal sulphide photocatalysts are still far from ideal in terms of photostability, stability, and catalytic performance.160 Therefore, one way to address these issues is employing MOFs as precursors to create functional materials with complicated architectures and tailored components (porous structure, single shell hollow structure, and porous structure).161

Yunhong Pi et al.162 reported a MWCNT@MOF-derived In2S3 hollow tube photocatalyst. In2S3 produced from 0.3% MWCNT@MOF showed 100% degradation efficiency in TC degradation induced by visible light. When compared to pure MOF-derived In2S3 and conventional In2S3 photocatalyst, it had an apparent reaction rate constant that was three to five times greater. Pi uses multiwalled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) as electron acceptors to improve the photoreduction capabilities of NH2-MIL-68(In) platforms. The rod-shaped MIL-68(In) can be employed as a precursor for further sulfation in addition to serving as a platform for MWCNT encapsulation. In efforts to isolate the active species, Pi discovered that h+ and O2 were the primary active species in the photodegradation of TC.

According to PL spectroscopy, the electron lifetime in the composite was extended due to a reduction in electron–hole pair recombination. The superoxide radical anion produced in hollow In2S3 was measured using ESR spectroscopy. The results show that the radical O2− was formed more efficiently in MWCNT@MOF-derived In2S3 systems. Pi postulated a mechanism for the accelerated photodegradation of TC on MWCNT@MOF-derived In2S3 heterojunctions based on the aforementioned experimental findings. Photoinduced electron–hole pairs are created when In2S3 is exposed to visible light and electrons are first stimulated from the valence band (VB) to the conduction band (CB). MWCNT works well as an electron acceptor to encourage the transfer of photogenerated electrons and as a photosensitizer to improve light harvesting. The findings demonstrate that the radical O2 is generated more effectively in In2S3 systems obtained from MWCNT@MOF. Based on the aforementioned experimental findings, Pi hypothesized a method for the improved photodegradation of TC on MWCNT@MOF-derived In2S3 heterojunctions. Visible light causes In2S3 electrons to be stimulated from the valence band (VB) to the conduction band (CB), resulting in photoinduced electron–hole pairs. MWCNT is an excellent photosensitizer to increase light harvesting and can also function as an electron acceptor to encourage the transfer of photogenerated electrons (Fig. 22).

Similarly, metal oxides (such as TiO2 (ref. 163 and 164) and ZnO (ref. 165)), as semiconductor materials with wide bandgap, have strong photoexcited hole ability,166 which makes them very suitable for the photocatalytic degradation of water pollutants.167 But the high recombination rate can only absorb UV and photogenerated electrons/holes and greatly limits its application in photocatalysis,168 which makes metal oxide photocatalysts driven by MOFs template also attract much attention.

4 Photocatalytic water splitting based on MOFs for hydrogen production

As one of the most important clean energy fuels,169 hydrogen, which has the highest energy density per mass,170 has the potential to effectively alleviate global energy shortages and widespread environmental pollution. The advantages of photocatalytic hydrogen production include its simplicity, speed, low cost, low pollution, and abundant feedstock. As a result, photocatalytic hydrocracking has received special attention in the research on converting the earth's abundant solar energy into environment-friendly alternative fuels.171 TiO2,172 BiVO4,173 MoS2,174 CdS,175 ZnS,176 and other traditional semiconductor catalysts have been used to promote photocatalytic H2 evolution in recent years. Most inorganic semiconductor photocatalysts are limited in their ability to be developed and used by inherent flaws such as wide bandgaps and high carrier recombination rates.144 Therefore, it is crucial to create photocatalytic materials that are more effective if hydrogen is to be produced by water splitting.

Therefore, MOFs with adjustable components and designable networks have attracted a lot attention in photocatalytic hydrogen production. MOF materials with well-aligned and adjustable pores that can be artificially engineered at the molecular level to manipulate light-harvesting metal nodes and organic linkages can be ideal platforms for photocatalytic hydrogen production applications.

Traditional inorganic photocatalysts such as TiO2 and CdS (ref. 177) still outperform MOFs in terms of photocatalytic activity. In comparison to inorganic photocatalysts (93%), MOF photocatalysts have a maximum apparent quantum efficiency (AQE) for water of 8.9%. 4.2 Semiconductor composite MOFs

Yu-Cheng Luo et al.179 describe a metal–organic cage [Pd6(RuL3)8]28+ (MOC-16) as a photochemical molecular device (PMD) component that simultaneously combines eight Ru2+ light collecting centers and six Pd2+ catalytic centers for effective and reliable H2 production.

It was possible to successfully heterodize a hybrid substance known as MOC-16@CZIF by incorporating a metal–organic framework (MOF) of ZIF-8, which was afterward changed into a carbonate matrix of Znx(MeIm)x(CO3)x(CZIF). With an order of magnitude more triple-excited electrons than the original MOC-16, this MOC@MOF-integrated photocatalyst inherits the its high efficiency and directed electron transfer in the picosecond domain. Water serves as the proton carrier to facilitate proton transfer, while the carbonate CZIF matrix gives MOC-16@CZIF water wettability and functions as a proton transfer station. The penetration of BIH's sacrificial agent into the CZIF matrix to promote conductivity as a result of its potent reducing power to induce free charge carriers, also improves electron transfer during photocatalysis (see Fig. S11†). Due to the high H2 activity produced by these synergies, the heterogeneous MOC-16@CZIF photocatalyst can maintain a turnover frequency of 0.4 H2/s, which is a 50-fold increase over the homogenous PMD.

Interest has been shown in a complex semiconductor, CdxZn1−xS, for its role in hybrid systems.180–184 As a solid solution, the bandgap of CdxZn1−xS can be adjusted more easily than that of CdS and ZnS. In this category, Cd0.2Zn0.8S showed the best photocatalytic activity. For Yun Su et al.,185 therefore, by varying the amount of UiO-66-NH2, a series of Cd0.2Zn0.8S@UiO-66-NH2 composites were created, and a hydrogen evolution rate of 5846.5 μmol g−1 h−1 was attained. The ability to transfer electrons generated on the HOMO of UiO-66-NH2 to the conduction band of Cd0.2Zn0.8S aids in the prevention of photoinduced electron–hole pair recombination (Fig. 24(a)). The results of PL spectra (Fig. 24(b)) and transient photocurrent responses (Fig. 24(c)) further confirm this view.

4.3 Nonmetallic organic dyes MOFs

The vast majority of metal organic frameworks have a bad photoactivity for the reason that they respond ineffectively to visible light.187 Doping methods, especially nonmetallic doping, therefore deserve special attention due to their efficient role in extending the optical response range.

Since 2009, g-C3N4 has been extensively employed in the hydrogen production industry.188 When compared to conventional inorganic photocatalysts such as CdS, TiO2, and WO3, g-C3N4 performs well in a variety of applications, including the degradation of pollutants and the splitting of water.189 The broad bandgap of g-C3N4 prevents it from being used in many situations, even though it partially reacts to visible light at 460 nm. As a result, g-C3N4 and MOF together are worth testing. By including g-C3N4 during the creation of ZIF-67 and then adding MoS2 nanoparticles to the surface of the g-C3N4/ZIF-67 structure, Zejin Wang et al.190 created an effective ternary photocatalyst. As a narrow-band semiconductor, MoS2 was improved further to effectively capture visible light and hasten charge separation.

When exposed to visible light for 4 h, the MoS2 photocatalyst modified with MOFs (ZIF-67) and g-C3N4 catalyst demonstrated a high hydrogen output of about 321 mol, which was about 30 times greater than that of pure g-C3N4 photocatalyst. Because MoS2 has been modified, the electrochemical characterization demonstrates that the g-C3N4/MOF/MoS2 photocatalyst interface has an obviously higher electron transfer efficiency than g-C3N4/MOF and g-C3N4. Under visible light irradiation, the ternary composite photocatalyst demonstrated high hydrogen generation of about 321 mol in 4 h, which is about 30 times more than that of pure g-C3N4. The electron transfer efficiency of the g-C3N4/MOF/MoS2 photocatalyst interface is greatly increased, according to electrochemical characterization. Because the heterojunction structure created by MoS2 loading makes it easier for the photogenerated electrons to move from EY− to the composite photocatalyst, the photogenerated electron–hole pairs are prevented from recombining. As a result, photocatalytic water splitting is more effective.

The interfacial effects between the catalysts and the construction of chemical bonds are very effective in facilitating photogenerated electron transfer. The S–C bond between CoS and g-C3N4 was successfully created by Zhaobo Fan et al.,191 utilizing a novel form of hollow cubic CoS synthesized from Co-ZIF-9. Photoelectrons may be swiftly transported to the CoS hydrogen evolutionary active site thanks to the S–C bond's role as an electron transport bridge (see Fig. S12†). In addition, data from time-resolved photoluminescence spectroscopy and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy demonstrate that the S–C bond serves as a bridge to quickly transfer photogenerated carriers in the composite, leading to effective hydrogen evolution. The hydrogen production was 9545 μmol g−1 after just 5 h, which was 53 and 12 times more than the yields of g-C3N4 and ZIF-9, respectively. The intrinsic mechanism of photoelectron transport across the S–C bond may be verified by DFT simulations.

When exposed to visible light, the EY molecules linked to the photocatalyst are initially photoexcited into a single excited state, EY1*, and subsequently undergo gap transfer to transform into a triple excited state, EY3*. EY3* will become EY− as a result of TEOA. The electrons carried by EY− will be transported to the CB of S-g-C3N4 and CoS as a result of the reduction of H2O and H2 evolution processes, after which EY will revert to the ground state. In addition, the VB, CB, and bandgap of S-g-C3N4 are determined to be 1.61 eV, −0.95 eV, and 2.56 eV, respectively, using the Mott–Schottky and Kubelka–Munk techniques. Furthermore, when exposed to visible light, S-g-C3N4 is excited, resulting in photogenerated electron–hole pairs. Electrons from the light created will have enough energy to ignite CB. The bridge effect of the S–C bond for the hydrogen evolution process transfers the electrons on the CB of S-g-C3N4 and the electrons transferred from EY to S-g-C3N4 to CoS. The open hollow cube CoS, to which the S-g-C3N4 nanosheet is linked, promotes electron transport in space. Multiple refractions and reflections of light will be caused by the light in the black hollow cube of CoS, allowing for the full use of light.

Peramaiah Karthik et al.192 took advantage of the strong π–π interaction brought by combining rGO and MOF to develop a MOF@rGO material with a highly enhanced photocatalytic activity, which could solve the problem of the very fast photocatalytic electron–hole recombination of MOF materials. Graphene's interaction with organic molecules causes strong interference and changes its electronic properties.

The strong π–π interaction between MOF and rGO, as opposed to the reduction in charge-carrier mobility brought on by the covalent functionalization of organic molecules on graphene, is what effectively separates electron–hole pairs. Through π-mediated charge carrier separation, MOF@rGO achieves a large apparent quantum efficiency of 0.66%, which is 9.1 times better than the original MOF's photocatalytic hydrogen generation activity.

4.4 MOF-derived photocatalyst for nanocomposites

The MOF-derived nanomaterials may retain the structural characteristics of the MOF matrix thanks to the characteristics of MOFs, such as their ordered 3D structure, vast surface area, high porosity, and uniformly scattered metal junctions, which endow them with exceptional catalytic activity. 5 Reduction of CO2 for the synthesis of renewable energy by photocatalysts based on MOFs

When fossil fuels are used, greenhouse gases such as CO2 that have harmful impacts on the environment are released in excess into the atmosphere.196 For academics throughout the world, the handling of CO2 has grown to be a top priority. Economically advantageous and ecologically preferable, CO2 may be used as a C1 building block in catalytic conversion to create high-value compounds.197 The standard potential for the conversion of one carbon dioxide atom into another is −1.90 V.198 The many classical reduction products of CO2 are kinetically unfavorable to be generated.199

Metal organic frameworks can provide or be endowed with clear isolation sites to immobilize catalytically active species. In addition, for the synthesis of renewable energy from photocatalytic reduction CO2 synthesis, MOFs can act as a platform to allow energy and electron transfer activities amongst the many species supported in MOFs.

5.1 Design of traditional MOFs

The use of traditional MOFs in photocatalysis is constrained by their poor charge conductivity and low CO2 consumption. However, from the perspective of the customization of MOF materials and the strong CO2 capture ability brought by high porosity, it is completely possible to realize the utilization of visible light by MOFs through the wise selection of metal ions and organic ligands with high light absorption and utilization abilities.200 Hai-Qun Xu et al.201 prepared PCN-222 with tetrakis(4-carboxyphenyl)porphyrin (H2TCPP) as the organic composition, sacrificed in TEOA. The HCOO− selectivity is about 100% in the visible range. With a high specific surface area (1728 m2 g−1), PCN-222 has 23.6 kJ mol−1 CO2 adsorption heat and can be easily regenerated in a low energy manner. The high absorption of CO2 resulted in the enrichment of CO2 molecules near the Zr6 metal center, which also improved the photocatalytic performance. Two electron trapping processes (τ1 is 47% and τ2 is 53%) are observed in the picosecond domain of the transient TA spectrum, reflecting the existence of electron trap states, which are energetically located in the bandgap of PSN-222 and effectively inhibit the harmful and radiative electron–hole recombination.

Yue Xie et al.202 reported a MOFs photocatalyst that enhanced the photoreduction activity of CO2 by modifying MIL-101-Cr with alkyl ammonia to create chemical adsorption points. Due to the differing ratios of absorbed and transformed CO2, MOF photocatalysts are substantially less efficient at using CO2 than other traditional semiconductor photocatalysts.203,204 Through post-modification, Xie effectively included several alkylamines, including ethylenediamine (EN), diethylenetriamine (DETA), and triethylenetetramine (TETA), into MIL-101-Cr. In comparison to the original MOFs MIL-101-Cr, MIL-101-SO3H, DETA, and TETA-modified products, the EN modified material produced the most CO at 47.2 μmol g−1 h−1 with a selectivity of 96.5%.

NY Huang et al.205 synthesized photocatalytically active Ru@Cu-HHTP by combining a copper-based MOF (Cu-HHTP; HHTP = 2, 3, 6, 7, 10, and 11-hexahydroxytriphenylene) with a photosensitizer [Ru(phen)3]2+ (phen = 1,10-phenanthroline). The performance study of Ru@Cu-photocatalytic HHTP resulted in a 69.5 mmol g−1 h−1. CO yield and 91.3% selectivity under ambient light. The photoelectrons generated by the encapsulated photosensitizer [Ru(phen)3]2+ were efficiently transmitted to the surrounding metal sites (CuO4) of Cu-HTP during the photocatalytic process, as evidenced by the significant decrease in Ru@Cu-HHTP caused by PL lifespan. The extraordinarily high photocatalytic activity of Ru@Cu-HHTP is mostly due to the improved charge transfer efficiency attained by the assembly of photosensitizer particles.

The study of NingYu Huang et al.206 also confirmed that integrating photosensitizer molecules into MOFs is an excellent choice to enhance its photocatalytic activity. Huang combined Ru dipyridine, a classical photosensitive unit, with Co imidazo, a unit with high CO2 reduction activity, and 1H-imidazo phenanthroline ligand by the heat solution method to obtain MAF-34-CoRu. In photocatalytic experiments, MAF-34-CoRu achieved CO selectivity of about 100% without the need for additional sacrificial agents and photosensitizers. The PL characterization results verified that the photosensitive unit and the catalytic unit had a good synergistic effect on the MOF.

Mahmoud Elcheikh Mahmoud et al.207 selected the photosensitive double (4′-(4-carboxyphenyl)-tripyridine)ruthenium(II) (Ru(cptpy)2) pillar to be included in Zr-MOF. The resulting MOF (AUBM-4) showed strong HCOOH reducing activity (HCOO−: 366 μmol g−1 h−1) under visible light with TEOA as a sacrificial agent. After the photoexcitation of Ru(cptpy)2, TEOA reduced the Ru center. Carbon dioxide is centrally coordinated with Zr and reduced by a ligand anion.

Zhi-Hao Yan et al.208 constructed an Eu–Ru (phen)3-MOF with an Eu(III)2 cluster as the connection node and photosensitive H3L ligand (Ru(phen)3-derived) as the connection node. In the performance evaluation of photocatalysis, CO2 was selectively reduced to formate (321.9 HCOO−) (μmol h−1 mmol MOF−1). The photoexcitation of metal ligands with photosensitivity and strong light absorption ability initiates the electron injection node, and EPR studies have shown that {Eu(III)}2 clusters become photocatalytic active centers after receiving photoexcited electrons. The results of TA measurements confirm the electron transfer kinetics––the TA spectrum of the H3L ligand shows a negative GSB signal (ground state bleaching) at 440 and 490 nm, overlapping with the excited state absorption (ESA) signal term at 440 to 700 nm––which is consistent with the transfer path of photoexcitation-induced electrons from the H3L ligand to the Eu metal center.

A CO2 molecule can be absorbed by the exposed Eu(III) center across the Eu–O junction. At the active Eu1 ion location, its estimated adsorption energy is −0.55 eV. After the photoinduced electron injection reduces Eu(III) to Eu(II), the electrons can be transferred to CO2 adsorbed at the active site. The CO2 molecules get 0.025 electrons from the successfully activated Eu(II)2, whereas the Eu(II)1 and Eu(II)2 ions lose 0.0073 and 0.004 electrons, respectively, during this process (Fig. 28).

5.2 Nanometal-loaded MOFs

Particle size adjustment for photocatalytic activity has been recognized to be applicable to precious metals such as Pt. The LSPR effect excited by noble metal nanoparticles under light irradiation can greatly enhance light utilization in photocatalysis. Therefore, many researchers also have great interest in MOFs materials composed of nanometal particles and regulate the photocatalytic activity of CO2 by adjusting the type, size, and compound position of nanometal particles.

By adding Ag nanoparticles with sizes ranging from 80 nm to 800 nm to the MOF skeleton, Fan Guo et al.210 created the MOF MIL-101(Cr)–Ag nanoparticle mixture. When the size of MIL-101(Cr)–Ag was reduced to 80 nm, the highest carbon dioxide photocatalytic reduction activity was attained, and the productivity of CO was 808.2 μmol g−1 h−1, and the productivity of CH4 was 427.5 μmol g−1 h−1. The noble metal nanoparticles significantly improved the photocatalytic utilization of light, leading to the creation of one of the most potent combined catalysts. The high catalytic activity of the catalyst can also be attributed to the high-density cells at the corners and edges of the small-sized mixed catalyst, which are beneficial for electron transport in the photocatalytic carbon dioxide reduction process.

A variety of MOF photocatalysts of various sizes were produced by Ningyu Huang et al.206 by adding Ag nanoparticles to the MOF frameworks. MIL-101(Cr)–Ag is visible between 80 and 800 nm. In the performance evaluation of photocatalysis, 80 nm MIL-101(Cr) demonstrated the greatest activity with a productivity of 808.2 mol g−1 h−1 for CO. 427.5 mol g−1 CH4 was produced. Ag nanoparticles significantly enhanced photocatalyst's use of light, and the resulting hybrid catalysts were very effective. In addition, the high cell density at the catalyst's edges significantly aided in the electron transport during the photocatalytic reduction of CO2.

To improve the photocatalytic effect utilizing the LSPR effect, Kyung Min Choi et al.211 presented a composite photocatalyst coated with metal nanoparticles using the MOF material. The covalent bond between the zirconium MOF, IOI-67 (Ren-MOFs), and the photocatalyst ReI(CO)3(BPYDC)Cl (BPYDC = 2,2′-dipyridine-5,5′-dimethylate) (ReTC), which converts carbon dioxide to carbon dioxide and avoids dimerization and inactivation. 5.3 MOFs modified by semiconductors and cocatalysts

The loading of nanometal particles is not the only way to modify MOFs materials: compositing with a catalytic active semiconductor or combination with a cocatalyst are options. Lu Ye et al.213 developed an ultrathin two-dimensional Zn porphyrin MOF(5,10,15,20-tetrakis(4-carboxyphenyl)porphyrin; TCPP nanosheet) as photosensitizer, [Co2(OH)L](ClO4)3 or ZIF-67 as cocatalyst to participate in the photocatalytic reduction of carbon dioxide. The study found that as the photosensitizer zinc-nano-MOF piece shows better than zinc-MOF body stronger charge transfer ability of the CO2 adsorption capacity (1103.8 cm3 g−1) and longer life electronic light-hole for life. The PL measurement results also confirmed the above results.

Similar to the classical semiconductor TiO2, Zn2GeO4 with wide bandgap has also been widely used in the photocatalytic reduction of CO2.214 In particular, Zn2GeO4 nanorods have a high degree of crystallinity and a more scattered energy structure, which leads to significant photoinduced electron mobility in the conduction band and prevents the majority of photoinduced electron–hole pair recombination.215 According to Hui Zhao et al.,216 Mg-MOF-74 was thoroughly coated by the hydrothermal synthesis of Zn2GeO4 nanorods when the Zn2GeO4/Mg-MOF-74 composite was synthesized. Alkyl ammonium adsorbed on Zn2GeO4 nanorods interacts favorably with H4DOBDC of Mg-MOF-74.

The intimate association between Zn2GeO4 and Mg-MOF-74 seen in infrared spectroscopy and TEM images was ascribed to the adsorption of tetramethylammonium hydroxide (TMAOH) on Zn2GeO4 and the interaction of this compound with the 2,5-dioxy-1,4-benzoate ester (H4DOBDC) of Mg-MOF-74. The binding of Mg-MOF-74 with nanorods increased the adsorption of carbon dioxide, as demonstrated by the carbon dioxide adsorption isotherm. The hydrothermal Zn2GeO4/Mg-MOF-74 catalyst displayed higher performance in converting carbon dioxide to carbon monoxide alone using water as the sacrificial agent as compared to a single Zn2GeO4or Zn2GeO4/Mg-MOF-74 physical combination. This is due to its increased carbon dioxide adsorption capacity and lower recombination of photogenic electron pore pairs. The enhanced charge transfer capabilities of the Zn2GeO4/Mg-MOF-74 catalyst are further shown by PL and EIS characterizations.

The use of Mg-MOF-74 increased the photocatalytic activity of Zn2GeO4 significantly. Zhao proposed numerous explanations for this.

(a) It enhances electron transfer from Zn2GeO4 to itself, hence preventing photoelectron–hole recombination.

(b) At low pressure, it has the maximum CO2 adsorption capacity.

(c) The presence of a significant number of open basic metal sites (Mg2+) facilitates CO2 activation.

(d) It can efficiently broaden the range of visible light that it can absorb. The photocatalytic reduction of CO2 may be accomplished by successfully transferring photoelectrons from Zn2GeO4 to the open basic metal sites (Mg2+) of Mg-MOF-74 under the synergistic action of Zn2GeO4 and Mg-MOF-74. 6 Research on photocatalytic mechanism of MOFs

Because of their large specific surface area, regular pore diameters, plentiful catalytic sites, and tunable metal frame architectures, MOFs have tremendous promise for photocatalytic water pollutant degradation, photocatalytic water splitting for hydrogen production, and photocatalytic CO2 reduction.223 Therefore, there are two basic theories of MOFs in the field of photocatalysis: one is MOFs that show good semiconductor properties and follow the semiconductor theory i.e., when MOFs is irradiated, the electrons produced by light leap from VB to CB and produce holes (h+) in VB. In this case, the photogenerated carrier e− (i.e., the photogenerated electron) has strong reducing property, the hole h+ has strong oxidizing property, and the two participate in redox reaction before recombination.

Another theory is the HOMO–LUMO theory. When a MOF is illuminated, its organic ligands absorb light, which excites them to produce electrons. These electrons are then transported from the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) and finally to the surface of the metal-oxide cluster, which is LMCT. Electrons are stimulated from the ligand donor orbital to the metal acceptor orbital when the metal center is lowered to a lower state, enabling them to absorb photons with reduced energy. In MLCT, the organic ligands of MOFs provide an empty, stable orbital when the metal core is oxidized to a higher valence state. As a result of electrons being excited from the local orbitals of the occupied metal to the local orbitals of the unoccupied ligand, long-wavelength light is absorbed.17

However, unlike TiO2 and other traditional semiconductors,224,225 there have been extensive and mature researches on the photocatalytic mechanism. As the “future star” in the field of photocatalytic application, the relevant researches on MOF materials not only started late but also brought about many design and modification strategies due to the complex quantity and variety of MOFs and the adjustable and controllable structure. It also shows that the further research of MOFs in related fields is disorganized. Therefore, in this chapter, we try to explain the specific mechanisms of MOFs in different fields of photocatalytic degradation of water pollutants, photocatalytic water splitting, and photocatalytic CO2 reduction from a further perspective in a more comprehensive and in-depth way and look forward to more outstanding contributions from the later generations in related fields.

Aiwen Wang et al.163 calcined MOFs to create an N-doped ZnO carbon framework, then grew layered Bi2MoO6 nanosheets on its surface to create S-type heterojunctions with a photodegradation efficiency ten times that of ZIF-8's sulfamethoxazole (SMX).

N–ZnO/C@BiM heterojunction should be a traditional type II heterojunction based on the band position as N–ZnO/C and Bi2MoO6 are both N-type semiconductors. The h+ migrates from the VBM of N–ZnO/C to the VBM of Bi2MoO6, and the e− that was isolated from the Bi2MoO6 CBM migrates to the CNM of N–ZnO/C. Through DFT calculations, Wang demonstrated the heterojunction structure's charge transport channel. The research revealed that in comparison to the Bi2MoO6 (001) surface, which has a work function of 5.98 eV, the N–ZnO/C (001) surface is expected to have a work function of 6.40 eV. This discrepancy suggests that charge transfer occurs at the interface between N–ZnO/C and Bi2MoO6.

The band edges of N–ZnO/C are curved downward owing to electron accumulation,228 whereas the band edges of Bi2MoO6 are bent upward due to electron loss. The surface of Bi2MoO6 is positively charged at the contact, whereas N–ZnO/surface C's is negatively charged. A built-in electric field will be created at the interface as a result of this charge transfer. Wang thus proposed a charge transfer pathway for a MOF-driven N–ZnO/C@BiM photocatalyst that uses light to stimulate N–ZnO/C and e− of Bi2MoO6 from VBM to CBM. The banded edges and internal electric field bending not only cause h+ recombination of e− and Bi2MoO6 stimulated by N–ZnO/C, but they also inhibit e− and h+ recombination in N–ZnO/C and Bi2MoO6. While maintaining the CB of Bi2MoO6 and usable e− and h+ in the VBM of N–ZnO/C; this S-type heterojunction mechanism consumes some ineffective electrons in the CBM of N–ZnO/C and h+ in the VBM of Bi2MoO6. As a result, the extraordinarily high redox capacity of N–ZnO/C@BiM is provided via this charge carrier transfer mechanism.

The surface composition and chemical state of Co3O4 were significantly altered by the addition of P. The transport and separation of charge carriers in Co3O4 are significantly enhanced by the P(δ−)–CO(δ+) link. In addition, the creation of proton adsorption sites and the development of H2 are both aided by the P(δ−)–CO(δ+) link. EY functions as an electron transporter during the process by which dye molecules (EY) are activated by visible light and finally fall from the HOMO level to the LUMO level. Under the influence of visible light, EY begins to become EY1* before going through the system to become the more stable EY3*. Then, EY3* removes the electrons from TEOA to produce the highly reactive EY free radical. The photocatalyst's conduction band receives the electrons last. Then, the TEOA donates electrons to EY3*, forming a free radical called EY−. Excellent at reducing substances, EY− ultimately sends electrons to photocatalyst CB to take part in HER. The H2O molecule is then adsorbed onto the transition state created by the lone pair electron in the P 3p orbital adsorbing a proton from the H2O molecule at this stage. The next action is to continue. A protonated transition state is created when electrons from the water molecule's O–H bond are transferred to the oxygen atom. A hydroxyl anion is also produced at the same time. In the end, when exposed to visible light, TEOA injected electrons into Co3O4 to consume photogenic holes, and EY abundantly supplied electrons for the reaction site. The two protons are coupled to produce an H2 molecule at the same time that a homogeneous reaction takes place at the P–H covalent connection. The catalyst can then cycle back and forth through the hydrogen evolutionary process, returning to its starting condition.

6.3 Mechanisms of photocatalytic reduction of CO2 based on MOFs

The multielectron step is generally more advantageous than single-electron reduction in reducing carbon dioxide to fuel precursors, such as CO, because the resulting molecules are more thermodynamically stable.

For example, carbon dioxide reduction often produces carbon monoxide (CO), formic acid (HCO2H), formaldehyde (HCHO), methanol (CH3OH), and CH4 as a result of the reduction of carbon dioxide (Fig. 40). Depending on the circumstances of the experiment, experiments have revealed that up to three of these derivatives can be created concurrently and in varying amounts. It is commonly known that none of the five derivatives, at least not in measurable amounts, are created simultaneously. These findings demonstrate how important experimental circumstances, thermodynamic and kinetic factors, and the overall reaction's outcome are. Protons and electrons are used in the basic process of carbon dioxide reduction, and eight protons and eight electrons are needed to completely convert carbon dioxide to CH4.233

A spongy nickel-MOF photocatalyst was designed and synthesized by Kaiyang Niu et al.234 using a photochemical process. The catalyst has a highly concentrated crystalline network structure. With a productivity of roughly 1.6 × 104 μmol g−1 h−1, it is quite active in converting carbon dioxide to carbon monoxide. The process yields approximately 100% selective CO synthesis with no detectable H2 generation.

The majority of the time, stiff ligands, most often aromatic carboxylic acid molecules such as terephthalic acid, are used to coordinate transition metal ions or clusters in MOFs to create their highly ordered crystal structures (TPA). Niu, taking into account that TEG was equivalent to TPA in terms of molecular length, laser-replaced a partly rigid ligand (e.g., TPA) in a traditional MOF with a soft molecule (e.g., TEG). The substitution of TPA linkers with TEG molecules, which lack the carboxyl groups required for a perfect frame structure, may prevent the formation of highly ordered MOF crystals, leading to disordered and deficient metal–organic combinations that are unable to successfully fix carbon dioxide. To do this, Niu used laser-induced solution processes to construct a model of a metal–organic carbon dioxide reduction catalyst that uses the Ni2+ ion as the active metal center, TPA as the stiff linker, TEG as the soft linker, and dimethylformamide (DMF) as the solvent. The synthetic catalyst has a crystal network structure with severe defects and the designation Ni (TPA or TEG). By creating significant defects as a result of mismatches between the soft Ni-TEG units and the hard Ni-TPA units, which are necessary for the spongy Ni (TPA/TEG) network structure, the formation of the Ni-TEG building units by laser light prevented the disordered, spongy Ni (TPA/TEG) from forming.

Given that noble metals have the capacity to decrease carbon dioxide, Niu discovered that when the MOF material was further doped with noble metal nanocrystals, specifically Rh and Ag, formic acid (HCOOH) with a concentration of 29.2 μM and acetic acid (CH3COOH) with a concentration of 72.5 μM were also observed. With the decoration of Rh and Ag nanocrystals, the amount of CO decreased significantly while the amount of liquid products increased significantly. On the Ni (NPA/TEG) catalyst adorned with RH or Ag, formic acid (HCOOH) with a concentration of 313.5 μM and CH3COOH with a concentration of 195.6 μM, respectively, were produced. As a Brønsted base that rapidly interacts with H2O to make CO (CO2− + H2O + e− → CO + 2OH−), Niu suggested creating a basic intermediate in the initial reaction step to facilitate the selective synthesis of CO from CO2. In our proton-deficient photocatalytic reaction medium (pH 8), water (a proton donor) can produce another nonbasic intermediate, H–(H2O + e− → H− + OH−),235 prior to H2 evolution (H2O + e− → H− + OH−). The proton transfer and H2 generation may be limited by the sponge-like Ni (TPA/TEG) catalyst because the Ni-TPA coordination unit does not allow for H binding at the active site. In addition, the adaptable Ni-TEG units, which are structurally well resistant to the water environment, allow for a disordered spongiform network structure in which open and defective structures provide more Ni2+ active sites to capture and stabilize CO2−-derived intermediates, resulting in efficient CO production.

Subsequently, a proton-coupled multielectron reaction procedure can then be used to further reduce the generated CO to liquid fuel. In an electrolyte with a pH of ∼8, HCOOH, CH3COOH, and CH3CH2OH are formed, respectively, by a step of one, four, and eight electrons connected to protons. The dehydroxylation of the generated CHO may be a crucial rate-limiting step in the production of CH3CH2OH. To compute and compare the catalytic performance of 2D cobalt–porphyrin organic frameworks, CoO clusters, ZnO clusters, and ZrO clusters as CO2-reduction photocatalysts, Cong Wang et al.236 employed density functional theory (DFT) for CoP, Co-PMOF, Zn-PMOF, and Zr-PMOF, respectively. According to the computed partial state densities (PDOS) of CoP, Co-PMOF, Zn-PMOF, and Zr-PMOF monolayers, only the bandgap of Co-PMOF is significantly reduced by the inclusion of the CoO cluster. In the CB and VB of Co-PMOF monolayers and other states close to Fermi level, the Co 3d state dominates. In contrast, the bands close to the Fermi level are slightly affected by the 3D electrons in the ZnO and ZrO-clusters of n-PMOF and Zr-PMOF monolayers. At the VB and CB, the primary O 2p electrons are similarly dispersed across a large area. Because of this, the energies of CB and VB and the peaks of Zn-PMOF and Zr-PMOF are comparable to those of CoP.

Wang's idea was supported by the Gibbs free energy calculations: the reduction of carbon dioxide starts with the production of *C–Co(I) bonds at the active site and proceeds to hydrogenation to create carboxyl (*COOH) intermediates or formate (*OCHO) intermediates. In the Gibbs free energy diagram, the formation of *COOH on these monolayers has internal heats of 0.24, 0.27, 0.28, and 0.26 eV, respectively, while the formation of *OCHO has internal heats of 0.92, 0.84, 0.94, and 0.91 eV, respectively, and is significantly more challenging than the formation of *COOH. Therefore, it is not necessary to take into account the steps that come after the OCHO intermediate. By interacting with protons and transferring electrons, COOH intermediates can continue to hydrogenate and create CO intermediates. Following this, there are two potential intermediates that can result from the hydrogenation of CO, with the creation of CHO being the more prevalent. Similar to *CHO, *CH2O, or *CHOH intermediates can be created by further reducing *CHO. Due to a lower barrier than the creation of *CHOH, the *CH2O intermediate can be hydrogenated to make *CH2OH or *OCH3 intermediates, which can then be further hydrogenated to produce *CH2 or CH3OH. On these catalysts, the synthesis of CH3OH is unquestionably more advantageous than the creation of CH2. In addition, OCH3 intermediates will undergo more hydrogenation to produce CH3OH. Additional calculations revealed that CH3OH might be further oxidized to produce CH4.

Under the catalysis of CoP, Co-PMOF, Zn-, and Zr-PMOF monolayers, the optimal reduction path from CO2 to CH4 is the same.

CO2 → *COOH → *CO → *CHO → *CH2O → *CH2OH → CH3OH → *CH3 → CH4.

The important intermediate species in this process that chooses the rate step is *CHO. In contrast to the other three monolayers, the core cobalt PDOS in the Co-PMOF monolayer has a D-band center that is further from the Fermi level, according to the PDOS for the *CHO intermediates. This shows that d electrons are distributed to *CHO in the Co-PMOF monolayer and exhibit clear bonding properties that favor electron conversion over electron transport. The D-band center of the central cobalt PDOS is extremely near the Fermi level in the other three monolayers, indicating that these d electrons are more likely to be free d electrons (Fig. 42).

7 Conclusion and perspective

7.1 Conclusion

Catalysis plays a significant role in both industrial and laboratory chemical transformations, and the sunlight-driven photocatalysis technology is key to solving energy crises and environmental problems. MOFs materials have piqued the interest of many researchers due to their enormous potential in this sector. MOFs have a well-defined, tunable, and controllable structure with specific pores, which offer an advantage over traditional heterogeneous catalysts, as well as the ability to selectively introduce substrates.

In the design of target MOFs materials, the composition of MOFs, bandgap engineering, application of photosensitized molecules, π–π stacking, and activation of porous structures are given priority. The IRMOFs series, ZIF series, MILs series, and PCNs series are among the traditional MOFs that are the focus of the majority of present research on MOF-based photocatalysts. However, most MOFs lack redox activity, making them almost impossible to drive solar energy. Therefore, several researchers are also focused on MOF modification, including the loading of semiconductors or cocatalysts, the application of LSPR effect, the construction of Z-type heterojunctions, the introduction of amine group functionalization, and the regulation of mixed linked body MOFs.

When using MOFs as heterogeneous photocatalysts, the active sites, such as metal nodes, organic ligands, and guest species, should be given priority as they are directly connected to photocatalytic activity. In the design of MOF materials, functional metal nodes or organic ligands can be chosen as active sites since their photocatalytic ability can be controlled by adjusting the active sites. Alternatively, active sites can be added to MOFs through post-modification techniques for functional and structural design and adjustment.

With the continuous development of heterogeneous photocatalysts based on MOFs, researchers have successfully applied more and more MOFs in photocatalytic degradation, photocatalytic water splitting, and photocatalytic reduction of CO2 based on MOF-based design and modification strategies. However, many MOFs have poor hydrothermal stability, making them highly dependent on electron sacrificial agents for photocatalytic applications and difficult to perform photothermal coupling, unlike many traditional semiconductor materials.

Therefore, utilizing MOFs as starting materials for synthetic catalytic materials may provide a solution to the aforementioned issues. The unique structure of MOFs obtained through thermal decomposition allows the synthesized heterogeneous catalysts to retain the unique controllable porosity and structural morphology of MOFs, while also making the introduction of photothermal coupling possible. However, as the latest research results in this field, the morphological changes of heterogeneous photocatalysts induced by MOFs during the pyrolysis process have been rarely reported and are still highly uncertain.

Despite their potential, MOFs still face numerous challenges in their use for optical catalysis. For instance, MOFs are often perceived as semiconductors, but their inorganic–organic alternating infinite network structure means that they lack obvious semiconductor characteristics. In addition, MOF-based photocatalysts, which contain many organic ligands, produce a significant amount of excitons and a clear exciton energy transfer mechanism. As such, research on the exciton effect in MOFs photocatalysis requires further attention.

Another issue is that the majority of the current mainstream photocatalytic MOFs have a relatively fixed structural composition, with most metal centers being Zr and Ti, and most organic ligands being photosensitive porphyrin macromolecules. When screening novel MOFs photocatalyst materials, it is crucial to explore new metal sites or organic ligands. Furthermore, the metal core of MOFs, loaded nanoparticles, and semiconductors work together to catalyze the reaction in heterogeneous photocatalysis based on MOFs. Research into the modification technique that involves linking functional groups and ligands, combining metal nodes, and creating additional synergies should be continued.

These challenges call for more comprehensive and in-depth discussions on the photocatalytic mechanism of MOFs. Due to the wide variety of MOFs and their adjustable and controllable structure, many researchers are uncertain about the design and modification strategies. Therefore, in this review, we aim to provide a more detailed explanation of the unique mechanisms of MOFs in photocatalytic water pollution degradation, photocatalytic water separation, and photocatalytic CO2 reduction. We also examine the photocatalytic mechanism of MOF-based heterogeneous photocatalysts from various angles. The relationship between different photocatalytic mechanisms proposed by different techniques, such as in situ characterization and density functional calculation, is discussed systematically, and we expect future research to make further outstanding contributions in related fields.

7.2 Perspective

The structure–activity link for MOF-based photocatalysts is challenging to establish due to their complex and diverse structures. Currently, research on MOFs is focused on synthesizing more intricate structures with enhanced functions and improved stability, with performance studies in specific application areas receiving increasing attention. For instance, the synthesis of MOFs for CO2 reduction requires not only high yield, but also higher product types and selectivity. Methane, methanol, and formic acid are generally preferred over carbon monoxide in research, and the pursuit of long carbon chain compounds has imposed new requirements on MOFs. The performance of MOFs with identical crystal structures but differing connecting nodes varies. Despite studying numerous MOF types, achieving consistent pore diameters and organized mesoporous pores in the framework remains challenging. MOF performance research has gained prominence as MOF synthesis and application have become inseparable. MOFs used in the photocatalytic degradation of water contaminants, photocatalytic water splitting, and photocatalytic CO2 reduction have become increasingly specialized and focused on the heterogeneous photocatalysis field.

Despite the significant progress in the design, creation, and use of heterogeneous photocatalysts based on MOFs, there are still several challenges that need to be addressed. These challenges include

(1) Preserving the fundamental structure of MOF-based photocatalysts in semi-industrial production processes while ensuring high yield. Furthermore, there is a need to explore new preparation technologies that do not require the use of excessive energy or chemicals.

(2) Reducing the dependence of MOF-based photocatalysts on electron sacrificial agents as achieving high photocatalytic activity under more general conditions remains a challenge.

(3) Improving the homogeneity and monodispersity of nano-MOF-based photocatalysts to ensure better catalytic activity and expanding the use of monoatomic catalysts on MOFs.

(4) Directly catalyzing CO2 synthesis of C2+ products with heterogeneous photocatalysts based on MOFs to promote sustainable utilization of resources.

(5) Enhancing the hydrothermal stability of MOF-based photocatalysts to facilitate their widespread practical use. This can be achieved through the preparation of nanocomposite catalysts induced by MOFs as a precursor sacrificial agent.

(6) Incorporating defects into heterogeneous photocatalysts based on MOFs and regulating their concentration and chemical characteristics.

(7) Conducting more specific and in-depth quantitative research to determine the specific photocatalytic mechanism of heterogeneous photocatalysts based on MOFs. This includes utilizing in situ characterization techniques such as in situ Raman, in situ XAFS, in situ FT-IR, and density functional theory (DFT) computation.
