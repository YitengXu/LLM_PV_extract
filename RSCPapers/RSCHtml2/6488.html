Abstract

Hybrid lead-halide perovskite solar cells (PSCs) are promising alternatives to silicon-based cells due to their high photovoltaic performance and low cost. We report herein fully printable perovskite solar cells with a mesoporous TiO2/Al2O3/C architecture in combination with mixed-halide MAPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskites. A maximum conversion efficiency of 13.49% can be achieved with an increased open circuit voltage of 1.01 V, which is higher than the MAPbI3-based devices. A reduced anomalous hysteresis in the Jâ€“V curve measurement has been demonstrated in perovskite solar cells based on MAPbI2.4Br0.6 perovskite, which is directly linked to the characteristically slow kinetics measured through electrochemical impedance spectroscopy.

Introduction

Hybrid lead-halide perovskite solar cells (PSCs) are presently under intense investigation owing to their high efficiency with easy processing.1â€“4 Over the last few years, perovskite solar cells have been reported to achieve a remarkably high efficiency of 20.1%.5 The outstanding performance of PSCs has been attributed to the unique properties of perovskite materials MAPbX3 (MA = CH3NH3; X = I, Br, or Cl), displaying excellent photovoltaic (PV) properties in terms of intense light absorption, excellent ambipolar charge mobility, small exciton binding energy, and apparent tolerance to defects.6â€“12 However, the state-of-the-art perovskite solar cells require noble metals and organic hole transport materials (HTM) for proper operation, which are expensive and require high vacuum instruments. Significant cost reductions are possible by replacing the noble metal counter electrode (CE) with a printable carbon electrode to fabricate fully printable perovskite solar cells.

The printable mesoscopic PSC using an inorganic oxide scaffold with a carbon CE can be constructed without the HTM deposition process.13â€“17 In this device, the perovskite film on top of the mesoporous TiO2 acts as both a light harvester and HTM simultaneously. Han et al. reported a fully printable perovskite device architecture using a double layer of mesoporous TiO2 and ZrO2 as a scaffold and porous carbon film as the CE, achieving a PCE of 12.8%.13 This work highlights a low cost, easy preparation and screen printable alternative electrode to replace noble metal electrodes. However, the micrometer-thick ZrO2 layer (1â€“2 Î¼m) between the TiO2 layer and the carbon layer challenges the charge transportation and hinders further improvement of the device performance.15 For instance, holes that are generated have to travel over a relatively long pathway in such a structure before reaching the carbon CE. This leads to a misbalance between the effective light harvesting/charge transportation and effective isolation of the carbon conductive material from the TiO2 electrode in PSC devices. These important characteristics, which are related to the device architecture and perovskite preparation, should be carefully controlled to optimize the PCE. Very recently, our group demonstrated an âˆ¼15% efficient fully printable PSC based on a quadruple-layer architecture of mesoporous TiO2/Al2O3/NiO/C.18 This work emphasizes the importance of charge extraction in perovskite solar cells, thus improving the devicesâ€™ open-circuit voltage (VOC) and output power ability.

The composition of the perovskite infiltrated in the mesoporous structure may also be important, which will extremely affect the PCE as well as other intrinsic charge responses in the device.19â€“22 Specifically, a mixture of multiple halogen elements in hybrid perovskites can bring several advantages, such as a tuned band gap, enhanced stability, and improved carrier transport.23â€“26 These benefits are important for efficient PSCs either with the mesoscopic or planar configuration. Seok et al. demonstrated an enhanced device performance with improved charge transport properties through the chemical management of MAPbI3âˆ’xBrx deposited in mesoporous TiO2 films.21 The stability of PSC under an ambient atmosphere was also greatly improved by the substitution of 10â€“15 mol% Br for I ions in perovskites.27 Furthermore, the addition of Br in the perovskite structure was demonstrated to improve the carrier transport and lifetime of the PSC devices as reported by Mora-Sero and co-workers.28 These benefits are important for carbon-based fully printable PSCs, which typically use an architecture with a thick inorganic oxide scaffold (1â€“2 Î¼m) as well as a fabrication procedure in ambient air (not a glove box). What's more, mixed-halide MAPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskites are particularly suitable for investigating the influence of perovskite preparation on the properties of PSCs, since any composition change comes together with changes in the crystal structure and absorption onset that are promptly monitored by using XRD diffraction patterns and optical absorption spectra, respectively.29

Previously, we demonstrated a fully printable perovskite solar cell with a triple-layer of the mesoporous TiO2/Al2O3/C architecture filled with MAPbI2Br fabricated by sequential deposition of solutions of PbI2 and MABr, achieving a PCE of 11.03%.30 This work highlights a fully printable PSC based on a wide bandgap perovskite MAPbI2Br (âˆ¼1.77 eV) which achieved an impressive photovoltage of about 1.08 V. However, the devices based on MAPbI2Br perovskites leads to a much lower JSC correlated with a blue shift of the absorption spectrum, which restrains the whole PCE of the device. What's more, the physical mechanism that rules the cell performance is not yet fully understood. For instance, it is still unclear why this HTM-free PSC based on MAPbI3, generally reported as TiO2/MAPbI3 heterojunction solar cells, has low VOC and thus results in a relatively low PCE < 13.0%. In addition, the anomalous hysteresis effect during currentâ€“voltage measurements has been observed in most PSC devices with different structures.31â€“34 However, a weak hysteresis effect was demonstrated in the HTM-free PSC with a carbon CE even though there is a relatively long pathway for the charge carriers to transport in such a structure before reaching the electrode. Therefore, despite the great efforts focused on the understanding of fully printable PSCsâ€™ performance, many open questions remain to be explored.

In this work, we exploited and focused on charge transportation and recombination processes for different perovskite compositions to address the aforementioned issues. We fabricated perovskite solar cell devices based on MAPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskites through a simple and facile post-synthetic halide exchange process to modify the compositional elements of the perovskite material. We found out that by incorporating Br into perovskites infiltrated in a mesoporous TiO2/Al2O3/C architecture, a prolonged charge carrier lifetime and improved charge extraction resulted in an overall energy conversion of 13.49% under AM 1.5G illumination testing conditions. A reduced anomalous hysteresis in the Jâ€“V curve measurement has been demonstrated in the PSC based on MAPbI2.4Br0.6 perovskites. Electronic impedance spectroscopy was applied to understand the internal electrical processes in such devices.

Experimental

Material synthesis

MAI and MABr were synthesized as follows: methylamine together with hydroiodic acid or hydrobromic acid were added with a mole ratio of 1:1 into a round-bottom flask and stirred in an ice bath for 2 h. The precipitate was recovered by putting the solution on a rotavap and carefully removing the solvents at 50 Â°C. The product of methylammonium iodide (MAI) or methylammonium bromide (MABr) was washed three times with diethyl ether and dried in a vacuum drying oven.

Device fabrication

Devices were fabricated on a fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) coated glass serving as a transparent electrode and substrate, respectively. A compact layer of TiO2 was deposited on the cleaned FTO glass by spray pyrolysis deposition with di-isopropoxytitanium bis(acetyl acetonate) solution at 450 Â°C. The mesoporous TiO2 films and Al2O3 paste used for screen printing were prepared as reported previously.18 Finally, a carbon black/graphite counter electrode with the thickness of about 10 Î¼m was coated on the top of the Al2O3 layer and further sintering was carried out at 400 Â°C for 30 min. After cooling down to room temperature, the resulting films were infiltrated with PbI2 by dropping a PbI2 solution in DMF (500 mg mlâˆ’1) that was kept at 70 Â°C. After drying at 70 Â°C for 30 minutes, the films were dipped into MAI solution (10 mg mlâˆ’1) and the films changed colour from light yellow to black during the dipping process in about 10 minutes, indicating the formation of the MAPbI3 perovskite. In the halide exchange process, the prepared devices were further dipped into the MABr solution (5 mg mlâˆ’1) for âˆ¼1 minute and âˆ¼3 minutes and dried at 90 Â°C for 30 minutes to form the MAPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskite in the mesoporous TiO2/Al2O3/C device. Thus, the samples based on MAPbI3âˆ’xBrx films were obtained and the corresponding samples with a dipping time in the MABr solution (5 mg mlâˆ’1) for âˆ¼1 minute and âˆ¼3 minutes were labeled as S1 and S3, respectively. The MAPbI3 device without the halide exchange process was also prepared and labeled as S0 for comparison. To measure the absorption spectra and XRD patterns of the perovskite films in the mesoporous TiO2/Al2O3/C device, the thick carbon CE was carefully removed.

Device characterization

A xenon light source solar simulator (450 W, Oriel, model 9119) with an AM 1.5G filter (Oriel, model 91192) was used to give an irradiance of 100 mW cmâˆ’2 at the surface of the solar cells. The currentâ€“voltage characteristics of the devices under these conditions were obtained by applying an external potential bias to the devices and measuring the generated photocurrent with a Keithley model 2400 digital sourcemeter (Keithley, USA). A similar data acquisition system was used to control the incident photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE) measurement. The devices were tested using a metal mask with an aperture area of 0.16 cm2 to prevent the scattering light. Film cross sections were obtained using an FEI Magellan-400 field emission scanning electron microscope (FESEM).

Results and discussion

The MAPbI3 perovskites were first deposited by the sequential deposition as described earlier.35 For the preparation of MAPbI3âˆ’xBrx based devices, a post-synthetic halide exchange process was carried out, i.e., the prepared devices were further dipped into the MABr solution with different time periods to form the bromine-substituted perovskite.36 Halide substitution occurs rapidly when the deposited perovskite film comes into contact with the halide solution in a mesoscopic scaffold (Fig. S1â€ ). The origin of VOC in PSCs have been typically ascribed to the difference between the conduction band minimum (CBM) of the perovskite and the HOMO of the HTM, or by the potential difference between the quasi-Fermi level of the electron (Efn) at the electron extraction layer/anode interface and the quasi-Fermi level of the hole (Efp) at the hole extraction layer/cathode interface under illumination, assuming that recombination within the device can be ignored.38 Therefore, when Efn and Efp are built up within the device under light illumination, MAPbI3âˆ’xBrx might lead to a larger difference between Efn and Efp as compared to MAPbI3, in spite of using the same electron (TiO2) extraction layer, thus resulting in a higher VOC. This result is in accordance with the reports that the perovskite materials contribute to Efn and Efp within the device.30,39

Conclusions

Fully printable perovskite solar cells based on MAPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskites were fabricated through a simple and facile post-synthetic halide exchange process to modify the compositional elements of the perovskite material. Considerable changes both in the lattice structure and the electronic band gap are demonstrated by incorporating Br into perovskites infiltrated in a mesoporous TiO2/Al2O3/C architecture. The devices based on TiO2/Al2O3/C(MAPbI2.7Br0.3) exhibit a higher VOC and JSC than the parent MAPbI3, showing an impressive PCE of 13.49%. Moreover, the device based on TiO2/Al2O3/C(MAPbI2.4Br0.6) demonstrated a significantly diminished Jâ€“V hysteretic behaviour in comparison with the parent MAPbI3 based device. The anomalous hysteresis in the Jâ€“V curve measurement is linked directly to the characteristically slow kinetics measured through electrochemical impedance spectroscopy.
