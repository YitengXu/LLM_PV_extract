Abstract

High-performance fiber-shaped energy storage devices (FESDs) based on Niâ€“Fe batteries are indispensable for the development of portable and wearable electronics. Construction of highly capacitive pseudocapacitance cathode/anode materials holds great potential for the fabrication of high-performance FESDs. In this study, CoP@Ni(OH)2 nanowire arrays (NWAs) with hierarchical coreâ€“shell heterostructure, directly grown on carbon nanotube fiber (CNTF), are prepared by a facile and environmentally friendly hydrothermal method, followed by phosphorization and chemical bath deposition process. CoP NWAs possess excellent electrical conductivity, and the core material and secondarily grown substrate can provide fast charge transfer and reaction kinetics between electrolyte and pseudocapacitance material. 3D spindle-like Î±-Fe2O3 (S-Î±-Fe2O3) architecture is well-dispersed and directly grown on CNTF via solvothermal method and post-annealing process. An FESD consisting of a CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF as the positive electrode, a S-Î±-Fe2O3/CNTF as the negative electrode, and KOH-PVA as the gel electrolyte is fabricated. The FESD achieves a maximum capacity of 0.203 mA h cmâˆ’2 and highest energy density of 81.0 mW h cmâˆ’3, which are mainly attributable to the uniquely hierarchical coreâ€“shell and spindle-like nanostructure. Furthermore, the device shows a high power density of 12000 mW cmâˆ’3 and a long cycle life with 85.3% retention after 3000 cycles at the current density of 30 mA cmâˆ’2.

Introduction

Academic and industry communities have transferred their focus to reproducible and continuously renewable energy resources, mainly because of the rapid consumption and negative environmental influences of fossil fuels.1â€“9 As reasonable choices, solar cells, fuel cells, secondary rechargeable batteries, lithium-ion batteries (LIBs), and supercapacitors provide enormous potential as energy conversion and storage devices.10â€“16 Safety concerns associated with non-aqueous LIBs and toxic Pb-acid and Niâ€“Cd batteries cannot be neglected owing to their use of highly toxic and combustible organic electrolytes.17â€“19 Aqueous rechargeable batteries based on Ni-metal, such as Niâ€“Fe, Niâ€“Bi, and Niâ€“Zn batteries, have attracted significant attention as substitutive forms of energy storage owing to their highly safe performance, high ionic conductivity, insignificant environmental issues, and low cost.1,4,5,7â€“9,20â€“22 However, the shortage and high price of Bi restrict its practical application.1,21,22 The dendritic crystal formation and morphological damage, occurring on Zn anodes during the chargeâ€“discharge process, negatively affect the maintenance of battery capacity.8,23 For example, Zong et al. reported that a hybrid zinc-battery with robust electrochemical performance was assembled using NiCo2O4 NWAs, grown on carbon-coated nickel foam (NiCo2O4/NiF@C) as the cathode, a zinc plate as the anode and 6 M KOH containing 0.2 M Zn(OAc)2Â·6H2O as aqueous electrolyte. However, application of alkaline aqueous electrolyte in as-mentioned hybrid zinc-battery does not make it a sufficiently safe energy-storage device. Furthermore, the weak flexibility of NiCo2O4/NiF@C cathode and zinc plate anode restrict its practical application in portable and wearable electronics.24 Compared with Niâ€“Bi and Niâ€“Zn batteries, Niâ€“Fe batteries have received much attention, because the anode materials for this battery type are mainly iron oxides containing Fe2O3 and Fe3O4. These oxides are considered promising candidates because they are nontoxic, highly corrosion resistant, abundant, and inexpensive.5,7,20,23,25 However, with the rapidly growing demand for the miniaturization of portable and wearable electronics, it is necessary to fabricate energy storage devices with high energy density and tiny volume.26â€“36 Fiber-shaped energy storage devices (FESDs) are the primary choice to meet the requirements of miniaturization.31,37,38 To the best of our knowledge, FESDs based on Niâ€“Fe batteries have not been reported until now. On this basis, the selection of cathode and anode materials with high capacitance and electrical conductivity, directly grown on a conductive fibrous current collector (binder-free), appears to be particularly important for constructing high-performance FESDs.20,23,33 However, the low specific capacitance of the single-component Ni-based cathode materials has seriously restricted their practical application.5,20 Moreover, conventional Fe-based materials have low specific capacitance and inferior rate capability.23,39 Current advances in novel coreâ€“shell nanostructured cathode materials and, in particular, three-dimensional (3D) anode materials have empowered researchers to restudy Niâ€“Fe batteries.

Transition metal phosphides such as CoP, Co2P, and NiP2 have been extensively investigated as novel cathode materials for ESDs owing to their high electrical conductivity and high redox chemical valences in comparison with other transition metal oxides, such as CoO, NiO, and Co3O4.18,40â€“44 Specifically, Co phosphides have been considered as potential promising low-cost competitive cathode materials for ESDs because of their high conductivity and stability.40,42,44 Furthermore, the smaller band gap of Co phosphides indicates higher conductivity than that of single-component oxides or even corresponding ternary Coâ€“Ni oxides.40,44,45 Additionally, single-metal phosphides show great promise in the construction of next-generation electrodes with increased specific capacitance resulting from their high electronic conductivity. However, further increasing the capacitance of binary CoP is still a tremendous obstacle. One prospective approach is to construct a hierarchical coreâ€“shell heterostructure with highly-conductive Co phosphides as the core and well-known pseudocapacitive transitional metal hydroxides as the shell layer.33,36,46 In a recent study of the establishment of the coreâ€“shell structure, the authors claimed that it is expected to provide optimized electrochemical performance for ESDs.

Numerous investigations have been undertaken of anode materials, suitable for constructing nanostructured Fe2O3 materials with different morphological features to overcome the aforementioned issues.20,23,36,47 For example, Shen and co-workers reported that Fe2O3 nanorods, grown on graphene foam/carbon nanotube hybrid films, showed good electrochemical performance.20 Wang et al. synthesized hierarchical Fe2O3 hollow spheres as lithium ion battery anode materials.47 Therefore, constructing Fe2O3 with a unique 3D morphology is a proven and effective method, utilized in building of high-performance Niâ€“Fe batteries.

To this end, we are focused on developing an electrode architecture that can offer coinstantaneous improvements in specific energy and power density, applicable to high-performance FESDs. We have prepared 3D well-aligned CoP nanowire arrays (NWAs)@Ni(OH)2 nanosheets (NSs) with hierarchical coreâ€“shell heterostructure and 3D Î±-Fe2O3 with a spindle-like nanostructure (S-Î±-Fe2O3), grown directly on carbon nanotube fibers (CNTFs: diameter of about 120 Î¼m) for use as binder-free positive and negative electrodes, respectively. Additionally, we have successfully fabricated a high-performance FESD based on a Niâ€“Fe battery, using the aforementioned positive and negative electrodes and KOH-PVA as gel electrolyte that achieved a high energy density of 81.0 mW h cmâˆ’3 at 1200 mW cmâˆ’3. Furthermore, the developed FESD can be cycled up to 3000 cycles with only 14.7% attenuation of capacity. This work provides a promising strategy for effectively constructing FESDs based on nanocomposites, which may smooth the way to developing high-performance, wearable, low-cost, portable, and safe energy storage technologies.

Experimental

Preparation of CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF

The CoP NWAs on a CNTF was synthesized by using a simple hydrothermal method and, subsequently, by phosphating. Before growing all samples on the CNTFs, the CNTFs were pretreated with O2 plasma at a power of 150 W for 8 min. Firstly, typically, 1.66 g mmol Co(NO3)2Â·6H2O, 0.42 g NH4F, and 1.71 g CO(NH2)2 were dissolved in 40 mL deionized (DI) water and stirred vigorously to form an uniform solution. The CNTFs were immersed in the solution, which was transferred into a 50 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave. Then the autoclave was sealed and maintained at 120 Â°C for 6 h. After cooling down to room temperature, the CNTFs with grown sample on their surface were washed repeatedly using DI water and dried for 12 h at 60 Â°C, forming the Coâ€“O/CNTF precursors. Thereafter, they were phosphatized at 350 Â°C in Ar for 2 h with a heating rate of 2 Â°C minâˆ’1 (0.1 g NaH2PO2Â·H2O as bonderite), the CoP NWAs/CNTF was obtained, and the mass loading of the CoP NWAs was measured to be 0.97 mg cmâˆ’2. The synthesized CoP NWAs/CNTFs were suspended into a homogeneous solution containing 10 g Ni(SO4)2Â·6H2O, 2 g K2S2O8, 5 mL of aqueous ammonia (28%), and 100 mL DI water for 30 min. Finally, the positive electrode material CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF was washed repeatedly using DI water several times and dried at 60 Â°C for 10 h in a vacuum (mass loading: 1.24 mg cmâˆ’2). The Co3O4 NWAs/CNTFs were also prepared by annealing the Coâ€“O/CNTF precursor at 420 Â°C in Ar for 2 h with a heating rate of 2 Â°C minâˆ’1 (mass loading: 0.93 mg cmâˆ’2). Furthermore, the Co3O4@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF was prepared by the same method for comparison (mass loading: 1.23 mg cmâˆ’2).

Preparation of S-Î±-Fe2O3/CNTF

The S-Î±-Fe2O3 grown on a CNTF was fabricated by using a facile hydrothermal method and a post annealing treatment. 4.05 g FeCl3Â·6H2O and 1.66 g C8H6O4 were dissolved in 100 mL DMF and stirred vigorously to form a uniform solution. The CNTFs were immersed into the solution which was transferred into a 50 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave. Then the autoclave was sealed and maintained at 100 Â°C for 14 h. After cooling down to room temperature, the CNTFs with grown sample were washed repeatedly using DI water and dried for 12 h at 60 Â°C, forming the Feâ€“O/CNTFs precursors. Then, they were subsequently annealed at 350 Â°C in air for 2 h with a heating rate of 5 Â°C minâˆ’1, and the S-Î±-Fe2O3/CNTF (mass loading: 0.78 mg cmâˆ’2) was obtained.

Assembly of CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF//S-Î±-Fe2O3/CNTF FESDs

The as-assembled FESDs were constructed using hierarchically-structured CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF as a positive electrode, S-Î±-Fe2O3/CNTF as a negative electrode, and KOH-PVA as gel electrolyte. The solid gel electrolyte was prepared by blending 11.2 g KOH and 10 g PVA in 100 mL DI water with intense stirring at 95 Â°C for 2 h until the solution turned clear. Thereafter, both CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF and S-Î±-Fe2O3/CNTF electrodes were immersed into the KOH-PVA gel electrolyte for 10 min and subsequently dried at 60 Â°C for 2 h twice. Finally, all-solid-state FESDs were triumphantly assembled by twisting the two electrodes together and dried at 60 Â°C for 12 h until the KOH-PVA gel electrolyte cured fully.

Results and discussion

Fig. 1 shows the process of preparation of CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs with hierarchical coreâ€“shell heterostructure and S-Î±-Fe2O3 directly on CNTFs to construct the high-performance FESD. For the positive electrode, CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs were directly grown on a CNTF via three processes, consisting of hydrothermal synthesis, phosphorization in argon, and chemical bath deposition. For the negative electrode, S-Î±-Fe2O3 was directly grown on a CNTF via a two-step process, which included hydrothermal synthesis and annealing in air. The FESD was assembled by twisting the two electrodes together with a gel electrolyte coating. CoP exhibits many excellent characteristics, such as high electrical conductivity, electrochemical activity, and chemical stability.40,41,45 Î±-Fe2O3 with a spindle-like nanostructure also possesses many remarkable properties, such as high specific surface area and negative potential.20,23 In present study, the CoP NWAs, grown on the CNTFs, not only act as electrically conductive and stable supports for the growth of the nanostructured metal hydroxide that acts as the cathode, but also promote ultrafast charge transfer and ion diffusion. S-Î±-Fe2O3 that serves as the anode, directly grown on the CNTFs, demonstrates prominent electrochemical performance. Therefore, the FESD assembled by these two electrodes can provide good technical support for the next generation of portable and wearable electronics.

Fig. 4 shows the electrochemical performance characteristics of the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF in 3 M KOH aqueous electrolyte. As is known, to acquire remarkable rate capability and specific capacitance of electrode materials with a hierarchical coreâ€“shell nanostructure, the core needs superior electrical conductivity, and the shell layer must have outstanding pseudocapacitance characteristics.33 Co3O4 NWAs/CNTF electrode suffers from low electrical conductivity and low rate capability, as shown in Fig. S11,â€  further weakening the rate capability of the hierarchical Co3O4@Ni(OH)2 NWAs coreâ€“shell nanostructure/CNTF electrode (Fig. S12â€ ). To overcome this limitation, we phosphatized the Co3O4 NWAs to transform them into CoP NWAs, which resulted in preferable conductivity and rate capability (Fig. S13â€ ). Comparison of the galvanostatic chargeâ€“discharge (GCD) curves of Co3O4@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF and CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrodes at current densities of 2 and 20 mA cmâˆ’2 reveals a drastically longer discharge time for the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode (Fig. 4(a) and (b)), signifying notably larger capacitance for this electrode. In addition, the IR drop in the GCD curve at a current density of 2 mA cmâˆ’2 for the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode is as low as 2.5 mV, which can be basically ignored. However, the IR drop for the Co3O4@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode reaches 23.7 mV (Fig. 4(a)). When the current density increases 10-fold (20 mA cmâˆ’2), the IR drop for the Co3O4@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode is as high as 249.4 mV, while that for the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode is only 24.5 mV (Fig. 4(b)), indicating that CoP NWAs can effectively decrease the IR drop and enhance electrochemical capacitance of the coreâ€“shell-like electrode. The typical CV curves of the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode are displayed at various sweep rates ranging from 1 to 10 mV sâˆ’1 (Fig. 4c). Fig. 4(d) shows the relationship between log(i) vs. log(v) from 1 to 10 mV sâˆ’1 for the cathodic and anodic peaks. Assuming that the measured current i obeys a power law relationship with the scan rate v, according to the equation i = avb, where a and b are adjustable parameters, b can be determined from the slope of the plot log(i) vs. log(v).49,50 On the basis of the approximations, b = 1 corresponds to surface-controlled current, whereas b = 0.5 indicates current, controlled by the semi-infinite linear diffusion. The slope of the lines in Fig. 4(d) is calculated to be â‰ˆ 0.80, indicating that the charge storage process is synergistically controlled by the capacitive and diffusion behaviors, further empowering the high rate performance. Fig. 4(e) shows the typical GCD curves of the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode at different current densities (from 2 to 20 mA cmâˆ’2) over the voltage window of 0â€“0.4 V. The nearly symmetric and nonlinear pattern, associated with the GCD curves, further confirms the existence of faradic redox reactions that suggest strong pseudocapacitive behavior of the active materials. As we expected, the capacity of the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode was as high as 0.689 mA h cmâˆ’2 at a current density of 2 mA cmâˆ’2, and an impressive capacity of 0.495 mA h cmâˆ’2 can be maintained even at a high current density of 20 mA cmâˆ’2, signifying that 71.8% of the capacity can be maintained with a 10-fold increase in current density from 2 to 20 mA cmâˆ’2 (Fig. 4(f)). This value is far higher than that for the Co3O4@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF (24.2%), indicating excellent electrochemical performance of the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs. To understand the superiority of the electrochemical performance of the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode in comparison with that of the Co3O4@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) studies were carried out. As shown in the Nyquist plots (Fig. 4(g)), the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode displays a dramatically smaller semicircle (related to the charge-transfer resistance, Rct) compared to the Co3O4@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode, confirming the increased efficiency of the electrochemical reaction occurring in the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode. Moreover, when compared with the Co3O4@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode, the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode has a lower diffusion resistance owing to its more ideal straight line in the low-frequency region. Thereby, the low electrical resistance and the IR drop, and fast charge transfer contribute to the large capacitance and good rate capability of the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode. To investigate the cycling life of the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF and Co3O4@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrodes, GCD measurements were repeated over 15000 cycles at 20 mA cmâˆ’2 (Fig. 4(h)). The extraordinary properties of the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF electrode make it a promising candidate for use in the construction of future flexible energy-storage devices.

Conclusions

In this study, through rational design, we developed a facile and environmentally friendly method to fabricate novel well-aligned hierarchical CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs coreâ€“shell heterostructures and spindle-like Î±-Fe2O3 grown on CNTF surfaces as the positive and negative electrodes, respectively. The CoP NWAs not only serve as a stable conductive support but also provide fast and efficient pathways for electrons, whereas the nanosized ultrathin Ni(OH)2 NSs largely shorten the diffusion paths and enable fast ion transport that results in high rate performance and long-term stability. Owing to the increased electrical conductivity of the CoP core material and the synergistic effects of the well-aligned CoP NWAs and Ni(OH)2 NSs, the capacity of the CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF is as high as 0.689 mA h cmâˆ’2. The S-Î±-Fe2O3 with ultrahigh specific surface area, directly grown on the CNTF, shows a high capacity of 0.474 mA h cmâˆ’2. We also assembled solid-state FESDs with the as-fabricated electrodes and a KOH-PVA gel electrolyte that successfully show an operating voltage of 1.6 V and deliver high capacity of 0.203 mA h cmâˆ’2, energy density of 81.0 mW h cmâˆ’3, and rate performance of 63.1%. More importantly, the as-assembled devices display an outstanding cycling property and strong, flexible mechanical performance. Therefore, the hierarchical coreâ€“shell heterostructure CoP@Ni(OH)2 NWAs/CNTF as the positive electrode and S-Î±-Fe2O3/CNTF as the negative electrode in the assembled FESDs show great potential for application in portable and wearable energy storage devices.
