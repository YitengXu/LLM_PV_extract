Abstract

The cyano-group (â€“CN) is an electron-withdrawing group, which has been widely used to construct high-performance fused-ring electron acceptors (FREAs). Benefiting from these FREAs, the power conversion efficiency of organic solar cells has recently exceeded 18%. However, malononitrile is a highly toxic substance used to introduce â€“CN during the synthesis of these FREAs. Therefore, the synthesis processes of most high-performance FREAs are typically harmful to the environment. Our previous work demonstrated that the electron-withdrawing ability of â€“CN is necessary for FREAs. Thus, the use of other electron-withdrawing groups instead of â€“CN to design environmentally friendly FREAs is feasible. We utilized seven electron-withdrawing groups, namely, â€“CNH, â€“NO, â€“CHO, â€“COâ€“CH3, â€“COâ€“OH, â€“COâ€“Cl, and â€“COâ€“Br, to replace â€“CN in the commonly used acceptor Y6 to design new FREAs (Y6-CNH, Y6-NO, Y6-CHO, Y6-COCH3, Y6-COOH, Y6-COCl, and Y6-COBr). Multi-scale theoretical calculation methods were used to investigate the photoelectronic properties of these new FREAs, including energy level, absorption spectrum, exciton binding energy, and electron mobility. The results showed that Y6-CNH, Y6-COCH3 and Y6-COOH are unsuitable for use as acceptor materials because of their high frontier molecular orbital energy level and weak electron affinity. The strong absorption intensity and weak exciton binding energy of Y6-CHO, Y6-COCl, and Y6-COBr indicated that they can absorb more solar energy than Y6 and excitons are easier to separate into free charges. The electron mobility of Y6-CHO (3.53 Ã— 10âˆ’4 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1) was found to be approximately 28 times that of Y6-COCl (1.24 Ã— 10âˆ’5 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1) and Y6-COBr (1.28 Ã— 10âˆ’5 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1). The possible synthetic routes to Y6-CHO are environmentally friendly. Therefore, â€“CHO is the most suitable electron-withdrawing group for constructing high-performance environmentally friendly FREAs. This work can provide a new molecular design perspective in experimental science for developing high-performance environmentally friendly FREAs.

Introduction

Organic solar cells (OSCs) have attracted considerable research attention in the past few decades due to their advantages of light weight, flexibility, solution processability and low cost.1â€“4 The bulk-heterojunction OSC is a widely used device structure that typically consists of a photoactive layer of electron donors and acceptors sandwiched between two electrodes. The photoelectronic properties of donors and acceptors play an important role in improving the performance of OSCs. A few years ago, the development of donors had been intensively investigated.5,6 During that time, fullerenes and their derivatives were used as acceptors.2 As fullerenes and their derivatives exhibit narrow and weak absorption of sunlight and poor adjustability of energy levels, the power conversion efficiency of fullerene-based OSCs is limited to 12%.7â€“10

Compared with fullerenes and their derivatives, nonfullerene acceptors (NFAs) exhibit variable energy levels, strong absorption of sunlight, and flexible synthetic methods.11â€“14 These NFAs, especially fused-ring electron acceptors (FREAs), have attracted considerable attention in recent years. The development of FREAs has rapidly increased the power conversion efficiency of OSCs from 6% to 18%.15â€“19 FREAs typically exhibit a classic acceptorâ€“donorâ€“acceptor (Aâ€“Dâ€“A) structure, that consists of an electron-rich core (D) with two electron-deficient terminal groups (A). 1,1-Dicyanomethylene-3-indanone and its derivatives (methylated or fluorinated) are widely used electron-deficient terminal groups.14,20,21 These terminal groups generally contain two cyano-groups (â€“CN), and a highly toxic substance called malononitrile must be used during the synthesis process. The toxicity of malononitrile is higher than that of HCN, which is one of the most hazardous species of cyanide compounds, as malononitrile could be completely converted to HCN in the mammalian tissue metabolism.22,23 Therefore, the environmentally harmful synthesis process of currently used high-performance FREAs limits their commercial applications. Our previous work demonstrated that the electron-withdrawing ability of â€“CN determines the ability of FREAs to accept electrons.24 Therefore, the use of other electron-withdrawing groups instead of â€“CN is feasible in the construction of environmentally friendly FREAs.

In this work, we used seven electron-withdrawing groups, namely, â€“CNH, â€“NO, â€“CHO, â€“COâ€“CH3, â€“COâ€“OH, â€“COâ€“Cl, and â€“COâ€“Br, to replace â€“CN in the commonly used acceptor Y621 for the design of new FREAs (Y6-CNH, Y6-NO, Y6-CHO, Y6-COCH3, Y6-COOH, Y6-COCl, and Y6-COBr). Quantum chemical calculations, molecular dynamics simulations (MD) and Monte Carlo (MC) calculations were applied to investigate the photoelectronic properties of new FREAs, including energy level, electron affinity, predicted open circuit voltage, absorption spectrum, exciton binding energy, and electron mobility. The lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy and electron affinity indicate the ability of materials to accept electrons. Both indicators demonstrated that Y6-CNH, Y6-COCH3, and Y6-COOH are unsuitable as acceptor materials. The spectral analysis showed that Y6-CHO, Y6-COCl, and Y6-COBr exhibit stronger absorption intensity and weaker exciton binding energy than Y6, thereby indicating that they can absorb more solar energy than Y6 and excitons are easier to separate into free charges. Meanwhile, the electron mobility of Y6-CHO was found to be about 28 times that of Y6-COCl and Y6-COBr. The comprehensive analysis of photoelectric properties showed that â€“CHO is a suitable electron-withdrawing group for constructing high-performance environmentally friendly FREAs. This result can provide a new molecular design perspective for experimental science to develop high-performance environmentally friendly FREAs.

Computational method

All quantum chemical calculations were carried out using the ORCA Revision 4.1.0 program.25 Geometry optimization of the ground, anion and cation states of all materials investigated in this work was carried out at the B3LYP/def2-SVP level with geometrical counterpoise correction to remove artificial overbinding effects from the basis set superposition error.26 Frontier molecular orbital energy levels, adiabatic electron affinity (AEA), adiabatic ionization energy (AIP), and electron reorganization energies (Î»âˆ’) were calculated at the B3LYP/def2-TZVP level. Since the PBE0 hybrid functional can estimate the reasonable absorption spectrum of most organic materials using time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT),27 we used the PBE0/def2-SVPD calculation level to investigate the absorption spectra and excited states of all materials in this work. In order to account for the major parts of dispersion force contribution to the energy, the atom-pairwise dispersion correction (D3ZERO)28 was used during this calculation. To match the actual situation of the absorption spectrum measured in the experiment, the conductor-like polarizable continuum model29 was used to simulate the effect of chloroform in the TD-DFT calculations. During these quantum chemical calculations, the alkyl chains were omitted to reduce the calculation cost.

MD simulations were used to construct amorphous thin films, which are formed through the mixture of electron donor material (PBDB-TF)30 and electron acceptor material (Y6-CHO, Y6-COCl, or Y6-COBr) to construct a bulk-heterojunction model in this work. In the MD processing, alkyl chains were introduced into the donor and acceptor materials due to their influence on molecular packing. MD simulations were carried out using the GROMACS program with general amber force field (GAFF).31 The restrained electrostatic potential (RESP) atomic charge calculated using the Multiwfn 3.7 package32 was used for the GAFF. The MD simulation process was as follows: (1) PBDB-TF (193) and electron acceptor (400) molecules (6-CHO, Y6-COCl, or Y6-COBr) were randomly placed in a large box (20 nm Ã— 20 nm Ã— 20 nm) to generate the initial models. (2) The initial models were subjected to 50000 steps of steep descent to remove undesired forces and structural distortion. (3) 420 K and 100 bar conditions were simulated at 200 ps to fill the empty space with molecules in the NPT ensemble quickly. (4) Then, models in the NPT ensemble were annealed between 420 and 300 K six times in 1.2 ns. (5) Finally, the models were maintained at a temperature of 300 K for 20 ns to achieve completely converged results. Hence, we obtained amorphous bulk-heterojunction films, which were used as the initial structure for MC calculations to estimate electron mobility.

The electron mobility of amorphous bulk-heterojunction thin films was estimated through a multiscale simulation process based on quantum chemical calculations, MD simulations, and MC calculations. The MC calculations were carried out as described in our previous work.33â€“35 The adjacent molecular pairs (the shortest interatomic distance is less than 0.5 nm) were constructed for each molecule under periodic conditions based on the model of amorphous films obtained from the MD simulations. The center-of-mass (COM) of each molecule was utilized as the hopping point in the MC simulations. During the hopping process, we first randomly selected one molecule (i) as the initial charge center. Then, the charge hops between the center molecule i and its adjacent molecule (j) with a probability of Pij = kij/Î£kij, where kij is defined as the electron hopping rate between the molecules i and j. The hopping time was defined as 1/kij, and the hopping distance was taken to be the molecular COM(i)â€“COM(j) distance. Finally, we used the hopping coordinate and time to build the relationship between the square diffusion displacement and the diffusion time to estimate the charge diffusion coefficient. The electron transfer integral between adjacent molecules was calculated using the B3LYP/3-21G method in this work.

Electron mobility

Once the excitons are separated into free charges, the mobility of holes and electrons will play a crucial role in the performance of OSCs. High electron mobility is an essential requirement for high-performance FREAs. We utilized a multiscale simulation method developed in our earlier studies33â€“35 to identify the electron mobility of possible high-performance FREAs (Y6-CHO, Y6-COCl, and Y6-COBr).

The electron diffusion process was simulated using the MC method to obtain the electron-diffusion coefficient. The electron-diffusion coefficient was determined by the slope of the squared diffusion displacement and diffusion time. The squared diffusion displacement of five typical simulations and averages of 2000 simulations versus simulation times for Y6-CHO, Y6-COCl, and Y6-COBr are shown in Fig. 5. The squared diffusion displacement of the average value of 2000 simulations shows a satisfactory linear relationship with diffusion time and was used to estimate the electron-diffusion coefficient. Based on the Einstein formula, the electron mobility of Y6-CHO, Y6-COCl, and Y6-COBr was estimated to be 3.53 Ã— 10âˆ’4, 1.24 Ã— 10âˆ’5, and 1.28 Ã— 10âˆ’5 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1, respectively. Therefore, Y6-CHO is the best candidate for developing high-performance FREAs because it exhibited the highest electron mobility among these materials.
