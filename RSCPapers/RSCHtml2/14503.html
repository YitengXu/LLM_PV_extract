Abstract

To push the boundary of the efficiency of perovskite nanocrystal-based photovoltaics, understanding the charge transfer at the interface of these nanocrystals is necessary. In an effort to understand the electronic effects of the substituents in the charge acceptor moiety, three electronically different small molecules (namely, chloranilic acid (CA), p-benzoquinone (BQ), and duroquinone (DQ)) were chosen and their detailed charge transfer dynamics were studied at the CsPbBr3 perovskite nanocrystalâ€“small organic molecule interface using steady state and time-resolved spectroscopic methods. The steady-state absorption and time-resolved emission studies reveal that all three molecules interact with the NCs in the excited state. Femtosecond transient absorption experiments indicate a faster ground-state bleach recovery in the presence of the three acceptors, compared with the pristine NCs. Utilizing band alignment analysis, the faster bleach recovery of the NCs in presence of the acceptors was confirmed to be because of electron transfer from the photo-excited NCs to the acceptor molecules. Moreover, the electron transfer rates fall in the Marcus normal region and can be explained based on the electronic effects of the substituents present on the acceptor molecules.

1. Introduction

Lead halide perovskite nanocrystals (PNCs) have received great research interest in the area of optoelectronics,1â€“3 with a major focus in photovoltaics,4â€“6 due to their impressive optical and electronic properties which include a high absorption coefficient,7,8 a narrow and intense emission,9 an outstanding charge carrier ability, a lower exciton binding energy,10 and a long ambipolar charge diffusion length.11â€“13 In addition, the ease of solution processibility of these PNCs has enabled researchers to tune their optical and electronic properties with respect to their size, shape, and dimensionality through altering the reaction conditions.14,15 Due to advancements in technology and material processing over the past few years, the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of perovskite based solar cells has exceeded 25%.16 The PCE of perovskite based light harvesting devices depends mainly on three major processes: (i) the generation of excitons, (ii) the dissociation of these excitons, and (iii) the transportation of these excitons towards the counter electrode. In real situations, the above-mentioned processes compete with electronâ€“hole internal recombination and charge carrier trapping into defect states.17,18 Thus, to overcome such competitive processes, in an optoelectronic device, PNCs have been sandwiched between electron- and hole-transporting layers, which extract the excitons from the nanocrystals prior to their trapping and internal recombination.

It has been observed that the performance of a perovskite is enhanced when it is coupled with different systems such as metal oxides (MOs),19,20 quantum dots (QDs),21â€“23 2D layered materials,22â€“25 and small organic molecules.15,26â€“30 For example, Liu et al. have demonstrated that when CsPbI3 QDs are coupled with TiO2 nanoparticles, âˆ¼99% of the photoexcited electrons from the CsPbI3 QDs are transferred to the TiO2 nanoparticles, leading to an enhancement of the photocurrent in perovskiteâ€“metal oxide hybrid systems.19 The enhancement of the PCE has also been explained by Ding et al. through interfacial engineering in CsPbBr3 NCâ€“CuInS2/ZnS QD composites.31 More importantly, these studies also reflect that it is not only the exciton formation in the NCs but also the dissociation of the excitons that plays a pivotal role in deciding the performance of the perovskite based photovoltaic devices. The key points for improving the device performance are the rapid dissociation of the excitons, suppressed internal radiative recombination, and reduced trapping of the charge carriers into defect states. Understanding the charge carrier dynamics at the interface of the PNCs in presence of acceptors is therefore crucial to fully exploit the potential of the PNCs.

In this context, there have been studies to understand the role of the metal oxide as an electron transporting layer. Charge transfer dynamics at the perovskite NCâ€“metal oxide interface have been investigated by Liao et al. using three different metal oxides, and the electron transfer rate was found to be maximum for CsPbBr3â€“SnO2 composites.20 The effect of the dimensionality of the PNCs on the ultrafast electron transfer has also been studied using 0D, 1D, and 2D CdSe nanoplates. The study revealed that the rate of electron transfer is maximum in the 2D PNCâ€“2D CdSe nanoplate composite compared with the 0Dâ€“0D composite.23 Analysis of the charge transfer dynamics at the perovskite nanocrystalâ€“MO/QDs interface becomes complex due to the presence of continuous and quasi-continuous energy states in the metal oxide (MO) and quantum dots (QDs), respectively. Therefore, to understand the movement of the charge carriers at the interface of PNCs, small organic molecules with discrete energy levels were introduced and detailed charge transfer dynamics were studied at such PNCâ€“organic molecule interfaces.15,26â€“30,32,33 Although the dependence of the charge transfer on the size,34 shape,15,23 and composition27 of the NCs and the distance between the NCs and the acceptor30 have been studied extensively, the electronic effects of the substituents in the acceptor moiety on regulating the charge transfer dynamics have not yet been explored, to the best of our knowledge.

Here, three different 1,4-benzoquinone based acceptor molecules,26,35 namely, chloranilic acid (CA), p-benzoquinone (BQ), and duroquinone (DQ) (Chart 1), were chosen to understand the effect of the substituents present on the electron transfer dynamics at the perovskite nanocrystalâ€“molecular acceptor interface. The effects that the electron-withdrawing and -donating substituent groups present on the acceptor molecules have on the charge transfer dynamics are investigated using steady state UV-visible absorption and emission, time resolved emission decay and femto-second transient absorption spectroscopy. We believe that the conclusions arrived at in our study will help in the design of promising small organic molecule-based electron acceptors for PNCs, which can be used in light emitting and light harvesting systems.

2. Results and discussion

2.1 Synthesis of CsPbBr3 NCs

CsPbBr3 NCs were synthesised using a hot injection method as reported by Protesescu et al.14 In brief, Cs2CO3 (0.1103 g) was loaded into a 50 mL two-neck round-bottom (RB) flask along with octadecene (ODE; 5 mL) and oleic acid (OA; 312.5 Î¼L), which was dried under vacuum for 1 h at 120 Â°C before being heated at 150 Â°C under an N2 atmosphere until the Cs2CO3 was dissolved. This solution was kept above 100 Â°C before injection to avoid the precipitation of Cs-oleate. In another 50 mL RB flask, PbBr2 (0.069 g) was taken with ODE (5 mL), OA (0.5 mL) and OLA (oleylamine; 0.5 mL). This mixture was dried under vacuum at 120 Â°C for 1 h. Then the temperature was increased to 165 Â°C under an N2 atmosphere. After complete dissolution of the PbBr2, 0.4 mL Cs-oleate solution was swiftly injected into the reaction vessel, and after 10 s the reaction was quenched using an ice-bath. The synthesised NCs were extracted by centrifuging the reaction mixture at âˆ¼8500 rpm for 10 min and then the precipitate was dispersed in toluene for the subsequent studies.

2.4 Time resolved photoluminescence measurements

In order to gain a deeper insight into the quenching mechanism, the time resolved photoluminescence spectra of the NCs were recorded in absence and presence of the quenchers. Fig. 3Aâ€“C show the time resolved decay spectra of the CsPbBr3 NCs in the presence of CA, BQ and DQ, respectively. The results indicate faster decay in presence of each of the three quenchers compared with the pristine NCs, indicating the excited state interaction of the NCs with each of the three quenchers. Since the SV plot of steady state luminescence quenching also appears to be linear in all cases, it is customary to recognise the quenching mechanisms as being solely dynamic in nature, for all three cases. However, careful analysis of the decay profiles tells a slightly different story. All of the decay profiles were fitted with a tri-exponential function using eqn (S1), and the time constants with their weightages (amplitudes) are given in the ESIâ€  (Table S2). The intermediate time constant of 8 ns for the pristine CsPbBr3 NCs is for band-edge electronâ€“hole recombination, whereas the two extreme time constants of 2 ns and 30 ns occur due to different trap state mediated radiative recombinations of electrons and holes.40 Upon the gradual addition of each of the quenchers, the time constants of the individual components are found to decrease successively (ESI,â€  Fig. S3). Now, if we look at the relative amplitudes of the individual components (ESI,â€  Table S2), we see that the relative contribution of Ï„1, i.e., A1, increases gradually with addition of the quencher. This increase in A1 is more pronounced in the case of CA. The component Ï„1 arises due to the recombination of electrons and holes via surface defect states of the NCs. An increase in the amplitude of this component suggests that the quencher molecules induce more defect states in the NCs via surface tuning. Excited state interactions may occur either through charge transfer from the photoexcited donor to the acceptor or via energy transfer from the excited donor to the acceptor. For efficient energy transfer there must be significant spectral overlap between the donor emission and the acceptor absorbance. As shown in ESI,â€  Fig. S7, the spectral overlap between the NCs emission and the quenchers absorption is negligible. Furthermore, a short distance (âˆ¼1 nm) between the donor and acceptor is required for efficient energy transfer to occur in the solution phase.41 However, the presence of large surface passivating ligands like oleic acid and oleylamine results in a large separation distance between the donor and acceptor systems. Therefore, we have excluded the probability of energy transfer and confirm that the excited state interaction is entirely due to charge transfer from the photoexcited NCs to each of the three quenchers.

2.5 Thermodynamics of charge transfer

Upon photoexcitation, electrons from the valence band of the NCs move to the conduction band, leaving behind holes in the valence band. In the presence of a suitable acceptor, either of these photo-generated charge carriers can be transferred depending on the thermodynamic feasibility of the processes. Previous studies have shown that both the charge carriers, electrons and holes, in the CsPbBr3 NCs have nearly equal effective masses (me* = 0.15m0, and mh* = 0.14m0; m0 = rest mass of the electron),26 unlike cadmium chalcogenide NCs (i.e., CdS, CdSe or CdTe NCs) where the holes have heavier effective masses than the electrons (for CdTe, mh*/me* = 4).43 Hence the mobility of the electrons and holes is identical in the CsPbBr3 NCs, in contrast to the cadmium chalcogenide NCs where the motion of the holes is retarded due to their heavier effective mass value. In the present study, either both electrons and holes or either of these two charge carriers can be transferred from the photoexcited CsPbBr3 NCs to the quencher molecules. To identify the type of charge carrier being transferred from the NCs to the acceptors, band energy level analysis was performed. Fig. 5 shows a schematic representation of the relative band energy alignment of the NCs and the three acceptor molecules. The positions of the valence band maximum (VBM) and conduction band minimum (CBM) for the NCs was taken from the literature,33 and the band energies of all the molecular acceptors were calculated using density functional theory (details are given in the ESIâ€ ). The band energy level alignment shows that the VBM of the NCs lies above the HOMOs of all the molecular acceptors, which rules out the possibility of hole transfer from the photoexcited CsPbBr3 NCs to any of the acceptors. However, the LUMOs of all three acceptors lie below the CBM of the NCs, which makes electron transfer thermodynamically feasible from the photoexcited CsPbBr3 NCs to all three acceptors.

2.6 Transient absorption measurements

Next, to unfold the excited state charge carrier dynamics on an early timescale, femtosecond transient absorption experiments (TA) were performed. The samples were dispersed in toluene with continuous stirring to avoid photobleaching; a 350 nm laser pulse was used for excitation of all the samples. The concentration of the quenchers used for this study was 0.1 mM in all three cases. Here, it should be noted that the three quencher molecules have negligible absorbance at 350 nm (close to zero, for the concentration of the quenchers used for TA experiment), whereas the NCs have a large absorption at 350 nm (ESI,â€  Fig. S8). Hence, the excitation wavelength is absorbed only by the NCs and the transient absorption profile gives the picture of the exciton dynamics of the NCs in the excited state selectively. The possibility of contamination of the TA profile due to quencher molecule absorption is thus eliminated. The pump fluence was kept low (6 Î¼J cmâˆ’2) to minimize the multiple excitons generation per nanocrystal. Fig. 6A and B show the transient absorption (TA) spectra of the bare NCs and the NCâ€“CA composite systems, respectively. The transient absorption spectra show a negative absorption ranging from 470 to 560 nm centred at 505 nm for all the samples, which is in accordance with the excitonic peak position of the ground state absorption. In addition, significant photoinduced absorption (PA) signals are observed on either side of the ground state bleach signal, which appear due to absorption from the lowest excitonic states (on the lower wavelength side) and from the hot-excitonic states (on the longer wavelength side).44 The observed negative signal is ascribed to the ground state bleach signal, originated due to the state filling of the photo-generated charge carriers in the valence band and conduction band edges.26,29,45 Stimulated emissions of the photo-induced excitons are also known to make a small contribution towards the negative bleach signal.46 Although the spectral shape of the ground state bleach signal of the NCs remains same, the OD value (negative value) decreases in the presence of the quenchers (the TA spectra of the NCâ€“BQ and NCâ€“DQ composites are given in ESI,â€  Fig. S9). The magnitude of Î”A of the ground state bleach signal at a particular delay time is proportional to the photoexcited carrier density at the band edge level.20,47 Hence, this decrease in the OD value suggests that the charge carriers in the band edge level of the NCs are involved in an additional deactivation channel in presence of the quenchers, along with their normal deactivation process. The recovery of the ground state bleach signal of the NCs in presence of each of the three quenchers becomes faster, which confirms the excited state charge transfer interaction between the NCs with the three quenchers. However, the bleach signal recovers at a faster rate in the case of CA compared with the other two quenchers. This indicates that charge transfer from the photoexcited NCs occurs at a faster rate to CA than to BQ and DQ.

Then, to obtain quantitative information on the charge carrier time constants at the interface of the NCsâ€“quencher systems, the ground state bleach recovery positioned at 505 nm was monitored and the corresponding bleach recovery kinetics were analysed. Fig. 7Aâ€“C represents the ground state bleach recovery kinetics of the NCs in absence and presence of CA, BQ, and DQ, respectively. It is evident that the ground state bleach signal recovers at a faster rate in the presence of each of the three quenchers compared with the pristine NCs. All the bleach recovery kinetics were fitted using a multi-exponential function, and the fitting parameters are given in Table 1. The kinetics of the ground state bleach signal are characterized by a growth (rise) component and two decay components. The growth time of the perovskite NCs mainly arises due to thermalisation of the hot charge carriers in the band edge level.47,48 Therefore, the time constant of 894 Â± 28 fs for the pristine NCs is assigned for hot electron relaxation to the band edge level. In presence of the quenchers, the observed growth time decreases, suggesting hot carrier transfer from the NCs to the acceptors, which is in agreement considering the fact that a 350 nm laser pulse was used for the sample excitation, which is much above the band edge excitation energy (the band gap, Eg, is 2.41 eV for the CsPbBr3 NCs; ESI,â€  Fig. S10). Here, it should be noted that although the rise time decreases in the presence of the quenchers, the changes are within the error bars, which indicates a negligible amount of hot electron transfer from the NCs to the acceptors (ESI,â€  Fig. S11). This may appear to be contradictory since excitons much above the band edge are generated by the 350 nm excitation pulse. However, a recent study has shown that CsPbBr3 NCs have discrete localized and continuous trap states beyond the band edge49 which can trap the hot excitons and prevent their transfer to the acceptors.

Of the two components in the bleach recovery decay kinetics, the longer component arises due to either (i) band edge electronâ€“hole recombination in the NCs or (ii) back electron transfer from the acceptor to NC.50,51 In the absence of an acceptor, the longer lifetime component (1.8 ns) is attributed to the band edge electronâ€“hole recombination. The value of the time constant of the longer component decreases in the presence of the acceptors, which reflects band edge electron transfer from the NCs to the acceptors. The values of the time constants given in Table 1 suggest that the band edge electrons are transferred to CA at the highest rate followed by BQ and DQ, respectively. The shorter decay component in the pristine NCs (âˆ¼42 ps) originates due to trapping of the electrons into the defect states of the nanocrystals. The time constant value of the shorter decay component decreases in presence of each of the three acceptors, suggesting trap state mediated electron transfer from the NCs to the acceptors.

It is to be noted that the maximum delay time in the femtosecond experimental set up is 5 ns, which brings an inherent limitation in estimating the larger decay components above 1 ns, and this leads to the apparent difference between the time constants for band edge electronâ€“hole recombination from time-resolved fluorescence decay and TA measurements.

Besides the values of the time constants, their amplitudes are also noteworthy in this context. As given in Table 1, the amplitude of the faster and slower decay components increases and decreases, respectively, in presence of the quencher molecules. This observation suggests that the quencher molecules introduce more surface defect states into the NCs by interacting with the surface ligands, as shown in Fig. 4. The possibility of the formation of deep trap states in the NCs by quencher molecules is eliminated because the deep trap states are formed due to antisite defects (when Brâˆ’ occupies the position of Pb2+ or vice versa) during the synthesis of the NCs, and the quencher molecules do not enter into the crystal structure to modify the lattice points (as evidenced by the TEM image of the CsPbBr3â€“CA composite in ESI,â€  Fig. S5).

The density of the defect states induced by CA is more than for the other two quenchers. This finding supports the results obtained from the PL lifetime quenching study and the FTIR spectra of the NCâ€“acceptor composites with respect to pristine NCs, as discussed in the earlier section. The introduction of defect states into the NCs by loosening the NC surface protective ligands, although it helps charge transfer from the NCs to acceptors, it comes at a cost to the stability of the NCs. Hence, it is always desirable to design an acceptor molecule that does not create trap states on the NC surfaces, which decreases the stability of the NCs.

In the present study, as shown in Fig. 5, the driving force is observed to be maximum for CA, followed by BQ and DQ, respectively, consistent with the observed electron transfer time constants considering in the Marcus normal region (ESI,â€  Fig. S12). This order of the electron transfer rate from the NCs to the three separate quenchers can also be explained by the electronic effects of the substituents present in the quencher molecules. Upon accepting one electron, the p-benzoquinone moiety forms the resonating structure (II) shown in Fig. 8, where the Ï€-electron density is delocalised over the ring. When the substituent groups (â€“Ri) are electron withdrawing in nature, the anionic form becomes more stable. In CA, two mutually para chlorine atoms (Cl) are present along with two hydroxyl (â€“OH) groups in the quinone moiety. The inductive effect (âˆ’I) of the electronegative chlorine atoms makes the quinoid ring electrophilic in nature. Thus, it can efficiently extract the electrons from the NCs. By contrast, DQ has four methyl (â€“CH3) groups that can release electron density towards the quinoid ring, leaving the ring less susceptible towards accepting more electron density. Therefore, the rate of electron transfer was the highest for CA and the lowest for DQ, with BQ in the middle.

3. Conclusion

In summary, the detailed charge transfer dynamics between CsPbBr3 NCs and three electronically different small acceptor molecules (namely, CA, BQ, and DQ) have been studied using a set of steady state and time-resolved spectroscopic techniques. The steady state absorption and time-resolved emission experiments confirmed that the CsPbBr3 NCs interact with the quencher molecules in the excited state, although hydrogen-bonding interactions exist between the surface ligands of the NCs and the quencher molecules. These H-bonding-type interactions tend to detach the surface ligands of the NCs. Furthermore, in femtosecond transient absorption, the faster ground state bleach recovery of the NCs in presence of each of the three quenchers confirmed the electron transfer from the photoexcited NCs to the quenchers, which was again supported by the band alignment analysis of all the components. The rate of electron transfer calculated from the Marcus theory is highest for CA, then BQ, and the lowest for DQ, and the trend of this electron transfer rate was explained using the electronic effects of the substituents present in the acceptor moieties. Although the rate of electron transfer is the highest in the presence of CA as it has the lowest lying LUMO, but the electronegative â€“OH group is involved in H-bonding interactions with the NCs surface ligands which affects the stability of the NCs. We believe that this study will help to identify suitable charge transporting materials depending on their core structure and different functionalities for perovskite based solar cells, which can improve the efficiency and the cost-effectiveness of the solar cell without compromising the stability of the NCs.
