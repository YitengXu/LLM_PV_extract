An overview on the current understanding of the photophysical properties of metal nanoclusters and their potential applications

Abstract

Photophysics of atomically precise metal nanoclusters (MNCs) is an emerging area of research due to their potential applications in optoelectronics, photovoltaics, sensing, bio-imaging and catalysis. An overview of the recent advances in the photophysical properties of MNCs is presented in this review. To begin with, we illustrate general synthesis methodologies of MNCs using direct reduction, chemical etching, ligand exchange, metal exchange and intercluster reaction. Due to strong quantum confinement, the NCs possess unique electronic properties such as discrete optical absorption, intense photoluminescence (PL), molecular-like electron dynamics and non-linear optical behavior. Discussions have also been carried out to unveil the influence of the core size, nature of ligands, heteroatom doping, and surrounding environments on the optical absorption and photophysical properties of metal clusters. Recent findings reveal that the excited-state dynamics, nonlinear optical properties and aggregation induced emission of metal clusters offer exciting opportunities for potential applications. We discuss briefly about their versatile applications in optoelectronics, sensing, catalysis and bio-imaging. Finally, the future perspective of this research field is given.

1. Introduction

Atomically precise metal nanoclusters have recently emerged as a new class of functional materials because of their unique photophysical properties which differ from those of metal nanoparticles.17 Metal clusters consist of a few to several hundreds of metal atoms with a diameter of <2 nm and they are in between the atomic regime and plasmonic metal nanoparticles (MNPs).1,6,8 As the size approaches the Fermi wavelength of metals (<1 nm), the quantum size effect originates in the NCs which culminates molecular like behavior such as discrete HOMOLUMO gaps, intense luminescence, unusual redox behavior, chirality, and intrinsic magnetism.1,914 In MNCs, the free electrons of metal atoms do not collectively oscillate in the presence of an electric field like metal nanoparticles.8,15 They exhibit distinct step-like absorption bands and intense photoluminescence (PL) due to quantized energy levels.8,9,16 Thus, a fundamental understanding of the origin of the photophysical properties of metal clusters is an important topic of research.10,1719 Due to superior resistance towards oxidation, gold has become the centre of attention in the synthesis of nanoscale particles.2022 In 1857, Michael Faraday synthesized a ruby red colored gold colloid via reduction of a gold salt with white phosphorus in a biphasic system.23 Mie solved Maxwell's equation to understand the interaction of light with a single gold NP and developed the electronic band theory of metals in 1960 for understanding the nature of electronic transitions in gold and other MNPs.24,25 The monodispersity of particles is an important concern in nanoscience and eventually controls the photophysical properties. Brust et al. have reported the synthesis of thiol protected gold nanoparticles in a two phase synthesis which is a seminal work in nanoscience.22,26 This synthesis includes the phase transfer of the gold(III) salt by the reduction of Au(III) to Au(I) and then further reduction of Au(I) to Au(0) is caused by NaBH4 to form Aun(SR)m where tetraoctylammonium bromide (TOABr) is used as a phase transfer agent.22,26 The extremely stable thiolate protected NPs can be stored for a long time in powder form that can be re-dissolved in a solvent.22,27 Generally, NPs are synthesized using thiol ligands such as long chain alkylthiols,27 arenethiols,28 and -substituted alkylthiols.29 Whetten and coworkers have reported sub-2 nm-sized Au NPs with a thiol:Au ratio of 3:1 and their synthesis method is now a universal method for thiolated Au NC synthesis.30 Magic sized Au NCs such as Au25(SR)18, Au38(SR)24, Au102(SR)44, and Au144(SR)60 NCs have been reported with their thermodynamic stability.3135 Later on, peptides, proteins, DNAs, amino acids and small water soluble ligands have been employed as capping ligands in aqueous phase synthesis of NCs.11,21,3640 Despite the lowered stability in the reduced state, adequate attention has been given to synthesize monodisperse Ag and Cu NCs owing to their higher abundance and better cost effectiveness.41,42 The photophysics of the particles becomes distinct with the diameter approaching 2 nm, inducing magnificent optical behavior such as intense PL, discrete electronic transitions, molecular-like relaxation of the excited state and two photon absorption and fluorescence in MNCs.16,43,44

The atomically precise composition of MNCs enables the investigation on the structureproperty relationship upon light irradiation. Among the other optical properties, PL is most important which empowers the NCs as a potential candidate for optoelectronic,45,46 photocatalytic,47,48 bio-labeling7,49 and sensing18,5052 applications. Traditional luminescent materials such as semiconductor quantum dots (QDs), carbon dots, organic fluorophores, and perovskite nanocrystals often have solubility and toxicity issues.3,53 In contrast, atomically precise NCs provide superior bio-compatibility, diverse solubility and good photostability.1,2 The tunable PL of metal clusters all over the visible and NIR region by controlling the core size and the nature of ligands has been reported.10,16,18,54 The PL mechanism is found to be controlled by metalligand interface chemistry.12,55 The applications of the structurally precise NCs are restricted because of their low QYs.3 Remarkable emphasis has been placed on controlling the photoemission of NCs and improving the QYs through selective control over the protecting ligand,5658 heteroatom doping,5961 temperature,62,63 solvent64,65 and pH.66 Now-a-days, aggregation induced emission (AIE) is an emerging method to enhance the PL emission.46,64,67,68 The fundamental understanding of excited state carrier dynamics of MNCs is very important for practical applications. Recently, several spectroscopic techniques have been used to understand the sharp transition from the metallic state of NPs to the excitonic state of NCs, the molecular-like internal conversion (IC), intersystem crossing (ISC), fluorescence and phosphorescence.17,6971

The present review focuses on the photophysical behavior of atomically precise MNCs (M = Au, Ag and Cu). Firstly, we describe different synthetic methods such as direct reduction, chemical etching, ligand exchange, metal exchange and intercluster reaction for metal cluster synthesis. The basic understanding of photoinduced phenomena in NCs such as optical absorption, PL, ultrafast excited state relaxation dynamics and non-linear optical properties is discussed. The governing factors such as the core size, capping ligand, heterometal atom, temperature, pH and aggregation or self-assembly of NCs for the photophysical properties of metal clusters will be addressed. Finally, the optoelectronics, sensing, catalysis and biological applications of NCs are highlighted. We may have missed out some excellent studies from the finest scientists due to our limited knowledge. In a nutshell, the structurally correlated optical behavior and optical property based applications of atomically precise Au, Ag and Cu NCs are highlighted (Fig. 1).

2. Synthesis of metal nanoclusters

Mono-dispersity is the main concern in research on NCs as they are often considered as nanomolecules having distinct molecular properties. Several synthetic methodologies have been developed to obtain a well-defined composition of NCs with rational tuning of chemical and physical parameters. Advanced characterization methods such as laser desorption ionization (LDI),30 matrix assisted laser desorption ionization (MALDI),11,34,72 electrospray ionization (ESI) mass spectrometry (MS),20,21,34 X-ray crystallography9,73 and post-synthetic separation techniques such as size exclusion chromatography (SEC)74 and polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE)21,75 are being used for determining the composition and structures of the clusters. Using the LDI-MS technique, Schaaff and coworkers have characterized a series of clusters with core masses of 2928k (1.7 nm, 146 atoms), 2322k (1.5 nm, 101 atoms), 1514k (1.3 nm, 75 atoms) and 8k (1.1 nm, 38 atoms) where k is 1000 atomic mass unit (amu).30 It is noted that the 8k species exhibited a distinct step-like absorption feature due to a strong quantum effect. Other methods such as softer ionization based electrospray ionization (ESI) and matrix assisted laser desorption ionization (MALDI) mass spectrometry are being used to characterize the metal clusters. A hydrophilic tripeptide glutathione (GSH) was used to synthesize Au NCs in aqueous medium where the sterically hindered GSH controls the size of NCs.76 Tsukuda and coworkers have characterized a series of GSH and homoglutathione (h-GSH) protected Aun clusters (n = 1039) using ESI MS and PAGE (Fig. 2).21 It is evident from PAGE that GSH protected NCs are better separated than h-GSH capped Au NCs (Fig. 2A). The nine different bands (19) in PAGE were identified using ESI-MS along with their corresponding isotopic distribution (Fig. 2B). The crude product contains a number of magic sized NCs ranging from Au10(SG)10 to Au39(SG)24. The number of GS-ligands vs. Au atoms is plotted in Fig. 2C and the stability of each NC is dependent on the valence electron count and electron shell closely associated with the specific composition. The fractionated clusters are found to be molecular in nature and exhibit size dependent optical characteristics. Major emphasis has been placed on thiolate protected Au NC synthesis and other Ag, Cu clusters etc. We will shed some light upon the synthesis of size specific NCs via different methodologies.

2.1. Direct reduction

The direct reduction method has been successfully employed to obtain Au, Ag, Cu and alloy NCs in both organic and aqueous media. The synthetic process includes rapid reductive growth of intermediate NCs and slow size focusing to monodisperse NCs in a NaBH4 reduction.77 With the advancement of characterization tools such as ESI-MS, MALDI-MS and X-ray crystallography, the atom by atom customization was made possible by careful modulation of each reaction parameter.78 Jin et al. have controlled the kinetics of the reduction reaction which led to the exclusive formation of phenylethanethiol (PET) capped Au25 through a two-step process. First Au(III) is reduced to Au(I) by thiols to form Au(I):SG intermediates and further reduction with NaBH4 turns Au(I) to Au(0) (Fig. 3A). The kinetics of Au(I):SG intermediate formation is critical towards monodispersity and controlled by maintaining very low temperature (0 C).31 As an indispensible part of a NC, the protecting ligand exerts a massive impact toward dictating the atomic precision.79 In a one pot system, Au144(SR)60 NCs with different thiol ligands were obtained by subtle tuning of the Au to thiol ratio (4), the solvent (methanol), and the presence of O2 and tetraoctylammonium halide.35 The NC synthesis via direct reduction is very sensitive towards pH,80,81 the reducing agent,81,82 temperature,83 and the metal to thiol ratio.82 Instead of NaBH4, other reducing agents such as hydrazine hydrate,81 CO80,82 and ascorbic acid84 have been employed in bottom up NC synthesis. Water soluble Au25 with cysteine as a capping ligand is obtained using CO as a reducing agent.82 The reaction proceeds through the reduction of thiolateAu(I) complexes to Au1015 NCs, the growth of NCs from Au1015 to Au1625 NCs and the size-focusing of Au1625 NCs to Au25 NCs. Recently, Chen et al. have rationalized the use of stoichiometric NaBH4 to obtain Au25(SG)18 NCs and predicted the balanced equation as follows: 32/x [Au(SR)]x + 8e = [Au25(SR)18] + 7 [Au(SR)2] (SR = thiolate ligand). Only 8 electrons (supplied by the reducing agent) are sufficient to react with 32 Au atoms to form pure Au25(SR)18 NCs.85 Moreover, in a mild and well controlled reduction reaction, the synthesis of NCs with different nuclearity is monitored by pH. Tuning of NCs, Au1012, Au15, Au18 and Au25 NCs was achieved by changing the reaction pH to 7, 9, 10 and 11, respectively.80 Xie and coworkers have investigated the role of NaOH in achieving monodispersity in the synthesis of mono-, bi- and tri-thiolate protected Au25 NCs.40 Temperature has a profound effect in deciding the composition of NCs in the direct synthesis process. In a typical synthesis of Au25(MHA)18 NCs (MHA = mercaptohexanoic acid), 40 C is found to be optimum to achieve monodispersity, whereas the temperature more than 40 C leads to mixed sized NCs.83 Xie and coworkers first reported bovine serum albumin (BSA) protein template magic sized Au25 NCs in a single step.11 BSA acts as both a protecting ligand and a reducing agent as tyrosine in BSA reduces Au(III) in basic pH (>10) and cysteine binds to Au atoms via the SH group. The same group later developed a scalable synthesis of GSH protected Ag, Cu and Pt NCs through a phase transfer cycle (aqueous to organic to aqueous). The growth of NCs is controlled by the mild etching ability of the phase transfer agent. The as-prepared luminescent NCs in organic medium are again transferred to aqueous medium (Fig. 3B).86

2.2. Chemical etching

Though one pot size focusing has emerged rapidly to achieve monodisperse MNCs, the complementary chemical etching is not far behind. Etching of NPs is presumably a process involving the removal of metal atoms from the outermost layer of an NP or NC core.96 The atomic structure and the electronic behavior experience a drastic change when the size of NPs is reduced to the sub-2 nm regime. In 2007, a ligand induced core size reduction i.e. core etching of gold nanocrystals to obtain a coordinating multivalent polymer was documented, PEI protected Au8 NCs with blue luminescence.19 Gold atoms from larger NCs can be removed sequentially to populate stable Aun NCs with lower nuclearity. The process is thermodynamically favorable as a series of polydisperse particles are converted to magic sized NCs. Excess ligand directed etching of a series of GSH protected Aun(SG)m clusters where [n, m] = [10, 10], [15, 13], [18, 14], [22, 16], [25, 18], [29, 20] and [33, 22] yielded Au25(SG)18 with n > 25 under aerobic conditions. The clusters with n < 25 are decomposed to Au(I)SG complexes, whereas Au25(SG)18 remains unaffected due to its extraordinary stability.97 pH dependent etching of mercaptosuccinic acid (MSA) protected Au NPs with GSH yields two different NCs, Au8 at pH 78 and Au25 at pH 3.98 Citrate capped charge-stabilized silver NPs are converted to red luminescent Ag NCs owing to the higher binding affinity of the thiol with Ag.99 Not only spherical nanoparticles but also the nanorods can be chopped down chemically with an excess ligand to sub-2 nm NCs.100 Au8 was synthesized from Au NPs and NRs with biomolecule (amino acid, protein, peptide and DNA) assisted etching.

Tsukuda and coworkers reported a synthesis of 8 kDa [Au38(SCn)24]z and 29 kDa [Au144(SCn)60]z (where z = charge states) through alkanethiol induced core etching.20 The Jin group reported a bulk scale preparative method of Au38(PET)24 NCs in a two phase ligand exchange cum core etching of Au:SG clusters.32 The yield was improved up to 25% by employing acetone as the solvent which regulates the complexation of Au with thiol (Fig. 5A). Polydispersed Aun (n = 38102) protected with GSH was used as a precursor for phenylethanethiol directed core etching in toluene at 80 C which yielded a truly monodisperse Au38(PET)24 as is evident from MALDI mass spectrometry (Fig. 5B).33 Au130(PET)50 NCs are found to be present during the synthesis of Au144(PET)60 clusters which is evident from the mass spectrometric study. Dass et al. have reported the synthesis of atomically pure Au130(SR)50 NCs from polydisperse Au NCs in excess ligand induced etching. The scalable synthesis also enables furnishing alloy Au130xMx(SR)50 (M = Ag, Pd and R = CH2CH2Ph, C6H13 and C12H25) NCs.101 The functional groups and their positions in the ligand are found to control the nuclearity in NCs. Using the core etching reaction with an excess ligand, a mixed sized product of Aun(MBT)m (MBT = methylbenzenethiol) was converted to highly monodisperse Au NCs (Fig. 5C). Depending on the position of CH3 in the benzene ring, the magic sized NCs vary from Au130(p-MBT)50 to Au104(m-MBT)41 to Au40(o-MBT)24. The subtle change in the CH3 position has a strong influence on the synthesis of the magic sized NCs due to a steric effect.72 GSH capped mixed sized Au NCs underwent a long time size reduction and yielded stable NCs in the presence of a small thiol ligand, mercaptopropionic acid.18 The number of Au atoms in NCs reduced from 15 to 12 to 6 with the progress of the ligand exchange cum core etching reaction. The size evolution is evident from TEM images as the average diameter of the NCs is decreased from 1.9 nm to 1.7 nm to 1.4 nm (Fig. 6A). A process of cyclic reductiondecompositionreduction gave rise to Ag:SG intermediates which were converted to red and green emitting Ag NCs.102 The red (700, 720 nm), yellow (570 nm) and blue (450 nm) emitting silver NCs are synthesized by mono- or biphasic etching.36,103

2.3. Ligand exchange

Ligand exchange of a pre-synthesized NC has become a powerful method to modulate the structure and properties of NCs because the protecting ligands have a significant influence on the nuclearity, geometry, bonding and electronic transitions. Binding of the new incoming ligand can have an influence on the core size, geometry, solubility, and PL behavior because of the modification of the electronic interactions. Chiral Au38(PET)24 NCs undergo complete ligand exchange with 4-tert-butylbenzenethiol (TBBT) at 80 C for more than 12 hours to form an entirely different FCC structured, achiral Au36(TBBT)24 NC.107 Later, Jin and coworkers proposed a mechanism for the ligand exchange reaction.108

Rod like Au38(PET)24 becomes structurally distorted and releases two Au atoms when bulkier TBBT approaches the cluster. In a dis-proportionation reaction, Au38(PET)24 NCs are converted to tetrahedral Au36(PET)24 and Au40(SR)24 (SR includes both PET and TBBT here) NCs (Fig. 7A). Au40(SR)24 NCs are transformed into extremely stable Au36(TBBT)24 in the presence of TBBT.108 Ligand exchange generally yields low nuclearity NCs with a fully reorganized surface environment. For example, Au20(TBBT)16 from Au25(PET)18 and Au133(TBBT)52 from Au144(PET)60 NCs were synthesized in the ligand exchange cum core conversion reaction with the TBBT ligand under different reaction conditions.109,110 On the other hand, the nuclearity of NCs increases when phosphine protected Au11 NCs undergo ligand exchange with GSH to form thermodynamically stable Au25(SG)18 NCs in aqueous medium.111 The increase in the core size is evident from the attainment of Au28(TBBT)20 from Au25(PET)18(TOA) and Au24(TBBT)20 from Au23(SC6H11)16(TOA) NCs.13,112 Several groups have used the ligand exchange assisted size focusing strategy to obtain a stable one sized NC from a mixture of NCs with a delicate control over the reaction kinetics.113,114 Thermo-chemical treatment of polydisperse Aun(SG)m NCs with excess PET and TBBT yields stable Au38(PET)24, Au36(TBBT)24 and Au30(TBBT)18 NCs.114 Single [Ag59(2,5-DCBT)32]3 NC (2,5-DCBT = 2,5-dichlorobenzenethiol) leads to three different Ag NCs as Ag44, Ag29, and Ag25 NCs upon exchange reaction with diverse thiol ligands.115 The hollow structure of [Ag44(4-FTP)30]4 (FTP = 4-fluorothiophenol) is converted to a non-hollow [Ag25(2,4-DMBT)18]via a disproportionation mechanism. The reverse reaction between [Ag25(2,4-DMBT)18] and 4-FTP results in [Ag44(4-FTP)30]4 by dimerization followed by a rearrangement reaction.116 The versatility of the ligand exchange strategy made it possible to synthesize bimetallic NCs through the selective replacement of SMS- motifs.117,118 The surface motif exchange (SME) in the [Ag44(SR)30]4 cluster results in well-defined coreshell [Ag32@Au12(SR)30]4 NCs.118 Pre-synthesized Pt1Ag24(DMBT)18 undergoes SME with adamentanethiol (HS-Adm) and PPh3 to form Pt1Ag28(S-Adm)18(PPh3)4 having a Ag4(SR)6(PPh3)1 surface motif encoated with the FCC kernel of Pt1Ag12.119 Ligand exchange may turn out to be an alternative, where the direct reduction fails to yield single sized doped NCs. For example, [PtAg28(BDT)12(TPP)4]4 (BDT = benzenedithiol and TPP = triphenylphosphine) is obtained from a ligand exchange reaction with Pt2Ag23Cl7(TPP)10 and BDTH2, whereas the direct bottom up synthesis method results in a mixture of [Ag29(BDT)12(TPP)4]3 and [PtAg28(BDT)12(TPP)4]4 NCs (Fig. 7B).120 The formation of single sized doped NCs was confirmed by ESI-MS (Fig. 7C). The GSH exchange reaction on the surface of N-acetylcysteine capped Au NCs is assisted by foreign silver atoms.121 Zhu et al. have developed an in situ ligand exchange strategy to obtain Au20Ag1(TBBT)15, Au21xAgx(TBBT)15 (x = 48), Au21xCux(TBBT)15 (x = 0, 1), and Au21xCux(TBBT)15 (x = 25) in a very short time which paves the way for reducing the time of the ligand exchange reaction.122 In spite of being a time consuming process, the ligand exchange methodology is found to be an efficient method to synthesize monodisperse homo- and heterometallic NCs.

2.4. Metal exchange

Metal exchange in a pre-synthesized NC or NP results in monodisperse doped NCs or a series of alloy NCs with different numbers of heteroatom doping. The number of dopant atoms, doping sites and the distribution in NCs are the key factors that control their luminescence, stability and catalytic activity.123 Metal exchange in atomically precise NCs is employed to obtain a number of structurally versatile alloy NCs.124 The galvanic replacement method based on the electrochemical potential difference of metals is a classical method of metal exchange. In this exchange process, M1 becomes oxidized with simultaneous reduction of M2 if M1 has a lower reduction potential than M2. Being reduced, M2 is accumulated on the surface of the M1 core resulting in an alloy NC.124 The process is schematically represented in Fig. 8A. Crown like Pd147 acts as a template in a galvanic exchange reaction where Au atoms are decorated in the corners of the crown forming a crown-jewel like structure (Fig. 8B) The controlled replacement of Pd with Au atoms in the crown-like Pd NCs is made possible due to the reduction potential difference of AuCl4/Au (0.99 V) and Pd2+/Pd (0.95 V) and the surface energy difference of Pd atoms in the top, edge and face positions of the crown.125 Pradeep's group has demonstrated the formation of 13 atom AuAg alloy NCs in a galvanic exchange of Ag7,8 NCs with AuSR complexes.126 Though the deciding factors for the composition are still not very clear, a number of alloy clusters protected with proteins,127 peptides128 and thiols106 have been synthesized through the redox reaction assisted exchange process.

The classical rule of oxidationreduction based on the reduction potential fails below the 2 nm size regime owing to the strong quantum confinement effect. Then metal exchange does not comply with the metal activity sequence (i.e. Fe > Cd > Co > Ni > Pb > Cu > Hg > Ag > Pd > Pt > Au) and instead the exchange reactions in NCs are related to the structural stability and electron shell closing (Fig. 8A).129 In an early study, Negishi et al. have reported Pd doping in stable dodecanethiol (DT) protected Au25 to form Pd1Au24(DT)18 with higher stability.130 A succeeding report revealed two Pd atom doping in Au38(PET)24 to form Pd2Au36(PET)24 NCs.131 The more reactive Pd doped in thermodynamically stable Au25 and Au38 NCs goes through antigalvanic exchange. Antigalvanic exchange is a process of more reactive metal atom doping in NCs and is as effective as galvanic exchange to tailor the structure of doped NCs.123,132 In a typical experiment, a set of metal atoms (Cu, Ag, Cd, Hg etc.) with higher reactivity than Au was incorporated into Au25(SR)18 maintaining the core size of NCs.133 The MALDI mass spectra of the NCs after metal exchange in Au25 NCs reveal different metal atom incorporation for Ag, Cu, Cd and Hg, depending on the electron count and structural stability of doped NCs (Fig. 8C). It revealed that all the surface metal atoms are not exchangeable.138 The metal exchange reaction enabled the achievement of atomically pure doped NCs with diverse novel properties.

3. Photophysical properties of nanoclusters

Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) is the most prominent feature in UV-Vis absorption spectra in noble metal nanoparticles.15 The free electron model explains the electronic behavior of gold, silver and copper NPs quite well.8 Owing to the collective oscillation of free electrons in such a large number of metal atoms, SPR arises in nanoparticles. In contrast, when the size of NPs approaches the Fermi wavelength of the metal, the collective oscillation of free electrons reduces dramatically. The continuous band structure splits into discrete energy states and the free electrons are confined into those states. For small particles, where the number of particles is finite, the spacing of electronic levels becomes reasonable and inversely related to the size of the particle.8,146 MNCs comprise a class of tiny NPs which are a collection of a few metal atoms protected with a monolayer of a capping ligand. The unusual and molecular like photophysical behavior is our focus and will be discussed in the view of optical absorption, PL, ultrafast carrier dynamics of excited state and non-linear optical properties (Fig. 10).

3.1. Optical absorption

The correlation of the structure and optical properties in MNCs has emerged as a contemporary research topic in nanoparticle chemistry. The optical response of MNPs is dominated by the collective oscillation of free electrons which gives rise to an SPR band.8 On the other hand, the size reduction in NCs results in single electron transitions within the discrete energy levels which in turn display step like absorption spectra in NCs. Being an indispensible part of the NCs, each and every metal atom contributes towards the optical response. Atomically pure Au144(PET)60 NCs display absorption bands at 517 nm and 700 nm, whereas the size reduction to Au25(PET)18 NCs gives rise to different absorption bands altogether.35 The Au25(PET)18 NCs show distinct absorption bands at 400, 450 and 670 nm. The spacing between the discrete energy levels increases as the size of the particles decreases causing a blue shift in the absorption band.8 The size and optical absorption relationship is followed in a series of pepsin templated Au NCs.38 The core size reduction of Au NCs during the etching reaction results in the shifting of absorption bands from 410 nm to 386 nm.18 As the surface protecting ligands largely contribute towards the electronic structure of NCs, there is no linear relationship between the size and optical band gap.147 The nature of optical transitions in MNCs remained elusive until the crystal structures were reported. In 2008, the crystal structure of [Au25(SR)18] with anionic charge and with TOA+ counter-ions has been reported and a stable optimized structure was obtained from density functional theory (DFT).9,73,148 Zhu et al. reported the crystal structure of [Au25(SR)18][TOA] and time dependent DFT (TD-DFT) calculations correlated the optical absorption peaks with electronic transitions (Fig. 11).9 The X-ray crystallographic study revealed that the Au25 NC is composed of a centered icosahedral Au13 core capped with an exterior shell of 12 Au atoms and 18 thiolate ligands. All thiolates are attached to the Au atoms via bridging binding mode instead of terminal binding mode. Each of the six AuAu pairs is bridged by an SR ligand and the remaining two SR ligands are attached to exterior Au and the icosahedral core (Fig. 11A). The NCs show well-defined bands at 1.8, 2.75 and 3.1 eV in the UV-vis absorption spectrum (Fig. 11B).

The calculated peaks lie in the red region of the experimental absorption peaks (Fig. 11D). The electron donation from CH2CH2Ph causes more negative orbital energies leading to an increase in the HOMOLUMO gap and blue shifting of absorption peaks. Anionic [Au25(SR)18] is highly stable due to its structural robustness and the eight electron shell of delocalized Au (6s) electrons.9 Taking into account the spinorbit coupling, the twin peaks at 1.67 eV and 1.90 eV in the absorption spectrum of Au25 can be explained as the triply degenerate HOMO orbitals split with a 0.2 eV energy difference.149 The HOMOLUMO positions of [Au25(SCH2CH2PhX)18] NCs were tuned by varying the substituent (X = H, F, Cl, Br, CH3, OCH3) in the para position of the aromatic ring of PET.150 The electronic absorption of Au25(SR)18 and Au38(SR)24 NCs was found to be dependent on temperature due to different electronphonon and excitonphonon interactions.151 The investigation revealed the appearance of vibronic feature, sharpening of peaks, shifting to higher energies and an increase in oscillator strength with the lowering in temperature.151

From X-ray crystallographic study, the structure of [Au23(SR)16] NCs is found to be made of a cuboctahedron-based Au15 kernel, two trimeric Au3(SR)4 staple motifs on the top and bottom and two Au(SR)2 staples on the front and back (Fig. 12A).152 DFT calculations with a model [Au23(SCH3)16] NC predict the absorption band at 530 nm which reasonably agrees with the experimental absorption peak at 570 nm (Fig. 12B and C). 3.2. Photoluminescence

Photoluminescence is one of the most attractive properties of nanomaterials due to the scope in diverse applications. In contrast to the traditional photoluminescent materials such as organic dyes, semiconductor QDs, and perovskite QDs, metal NCs exhibit extreme photostablity, superior biocompatibility and tunable emission.16 As the size approaches the Fermi wavelength of electrons, the electronic transitions in NCs become completely different from those in plasmonic NPs. The luminescence emerging from sub-2 nm sized NCs becomes the centre of attention due to its tunable nature over a wide range of the visible region.10,18 Owing to their precise structure, the influence of the core size and protecting ligand has been closely investigated in recent times. Moreover, the valence electron count, oxidation state of the metal, crystal structure, temperature, and pH are crucial to control the PL of NCs. Although the exact mechanism is yet to be fully established, we will highlight the major factors which regulate the PL behavior (Fig. 10).

3.2.2. Influence of ligands. It is seen that the protecting ligand influences the PL of NCs because of the electronic interactions. A variety of ligands such as long chain alkylthiols, phenylalkanethiols, peptides and proteins behaved similarly toward dictating the composition and structure of Au NCs (Au25(SR)18).11,31,40,97 Thus, the effect of the ligand on the optical properties of NCs has not been emphasized. With the revelation of different PL behavior in similar sized NCs, the effect of functional groups in capping ligands has been realized based on the electronic transitions of NCs.157,158 The emission wavelength varies from 700 nm to 1300 nm depending on the core atoms of Au clusters from 11 to 201 with different ligands.157 The participation of ligand to metal charge transfer towards the generation of PL was first evidenced in Au28(SG)16 NCs.156 Later, the composition was unambiguously assigned to Au25(SG)18 using the mass spectrometry and crystallographic study.9,21 Due to the lack of a suitable detector, the NIR emission of many Au NCs was not understood previously. Structurally precise Au25(SG)18 NCs exhibit both visible (800 nm) and NIR (1100 nm) luminescence owing to the relaxation from their excited state through fluorescence and phosphorescence with substantial contribution from the ligand shell.156 Whetten and coworkers used two different detectors for visible and IR (4001550 nm) luminescence measurements. The first setup includes a thermoelectric coupled CCD camera gated with a programmable pulser. The other luminescence detector covering the infrared region was a Nicolet Fourier transform Raman spectrometer coupled with an InGaAs detector.

The composition as well as the electronic behavior of alloy NCs can be engineered precisely.123,133 Bootharaju et al. reported an enhancement (25 fold) of PL intensity in doped AuAg24(DMBT)18 compared to undoped Ag25 NCs.59 A series of Ag atoms are incorporated into rod shaped Au25 NCs which unveils that the position of the Ag atom influences the photophysical properties.169 Alloying up to 12 Ag atoms (AgxAu25x, x = 112) does not affect the PL significantly, whereas incorporation of 13 Ag atoms drastically increases the PL QY 200 times. In the alloy Ag13Au12 NCs, the 13th Ag atom occupies the central position of biicosaheral Ag13Au12 NCs and the HOMO and LUMO position is modified. The strong sd hybridization in Au25 NCs weakens in Ag13Au12 NCs leading to higher electron mobility with faster electron hole recombination.169 Wu et al. investigated the PL of a series of doped MAg24 (M = Pd, Pt, Au and Ag) NCs with the dopant atom occupying the central position of the metal core in both crystal and solution phases.65 A blue shift of the PL band (1042 nm) of Ag25(DMBT)18 NCs is observed upon doping. 3.2.4. Influence of the surrounding environment. Apart from the core size and protecting ligand, the PL behavior of NCs is altered by different external factors such as temperature, solvent, pH etc. Strong temperature dependent emission of Au25(PET)18 and Au38(DT)24 arises due to the charge transfer from the ligand to metal core.174 The emission maxima of buckyball like Ag180 NCs shifts from 723 nm to 623 nm and enhances the PL intensity with lowering of the temperature from 293 K to 93 K.62 Phosphine and thiolate co-protected Ag18 NCs display red emission (690 nm) at 298 K while dual emission at 585 nm and 690 nm is observed at 88 K (Fig. 20A).175 The microsecond and nanosecond scale lifetimes indicate two different emissive states; one from spin forbidden triplet phosphorescence and the other one for LMCT from the S or P 3p to Ag 5s orbital, respectively. The enhancement of the lifetime with the decrease in temperature indicates a decrease in the non-radiative decay pathways of excited states and consequently an increase in the emission intensity.175 Ag12 NCs with a naphthalene-based thiol ligand also exhibit temperature responsive dual emission.63 The thermochromism of NCs is essentially based on the restriction of non-radiative energy dissipation of the excited state by hindering the intra- and inter-NC vibrations and facilitating radiative decay upon cooling.63 GSH protected Au25 showing solid state emission at 700 nm experiences PL quenching with a decrease in temperature. The decrease in emission intensity at lower temperature is attributed to the open shell structure of Au25 leading to non-radiative loss of emission energy.113 Temperature responsive luminescence of MNCs can be considered as a molecular thermometer with an enhanced application perspective.

MNCs, being composed of diverse functional groups in the surface ligand, often display solvent specific optical behavior. The nature of the solvent (polarity, protic or aprotic, coordinating or non-coordinating) controls the electronic properties which eventually influence the emission of NCs.65,176,177 Water soluble hyperbranched polyethyleneimine (PEI) templated Ag NCs were dispersed in different solvents including alcohols, DMF, DMSO, THF etc.177 The intense blue emission of Ag NCs turns into an intense yellow emission as the solvent is changed from water to THF. They show green emission when dispersed in ethylene glycol monomethyl ether (EGME). The dipolar change in THF affects only the excited state by redistribution of energy from PEI to the Ag core.177 DNA coated Ag NCs have also been reported to exhibit solvatochromism due to the change in the shape of NCs and dielectric alteration in close proximity in solution.178 The emission of doped NCs is found to be solvent dependent as the PL intensity of PtAg24 NCs increases 100 fold in CH3CN than in DCM.65 A number of NCs are susceptible to aggregation when exposed to a different dispersion medium.68 The change in the solvent polarity reorients the M(I)S shell in the coreshell type NCs resulting in aggregation and consequent enhancement or quenching of emission.64,68,179 The solvent directed aggregation of NCs will be discussed in the next section.

The QYs of most NCs have been found to be quite low to date in spite of the advancement of designing luminescent MNC synthesis. The limitation has been partly resolved by several researchers through a modification of the ligand shell in NCs. The Xie group has developed a biphasic synthetic strategy for Au, Ag, Cu and Pt NCs by introducing a hydrophobic salt.86 This phase transfer agent provides a mild etching environment which leads to highly luminescent NCs. Lee and coworkers observed the temperature dependent luminescence of Au22(SG)18 controlled by the rigidity of the ligand shell. To rigidify the Au(I)thiolate shell a tetraoctylammonium cation was allowed to react with the carboxyl groups of GSH. The PL QY of TOA bound Au22 in toluene is enhanced to 60% with a blue shift from 670 nm to 630 nm.180 Afterwards, the same group reported a 37 fold enhancement of emission of an Au22-Py-TOA (Py = aminopyrene) conjugate system through photoinduced energy transfer and rigidification of the ligand shell.181 On the other hand, BSA protected Au25 exhibits red emission at 650 nm with higher QY due to the rigid interlocked structure compared to other Au25 NCs with open chain ligands. Enzyme digestion of BSA-Au25 weakens the compact structure of NCs leading to PL quenching while the rigidification of the ligand shell in Au10(SG)10 with the TOA cation enhances the PL intensity and excited state lifetime.182 Ion pairing in alloy [PtAg28(BDT)12(PPh3)4]4 NCs increases the QY up to 17.6% and the bulkier counter ion (TOA) is proved to be more effective by covering the maximum surface of NCs and making the structure more rigid.183 Most of the surface engineering aids the transfer of NCs to organic media, whereas Deng et al. utilized hostguest recognition to modify the surface of ATT-AuNCs (ATT = 6-aza-2-thiothymine) by L-arginine in aqueous medium.184 The guanine group in arginine forms H-bonds with ATT resulting in the suppression of non-radiative energy loss in the excited state via hindering the rotation and vibration of ligands. Consequently, the PL intensity at 528 nm becomes 34 times higher than that in ATT-AuNCs and the excited state lifetime increases from 13 ns to 86 ns. Upon HCl treatment, the PL emission intensity at 700 nm of the newly formed NCs increases 10 times due to the transformation of the core size and the ligand shell.66 Trypsin template Cu NCs synthesized at pH > 7 display blue emission, whereas a bright yellow emission is observed in acidic pH.81 It has been reported that the NIR luminescence intensity of monothiol, MSA and tiopronin protected Au NCs increases with excess ligands and at 50 C.185 The selective oxidation of a dithiolate layer upon purification of lipoic acid protected Au22LA12 NCs causes an enhancement of the QY from 12% to 10%. The six S atoms become oxidized to SO3 and another 18 S atoms form AuS covalent bonds. The unfamiliar observation of emission enhancement upon oxidation of surface ligands will shed light on the contribution of ligand shell binding on the photophysical behavior.12

3.2.5. Aggregation induced emission. Aggregation induced emission (AIE) is a phenomenon of generation or enhancement of luminescence from non-luminescent or weakly luminescent materials, respectively. Tang et al. have proposed the AIE mechanism for the appearance of solid state emission of siloles (hexaphenylsilole-HPS) which is non-luminescent in the solvent.186 Based on their extensive research, it is now well established that the restriction of intramolecular motion (RIM), restriction of intramolecular vibration (RIV) and restriction of intramolecular rotation (RIR) are responsible for the emission enhancement in aggregated states.187,188 The core shell structure of several NCs having long SMSMS motifs influences the emission mechanism.67,68,189 The extent of aggregation can be controlled by monitoring several chemical (polarity or pH) and physical (solid state or pressure) parameters.64,68,190192 Xie and coworkers have described the AIE phenomenon for non-luminescent Au(I)SG oligomers which turned out to be highly emissive in ethanol. The PL intensity of Au(I)SG complexes is found to increase and the bright red-yellow emission appeared with the increase in the EtOH content in the medium. The Au(I)SG complexes were allowed to condense on the Au (0) core to form a coreshell type NC with bright orange luminescence. A large Stokes shift (>200 nm) and microsecond lifetime suggest the contribution of charge transfer states in luminescence.179 Following the same analogy, the intense red emission at 665 nm of Au22(SG)18 was correlated with the mechanism of AIE.67 The optical behavior of Au22(SG)18 NCs is found to be different from those of Au22(SG)16 and Au22(SG)17. This contradiction arises due to a high SG/Au ratio (0.82) in Au22(SG)18 NCs and a corestaple structure consisting of a prolate Au8 core protected by two pairs of interlocked GS-[AuSG]3 and GS-[AuSG]4. With a precise formula, the reason behind the strong luminescence is ascribed to the aggregation of surface staples on the Au (0) core.67

AuAg and CuAu alloy NCs have been reported to show AIE involving the surface staples on the M(0) core.189,193195 The controlled aggregation of Cu(I)SR complexes on Au(0) results in soluble Au2Cu6 NCs showing red luminescence with 11.7% QY. The intense luminescence is attributed to the LMCT from the aromatic moiety of the ligands to the Cu(I) motifs on the surface.193 The surface motif regulated AIE mechanism is also evident in Cu NCs by regulating the solvent polarity64,104 and pH.191 Water soluble, polydisperse Cu3432(SG)1613 NCs exhibit emission enhancement upon freezing which eventually manifests the restriction of intramolecular motion. The motion of surface motifs was regulated by gradually changing the solvent environment with the introduction of EtOH in aqueous medium. In a 90% EtOHwater mixture, the emission maxima shifted from 625 nm to 597 nm with a 36 fold increase in PL intensity (Fig. 21AC). The hydration shell around the solvated NCs gets disrupted in a less polar environment and the NCs come closer due to Cu(I)Cu(I) interactions forming compact aggregates. A mechanism based on LMCT via CuS bonds has been proposed because of the 265 nm Stokes shift and long lifetime (115.9 ns) of Cu3432(SG)1613 NCs. Finally, the lifetime increases to 239.5 ns in 40% EtOHwater solution (Fig. 21D). The dynamics in the excited state involves an intersystem crossing from S1 to T1 followed by the relaxation to a low lying triplet state T2. Being a charge transfer state, T2 is stable in polar media and loses its stability in less polar media. Afterwards, phosphorescence takes place directly from T1 resulting in a blue shift and reinforcement in the emission intensity (Fig. 21E).64

3.3. Excited state dynamics

The fundamental understanding of the nature of the excited state in NCs is a foremost task of spectroscopic research. The metallic to non-metallic transition in a series of thiolate-protected Au25, Au38, Au144, Au333, Au520 and Au940 was investigated using pump power dependent transient absorption (TA) spectroscopy. The metallic to non-metallic transition between Au144 and Au333 is revealed by the difference in electronphonon coupling.17 Giant 246 atom Au NC was evidenced to possess non-metallic behavior.202 With distinct absorption peaks at 400, 460, 600 and 800 nm, the Au246(SR)80 (SR = p-methylbenzenethiolate) NCs did not show any pump power dependence like metal NPs. The ground state bleaching (GSB) occurs at 10 ps which is in accordance with the absorption peaks while the broad excited state absorption (ESA) is observed from 650 to 800 nm. After global fitting, three decay parameters were obtained: 1.9 ps for internal conversion (IC) to lower excited states, 4.6 ps for relaxation to the ground state and 73 ps for direct relaxation of the hot excited state. The pump fluence independence and oscillatory wave packet motion on the ground state potential energy surface revealed the non-metallic behavior of Au246.202 The sharp non-metallic to metallic transition takes place within the size regime of Au246 to Au279.203 Considering Au25(PET)18 as a superatom, the relaxation of excited states was correlated with the electronic structure. After excitation, a rapid internal conversion (<200 fs) within the multiple electronic levels of the Au13 core takes place and core to semi-ring relaxation occurs in 1.2 ps.204 Mono-anionic Au25(SR)18 NCs exhibit a decay time 1000 times faster than the neutral [Au25(SR)18]0 NCs while both of them show strong electronphonon coupling.205 In contrast to anionic NCs, Au25(SR)180 exhibits pump wavelength independent TA spectra though the GSB is in coherence with the absorption spectrum. The correlation of optical transitions with the crystal structure was investigated with the comparison of rod shaped Au25 and spherical Au25 and rod shaped Au38 NCs.206 The temperature dependent optical absorption of Au25 NCs reveals smaller electronphonon coupling and a minimal increase in oscillator strength indicating negligible involvement of shell-gold phonons with core-gold excitons. Additionally, wavelength dependent decay dynamics of spherical Au25 and rod shaped Au38 NCs arises due to cascade relaxation from the core to shell of NCs while that of Au25 rods is wavelength independent.206 Two structural isomers of Au38(PET)24 NCs, thermodynamically stable Au38Q and low temperature stable Au38T showed distinctly different relaxation dynamics owing to their different atomic structures (Fig. 23).207 Bi-icosahedral Au38Q having a Au23 core shows a 1.5 ps decay time followed by nanosecond relaxation to the ground state. The rapid decay component becomes faster by 50% (1 ps) in the Au38T isomer. The picosecond decay arises due to coreshell charge transfer or an electronic rearrangement within the metal core. The unique mono-icosahedral Au13 capped by an Au12 tri-tetrahedron by sharing two atoms in Au38T helps accelerate the decay (Fig. 23).

A comparative study of Au25(SR)18 NCs protected with hexanethiol and GSH reveals that the growth time is independent of the passivating ligand, whereas the luminescence decay kinetics depends on them due to the involvement of SAuSAuS semi-ring states.55 The excited state decay proceeds through manifold electronic transitions which is evident from wavelength dependent decay and growth kinetics and from the presence of long lived higher excited states.

The periodic FCC series of Au28(SR)20, Au36(SR)24, Au44(SR)28 and Au52(SR)32 NCs (SR = SPh-tBu) exhibit unique characteristics compared to icosahedral Au25(SR)18 and Au38(SR)24 NCs.69 TA spectra of all four NCs feature broad excited state absorption (ESA) spanning over the visible range and the GSB corresponding to the ground state absorption (600 nm for Au28 and Au36, 700 nm for Au44 and 800 nm for Au52) of the NCs. In general, a smaller HOMOLUMO gap gives rise to faster carrier recombination, whereas global analysis of the TA spectra reveals that the excited state lifetime increases from 59 to 135 ns with a decrease in the HOMOLUMO gap from 1.77 eV (for Au28) to 1.39 eV (for Au52) (Fig. 24A). The appearance of molecular like IC rather than coreshell relaxation along with the slower hot carrier relaxation and band edge recombination with increasing size suggest the absence of surface trap states and the exclusive contribution of the metal core.69 Cysteine protected Cu7 (Cu7L3) NCs possess similar dynamics which was probed by ultrafast upconversion spectroscopy. The fastest decay within 326.8 fs occurs due to the internal conversion from the S4 state to the S1 state just after photo-excitation (Fig. 24D). In the S1 state, structural reorganization occurs in trigonal bipyramidal Cu7L3 NCs within 4.8 ps due to changes in CuCu bond lengths (Fig. 24E). The nanosecond decay parameter signifies the relaxation to the ground state (S0) resulting in intense fluorescence.70 A comparative study was performed for Au25(SR)18 NCs with two different ligands upon interband (400 nm) and intraband excitation (800 nm). The higher energy excitation (400 nm) causes the rise of an electron to the high energy states in the sp band from the ligand band. Afterwards it comes down to the LUMO on a time scale of 700 fs which was achieved from the positive ESA component of 0.69 0.17 ps and the hole in the ligand band relaxes nonradiatively in 4 ps. The solvent and ligand independent dynamics in intraband excitation suggests the involvement of the Au core exclusively.211

3.4. Nonlinear optical properties

In 1908, Gustav Mie investigated the interaction of light with gold nanoparticles.24 From then to date, scientists have made tremendous effort to investigate the non-linear optical (NLO) response of gold nanocrystals i.e. nanoparticles having a quasi-continuous band structure. On the other hand, Au NCs having a diameter <2 nm possess discrete energy levels and molecular like HOMOLUMO transitions. The evolution of the optical properties from NPs to NCs is a strong function of the size and the NLO properties emerged as the primary attention of investigation to reveal the transition from the nanocluster to nanocrystalline state. As a consequence, two photon absorption (TPA), two photon fluorescence (TPF), second harmonic generation (SHG) and third harmonic generation (THG) are extensively studied.43,216 Goodson and coworkers have reported two photon fluorescence of hexanethiolate protected Au25 NCs centered at 830 nm with 1290 nm excitation (Fig. 26A).217 The pump power dependence of fluorescence gave a slope of 2 which signifies the occurrence of TPF (Fig. 26B). The measured TPA absorption cross section at 1290 nm is 2700 GM (1 GM = 1050 cm4 s photon1) which is higher than those of many organic chromophores. The pump power dependence of 510 nm emission has a slope of 2 (Fig. 26C and D) and the TPA cross-section increases with the increase in the size of the cluster from Au25 (1.1 nm) to Au2406 (4 nm) (Fig. 26E). 4. Applications

Tunable luminescence properties, photostability and the biocompatible nature of metal nanoclusters have enthralled the scientific community for their applicability in versatile applications such as optoelectronics, sensing, catalysis and bioimaging.1,3,4,45,49,52 Here, we will highlight the use of MNCs in various emerging fields such as optoelectronics, catalysis and sensing areas (Fig. 28).

4.1. Light harvesting applications

MNCs are being used for optoelectronic applications using solar energy conversion where photocurrent generation occurs due to the migration of photogenerated electrons and holes towards opposite electrodes. The discrete molecular like energy levels of NCs are favorable for designing feasible light-driven applications. Recently, pyrene functionalized Au25 NCs have been used for light-energy conversion where Au25 NCs can act as the electron donor and an anion radical of pyrene allows the directional movement of electrons.225 Tatsuma has demonstrated photonelectron conversion by using a series of GSH-Au NCs adsorbed on TiO2.226 Several research groups have designed Au NC sensitized TiO2 based solar cells where photoexcited electrons are transferred to the LUMO of TiO2 and the holes are transferred to a suitable hole acceptor leading to the light to current conversion.227,228 The size of Au NCs is significant in the Au NC sensitized solar cells due to the difference in charge injection and recombination kinetics and light absorbing ability. The maximum power conversion efficiency of 3.8% is achieved by using Au10, Au15, Au18 and Au25, Au18(SG)14 NCs (Fig. 29A).228 The mobility of electrons among discrete energy states has been utilized in a nanocomposite system of cysteine protected Cu7L3 with functionalized reduced graphene oxide (RGO). Photoexcited electrons from the LUMO of a Cu NC (3.7 eV) are transferred to the RGO energy level (4.42 eV) which is evident from ultrafast spectroscopy.70 Again, Au NCs have been reported to act as electron acceptors in an Au NCGO hybrid system.229 The photoinduced electron transfer from Cu NCs to methyl viologen derivatives is utilized in determining the characteristics of the pockets of BSA.230 The transport of electrons in MNCs generates an electronhole pair upon excitation and the unidirectional transfer of electrons or holes to suitable materials gives rise to a photocatalytic redox reaction and will be discussed in the catalysis section.

It is already known that fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) in a donoracceptor system is a classic method for versatile artificial light harvesting applications in nanoscience. A suitable donor and acceptor pair with spectral overlap between the emission peaks of the donor and absorption peaks of the acceptor with perfect orientation is needed for a successful energy transfer process.3 Pacific blue (PB) dye attached BSA protected Au NCs are found to be suitable for efficient energy transfer where pacific blue (PB) acts as an energy acceptor and the luminescence intensity increases two fold after the energy transfer process. Moreover, the same NCs can act as an energy donor when they are attached with a NIR emitting dye, Dy-750. The FRET process has been used for studying DNA hybridization, molecular beacons and biological labeling where clusters are used as probe molecules.231 GSH protected Au25 NCs and AlexaFlour 488 (A 488) attached to the opposite end of DNA showed energy transfer from A 488 to the Au NC. The externally added Ag+ ions bind with the cytosine residue of DNA and destruct the rigid structure. As a result, the FRET efficiency decreases in the presence of Ag+ due to a change of the distance between the donor and acceptor which helps in probing the conformation of DNA (Fig. 29B).232 An Alexa546-ssDNA templated Ag15 dyad has been reported to manifest 94% FRET efficiency.233 The fluorescence quenching of a DNA-intercalated dye has been reported with Au25 which is wavelength dependent and distinctly different from that of Au NPs.234 pH dependent energy transfer takes place in a conjugate of GSH Au22-fluorescein which was able to probe intracellular pH (Fig. 29C).235 Several semiconductor QD-MNC hybrids have been designed for successful FRET processes for understanding the influence of capping agents.5,236 The FRET efficiency in an Au25 NC-CdTe hybrid is sensitive to the capping ligand present, GSH, cysteine and BSA because the number of Au NCs attached per CdTe varies with ligands. The maximum energy transfer efficiency of 83% was observed in a BSA-Au25 NC-CdTe composite which was dependent on the nature of the ligand (Fig. 29D).237 A BSA capped Au25-coumarincarbon dot hybrid system has been designed for white light generation by using cascade energy transfer.238 Coumarin dye serves as a bridge between donor carbon dots and acceptor Au NCs enhancing the FRET efficiency from 63% to 83%. The triad assembly gives rise to successful white light generation with 19% QY.238 Moreover, the AIE characteristics i.e. long lifetimes and large Stokes shifts of MNCs are beneficial for designing LEDs using several composites of chitosan,239 polyurethane,240 substituted tetraphenylethene,241 and substituted biphenyl compounds.242 Self-assembled Cu NCs have been incorporated as phosphors in colour conversion layers of LEDs owing to their strong and tunable emission.45,46,196,199 The tunable emission of self-assembled Cu nanosheets was achieved with different Au(I) doping and a white LED was fabricated by combining the differently emissive nanosheets (Fig. 29E).196 The constantly improving emission properties and excited state behavior will enable us to design more efficient light harvesting systems based on metal clusters.

4.2. Sensing applications

The design of metal cluster based sensors is another active field of research in nanoscience where analytes are recognized by the change in the optical behavior of NCs.52 PL quenching occurs due to analyte induced aggregation when analytes bind through metallophilic interactions. On the other hand, PL enhancement is found when metal deposition occurs on the NC surface. It is found that MUA capped Au NCs interact with Hg(II) via carboxyl groups of MUA which causes fluorescence quenching and the limit of detection (LOD) of Hg(II) is found to be 5.0 nM.243 Cu2+ detection has been reported with poly(methylmethacrylic) acid (PMAA) protected Ag NCs.244 Al3+ sensing by PEGylate MSA AuAg NCs,106 and detection of Zn2+ by histidine and MPA co-protected Au NCs245 have been reported through ligand induced aggregation. The red emission of BSA protected Au NCs is completely quenched in the presence of Hg(II) owing to the interaction of Hg2+ and Au+ and the LOD is found to be 0.5 nM.246 The luminescence quenching of GSH and MPA co-protected Au NCs occurs in the presence of Pb2+ and the detection limit is found to be very low as 10 nM (Fig. 30A and B).18 The deposition of any foreign metal atom in the core of MNCs may lead to the galvanic or anti-galvanic exchange which eventually modifies the absorption and luminescence properties.50,247,248 FRET based systems have been designed using metal clusters to recognize a specific toxic ion. A composite of BSA capped Au NCs and GSH protected CdTe QDs is being used to detect both Hg2+ and F ions as the emission of CdTe QDs at 553 nm is quenched upon addition of Hg2+ and an enhancement of the PL intensity occurs in the presence of F. The quenching of the emission intensity in the composite is attributed to the salt induced aggregation leading to the selective detection of Hg2+ with an LOD of 9 nM (Fig. 30C). On the other hand, F ions disrupt the H-bonding between GSH and BSA preventing the energy transfer from QDs to Au NCs and the probe is able to detect F with an LOD of 117 nM by the restoration of QD emission (Fig. 30D).51

4.4 Catalytic applications

Structurally precise MNCs have been proved to be a class of model catalysts owing to their monodispersity, high surface to volume ratios, versatile crystal packing, presence of low-coordinated sites and sharp electronic changes upon atomic level doping.4 Compared to bulk gold, discrete energy levels of Aum(SR)n NCs ignited the scope of electrocatalysis, photocatalysis and organic catalysis. The appearance of non-metallic behavior in sub-2 nm particles i.e. MNCs indeed enhances their catalytic activity compared to their larger analogues.17,271 The exhaustive use of fossil fuels in industrial and transport purposes has caused the depletion of conventional energy sources and poses severe threat to the environment. The photocatalysis and electrocatalysis of MNCs have emerged because of the requirement of green, sustainable alternative energy sources.

The directional movement of electrons in NCs upon light irradiation has been utilized in different photocatalytic reactions such as water splitting, CO2 reduction and dye degradation. Photoinduced electron transfer takes place from ZnO NPs to Au NCs in their composites where the electron transfer rate is dependent on the size of NCs.272 Among Au25, Au144 and Au807, Au807(SG)163 NCs shows the maximum catalytic activity in the degradation of the organic pollutant, thionine. In this visible light driven photocatalysis, the Au25(SR)18 NC showed 1.6 times higher photocatalytic activity of TiO2 (Fig. 33A and B).48 TiO2 nanotube arrays were attached with Au NCs and the composite systems were found to be suitable for water splitting, organic pollutant decomposition and solar cell sensitization under a solar light simulator (Fig. 33C).47 GSH protected Au NCs act as a cocatalyst in the water splitting reaction using BaLa4Ti4O15 as a photocatalyst.273 The Xiong group has covalently grafted metal cations (Fe2+, Co2+, Ni2+, and Cu2+) via a bridging ligand cysteine. The whole assembly was utilized in CO2 photoreduction with visible light and the d band of the transition metal regulates CO2 activation by dictating electron transfer from Au NCs and CO2 activation.274 The architecture of a suitable nanostructure with MNCs can gear up the applications in photocatalytic CO2 reduction or hydrogen generation. Au25(PET)18 loaded on TiO2 acts as a semiconductor with a small band gap to absorb visible light, facilitates electronhole separation and produces singlet oxygen (1O2) causing dye degradation.275 Upon aerial exposure, the stable anionic [Au25(PET)18] undergoes oxidation to the neutral form which has different electronic and crystal structures and the redox reaction is found to be reversible.276 The reversible redox behavior of Au NCs was utilized in catalytic oxidation of CO and conversion of CO2 and many other reactions. Au25 acted as a catalyst in the CO2 CO conversion reaction with a 90 mV formal potential and showed peak CO production at 1.0 V vs. reversible hydrogen electrode.277 The negatively charged Au25(PET)18 stabilized CO2 and H+ and promoted the reaction CO2 + H+ + 2e CO + H2O, whereas the cationic Au25(PET)18+ promoted CO oxidation, CO + 2OH 2e CO2 + H2O, by stabilizing coadsorbed CO and OH. This study revealed the role of charged active sites in the catalytic activity of NCs.278 Later, the Jin group designed a carbon negative CO2 management technology using the Au NCs as the catalyst.279 Theoretical calculations suggest that the Au NCs facilitate CO2 reduction by partial removal of thiol ligands and opening of active sites.280 The charge state of Au25(DT)18 NCs affects the electroreduction of O2 to form H2O2 and the negatively charged NCs were found to exhibit the maximum catalytic activity.281 The oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) catalyzed by silver NCs proved to be a potential candidate for alkaline fuel cells.282 A composite of Aun/CoSe2 (n = 25, 144 and 333) enhances the rate of the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) in water splitting involving the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER). Au25/CoSe2 displays a much improved overpotential (0.43 V at a current density of 10 mA cm2) compared to plain CoSe2 (0.52 V) due to successful formation of an OOH intermediate and desorption of oxygen molecules (Fig. 32A).283 The HER has been reported to be catalyzed by MoS2 in the presence of an Au NC cocatalyst.284 DNA templated Au NCs integrated with carbon nanotubes enhance the performance of bilirubin oxidase (BOD) catalyzed enzymatic ORR by improving the electronic interactions between the electrode and the enzyme active site.285 pH dependent electrocatalytic activity of GSH protected Au25 NCs helps in atmospheric sensing by catalyzing the oxidation of ascorbic acid and dopamine.286

5. Conclusion and perspective

Quantum sized metal NCs formulated as Mn(SR)m (M = Au, Ag and Cu) have been proven to be potential functional novel nanomaterials. The major concern in synthesizing metal clusters is molecular purity and understanding their structureproperty relationship for future applications. We have summarized several well-developed methodologies such as direct reduction, chemical etching, ligand and metal exchange and intercluster reactions to obtain monodisperse homometallic and heterometallic NCs. The crystal structure of metal clusters eventually controls the electronic transition and physical properties of metal clusters. The photophysical properties of these clusters are found to be dependent on the core size and the nature of ligands because the charge transfer from the ligand to metal core is found to be influenced by the electronic structure, functional groups, electronegativity of the metal, valence state and presence of heteroatoms. Apart from these structural parameters, surrounding parameters such as temperature, pH, solvent and modification of the surface also play an important role in the photophysical properties of the metal clusters. Aggregation induced emission is a contemporary topic of research to improve the QY of the NCs and control the emission over the wide range of visible and NIR regions. The investigation on the excited state relaxation process with femtosecond pumpprobe spectroscopy enlightens the molecular like processes in NCs. Moreover the non-linear optical behavior enables several optical limiting applications of NCs. We have covered the optical transition based applications in photovoltaics, photocatalysis, white light generation, luminescence based sensing, bioimaging and catalysis. Application of luminescent metal nanoclusters is still in the embryonic stage, and further investigations in this field are necessary for an in-depth understanding of the phenomenon. The determination of molecular purity is a challenge because of the difficulties of crystallization of NCs due to the presence of a number of functional groups at the surface. Additionally, Cu NCs are very difficult to stabilize in solution for a long time due to their susceptibility to aerial oxidation. Though diverse strategies such as surface modification and AIE have been developed to improve the PL behavior, the QYs of most NCs reported to date are not satisfactory. Moreover, the exact mechanism of PL is not known as the core size, oxidation state, protecting ligand and surrounding parameters collectively dictate the emission. The particular influence of the M(0) core, M(I)S surface motifs and bonding interactions with the ligand is still elusive. The LMCT process needs more attention which can be served by studying excited state relaxation in an ultrafast time scale. The photovoltaic application is hindered as most of the NCs have maximum absorption in the UV range of the spectrum. More NCs with visible range absorption still need to be developed. Biological applications require hydrophilic NCs with very high PL QY, whereas the catalytic applications demand a structurally precise formula of NCs. We hope that the efforts devoted by researchers will overcome the difficulties in the coming years. Therefore, such atomically precise luminescent metal clusters would be beneficial for future applications in efficient light harvesting or chemical sensing applications.
