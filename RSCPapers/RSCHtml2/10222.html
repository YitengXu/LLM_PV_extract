Abstract

Due to the ongoing industrial revolution, solar-driven water splitting and pesticide degradation are highly sought to cope with catastrophes such as depleting fossil reservoirs, global warming, and environmental degradation. The development of stable and efficient photocatalysts has been extensively attempted for past several decades. Herein, SnO2-based nanomaterials (NMs) have been extensively explored due to their higher stability and wide band gap semiconducting properties. Thus, this review summarizes the recent innovations made on SnO2-based NMs/electrodes/scaffolds for highly efficient photocatalytic water splitting and pesticide degradation based on the type of dopants, their nature, concentration, sensitizers, chemical additives, scavengers, etc., with the corresponding mechanistic aspects. The theoretical aspects of SnO2-based NMs have also been elaborated. Various trend-setting modifications such as band gap engineering, enhanced charge separation, and electron transfer with various SnO2-based NMs are discussed in light of the search for real-life photocatalysts.

1. Introduction

Energy and environment are the crucial areas throughout the world that demand immediate scientific attention. Global energy consumption and demands have significantly escalated over the past years owing to an exponential increase in population, urbanization, and industrialization. Although conventional fossil fuels have been extensively investigated to meet the energy demand, their implementation has led to the emission of CO2 and other greenhouse gases such as CH4 and NH3. They are known to cause detrimental effects on biodiversity and environment, leading to global warming. Therefore, there is a paucity of strategies that are beneficial for meeting today's energy demands and enhancing the development of renewable energy resources. The implementation of solar energy to enable photo-driven mechanisms has gained immense attention.1 Hence, promoted interest in photocatalytic energy production can provide a suitable alternative for fossil fuels. Nevertheless, this demands focused research and persistent innovation in solar cell efficiency, existing material modification, adoption of new H2 generation strategies, dye and pesticide degradation, and heavy metal removal from wastewater.27 Photocatalytic H2 generation using nanostructured materials is an attractive and demanding area of research, providing an opportunity to convert abundantly available photonic energy into usable chemical energy without harmful emissions and at an economical cost. Nanochemistry allows the utilization of inexpensive strategies to harvest sunlight by expeditiously capturing photon energy over a wide range of spectra.8 In addition, it also provides an avenue for pesticide degradation.9 Various materials such as perovskites, sulfides, graphene-based NMs, CdS, WO3, and TiO2 have been explored to harness abundant solar energy to catalyze water splitting and pesticide degradation.1018 Reviews summarizing water splitting and pollutant degradation using perovskite oxide-based photocatalysts are available in the literature.1 Furthermore, graphene-based photocatalysts have attained utmost importance in photocatalytic reactions owing to their 2D layered structure and electronic properties.19 Literature reports N-doped CaTiO3 integrated with reduced graphene oxide (rGO) and ternary nanocomposites of CaTiO3 with MoS2 fabricated on graphene nanosheets as an efficient photocatalyst for pesticide remediation and photocatalysis.20 Also, CaTiO3 with sulfur (S)-enriched MoS2 were fabricated on rGO via a facile hydrothermal approach, and showed enhanced photocatalytic performance with utilization in diverse applications.21 The resultant ternary complex shows enhanced photocatalytic performance owing to reduced charge recombination, high surface area, and increases H2 generation.20,21 On this note, there is a diverse array of literature that is available on various materials associated with photocatalytic degradation and environmental remediation. However, due to interesting physicochemical properties, SnO2 nanoparticles (NPs) have always garnered particular interest in industrial and photocatalytic applications.22,23

The methodology of SnO2-based NPs as a photocatalyst is economical and simple to perform. Further, photocatalytic reaction mediated by SnO2-based NPs is among the cogent mechanisms as an efficient photocatalyst to harness solar energy with potent applications in organic pollutant degradation. This strategy is much more appealing due to the abundant and renewable nature of sunlight. To date, several photocatalysts such as TiO2 and ZnO have been intensively investigated for their photocatalytic ability.24 The present review critically highlights photocatalytic water splitting for the evolution of H2 and O2 utilizing SnO2-based NMs such as doped and heterostructured NPs, and the mechanistic aspects of pollutant degradation as well as industrial utilization. The choice of SnO2 over TiO2 is considered because of its less toxicity, cost effectiveness, and wide band gap; however, no comprehensive review on SnO2 with insights into photocatalytic H2 generation and environmental remediation is available to the best of our knowledge.24 Therefore, addressing this knowledge gap is important for the development of a sustainable society.

Literature provides promising prospects of SnO2-based NMs in energy and environmental science. The significance of SnO2 can be accessed from the reviews focusing on the significance of SnO2 in perovskite solar cells (PSC), transparent conductors, electrochemical, and gas sensors.2529 In a recent review, the photocatalytic performance of SnO2 such as organic pollutant degradation, CO2 and Cr(VI) reduction, purification of air, and sterilization has been discussed along with the basic aspects of photocatalytic improvement strategies.30 However, to the best of our knowledge, the photocatalytic aspects of SnO2 nanostructures with special reference to water splitting and pesticide degradation have been summarized for the first time; thus, a pivotal motive of this review is to give a comprehensive outline of the recent advances in water splitting and pesticide degradation via modified SnO2.3133

1.1 Photocatalytic hydrogen generation

Fossil reservoirs are currently the primary energy sources and are depleting at a fast pace due to population expansion and increased energy demands.34 Hydrogen as a synfuel has been considered as a clean and sustainable energy source with a high heat of combustion globally.3538 Some H2 production methods, such as steam methane reforming method and coal gasification, are fossil fuel-dependent and generate CO2 along with H2 at high temperature and pressure, and hence, are not a long-lasting solution.39,40 Therefore, it is of utmost importance to use renewable resources for H2 production. One such reliable and environment-friendly method is to generate H2 through solar-driven processes such as photothermochemical, photobiological, photoelectrochemical, and photocatalytic approaches (Fig. 1).4146 In photothermochemical water splitting, large solar concentrators are established to facilitate solar energy.
Fig. 1 Different solar energy-based approaches for H2 generation.

In photobiological water splitting, enzymes are used for the production of hydrogen.47 Photocatalytic or photoelectrochemical water splitting utilizes solar energy and produces H2 on the surface of the catalyst. They are the most promising alternatives to address the environmental and energy crisis. In these methods, omnipresent primary sources, such as water and solar energy, exhibit no adverse effects.48 Moreover, the emission of greenhouse gases is zero, which is a crucial issue in H2 production from fossil fuels.49,50 Photocatalytic water splitting has been considered a potential way for the utilization of solar energy to produce sustainable energy sources.5154

H2 generation ability using photocatalytic materials has been explored since 1972. Fujishima and Honda first carried out water decomposition using a photoelectrochemical cell, comprising of an n-type TiO2 anode and a Pt cathode.55 Henceforth, numerous photocatalysts have been established for highly stable water splitting reactions.5659 Shudo et al. reported the photocatalytic reaction of rGO and transition metal hybrids for H2 generation.60 Zhang et al. studied water splitting using the Pt/g-C3N4 photocatalyst without sacrificial reagents,61 while Liu et al. discussed water splitting on silicon nanowires.62 In contrast, Kudo et al. investigated water splitting on tantalate photocatalysts for H2 and O2 evolution, wherein NiO/NaTaO3:La tantalate photocatalyst was reported to show the highest activity over the others.63

Photocatalytic water splitting mechanism utilizing semiconductor nanostructured materials involves the following steps: excitation of electrons from the low energy valence band (VB) to the high energy conduction band (CB) when irradiated with light energy that is equal to or greater than the semiconductor band gap, leading to photogenerated electronhole pair formation. The next step involves charge separation; afterward, the photogenerated carriers are transferred, and finally, the surface reacts with water to produce H2 and O2 gases (Fig. 2).


Fig. 2 The mechanism of the photocatalytic water splitting reaction.

Therefore, photocatalytic materials must fulfill the following requirements: (1) having an appropriate band structure promoting charge separation, (2) the VB of the semiconductor must be more positive than the oxidation potential of water (+1.23 eV) to produce O2 and the CB must be more negative than the reduction potential of water to produce H2 (0.0 V), (3) must be stable in an aqueous medium when irradiated with light, and (4) must exhibit stability against photocorrosion, and must be structurally stable after prolonged application. These critical factors in the photocatalytic splitting of water determines suitable semiconducting nanostructures that can capture light and generate electronhole pairs. However, there is always the possibility of the rapid recombination of the photogenerated electronhole pairs, thus significantly affecting the efficiency. Hence, to minimize the recombination of charge carriers, dopants, composite materials, heterostructures, and sacrificial agents are employed.

Similarly, the primary mechanism behind photoelectrochemical water splitting (PEC) plays a crucial role to convert solar energy into an efficient fuel, dihydrogen. The mechanism involves the immersion of photovoltaic materials into an electrolyte containing the redox couple. One of the semiconducting materials of the redox couple is photosensitive, which absorbs light on exposure. Semiconductor photocatalysts on capturing light carry out reduction and oxidation processes. Additional voltage to carry out the redox reactions at the electrodes is provided by an electric current. On the absorption of photons, electron excitation takes place from the photoelectrodes to generate the electronhole pairs. 1.2 Pesticide degradation

Population, industrialization, civilization, agricultural, and domestic activities pollute surface and ground waters. Among several pollutants, high amounts of pesticides, fluoride, arsenic, cadmium, lead, and other heavy metals have been reported in ground and surface waters across the world.6466 High input levels of fertilizers, growth promoters, or pesticides are heavily used in agriculture for crop production. Being non-biodegradable, they get bioaccumulated and severely affect human health. They pollute water bodies and groundwater resources through leaching, runoffs, open disposal of used containers, etc., from agricultural fields.67

Researchers have made significant investigations from various disciplines to address pesticide removal from water and food.68 Various kinds of physical, chemical, and biological strategies have already been reported in the literature to remove pesticides.6972 These methods include adsorption,73,74 filtration,75,76 chemical precipitation,77,78 oxidation,79,80 and photocatalytic degradation.8183 Among these, photocatalytic degradation is an efficient method for pesticide excision.84 It involves degradation via light absorption through a direct or indirect mechanism. In direct photodegradation, a light absorber rearranges or cleaves some bonds to form a stable product. In indirect photodegradation, light is absorbed by photosensitizers such as lignin, Cu(II), O2, and NO3, and is transferred to produce reactive intermediates for degradation.85 The photogenerated positive holes in the VB oxidize surface-adsorbed H2O or surface hydroxyl groups (OH) to generate hydroxyl radical (OH). At the same time, the electrons in the CB contribute to the formation of superoxide radicals (O2) by reducing molecular oxygen. The generated OH can oxidize organic compounds and eventually carry out mineralization. Hence, these reactive radicals decompose pesticides into less toxic substances without secondary pollution (Fig. 4).

Hence, it is necessary to detect and identify these intermediates to conclude whether or not these intermediates are harmful. NMs play a crucial role in detecting and destroying pesticides.87 The mechanism for the degradation of few pesticides is explained below.

Mills et al. proposed a simple mechanism to identify intermediates for the photocatalytic degradation of pentachlorophenol (PCP) using TiO2. In a nutshell, a suitable band gap possessing the semiconducting material that can absorb photons to generate reactive species is a must to assist pesticide degradation. Graphene-based NMs are very well known in the field of toxic substance removal from water. A review by Kommu et al. presents graphene-based materials for the removal of contaminants from wastewater.90 To meet the energy crisis and pesticide degradation for environmental remediation, SnO2-based NMs are considered as an immediate choice owing to their variable band gap nature.

2. SnO2: an obvious choice and unique characteristics

So far, various metal oxides have been investigated for photocatalytic reactions.12 TiO2 is the most commonly known semiconductor used for photocatalytic reactions. However, it absorbs only in the UV region of the visible spectrum (200400 nm), thus limiting its photocatalytic performance. Apart from TiO2, other photocatalysts such as ZnO, WO3, Fe2O3, CuO, and CoO have also been reported for photocatalytic reactions. However, they also have certain drawbacks. For instance, ZnO significantly undergoes photocorrosion when irradiated for electron excitation from the VB.91 Also, it is unstable in aqueous solutions.92 On the other hand, WO3 has lower energy conversion efficiency due to relatively low reduction potential, limiting its use as a photocatalyst.93 Moreover, the CB level of WO3 is low; therefore, it is known mainly for O2 evolution and not for H2 evolution. Similarly, CoO is reported to have a short half-life and faster deactivation, makes it less favorable for photocatalytic reactions. Besides the above-mentioned metal oxides, SnO2 possesses the characteristics necessary for a photocatalyst to work under UV-visible light irradiation and, at the same time, makes up for the limitations encountered with oxides such as ZnO and CoO.

SnO2 is a significantly stable oxide and an n-type wide band gap (3.6 eV) semiconductor. Due to environmental compatibility and versatile functionality, it has a plethora of applications such as gas sensors, water splitting reactions to generate energy, electrocatalysts, anodes in Li-ion batteries and storage, electrodes in dye-sensitized solar cells, and organic pollutant degradation.32,9498 In comparison, nanocrystalline SnO2 exhibits excellent physical, chemical, optical, magnetic, and conducting properties. Hence, researchers are making considerable efforts to develop SnO2 with variations in the structural arrangements such as nanofibers, nanowires, nanorods, and nanosheets.99103 In addition, SnO2 is cheap and non-toxic; hence, it could be an essential photocatalyst for carrying out chemical reactions in the presence of solar energy. It demonstrates excellent chemical stability over a range of pH, different solvents, higher temperature range, and is therefore used for the removal and degradation of toxic dyes and organic pollutants from wastewater.104,105

SnO2 crystallizes in the rutile structure (direct energy band gap, 3.6 eV) (Fig. 8), possesses tetragonal symmetry, and adopts the P42/mnm (D144h) space group, with a = b = 4.755 and c = 3.199 .106 The crystal structure of SnO2 is a slightly distorted octahedra with six coordination from oxygen ions in the vicinity of the central Sn(IV) metal ion. Fig. 9 shows the rutile SnO2 structure as seen in different orientations. The SnO2 VB originates from the mixing of O 2p-orbital and Sn 5s- and 5p-orbitals, while the CB comprises mostly of the 5s-orbital.

2.1 Photocatalytic SnO2: barriers and defect chemistry

As stated previously, SnO2 stability along with a suitable band structure largely favors its use as a photocatalyst. Some inherent features of SnO2 need modification before their utilization as an efficient photocatalyst. For example, the wide band gap of a material only absorbs UV light, which is a small portion of sunlight. Furthermore, photogenerated electrons and holes in SnO2 are short-lived and can recombine readily, which tremendously affects its photocatalytic performance.

Several attempts have been made to enhance the photocatalytic performance and visible-light response of SnO2, including noble metal loading, ion doping, composite semiconductors, and heterojunctions. A few have proved to be very useful in increasing H2 evolution and degradation of pesticides. Modified SnO2 is better than pure SnO2 in terms of reduced charge recombination rates, improved light-harvesting efficiency, increased rate of electron transfer, and more defects and OVs, resulting in enhanced photocatalytic behavior.95,106108

Introducing defects results in enhanced optical, electrical, and catalytic characteristics such as light absorption in the visible region and higher photoluminescence.109 Predominantly, the defects in metal oxides are lattice point defects. They are associated with cation or OVs and correlate with the preparation methods. One of the methods that results in OVs and metal interstitials is thermal treatment with hydrogen. In this method, hydrogen interacts with lattice oxygen and abstracts it from the lattice position, giving surface OVs.110 Another method to generate OVs is either by doping or by changing oxygen ion concentration. Doping the lattice with metal or non-metal ionic species is considered to be a preferential method for generating OVs. For example, when SnO2 is doped with a lower valence cation, it facilitates OVs formation.111 Similarly, anion doping such as N doping introduces substantial OVs in the SnO2 lattice.112 SnO2 is an n-type semiconductor as the impurities create filled states near the CB level in its band gap region.113 OVs are generated on the surface of metal oxides by substituting oxygen from their lattice sites. When in-plane oxygen and bridge oxygen are missing from their respective sites, then these defects are defined as in-plane OV and bridge OV. The vacancy formation energy can be calculated using the following equation.

EOV = Edefect Eperfect + 1/2Eoxy
where Edefect, Eperfect, and Eoxy are the energy of defected SnO2 surface, energy of the perfect SnO2 surface, and the chemical potential value of oxygen molecule calculated in the triplet state, respectively. The EOV values for bridging OV, in-plane OV, OV on top of 2nd layer, bridging vacancy in 2nd layer, and in-plane OV in 2nd layer are 1.92, 3.32, 2.87, 3.37, and 4.09 eV, respectively.114 Bridging OV has the lowest vacancy formation energy; hence, it is easy to detach the oxygen from the bridge site. Each bridging OV generates five-coordinate corresponding Sn atom on the surface of SnO2 (Fig. 10).115 When the ideal surface is heated above 500 K, it leads to the loss of bridge oxygen to form bridge OV. However, when the temperature is above 750 K, it leads to a loss of in-plane oxygen and forms in-plane OV.116,117

Oxygen, tin atoms, and oxygen vacancies are represented with red, dark grey, and yellow balls, respectively. Copyright 2021 Elsevier. All rights reserved.115

Both type of OVs are almost invisible and elusive, and affect the electrical conductivity and atomic structure of metal oxide NPs along with intrinsic physical or chemical properties.106,118120 It also narrows the band gap and enhances the conductivity of the semiconductor. In-plane OVs rapidly enhance the surface conductivity of semiconductors than bridging OVs.117

Wang et al. demonstrated an excellent gas sensing behavior of SnO2rGO hybrids toward one ppm NO2 with a response of 3.80 due to increased OVs.121 Kamble et al. reported band gap narrowing in SnO2 NPs due to the OVs' deep donor bands, created by the combustion method.122 Kumar et al. synthesized OV-engineered leaf-templated CaTiO3 materials for photocatalytic H2 generation reactions and demonstrated through various spectroscopic techniques that OVs play an important role in boosting the photocatalytic performance. Therefore, OV formation in the engineered material is of great importance as it influences the photocatalytic performance. The optimum OV sites are responsible for a larger surface area and prevents the recombination of charge carriers.123 In contrast, SnO2 OVs are generated due to charge compensation by the transfer of electrons by reducing Sn4+ to Sn2+ ions.113 Zhang et al. provided an in-depth cognizance of OVs role in designing defective ceria oxide for photocatalytic water oxidation.118 Yang et al. explained increased width of VB and narrowed the band gap of SnO2 NPs, facilitating efficient charge transfer and separation.124 DFT calculations found that OVs in rutile SnO2 affect the overlap of the new valence state with the VB edge. Hence, SnO2 having defects was found to have a narrower band gap to 2.4 eV due to the widened VB, consequently enhancing the light harvesting efficiency. Interestingly, by tuning the size and shape of SnO2 NPs, the optimized electronic structure can be accessed and hence better photocatalytic performance is achieved over pure SnO2.125

2.2 Synthesis of nanostructured SnO2

Altering the shape and size could lead researchers to customize several properties that the material exhibit: (1) chemical, (2) mechanical, (3) electronic, and (4) magnetic properties. Moving toward nanodimensions would often lead to undetermined characteristics about the material and would open whole new possibilities for various applications in different fields.126,127 In the case of SnO2, the atomic distribution and defects created can alter the surface energy and based on it, the surface area and energy could be enhanced to several orders of magnitude at nanodimensions.128 Few synthetic methods involve the thin film deposition of materials onto the substrate by physical means (physical vapor deposition method), whereas other methods involve electrically charged polymeric solution to make a loop through the bending of the fiber. The formation of ultrathin nanofibers takes place, exhibiting a range of properties, which differentiates them to form 1-D nanostructures.128,129

The performance of nanostructured SnO2 can be tuned by different synthetic strategies: (1) wet chemical route, (2) microwave-assisted method, (3) thermal deposition, (4) physical vapor deposition (PVD) method, (5) electrospinning method, (6) spray pyrolysis method, and (7) chemical vapor deposition (CVD) method. 2.3 Factors affecting photocatalytic activity

Photodegradation and photocatalytic water splitting by a photocatalyst depend on external parameters such as amount of catalyst, temperature, pH, contaminant concentration, intensity of light, and surface area. The following parameters are discussed.

(1) Amount of the catalyst: the photocatalytic degradation efficiency is initially enhanced by the addition of the catalyst until an optimum amount of the catalyst is reached. After that, the degradation efficiency steadily decreases. The increase in the degradation corresponds to the enhanced surface area of the catalyst in solution. However, beyond an optimum concentration, the turbidity in the solution is increased, which restricts the light transmission in the solution and decreases the degradation efficiency as only the surface particles of the catalyst remain photocatalytically active.165,166 Mohanta et al. performed the degradation of clothianidin using different concentrations of doped SnO2, i.e., 1.0 to 5.0 mg L1. An increase in the degradation efficiency was obtained when the catalyst's concentration was increased up to 3.0 mg L1. However, on further increasing the catalyst amount, the efficiency decreases up to 5.0 mg L1 catalyst due to the enhancement in the turbidity of the pesticide solution (Fig. 22a).167 The degradation of fenamiphos contaminant is checked using different amounts of the TiO2 catalyst (50 mg L1 to 250 mg L1) by Fenoll et al. The optimum amount for the degradation of fenamiphos was found to be 100 mg L1 as after that, the extent of degradation declined.168


Fig. 22 (a) Effect of concentration of the catalyst dose, (b) concentration of clothianidin, and (c) pH on the photocatalytic degradation efficiency (Copyright 2020 Elsevier. All rights reserved).167

(2) The concentration of the contaminant: basically, the photodegradation efficiency declines with an increase in the concentration of the contaminant.169 This behavior is attributed to the absorption of light by the organic dyes, which results in the shrinking of the available light radiations to activate the photocatalyst. Moreover, the organic dyes occupy the photocatalyst surface; in turn, fewer active sites are available for photodegradation.170,171 Mohanta et al. performed degradation using doped SnO2 for different concentrations of clothianidin (0.1 mg L1 to 1.5 mg L1). When the concentration of clothianidin was enhanced from 0.1 mg L1 to 1.5 mg L1, the degradation performance declined (Fig. 22b).167 Khodami et al. checked the photodegradation efficiency of the ZnO/SnO2/nanoclinoptilolite catalyst by degrading 4-methylbenzoic acid (MBA) and 2-chloro-5-nitrobenzoic acid (CNBA). The experiment was performed for 220 mg L1 concentration of both the contaminants. The photodegradation efficiency was increased up to 5 mg L1; after that, the efficiency toward both the contaminants decreases with increasing the pesticide concentration.172

(3) Effect of pH: the photodegradation efficiency is dependent on the pH value of the solution. Mostly, low pH values are considered to be favorable for the photodegradation of pollutants.173 The interaction of organic pollutants with the photocatalyst is enhanced in a slightly acidic medium, improving the photodegradation activity. But when the pH value is below a definite threshold, the photodegradation efficiency declines significantly.174 Abdullah et al. stated that photodegradation is enhanced when the pH of the reaction mixture lies between the pKa value of the organic acid and the pH of point zero charges (pHzpc) of the nanocomposite. This generates electrostatic interaction of the deprotonated acid with the positively charged surface of the photocatalyst.175 This is confirmed by Mohanta et al., who performed the experiment to degrade clothianidin using Au@SnO2/rGO nanocomposite at different pH values (3, 7, and 11). The pHzpc value of the nanocomposite was approximately 4. The degradation rate is highest and comparable at pH 3 and 7. However, the rate is declined when the pH of the solution is basic, i.e., 11 (Fig. 22c).167 Also, the generation of H2 gas via photocatalytic water splitting is dependent on the concentration of protons, i.e., the pH value. The generation of H2 is more effective when the solution is weakly basic than a strongly basic or acidic medium.176 Wu et al. observed maximum H2 generation in a weakly basic solution (pH 10), while a lesser amount of H2 was obtained at a pH value of 2 using CuOx/TiO2.177 Similarly, Fujita et al. showed the effect of pH on photocatalytic H2 production using the NiO/TiO2 catalyst. The photocatalytic production rate of H2 is the highest at pH 6.6, while the production rate is less in the pH range from 0 to 11 of the solution mixture (Fig. 23).178

4.1 Doping

Dopants alter the crystal structure of SnO2 and the impact of doping on the conductivity, resistivity, band gap position, etc., has been seen in the literature. For SnO2 modification and band gap engineering, ion doping is a promising path that results in enhanced efficacy. The doping of the catalyst leads to increased charge separation, distorted lattice, and hence results in enhanced photocatalytic activity.120 The Fermi level in doped semiconductors shows either a red or blue shift, as reflected by band gap engineering. Doped materials show generated energy states either below the CB edge or above the VB edge. These new states perform charge trapping and ultimately lead to wavelength extension to the visible region. Every dopant atom represents strong local perturbation to CB, causing large electron scattering in the conduction band, thereby reducing the mobility. Besides that, dopants that substitute for oxygen would mainly perturb the VB, leaving the CB relatively free of scattering with high mobility.209 Either OVs or interstitial tin atoms (Sni) are anticipated to be donors in pure SnO2.210

Vacancy sites are created in SnO2 when its lattice is doped with elements whose ionic radius are similar or different to Sn4+ ions (0.071 nm), such as Li (0.090 nm), Al (0.069 nm), Ga (0.076 nm), and In (0.094 nm). These elements substitute Sn and create a vacancy in and above the VB maxima. However, the introduction of dopants into the lattice occupies the interstitial site and donates an electron to the system, and thus, acts as a donor defect. An essential aspect of doping is its optimum concentration. For example, 8% doping is found to be optimum for Al-doped SnO2, whereas 20% doping is promising for Ga-doped SnO2. Other donor dopants on the Sn site are phosphorus, arsenic, and antimony. They substitute Sn in the SnO2 lattice, creating negative charges. Some elements that have one valence electron less than O and one valence electron more than Sn exhibit amphoteric behavior when doped into SnO2 and are group V elements. When they substitute oxygen atom and substitute for the Sn site, they act as acceptor dopants and donor dopants, respectively. The importance of vacancies has been discussed by Kumar et al., focusing on photocatalytic hydrogen generation application of vacancy-engineered materials.211

SnO2 doping can be of two types: (1) p-type doping and (2) n-type doping. p-Type doping can be introduced with cations of lower valency than that of Sn, acting as electron acceptors, whereas n-type doping can be introduced with cations having higher valency than of Sn, acting as electron donors (Fig. 35).

4.2 Chemical additives

Chemical additives such as scavengers and sacrificial reagents play a substantial role in improving the photocatalytic performance. After the formation of photogenerated electrons and holes, there is a probability of recombination. If recombination occurs predominantly, it causes a waste of energy and minimizes the quantum yield.219 To avoid recombination, electron donors, also known as hole scavengers or sacrificial reagents, are used in photocatalytic water splitting.220,221 They remarkably enhance the H2 yield by irreversibly reacting with the VB holes, resulting in electronhole separation and higher quantum efficiency. They are consumed in the reaction, and hence, to sustain H2 production, their continual addition is required.222

Alcohols, acids, hydrocarbons, and various organic compounds have been used as electron donors in water splitting. Zielinska et al. compared various organic donors such as acetic acid, formic acid, formaldehyde, 2-propanol, and methanol, and found that formic acid is the most efficient organic donor for H2 evolution.223 Nada et al. discussed the role of various sacrificial donors and ranked them based on the enhancement in the amount of H2 evolved as EDTA > methanol > ethanol > lactic acid.224 A sacrificial agent helps SnO2 to carry out either oxidation or reduction. The primary function is to react with one of the charge carriers. Simultaneously, another charge carrier is made free to perform either oxidation or reduction. Chemicals whose oxidation potentials are lower than that of water are ethanol, methanol, and ethylene glycol, which are used as sacrificial agents to inhibit electronhole recombination. Haw et al. showed the mechanism for the generation of hydrogen gas using SnO2 QDs from non-aqueous medium, i.e., glycerol and aqueous phase, and S2/SO32 as the hole scavengers (Fig. 39).225

4.3 Dye sensitization

Dye sensitization is another approach to enhance the photocatalytic performance. Some dyes are sensitive to visible light, absorb it, and then transfer the excited electrons to the semiconductor photocatalyst, preventing charge carrier recombination and enhance the efficiency of water splitting reactions. Sensitizers act as an antenna that transfer absorbed light to the semiconductor and help in carrying out the excitation of electrons. Some dyes are sensitive to visible light and hence can be used in photocatalytic reactions. When irradiated with visible light, electrons from excited dyes are injected into the CB of semiconductors, which further carries out necessary photocatalytic reactions to reduce H2O to generate H2 gas.198,226

Yang et al. studied the H2 evolution rate using Sb-doped SnO2 sensitized by EY under irradiation by light (Fig. 42). 4.4 Heterostructure

It is reported in the literature that when SnO2 is coupled with other semiconductors having a small band gap whose CB lies at a more negative value, then the CB electrons are transferred from a small band gap semiconductor to the CB of SnO2. This method is known as semiconductor coupling. Coupling or semiconductor composition is an efficient method that allows the utilization of a large portion of visible light for enhanced photocatalytic results. There should be a difference in the band gap values for the successful coupling of the semiconductors to prepare the composite. As the band gap of SnO2 is large, it should be coupled with a small band gap semiconductor whose CB level is at a more negative position than SnO2, and there should be an easy and fast electron transfer from SnO2 to the semiconductor. It makes the electrons available for H2O reduction to generate H2 gas, provided that the semiconductors should be photocatalytically stable and not undergo photocorrosion.

It has been reported that SnO2 coupled with CuO leads to higher photocatalytic H2 generation.228 SnO2 is an n-type semiconductor, whereas CuO is a p-type semiconductor and their coupling results in the formation of an n-SnO2/p-CuO heterojunction at the interface. CB of CuO is at a lower level than SnO2. When irradiated with light, photogenerated electrons and holes occur in the CB and VB of SnO2, respectively. The transfer of electron occurs from SnO2 to the CB of CuO. This migration effectively separates the photogenerated electrons and holes on the SnO2 surface, resulting in enhanced photocatalytic activity. 5. Concept of SnO2-based photoelectrochemical H2 generation

Photoelectrochemical reactions come under a particular case of photochemical reactions. In photoelectrochemical water splitting reactions, the electric current is directed to carry out redox chemical reactions at the electrode surface to transfer electrons to produce H2 gas.254 Sheza et al. incorporated SnO2 NPs into g-C3N4 sheets to prepare the g-C3N4/SnO2 nanocomposite. 6.1 Chemical oxidants

External donors or acceptors play an essential role in photocatalytic water splitting along with NPs and improve the photocatalytic degradation of water contaminants. This electron improved efficacy is either due to (1) prevention of recombination of charge carriers, taking electrons in the CB, (2) increase in the hydroxyl radical concentration or other oxidizing species necessary to carry out degradation, and (3) increase in the intermediate compounds oxidation rate.

The few reported electron acceptors in the literature are H2O2, KBrO3, and K2S2O8.86,187,296 Metal oxides have resulted in higher degradation rates of pesticides or herbicides with these oxidants. H2O2 is the most commonly used oxidant for this application and has been found to be cost-effective. H2O2 addition is known to increase the concentration of hydroxyl radicals, and ultimately, recombination between the electrons and holes is prevented due to reaction with electrons in the CB. Miguel et al. studied the effect of H2O2 using rutile-structured TiO2 for the photocatalytic degradation of 44 organic pesticides in natural water (Ebro river basin in Spain).297 H2O2, being a powerful oxidant, prevents the recombination of photogenerated electronhole pairs. On the other hand, as an electron acceptor, it captures electrons present in the CB to produce radicals for surface reaction necessary for degradation. Besides having beneficial effects as an oxidant and electron acceptor, H2O2 sometimes has damaging effects on the degradation process. It proves to be helpful at an optimum concentration range, but the beneficial effects do not persist more at higher concentrations. The inhibition effects could be explained by the surface modification of the photocatalyst by H2O2 adsorption, scavenging photogenerated holes in VB, or consuming hydroxyl radicals. Thus, for favorable results, an optimum ratio of H2O2 concentration to the organic pollutant is a must to avoid detrimental effects.298

Other oxidants such as peroxodisulphate, chlorate, iodate, and bromate are also considered to enhance the effectiveness of degradation reactions. Among these, peroxodisulphate attracts more attention as it generates sulfate anion radical (SO4) upon decomposition, which is itself a powerful oxidant. However, chlorate has not gained much importance for increasing the effectiveness compared to iodate and bromate.299 They are known to trigger photocatalytic degradation by increasing the concentration of hydroxyl radicals, inhibiting the recombination of electronhole pairs. The addition of chemical oxidants is beneficial for optimum concentration only because the benefits do not persist as the concentration exceeds.

6.2 SnO2 heterojunction

The concept of heterojunction formation is important to facilitate easy electron transfer to carry out essential reduction and oxidation reactions. In the type II heterojunction C-TiO2/CoTiO3, C doping was performed to extend the region of visible light absorption and also upshifted the position of the VB of TiO2 to match the band structure with CoTiO3. The so-formed heterojunction exhibited enhanced photocatalytic degradation activity.300,301 Similarly, zinc-based oxide heterojunctions have been known as visible light-active photocatalysts for photocatalytic hydrogen generation and environmental remediation. SnO2 heterojunctions/nanocomposites are very well known in many application fields such as photoreduction and antimicrobial behavior.275,302,303 SnO2, when supported over graphene, exhibits remarkable results in the degradation of organic pollutants. Wang et al. prepared the Cu2O/SnO2/graphene nanocomposites and evaluated their photocatalytic performance through the degradation of pendimethalin. The transfer of photoexcited electrons from the SnO2 surface to graphene lowers the recombination of photogenerated charge carriers and hence showed better photocatalytic degradation reactions.279 Yadav et al. found that the ZnO/SnO2 nanocomposite showed promising results for the photocatalytic degradation of pesticides compared to pure oxides ZnO and SnO2. On light irradiation, the photogenerated electrons and holes are carried by the CB of SnO2 and the VB of ZnO; in this way, the heterojunction formed has an essential role in the separation of charge carriers and thus has comparatively better results compared to pristine SnO2.282 SnO2:Cu2O could degrade trifluralin after 2 h of light irradiation. On light irradiation, photo-induced holes were generated from Cu2O. Further, the lower position of the VB of SnO2 than that of Cu2O leads to the oxidation of holes generated from SnO2 to carry out the degradation of trifluralin.281 Mohanta et al. proposed the degradation performance of clothianidin using a ternary heterojunction (AuSnO2rGO) (Fig. 47). 97% degradation of the pesticide was shown after 120 min of UV-LED irradiation, thus proposing two degradation mechanisms. Superoxide radical anion species were found to be responsible for the effective degradation of pesticides and were investigated using LC/HRMS and ESR techniques. Excitation causes the distribution of holes and electrons on their respective VB and CB. Pt acts as a bridge for carrier transport. Photogenerated electrons and holes in the CB of In2O3 and the VB of SnO2, respectively, can be protected for desired redox reaction by the Pt recombination center. The formation potential of hydroxyl and superoxide radicals occurs as the SnO2 VB potential is more positive and the In2O3 CB potential is more negative, respectively. Thus, the hydroxyl and superoxide radicals formed can react with 2,4-DCP to generate small organic molecules, which are degraded to CO2 and H2O by mineralization.156

7. Industrial approach

Indisputably, the SnO2-based NMs has garnered enormous reputation as a photocatalyst. Beyond this, these materials have also been explored on the industrial scale for several applications. Industries have widely implemented SnO2-based sensors to detect gases and other volatile compounds.304306 SnO2-Based sensors exhibiting temperature sensitivity have been developed, which exhibit low operating temperature, as well as low production cost and power requirement, making them useful for industrial applications.304 Recent investigations have revealed the importance of SnO2 as a potential alternative to TiO2 in PSC, on account of the optimal energy level alignment, high electron mobility, outstanding stability under heat, and light. Recently, an SnO2-based PSC displayed a phenomenal performance with power conversion efficiency exceeding 25%, which has attracted the attention of researchers and people from the industry.307309 The industrial application of SnO2 in dye-sensitized solar cells is not compared to conventionally established materials. SnO2 nanocomposites, ultrathin SnO2 layers, SnO2 nanofibers, SnO2 microspheres, etc., have been responding excellently in dye-sensitized solar cells.310313 On considering the risk evolution, there is a need to highlight that SnO2 is generally considered as non-toxic owing to the lack of toxicity of bulk material.314,315 Owing to structural changes required to accommodate the volume changes during the chargedischarge process, SnO2 also stands out as an attractive electrode for Li-ion batteries at an industrial scale.

There has been immense interest by scientists for the innovation of optimal photocatalysts that possess improved quantum efficiency and photostability for H2 generation. However, the scientific literature is limited; therefore, innovative strategies for optimizing reactor design is very important for industrial implementation. A recent investigation by Yan et al. has demonstrated a novel strategy for the fabrication of SnO2 QDs and g-C3N4 for photocatalytic H2 generation. They have demonstrated enhanced photostability and quantum efficiency of the proposed material.201

From an industrial perspective, designing cost-effective photocatalytic reactors for the generation of H2 or O2 generated through photochemical water splitting can be stored into cylinders and used to run vehicles (replacing fossil fuels), while oxygen cylinders can be used to meet the oxygen crisis during a pandemic.316

8. Challenges and future aspects

Various studies and reports have projected SnO2-based NMs as an excellent photocatalyst to resolve energy-related environmental crisis by H2 generation and residual pesticide degradation, which pose a serious threat to human health, aquatic life, etc. Hence, SnO2 has proved to be a potential alternative, which can provide a sustainable solution to burgeoning environmental issues. To increase SnO2 applications, attempts for surface modification using adsorbents, carbon material coupling, various dopants, and co-dopants are required, which demands constant improvization in existing knowledge. In addition, applications could be widened when different morphologies such as nanowires, nanosheets,317 nanocomposites, nanorods, and nanospheres are taken into account. Morphological tuning exhibits comparatively better performance owing to the nanodimensions. However, to mitigate the environmental concerns and address sustainable energy resources, serious efforts are required to develop a cost effective green synthetic protocol for SnO2-based materials. Nanoscale compounds have the ability to form heteroepitaxial junctions, which has been validated for the SnO2@TiO2 heterostructure. It has improved the photocatalytic activity and has a high potential for several photocatalytic reactions.204

Further, the implementation of Z-scheme-based mechanism for the photocatalyst using SnO2 has the ability to mimic the chlorophyll-mediated degradation, and therefore, could be a suitable alternate for pollutant degradation. This is because the Z-scheme approach enhances the spectral scope, carrier separation, as well as the redox capacity. Recently, Bao et al. demonstrated the role of carrier transport medium in the interceding of Z-scheme hybrid heterostructures, where the CB electron of the photocatalyst migrates via the carrier medium to pair with the VB holes in the other photocatalyst. They fabricated a flower-like SnO2@g-C3N4 nanocomposite with improved photocatalytic performance. Here, the Sn2+/Sn4+ redox pair plays a critical role as the charge medium, facilitating H2O adsorption and carrier separation, consequently proposing an affirmative strategy for the innovation of Z-scheme-mediated photocatalytic nanocomposites.144 Another major limitation pertaining to the development of photocatalysts is the generation of electronhole pairs and also their lifetime. Recently, a novel strategy was proposed by Yan et al., in which SnO2@g-C3N4 has an in-build electric field at the interface of carbon and SnO2, resulting in a significant increase in the electronhole pair lifetime, thereby boosting the photocatalytic efficiency.201 Consequently, this novel approach can be utilized for heterostructure construction with improved photocatalytic performance.

The need for a sustainable development path and the need to cope with global water pollution issues require the development of novel and effective ways to detect and remove pollutants. With appropriately modified SnO2 along with band gap engineering, generating hydrogen gas will satisfy the energy demands in the future and will save the earth from exploitation, being a cleaner fuel.

9. Conclusions

This review emphasizes on the innovative strategies executed by researchers to replace fossil fuel consumption with energetically abundant resources (sun and water). Also, it promises to play a hand toward environmental conservation via promising photocatalytic pesticide degradation without any harmful residues. SnO2 (rutile) along with other characteristics related to the band structure, oxygen vacancies, etc., have been elaborated for better understanding. A detailed discussion on the synthetic strategies to access SnO2-based NMs have been incorporated. Each modification leads to a subtle variation in the band structure of SnO2, which in turn affects their photocatalytic performance. Experimental findings in conjunction with theoretical explanation indicated that these modifications have made a positive impact on the photocatalytic activity of SnO2-based NMs. Rationally designed SnO2-based NMs can tune the need for a sustainable path by satisfying future energy demands. Fundamental and applied knowledge gained from SnO2-based devices will thus be invaluable for researchers in designing next-generation materials for emerging technologies in energy conversion and storage, pesticide degradation, and gas sensing under moderate and extreme environments. Henceforth, this review presents in-depth knowledge and opens up new possibilities for researchers to extend the energy and environment-related aspects to SnO2, photocatalytic water splitting, and pesticide degradation. The researchers working in this field will be encouraged further to take up challenges for the synthesis of currently unexplored SnO2-based NMs with extensive photocatalytic performance and exploration in different fields.
