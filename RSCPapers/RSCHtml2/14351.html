Photochromism into nanosystems: towards lighting up the future nanoworld

Ling Wang and Quan Li *
Liquid Crystal Institute and Chemical Physics Interdisciplinary Program, Kent State University, Kent, Ohio 44242, USA. E-mail: qli1@kent.edu

Received 7th October 2017

First published on 18th December 2017

Abstract

The ability to manipulate the structure and function of promising nanosystems via energy input and external stimuli is emerging as an attractive paradigm for developing reconfigurable and programmable nanomaterials and multifunctional devices. Light stimulus manifestly represents a preferred external physical and chemical tool for in situ remote command of the functional attributes of nanomaterials and nanosystems due to its unique advantages of high spatial and temporal resolution and digital controllability. Photochromic moieties are known to undergo reversible photochemical transformations between different states with distinct properties, which have been extensively introduced into various functional nanosystems such as nanomachines, nanoparticles, nanoelectronics, supramolecular nanoassemblies, and biological nanosystems. The integration of photochromism into these nanosystems has endowed the resultant nanostructures or advanced materials with intriguing photoresponsive behaviors and more sophisticated functions. In this Review, we provide an account of the recent advancements in reversible photocontrol of the structures and functions of photochromic nanosystems and their applications. The important design concepts of such truly advanced materials are discussed, their fabrication methods are emphasized, and their applications are highlighted. The Review is concluded by briefly outlining the challenges that need to be addressed and the opportunities that can be tapped into. We hope that the review of the flourishing and vibrant topic with myriad possibilities would shine light on exploring the future nanoworld by encouraging and opening the windows to meaningful multidisciplinary cooperation of engineers from different backgrounds and scientists from the fields such as chemistry, physics, engineering, biology, nanotechnology and materials science.

1. Introduction

Nanotechnology which focuses on the observation and manipulation of matter at the nanoscale is recognized as a mainstream technology of the 21st century.13 During the last few decades, the scope of nanotechnology has grown from complete obscurity and just a scientific curiosity at the beginning into a truly interdisciplinary and cutting-edge endeavour and an enabling industrial enterprise worth trillions of dollars. Through revolutionary nanotechnology, an unprecedented variety of new intelligent materials and sophisticated devices could be expected, which might find applications in various fields ranging from the energy and safety issues to global public health. In the future nanoworld, it will no longer be a fantasy for us to experience how medical nanodevices would work in our body for therapeutic purposes like what happened in the old Fantastic Voyage movie. However, it might not be possible to talk about the scope of nanoscience without mentioning the scientific giant Richard P. Feynman, the 1965 Nobel Prize Laureate in Physics. In the Annual Meeting of the American Physical Society in 1959, he drew the world's attention to the possibilities of manipulating and controlling matter on a small scale with his famous and visionary talk There's plenty of room at the bottom, in which he brilliantly outlined and projected numerous possibilities in the future nanoworld such as manipulating individual atoms and molecules, miniaturizing computer components to reach the atomic scale, and the challenge of fabricating very small devices such as making an infinitesimal machine like an automobile.4,5 In the 1990s, the invention of the scanning tunneling microscope (STM), and the atomic force microscope (AFM) and other breakthroughs laid a solid foundation for today's nanotechnology,69 thus greatly bridging the chasm between our macroscopic world and the nanoscopic world of individual molecules or atoms, nanoparticles and functional nanostructures. Since then, massive research efforts and tremendous progress have been made towards exploring the nanoworld.

One of the promising strategies to explore nanoscience and nanotechnology is the bottom-up approach, in which well-defined nanostructures are built by the direct use of nano- or subnano-scale objects such as atoms or molecules. However, manipulating and controlling nanoscale objects and their nanostructured material properties turned out to be far more difficult and very challenging. The ability to controllably handle the smallest matter is a fundamental enabling technology for nanoscience and nanotechnology. In this regard, multitudinous nanosystems that can respond to various external stimuli such as light, temperature, mechanical forces, ultrasound waves and electromagnetic fields have been developed.1014 Of the exogenous stimuli-responsive nanosystems, those driven by light have aroused enormous interest since light as a stimulus is not only noninvasive with a high level of remote spatiotemporal resolution,15,16 but also widely utilized in natural and living systems such as human vision and photosynthesis in plants.17,18 In 1986, Ashkin et al. pioneered the optical tweezers technique that used a focused laser beam to manipulate tiny microscale objects based on the trapping forces from laser radiation pressure.1921 Since their discovery, optical tweezers have become important tools for exploring the world of biology, physical chemistry, nanoscience and nanotechnology as well as soft condensed matter physics. To on-command trap nanoscale objects of a few tens of nanometers in size, various optical NanoTweezers with higher trapping forces have been recently developed according to near-field optical trapping techniques, such as plasmonic optical tweezers, slot waveguides and whispering-gallery-mode resonators.2234 Although they allow us to physically control, move and rotate nano/microscale targets without physical contact, optical tweezers suffer from certain limitations and drawbacks such as a complicated setup and the need for a highly focused laser beam.

The most studied and versatile method of optical manipulation at the nanoscale has been to introduce photochromism into nanosystems, which directly endows the resultant atomic or molecular building blocks, and well-ordered nanostructures or advanced materials with fantastic photoresponsive capabilities and more sophisticated functions. The word photochromism derives from two Greek words meaning light and colour, which originally referred to the phenomenon of light-induced reversible colour changes.35,36 Nowadays, photochromism has become a more general term used for describing the reversible chemical transformation of a chemical species between two forms with distinguishably different absorption under appropriate electromagnetic radiations such as UV, visible, and infrared illuminations.3741 The interconversion between distinct states is typically accompanied by evident changes in certain chemical and physical properties of photochromic systems including absorption spectra, fluorescence emission, refractive index, dielectric constant, electron conductivity, magnetic properties, electrochemical properties, dipole interaction, coordination properties, solubility, viscosity, etc. Photochromism could be observed in both organic and inorganic compounds, as well as in biological systems. There are various organic compounds that exhibit photochromism, such as some hydrazones,42,43 sulfoxides,44,45 indigos,46,47 semicarbazones,48 dihydropyrenes,49 stilbene derivatives,50o-nitrobenzyl derivatives,51 and donoracceptor stenhouse adducts.52,53 Upon light irradiation, the most common processes of photochromism in these compounds are trans-to-cis isomerization and pericyclic reactions. Typical photochromic molecules including azobenzenes, stilbenes, dithienylethenes, and spiropyrans, and their photoisomerization processes are illustrated in Fig. 1. Azobenzenes could experience trans-to-cis isomerization under UV irradiation, and the cis isomer would be driven back to its trans form either by visible light or by thermal relaxation.54,55 Like azobenzenes, stilbenes undergo an analogous trans-to-cis isomerization under UV irradiation, but the cis form is often metastable having the tendency to undergo irreversible cyclization or oxidation.56 Diarylethenes can transform from the colourless ring-open state to the coloured ring-closed state under UV irradiation. The reverse process is thermally stable and can be only driven by visible light irradiation.57 For spiropyrans, UV irradiation leads to photochemical cleavage of the CO bond and transformation from the initial colourless state to the coloured merocyanine.58 When the UV source is removed, they can thermally relax back to colourless initial states quickly. It should be noted that a large change in polarity and charge distribution could be obtained upon photoisomerization between the spiropyran state and the merocyanine state. In short, these photochromic molecules could be reversibly interconverted between two isomeric states with distinct chemical structures and consequently different molecular, optical, electronic and/or magnetic properties upon appropriate light irradiation. Photochromic molecules have received vast attention in multidisciplinary fields such as chemistry, physics, biology and nanotechnology. There is no doubt that photochromism into nanosystems would greatly light up our future nanoworld, where photochromic molecules could function not only as tiny light-driven molecular manipulators, actuators and engines, but also as smallest optical memory and logical devices in data storage and information technology as well as in superresolution imaging and nanoscopy beyond the optical diffraction limit.

This Review is an attempt to highlight the recent developments and advancements in diverse light-driven dynamic nanosystems functionalized with photochromic moieties under a single umbrella. For different nanosystems, some fundamentals including design principles, fabrication as well as their potential applications are discussed, in which sufficient background is succinctly furnished wherever warranted. We begin with an overview of photochromism into nanomachines, where light-driven rotaxane-based molecular machines and rotary molecular motors are mainly introduced. We then showcase the design, fabrication and applications of multitudinous photoresponsive nanosystems including nanoparticles (noble metal nanoparticles, mesoporous silica nanoparticles, fluorescent nanoparticles and magnetic nanoparticles), nanoelectronics (molecular junctions, organic field-effect transistors and photoswitchable superconductivity), metalorganic nanoassemblies (metalorganic molecular cages and metalorganic frameworks), supramolecular self-assembled systems (supramolecular amphiphiles, supramolecular polymers and liquid crystalline superstructures) and biological nanosystems (photoswitchable nucleic acids, photocontrolled peptides and proteins as well as photoswitchable ion channels and receptors). The last section of this Review provides an outlook on the future scope, challenges and opportunities of photochromism into nanosystems. We believe that it is necessary to assemble the scattered literature of this truly interdisciplinary vibrant and exciting research field, which is expected to provide fascinating impetus towards further rapid development of this intertwined endeavor embracing photochromism, nanoscience, nanotechnology, intelligent advanced materials and beyond.

2. Photochromism into nanomachines

Nanomachines or molecular machines are omnipresent in nature. Almost every biological process involves molecular machines, from the basic cell movement, transporting cargoes around the cell, muscular contractions to harvesting the solar energy, etc.59 As mentioned above, Richard Feynman proposed the possibility of manufacturing infinitesimal machinery.60 Inspired by biological phenomena and the success of man-made machines in the macroscopic world, extensive efforts have been currently devoted to developing nanomachines or molecular machinery. A molecular machine is typically defined as an assembly of many distinct molecular components that are designed to perform machine like movements (output) as a result of an appropriate external stimulation (input).61 In the past several decades, various stimuli-driven molecular machines were designed and synthesized.6266 The 2016 Nobel Prize in Chemistry has been awarded to Jean-Pierre Sauvage, J. Fraser Stoddart and Ben L. Feringa, for the design and synthesis of molecular machines.67,68 Two major technologies have been established to construct many complex nanostructures with machine-like functions. The first is based on mechanical bonds with topological entanglement and the second involves the isomerisable double bonds. Herein, recent developments in light-powered dynamic nanomachines are discussed. Operating molecular machines by light energy stimulation offers multitudinous advantages. As a renewable energy source, external optical irradiation could act not only as energy quanta in photochemically operating processes, but also as an information element for monitoring the dynamic motions of nanomachines.69,70 Moreover, light stimuli could be applied to nanomachines without physical contact, and the energy quanta could be carefully controlled by tuning the wavelength, intensity, and polarization of light.

2.1 Rotaxane-based molecular machines

Rotaxanes and their derivatives are the most investigated systems towards molecular machinery, where the individual parts of interlocked molecular assemblies based on mechanical bonds are not directly held together by covalent bonds but inseparably entangled through, for example, loops and stoppers; thus they could move relative to each other in a controllable manner.71,72 As shown in Fig. 2a and b, rotaxanes are minimally composed of a dumbbell-shaped molecule which is surrounded by the ring of macrocyclic compound and terminated by the stoppers of bulky groups. When the stoppers are absent, the assembled species are often called as pseudorotaxanes where the axle-type and ring components are separated in solution (Fig. 2c). In the molecular machines based on rotaxanes, the mechanical bond could simultaneously stabilize the machinery system and enable various mutual arrangements of the molecular components. The amplitude of the intercomponent motion in the three directions is limited by the interlocked architecture and the stability of a specific arrangement is typically determined by the strength of the intercomponent interactions that can be facilely modulated by external optical control. It should be noted that both translational and rotational molecular motions could be obtained in rotaxanes, corresponding to the shuttling of the ring along the axle (Fig. 2a) and the rotation of the ring around the axle (Fig. 2b), respectively.7375

Artificial molecular muscles with collective extension and contraction effects have been developed to achieve the macroscopic mechanical task by combining the topology of rotaxane dimers with the controllable molecular shuttle of the resulting machines.7679 Dawson et al. demonstrated a light-driven molecular muscle.80 In the rotaxane dimer, -cyclodextrin (-CD) worked as the macrocyclic component while photoresponsive stilbene units functioned as the axle component. It should be noted that trans-stilbene moieties could be readily accommodated within -CD, but not cis-stilbene groups. Light-driven dynamic transformation between the trans- and cis-stilbene isomers would cause the CDs to move between the binding sites. The movement of the CDs off the stilbene shortened the distance between the blocking groups and the length of the rotaxane. As a result, the extended, intermediate, and contracted states of the molecular muscle were achieved with appropriate optical control. The same principle has been exploited to develop a light-driven molecular information ratchet. The rotaxane was made of a dibenzo-24-crown-8-based macrocycle which was mechanically locked onto a linear thread by bulky 3,5-di-t-butylphenyl groups situated at either end.81 The -methylstilbene unit was used to divide the axle asymmetrically into two compartments. In the trans state of the stilbene unit, the macrocycle moved randomly along the full length of the axle, whereas the ring was trapped in one of the two compartments in the cis state of the stilbene. Upon different light irradiation, the information about the macrocycle location would determine the kinetics of the macrocycle shuttling between two compartments on the axle. The molecular machines were thus operated by an information ratchet mechanism, where the knowledge of a particle's position could be utilized to control its transport away from equilibrium.

Avellini et al. reported light-directed memory effects in a photochromic dynamic molecular rotaxane shuttle,82 where optical and orthogonal chemical stimuli were used to achieve full control of the residence of the target rings between two different sites on the axle. As shown in Fig. 3a, functional multicomponent units were incorporated into the rotaxane. The ring component was -electron-deficient cyclobis(paraquat-p-phenylene) (CBPQT4+). The -electron donor recognition sites of the dumbbell component were comprised of 1,5-dioxynaphthalene (DNP) and tetrathiafulvalene (TTF). A photoresponsive 3,5,3,5-tetramethylazobenzene (TMeAB) unit was located between the DNP and TTF units, which could be reversibly modulated between its cis and trans configurations upon light irradiation. The CBPQT4+ ring preferred to reside on the TTF unit rather than on the DNP unit because the TTF unit is more -electron-rich than the DNP unit. Fig. 3b illustrates the switching cycle of the resulting rotaxane. Oxidation of TTF resulted in the shuttling of CBPQT4+ from the TTF+ to the DNP site. UV light irradiation chemically converted the TMeAB unit from trans to cis configuration. The neutral TTF unit with CBPQT4+ resided on the DNP unit after back reduction. Subsequent photochemical or thermal cis to trans back isomerization opened the gate and therefore enabled the replacement of CBPQT4+ onto the TTF primary recognition site. This kind of system could be used for signal processing and data storage. The data could be firstly written on the rotaxane by chemical oxidation, and then locked by UV irradiation. It should be noted that the oxidized species could be reduced back to the original state without losing the written data for a remarkably longer time. The light irradiation herein not only protected the nonoxidized rotaxanes from accidental writing, but also locked the data previously recorded by oxidation.

The supramolecular pseudorotaxane complexes composed of an axle-type guest and a ring-type host are another important prototype of artificial molecular machines.83 Credi et al. demonstrated the light-driven threadingdethreading of a pseudorotaxane in solution based on photoinduced proton transfer.84 Stable pseudorotaxane structures with 4,4-bipyridinium derivatives were formed by tris(N-phenylureido)calix[6]arene C in apolar solvents.85 The compound AH2+, obtained by protonation of the pyridine nitrogen of 4,4-pyridylpyridinium axle-like A+, could thread into the cavity of C. Deprotonation of AH2+ with a base such as tributylamine in CH2Cl2 would, however, lead to dethreading of the pseudorotaxane. To achieve the light-driven self-assembly and disassembly of this pseudorotaxane, photochromic spiropyran molecules with photocontrolled acidbase properties were introduced. It is known that the colourless SP would be converted into the yellow protonated merocyanine form MEH+ in the presence of an acid.86 Visible light irradiation of MEH+ led to backisomerisation to SP which released a proton.87 The switching cycle of pseudorotaxane is illustrated in Fig. 4. In the mixture of the complex [CAH]2+ and SP, a thermal proton transfer from AH2+ to the SP resulted in the formation of MEH+ and deprotonated guest A+, thus A+ dethreads from the cavity of C. Subsequent visible light irradiation of MEH+ led to an opposite proton transfer and A+ was converted into AH2+. The AH2+ would rethread into the calixarene macrocycle, which provides an interesting platform for light-driven dynamic operation of photoinactive acidbase controllable molecular machines.

In 2012, Credi et al. reported the design, construction and dynamic operation of another supramolecular assembly, where the molecular axle could pass unidirectionally through the cavity of a molecular ring under chemical and photochemical stimulation.88,89 The pseudorotaxane was composed of a molecular ring and an interesting nonsymmetric molecular axle. The molecular axle was made of a passive pseudostopper (D) at one end, a central recognition site (S) for the ring, and a bistable photoswitchable unit (P) at the other end. In the initial state, the ring could thread onto the axle through the trans-azobenzene unit, and thus a pseudorotaxane with the ring at the recognition site S was achieved. UV irradiation would isomerise the trans-azobenzene unit towards its cis-configuration, which generated a high steric barrier from the ring's dissociation. The dethreading of the ring over the D end of the axle could be realized by adding K+ ions as a competitive guest for the crown ether ring. The visible-light-driven reverse cis-to-trans isomerization of azobenzene would enable the rethreading of the ring. They further demonstrated light-driven dynamic unidirectional motion in another supramolecular system without the addition of a competitive guest.90 As illustrated in Fig. 5a, the molecular components of the system were made of the molecular axle and the macrocyclic ring 2,3-dinaphtho[24]crown-8 ether. On the molecular axle, a photoswitchable trans-azobenzene unit was located at one end, a passive methylcyclopentyl pseudo-stopper at the other end, and a central ammonium recognition site for the macrocycle. As illustrated in Fig. 5b, the ring entered exclusively from the trans-azobenzene side of the axle, affording the pseudorotaxane where the macrocycle encircled the recognition site because of the hydrogen-bonding interactions between the ammonium centre and the oxygen atoms, as well as the -stacking forces involving the azobenzene and naphthalene units. Photoinduced trans-to-cis isomerization of photochromic azobenzene would result in destabilization of the supramolecular complex and dethreading of the components. The reverse cis-to-trans isomerization of azobenzene upon visible light irradiation would enable the rethreading of the ring into the axle once again. The cyclic motion driven solely by light is expected to open a new door for light-directed dynamic motion with a minimalist molecular design.

It is a vital task to develop molecular machines in order to generate considerable external force in the macroscopic world by using controlled molecular-level motion. Leigh et al. demonstrated the light-driven macroscopic directional transport of the liquid droplets by using the nanometer displacement of photoswitchable molecular shuttles on the surface.91 The dynamic change in the resulting surface properties was used to propel the droplet along a surface and up a slope that was against the force of gravity as shown in Fig. 6. Upon UV irradiation of the liquid drop and adjacent section of the surface, the trans/cis ratio of shuttles on the surface began to decrease and the irradiated surface became less polarophobic. The decrease in the contact angle at the right-hand side of the liquid droplet led to a Laplace pressure gradient inside the droplet, and thus the forward edge of the droplet began to creep along the surface which resulted in the elongation of the droplet and the wetting of the surface. The droplet continued to spread further along the surface with the progressive decrease of the trans/cis ratio on the irradiated surface. When the original position of the rear end of the droplet became so energetically unfavourable at a critical point, the progressive driving force that increased the wetting at the front end of the droplet would result in contraction of the liquid droplet from the rear. As a result, sudden and quick transport of the liquid droplet towards the low trans/cis ratio region of the surface could be observed.

2.2 Light-driven rotary molecular motors

Motors are central to any molecular machine which essentially drive the function of overall mechanical construction and move the entire system far from equilibrium.92 The development of molecular motors has therefore been key to promoting the field of nanomachines.9395 Although rotational movement was achieved in various molecular machines including rotaxane systems, the rotation angle was rather limited and the goal remained elusive.9699 In 1999, Feringa et al. made a breakthrough by designing and synthesizing the first example of a rotary molecular motor based on an over-crowded alkene molecule, where two identical chiral components were connected by a central carboncarbon double bond as shown in Fig. 7a.100 Upon irradiation at 355 nm, the central double bond photoisomerised from the most stable cis conformer to a higher energy trans-conformer. Next, favourable helicity inversion facilitated by thermal fluctuations gave the stable trans-conformer. A second photo-excitation converted the compound to a higher energy cis conformer that underwent thermal helicity inversion to return the system to the initial conformational state. Complete 360 rotation of one-half of the molecule with respect to the other could be thus enabled by photoisomerization followed by strain-induced diastereomeric helix inversion. The continuous rotation could be obtained as long as the compound was irradiated by constant UV light above a critical temperature. In the past decades, the structural improvements of these molecular motors have dramatically increased the rate of rotation. The structure of the second-generation motor as shown in Fig. 7b allows for efficiently and optically tuning the motor's properties, where the speed of the photochemical step is of the order of 1 or 2 ps. Interestingly, the motor was found to be very effective at inducing helical organization in a liquid-crystal film, and the molecular motor in a liquid-crystal film could be used to rotate a macroscopic object thousands of times larger than itself when photoirradiated (Fig. 7c).101,102

It is worth noting that advanced molecular motors based on over-crowded alkenes have been developed for diverse applications such as switching the chirality of an organocatalyst and acting as the propeller of nanocars.103105 In 2006, Tour et al. demonstrated for the first time a motorized nanocar which bears a light-activated unidirectional over-crowded alkene molecular motor and an oligo(phenylene ethynylene) chassis and axle system with four carboranes to serve as the wheels (Fig. 8).106 It was found that the motor indeed rotates upon irradiation with 365 nm light, where the fullerene-free carborane wheel system is an essential design feature for motor operation. They also reported the photoinduced translation of molecular machines across a Cu(111) surface by characterizing single molecules both before and after illumination.107 To build a four-wheel-drive molecular nanocar, Feringa et al. designed and synthesized an unprecedented molecule with four chiral units of unidirectional rotary motors (Fig. 9).108 The rotary motors underwent geometric changes as a result of sequential configurational and conformational switching induced by electronic and vibrational excitation, respectively. The activation of the conformational changes of the molecular rotors through inelastic electron tunneling could propel unidirectional motion of the molecule across a Cu(111) surface. Recently, they further reported a molecular rotary motor that translated light-driven unidirectional rotary motion to controlled movement of a connected biaryl rotor.109 It was found that achieving coupled motion of the distinct parts of this multicomponent mechanical system required precise control of multiple kinetic barriers for isomerization and synchronous motion, resulting in sliding and rotation during a full rotary cycle, with the motor always facing the same face of the rotor. These works would undoubtedly open the door for exploring more sophisticated molecular machines with directionally controlled motion.

Inspired by the ATP-ase system,110 Feringa et al. reported an artificial surface-mounted motor. Functionalization of the light-driven molecular motor by two alkyl thiols enabled the self-assembly onto a gold nanoparticle surface (1-Au).111 As shown in Fig. 10, two light-driven dynamic isomerization processes (steps 1 and 3) each followed by thermal helix inversion (steps 2 and 4) resulted in a full 360 rotation of one-half of the molecule with respect to the other half. UV irradiation of the stable (2R)-(M)-isomer on 1-Au resulted in cis-to-trans isomerization, which inverted the helicity of the resulting system towards the (2R)-(P)-isomer. Under thermal relaxation, this unstable isomer underwent helix inversion towards the initial (2R)-(M)-isomer, where a net 180 rotation of the upper half of the molecule happened in an anticlockwise fashion with respect to the lower half. In this way, a full rotary cycle was completed if the sequence was repeated in steps 3 and 4. It is expected that the development of such fully functional surface-mounted rotors should be a large step towards the construction of more elaborate nanomachines that controllably work on solid surfaces.


Fig. 10 A light-driven reversible rotary motor immobilized on a gold surface. Reprinted with permission from ref. 111. Copyright 2005 Nature Publishing Group.

To perform the macroscopic work by light-powered dynamic molecular machines, Giuseppone et al. have recently introduced the molecular motors that rotated at high frequencies into polymer gels, and light-driven dynamic contractions of these materials were achieved at both the nanoscopic and macroscopic levels as shown in Fig. 11.112 In the experiments, the motor was functionalized with four arms, two poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) chains with azide functions at their termini, and the other two PEG chains terminated by propargyl groups. The polymer gels were synthesized by copper-catalyzed azidealkyne cycloaddition (CuAAC) reactions under high concentrations. Upon UV irradiation, the polymer chains coiled up, thus reducing the overall dimensions of the millimetre-sized gels. The actuation was believed to result from the work performed by the motor in twisting the polymer chains and transforming a nanorotation into a contraction event at higher scales. Beyond mechanical motion, further conversions of this energy in other functional processes, such as catalysis and transport, represent some of the next possible challenges for such chemical systems. Very recently, Giuseppone et al. further reported the dual-light control of nanomachines with a molecular motor and modulator subunits.113 The photostationary state of the system could be tuned by modulation of frequencies using two irradiation wavelengths. Under this out-of-equilibrium condition, the contraction or expansion of the resulting materials could be controlled by the net flux of clockwise and anticlockwise rotations between the motors and the modulators.

3. Photochromism into nanoparticles

Nanoparticles (NPs) are typically defined as colloidal particles with a size range from 1 to 100 nanometres. The reduced dimensions and the different types of constituent materials such as metals and semiconductors make nanoparticles rich of physicochemical characteristics and thus particularly versatile for numerous applications in diverse fields spanning from optics, electronics and catalysis to drug delivery and biological sensors. The integration of photoresponsive units into the structure of nanoparticles offers the great advantage of activating specific functions with high specificity and selectivity. Herein, we mainly discuss photochromism into different functional nanoparticles including noble metal nanoparticles, mesoporous silica nanoparticles, fluorescent nanoparticles and magnetic nanoparticles.

3.1 Noble metal nanoparticles

Noble metal nanoparticles, especially of gold (Au) and silver (Ag), have been intensely explored in the field of nanoscience and nanotechnology owing to their spectacular properties.114 The noble nature of these NPs as well as their low cytotoxicity makes them attractive candidates for biological and environmental applications. A large proportion of the study of noble metal NPs is done with Au NPs, which have been taken as examples to demonstrate the relevance and diversity of the area of NPs in general. However, the performances of photochromic molecules harboured on the surfaces of noble metal NPs are often reduced due to the quenching of the electronic excited states.115117 Many methods have been developed to achieve efficient switching of molecular switches. For example, stable ligands were used to create a mixed monolayer and thus dilute the surface density of the photochromophores to recover their photoactivities.118,119 Long alkyl chains have been applied to limit electronic interactions between the gold NPs and excited states of the photochromophore.120,121 Introduction of cyclodextrin with the azobenzene ligand could also enable a hostguest photoisomerizable molecular shuttle on the nanoparticles.122 It was found that photochromic excited-state quenching is mainly determined by two factors: (i) distance between the photochromophore and NPs and (ii) overlap between the absorption of the photochromophore and the surface plasmon resonance (SPR) band of NPs.123,124

Tan et al. demonstrated a light-driven reversible phase transfer of -CD-capped gold nanoparticles between toluene and water phases by hostguest chemistry.125 The photoresponsive system was composed of an azobenzene-based surfactant (azo-ligand) and thiolated -CD (Fig. 12a). The alternating UV and visible light irradiation of azo-ligands led to reversible trans-to-cis isomerization. The trans isomer could enable the formation of a stable inclusion complex with -CD, but not the cis isomer (Fig. 12b). The -CD-coated Au NPs were initially dispersed in water as a consequence of the secondary hydroxyl groups of -CD, while the trans-azo-ligands were dissolved in toluene (Fig. 12c). After agitation, the azo-ligands in the trans-state would bind to the -CDs on the Au NP surface, and the hydrophobic alkyl chains were introduced to the periphery of the Au NP thus forming a reverse micelle-like structure. As a result, the Au NPs were converted from hydrophilic to hydrophobic and thus were transferred from water to toluene. UV irradiation of the azo-ligand led to the transformation from the trans to the cis state, and the azo-ligand in the cis-state dissociated from the surface of Au NPs. In this way, the Au NP surface recovered its original hydrophilic property, thus transferring the Au NPs back into water from toluene. It should be noted that the toluene-to-water transfer process was fully reversible with the cis-to-trans isomerization of azo-ligands under visible light irradiation. The method of light-driven reversible phase transfer based on molecular recognition should also provide us insight into the surface engineering of functional nanoparticles with potential applications in diverse research areas.

Based on the light-directed reversible assembly/disassembly of CD host and azobenzene guest, Zhao et al. demonstrated photoswitchable supramolecular catalysis of ester hydrolysis by using hostguest competitive binding of a Zn-coordinated -CD dimer catalyst with azobenzene units on the surfaces of Au NPs in the presence of reaction substrates.126 Au NPs were chosen as the catalytic support where the CD-based supramolecular catalysts could be reversibly immobilized by alternating UV- and visible-light irradiations. As shown in Fig. 13, the -CD rings were initially occupied by the trans-azobenzene units, thus the supramolecular catalyst would not recognize the reaction substrate and therefore its catalytic activity was passivated. After the trans-to-cis photoisomerization of the azobenzene units on the Au NPs' surface under UV irradiation, the cis-azobenzene units were unable to bind with the -CD rings as tightly and thus the catalyst became activated for catalyzing the hydrolysis reactions of the substrates. Upon visible light irradiation, the cis-to-trans isomerization of azobenzene enabled the formation of the inclusion complex with the trans-azobenzene and -CD, and the catalytic reaction was thus terminated. The catalytic reaction could be switched on and off under repeated UV and visible light irradiation, which paves the way for developing the next generation of catalytic materials with facile control over immobilization and catalytic activity.

Light-directed aggregation can also be achieved for NPs functionalized with azobenzene ligands, which results from the large difference in the dipole moments between their trans and cis isomers.127,128 Klajn et al. demonstrated the light-guided reversible self-assembly of gold nanoparticles grafted with different azobenzenes (Fig. 14).129 Upon blue light irradiation at 420 nm for 3 min, the azo-functionalized Au NPs (2.5 nm NPazo) were found to aggregate together in a near quantitative fashion as confirmed by TEM images. Subsequent UV irradiation could disassemble the aggregates. Interestingly, it was found that the NPs grafted with the parent azobenzene molecule (2.5 nm NPparent azo) exhibited an opposite behavior, where the reversible assembly and disassembly were driven by UV and blue light irradiation, respectively.130,131 They further demonstrated the selective self-assembly of specific components in a hybrid system with NPs of two different sizes, and each kind of NPs was chemically grafted with a different azobenzene ligand. A mixture of azo-decorated 2.5 nm Au NPs and 5.5 nm Au NPs grafted with the parent azo was prepared (2.5 nm NPazo and 5.5 nm NPparent azo). Both types of NPs exhibited superior solubility in a non-polar toluenehexane solvent. The trans-to-cis isomerization of azo upon blue light irradiation triggered the aggregation of the 2.5 nm NPs. Subsequent UV exposure of the mixture resulted in the simultaneous cis-to-trans isomerization of azo and trans-to-cis isomerization of parent azo, which allowed the simultaneous disassembly of the aggregates of the 2.5 nm NPs and assembly of the aggregates of the 5.5 nm NPs. In the future, it would be very interesting to introduce three or more types of NPs with different azobenzenes that could respond to multiple colours of light. Manipulating such multicomponent mixtures of NPs with light is envisioned to provide a promising platform for the development of more complex nanostructures, advanced materials and multifunctional devices.

Light-induced dynamic self-assembly has been used to direct the dynamic assembly of NPs into well-ordered, three-dimensional superstructures such as optically reversible or irreversible supraspheres and crystals with various sizes.132134 The degree of structural reversibility was found to depend not only on the strength of dipoledipole interactions between the NPs but also on the extent of their covalent binding. For the systems with low surface concentrations of dithiol ligands, the assemblies were fully reversible and could be toggled between disordered and crystalline states for many times upon irradiation with light of appropriate wavelength. As shown in Fig. 15, UV irradiation of azobenzene-functionalized NPs enabled them to organize into three-dimensional reversible crystals and these crystals were stable only under continuous UV-irradiation. Interestingly, they would transform into free NP phases under visible light irradiation or thermal relaxation. In contrast, the azobenzene ligands would permanently cross-link the assemblies for the systems with higher concentrations. The research is expected to open a new door for light-directed synthesis of more complex nanostructures with tailorable properties and multifunctional devices.135

Klajn et al. also reported light-directed dynamic self-assembling nanoflasks of different Au NPs decorated with azobenzenes as shown in Fig. 16.136 Upon UV irradiation of hybrid NPs in toluene, the trans-to-cis photoisomerization of photochromic azobenzene increased the dipole moment, which led to a strong attractive dipolediploe interaction among the functionalized NPs. When the dipoledipole interaction force was strong enough, the repulsions among the NPs were overcome, and as a result the NPs were observed to self-assemble into nanoflasks. The process could be used to trap diverse molecules from the surrounding bulk solution. It was found that the trapped molecules in the confined nanoflasks underwent fast chemical reactions with stereoselectivities which were significantly different from those in bulk solution. Interestingly, visible light irradiation at appropriate wavelength would result in the disassembly of the nanoflask, and thus the reaction product could be released into bulk solution. These nanoflasks could function as potential nanoreactors for various chemical reactions in a confined environment including enantioselective reactions and controllable polymerization of trapped monomers, as well as allow development of nanodevices with programmable nanostructures.

The idea of photoactive NPs could be also utilized to store graphical or textual information due to the metastable and reversible attributes.137 Klajn et al. demonstrated light-driven reversible self-assembly of Au NPs that were not grafted with photochromic molecules (Fig. 17).138 A photoresponsive medium loaded with spiropyran (SP) was used to control the interparticle hydrogen bond interactions between Au NPs grafted with the ligands with COOH groups. Interestingly, the 5.5 nm Au NP suspension was found to be stable in methanol with a small excess of a strong acid. Adding photochromic SP into this acid-stabilized solution led to the formation of MCH+ and a fast self-assembly of Au NPs. Irradiation of blue light resulted in the release of H+ from SP and the acidity of the medium was increased. As a result, hydrogen-bonded bridges among NPs were broken, and thus the NPs were re-solubilized. This light-driven reversible assembly and disassembly behaviour was further applied for dynamically catching and releasing the guest molecules of nitrobenzoxadiazole (NBD)-based dye capable of forming hydrogen bonds. It was found that every nanoparticle could trap up to 100 molecules and alternating UV and visible light irradiation would lead to reversible trapping and release of the guest molecules. The self-erasing images in polymer gels were developed by using this strategy, where an apparent colour tuning between yellow and red could be obtained by light-driven reversible assembly and disassembly processes of hybrid NPs. Towards this end, all the components of the above system were dispersed in poly(ethylene glycol) gel developed from free-radical polymerization in methanol. The resulting gel exhibited yellow colour due to the presence of MCH+. Red patterns could be developed by blue light irradiation through a mask, which resulted from the disassembly of aggregated NPs. Interestingly, the red pattern disappeared spontaneously with the passage of time, when the metastable NPs reversed back to their original state, thus self-erasing the image on the gel. Moreover, the same group also reported light-driven reversible assembly of nanoparticles in aqueous media.139 In this aqueous system, a water-soluble photoacid was used. Visible light irradiation would decrease the pH of the solution, thus inducing the formation of dynamic NP aggregates. Rapid disassembly would take place upon keeping the samples in the dark, and thus light-driven reversible assembly of nanoparticles in aqueous media was achieved.

3.2 Mesoporous silica nanoparticles

Mesoporous silica nanoparticles (MSNs) are known as excellent inorganic supports with tailored characteristics for various applications such as catalysts,140,141 drug delivery,142,143 and other theranostic or biomedical systems.144146 MSNs are typically defined as solid nanostructured silica materials with diverse periodic mesopores such as cubic, lamellar, or hexagonal structures ranging from 2 to 50 nm.147 Since the first discovery of MCM-41 in 1992, many synthetic strategies have been proposed for fabricating various highly ordered MSNs such as MCM, SBA and MSU.148 MSNs have been found to exhibit many advantageous characteristics such as high rigidity, high surface areas, excellent optical transparency, good biocompatibility and chemical stability, tailored morphologies and uniform pore sizes, and facile functionalization of pores and surfaces.149 Interestingly, the tunable pores could be used to load various cargo molecules, where the robust framework could effectively protect the loaded cargo from denaturation and enzymatic degradation due to environmental variations. Recently, considerable breakthroughs have been achieved in the modification of pore interiors or orifices and outer surfaces of MSNs with various organic functionalities.150 Introducing photochromic molecular switches into MSNs is expected to facilitate the controllable capture and on demand release of cargo molecules or drugs, thus providing a novel platform for future nanotherapeutics with both spatial and temporal external control. In general, two types of controllable release systems could be achieved in light-driven MSNs, i.e., the nanoimpellers with azobenzene groups installed inside the nanopores of MSNs and the nanovalves with supramolecular or mechanically interlocked entities positioned at the pore openings of MSNs.124

Zink et al. firstly demonstrated light-driven dynamic nanoimpellers of MSNs to deliver and release anticancer drugs into living cells.151 As shown in Fig. 18a, the MSNs were functionalized with azobenzene molecules, where azobenzene groups were fixed in the pore interiors with one end free to undergo photoisomerization and the other end attached to the pore walls. In the traditional uses of azobenzene, light irradiation at appropriate wavelengths is typically used so that a maximum extent of only one isomer could be obtained. However, the photochromic azobenzene for nanoimpellers needs to be driven by the light with an appropriate wavelength where both isomers exhibit the same extinction coefficients.152 In this way, the pendant photochromic azobenzene groups undergo continuous wagging that imparts dynamic motion upon the guest molecules trapped inside the nanopores, and therefore forcing the expulsion of these guest molecules from the pores. They also reported that azobenzene derivatives could function as both impellers and gatekeepers in and on MSNs, such that guest molecules could be expelled from MSNs under photocontrol.153 It was found that a small azobenzene derivative could generate efficient photocontrol release of dyes or drugs when positioned at the pore interiors of MSNs with diameters of about 400 nm (Fig. 18b). Interestingly, a larger azobenzene derivative was needed for controllable release when attached to their pore openings (Fig. 18c). When the pore openings were capped with small unsubstituted azobenzene groups, the leakage of guest molecules was often observed even without external light irradiation. Zink et al. further reported light-driven dynamic catch and release of cargo guest molecules from the nanoimpeller-based MCM-48 with cubic pores.154 The photochromic azobenzene nanoimpellers attached to the interiors of the cubic pores were found to exhibit dynamic wagging movement, which could subsequently prevent and expulse the guest molecules with light irradiation at appropriate wavelengths. Moreover, many other photochromic molecules such as spiropyrans have been used as nanogates of MSNs for optically controllable release of various cargo molecules.155157

UV light is often not preferred for clinical disease therapy due to various drawbacks including low penetration of tissues and high risks for DNA damage, etc. Many efforts have been devoted to developing longer visible and NIR wavelength systems for biological applications.158162 Shi et al. demonstrated a NIR-driven controlled release system based on MSN-coated upconversion nanoparticles (UCNPs) (Fig. 19), where UCNPs were located within the cores of MSNs and photoresponsive azobenzenes were chemically grafted onto the surfaces of mesopores as light-driven impellers.163 Upon NIR exposure at 980 nm, the UCNPs in the cores could emit photons in both UV and visible regions, which were subsequently absorbed by photochromic azobenzenes attached to the mesopores. The simultaneous UV and visible emissions generated from UCNPs led to continuous wagging movement of the photochromic azobenzene groups with free terminals. The light-driven back and forth dynamic wagging motion of these azobenzene molecules would function as a molecular impeller, leading to the release of Dox loaded in the mesopores in a controllable fashion. It was also found that the released amounts of Dox from the mesopores could be precisely regulated by modulating the duration and intensity of the external NIR irradiation.

The introduction of photoresponsive supramolecular switches into the pore openings of MSNs would give birth to another kind of light-driven nanovalve with controllable release capability. Zink et al. demonstrated the pioneering work of light-operated supramolecular nanovalves based on MCM-41 nanocontainers grafted with photochromic azobenzenes and pyrene-substituent -CD (Py--CD).164 Due to the high binding affinity in aqueous solution between -CD and trans-azobenzenes, -CD could easily thread onto the stalks and bind to trans-azobenzene units, and as a result the nanopores were sealed and the release of the loaded cargo was stopped (Fig. 20). Upon UV irradiation at 351 nm, the trans-to-cis photoisomerization of azobenzene units led to the dissociation of -CD rings from the stalks because of low binding between -CD and cis-azobenzene derivatives, thus opening the gates to the nanopores and releasing the cargo. Similar to azobenzene-based systems, Yang et al. demonstrated MSNs functionalized with the cinnamamide moiety as photoswitchable nanovalves.165 Moreover, inorganicorganic systems based on -CD-appended polymers and azobenzene-immobilized MSNs were also investigated.166 Interestingly, MSNs functionalized with both nanovalves (on the outer surface of the NPs) and impellers (inside the pores) have been reported to enable a dual-controlled release system that could function as an AND logic gate.167 The ability to controllably release stored drug molecules in response to an external light source makes these systems applicable for light-operated intracellular drug delivery and beyond.

To avoid the release of pore-blocking units in the operation, Zhao et al. functionalized the MSNs with a photoresponsive [2]rotaxane with a photochromic azobenzene axle and an -CD ring, where the -CD ring could thread into the linear azobenzene axle with a preattached stopper at one end.168 As shown in Fig. 21, in the initial state, the -CD was located at the trans-azobenzene position that was far away from the surface of hybrid NPs. In contrast, UV light (365 nm) irradiation would move the -CD to the azole/ethylene glycol position that was close to the surface of hybrid NPs, and as a result the pore of NPs was physically blocked. Interestingly, the visible-light-driven reverse cis-to-trans photoisomerization of photochromic azobenzene would reopen the pore with the movement of the -CD back to its origin position. The back and forth movements of the -CD ring along the azobenzene axle resulted in reversible closing and opening of nanopores, which enabled both drug storage and remote-controlled release. Moreover, NIR-driven nanovalves of MSN-coated Au NPs have been reported as an on-command drug delivery system, where NIR light was absorbed by Au NPs and the resulting plasmonic heating decreased the binding affinity of supramolecular switches.169,170 The photothermally driven systems are expected to provide important prospects for noninvasive controlled drug release superior to other controllable techniques.

3.3 Fluorescent nanoparticles

Photoswitchable dynamic materials with tailored fluorescence have attracted increasing attention worldwide due to their fundamental and technological importance in diverse fields including optoelectronic devices, chemical sensing, ultrahigh-density optical data storage, fluorescence microscopic techniques, etc.171 The development of photoswitchable fluorescence-based detection is expected to stride over the long-standing bottlenecks of the conventional fluorescence analytical systems.172,173 The molecular probes with photoswitchable fluorescence are believed to play a revolutionary role in developing wide-field and super-resolution fluorescence imaging, and thus we could visualize the subcellular features with the nanoscale spatial resolution beyond the diffraction limit.174,175 Compared to the fluorescent proteins and single-molecule fluorophores, fluorescent nanoparticles exhibit many advantageous aspects such as higher photostability, improved signal/noise ratio, and facile conjugation with target biomolecules.176,177 For the purpose of fluorescent bioimaging, various kinds of nanoparticles have been developed including quantum dots, carbon dots, upconversion NPs, semiconducting polymer dots, noble metal NPs, two-dimensional inorganic quantum dots, fluorescently doped silica and solgel systems, etc.178180 Herein, we review several representative types of photoresponsive nanoparticles with switchable fluorescence such as quantum dots (QDs), polymeric nanoparticles and organic and hybrid nanoparticles as well as their applications.

Photoswitchable luminescence is typically achieved by hybridizing a fluorescent donor unit and a photochromic acceptor unit in nanoparticles. Li et al. reported photoswitchable luminescence in coreshell CdSeZnS QDs fuctionalized with spiropyran switches (Fig. 22).181 Spiropyran molecules underwent reversible transformation between the ring-closed SP state and the ring-open MC state under alternating UV and visible light irradiation. MC species were found to exhibit intense visible absorption that exhibits excellent overlap with the emission band of QDs. The fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) from QDs to MC moieties could quench the luminescence of QDs. Under visible light irradiation, MC species would be transformed back to the counterpart SP species without obvious visible absorption, thus deactivating the luminescence quenching of QDs because of the energy mismatch. As a result, photoswitchable reversible luminescence emission of QDs could be realized by the light-driven dynamic interconversion of SP between the two distinct states. Jovin et al. developed photoswitchable QDs by coating the nanoparticle's surfaces with photochromic diheteroarylethene polymers and second fluorescent dyes.182184 The tunable multi-coloured emission ratio of the QDs was achieved upon alternating UV and visible light exposure, where QDs functioned as FRET fluorescence donors, and photochromic diheteroarylethene acted as an acceptor upon activation by ultraviolet light. Based on the FRET mechanism, photoswitchable luminescence of UCNPs was also achieved by attaching different photochromic molecular components onto their surfaces.185,186 Very recently, Kim et al. demonstrated light-driven fluorescence modulation of QDdye conjugates.187 Multicolour on-demand photopatterning was developed using hybrid QD solid films, and simultaneous multicolour super-resolution was demonstrated by exploiting the multiplexing capability of QDs.

Photoswitchable nanoparticles with tunable fluorescence could also be fabricated by using the reprecipitation strategy.188 Organic aggregates or nanoparticles could be prepared by adding the concentrated solution of the target organic compound dissolved in a good solvent into the excess of a poor solvent. Park et al. fabricated neat organic nanoparticles from a dyad composed of an aggregation-induced-emission (AIE) moiety and a photochromic diarylethene moiety, where tetrahydrofuran (THF) was used as the good solvent and water was used as the poor solvent.189 The resulting organic nanoparticles were found to exhibit reversible fluorescence photoswitching under alternating UV and visible light irradiation. The reprecipitation-based doping technique is another way to prepare composite organic nanoparticles. Yao et al. reported composite nanoparticles with photoswitchable fluorescence based on photochromic spirooxazine derivatives.190 Recently, Su et al. reported the fabrication of photoswitchable fluorescent NPs based on a diarylethene benzothiadiazole (BTD) dyad PF with excellent fluorescence (Fig. 23a). The NPs of PF were prepared by the reprecipitation method in a H2O/THF 80:20 mixture.191 In the initial state, PF(OF) NPs exhibited red emission with max = 615 nm, a lifetime of 9.6 ns and high fluorescence quantum yield F = 0.65. UV irradiation at 365 nm led to the complete quenching of the fluorescence from the NP suspension, whereas visible light irradiation at 488 nm would switch on their fluorescence (Fig. 23b). The reversible process could be repeated many times with excellent fatigue resistance. The giant amplification of fluorescence photoswitching enabled the outstanding photoswitchable properties due to the efficient intermolecular FRET between the fluorescent units and the photochromic moieties in their closed form within the NPs (Fig. 23c). The strong nonlinear photoswitchability could find many potential applications where sensitive fluorescent photoswitches with a minimum number of photons are required, such as optical recording or subdiffractive fluorescence microscopy.

A polymer matrix could be also utilized as a framework for carrying the photoactive components to develop fluorescent NPs with photoswitchable attributes, where the photoresponsive components can be either physically embedded in the cross-linked polymer matrix or covalently incorporated into the polymer skeleton. The light-driven reversible SPMC interconversion could switch the red fluorescence of these polymeric NPs on and off under alternating UV and visible light irradiation. It should be noted that modifying the molecular structure of photochromic compounds or introducing additional fluorophores enabled new types of various polymeric NPs with novel photoswitchable fluorescence.193198 Additionally, many other polymer matrices such as amine-terminated dendrimers199 and conjugated polymers200202 have been utilized for the construction of photoswitchable fluorescent NPs.

3.4 Magnetic nanoparticles

Magnetic nanoparticles are currently in the limelight of research owing to their attractive properties and potential applications in the areas of nanocatalysts, biomedicine, microfluidics, magnetic resonance imaging, etc.209215 Developing magnetic materials with photoinduced magnetization is becoming increasingly important for next-generation high-density information memory and storage devices.216,217 Photomagnetic effects have been extensively demonstrated in many systems such as diluted magnetic semiconductors, cyanometalate-based compounds, light-induced excited spin-state trapping compounds, and manganite films.218227 However, most of such phenomena were observed at a very low temperature, which limits their potential practical applications. To overcome these hurdles, photochromic molecules have recently been incorporated into the magnetic nanoparticles, where the magnetic properties of such composite systems could be modulated with remote light.

Einaga et al. firstly reported photoinduced magnetization in superparamagnetic iron oxide (-Fe2O3) NPs functionalized with a mixed self-assembled monolayer (mSAM) of n-octylamine and 4-butoxy-4-[(8-hydroxy)octyloxy]-azobenzene, where n-octylamine could dilute the azobenzene SAM and provide free volume for its isomerization (Fig. 26a).228 The superparamagnetic properties of the resulting NPs were characterized by superconducting quantum interference device (SQUID) measurements. The influence of external light irradiation on the magnetic properties of hybrid nanoparticles at 5 K was investigated. The initial magnetization of hybrid nanoparticles at a magnetic field of 10 G was found to increase from 0.279 to 0.305 emu g1 under UV light irradiation. This increased magnetization could be maintained for several hours even after stopping the illumination. The magnetization value would decrease from 0.305 to 0.280 emu g1 upon visible light irradiation for another 10 min. The light-driven reversible changes in the level of magnetization were repeated many times, and the total change of the magnetization value was about 9.3%. Interestingly, the same tendency for changes in magnetization by light irradiation was observed at a temperature of 300 K (Fig. 26b). The underlying mechanism of photoinduced magnetization might be the photoisomerisation-induced changes in either the ligand fields of the surface iron atoms or the electrostatic field around the NPs. The same research group also demonstrated light-driven reversible switching of ferromagnetic FePt nanoparticles coated with azobenzene ligands (Fig. 26c).229Fig. 26d shows the influence of light irradiation on the magnetic properties of the hybrid FePt nanoparticles at 300 K. Upon UV irradiation, the initial magnetization value at 10 G was found to increase from 3.13 to 3.44 emu g1, and visible light irradiation would decrease the magnetization value from 3.44 to 3.21 emu g1. Recently, photoinduced switching of magnetization has also been observed in azobenzene-functionalized Fe nanoparticles.230 These pioneering works are expected to open up a brand-new door for their practical application in the field of advanced optical magnetic recording systems.

Many reports have shown that ferromagnetism could be also observed at AuS interfaces, where ferromagnetism was related to Au 5d localized holes because of the charge transfer from the Au surface atoms to the S atoms of the organic-ligands upon the formation of the AuS bonds.231234 Since the charge transfer from Au to S atoms acted as a trigger for generating ferromagnetism, the magnitude of the magnetic moments could vary with the metal work functions. Einaga et al. demonstrated light-induced reversible magnetization changes in gold nanoparticles passivized with azobenzene ligands (Fig. 27a).235237 It was found that the size of the NP core played a key role in controlling their magnetic properties. Reducing the particle size to 1.7 nm could enhance not only the magnetic properties, but also the photoisomerization efficiency of azobenzeneAu NPs. Fig. 27b illustrates the influence of light irradiation on the magnetic properties of 1.7 nm azobenzeneAu NPs at 300 K. Upon UV irradiation, the initial magnetization value decreased from 0.24 to 0.18 emu gAu1 under an applied field of 5 T. The decreased magnetization value was maintained for several hours at room temperature even after the illumination was stopped. The very slow recovery of the magnetization might result from the cis-to-trans thermal back-isomerization of the azobenzene moiety. 4. Photochromism into nanoelectronics

Optoelectronic devices are the basis of the modern informational world. However, Moore's law indicates that the top-down fabrication techniques developed for conventional silicon-based electronics have nearly reached their miniaturization limitation. Nanoelectronics or molecular electronics is known as a promising bottom-up approach to break through the development bottleneck of current microelectronics, which aims at the application of nanoscience and nanotechnology to electronics.238,239 Introducing photochromism into nanoelectronics could confer on them the potential to respond to remote light stimuli at appropriate wavelengths, which would not only produce a new generation of advanced intelligent devices with smaller dimensions, superior performances and multiple functionalities, but also enable us to probe and understand their intrinsic properties at the atomic- and/or molecular-length scale.240242 The marriage of nanoelectronics with photochromic molecules might also lead to direct observation of novel effects and fundamental discovery of physical phenomena beyond traditional materials or approaches.

4.1 Molecular junctions

Developing the nanoelectronic devices based on the properties inherent in individual molecules has witnessed remarkable technological and theoretical advancements. The concept of molecular junctions with an electrodemoleculeelectrode structure potentially enables the electron transport of molecular electronics through individual molecules.243245 In such junctions, the currentvoltage characteristics are highly dependent on the connection between the electrodes and the molecule. When embedding the photochromic molecules in-between two electrodes, the conductance of the molecular junction could be gated with an external remote control. Photoswitchable conductance of molecular junctions has been demonstrated through positioning azobenzene and diarylethene molecules between two planar surfaces.246249 Samor et al. developed metalmoleculemetal junctions by contacting a gold-supported self-assembled monolayer (SAM) with a metal-coated atomic force microscope (AFM) tip, where the thiolated azobenzene was found to form monocomponent well-ordered SAMs on Au(111) and provide a surface packing which allowed for efficient and reversible isomerization (Fig. 28a).246 Upon UV irradiation, a marked increase in current was observed due to the conformational change of the azobenzene molecules from trans to cis form. Interestingly, a 30-fold decrease in resistance was found upon transformation to the cis state, which is consistent with the decrease in the tunneling barrier length related with the molecular conformational change. Nishihara et al. reported light-controlled reversible conductance switching of molecular junctions prepared by contacting a diarylethene-functionalized silicon-(111) surface with a conductive metallic AFM tip (Fig. 28b), where the closed-form diarylethene on the silicon surface exhibited higher electrical conductance than its open-form.249 UV illumination at 313 nm of the devices resulted in an increase of the initial current of the silicon surface, and visible light irradiation at 578 nm for 2 h would reverse the current back to the value for the initial state. The light-driven reversible switching of conductance presumably resulted from the differences in molecular geometry and in -conjugation between ring-open and ring-closed forms.

Another popular method for developing photoswitchable molecular junctions is to use metal nanoparticles functionalized with photochromic molecules.250 Feringa et al. reported light-controlled reversible conductance switching in the molecular devices with diarylethene-functionalized gold nanoparticles, where gold nanoparticles were ordered in 2D nanoparticle arrays and photochromic diarylethene worked as a molecular bridge between neighboring nanoparticles (Fig. 29).251 Each metalmoleculemetal junction involved one or at most a few molecular bridges due to the small size of nanoparticles. Visible-light irradiation decreased conductance rapidly, whereas UV irradiation led to a steep increase of conductance, and the reversible conductance switching cycles could be repeated many times by alternating UVvisible irradiation (Fig. 29d). Irie et al. systematically investigated the relationship between photoswitchable conductance and molecular structure of photochromic diarylethenes in the devices composed of gold nanoparticle networks and photochromic diarylethenes (Fig. 30).252 For molecular devices based on Au-1a and Au-2a, UV light irradiation increased the conductance significantly and then visible light irradiation decreased the conductance. Compared to the Au-1a nanoparticle network, networks based on Au-2a exhibited much faster photocycloreversion reaction and reversible photoswitching behaviors. Interestingly, diarylethene 3 was found to show an opposite behavior to 3-thienyl-type diarylethene 1 and 2. For the Au-3b nanoparticle network, the conductance increased upon irradiation with visible light. The study clearly indicated that the light-induced conductance switching behavior is closely related to the change of the -conjugation length of the molecule.

Compared with metal electrode materials, carbon-based materials possess many advantages, such as tunable and superior electronic properties and good compatibility with organic molecules. Introducing photochromism onto carbon-based electrodes is a promising avenue towards photoswichable electronic devices. Cuniberti et al. performed the theoretical study of carbon nanotubemolecular azobenzenecarbon nanotube (CNT) junctions. They predicted that it is possible to manipulate the conduction properties of the resulting junction by changing the topology of the contacts between azobenzene and CNTs, and even the chirality of CNTs.253 It was also predicted that the conductivity of CNTs could be tuned by photoinduced isomerization of photochromic molecules.254,255 Nuckolls et al. demonstrated light-driven reversible conductance switching of single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) functionalized with pyrene- or alkane-tethered spiropyrans.256 The conductance of the devices could be switched back-and-forth between low and high conductance upon alternating UV and visible light irradiation.257 The same research group also reported reversible current switching of SWCNTs bridged with two different diarylethene molecules via amide formation, where the SWCNT point contacts were developed by electron beam lithography (Fig. 31).258 The thiophene-based devices (X = S) showed only one-way switching from open to closed state, changing from a lower conductance state to a higher conductance state. As the electrode material, graphene shows important advantages over SWCNTs since it is a zerogap semiconductor that does not have the inherently variable diameter and chirality of SWCNTs. This observation was ascribed to the reversible conformational transformation of the azobenzene units between trans and cis isomers driven by light of different wavelengths. They also developed a series of graphenediarylethene molecular junctions, where the diarylethene backbones were modified with rationally designed sides and anchoring groups to tailor the energy level alignments at the moleculeelectrode interface (Fig. 33a and b).260 Upon low-intensity UV light irradiation, all the devices showed reproducible, substantial photoswitching from the low-conductance (off) state to the high-conductance (on) state. Unfortunately, continuous reversible switching was not realized due to the strong coupling between the molecules and the electrodes. Recently, the same research group reported a light-driven reversible molecular electrical junction composed of a single diarylethene molecule and graphene electrodes by rationally engineering the molecular structure of diarylethene, where three methylene (CH2) groups were incorporated into each side of the molecular backbone to decrease the effective moleculeelectrode coupling (Fig. 33c).261 The resultant molecular junction showed a fully reversible photoinduced conductance switching with excellent accuracy, stability, and reproducibility. In this work, the molecular engineering enabled the modulation of the moleculeelectrode coupling strengths, which indicates that molecules with suitable conductive properties and good anchoring to solid-state electrodes should be key components for future nanoelectronics.262

4.2 Organic field-effect transistors

Although considerate advancements have been achieved in developing photoswitchable molecular junctions that provide the possibility of transporting charges through single molecules, the related research in this field is still in a preliminary stage and far from practical applications due to the limitations of their technological relevance. As is known, organic field-effect transistors (OFETs) are the key element of plastic microelectronics, where the charge transport in a semiconducting material can be modulated by electrical gating.263265 Incorporating novel functionalities into conventional OFETs has recently become a new trend.266,267 The integration of photochromism in OFETs allows us to develop a multifunctional device capable of simultaneously responding to different independent stimuli. Herein, we mainly review the recent advancement of photoswitchable OFETs by introducing photochromism into different parts of OFET configuration, such as doping in channel/dielectric layers, interface/surface engineering, and direct use of photochromic layers as channel layers.

The electrical current through the channels could be modulated by directly mixing photochromic molecules into channel layers as dopants. Guo et al. developed a spiropyran-doped poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) film as a transistor channel (Fig. 34).268 The low-conductance state of the OFET channel was gradually changed into a higher conductance state under UV irradiation for about 3 minutes (Fig. 34c). Visible light irradiation of the devices for about 8 minutes could restore the initial drain current value. The underlying mechanism is the charge transfer between the P3HT backbone and the phenoxide unit of the spiropyran, where the light-induced phenoxide group in the open-form spiropyran could act as a charge trap thus leading to a decrease in mobility. Orgiu et al. proposed a similar approach by doping diarylethene molecules into a P3HT polymer layer.269,270 Light driven switching of the drain current through the P3HT channel could be achieved. UV-irradiation-induced ring-closed isomers would raise the HOMO level and trap holes in the channel layer. In contrast, the open-ring isomer had a lower HOMO level and had no impact on the drain current. Raimondo et al. achieved photoswitchable OFETs by doping azobenzene-functionalized gold nanoparticles into P3HT layers, where the gold nanoparticles promoted conformational change by a plasmon effect under external light irradiation.271 Moreover, Ishiguro et al. developed photoswitchable OFETs by doping photochromic spiropyran into the poly[bis(4-phenyl) (2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)amine] (PTAA) channel layer (Fig. 35).272,273 These methods of blending organic semiconductors with different types of photochromic molecules are very appealing for mass production of devices. However, numerous challenges await to be resolved, such as phase segregation in bicomponent films and miscibility in a common solvent.

4.3 Photoswitchable superconductivity

Since the first observation of superconductivity in mercury by Onnes,280 extensive efforts have been devoted to discovering new classes of materials, to improving their superconducting properties, or to understanding their mechanisms.281285 Manipulating the superconductivity of condensed matter by remote optical sources is of paramount importance in the field of modern electronics such as optical memory or switching devices. Light-driven irreversible changes of superconducting properties have been reported in some systems such as alkali-metal fullerides and YBCO films.286288 Einaga et al. firstly reported the reversible photoinduced effects on superconductivity by grafting a photochromic azobenzene SAM upon Nb thin films.289 The negative surface dipole of trans-state azobenzene was found to decrease the work function of bare Nb substrates, and therefore the charge transfer from the Nb substrate to the organic monolayer was induced. Such charge transfer processes decreased the inhomogeneities of the electron distribution along the surface. The charge transfer could be reversed with trans-to-cis photoisomerization upon UV light irradiation due to the positive surface dipole of cis-state azobenzene. In this way, the superconducting current value could be reversibly modulated by alternating UV and visible light irradiation.

Recently, Suda et al. demonstrated a photoactive field effect transistor device, where the superconductivity could be optically controlled through the excitation of a gate made from spiropyran.290 The photoactive superconducting device was fabricated by laminating a thin single crystal of -(BEDT-TTF)2Cu[N(CN)2]Br (-Br) [BEDT-TTF:bis(ethylenedithio)tetrathiafulvalene] on top of an Al2O3(or HfO2)/Nb:SrTiO3 substrate coated with a self-assembled monolayer of spiropyran derivatives (Fig. 39). Spiropyran served as the gate and was reversibly photoisomerised from a nonionic to a zwitterionic form. In its neutral form, no field was applied to the thin single crystal. Upon photoexcitation with UV irradiation, the formation of zwitterions in the spiropyran film injected the holes into the -Br, thus generating a superconductor at low temperatures. In contrast, the film would return to its neutral state upon visible light irradiation. Herein, the photo-induced transition to a superconducting state at low temperature was closely related to the unique properties of spiropyran such as photoisomerization at low temperatures. An enormous electric field was generated in the electric double layer upon photoisomerization of spiropyran, which is difficult to achieve using common gate materials. This work also demonstrated the importance of controlling interfaces in order to determine the electric and magnetic properties of nanostructures.291

5. Photochromism into metalorganic nanoassemblies

Metalorganic nanoassemblies have captured the increasing attention of chemists worldwide due to their great promise in applications such as catalysis, gas storage, drug delivery and beyond.292 These nanomaterials involve metal moieties such as ions or clusters linked by organic ligands, and they possess tunability in terms of composition, internal structure, morphology, dimensions and properties, making possible their rational design for many potential applications. Based on the geometry and connectivity of the building components, we could develop multifunctional nanostructures with different geometrical dimensions such as 0D discrete polyhedra, 1D chains or ladders, 2D sheets or bilayers, and 3D extended networks.293,294 Recently, extensive efforts have been made to develop 3D metalorganic nanostructures, such as metalorganic molecular cages and metalorganic frameworks. Introducing photochromism into the interior or other parts of these 3D metalorganic nanoassemblies could enable the controlled capture and release of guest molecules under external light stimuli.

5.1 Metalorganic molecular cages

Metalorganic molecular cages are also called as supramolecular coordination self-assemblies. Large windows in the molecular cages could serve as the gateway passage for small molecules, which provides numerous opportunities for their biophysical applications. In these molecular cages, the cavity, periphery and windows could be facilely modulated by modifying the organic ligand or changing the metal ion. Recently, photoswitchable metalorganic molecular cages have been developed by using functionalized photochromic ligands, and the tuning of structures has been demonstrated for biochemical applications.295301

Fujita et al. incorporated photoresponsive azobenzene into the interior of a coordination cage, where the cage's binding affinity for a specific guest was modulated upon light irradiation.302 As shown in Fig. 40, 24-pyridine-containing azobenzene bridging ligands were spontaneously assembled into a spherical complex M12L24 with the addition of 12 metal ions. The hydrophobic guest molecules such as pyrene and 1-pyrenecarboxaldehyde could be captured into the core due to the hydrophobic accumulation of trans-azobenzene units in the sphere. UV light irradiation could make the interior of the complex less hydrophobic due to the formation of cis isomers, which resulted in the release of guest molecules. After heating at 50 C for 12 h, all the azobenzene moieties returned to the trans form and the guest molecule was encapsulated again. The photoisomerization of azobenzene chromophores in the complex resulted in reversible guest uptake into the spherical complex.303 The successful affinity control through the photoisomerization of the chromophore should hold great potential for the preparation of a variety of photoresponsive molecular nanoparticles where the interior environments could be optically modulated.

The fine modulation of the binding affinity for anionic guests might find potential applications in the fields of drug delivery, supramolecular catalysis, and advanced functional materials.

5.2 Metalorganic frameworks

Metalorganic frameworks (MOFs) are an emerging class of porous materials developed from organic linkers and metal-containing nodes, which have attracted significant attention in diverse fields because of their structural and functional tunability.306 The 3D extended networks of MOFs have uniform pore diameters in the range of 3 to 20 , and they exhibit unprecedented high void volumes, internal surface areas, and low densities.307310 MOFs have been demonstrated to be highly promising for many applications such as gas storage, drug delivery, molecular sensing, separation/purification, heterogeneous catalysis, energy storage and conversion, etc.311,312 Recently, extensive research has focused on developing photoswitchable MOFs, where light-directed dynamic structural changes of MOFs could be realized through the incorporation of photochromic molecules.313315

To achieve photoresponsive MOFs, the most direct strategy is to load an existing MOF with photoresponsive guest molecules. Kitagawa et al. demonstrated azobenzene-loaded Zn2(terephthalate)2(triethylenediamine) MOFs (Fig. 43).316 The light-induced trans-to-cis isomerization of azobenzene led to a remarkable structural change of the host MOFs. The MOFs with trans-form azobenzene were found to not adsorb N2 because of the close contact between azobenzene and the host framework, i.e., the pore of host MOFs was blocked. Upon UV irradiation, azobenzene isomerised towards its cis-form, and subsequently the MOFs expanded into a tetragonal form, leading to an 8.3-fold increase in N2 adsorption. It was also found that such photoisomerisation was closely related to the close packing of photochromic guest molecules within the MOF pores.317

Zhou et al. developed light-driven MOFs by directly incorporating photochromic azobenzene side chains into the organic linkers of MOFs (Fig. 44a).318 The UV-irradiation-induced trans-to-cis photoisomerisation could decrease the CO2 uptake of resultant MOFs and a drop in CO2 uptake of as much as 54% on formation of the cis-isomer was observed. It is believed that the phenyl rings of the cis-azobenzene molecules could block the main adsorption sites near the metal clusters. The light-driven CO2 adsorption capacity could be reversed back by visible light irradiation or thermal relaxation. Yaghi et al. demonstrated another MOF containing pendant azobenzene and Mg nodes, which they designated as azo-IRMOF-74-III, by using a linear terephthalic acid-based three-ring linker (Fig. 44b).319 The linear stacking of magnesium clusters and organic ligands led to the formation of non-interpenetrated 1D hexagonal pores. The propidium iodide dye could be loaded into the MOF without being released under ambient conditions. Light irradiation at 408 nm resulted in the rapid wagging motion of azobenzene in the pores of MOFs, which subsequently triggered the release of the guest dye. This light-driven reversible release of various cargos could be achieved by alternating UV and visible light irradiation.

Photoswitchable MOFs could also be developed by directly incorporating photochromic groups into the backbone of the linker instead of a side chain, which is expected to achieve a much large and dramatic light-induced structural transformation due to the directly photoinduced structural changes of the framework itself. Hill et al. reported photoswitchable triply interpenetrated MOFs [Zn(AzDC)(4,4-BPE)0.5] by using two different photochromic linkers trans-1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)-ethylene (4,4-BPE) and 4,4-dicarboxylate (AzDC).320 As a result of the two photoresponsive groups, light-driven framework isomerization was found to strongly affect the pore dimensions. Irradiation of the resulting MOFs with light of appropriate wavelength enabled the uptake and release of CO2 in real time (Fig. 45). Additionally, the strong photodynamic carbon capture of CO2 has also been demonstrated in porous aromatic frameworks via incorporation of diarylethene with the carboxylic acid groups as guest molecules.321 These photoswitchable MOFs should provide a great avenue for low-energy carbon dioxide capture and release as well as for industrial applications.

Very recently, Aida et al. reported an azobenzene-containing, zirconium-based metalorganic framework (azoMOF) as shown in Fig. 46.322 Upon UV irradiation at 365 nm, the trans-to-cis photoisomerization of azobenzene pendants was able to furnish a cis-isomer content of 21% (azoMOF21%) in 30 min at the photostationary state and visible light irradiation or heating could drive backward isomerization into azoMOF1%. The diffusion rate and the amount of CO2 adsorbed into the nanochannels of azoMOF decreased considerably when the cis-isomer content increased. 6. Photochromism into supramolecular self-assembled systems

Supramolecular self-assembly could potentially offer an efficient bottom-up approach to construct well-ordered nanostructures at multiple length scales and tailor the physical properties of the resultant functional materials from small molecular building blocks.330335 Supramolecular multicomponent systems exhibit high-performance tunable functionality due to the intrinsic dynamic attributes of non-covalent interactions such as hydrogen bonding, -stacking, and van der Waals interactions. Considerable efforts have been made to develop a variety of supramolecular systems and to investigate their applications in the areas of catalysis, biosensors, nanomedicine, functional materials, electronic devices, etc.336338 Introducing photochromism into these supramolecular self-assembled systems would endow the resulting nanostructures or advanced materials with intriguing photoresponsive properties.339,340 Herein, recent advancements of photoswitchable supramolecular self-assembled systems such as supramolecular amphiphiles, supramolecular polymers, and light-driven liquid crystalline superstructures are mainly discussed.

6.1 Supramolecular amphiphiles

Supramolecular amphiphiles with their hydrophilic and hydrophobic parts linked by noncovalent interactions are able to self-assemble in solution or at an interface and form diverse nanostructures with a high degree of structural complexity, such as micelles, vesicles, monolayers, and lyotropic mesophases.341343 The hydrophilic part of amphiphiles is preferentially immersed in water, while the hydrophobic part preferentially resides in the air or in the nonpolar solvent. Supramolecular amphiphiles have been widely and actively investigated in many research fields, such as candidates for drug delivery, fabrication of novel organic nanotubes or nanofibers for electrical devices, templates for processing well-defined materials, etc.344348 Introducing photochromic molecules into building blocks of amphiphiles could lead to the fabrication of supramolecular architectures whose nanostructures or microstructures could be facilely tuned by external light irradiation.

Lin et al. designed a photoactive pseudo anioniccationic surfactant system, where the photochromic sodium (4-phenylazo-phenoxy)-acetate (AzoNa) was coupled to the conventional cationic surfactant CTAB.349 The mixture of 30 mM CTAB and 50 mM AzoNa was found to undergo four distinct states under different UV irradiation times (Fig. 49). The mixture was observed to experience continuous changes from viscoelastic solution with flowing birefringence, biphasic solution, viscoelastic solution without flowing birefringence to water-like solution with the increase of UV irradiation time. Further investigations indicated that the resulting systems exhibited multi-state nanostructures including vesicles, wormlike micelles, lamellar structures, and small micelles under different irradiation times due to trans-to-cis isomerization. The multi-scale and multi-state molecular self-assemblies could be reversibly controlled with alternating UV or visible light irradiation. The authors claimed that this peculiar phenomenon resulted from light-driven dynamic changes in the dipole moment and geometry of the azobenzene group, which is believed to impart a significant effect on the molecular packing of the resulting surfactant aggregates. The light-directed molecular self-assemblies would provide the possibility of constructing smart and functional materials in a remote non-covalent bottom-up manner.350

The work could inspire the smart design of biomimetic vesicles and help to understand the mechanism of cell deformation.

6.2 Supramolecular polymers

Supramolecular polymers, with monomeric building blocks held together by dynamic noncovalent bonds, have recently attracted considerable attention because the marriage of supramolecular chemistry and polymer chemistry makes them ideal candidates for smart materials.371373 The reversible and tunable nature of noncovalent interactions and dynamic covalent bonds endows supramolecular polymers with some intrinsic properties including degradability, self-healing, easy recycling, adaptation and stimuli-responsiveness, which usually could not be provided by conventional covalently bonded polymers. Macrocyclic hosts such as cyclodextrins, crown ethers, cucurbiturils, calixarenes, and pillararenes have been widely used as building blocks in the fabrication of hostguest supramolecular polymers. Multiple hydrogen bonds, stacking interactions and metalligand coordination have been extensively used to bring the building blocks together to produce supramolecular polymers.374376 Introducing photochromic molecules into supramolecular polymers is expected to endow the resultant systems with intriguing responsive behavior to light, and such photoresponsive supramolecular polymers could find many potential applications in the areas of photochemical actuation, reversible optical storage, drug delivery, tissue engineering and beyond.

Scherman et al. demonstrated the light-driven cucurbit[8]uril (CB[8])-based supramolecular polymerization with photochromic azobenzene monomers, where polymerization and depolymerization could be achieved through the light-induced reversible trans-to-cis (i.e., EZ) isomerization of photochromic azobenzene-containing guest molecules (Fig. 56).377 The linear supramolecular polymers were obtained in the mixed solution of CB[8] and E,E-azo with a 2:1 ratio due to the charge-transfer interaction between the viologen and azobenzene units. The photoisomerization from the E,E-azo to its Z,Z-form upon UV irradiation was found to result in the disassembly of the supramolecular polymers into a ternary complex. They also applied this strategy for nanopatterning, functional surface engineering, and photo-rheological fluids.378,379 These hostguest complexes that could be remotely switched between highly thermostable photostationary states in aqueous media should be very valuable molecular systems in biological and materials sciences and are also of interest in information storage technologies and for the construction of molecular machinery.

6.3 Light-driven liquid crystalline superstructures

Liquid crystals (LCs) are known as self-organized functional soft materials that possess both crystalline order and fluidic mobility from molecular to supramolecular and macroscopic levels.396398 Besides their wide applications in flat panel liquid crystal display devices, LCs are nowadays playing increasingly important roles in multidisciplinary fields such as nanoscience, materials science, biomedical science and biological science. Multitudinous liquid crystalline phases with different ordering of molecules could be observed.399408 For example, the nematic phase shows only orientational order with the self-assembly of molecules along the director, whereas the smectic phase exhibits both translational and orientational ordering where the molecules self-organize into layers. When chirality is introduced into achiral nematic LCs, LC molecules could spontaneously self-organize into the cholesteric phases with macroscopic helical structures or blue phases (BPs) with periodic cubic lattices. Importantly, the intrinsic self-organization of liquid crystalline materials bestows them with intelligent responsiveness to diverse external stimuli such as light, temperature, electric field, mechanical force, magnetic field and even chemical and electrochemical reactions. The decoration of these stimuli-responsive liquid crystalline superstructures with photochromic moieties would confer them photoresponsive capacity at specific wavelengths.409411 This strategy could bring us a variety of new self-organized functional materials, where by using light as fuel, the local optical, mechanical, and electrostatic variations of advanced soft materials could be remotely controlled for both fundamental and technological investigations.

In the cholesteric superstructures, the LC molecules exhibit the spatial orientation in a helical fashion along the helical axis, where the helical superstructures are typically characterized by handedness and helical pitch. The handedness describes the direction in which the molecular orientation rotates along the helical axis, while the pitch (P) is the distance over which the director rotates a full 360. According to Bragg's law, the cholesteric superstructure with appropriate alignment could selectively reflect circularly polarized light (CPL) with the same handedness as its helix. The reflection wavelength () is typically determined by = nP, where n is the average refraction index of LCs. To tune the reflection wavelength in cholesteric LCs, many efforts have been made to fabricate light-driven helical superstructures with tunable pitches by doping photochromic chiral switch with superior properties into achiral LC media.413418 For example, the pitch of cholesteric superstructures becomes larger upon UV light irradiation, which enables the dynamic reflection colour tuning from blue, green to red and vice versa under visible light exposures (Fig. 63a). Our group designed and synthesized a series of powerful binaphthyl-derived or diarylethene-based chiral dopants which exhibited high helical twisting powers (HTPs) as well as large HTP changes under different light irradiation. The resulting cholesteric superstructures were able to phototune the reflection colour across the full visible, even infrared spectra, which is expected to find potential applications in advanced devices such as reflective displays, tunable LC lasers, and tunable colour filters or reflectors.419426 Furthermore, light-driven dynamic handedness inversion of cholesteric superstructures has been obtained by doping unique light-driven chiral molecular switches or motors into achiral liquid crystal media.412,427429 Upon alternating UV and visible light irradiation, the cholesteric helix of one handedness unwinds, and disappears at a critical point with infinite pitch, and then rewinds through self-organization into another helix of the opposite twist sense (Fig. 63b). However, the rational design of such chiral molecular switch or motor with handedness inversion capability is not trivial, and such an event of handedness inversion of LCs driven by light is often encountered by chance. Recently, our group successfully developed the light-driven chiral molecular switches based on azoarenes, dithienylcyclopentenes and cyclic azobenzenes, which enabled reversible handedness inversion of self-organized helical superstructures under alternating UV and visible light irradiation.430432 These achievements are expected to open a brandnew door for liquid crystalline applications in the development of advanced materials and intelligent devices which involves the chirality and the CPL with variable wavelength.

UV light exhibits poor penetration through the substrate and might lead to material decomposition. Near-infrared (NIR) light is preferred for some applications in the fields such as aerospace, life science and materials science due to its superior penetration and invisibility for remote activation of materials with low interference. Our group reported NIR-light-directed reversible tuning of photonic reflection of self-organized helical superstructures doped with upconversion nanoparticles (UCNPs).433 The basic idea is that UCNPs could absorb the external NIR irradiation to emit both UV and visible light, which significantly overlaps with UV and visible absorption of a novel binaphthyl-derived azobenzene chiral switch. At the high NIR power density, the UV emissions of UCNPs are prominent and decisive to drive the trans-to-cis isomerization of photochromic chiral azobenzene, whereas the visible light generated from the same UCNPs at the low power density triggers the reverse cis-to-trans photoisomerization. The reversible photoisomerization led to large HTP variations of the chiral switch. As a result, the nanoparticle-impregnated cholesteric superstructure could exhibit NIR-light-driven reversible tuning of RGB reflection colours by modulating the excitation power density of a 980 nm NIR laser. Recently, we judiciously designed and synthesized the novel coremultishell UCNPs with dual wavelength NIR light transduction properties which furnished UV and visible luminescence on demand upon NIR irradiation at different wavelengths.434 The UCNPs were used as nanotransducers to drive reversible photocyclization of photochromic chiral diarylethene as illustrated in Fig. 64. Interestingly, the self-organized helical superstructure loaded with the UCNP nanotransducers and photochromic chiral diarylethene was found to exhibit light-driven reversible handedness inversion by the alternating use of 980 nm and 808 nm NIR light. Moreover, we also achieved NIR-driven reversible tuning of self-organized liquid crystalline superstructures by combining the thermotropic properties of LCs with the photothermal effect of mesogen-functionalized gold nanorods and graphene derivatives.435437 These works might provide important impetus for developing advanced functional stimuli responsive materials and their potential applications in sensing, biological imaging, photoactivation, smart molecular devices, etc.

The helical axis of cholesteric superstructures could be controlled by surface alignments or by applying external voltage, which builds the foundation for their practical application such as reflective displays, thermography, mirrorless lasing and tuneable colour filters. However, dynamic, remote and continuous control over the helical axis in 3D space is quite a challenging task. Recently, we reported the breakthrough of light-driven 3D control of the helical axis in cholesteric superstructures by using a photochromic chiral diarylethene.438 Upon continuous irradiation with UV light, the standing helix transformed to a lying state and the lying helix rotated in the plane of the LC cell until the system reached its photostationary state (Fig. 65). The system can be driven back in the opposite direction by visible light irradiation. The facile 3D control over the helical axis of the cholesteric superstructures has also been utilized in a specially engineered bilayer LC cell to obtain 2D diffraction gratings. Interestingly, reversible transformation between 2D to 1D and 1D to zero-dimensional (off state) diffraction states has been achieved under alternating UV and visible light irradiation. This work is a step towards the realization of complex, light-activated smart systems and dynamic, reconfigurable three-dimensional architectures.

Self-organized chiral microspheres, with the chiral LCs confined into micrometer-sized spherical configurations, have received considerable attention since they could facilitate the formation of numerous supramolecular architectures with intriguing phenomena and unprecedented functionalities.458465 Our group reported light-driven dynamic chiral liquid-crystalline microshells through the capillary-based microfluidic techniques, where the photoresponsive cholesteric LCs were developed by doping a photochromic chiral azobenzene into an achiral nematic host (Fig. 68a).466 Upon loading with DCM laser dye, the resulting cholesteric microshells were found to exhibit laser emissions in all optical directions when pumped with an external pulsed laser. These observations would not only provide an attractive platform for exploring fundamental micro/nanophotonics with geometric confinements but also hold great potential for various applications including advanced all-optical devices, next-generation communication systems and beyond.

Stimuli-responsive liquid crystalline polymers have been identified as potential enablers for the realization of novel biomedical devices, soft robotics and morphing structures.469478 Introducing photochromic molecules into liquid crystalline polymers is known to photogenerate various macroscale 3D motions such as bending, twisting, buckling, rotating and oscillating.479482 Recent efforts have been devoted to achieving more complicated and sophisticated functions towards artificial machines that could convert light-driven molecular events into macroscopic work. These functional materials have potential applications in artificial muscles, soft robotics, and many other micromechanical systems.

Very recently, Broer et al. demonstrated an unprecedented light-fuelled wave machine by incorporating photochromic azobenzene derivatives with fast cis-to-trans thermal relaxation into liquid crystalline polymers (Fig. 72).485 Towards this end, they firstly designed and synthesized the polymerizable liquid crystalline azobenzene-based derivatives with reducing thermal relaxation time according to two well-known strategies, i.e., adding a pushpull group and forming a tautomerizable azohydrazone.486 The photoactive liquid crystalline polymer films were fabricated in splay-aligned configurations with homeotropic alignment at one surface and planar alignment at the other, which provided the largest deformation in free-standing films due to the expansion and shrinkage behavior. Constant UV irradiation was found to drive continuous millimetre-scale movement of the films, which resulted from both UV-induced trans-to-cis photoisomerization and the reverse cis-to-trans process owing to fast thermal relaxation. When attached through both ends of the resulting LC films to a substrate, they exhibited continuous, directional, macroscopic mechanical waves under constant light illumination. These photoactive films could find potential application in light-driven locomotion and self-cleaning surfaces as well as photomechanical energy harvesting and miniaturized transport.

7. Photochromism into biological nanosystems

Controlling the activity of biological nanosystems such as biomolecules in a spatiotemporal and reversible manner is expected to open up new opportunities for exploring physiological processes at the single-cell level in a living organism.487489 Introducing photochromism into biological nanosystems through functionalization with bistable photochromic molecules has been the current focus of research.490494 A combination of biologically active macromolecules with photochromic molecules endows these hybrid biosystems with both spatial and temporal precision as well as biological specificity, which is expected to provide non-invasive regulation of biological processes and activities in living systems. It is worth noting that photopharmacology is known to utilize molecular photoswitchable moieties to establish control over the action of bioactive molecules.495 Important breakthroughs have been made in the fields of light-controlled diabetes,496 neurology,497 cancer chemotherapy,498 antimicrobial agents,499etc. Herein, we mainly introduce the recent advancement of photoswitchable biological nanosystems with molecular photoswitches including nucleic acid structure and function, peptide structure and function, as well as biological channel and receptor modulation.

7.1 Photoswitchable nucleic acids

The possibility to functionalize nucleic acids and their analogs with photochromic molecules could enable reversible photocontrol of their replication, transcription, and translation processes. Towards this end, many research studies have been carried out to control the structure and binding properties of nucleic acids by introducing photochromic molecules into their diverse structural positions. The photochromic stilbene and azobenzene derivatives were incorporated into the phosphate backbone of DNA oligomers. Photocontrol of hairpin formation from DNA oligomers was observed, where the trans-form of photochromic molecules enabled the formation of stable hairpins in self-pairing sequences while the resultant cis form upon light irradiation destabilized the hairpin structure.500506 When photochromic molecules were introduced to nucleotides, the catalytic RNA cleavage rates of the 1023 deoxyribozyme could be reversibly modulated by light.507 Interestingly, Komiyama et al. demonstrated the photoregulation of RNase H activity with azobenzene-tethered DNA (Fig. 73).508 A series of DNA antisense strands were designed and synthesized with an increasing number of azobenzene molecules. As shown in Fig. 73b, the system was composed of RNA (substrate), antisense DNA without azobenzene, and the corresponding sense DNA incorporating azobenzene moieties. When azobenzenes in the sense DNA adopt the trans-form, it strongly hybridizes to the antisense DNA, and thus RNase H did not digest RNA. UV light irradiation isomerized azobenzene moieties from the trans to cis form, and sense DNA was released due to the instability of the sense/antisense duplex. The released antisense DNA was hybridized to RNA and the duplex of the antisense DNA and RNA was formed, where RNase H could bind the hybridized RNA and digest it. UV light-driven efficient onoff control of RNA digestion could be achieved in the sense strand with three or more azobenzene molecules. The same research group also reported light-directed transcription reaction by T7 RNA polymerase.509 It was found that the transcription could be optically, reversibly and efficiently modulated by using the azobenzene-tethered promoter. The light-driven reversible cis-to-trans isomerization of azobenzene provided two different states of the promoter, which enabled the reversible onoff switching of RNA production. The underlying mechanism was based on the change of the local structure of the DNA duplex by trans-to-cis isomerization of azobenzenes.

Tan et al. demonstrated the manipulation of enzyme inhibition by a nucleic acid probe grafted with an azobenzene (Fig. 74).510 The DNA sequence of the 15-base-long thrombin aptamer was functionalized with photochromic azobenzene and ligated to the aptamer sequence via a polyethylene glycol (PEG) chain. It should be noted that extensive efforts have been recently made to photocontrol the enzymatic activity, where optical remote command by UV, visible and even infrared lights has been achieved.511517 For example, Aida et al. demonstrated a water-soluble adhesive photoswitch that could selectively bind to a target enzyme and thus photochemically modulate its enzymatic activity even in cell lysates (Fig. 75).514 These research studies open a new window for biological and biochemical studies with the need for remote control of enzyme activity.

7.2 Photocontrolled peptides and proteins

The introduction of photochromism into proteins and peptides would enable the light-driven dynamic control of their structure and function. The photoisomerization of photochromic molecules could be utilized for modulating the proteinDNA interactions, proteinprotein interactions, as well as other intracellular signalling processes, thus working as a convenient tool for exploring protein-folding dynamics and beyond. Various azobenzene- or spiropyran-based photoresponsive amino acid residues have been incorporated into polypeptide side chains, in which light irradiation could lead to major conformational changes in the peptide structure. It was found that the photoresponsive behavior of the polypeptides was closely related to the choices of solvent, pH, temperature, etc.522528 The photoresponsive behaviors have also been investigated by incorporating photochromic molecules into the backbone of cyclic peptides. Schutt et al. reported the photocontrol of cell adhesion in a photoresponsive cyclic heptapeptide.529,530 Beyermann et al. reported the photochromic cyclic peptides with azobenzenes, where the contraction of muscle fibers was in vivo controlled when transfected to living cells.531 Komarov et al. demonstrated that the biological activity of diarylethene-containing cyclic peptidomimetics could be effectively controlled by ultraviolet/visible light.532 Moreover, many research studies have been carried out to photocontrol the stability of -hairpins, where the interaction between linked antiparallel peptide strands could be dynamically modulated.533538

Mascarenas et al. demonstrated the photocontrol of the site-specific DNA binding affinity of a photochromic azobenzene-linked peptide.539 The employed protein was able to recognize and bind the DNA and the amino acids from the basic regions of the yeast transcription factor GCN4. Interestingly, it was found that a more efficient DNA binding (6070 times) by the GCN4 derivative could be achieved with the help of trans-to-cis photoisomerization of the azobenzene. As shown in Fig. 78a, UV light irradiation could facilely direct the sequence-specific DNA binding, and the DNA binding led to the changes of peptide segments from a random coil to an -helix structure. Gogoll et al. demonstrated the photocontrol of hydrolytic activity in an artificial hydrolase (HN1) functionalized with the photoresponsive stilbene. Compared to unmodified HN1, a 25% increase in activity was achieved for the cis-isomer, whereas a 12% decrease in activity was observed for the trans-isomer.540 As shown in Fig. 78b, light-driven isomerization of the stilbene towards the cis-isomer led to dimerization of the peptides, where the photoinduced arrangement of the helical segments was believed to alter the catalytic activity.

7.3 Photoswitchable ion channels and receptors

Ion channels and receptors are made up of proteins that span the membrane of cells and function to transport ions or metabolites across cell walls. The combination of photochromic molecules with receptor proteins has proved to be an effective means for rapidly and reversibly controlling channel function that would help to deepen our understanding of channel-related processes and function. The development of photoswitchable endogenous receptors and channels is believed to bring us robust treatment tools for various diseases.543,544 The nicotinic acetylcholine (nACh) receptors are known as ligand-gated ion channels that are found in many tissues of the human body. The nACh ion channel would open upon binding of acetylcholine, which allows the potassium and sodium ions to flow through the channel and thus a change in the cell's membrane potential is brought about. Many efforts have been made to develop the photoswitchable nACh receptors.545549 Erlanger et al. reported a photoswitchable, engineered receptor by using a trimethylammonium (TMA) group at one end and the azobenzene possessing a bromide at the other end (Fig. 80).550 The TMA group functioned as the binding pocket for the nACh receptor. When azobenzene was in its trans form, the interaction between the TMA group and one of the nACh receptor binding sites (red sphere) led to the changes of the ion channel conformation which allowed the ion flow. When azobenzene was in its cis form, the TMA group was no longer able to bind the active site, which resulted in the closing of the channel. Kramer et al. demonstrated the light-driven dynamic control of neuronal nACh receptors with photoswitchable tethered agonists and antagonists.551 These engineered receptors would not only provide us attractive tools for dynamically controlling the pentameric ligand-gated ion channels in a remote and controllable manner, but also open a brand-new door for exploring the physiological and pathological functions of neuronal nACh receptors.

Gramicidin A channels are another important topic of biological research.552,553 Woolley et al. reported light-driven reversible opening and closing of gramicidin A channels (Fig. 81a).554 Both the entry and exit of a gramicidin A channel were attached by an azobenzene tether with a cationic group at the end. Schreiber et al. developed a photoswitchable gramicidin A channel by attaching both ends of an azobenzene moiety with two gramicidin A monomers (Fig. 81b).555 When the azobenzene was in its cis form, the two gramicidin A monomers form one long pore spanning the lipid membrane, allowing ion transport. When the azobenzene moiety was isomerized to its trans form, the monomers would no longer be able to align in one line, therefore limiting ion transport. Moreover, the possibility of photomodulating channel conductance was also demonstrated.

8. Conclusion and perspective

Photochromic molecules and their promising photochromism processes are playing increasingly important roles in various emerging scientific and interdisciplinary areas of research, ranging from physics, chemistry and materials science to biology and nanotechnology. Nowadays, extensive efforts have been made to develop light-driven dynamic nanosystems with remote control and non-invasive attributes. The incorporation of photochromic molecules into diverse nanoarchitectures and resulting functional materials endows them with intriguing photoresponsive properties, which is expected to drive future nanoscience and nanotechnology community towards the development of advanced intelligent devices with novel, multiple and versatile functionalities. When photochromism is prescribed into artificial nanomachines, light-driven diverse motions at the nanoscale are facilely achieved, which have been further utilized for generating external force to carry out the appealing task in the macroscopic world in a remote controllable fashion. The functional nanoparticles grafted with photochromic molecules could exhibit interesting photoresponsive physical and chemical properties such as reversible self-assembly, switchable nanofluorescence, and photoinduced magnetization. The marriage of nanoelectronics with photochromic molecules enables us to optically manipulate their electronic performances at both nanoscale molecular levels and microscale levels. Upon introducing photochromism into the interior or other parts of 3D metalorganic molecular cages and metalorganic frameworks, we could achieve the controlled capture and release of guest molecules under an external optical control, which possesses great promise in numerous applications such as catalysis, gas storage, and drug delivery. Photochromism has also enabled us to develop various photoswitchable supramolecular self-assembled systems such as supramolecular amphiphiles, supramolecular polymers, and light-driven liquid crystalline superstructures. By functionalization of biomolecules with bistable photochromism, it is possible to spatiotemporally control the bioactivity of various biological nanosystems such as nucleic acids, peptide structure, as well as biological channels and receptors. In addition, some researchers have endeavoured to develop many other photochromic nanosystems such as photoactive carbon nanomaterials,563 photoactive silica-based nanostructured materials,564 photoresponsive hydrogel based biomaterials,565 photoresponsive molecular crystals,566569 photocontrollable surface wettability,570,571 and remote-photocontrolled chemical reactions.572,573

Although great steps have been taken with regard to photochromism into nanosystems, the development is still in a very preliminary stage. To exploit the full potential of photochromic molecules and functional nanosystems, there are still many basic challenges and critical issues that remain to be resolved. The photoswitching efficiency and photoreversibility of photochromic molecules are often reduced or even lost when linked to nanosystems such as metallic surfaces, which to a large extent affects the whole photofunctionality of the resultant nanoarchitectures. It has been reported that the interactions between photochromic molecules and surfaces could result in various negative effects on the confined molecules including the destabilization of one of the two metastable ground states, the reduced lifetime of the photoexcited state, etc.574576 To surmount these hurdles, different linking groups such as rigid conjugated anchors, flexible nonconjugated anchors, and multipod anchors have been designed for bridging the photochromophores and nanosystems. It has been found that photoswitchable efficiency and photoreversibility are closely related to the molecule surface distances, extent of conjugations, intermolecular spaces, etc.115124 However, systematic investigation of the moleculesurface interactions is still rather limited and the underlying mechanism remains somewhat obscure. Furthermore, the photochromic molecules are mostly responsive to UV or visible light with a wavelength ranging from 250 to 550 nm. Many efforts have been made to design and synthesize the molecules with visible switching wavelengths,577581 but the design and synthesis of effective photochromic molecules that could be operated at longer wavelengths, such as the infrared with superior penetration, low interference and invisibility for remote activation, remain a major challenge. Another opportunity is to design and synthesize new photochromophores with superior photoswitchable efficiency and photoreversibility to increase the versatility of the photochromic family.582591 For example, Yam et al. recently found that photochromic heterocycle-fused thieno[3,2-b]phosphole oxides could function as visible light switches without sacrificing photoswitching efficiency.592 For biological applications, the issues of toxicity and metabolic stability of photochromic molecules and the resulting nanosystems will have to be addressed. It is of paramount importance to develop a very facile and more general methodology for the appropriate incorporation of photochromic moieties into different nanosystems that could stimulate great breakthroughs of light-driven dynamic nanosystems. The up-to-date advancements in this fascinating field have enabled us to master the nanoarchitectures of discrete objects with nanometer dimensions, but it is still a formidable challenge and far more difficult to optically manipulate the structure, function, or activity of molecular systems and nanoscale devices in a way just like what we observe in Nature's highly complex and integrated dynamic biosystems. While nanoscientists try to uncover the fledgling potential of nanotechnology and to explore the nanoworld, it should be remembered that learning from nature to design future multifunctional nanosystems might be very helpful to answer key questions on the fundamental difference between the macroscopic and nanoscale molecular world. There is no doubt that photochromism into nanosystems has been and will continue to be a hot topic in the prosperous area of nanoscience and nanotechnology, which might give birth to multifunctional reconfigurable and programmable materials with more complex structures and myriad possibilities. The exciting and dynamic progress in the past decades has provided a solid foundation for potential applications of multitudinous functional nanostructured materials. It is anticipated that our future nanoworld would be lit up under the joint efforts and meaningful cooperation of engineers from different backgrounds and scientists from multidisciplinary fields such as photophysics, photochemistry, photobiology, supramolecular chemistry as well as optical technology and engineering.
