Abstract

Catalysts play an important role in the energy conversion reaction process. With the continuous development of the energy sector, researchers are constantly exploring and developing new high-performance, inexpensive catalysts to meet the demand. Transition metal phosphides (TMPs) as ever-evolving catalytic materials have received more and more attention. Many studies have suggested that transition metal phosphides could be ideal catalysts due to their low cost, abundance, and high efficiency. In many areas, TMPs can be applied as stable and efficient catalytic materials. Therefore, many kinds of TMPs are designed and synthesized by different methods. This article has summarized the recent research progress of TMPs, including their preparation methods and morphologies. Moreover, the application and catalytic performance of TMPs in different catalytic fields are further elaborated, and views on the opportunities and challenges faced by phosphide catalysts are presented.

1 Introduction

With recent economic and social development, the demand for energy dramatically increases as well.1â4 Currently, exploring new forms of energy conversion to improve energy efficiency is a research hotspot around the world. There are many forms of energy conversion, such as the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) in the water splitting process, oxidation reduction reaction (ORR), methanol oxidation, etc.5â7 For instance, in the water splitting reaction (including the HER and OER), the required voltage is theoretically 1.23 V. However, due to the existence of reactive energy barriers, the voltage required in the actual electrochemical reaction is much larger than the theoretical value.8â14 In many energy conversions, the catalyst is indispensable in the reaction. The use of catalysts in the conversion can significantly reduce the energy consumption in the conversion process and increase the energy conversion rate.15

In order to reduce the energy required for electrochemical reactions, researchers have begun to explore catalyst materials. Precious metal-based catalysts exhibit very good catalytic performance in many energy conversion fields. It is well known that Pt and its composites have excellent HER catalytic properties, as it has a very low overpotential in both acidic and alkaline solutions. In addition to the HER direction, Pt based catalysts also exhibit excellent catalytic performance in other fields, such as the ORR, photocatalysis, etc. Although noble metal-based catalysts possess good catalytic performance, their high cost and other disadvantages limit their potential for large-scale applications. Therefore, many studies have focused on the development of new, low-cost and high-efficiency catalysts to replace noble metal based catalysts.16â25

With advances in characterization techniques and theoretical calculations, researchers have gained a deeper understanding of catalytic materials and can also design catalysts with excellent performance. For instance, the catalyst is essential in the HER process. Hydrogen adsorption and desorption on the surface of the catalytic material determine the catalytic performance. Therefore, the ideal catalyst should have a relatively balanced ability.26,27 The Sabatier principle pointed out that the catalyst surface which has intermediate bonding energies with reactive intermediates can achieve optimal catalytic performance.28 Parsons et al. indicated that the maximum exchange current density could be reached when the hydrogen adsorption free energy was close to thermoneutral (ÎGH â¼ 0).29 NÃ¸rskov et al. calculated ÎGH on different transition metals via density functional theory (DFT) calculation. They suggested that ÎGH is a useful descriptor in the selection of new electrocatalysts for the HER.26

Recently, a variety of catalysts have been developed, mainly based on metal-based catalysts. Among these catalysts, transition metal based catalysts exhibit impressive electrochemical performance as electrode materials.30,31 The commonly used transition metal elements are mainly Co, Fe, Ni, Mn, etc. Non-metallic elements generally used in electrode materials are C, N, O, S and P32,33 Catalytic materials composed of these transition metal elements and non-metallic elements exhibit excellent electrochemical performance in many fields. For example, transition metal sulfides (NiS2, NiCo2S4, CoNi2S4, NiMoS, etc.) can not only be applied in the supercapacitor area, but also have the ability to photolyze water or electrolyze water.34â39 Transition metal oxides, such as NiCo2O4, can be used not only in the direction of energy storage (supercapacitors and Li-ion batteries), but also as electrocatalytic materials (OER and photocatalysis).40,41 Among the transition metal-based catalysts, transition metal phosphides (TMPs) are currently representative catalytic materials.

TMPs have been synthesized and applied for a long time. In the early days, they were mainly employed in hydrodesulfurization (HDS).42 In 2005, Ping Liu et al. used X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and DFT calculations to investigate the Ni2P catalyst in the desulfurization process of thiophene (a typical test molecule in HDS studies) and found the complex and important role that P sites play in the phosphide. Firstly, the authors suggested that the formation of NiâP bonds produces a weak âligand effectâ (minor stabilization of the Ni 3d levels and a small Ni â P charge transfer) that allows a high activity for the dissociation of thiophene and molecular hydrogen. Secondly, because of an âensemble effectâ of P, the number of active Ni sites present in the surface decreases a lot, which prevents the system from deactivation induced by the high coverage of strongly bound S. The authors also suggested that the P sites in Ni2P are not simple spectators, and they provide moderate bonding to the products of the decomposition of thiophene and the H adatoms necessary for hydrogenation.43 Inspired by HDS, the authors connected a similar mechanism of HER, which also involved reversible association and dissociation of hydrogen atoms in the process like HDO. Later, the authors employed DFT-GGA calculations to investigate the HER activity of Ni2P (001) and compared it with those of other materials. The article reported that the good behavior of Ni2P in the HER is associated with an ensemble effect, where the concentration of highly active Ni sites in the surface is decreased by the presence of P. Besides, the P sites are not simple spectators and provide moderate bonding to the reaction intermediates, due to these factors the surface has proton-acceptor and hydride-acceptor centers that work to facilitate the HER.44

The recent development of transition metal nitrides as another promising family of transition metal-based catalysts enlightened us about the application of TMPs in energy conversion. The metalâN4 chelate bond in the macrocycles (MâN4-macrocycles), possessed from Fe or Co polyaniline, porphyrin, and phthalocyanine compounds are generally recognized as an active site for the efficient reduction of oxygen, even outperforming the well-known Pt/C.45 The reason from the perspective of molecular orbital theory is that the MâN4 structure complexes with the conjugated Ï-electron system, which serves as the center of oxygen activation. This causes an increase of the OâO bond distance, and thus, stimulates the ORR on the surface of the catalysts.46 Given this, phosphorous (P), belonging to the same nitrogen group, is likely to possess superior chemical properties compared to the N atom due to the existence of multi-electron orbitals. Even though the relevant theoretical explanation has not been suggested, the wide application of TMPs in various energy conversion fields has proved it worth searching.

Recently, TMPs have received more and more attention due to their unique electrochemical properties. TMPs have demonstrated good catalytic performance in many fields. For example, in the HER direction, TMPs have proven to be high-performance catalysts which have high activity, high stability, and close to 100% faradaic efficiency in a strong acid, a strong base and a neutral medium while also having high catalytic efficiency. Besides the HER, TMPs also show great potential for applications in other areas.47,48 In this review, we investigate and survey the recent progress of cost-effective and high-performance transition metal phosphides as catalysts for energy conversion, such as HDO, HER, ORR and photocatalysis. For the convenience of discussion, we mainly focus on the preparation methods, morphologies and applications of TMPs in the field of catalysis. This work aims to provide insights on the advances of transition metal phosphides. Finally, the prospects for the development of TMPs as catalysts in the future will also be discussed.

2.1 Single transition metal phosphides

Single TMPs are the most common class of phosphides. Researchers have prepared a variety of phosphides via different synthetic methods. The morphologies of these phosphides are also different. Cobalt and nickel phosphides are the most common phosphides. Many interesting morphologies for Co- or Ni-based phosphides have been reported. Chua et al. fabricated nanostructured CoP particles via a two-step process. They first prepared Co3O4 nanocubes via a facile hydrothermal method. This process can be performed at 120 Â°C. In the next step, they converted the crystalline Co3O4 to CoP particles by chemical vapor deposition. This structured CoP exhibited good performance for the HER.49 Similarly, Li et al. also prepared CoP compounds with a similar morphology. The synthesis process is shown in Fig. 1a. They first prepared carbon nanotubes (CNTs), and then adopted metalâorganic frameworks (MOFs) as templates to synthesize ZIF-67-CNTs. Finally, CoP-CNTs were obtained by annealing and phosphating processes. The CoP-CNT hybrids exhibited excellent electrochemical performance.50

2.2 Bimetallic phosphides

In addition to single transition metal phosphides, bimetallic phosphides currently occupy a very important position. Compared with single TMPs, many bimetallic phosphides are more prominent in the field of catalysis. Recently, varieties of bimetallic phosphides have been prepared by different methods. Some of these phosphides exhibit excellent catalytic performance that is comparable to those of precious metal-based catalysts. In this part, we will focus on the preparation of some excellent phosphides and their special morphologies.

Hu et al. fabricated a novel bimetallic-structured NiCo2Px electrocatalyst via a facile strategy. 3 Applications of phosphides in energy conversion

Phosphides have a very wide range of applications, and many phosphides exhibit impressive electrochemical performance in the hydrogen evolution reaction, oxygen evolution reaction, hydrodeoxygenation, oxygen reduction reaction, etc. In this section, we will give a brief introduction to the latest developments of transition metal phosphides in the direction of energy conversion.

3.1 Hydrodeoxygenation

Fossil fuels are still the main source of energy worldwide, but the high dependence on this single form of energy is not a long-term solution for the sustainable development of mankind. Thus, researchers from all over the world have been focusing on the study of new energy to alleviate the current severe energy problems. Under this condition, researchers found that bio-oils, mainly produced from the liquefaction or pyrolysis of lignocellulosic biomass, with the advantages of the wide range and ready availability of raw materials have been regarded as promising alternative sources of fuels. Although, compared with conventional fuels, bio-oils have limitations. The greatest limitation is a much higher oxygen content (45â50 wt%).119 This will lead to many undesired properties like low heating value, low thermostability, high viscosity, and poor miscibility with other petroleum components. To reduce oxygen content is an indispensable part to make the conversion of bio-oils to fuels possible. Hydrodeoxygenation (HDO) plays a very important role in the process to upgrade pyrolysis bio-oils to produce suitable biofuels or blends to be added into existing fuels.120

The conversion of oxygenated groups in bio-fuels to upgrade these oils aims to improve their properties including increasing their thermal and chemical stability, heating value, and volatility. The total or partial removal of oxygenates from pyrolysis liquids is mainly performed by HDO (eqn (1)). The idealized stoichiometric equation is as follows:

Although H2 consumption is needed in the HDO of pyrolysis oils, the high heat value of the product and non-emission of waste gas still make its practical application meaningful. To improve the efficiency, the HDO reaction is usually performed at high temperatures and high pressures in the presence of a catalyst. As we can see in the equation above, water will be generated during the HDO reaction, which coupled with the water present in the pyrolysis oil may cause an adverse effect on the lifetime of the catalyst. Even worse, many compounds produced in the thermal degradation of lignin, like guaiacol (GUA, 2-methoxyphenol) and substituted guaiacols, tend to form heavy hydrocarbons and coke which will reduce the activity of the catalyst and even make it poisoned. Thus, the focus of recent research remains to be finding efficient and steady catalysts.121

In modern refineries, the industrial hydrotreating catalysts are mainly based on molybdenum or tungsten sulfides, like NiMoS, CoMoS, etc. which also show great activity in removing oxygenated groups from bio-oils.122,123 Although sulfide catalysts show pretty high selectivity and activity in HDO of bio-oils, because of the loss of sulfur in the catalytic process, these metal sulfide catalysts will suffer from progressive deactivation, and finally become inactive, and besides, the lost sulfur species will contaminate the cleaned products.124 Noble metal catalysts, as the most satisfactory choice, can almost avoid all the problems above while still exhibiting high activity, but the expensive prices dramatically limit their large-scale commercial applications. In recent decades, non-noble metal catalysts have been well studied, and non-noble metal phosphide catalysts with the advantages of acceptable catalytic activity and cost effectiveness, provide new aspiration for the studies of potential bio-oil HDO catalysts.

3.2 Photocatalysis

Energy crisis has become a worldwide issue as the consumption of fossil fuels, as well as a series of consequential environmental problems. Hydrogen, a promising energy carrier with little pollution, has attracted a lot of attention in human society. Besides electrochemical hydrogen production by the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), the solar driven generation of hydrogen (or artificial photosynthesis hydrogen) is another environmentally friendly way that deserves research.131,132 Generally, there are three main types of artificial photosynthesis system. Photoelectrochemical cells (PECs), as one of the most promising and environmental technologies for clean energy, are the firstly studied system.133 In principle, PECs are made up of two sides: an n-type semiconductor as the photo-anode and a p-type semiconductor as the photo-cathode. Under light irradiation, the electrons and holes are generated in the semiconductor photoelectrode. The electrons on the surface of the cathode can reduce protons to hydrogen, while the holes on the photoanode surface oxidizes water to oxygen.134 However, a power supply is needed to provide a large external bias, which leads to unnecessary energy consumption. In order to overcome this shortcoming, semiconductor powder systems have been proposed, which generate photovoltage by itself to build the âmicro-PECâ system. The typical âmicro-PECâ involves three steps: (1) generation of photoexcited electronâhole pairs under light irradiation in the bulk, (2) migration of the photoexcited electrons and holes to the surface of the âmicro-PECâ without recombination, and (3) reactions towards hydrogen evolution and oxygen evolution. In theory, the process of water splitting involves an energy barrier presented in the forms of Gibbs energy (ÎG0 = 237.13 kJ molâ1), equaling 1.23 eV photon energy. However, the photon energy to drive the water splitting reaction is usually larger than the bandgap of the semiconductor because of the existence of an activation barrier in the charge transfer process between the photocatalyst and H2O molecules.135 Besides, the backward reaction of the formation of H2O from H2 and O2 has also diminished the catalytic efficiency. As a more advanced device, the Z-scheme water splitting system, inspired by natural proceeds in green plants, has been developed during the last two years.136 A Z-scheme type photocatalyst is a kind of system imitating the natural photosynthesis process. With the help of shuttle redox mediators (electron shuttles, often working as ionic type redox pairs, conductor-like metal nanoparticles or reduced graphene oxide), the excited electron of photosystem I is transferred to photosystem II to combine with the hole. And the left hole and excited electron in photosystems I and II can be used for reductionâoxidation reactions. Therefore, the Z-scheme system possesses superiority in this respect. Besides, the Z-scheme system can not only separate the excited electrons and holes, but also retain stronger redox ability, which is more beneficial to involve in related reactions.137 Unlike semiconductor powder systems, except for the two half reactions hydrogen reduction and oxygen oxidation, there is an appropriate shuttle redox mediator that could avoid the backward reactions. However, the obstacles of the Z-scheme are also the backward reactions, which are the reduction and oxidation of redox couples on the hydrogen photocatalyst and oxygen photocatalyst, respectively.138 In addition, complicated interfacial situations like a low transfer rate of carriers would result in energy loss. Consequently, the efficiency of the artificial Z-scheme system is far below the theoretical value.139 Therefore, photon utilization is not as high as the other two systems, and the selection of the oxidationâreduction reaction is limited. All these problems restrict its improvement of water splitting activity. Generally, co-catalysts are usually utilized to enhance the abilities of hydrogen and oxygen evolution. The most common strategy is to employ co-catalysts on the surface of semiconductors, particularly for hydrogen reduction sites. The improvement of hydrogen evolution by HER co-catalysts would be higher than that of oxygen evolution by OER co-catalysts.140

Recently, noble metals such as Pt and their metal-based catalysts remain the benchmark HER photocatalysts, but the fatal weakness of noble metals themselves limits their scalable application, such as the side reaction of the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) because of the lower Fermi level and good electron trapping abilities and the limited reserves.141 Thus, alternative co-catalysts based on non-precious metals and metal-free materials are necessary for the investigation of suitable catalysts. In the overall photocatalytic water splitting system, non-Pt metals or metal oxides are always employed in the co-catalysts for HER sites, as well as in the non-cocatalyst photo harvesters.

The photoreduction deposition method is an efficient strategy for the preparation of a stable, environment-friendly and robust HER co-catalyst. Recently, many non-noble metal photocatalysts prepared by the photoreduction deposition method have been reported. Among them, transition metal phosphides exhibit a superior catalytic activity. Since 2014, transition metal (Fe, Co, Ni, and Cu) phosphides have been proposed as co-catalysts with CdS or g-C3N4 for photocatalytic hydrogen generation and their performance were good. 3.3 Hydrogen evolution reaction

With the development of human society and the surge of global population, people's demand for energy is growing dramatically. The uncontrolled exploitation and use of fossil fuels is intensifying global warming and accelerating the energy crisis. Under this condition, studies to find a new alternative energy have become urgent. Hydrogen, having many advantages like the highest gravimetric energy density, environmental friendliness, and a wide range of resources, is the most ideal candidate for alternative energy.149 The HER, namely the Hydrogen Evolution Reaction from the electrolysis of water, is the main approach to produce H2. Currently, the most efficient catalyst for the HER in alkaline media is Pt, but its scarcity and whopping price make its practical use on a large scale impossible, and thus, finding an efficient and easily obtainable catalyst for the HER is the focus of research.

The overall process of water electrolysis has two half reactions: HER (2H+(aq) + 2eâ â H2(g)) on cathodes, and OER (2H2O(l) â 4eâ + 4H+(aq) + O2(g)) on anodes.150 There are generally two steps for the HER in acidic media, the first is the absorption of H+(aq), and the other is the desorption of hydrogen from the catalyst.151

The first step is a discharge process, namely a Volmer reaction. In this step, H+ in the electrolyte is transformed into an intermediate state on the active site of the catalyst by capturing an electron:

H+(aq) + eâ + Cat â Hads (Volmer reaction);

From the first step, it is observed that different catalysts with various absorption ability toward H+ will cause the Hads coverage on that catalyst to be low or high, which will lead to two different mechanisms for hydrogen generation. Therefore, the second step can be done in two different ways.

When Hads coverage on the surface of this catalyst is low, the Hads tends to combine with another electron and proton on the original active site to form H2 and then desorb from that site, which is named a Heyrovsky reaction:

Hads + H+(aq) + eâ â H2(g) (Heyrovsky reaction);

When combined with high Hads coverage, the Hads on different active sites is inclined to couple with each other to evolve H2, which is called a Tafel reaction:

Hads + Hads â H2(g) (Tafel reaction);

From the reactions above, the overall process could be described thermodynamically as the repeated adsorption and desorption of Hads from active sites. Depending on the Sabatier principle, a good HER catalyst should be able to combine with Hads easily to form a strong bond but the bond should also be weak enough for improving the process of hydrogen generation.152,153 There is no way to directly measure the values of the surface-intermediate bonding energy, and therefore the quantitative relationship between the energetics of the Hads intermediate and the HER electrochemical reaction rate is difficult to determine. From the aspect of physical chemistry, Gibbs free energy (ÎGHads) can be used to evaluate both Hads adsorption and H2 desorption, which can be obtained through DFT on certain lattice planes of catalysts:

ÎGHads = ÎEZPE + ÎEHads â TÎSH
where ÎEZPE is the difference of the zero-point energy between the adsorbed H (Hads) and isolated H, ÎEHads is the differential hydrogen chemisorption energy, T is the temperature, and ÎSH is the entropy changes between the process of adsorption and desorption of H. According to Sabatier's principle, a catalyst with ÎGHads = 0 is the most ideal one, under which condition the overall reaction has the maximum rate.154 And the reaction rate could be evaluated by HER exchange current density (j0), and thus NÃ¸rskov et al. studied the HER performance of a series of metal materials, and introduced a âvolcano plotâ which revealed the hierarchical kinetics among metals and the close correlation between exchange current density (j0) and the free energy change for Hads adsorption ÎGHads.155

In typical and well-known volcano plots, nearly thermoneutral Pt in the middle is the most efficient catalyst for the HER, while Au and Ag located in the bottom-right are poor catalysts. The reason is that Au and Ag metals could hardly form a strong bond with Hads, making the ad/desorption process impossible. However, because of the whopping price and scarcity, it is impracticable for Pt to be used as a HER catalyst on a large scale. Thus, the development of non-noble metal electrocatalysts is the emphasis of research. Over past years, a series of transition metal selenides,156 chalcogenides,157 borides,158 nitrides,159 carbides,160 and phosphides161 as the potential candidates for catalyzing the HER have been well studied.

4 Conclusions

Recently, TMPs as cost-effective materials have shown excellent catalytic properties in many directions, such as the HER, ORR, MOR, etc. Some of these phosphides even show performance comparable to those of noble metal catalysts. Therefore, TMPs have attracted more and more researchers. In this article, we mainly introduced and summarized the recent progress of TMPs in the direction of energy conversion. We focused on the current common phosphides and introduced their synthesis methods and special morphologies. Some TMPs with outstanding performances in the direction of energy conversion have also been reviewed. Although a variety of phosphides have been prepared in recent years, these phosphides have already been widely used more in the energy conversion area. Though many related research results have been obtained, TMPs still have great development potential and application prospects. Herein, we will highlight some of the insights into their current challenges and future directions.

(1) Although many phosphides have been prepared, the main transition metals used are still Co, Fe, Ni, etc. To further improve the catalytic performance and development of TMPs, researchers could try to explore other TMPs to prepare phosphides, such as Cu, Mn, Zn, etc. This might be able to further enhance their catalytic performance and improve their practical performance.

(2) In the preparation of phosphides, the most commonly used method is still the gasâsolid reaction. This method is relatively simple compared to many other phosphating procedures, such as high temperature organic phosphating. The source of phosphorus used is sodium hypophosphite. The reaction conditions required are also relatively mild. However, because of this, explorations to prepare new phosphides are limited. Although this method is relatively simple, there are also some drawbacks. The most obvious point is that the amount of phosphorus required for this process is large. In order to solve this problem, we can adopt different phosphating ideas, such as changing the form of the gasâsolid reaction, or directly using a phosphorus-containing compound to prepare phosphides.

(3) Researchers have demonstrated that many phosphides have excellent catalytic properties through various test characterization methods. It is well known that the addition of phosphorus does improve their performance. Some tests and simulation calculations, such as EIS, ECSA, DFT, etc., have also been employed to explain their excellent electrochemical performance. However, in some systems, the mechanism of action of phosphorus atoms in the reaction cannot be fully explained. Moreover, there are still very few studies on how to use theory and calculations to predict which phosphides have excellent electrochemical performance. Putting more effort into the mechanism of action and simulation calculations will be very beneficial. This may help us to make a more efficient phosphide catalyst, while also saving more energy.

(4) As a variety of phosphides are prepared, they are also applied in some areas, such as HDO, HER, ORR, methanol oxidation, etc. Phosphides also show impressive catalytic performance in these areas. However, the applications of phosphides in the catalytic direction are not comprehensive. For example, some TMPs do exhibit excellent catalytic performance in the HER field, but this also prevents them from being used in other directions. In the HDO direction, the most widely used phosphide is still Ni2P. We should try to apply various kinds of phosphides in different fields and investigate their performance in different directions. This facilitates the rapid expansion of phosphides in the development of catalytic materials and catalytic applications.

(5) For TMPs, the P sites often play the role of spectators and moderate bonding providers for the products. Therefore, we can generally adjust the distribution density of metal and non-metal sites on the catalyst surface to moderate the desorption and adsorption capacity of protons and other involved ions, making the catalysts most efficient for various catalytic reactions. Moreover, the active site is also critical for the catalysts. It is also an important direction to improve the activity of the catalyst by increasing the number of active sites. The number of active sites can be increased, for example, by constructing a three-dimensional structure or by using the catalytic material itself as the porous electrode.

Although the current development of phosphides has encountered some challenges, they are still very promising catalytic materials. We believe that with the development of synthesis methods and the deep understanding of the mechanism, TMPs will occupy a very important position in many practical applications.
