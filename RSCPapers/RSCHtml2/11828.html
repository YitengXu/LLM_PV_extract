Two-dimensional wide band-gap nitride semiconductor GaN and AlN materials: properties, fabrication and applications

Abstract

Gallium nitride (GaN) and aluminium nitride (AlN), as the representatives of new-generation wide band gap semiconductor materials, have become a hot spot in the semiconductor field due to their excellent characteristics. Benefiting from the unique two-dimensional (2D) layered structure and outstanding properties, 2D materials have proven to be good candidates for electronic and photoelectric devices, energy storage and conversion devices, and wearable devices. In recent years, plenty of research has been conducted for exploring the properties and fabrication of 2D GaN and AlN materials for different application purposes. In this review, the recent progress in 2D GaN and AlN materials, their properties, fabrication methods, and potential applications is summarized. Moreover, the challenges and the development trend of 2D group-III nitride semiconductor materials in the future are prospected.

1. Introduction

Graphene was first prepared by mechanical stripping in 2004.1 Its success shows that certain layered materials can be manufactured in a way that forms a stable thin layer, just one or a few atoms thick. Since the discovery of graphene, a wave of research on two-dimensional (2D) materials has aroused around the world. So far, dozens of 2D materials have been discovered or prepared.2 The first is the graphene family represented by graphene and h-BN (white graphene), followed by the transition-metal dichalcogenide (TMDC) family,3,4 represented by semiconducting dichalcogenides5 and metallic dichalcogenides,6 and finally 2D oxides, such as layered Cu oxides7 and perovskite-type oxides.8 Compared with traditional three-dimensional (3D) materials, these 2D materials exhibit many unique properties. For example, graphene has high carrier mobility,9 good thermal conductivity10 and high transparency.11 It is an ideal material for transparent conductive nanomembranes, which can enhance the transverse diffusion of injection current.2 Materials in the TMDC family exhibit properties such as semiconducting, superconducting, and half-metallic magnetism.12 These different properties make them have great potential applications in catalysis, energy storage, sensing and beyond.2 Compared with 3D oxides, 2D oxides have a lower dielectric constant and wider band gap.13 These properties of 2D materials enable them to be used in a wider range of fields. It has been reported that 2D materials have a wide range of applications in field-effect transistors (FET), photodetectors (PDs), transparent conductive electrodes, deep ultraviolet light-emitting diodes (DUV-LEDs) and other fields.14–19 As a result, there has been great interest in the exploration of 2D materials.

In the field of semiconductor materials, GaN and AlN are the representatives of new-generation wide band gap semiconductor materials. Because of their excellent physical properties, they have been widely used in the manufacturing of LEDs,20,21 laser diodes (LDs),22,23 PDs,24,25 and solar cells,26,27 playing an important role in solid-state lighting, flat panel displays, solar energy, electronic power and other fields.2 The growth and application of these materials has been developed for many years, however, these efforts just mainly focused on their bulk structures. If group-III nitride semiconductor materials are to be used in smaller devices, they need to be explored at the nanoscale. Compared with the bulk structure, the 2D group-III nitride semiconductor material has a thin atomic structure and a high-activity surface. The combination of these two advantages can make the material exhibit more excellent and interesting properties.28 In terms of GaN, 2D GaN has a larger band gap,29 blue-shifted photoluminescence (PL) emission peak and higher internal quantum efficiency30 compared with bulk GaN. Moreover, due to the decrease of thickness, the lattice structure of 2D GaN is distorted, which will reduce the surface energy and make the crystal structure more stable. At the same time, it will cause the breaking and formation of the bond, resulting in the change of energy band structure, electrical conductivity, carrier mobility and other physical properties.28 So far, research studies have shown that when a group-III nitride semiconductor material is made into a 2D material with a thickness of only a few atoms, an ultra-wide band gap will be generated due to the quantum confinement effect, and under the excitation light with electronic activity, it has more excellent electron mobility and will have greater potential for heterogeneous integration.29,31 At the same time, the ultra-thin thickness of 2D nitride semiconductor materials gives them optical transparency and greater flexibility, and the in-plane covalent bond gives them great mechanical strength. These two characteristics make the nitride semiconductor materials have greater potential for isomerization integration, and are ideal materials for flexible devices.32,33 In addition, the high specific surface area and large lateral size of 2D materials make them highly surface-active and suitable for surface-dependent applications.34–36 Furthermore, 2D materials have the advantages of wide spectral response and low dark current, which are suitable for the construction of high-performance and power devices.28,37,38 Some scientists also predict that 2D nitride semiconductors will have abnormal thermal conductivity and orbital driven ultra-low thermal conductivity.39,40 Therefore, if group-III nitride semiconductor materials can be grown or prepared into a 2D structure in some effective ways, the application fields of group-III nitride semiconductor materials will be expanded significantly. However, group-III nitride semiconductor materials do not have layered structures like graphene. They have inherent strong covalent bonds in 3D space, which makes it very difficult to mechanically peel them layer by layer to convert them to a 2D morphology, and greatly hinders 2D anisotropic growth.41 Therefore, the fabrication of ideal 2D group-III nitride semiconductor materials is still in its infancy.

In this article, we review the latest research progress in 2D GaN and AlN, including nanosheets, nanobelts, monolayers (ML) or bilayers, and quasi-2D (films, nanomembranes) materials. First, some theoretical calculation results of the 2D GaN structure and the fabrication methods of 2D GaN and AlN which appeared in recent years are introduced. Then, we also summarize the latest research progress in 2D GaN and AlN devices, such as FETs, LEDs, Li-ion batteries, and so on. Some properties of 2D GaN and AlN are also introduced. 2. Structure and properties

At present, there are few reports about 2D group-III nitride semiconductor materials. As a result, understanding of their 2D properties remains largely superficial. As group-III nitride semiconductor materials change from bulk to just one or several layers, they can be expected to exhibit different properties than bulk. Here, we summarize some properties of 2D group-III nitride semiconductor materials explored by theoretical calculations, focusing on the structure and electronic properties of 2D GaN, hoping to provide some references for the exploration of 2D properties of nitride semiconductor materials.

Single crystal GaN possesses a wurtzite structure, with a C6v-symmetric (100) and a c-elongated hexagon (0001) plane. It is a typical two-dimensional non-layered material. By comparing the geometric structures of ML GaN and bulk GaN, Qin et al. found that when the bulk GaN is transformed into ML GaN, the Ga-N bond along the c-axis in the bulk GaN is destroyed, and the sp3 orbital hybridization in the original GaN4 tetrahedron is completely destroyed, which converts the original buckled structure into a planar structure (Fig. 1a). It forms sp2 hybridization.40 This change causes the lattice constant of ML GaN to increase.42 Planar sp2 hybridization also makes the Ga–N bond in the layer possess strong covalent properties, and the closest neighbor Pz orbital forms a π bond similar to that of graphene.43 The charge density difference of ML GaN also changes (Fig. 1a). The ability of the Ga atoms in ML GaN to lose electrons is reduced, as shown in Fig. 1a(ii) showing the reduction of the green region in the center of the honeycomb lattice. For ML and bulk GaN, the charge transfer of each Ga–N bond is 0.453 e and 0.358 e, respectively. The higher the charge transfer in ML GaN, the stronger the covalent bond polarization, which is consistent with the more localized charge density in Fig. 1a(ii).40

The change of geometric structure and electronic structure has a significant effect on the heat transfer performance of ML GaN. At the same time, due to the large difference in the radius of the Ga and N atoms, the smoothness of ML GaN decreases, which also has some effects on its performance. The uneven distribution of charge density corresponding to the strongly polarized covalent bonds in ML GaN induces additional long-range electrostatic coulomb forces during atomic vibration. As the atoms oscillate near equilibrium, the additional forces put the atoms under greater constraint, which corresponds to a larger nonharmonic coefficient in the Taylor expansion of the system energy relative to atomic displacement. The greater the inconsonance, the stronger the intrinsic phonon–phonon scattering and the lower the lattice thermal conductivity (κ). Therefore, the strongly polarized covalent bond of ML GaN can lead to greater phonon anharmonic, resulting in a lower κ of ML GaN.40 They also solved the κ at 300 K for several substances derived from the phonon Boltzmann transport equation by an iterative method (Fig. 1f). The κ of ML GaN is 14.93 W m−1 K−1, much lower than that of the bulk (325.41 W m−1 K−1). This low κ is very beneficial for thermoelectricity. For ML GaN, isotopic scattering is relatively less important due to strong phonon–phonon scattering, so the effect on the κ is not so great. In ML GaN, FA phonon molecules contribute the most to phonon transport, similar to graphene and ML BN. Due to the reflection symmetry of the planar honeycomb structure, the phonon scattering process with odd phonon modes is largely suppressed, which leads to the low phonon scattering rate (equivalent to the large phonon lifetime), and finally leads to the dominant role of κ.40

Through the analysis of phonon anharmonicity, it is found that in addition to the main scattering channel, ML GaN also contains narrow scattering channels of the odd FA phonon mode (Fig. 1f), such as FA + LA → FO, because the difference in radius and mass between the Ga and N atoms is not very smooth, which leads to a lower symmetry group. The reduction of symmetry breaks the phonon–phonon scattering selection law and the appearance of additional scattering channels.40 In addition, H. M. Wang et al. analyzed the phonon scattering channels of monolayer hexagonal aluminum nitrate (h-AlN), and calculated the κ of monolayer h-AlN and graphene by an iterative method and RTA method, respectively (Fig. 1g).44 It is found that the trichromatic rate of the FA phonon mode of h-AlN is mainly due to the absorption process. Some other exceptionally narrow scattering channels have been found in h-AlN, similar to ML GaN, involving an odd number of FA phonon modes, such as FA + FO → FO. It can be explained by the phonon–phonon scattering selection rule based on symmetry. Due to the large difference in radius and mass between the Al and N atoms, the planar structure of h-AlN is a little rough, which destroys the reflection symmetry of h-AlN to some extent. Therefore, additional scattering channels are not completely prohibited in h-AlN as the reflection symmetry involves odd numbers of the FA phonon mode. As a result, the FA phonon mode has a larger scattering rate in h-AlN, which is equivalent to a smaller relaxation time, leading to a decrease in its thermal conductivity. At 300 K, the κ of a single layer of h-AlN is 73.43 W m−1 K−1. It is on the same order of magnitude as h-GaN and two orders of magnitude lower than single-layer graphene. The slack formula explains that the greater the mass of an atom, the lower the thermal conductivity. In general, the temperature-dependent behavior of two-dimensional materials, such as graphene, follows the 1/T law.39 But h-AlN is an anomaly. The value calculated by RTA is lower than that obtained by the iterative method. The underestimation of the RTA method is due to the fact that it treats the normal process as thermal resistance when in fact the normal process contributes no thermal resistance. However, the differences in the κ of h-AlN calculated by the two methods are much smaller than those obtained for graphene, suggesting that the Umklapp process plays a dominant role in phonon–phonon scattering in the monolayer h-AlN.

Sanders et al. used the first-principles density functional theory to calculate and compare the band structures of bulk and 2D GaN.45Fig. 1b shows the band structure of single-layer GaN and double-layer GaN. The maximum value of the valence band and the minimum value of the conduction band of the two structures appear in the same position, both of which appear at Γ. After calculation, the band gap of single-layer GaN is 2.95 eV, larger than that of the bulk GaN (1.79 eV), which is caused by the quantum restriction. The band gap of the double-layer GaN is 1.32 eV, which is lower than that of the bulk GaN. This is because the band gap in the double-layer GaN is reduced due to the Stark effect of the strong polarization field. The GW gap of single-layer GaN is 6.32 eV and that of double-layer GaN is 4.00 eV. Since the polarization field of the independent 2D GaN is not shielded by the surrounding vacuum, the gap is more strongly affected by the Stark effect of quantum constraints. In a single-layer structure, the electric field is stronger than the double-layer structure, and the small thickness of the plate inhibits the spatial separation of electrons and holes, so the Stark effect on the gap is very small.

By comparing 2D nitrides of planar and buckled structures, Z. Y. Albalushi et al. found that the stability of the buckled structure was improved relative to the plane due to the reduction of binding energy.29 Surface passivation can affect stability. When the unsaturated state is not properly passivated, a planar structure will be obtained. When the two layers of nitrides of the planar structure come into contact, a super van der Waals bonding will occur and a semi-metallic behavior will happen. The structure in the bent form is most stable when the unsaturated state is properly passivated.46 As can be seen from Fig. 1d, compared with the planar structure, the binding energy of the independent buckled structure is more negative. Therefore, regardless of the number of layers, the independent buckled structure is considered as the preferred configuration of the two-dimensional nitride. The change in atomic structure leads to a significant difference in electronic structure. In 2D GaN, due to quantum constraints, the band gap increases significantly (3.42 eV) relative to the bulk GaN.31 In addition, the planar structure shows an indirect band gap of 4.12 eV, while the buckled structure still retains a direct band gap of 5.28 eV (Fig. 1d). Due to the quantum confinement effect, both of them are larger than the direct Eg of wurtzite bulk GaN (3.42 eV). This has a major impact on optoelectronic devices. The buckled structure is the most thermodynamically stable and feasible candidate material for tunable optoelectronic devices (Fig. 1e).

Cai et al. studied m-plane GaN multilayers,47 which are shown in Fig. 2a. It is found that the N and Ga atoms of GaN on the non-polar m-plane and polar c-plane have different locations. On the c-plane, the N and Ga atoms are in different layers. On the m-plane, GaN forms a nonpolar structure, where the N and Ga atoms are equally arranged in each sublayer of the monolayer, forming a buckled hexagonal structure. This difference can lead to a range of structural and electronic properties. The monolayer GaN structure is optimized into a planar regular hexagonal structure, which is the same as the optimized c-plane ML GaN. The monolayer has a bond length of 1.85 Å and a bond angle of 120°. From the bulk GaN to the monolayer structure, the number of bonds between Ga and N decreases from 4 to 3, sp3 hybridization changes to sp2 hybridization, and the structure changes from tetrahedron to planar hexahedron. However, when the number of layers increases, the structure will also change significantly. As shown in Fig. 2a(iv), the three-layer m-plane GaN is composed of three bending hexagonal molecular layers, which is completely different from the c-plane GaN multilayers stacked with planar hexagonal structures, as well as the 8|4 hybrid c-plane GaN layers alternately composed of an octagon and a square.48–50 The coordination number of the Ga or N atoms in each layer is different, which results in the difference of bond lengths and bond angles, and the deviation of the outer bond angles from the body is more obvious than that of the central layer.

At the same time, they also investigated the band structure of m-plane GaN with different layers, which is shown in Fig. 2b. As can be seen from Fig. 2b, the position of the conduction band minimum (CBM) of m-plane GaN with different layers does not change, but the position of the valence-band maximum (VBM) will move from Y to Γ with the increase in layers. Both lattice constants and band gaps increase with the increase in the number of layers, indicating that the band gaps increase with the increase of interatomic interactions. It can be seen from the electronic structure diagram of orbital projection that the surface PZ orbital of N1 has a great influence on VBM.47

Kolobov et al. compared the charge density difference (CDD) isosurfaces and Mulliken charge between the bulk wurtzite phase of GaN and a 2 ML GaN.50 This is shown in Fig. 2c. It is found that all the Ga–N bonds in bulk GaN are equivalent, but the interlayer covalent bonds are much weaker than the in-plane bonds in 2 ML GaN. This is a consequence of the 2D nature of the slab only, and it is also a factor leading to the instability of the wurtzite phase in a few-layer limit. By simulating the band structure (Fig. 2d and e), it is found that, different from the direct band gap in the Haeckelite phase, the band gap in the planar phase is indirect. The band gap difference is about 0.3 eV, which is less than the thermal energy of the Haeckelite phase at room temperature, allowing the material to efficiently obtain a direct band gap, making it very attractive for electro-optical applications.

Sarma et al. conducted a simulation study on the mechanical properties of monolayer GaN.51 They studied the mechanical behavior of a monolayer of GaN at different temperatures through molecular dynamics simulations. The crack propagation was observed at 700 K and 1300 K, respectively. The transition from brittleness to ductility was confirmed and it was found that the transition was controlled by dislocation migration. Peng et al. also studied the mechanical properties of monolayers of GaN.52 It was found that a GaN monolayer was harder than a BN monolayer, and its Poisson's ratio was 0.43, about 1.9 times that of a BN monolayer. Moreover, the nonlinear elastic deformation of a GaN monolayer is as high as the ultimate strain, and the deformation failure behavior and ultimate strength are anisotropic.

Peng et al. also calculated the mechanical properties of an AlN monolayer.53 It is found that it is stable under different strain directions and loads. An AlN monolayer can withstand the relatively large uniaxial strain and small biaxial strain before fracture, and similar to a GaN monolayer, its second-order elastic constant (including plane stiffness) increases monotonically with the increase of pressure, while the Poisson's ratio decreases monotonically. The Poisson's ratio of AlN monolayers is about 1.6 times that of BN monolayers, which is smaller than that of GaN monolayers.

3. Preparation methods of 2D GaN

3.1. UV-Assisted electroless chemical etching

In 2014, ElAfandy et al. demonstrated the use of chemical stripping to obtain completely threading dislocation-free (TD-free), ultrathin (20–30 nm), free-standing, tension-free single-crystal GaN nanomembranes for the first time.54 The method uses a HF-based electrolyte (CH3OH + H2O2 + HF) and UV-assisted chemical etching of n-GaN. The success of this method mainly depends on the equilibrium state between the Fermi level on the surface of n-GaN and the electrochemical potential of the HF-based electrolyte, so that the surface charge region (SCR) appears at the interface between them.55 This surface charge region can cause the nearby band to bend upward, as shown in Fig. 3a. Incident ultraviolet light excites electron–hole pairs, and chemical etching begins. To reduce the dislocation density, they used a special method to prepare the GaN template. The GaN template used in this method is used to grow two layers of GaN with different masses by controlling the temperature used in the hydride vapor phase epitaxy (HVPE) method. Under different temperatures, the epitaxial GaN will produce different gallium vacancy densities. Under high temperatures, the epitaxial GaN has a higher mass, while the vacancy density of gallium is lower and vice versa. The upper layer of the template is a low-temperature GaN layer, which has a higher gallium vacancy density and a lower etching rate. In contrast, the high-temperature GaN layer under the template has a higher etching rate, and the screw dislocation will terminate at the junction of the two layers, as shown in Fig. 3a(iii). During the etching process, the etching holes are first formed in the LT-GaN region and extend downward. With the deepening of the holes, a considerable amount of UV photons can reach the HT-GaN region and generate e–h pairs on the domain walls to assist the etching of HT-GaN. Due to the low VGa density in the HT-GaN region, the etching rate is accelerated, resulting in lateral etching.56 The existence of these two etching mechanisms at the same time leads to the formation of undersurface cavity structures, or pore domains.57,58 Subsequently, the domain walls are etched away allowing the coalescence of the pore domains, finally forming suspended GaN nanomembrane on the top of a porous GaN layer (Fig. 3b).

3.2. Electrochemical etching

In 2014, Han et al. prepared GaN single crystal nanomembranes by the electrochemical etching (ECE) method and successfully transferred the nanomembranes to other substrates for application.59 To prepare GaN single crystal nanomembranes, multi-layered GaN structures having nanomembrane layers on top of heavily-doped GaN sacrificial layers were grown by the MOCVD process on sapphire. A 10 μm × 10 μm square hole was etched with Cl-based inductively coupled plasma (Cl-ICP) on the multilayer GaN structure layer to expose the heavily-doped GaN sacrificial layer at the bottom. The photoresist was used during the Cl-ICP etching to protect the upper surface of the membrane during subsequent ECE processes. In the process of ECE, the area around each square hole gradually combines with the adjacent area. At this time, the nanomembrane is automatically separated from the substrate, and finally, a GaN single crystal nanomembrane with a thickness of 90 nm is obtained, as shown in Fig. 3e. The nanomembrane was fabricated into a device, as shown in Fig. 3f–h. The performance of the device will be described in detail in Section 5.1.1.

3.3. Nitridation reaction

3.3.1. Ga2O3 template. In 2017, Liu et al. used a template approach to synthesize GaN nanosheets.60 They first prepared ultrathin metastable γ-Ga2O361 at 5–10 nm by a modified hydrothermal method. Then, the in situ GaN nanosheets were prepared by the nitridation reaction of γ-Ga2O3 and ammonia gas. The whole preparation process is shown in Fig. 4a(ii). The selection of metastable γ-Ga2O3 as a template for the indirect synthesis of GaN nanosheets is inspired by the various nanostructures obtained by the template synthesis method.62,63 Metastable γ-Ga2O3 has a cubic structure, and the lattice constants of the (220) plane are close to the values in the (100) plane of wurtzite-type hexagonal GaN (a = 0.318 nm, c = 0.518 nm), as shown in Fig. 4a(i) and (iii). γ-Ga2O3's unstable structure, symmetry, and close lattice constants enable it to be easily converted into a hexagonal structure during an appropriate nitridation process. In addition, the γ-Ga2O3 framework can be well maintained in the form of nanosheets, which plays an important role in obtaining layered GaN nanostructures, as shown in Fig. 4a(iv). After XRD characterization before and after nitriding, it can be determined that the γ-Ga2O3 phase can be completely transformed into a GaN phase, and pure GaN nanosheets can be obtained. The GaN nanosheets obtained are shown in Fig. 4b. In the synthesized GaN nanosheets, the atoms are still arranged in a stacking sequence of ABAB when it is observed along the [0001] orientation, and the distance of adjacent lattice fringes (0.28 nm) is in good agreement with the (100) plane spacing of wurtzite-type GaN. They also studied the optical emission and band gap of the obtained GaN nanosheets, as shown in Fig. 4b. When γ-Ga2O3 is transformed into GaN nanosheets, the absorption edge shifts to about 370 nm, and the absorption tail extends to 500 nm. The calculated band gap is 3.30 eV, which is much smaller than theoretical predictions. They speculate that it is due to oxygen impurities and dislocations in the GaN host lattice.

Recently, Li et al. proposed a polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) printing strategy, taking advantage of the fact that the Ga metal is a liquid at room temperature and its surface can be passivated/oxidized very quickly in air,64 to obtain a large-sized, highly transferable 2D Ga2O3 thin layer. Moreover, the obtained 2D Ga2O3 layer can be used as an ideal template to transform into a 2D GaN nanomembrane by the nitriding reaction.65

Fig. 4c shows the preparation process of a 2D Ga2O3 amorphous layer. First, the Ga metal is heated to melt and then transferred to the tape using a plastic dropper. The liquid Ga on the tape is controllably brushed onto the PDMS substrate. At this point, the liquid Ga is exposed to the atmosphere and can easily form an extremely thin oxide skin on the surface. The adsorption of oxygen on the PDMS substrate also accelerates the oxidation process, which leads to continuous deposition of the 2D Ga2O3 layer. Then, taking advantage of the strong van der Waals force and the liquidity of Ga, a large area and transparent 2D Ga2O3 layer can be directly obtained by wiping. Then, the PDMS substrate is directly inverted on the SiO2 or Si substrate, and the PDMS substrate is removed by heating to complete the 2D Ga2O3 transfer. The 2D Ga2O3 amorphous layer can be converted into 2D Ga2O3 crystals after heat treatment at 800 °C for 30 minutes in air (Fig. 4d).

In addition, by nitriding the 2D Ga2O3 amorphous layer template in a horizontal tubular furnace, 2D GaN crystals can be obtained. The reaction chamber is first evacuated to eliminate the effects of oxygen. Then, the reaction chamber was heated to 800 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1, and NH3 with a flow rate of 50 sccm was added as the reaction gas. The nitriding reaction could be completed after 10 minutes of continuous reaction. Despite the reaction temperature being as high as 800 °C, there is no damage or breakage on the 2D GaN surface (Fig. 4e). Compared with 2D Ga2O3, its thickness is reduced by half due to the partial decomposition of Ga2O3 and the arrangement of the Ga and N atoms. The 2D GaN crystal obtained by the reaction has a regular and clear lattice fringe with a hexagonal configuration. The measured interplanar spacing and included angle between the neighboring planes are 0.28 nm and 60°, respectively.

3.3.2. Liquid metal gallium. In 2018, Chen et al. achieved the growth of 2D GaN single crystals by a surface-confined nitridation reaction via CVD.30 They synthesized 2D GaN on liquid metal gallium, which is the easiest material to synthesize 2D GaN, and used urea as the nitrogen source for the synthesis.66–68 The method to successfully obtain 2D GaN is mainly due to the design of a surface tension-induced stratified structure (Ga/Ga-W), using tungsten foil as the substrate, so that the surface-constrained nitridation reaction occurs during the chemical deposition process. The preparation process is roughly as follows: first, the used gallium metal particles, tungsten foil and urea are pretreated to a certain extent, so that the metal gallium particles are decomposed into small droplets in hot ethanol and then frozen into very small gallium spheres. Then, the pretreatment urea was placed in the upstream area (zone1) of the reactor (Fig. 5a) as the precursor, and the gallium spheres were placed on the tungsten foil far away from the central zone2 of the quartz tube furnace. After heating, the gallium droplets would diffuse on the tungsten metal surface to form a Ga/Ga-W structure after reaching a certain temperature. At this point, the quartz tube is moved so that the Ga-W substrate is moved to the central region (zone2). After the surface-confined nitridation reaction, the 2D GaN is formed on the surface of liquid gallium.

3.4. Graphene assisted growth

The following methods for preparing 2D GaN all involve graphene. In 2010, Chung et al. explored the graphene growth of a GaN thin nanomembrane microstructure for the first time.70 They used high density vertical arrangement of ZnO nanowalls as an intermediate layer to heteroepitaxial transferable GaN thin films on the graphene. Although the film has not reached a nanometer level, this method has laid the foundation for the later use of graphene to prepare 2D GaN.

In 2016, Robinson et al. demonstrated the synthesis of 2D GaN using epitaxial graphene via a migration-enhanced encapsulated growth technique.29 The method was performed in a customized vertical two-flow showerhead cold-wall reactor. They performed in situ hydrogenation on epitaxial graphene, formed by the sublimation of silicon on the surface of SiC, to convert the graphene to quasi-freestanding epitaxial graphene (QFEG).71 The effect of hydrogenation is to passivate the suspended bond between the graphene buffer and the SiC, leaving the graphene as a separate layer,72 creating a lower energy original interface. After 60 cycles of trimethylgallium, the intercalation layer of gallium is formed between QFEG and SiC. After the intercalation layer is formed, it is heated and kept warm, and then ammoniated with ultra-high purity ammonia water. The whole synthesis process, the HAADF-STEM cross-section and the elemental EDX mapping after synthesis are shown in Fig. 7a and b. After cooling, the 2D GaN is obtained (Fig. 7c). During the synthesis process, point defects and wrinkles in graphene act as penetration sites, which facilitate the insertion of Ga atoms (Fig. 7c). Ga atoms accumulate at the defects of graphene, preferably forming GaN nuclei, which provide enough energy for insertion. Therefore, the synthesized 2D GaN tends to be distributed near the graphene wrinkles.

In 2019, Li et al. used a plasma-enhanced metal–organic chemical vapor deposition (PEMOCVD) method to achieve the epitaxial growth of 2D GaN with a well-controlled lattice structure and band gap on the graphene/Si heterogeneous substrate by controlling the energy and growth temperature of plasma during the reaction.73 This method begins with the preparation of a graphene/Si substrate. They used the CVD method to grow out of graphene on a Cu foil. The graphene–Cu foil is then placed in the FeCl3 copper etchant solution to dissolve the Cu foil. At this time, the graphene remains on the surface of the solution due to surface tension, and can be easily transferred to the 2 inch Si substrate to form the graphene/Si heterostructure. The heterostructures are then annealed in the electron cyclotron resonance PEMOCVD growth chamber, where hydrogen was used under certain conditions to break the C–C bonds in graphene and passivates the dangling bonds between the graphene and Si substrate.29,71 Trimethylgallium (TMGa) and NH3 assisted with plasma are used as precursors of Ga and N, respectively. Ga atoms are deposited directly onto graphene/Si heterostructures by TMGa flows, and N atoms are deposited by NH3 flows. The plasma is generated through electron cyclotron resonance using high-purity nitrogen. The three processes – hydrogenation, intercalation and ammonolysis – are repeated for 10 cycles. It can be seen from Fig. 7f that the lattice structure of 2D GaN obtained is P63MC. Ga and N atoms were also found in the graphene/Si heterostructure (Fig. 7f).

Feng et al. grew single-crystalline GaN thin films on Si by using graphene and SiO2 as interlayers.74 The epitaxial process is shown in Fig. 7g. Unlike the method used by Li et al., they inserted a SiO2 layer between the graphene and Si to further screen the asymmetric surface domains of the substrate. They first transferred the graphene onto a SiO2/Si substrate and then loaded the substrate into a MOCVD system. Nitriding is carried out under NH3 flow. Subsequently, by using trimethylaluminum and ammonia as precursors of aluminum and nitrogen, an AlN nucleation layer with a thickness of about 10 nm was grown to promote the transverse growth of the GaN layer, and then trimethylgallium was used as precursors of gallium to grow GaN thin films. The XRD image of GaN films shows that only the peak corresponding to the wurtzite c-axis GaN is observed (Fig. 7h), indicating that epitaxial GaN obtained by this method has good orientation. In the AFM scanning region of 3 × 3 μm2 (Fig. 7h), the GaN thin film shows a step-flow surface morphology and the root mean square (RMS) roughness is only 0.46 nm, which shows that the epitaxial GaN thin films obtained by this method have good morphology characteristics.

Recently, Yu et al. successfully grew epitaxial GaN films on graphene/SiC substrates by MOCVD.75 They prepared graphene directly on a SiC substrate by thermal decomposition. First, a 4H-SiC substrate is annealed in an Ar atmosphere for a period of time to remove scratches and oxygen on the surface. Then, the surface graphitization is directly realized by heating to 1600–1700 °C for about 2 to 5 hours in an ultra-pure Ar atmosphere. Then, the graphene surface is pretreated with nitrogen-plasma, and the C–N-related dangling bonds are introduced to promote the growth of GaN films (Fig. 7i). The epitaxial growth of GaN films is carried out with TMGa, TMAl and NH3 as precursors of Ga, Al and Ns, respectively, and H2 and N2 as carrier gases. A 40 nm thick low temperature AlN buffer layer (780 °C) is first grown on graphene, and then a 160 nm thick high temperature AlN buffer layer (1080 °C) is grown. Finally, a 2 μm thick undoped GaN layer is grown at 1050 °C. If the graphene is not pretreated by nitrogen-plasma during this process, the lack of dangling bonds on the surface of the graphene is unfavorable to GaN nucleation, resulting in the failure of GaN to form a continuous film. After pretreatment, dangling bonds can be formed on the surface of graphene, which enhances the nucleation ability and forms continuous films (Fig. 7j). They also further grow InGaN/GaN multiple quantum wells (MQWs) on GaN layers. The PL spectra (Fig. 7k) at a low temperature of 10 K showed that the emission wavelength of MQWs is redshifted by 21 nm due to the insertion of graphene, which increased the content of In.

Sun et al. took advantage of the high hydrophilicity of graphene oxide (GO) and the strong affinity of oxygen-containing functional groups to inorganic ions76 to guide the preparation of 2D GaN using GO sheets as templates.77 They first obtained a suspension of GO using a modified Hummer process, and then dissolved the metal Ga into hot sulfuric acid to obtain a solution of Ga2(SO4)3. Then, the GO suspension was stirred and mixed with the Ga2(SO4)3 solution, and after a certain period of time, ammonia (NH3·H2O) was added to react to form gallium hydroxide (GaOOH), and then the composite material (GaOOH/GO) was frozen. Finally, the composites are heated in an ammonia (NH3) atmosphere to form 2D GaN through the nitriding reaction. The whole process is shown in Fig. 8a. As can be seen from Fig. 8b, the obtained GaN nanosheets present a hexagonal shape with a horizontal size of about 1–5 μm. The (100) lattice plane spacing of 2.76 Å also reveals its hexagonal structure (Fig. 8c). Compared with GaN powders, the X-ray diffraction peaks of GaN NSs are puny and broad (Fig. 8e), which is the manifestation of its ultra-thin characteristics, as shown by the AFM image (Fig. 8d) with a thickness of 14.5 nm. Fig. 8f shows the long-term cycling performance of the material as an electrode material. During the cycle, the Coulombic efficiency increases rapidly. And after a certain number of cycles, the specific capacity increases significantly. After the initial cycle stability, it still exhibits high electrochemical performance.

4.1. Chemical vapor deposition (CVD)

In 2007, Zhang et al. synthesized AlN nanosheets by CVD using a Si substrate.78 The gold-plated Si substrate is placed over a quartz crucible containing pure aluminum powder with the gold face facing the crucible. The XRD spectra (Fig. 9b) show that all diffraction peaks are in line with the wurtzite AlN structure, and the (110) diffraction peak is obviously the strongest, indicating that the synthesized nanosheets have a preferred orientation.

4.2. Metal–organic vapor phase epitaxy (MOVPE)

In 2009, Mei et al. successfully prepared ultra-thin porous AlN/GaN crystal nanomembranes on a Si substrate by a selective silicon etching method, which can self-assemble into various geometric shapes.80 They first etched a Si substrate using a chemical etching process to remove the oxide layer and produce a hydrogen-terminated surface. The treated Si substrate was then exposed to trimethylaluminium (TMAl) for a period of time. At a certain temperature, the AlN layer was grown with a mixture of TMAl and ammonia by the MOVPE method, and then the GaN layer was deposited with TMGa and ammonia. The sample is then subjected to photolithography and SiCl4 gas reactive ion etching to form different patterns on its surface.81 In a certain proportion of HNO3 and HF mixture, the Si sacrificial layer under the AlN/GaN layer is etched away. Finally, a supercritical point dryer was used to avoid the nanomembrane structure collapsing due to the surface tension of the aqueous etchants in order to obtain a complete nanomembrane.

A series of AlN/GaN nanobranes (Fig. 9c) with different shapes were obtained by using this method. In the CL of 4.2 K (Fig. 9d), it is found that the bandgap related emission of GaN occurs in the un-etched area at ∼350 nm (∼3.54 eV). At 500 nm, a relatively wide visible emission band appears due to the defects in nanomembranes produced during etching. Compared with the unirradiated bandgap emission, the bandgap emission is obviously enhanced when the electron is irradiated. They explained that this phenomenon is caused by the desorption of water adsorbed by the ultra-thin porous nanobranes during the wet-chemical etching process, which improves the GaN bandgap CL emission.

4.3. Molecular beam epitaxy (MBE)

In 2013, Tsipas et al. first reported the epitaxial growth of ultra-thin AlN nanomembranes on single crystal Ag(111) substrates, and studied the structure and physical properties of the prepared 2D AlN.82 They used plasma-assisted MBE to achieve the preparation of 2D AlN. The selected substrate is the cleaned Ag substrate. The cleaning method is to sputter the surface of the Ag substrate with a 1.5 keV Ar ion source under a vacuum. In order to eliminate the damage caused by sputtering, the Ag substrate is annealed at 500 °C for a period of time. The sputter-annealing process is repeated several times to ensure that the surface of the Ag substrate is free of C and O contamination. After the exposure of reactive atomic nitrogen beams from a remote radio frequency plasma source, 2D AlN nanomembranes were deposited by evaporating the Al metal from a cold lip effusion cell using a pyrolytic boron nitride crucible.

In 2019, Borisenko et al. also successfully grew 2D AlN using this method. They chose a graphene/SiO2/Si heterosubstrate for growth.83 First, the required graphene layer was obtained by chemical vapor deposition. Before depositing the graphene layer by chemical vapor deposition, the Cu foil substrate required for deposition is electrochemically polished in phosphoric acid, and then the polished Cu foil is annealed in a chemical vapor deposition equipment for a period of time. Then, using n-decane (C10H22) as the carbon source,84n-decane is transported to a chemical vapor deposition device through a bubbler system to complete the preparation of multi-layer graphene films. At this point, the graphene films are grown on the Cu foil substrate. In order to successfully transfer the graphene films to the amorphous substrate without damaging the crystallinity, they used the wet-chemical room temperature transfer method to transfer the graphene films to the SiO2/Si substrate to form the graphene/SiO2/Si heterostructure. The substrate required for growth is prepared and annealed in a vacuum for a period of time. Then, a 20–30 nm thick AlN layer is grown by PA-MBE in a nitrogen-rich mode. Although this method has been successfully used to grow an AlN nanocrystalline layer, it has not been stripped and applied.

Wang et al. prepared atomically thin hexagonal AlN nanotubes with controllable wall thickness by epitaxial casting and selective thermal evaporating GaN/AlN core/shell nanowires.85 The first step is the epitaxial casting process. GaN nanowires (NWs) and AlN shells are self-assembled on cleaned and nitriding Si substrates by SVT Associates (SVTA) plasma-assisted MBE. First, the growth of GaN NWs is interrupted after growing at 800 °C for a period of time under N-rich conditions. AlN is then deposited at the same temperature, and the thickness of the AlN shell is controlled by the growth time. The second step is selective thermal evaporation. First, GaN/AlN core/shell NWs are stripped in an isopropyl alcohol solution by ultrasound. The mixture is then dropped on the hydrophilic Si substrate treated by oxygen plasma, dried naturally, and baked at 100 °C for 10 min. Then, it is loaded into a UHV-MBE chamber and thermal evaporation is carried out between the decomposition temperature of GaN and AlN. The entire process is shown in Fig. 10a.

4.5. Metal–organic chemical vapor deposition (MOCVD)

In 2018, Wang et al. successfully achieved the epitaxial growth of 2D AlN layers between graphene and silicon substrates using MOCVD.89 The Si substrate is chosen because of the high symmetry of the Si surface,90 which can stably adsorb Al and N atoms. In addition, the lattice mismatch between Si and AlN is about 19%,91 which can generate tensile stress to drive the transformation of 2D AlN from the R3m structure to the P63MC structure.92–94 The whole deposition process is shown in Fig. 12a. The first step is the preparation and transfer of graphene, to prepare a graphene/Si heterostructure. They adopt the plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition method to synthesize a few layers of graphene on copper foil, then spin-coated a polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) layer on the graphene layer, and then the Cu foil-carrying graphene is placed in a certain proportion of the mixture solution of CuSO4, HCl and H2O and deionized water for a period of time to dissolve the Cu foil substrate. Then, the remaining graphene layer coated with PMMA is transferred to the Si substrate, and then the PMMA coating on the surface of the graphene layer is removed with acetone. After cleaning, the heterostructure of graphene/Si is prepared, which is used for the subsequent epitaxial growth of 2D AlN. After that, the graphene/Si heterostructure is placed into the MOCVD growth chamber and annealed for a period of time, and then the epitaxial growth of 2D AlN was performed using TMAl and NH3 as precursors of Al and N, respectively. As the 2D AlN layer grows between the graphene and Si substrate, the flow of H2 opens the C–C bonds of the graphene (Fig. 12b-iii). After that, the Al and N atoms in the precursor are deposited on the graphene/Si heterostructure (Fig. 12biv–vi). This process is repeated many times, and finally, a 2D AlN sandwich structure is formed between the graphene layer and the Si substrate (Fig. 12c). Finally, the graphene/2D AlN/Si substrate structure is cleaned with ultrasonic deionized water, and the topmost graphene layers are washed off, exposing the 2D AlN layer (Fig. 12d). The removal of graphene can be confirmed by Raman spectra (Fig. 12e).

In the presence of graphene as a buffer layer, AlN will undergo quasi-van der Waals epitaxial growth on graphene. At this point, the effect of lattice mismatch between AlN and graphene is no longer significant. In addition, the low migration barrier of metal on graphene leads to the easy diffusion of adsorbed atoms, which promotes 2D lateral growth.95 Therefore, using graphene as a buffer layer to grow 2D AlN is an ideal method. However, there are no dangling bonds on the surface of graphene, which can inhibit the nucleation of AlN, limiting the growth of large-area single crystals.

Chen et al. treated graphene with N2 plasma, solved the problem of AlN nucleation on graphene by introducing pyrrolic nitrogen doping, and successfully grew large-area AlN thin films.96 They first grow graphene on sapphire via the CVD method and then treat the graphene/sapphire substrate with N2 plasma for 30 seconds. Finally, high-temperature AlN MOCVD growth with TMAl as a precursor is carried out at 1200 °C. The process is shown in Fig. 13a. The surface of the AlN film is flat and shows an atomic platform in the AFM image (Fig. 13b).95 In addition, a good epitaxial relationship is maintained between AlN and sapphire, and the strain between them is largely relaxed due to the presence of graphene (Fig. 13c). By calculating the internal stress of AlN, it was also found that the internal stress of AlN decreased from 16.0 GPa of AlN/sapphire to 14.9 GPa of AlN/graphene/sapphire (Fig. 13d).95 Therefore, the graphene layer improves the quality of AlN growth by weakening the AlN/sapphire interaction. High-quality AlN is used in the manufacturing of DUV-LED (Fig. 13e), as detailed in Section 6.2.

5. Applications of 2D GaN

As one of the representatives of new-generation wide band gap semiconductor materials, GaN has excellent properties such as high carrier concentration, high breakdown field strength and corrosion resistance.99–101 In combination with the special tetrahedral coordination structure of wurtzite GaN, GaN has been used in many optoelectronic applications.102 As for GaN, the change of dimension will lead to the change of its structure and electrons, so that GaN has more different properties and can be used in more fields.102 With the emergence of 2D materials, the atomic configuration and electronic characteristics of 2D GaN have changed greatly, and a lot of research has been conducted on this. The lattice structure of ML GaN transformed into a graphene-like configuration which leads to the increase of its energy gap in the deep-ultraviolet range.29 Moreover, the ML 2D GaN can emit light in the deep ultraviolet range, which makes it promising in the fields of sterilization and water purification.45 The carrier migration in 2D GaN ML is highly anisotropic, which makes it have potential applications in high performance electronic devices.103 The thermal conductivity of ML GaN with a planar honeycomb structure is more than two orders of magnitude lower than that of graphene, which makes it have a broad application prospect in the energy conversion fields such as thermoelectricity.40 In addition, due to the difference in electronegativity between the doping elements and 2D GaN, the atom structure and charge distribution closest to doping elements in 2D GaN will change significantly, and the doping elements can adjust the absorption coefficient and reflectivity of 2D GaN. The discovery of these properties provides a reference for the application of p-type 2D GaN nano-optoelectronic devices in UV.104 The brittleness of 3D GaN limits its application in flexible devices. Compared with 3D GaN, 2D GaN-based flexible devices show stronger mechanical properties105 and can be used in high-performance and strainable RF devices.106 Many studies on the performance of 2D GaN show that 2D GaN has more interesting performance compared with bulk GaN, which can make up for some shortcomings of bulk GaN and make GaN applied in a wider range of fields. The following is an introduction to some of the devices integrated using 2D GaN.

5.1. Electronic devices

2D GaN has been intensively studied for many years since they have extensive applications in high-efficiency electronic and optoelectronic equipment such as FET, HEMT, LED, detector, and so on by theory and experiment, which will be potential channel materials for next-generation electronic and optoelectronic devices.
5.1.1. Field effect transistors (FETs). In 2014, Han et al. prepared a GaN nanomembrane (NM) by electrochemical etching and successfully transferred the NM to SiO2/n-Si(001) substrate to prepare GaN NM FET as shown in Fig. 3f.59 The Ga-polar surface of the GaN NM on the surface of SiO2 faces downward. After testing, as shown in Fig. 3g and h, the device exhibits typical current saturation when the knee voltage is about 5 V, and the upmost one is biased at 50 V. According to Fig. 3h, the maximum gm is 0.55 mS mm−1 (0.167 mA V−1) and the maximum drain current density is 12 mA mm−1. By calculation, the on/off ratio is about 105, resulting in a maximum mobility of 154 cm2 V−1 s−1. The threshold voltage variation caused by the memory effect of the gate is also observed during the test, and the same effect has been found in GaN nanowire FETs.107,108 This phenomenon may be related to the traps in SiO2 or at the SiO2/Si or GaN NM/SiO2 interfaces. These traps can form the effect of dynamic discharge, and higher saturation current can be observed at higher test speeds. Although the device is only in the initial stage of fabrication, the device exhibits the same performance as traditional GaN/sapphire structure devices,109 and shows better performance than the other inorganic flexible thin film transistors.110 Therefore, the application of 2D GaN in FETs still has great development potential.

In 2018, Chen et al. also constructed 2D GaN FET devices,30 using mechanically exfoliated graphene films as the contact layer in order to achieve better Ohmic contact (Fig. 5ei and ii).111 The IdS–VG characteristic curve with VDS = 1 V and the gate-dependent IdS–Vds characteristic curves were obtained, as shown in Fig. 5e(iii). Finally, the mobility of 160 cm2 V−1 S−1 and the on/off ratio of about 106 were calculated.

Over the last decade, GaN devices have been fundamental and essential elements to meet these requirements for high-power amplification of the radiofrequency (RF) signal in MHz to GHz frequencies and beyond, especially for military applications. Flexible GaN thin films can enable future strainable and conformal devices for transmission of RF signals over large distances for more efficient wireless communication. In 2017, Glavin et al.106 demonstrated strainable high-frequency RF GaN devices first time, with epitaxial growth on 2D boron nitride for chemical-free transfer to a soft, flexible substrate (Fig. 14a and b). The influence of strain on the RF performance of flexible GaN HEMT devices is evaluated, demonstrating cutoff frequencies and maximum oscillation frequencies greater than 42 and 74 GHz, respectively, at up to 0.43% strain, as shown in Fig. 14c and d. Flexible, high-performance GaN devices represent a significant advancement toward conformal, highly integrated electronic materials for RF applications.

The Cu-GaN monolayer was proposed as a promising candidate for CO and HCHO detection around dry-type transformers to evaluate the operation status of the power system.

5.1.5. Piezoelectric devices. GaN is a promising piezoelectric material due to its high mechanical robustness, chemical stability, environmental compatibility, and easy doping control. 5.2. Energy storage and conversion

5.2.1. Li- and Na–ion batteries. In the preparation of Li- or Na–ion batteries, one of the biggest challenges is to find suitable anode materials. Zhang et al. believed that 2D GaN is a good candidate material.132 As the 2D GaN layer exhibits stable Li/Na adsorption and low theoretical voltage, it can also achieve high storage capacity and extremely fast Li/Na diffusion. The first-principles calculation of 2D GaN ML was carried out to study its feasibility as an electrode material. Through the calculation of three migration paths (Fig. 19a), they found that the diffusion barriers of Li and Na ions in the case of 2D GaN as an anode material are much lower than that of other 2D anode materials. The diffusion barrier of Li is 79 meV, and the diffusion barrier of Na is only 22 meV, as shown in Fig. 19b. These data indicated that there is an extremely fast ion diffusion velocity on the 2D GaN layer. In addition, in their study on Li/Na storage capacity on 2D GaN, through the layer-by-layer calculation of adsorption energy, the average open circuit voltage (OCV) of Li is 0.060 V and that of Na is 0.050 V. The maximum storage capacity of Li and Na is 938 mA h g−1 and 625 mA h g−1, respectively (Fig. 19c and d). At the same time, it is also found that when the 2D GaN is adsorbed by Li and Na atoms, it will show metallization, which ensures the good electrical conductivity as an electrode. These results indicated that 2D GaN is an excellent electrode material with fast ion diffusion and high storage capacity, and will have great potential in the field of battery development.

5.2.3. Catalytic performance. In technological applications, the reaction requires an appropriate catalytic material. The catalysis can be either homogeneous or heterogeneous. In the latter case, the material is usually a nanoparticulate semiconductor with a band gap suitable for absorption of photons in the visible range of the solar spectrum. Furthermore, the conduction band (CB) or valence band (VB) edges of the catalyst should be aligned appropriately for driving the hydrogen or oxygen evolution reactions (HER or OER) and nitrogen reduction reaction (NNR), respectively.134 These reactions play a crucial role in the implementation of electrochemical energy conversion processes, including fuel cells, water splitting and ammonia synthesis. As a wide direct-gap semiconductor, GaN has emerged as a new-generation material for solar water splitting due to its unique structural, electrical, and optical properties.135–137 To date, a variety of 2D nanomaterials have been identified as promising candidates for photocatalytic or electrocatalytic applications, in association with their novel physical and chemical properties, including robust mechanical properties, high specific surface areas, and abundant active sites.138

In 2020, Li et al.139 systematically examined the prospect of using a single transition-metal (TM)-atom-doped g-GaN monolayer as an electrocatalyst for artificial nitrogen reduction via density functional theory calculations and the computational hydrogen electrode model. The Mo-doped g-GaN (Mo@g-GaN) monolayer is the only electrocatalyst predicted to be feasible for the NRR with a low onset potential of −0.33 V, as shown in Fig. 21a. Purwiandono et al. prepared a GaN paste electrode from 2D hexagonal-shape GaN (HS-GaN) synthesized by a reaction in a molten salt using Ti plate as a substrate, and compared the photo-electro-chemical properties of the electrode with random-shaped GaN electrodes.69 They found that 2D HS-GaN generated higher photocurrent due to its more efficient water oxidation, which was about twice as high as that of random-shaped GaN, and the donor density of the 2D HS-GaN sample is one order of magnitude higher than that of the random-shaped. In 2021, Jing et al.138 systemically investigated the electrocatalytic properties of TM-atom-doped g-GaN monolayers by means of density functional theory. The Ni/g-GaN exhibited the best catalytic performance for both the OER and ORR with small overpotentials (0.26 and 0.38 V, respectively), as shown in Fig. 21b. And the TM atom d-band centers were found to linearly correlate with Gibbs free energies of HO* and HOO* species. A.V. Akimov et al.134 presented the first time-domain theoretical study of the initial steps of photocatalytic water splitting on the GaN surface, which shows that the hole loses its excess energy within 100 fs and localizes primarily on the nitrogen atoms of the GaN surface, initiating a sequence of proton-transfer events from the surface N–H group to the nearby OH groups and bulk water molecules. They constructed a comprehensive kinetic model of the processes taking place during the initial stages of the photoinduced water oxidation reaction on the GaN (10–10) surface (Fig. 21c). The behavior of photoexcited carriers in a GaN photoanode was investigated by using an in situ PL measurement system in an electrolyte (Fig. 21d).140 The near-band-edge (NBE) emission was observed in the electrolyte at around the flat-band potential of −0.47 VRHE. Only the relative PL intensity of 565 nm, the YL peak, increased above the significant onset, indicating that the deep levels were partially filled with holes, and the partial occupation of the midgap states is the key to efficient utilization of photoexcited carriers for the water splitting reaction.

The overdependence of modern society on fossil fuels leads to huge CO2 emissions, which induces adverse climate changes due to its greenhouse effects. Provided the sustainable H2 sources from renewable energies, such as wind or solar, a viable technology by hydrogenating CO2 to hydrocarbons (HCs, e.g., CH4, C2–C4 olefins, and gasoline) and oxygenates (methanol, ethanol, acetic acid, dimethyl ether, etc.) can sustainably convert renewable resources to chemicals and fuels. Thus, considerable efforts have been made for this subject in recent years, and significant advances in the hydrogenation of CO2 to CO, HCs, and oxygenates have been achieved.141 The plasmon-driven photoelectrochemical CO2 reduction was investigated with Au/p-GaN photocathodes, as shown in Fig. 22a. The Au/p-GaN device improved the selectivity of CO2 reduction to CO with a significant reduction in H2 evolution relative to the bare p-GaN surface.142 Zhou et al. fabricated a unique GaN:Sn nanoarchitecture directly on planar silicon for the artificial photosynthesis of formic acid from CO2 using H2O as the only reductant.143 The formic acid was produced at an underpotential of 220 mV (+0.02 V vs. RHE) and an astonishing turnover frequency (TOF) of 107 min−1 was obtained at a low potential of −0.53 V with a 7.5 mA cm−2 current density and high faradaic efficiency of 76.9% under standard one-sun illumination, corresponding to an appreciable productivity of 201 mmol cm−2 h−1. In 2021, Liu et al.144 demonstrated that the wurtzite-structure GaN is active, stable, and selective for the direct hydrogenation of CO2 to dimethyl ether (DME). The transient, operando DRIFTS, and DFT results rigorously revealed that DME is formed as the primary product via the coupling of CH3* and HCOO* (Fig. 22b). Li et al. discovered the thermal catalytic activity of GaN in nonoxidative alkane dehydroaromatization for the first time, as shown in Fig. 22c. GaN powders with a wurtzite crystal structure showed superior stability and reactivity for converting light alkanes, including methane, propane, n-butane, n-hexane and cyclohexane, into benzene at an elevated temperature with high selectivity.145 These results indicated that 2D GaN will have great application prospects in the field of catalysis.

6. Applications of 2D AlN

As one of the representatives of ultra-wide bandgap semiconductor materials, AlN has many excellent properties, such as stable performance at high temperatures, low energy loss, good ultraviolet light transmittance, etc., so it has received extensive attention from the world. AlN bulk crystals have broad application prospects in the fields of high-efficiency optoelectronic devices, high-power and high-frequency electronic devices, ultra-high voltage power electronic devices, deep ultraviolet warning and deep UV-LED disinfection lamps.146 In recent years, the structural stability and electronic properties of 2D AlN have aroused great interest and become a research hotspot. In 2D AlN, the chemical bond between the Al atom and N atom is formed by the bonding of Al-Pz and N-Pz orbitals.147 The difference in electronegativity between the Al atoms and N atoms causes electrons to be transferred from Al to N, which leads to some ionic bonds in 2D AlN that are not pure covalent bonds.148 This charge transfer from Al to N gives 2D AlN some unique properties. For example, the Al atoms and N atoms in the 2D AlN are four-coordination and three-fold-coordination with the dangling bond, which are the active sites of gas adsorption and are very helpful for the adsorption of gas molecules.149,150 Therefore, 2D AlN has potential applications in sensors. These characteristics of 2D AlN make it have application potential in more fields. The following is the introduction of 2D AlN application.

6.1. Li- and Na-ion batteries

In 2018, Sengupta used density functional theory to simulate the lithium and sodiation properties of 2D AlN sheets.151 After calculation, the maximum storage capacity of Li and Na adsorbed by 2D AlN is 500.8 mA h g−1 and 385.3 mA h g−1. In most cases, Li atoms have stronger adsorption on 2D AlN (Fig. 23a). The maximum OCV is 1.49 V and 1.86 V, the average is 0.78 V and 0.94 V (Fig. 23b), and the minimum diffusion potential energy for different paths is 0.40 eV and 0.15 eV (Fig. 23c), respectively. These results indicate that 2D AlN can be used as an anode material in Li- or Na-ion batteries, and has a great potential in the application of supercapacitors and ion–air batteries.

In the process of research, Sengupta also compared the transmission spectra of 2D AlN with and without Li/Na adsorption (Fig. 23d–f), and found that the addition of Li/Na improves the Fermi level of 2D AlN, and the adsorption change of Fermi energy will result in a significant change in the transmission spectrum. Therefore, the change in the transmission spectrum can be used to distinguish whether 2D AlN adsorbs Li or Na atoms. The electrical sensitivity and selectivity of 2D AlN make it useful for chemical/biosensor applications.

6.2. Deep ultraviolet light-emitting diodes (DUV-LEDs)

Chen et al. grew a DUV-LED structure directly on a graphene/sapphire substrate with a continuous smooth surface.96 Through characterization, it is found that the DUV-LED exhibits good rectifier performance. The optical output power (LOP) of LED on the graphene/sapphire substrate is linear to the input voltage with a slope of about 20 μW mA−1 (Fig. 13f). Moreover, due to stress relaxation, the peak position of the DUV-LED did not change (Fig. 13g), further confirming the role of graphene. In addition, the DUV-LED also shows a low turn-on voltage, high output power and excellent reliability.

In 2020, Chang et al. also prepared a 272 nm DUV-LED using a high-quality 2D AlN nanomembrane with low strain and low dislocation density grown on graphene, and carried out a series of EL and FDTD simulation characterization studies of the device.97 It is found that the output power of the LED is increased by about 22% compared with that of the LED prepared by the AlN buffer substrate, and the wavelength shift of the LED under large current is very small, which can be ignored. This work has greatly improved the performance of DUV-LEDs and confirmed the application potential of 2D AlN in DUV-LEDs.

6.3. Field effect transistors (FETs)

Yang et al. successfully grew DPA crystals on AlN, and used the heterostructure to prepare transistors (Fig. 11g).86 The smooth surface of the crystal enables significant, atomically sharp interfaces between the Au/AlN/DPA/Au stack layers (Fig. 11g). The device has a saturation mobility of 6.8 cm2 V−1 s−1, a high on/off current ratio of 108, and a low operating voltage of −5 V. The subthreshold slope (SS) is 104 mV dec−1 (Fig. 11h). It is worth mentioning that this result is one of the best performances of organic field-effect transistors (OFETs) based on the high-k dielectric layer. Then, they quantitatively calculated the defect state density of the semiconductor/medium interface with the subthreshold slope, which was about 2.3 × 1011 cm−2 eV−1. This defect state density is relatively small, reflecting the formation of a high-quality interface between the DPA and AlN crystal. In general, the transistor system exhibits relatively high performance. The combination of DPA and AlN improves the performance of the entire transistor.

6.4. Other applications

Based on first-principles calculations, Zhang studied and compared some properties of fully hydrogenated and semi-hydrogenated AlN nanosheets.152 The results show that the fully hydrogenated single bilayer AlN nanotube is an indirect bandgap semiconductor, while the thicker bilayer AlN nanotube is a ferromagnetic metal. This work reveals that the electronic and magnetic properties of 2D AlN can be controlled by chemical functionalization, enabling applications in the fields of electronics and spintronics.

Wang et al. studied gas adsorption on 2D AlN.153 It is found that in 2D AlN, the Al–N bond is polarized due to the charge transfer from the Al atom to the N atom. Therefore, 2D AlN can adsorb gas molecules through electrostatic and polarization interactions, and has the potential as a gas sensor for CO2, CO, H2, O2 and NO. In addition, when the adsorption energy is greater than 0.50 eV, the gas molecules on the solid surface can be effectively captured.154,155 Only the adsorption energy of CO2 is greater than 0.50 (Fig. 24b), thus 2D AlN can also be used as an adsorption material for separating and capturing CO2 from mixed gases. 7. Summary and outlook

In this review, we introduce the performance of 2D GaN and 2D AlN obtained through theoretical calculations, as well as the latest research progress in the fabrication of 2D nitride materials and devices.

Through simulation and theoretical calculations, the structure of 2D GaN and 2D AlN changes with the thickness change. For example, a single-layer GaN is displayed as a planar hexagonal structure. With the increase in the number of layers, a buckling structure will be formed, and the buckling of the surface layer increases, while the lattice constant decreases and becomes more and more like the bulk structure. This variation in structure also leads to changes in properties, resulting in more excellent properties such as low thermal conductivity, strong UV luminescence and excellent mechanical properties. Based on these theoretical studies, excellent progress has been made in the fabrication of 2D nitride materials and devices. More and more fabrication methods have been developed from the original chemical stripping method, and the ways to obtain 2D GaN and 2D AlN are becoming simpler and more efficient. The improvement of the fabrication method also lays a foundation for the application of various devices. A variety of devices based on 2D GaN and 2D AlN have been fabricated, including FETs, LEDs, sensors, energy storage conversion, etc. In addition, they also have potential applications in catalysis and energy storage.

Looking into the future, although some progress has been made in 2D GaN and 2D AlN research, there are still some limitations:

(1) Due to the structure of 2D GaN and 2D AlN (non-layered structure), it is difficult to obtain the 2D state of GaN and AlN, unlike graphene (layered structure), which can be easily prepared by mechanical stripping. At present, these materials are still obtained by some complex chemical means and are not easy to transfer. The fabrication cost is high, the efficiency is low, and the industrial production standard is not reached. These factors will also limit the exploration of its applications in devices.

(2) Although 2D GaN and 2D AlN have been fabricated in the laboratory, their quality and size are still difficult to control well, which makes their theoretical performance unable to be fully utilized and affects the use of materials.

(3) The exploration on the application of 2D GaN and 2D AlN is still limited. Most of the explorations at present are still theoretical calculations, and there is still a lack of actual device performance support.

Based on these deficiencies, we speculate the development prospects of 2D GaN and 2D AlN:

(1) Explore more distinctive new preparation methods. On the one hand, the method should be as simple and quick as possible to achieve large-scale preparation; on the other hand, this method should be able to improve the quality of 2D materials and facilitate the dimensional control of 2D materials.

(2) The exploration scope of the application of 2D GaN and 2D AlN should be expanded, not only on the existing devices, but also make better use of its characteristics to explore more applications in unknown fields.

(3) With the emergence of better fabrication methods, the synthesis of 2D GaN and 2D AlN will be more convenient, the quality will gradually improve, and the performance of the device will also be further improved. In addition, the characteristics of 2D GaN and 2D AlN will be fully displayed in the device.
