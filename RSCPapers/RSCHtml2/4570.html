Ultrafast interfacial charge transfer from the LUMO+1 in ruthenium(II) polypyridyl quinoxaline-sensitized solar cells

Abstract

This paper describes the implementation of robust and modular sensitizers containing aromaticamphiphilic ligands to provide new insights into the relationship between the molecular structure and electron injection process governing the efficiency of dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs). The significance of this work lies in the combination of favorable experimental and theoretical results in a new class of Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes with the molecular formula of [Ru(E101)(Dicnq)x(Y)] which is named M101M104 when X = 1 and Y = bpy, X = 1 and Y = phen, X = 2, and X = 1 and Y = 2 NCS, respectively. E101 and Dicnq ligands are 1,10-phenanthroline-5,6 heptan ammin and 6,7-dicyanodipyrido[2,2-d:2,3-f]quinoxaline, respectively. The good agreement between the experimental and the time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT)-calculated absorption spectra of the M101104 sensitizers allowed us to provide a detailed assignment of the main spectral features of the investigated dyes. M102 which contained phen as an ancillary ligand had the best photovoltaic performance which can be attributed to the higher light harvesting of M102 in the visible light region. A DSSC based on complex M102 without the E101 ligand did not show any observable power conversion efficiency (PCE), indicating the importance of the amphiphilic ligand, E101. Transient absorption studies indicated that the ratio of kreg/krec (krec = the rate constant of the recombination of the dye and kreg = the rate constant of regeneration in the presence of the electrolyte) for M101104 is 1.1, 2.9, 1.3, and 1.2, clearly confirming a weak competition between dye regeneration and recombination. Therefore, because this ratio for M101, 103, and 104 is small, kreg krec, the operation of the device has been limited by back electron transports, subsequently enhancing the recombination process. However, the rate of recombination is relatively normal for an efficient DSSC, while the rate of regeneration is very low. Subsequently, the PCE will be poor, confirming the role of aliphatic chains in reducing the recombination process. To obtain a deeper insight into the charge transfer process in the investigated devices, ab initio DFT molecular dynamics simulations and quantum dynamics of electronic relaxation were carried out, clearly showing that the interfacial electron transfer (IET) time scale particularly depends on the type of ancillary ligand. The IET results substantially proved that M102 has the fast lifetime of 2.3 ps and 90 fs for the LUMO and LUMO+1, respectively, indicating the experimentally higher PCE of M102 compared to the other three investigated sensitizers.

1. Introduction

Among the components of a dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSC), the ancillary ligand represents outstanding multifunctional photophysical and photochemical abilities and is therefore crucial in determining the overall power conversion efficiency (PCE).1 The use of ancillary ligands having amphiphilic chains and/or a -extended aromatic unit permits the inhibition of the charge recombination process and collection of a large fraction of sunlight.

The most common modification in the design of a sensitizer for DSSCs involves the appropriate choice of an ancillary ligand through the tuning of the ground and excited-state properties for achieving better light harvesting. In this regard, several approaches have been successfully reported for enhancing the light harvesting abilities of ancillary ligands2 such as the incorporation of the C^N ancillary ligand to construct a ruthenium cyclometalated sensitizer.36 Some other approaches include: the extension of -conjugation through the incorporation of aromatic rings into different positions of ancillary ligands for improving the molar extinction coefficient () along with the red shift of the absorption spectrum; introducing electron donating/accepting groups in the ancillary ligand for effectively matching the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the dye with the redox potential of 3I/I3 and the lower limit of the conduction band in TiO2;7 using amphiphilic long chains which lead to a slow recombination process by preventing the water-induced desorption of the dye from the TiO2 surface;8 and suppressing molecular aggregation on the surface.916 All these factors are presumed to improve the overall performance of DSSCs through ancillary ligand substitutions.

As a remarkable report on the role of amphiphilic ancillary ligands in DSSCs, an interesting amphiphilic polypyridyl ruthenium dye, Z907, in combination with a photochemically stable fluorine polymer and poly(vinylidenefluoride-co-hexafluoropropylene) to solidify a 3-methoxypropionitrile-based liquid electrolyte was synthesized and investigated in order to obtain a quasi-solid-state gel electrolyte.17 The DSSC based on this conjugated system in full sunlight (air mass 1.5, 100 mW cm2) showed a conversion efficiency of over 6% and resulted in extraordinarily stable cell performance under both thermal stress at 80 C and soaking with light. In addition, after 1000 h of light-soaking at 55 C, the cells covered with an ultraviolet-absorbing polymer film retained 94% of their initial performance. After this exciting report, an exponential growth was observed in studies and publications dealing with the modification of ancillary ligands based on amphiphilic and aromatic moieties in DSSCs.18,19

We presented a new design concept for ruthenium sensitizers and reported a detailed study of DSSCs based on amphiphilic-substituted phenanthroline ligands for the first time. The careful selection of both aliphatic and aromatic moieties on ancillary ligands eventually led to the improvement of photovoltaic performances. The selection of a suitable anchoring group is the second approach for improving the efficiency of DSSCs.

Among the different mechanisms of assembling the dye onto a metal oxide substrate surface including covalent attachment, electrostatic interaction, hydrogen bonding, hydrophobic interaction, van der Waals forces, or physical entrapment, DSSCs almost exclusively employ covalent bonding between the dyes and the TiO2 surface atoms in order to ensure strong coupling, a homogeneous dye distribution, and device stability. In contrast, other interactions usually involve weaker bonds and accordingly render adsorption quasi-reversible and unstable.1,20

Historically, the most frequently used anchors in DSSCs are carboxylic acid and cyanoacrylic acid groups.2127 However, dyes with COOH as the anchor unit will dissociate on the TiO2 surface at long irradiation times.27 Thus, it is pivotal to increase the binding strength of the anchor unit. It is known that the metal center forms strong chelating bonds with the heterocyclic ligand.

Because of the recent exponential growth of DSSC research, many new anchors have also emerged and been tested. This trend has significantly increased the choice of available materials and facilitated the understanding of DSSCs.

Recently, pyridine,28 phosphinic acid,29 and 8-hydroxylquinoline27 have been investigated as anchoring groups. Nevertheless, the efficiencies of DSSCs based on these acceptors still lag behind those of cyanoacrylic acid counterparts. The bottleneck of this effort lies in either the low electron-injection efficiency or the limited broadening of absorption spectra.

In this study, we employed the cyanide group as the anchoring group as it had received considerable attention in recent studies.

Tetracyanate anchors have been included in several organic dyes and have demonstrated their capacity to sensitize TiO2 surfaces.30 However, the PCE of the devices is usually low (<0.25%). Apart from its coordination capacity, the tetracyanate group is a strong electron acceptor and has shown good performance in the presence of additional anchors such as rhodanines and carboxylic acids.31

The important features of these chromophores include intramolecular charge-transfer interactions, potent redox activities, and nonplanar molecular arrangements. The last feature is expected to contribute to the suppression of dye aggregation in DSSCs. In addition, the chromophores do not possess any carboxylic acid groups.30

This result suggests the possibility that cyanated derivatives can efficiently adsorb onto TiO2. It is interesting to examine if this is also true for the geminal dicyanated compounds in the present research.

These classes of Ru polypyridyl complexes have gained interest due to their more favorable light harvesting abilities and long-term thermal and chemical stability compared to other conventional sensitizers for the feasible large-scale commercialization of DSSCs. Therefore, the main idea is to inspire readers to explore new avenues in the design of new sensitizers for DSSCs based on different ancillary and anchoring ligand substitutions.

2.2 Computational studies

The choice of the basis set is a crucial step for DFT and time dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT) calculations. DFT/B3LYP method was extremely used for theoretical studies of ruthenium polypyridyl complexes. DFT calculations with the B3LYP functional and LanL2DZ basis set are able to predict the structures of these complexes quite reasonably.32 All calculations were done with the 6-31G(d,p) basis set for the lighter elements (Cl, O, N, C and H). For the transition metal (ruthenium), the effective core potential basis set LANL2DZ (Los Alamos ECP plus double-zeta) was used for the valence electrons and the core electrons were treated with the LANL2DZ effective core potential. After geometry optimization, a TDDFT calculation was performed to assign the transient absorption spectra of M101M104 with the B3-LYP/6-31G(d,p) + LANL2DZ level using an explicit polarizable continuum model (PCM) for solvent. The model system was initially defined according to the unrelaxed anatase nanostructure. The semiconductor nanostructure was composed of 64 TiO2 units, with angles and bond lengths defined according to available references.33 The geometry of the nanostructure was optimized with the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP/VAMP), implementing DFT to treat the Hartree and exchangecorrelation electronic interactions. The PerdewWang34 generalized gradient approximation was implemented for the exchangecorrelation functional in conjunction with ultrasoft Vanderbilt pseudo-potentials35 to describe the ionic interactions. The KohnSham (KS) Hamiltonian was projected onto a plane-wave basis set and high-efficiency iterative methods were implemented to obtain the KS eigenstates and eigenvalues. Self-consistency was accelerated by means of efficient charge density mixing schemes.

IET software was obtained from Batista Group and run considering the conditions reported in the literature.36 Computing the time-dependent wave function |(t) = iBi(t)|i, expanded in the basis set of AOs |i, required the propagation of expansion coefficients Bi(t)qQiqCqexp[(i/)Eqt], where Cq is the expansion coefficient of the initial state |(0) = qCq|q.

2.5. Fabrication of DSSCs

2.5.1 Fabrication of porous-TiO2 electrodes. To prepare the DSSC working electrodes, the transparent conductive oxide (TCO) glass plate used as the current collector was first cleaned with a detergent solution using an ultrasonic bath for 15 min and was then rinsed with water and ethanol. Afterwards, the TCO glass plates were immersed in a 40 mM aqueous TiCl4 solution at 70 C for 30 min and washed with water and ethanol. A layer of TiCl4 was coated on the TCO glass plates by screen-printing and kept in a clean box for 3 min so that the layer could relax in order to reduce surface irregularities. The plates were then dried for 6 min at 125 C. After drying the films at 125 C, the mesoscopic TiO2 films used as photoanodes consisted of layers of TiO2 (a 13 m thick transparent layer (6 + 4 + 3 m in thickness) of 25 nm TiO2 anatase nanoparticles and a 10 m thick scattering layer of 400 nm anatase TiO2 particles). The multi-layer films were heated to 520 C and sintered for 30 min, then cooled to 80 C and immersed in the dye solution at room temperature for 16 h.
2.5.2 Preparation of counter Pt-electrodes. To prepare the counter electrode, the TCO sheet was washed with H2O and 0.1 M HCl solution in ethanol and cleaned by ultrasound in an acetone bath for 10 min. After removing the residual organic contaminants by heating in air for 15 min at 400 C, the Pt catalyst was deposited on the TCO glass by coating with a drop of H2PtCl6 solution (2 mg of Pt in 1 mL of ethanol) while repeating the heat treatment at 400 C for 15 min.
2.5.3 DSSC assemblage. The dye-covered TiO2 electrode and Pt-counter electrode were assembled into a sandwich-type cell. Two clips were used to hold the two electrodes together at the corner of the plates. The electrolyte was injected into the space between the two electrodes.

3.1 An introduction to the reasonable aspects of the design of complexes

The logic of designing the molecular structure of complexes is rationally based on three functions: (i) the incorporation of ligand E101 which contains an aliphatic carbon chain and reduces the aggregation phenomena; (ii) the presence of an ancillary ligand including bpy, phen, and NSC in order to enhance light harvesting abilities; and (iii) the introduction of Dicnq as an anchoring ligand with the CN group which coordinately links onto the semiconductor surface. All three parts cooperatively contribute to enhance electron regeneration and reduce charge recombination. Two remarkable novelties in the design of molecules can be observed: (i) the attachment of aliphatic carbon chains through the nitrogen atom to the phen moiety, and (ii) the attachment of the CN group to the phen quinoxaline moiety. In a certain sense, both anchoring and ancillary ligands meticulously designed based on phen indicate the versatile substitution of the phen ligand. The UV-vis of complexes was experimentally investigated in the dimethylformamide (DMF) solution (Fig. 3) and subsequently the nature of every excitation was confirmed by TD DFT studies. In the UV-Vis spectra of complexes, two regions appeared: (i) the sharp band in the region of the wavelength of 300350 nm which can be attributed to the intra-ligand charge transfer, ILCT ( *) of aromatic moieties, and (ii) the broad band in the region of 450600 nm which differs by the substitution of an ancillary ligand. The origin of the higher wavelength transition can be attributed to the metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) from the filled t2g of Ru2+ to the empty orbitals of ligand's *.40 The zoomed region was introduced from visible to near-infra-red regions where the lightest harvesting would occur. The molar extinction coefficients () at the maximum wavelength of MLCT were estimated to be about 12150, 14375, 8720, and 11900 cm1 M1 for M101104, respectively, indicating that M102 showed a higher than the benchmark emitters such as N3 with the of 13550 cm1 M1 under the same condition. 3.3 DFT and TD-DFT studies

To achieve a deeper insight into the band absorption assignment, we carried out conventional TD-DFT calculations (singletsinglet states only contributing to absorption) on the examined complexes. The results confirmed the general assignment of low-energy features to MLCT and higher energy ones to ILCT excitation (Fig. 4 and Table 1). The full data set of the optimized molecular structure is given in ESI S3. As shown in TD DFT results, the most contribution of the transition of the lowest energy of the MLCT absorption band took place from the HOMO to the LUMO, nearly 70%. Interestingly, higher energy bands of all the studied complexes mainly originated from the HOMO1 and HOMO2 to the LUMO+1 and +2. The data are summarized in Table 1. The origin of ILCT bands was largely based on lower HOMO (HOMO1 and 2) to higher LUMO (LUMO+3). The DFT calculation clearly showed that the density of electron in the HOMO mainly located on the ancillary ligand and metal center. Nevertheless, the density of electron in the LUMO was delocalized on the Dicnq moiety. 3.5 Charge career dynamic studies

3.5.1. Transient absorption microscopy. Transient absorption microscopy was employed to investigate the kinetics of dye regeneration (kreg) by the electrolyte and the kinetics of the recombination (krec) of photoinjected electrons in the semiconductor with the oxidized dye in the absence of the electrolyte.52 The device operation of a DSSC directly depends on the competition between kreg and krec. In the normal condition, the kinetics of recombination is much slower than that of regeneration, kreg krec, resulting in the charge recombination between oxidized dyes, and the charge-injected electrons are not an important loss for limiting device efficiency.53 The decay upon the induced light absorption was monitored by laser excitation of dye M101M104-sensitized TiO2, in the presence and absence of the electrolyte (Fig. 9), with the lifetime and constant rate summarized in Table 3. For all four samples, there is a weak competition between kreg and krec. However, the decay data clearly showed that the regeneration of dye M10 by reduction by the electrolyte (k = 0.233 ms) is almost threefold (2.9) faster than by recombination with TiO2 photoinjected electrons (k = 0.0.080). This competition between kreg and krec (kreg/krec) for M101, M103, and M104 is about 1.1, 1.3, and 1.2, respectively. These results indicate that M101, M103, and M104 show kreg krec, confirming that the back electron transfer reaction and recombination process limit the PCE of devices. However, in an useful comparison, the half time of regeneration and recombination of the champion sensitizer named N719, cis-bis(thisionato)bis(2,2-bipyridine-4,4-dicarboxylato)ruthenium(II) (di-tert-butylammonium salt), is about 1 s and 1 ms, respectively, confirming the fact that the order of the reg of the investigated dyes is in the range of N719, while reg is much slower than that of N719.54 This relatively faster recombination can be attributed to aliphatic chains in the ancillary ligand (E101) which are absent in N719.

3.6 Interfacial electron transfer simulation (IETs)

In addition to the experimental tools for determining the different attachment modes of an anchoring group of a dye to a semiconductor, theoretical efforts such as DFT calculations have extensively been employed to study the interface phenomena in DSSCs. Among the powerful simulation software programs for estimating interfacial electron transfer in the dye/TiO2-anatase surface, ab initio DFT molecular dynamics simulations and quantum dynamics of electronic relaxation have widely been used.56 In summary, the process of sensitization in DSSCs starts via light absorbance by the dye attached onto the TiO2 surface, allowing to excite the electron from the steady state of the dye to its excited state. After that, the generated charge goes to the conduction band of TiO2 and, therefore, currents in the external circuit.57 The zoomed region of IET studies conceptualized the rate of electron transfer from the LUMO of the dye to the conduction band of the semiconductor, identified as the formation of a complex of an excited dye with a small size of the TiO2 cluster (TiO2)n (n is generally <80). This small cluster size is sufficient for optoelectronic property calculations on TiO2 and dye/TiO2 nanocomposites for DSSC applications.58 The IET simulations were performed using the supercell M101104-TiO2 with lattice vectors which are given in Fig. 11. The efficient electron transfer in the dye/TiO2 surface strongly depends on the matching between the energy level of the LUMO of the dye and the conduction band of TiO2 which is precisely pointed out by the density of states (DOS).57 The electronic coupling strength indicates that the photoinduced charge transfer has effectively occurred. DOS figures clearly depict the quantity of overlapping of the LUMO of the dye and the conduction band of TiO2. As shown in Fig. 12, all four dyes consist of a number of LUMOs which have shown a good overlap degree with the conduction band of TiO2. Therefore, we concluded that the attachment of the CN anchor group onto the TiO2 surface is possible and electron transfer happens subsequently. In what follows, we address the quantities of the rate of interfacial electron transfer and the population of LUMOs along with time.

Finally, it is important to compare the results reported in this paper with the results of another sensitizer, i.e. TiO2. In a pioneer study, Batista indicated that the rate of IET transfer from the LUMO orbital of catechol to TiO2 at room temperature is = 6 fs.59 However, IETs on the catechol anchoring group which substituted terpy ([MnII(H2O)3(catecholterpy)]2+/TiO2(terpy)2,2:6,2-terpyridine) indicated a typical electron injection rate in the range of 50100 fs.60 In an interesting report, Hirendra Nath Ghosh et al. have synthesized Ru(II)- and Os(II)-polypyridyl with pendent acetylacetone (acac) functionality, in which time-resolved transient absorption spectroscopic studies in the femtosecond time domain have been carried out. The electron injection process can be found to be fitted with two time constants: a pulse width limited <120 fs component and a slower 1.21.4 ps component. Results of such studies reveal that the electron injection rates from the metal complex based LUMO to the conduction band of TiO2 are faster than one would expect for the analogous complex in which the chromophoric core and the anchoring moiety are separated with multiple saturated CC linkages.61

In light of IETs investigations into the acetyl acetonate anchoring group, the charge injection rate from the LUMO of 4-acac-coumarin molecule to the TiO2 surface was estimated to be about 15 fs.62 In what follows, the simulation of IET, using the 3-(4-nitrophenyl)pentane-2,4-dione (NPA-H) LUMO+2 as the initial state, shows ultrafast IET with an injection time scale of approximately 12 fs.63 Considering the above results, our IET characterization studies clearly indicate that the IET from the LUMO is in the range of ps, while the lifetime of the LUMO+1 happens in the fs range. Given the fast injection and the low energy of the transition relative to the LUMO state, injection from the M101104 LUMO + 1 appears to be ideal.

Now, if we go back to IV results, it appears that M104 had the most open voltage circuit among all other sensitizers as well as an ultra-fast injection electron, especially from the LUMO+1, demonstrating M104 as a promising modified structure. Therefore, we propose that the fast IET contributes to lowering charge recombination from the conduction band of TiO2 to the I3/3I electrolyte energy level which determines the value of Voc.

It is worth pointing out that the main contribution of the LUMO+1 in all four dyes is exclusive to the absorption band in the range of 470490 nm in the UV-Vis spectrum (the molar extinction coefficient of UV-Vis and the IPCE value of M102 are significantly higher than those of others).

Further studies on efficient sensitizers are in progress, and we believe that improvements can be achieved by a thorough optimization of aliphaticaromatic composite structures with respect to, for example, carboxylic anchoring groups, -extended aromatic ancillary ligands of M102, and functionalized M102.

4. Conclusion

Although a variety of sensitizers have been reported in dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) as the most promising components to achieve high PCE, the preparation of a new sensitizer is still required. In particular, the new generation of the architecture of a sensitizer is based on multifunctional ancillary ligands which guaranty a number of photophysical and photochemical properties.

As a part of our project entitled The influence of ancillary ligands on DSSCs,64 four new ruthenium polypyridyl sensitizers based on dicyano quinoxaline as the anchoring ligand and E101 containing amphiphilic-phenanthroline as well as a polypyridyl ancillary ligand have been experimentally designed and thoroughly characterized for use in DSSCs, revealing the importance of all three parts of the sensitizer to achieve high efficiency.

To investigate the dye regeneration and recombination process and excited-state lifetime, the charge carrier dynamic study including transient absorption microscopy and time-resolved photoluminescence decay was carried out. Transient absorption studies clearly indicated a weak competition between kreg and krec, confirming that the back electron transport limits the operation of the device. Surprisingly, the lifetime obtained from the time-resolved photoluminescence decay was in the range of 350450 ns which belongs to long excited-state lifetimes in ruthenium polypyridyl sensitizers.

The computational section of this paper addressed the influence of the molecular structure of the sensitizer on the electron regeneration rate in the LUMOs of sensitizers to the TiO2 semiconductor. In contrast to previous reports in which the LUMO orbital showed a lifetime in the range of fs, the time scale of the lifetime of the LUMO+1 was in the range of below 100 fs which is ideal for an efficient interfacial electron transfer from the sensitizer to the semiconductor.

We considered, in particular, the correlation between the photovoltaic performance of DSSCs based on the investigated complexes and their interfacial electron transfer simulation. Our main findings are:

(1) The time scale of the lifetimes of electron regeneration from the LUMO to the TiO2 surface is in the region of ps which is a long time for electron regeneration for DSSCs.

(2) The lifetime of the LUMO+1 is below 100 fs which is ideal for an efficient electron transfer to the TiO2 surface.

(3) To design an efficient sensitizer, a number of structure requirements are suggested: (i) the presence of an ancillary polypyridyl ligand; (ii) phen is a promising ancillary polypyridyl ligand; (iii) the examined complexes without an amphiphilic ligand show very weak PCE; and (iv) the Dicnq ligand which contains phen, the quinoxaline moiety, and two adjacent CN groups can be a promising anchoring ligand for future studies.

(4) The combination of two different factors, including the higher molar absorption coefficient and the faster interfacial electron transfer rate from the LUMO and LUMO+1 of M102 compared to other complexes, leads to the higher photovoltaic performance of M102.

We believe that our study rationalizes a number of experimental observables, casting a direct connection between the molecular structure of the sensitizer and its interfacial electron transfer lifetime from possible LUMOs. In terms of connection to solar cell operation, our findings contribute to the recognition of the main excited states which effectively make the injection of electron from LUMOs to the conduction band of TiO2.
