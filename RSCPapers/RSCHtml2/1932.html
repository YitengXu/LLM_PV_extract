Periodically-ordered one and two dimensional CdTe QD superstructures: a path forward in photovoltaics

Moumita Kar a, Biplab Rajbanshi a, Ritabrata Sarkar b, Sougata Pal b and Pranab Sarkar *a
aDepartment of Chemistry, Visva-Bharati University, Santiniketan-731235, India. E-mail: pranab.sarkar@visva-bharati.ac.in
bDepartment of Chemistry, University of Gour Banga, Malda-732103, India

Received 23rd June 2019 , Accepted 8th August 2019

First published on 9th August 2019

Abstract

By using the state-of-the-art theoretical method, we herein explore the potentiality of covalently linked periodically-ordered 1D chain, 2D hexagonal and square ordered superstructures of CdTe QDs in photovoltaics. One of the major factors that controls the photovoltaic efficiency is the electronhole recombination which in turn depends on the spatial separation of these charge carriers. Our theoretical findings show that the HOMO and LUMO states are localized at two different ends of the assembled superstructures. This result indicates large spatial separation of photoexcited charge carriers which prolongs the carrier lifetime and thus reduces the chance of electronhole recombination. We have also attached an acceptor fullerene molecule with the CdTe QD superstructure and studied the electronic structure of the composite system. The photoexcited electrons of the assembled QDs potentially transfer to the low energy lying conduction band of fullerene and show a large spatial charge separation. The assembled QDfullerene composites exhibit a high photoconversion efficiency of 19.3%, opening up new possibilities for designing efficient solar energy harvesting devices based on assembled QDs.

1. Introduction

The conversion of the most important renewable energy, solar energy, to electricity is currently a topic of great interest to researchers concerned about the depletion of fossil fuel resources and global warming issues. Although initial efforts were predominantly focused on dye-sensitized or Grtzel-type solar cells, in the recent past, quantum dots (QDs) of group IIVI, IIIV and IVVI semiconductors have emerged as promising building blocks for photovoltaic applications.14 The tunability of electronic energy levels by varying the size, shape and composition of the QDs allows more flexibility in designing QD-based devices.511 Multiexciton generation (MEG) in QDs increases the efficiency of QD-sensitized solar cells.1214 Owing to their ease of synthesis using colloidal solution processability and being inexpensive abundant materials, QD-based solar cells have attracted remarkable research interest and provide a viable route for delivering high efficiency devices.

By a self-assembly process, colloidal QDs can periodically be ordered into one-dimensional (1D), two-dimensional (2D) and three-dimensional (3D) superlattices, driven by dipoledipole, van der Waals, hydrophobic, electrostatic and covalent interactions. The assembled QDs have the potential to show various functionalities and can be used in photodetectors,15 catalysis,16,17 photocatalysts,18,19 field-effect transistors and circuitry,2023 biosensors,24 light-emitting diodes,25 solar cells2630 and thermoelectric devices.31 The QD superlattices have a combined advantage of size tunable quantum confined electronic energy levels along with efficient charge transfer3234 and lead to a record breaking performance of these systems. Experimental studies have shown that the carrier mobility of electrons in QD superlattices increases by many orders of magnitude as compared to single QDs and the lifetimes of mobile electrons are much longer than those of excitons and finally can achieve high photoconversion efficiency.3538

Among different semiconductor nanoparticles, CdTe nanoparticles have drawn great attention due to their versatile applications from biological labelling to third generation solar cells.3946 In 2002, Tang et al.47 for the first time showed that thioglycolic acid (TGA) capped CdTe QDs are spontaneously assembled into highly luminescent nanowires, driven by strong dipoledipole interactions. Subsequently, there are several articles of self-assembled CdTe QD linear chains,48 nanowires,4951 nanotubes,52 and nanoribbons,53,54 governed by electrostatic, van der Waals, and dipoledipole interactions. In 2006, Tang et al.55 and Zhang et al.56 first observed that 2-(dimethylamino) ethanethiol capped CdTe QDs are assembled into 2D hexagonal sheets, driven by electrostatic interactions. Very recently, researchers have shown that thiol capped CdTe QDs have been assembled into 3D superlattices too.5760

The growth of self-assembled nanostructures is also assisted by mixing different ligand capped QDs. The simultaneous presence of TGA and cysteine (or cysteamine) ligands lead to the formation of highly luminescent CdTe QD nanorods, driven by both electrostatic and covalent interactions.61,62 In another recent work, Zhang et al.63 have observed that the presence of both TGA and 1-thioglycerol (or cysteamine) ligands lead to a stable 1D CdTe QD nanostructure. Deng et al.64 have shown that the mixed ligand systems are assembled into 1D, 2D and 3D networks. Very recently, Evers et al.65 and Baumgardner et al.32 have experimentally observed that, by controlling the temperature and other reaction conditions, one may obtain 1D chains or 2D QD superlattices, the latter with hexagonal or square ordered superstructures.

Although there are many experimental studies devoted to the fabrication of CdTe QD superlattices, theoretical studies addressing the detailed electronic structure of such systems are still lacking in the literature. For real world wide-range applications of the QD superlattices, understanding of the electronic structure of these systems is of paramount importance. In this article, we present a systematic study on the detailed electronic structures of periodically-ordered covalently linked finite 1D linear chain, 2D hexagonal and square ordered superstructures of CdTe QDs. Our objective is to gain in-depth qualitative understanding of the photogenerated charge generation, separation and recombination dynamics to explore their applicability in photovoltaic devices. It is well known that fullerene and its derivative phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) are good acceptor molecules because they have low electron transfer reorganisation energy due to delocalized -conjugated electronic structures.6669 Hybrid solar cells comprising an inorganic CdTe QD as a sensitizer and an organic PCBM component as an acceptor are well studied.70,71 We have herein studied the CdTe QD assembled superstructurefullerene hybrid nanocomposites to understand the efficiency of this new nanohybrid as an efficient solar energy harvester.

2. Details of computation

We have employed the self-consistent-charge density-functional tight-binding (SCC-DFTB) method to study the electronic structure of one and two dimensional CdTe QD assembled structures. The SCC-DFTB method is a parametrized density functional method and is discussed in detail elsewhere.7275 This method makes a compromise between reasonable accuracy and computational cost and therefore can be used to study a system containing a large number of atoms. We have provided a detailed discussion on the SCC-DFTB method in the ESI. In one of our previous studies, the accuracy of the SCC-DFTB parameter set for CdX (X = S, Se, Te) and their interactions with several different systems such as H, C, N, and O have already been established.76 Using this parameter set we have herein studied the electronic structure of the CdTe QD assembly. All the SCC-DFTB calculations are performed using the DFTB+ program package.77 For geometry optimizations, we have used the conjugate gradient algorithm with a force tolerence of 0.0005 eV 1.

We have herein chosen four different thiol-capped stoichiometric CdnTen quantum dots (n = 17, 34, 65, and 98) for the zinc blende structure. The radii of these QDs are 0.91, 1.08, 1.28, and 1.45 nm, respectively. In this context, we would like to mention that there are several previous experimental and theoretical studies on both stoichiometric and nonstoichiometric CdTe clusters along with the effect of stoichiometry on their stabilities and electronic properties.7882 We have studied only the stoichiometric ones. Chaban et al. in a recent study on a CdSefullerene composite system have shown that the photoconversion efficiency increases by 23 orders of magnitude in covalently linked systems as compared to non-interacting composite systems.83 So, we have studied a covalently linked QD assembly. We have modeled the QD assembled structures where the QDs are connected to each other through amide linkage. Therefore, two different sets of bifunctional capping ligands are used such as S(CH2)4H2 (4-mercaptobutyl amine) and S(CH2)4COOH (5-mercaptovaleric acid). All the surface Cd atoms of QDs are passivated by thiol groups (SH) except those which are functionalized by bifunctional ligands where the thiol groups at one end are used to passivate the surface Cd atoms of CdTe QDs whereas the acid and amine groups at another end are used to connect the QDs through amide (CONH) linkage by elimination of H2O. And finally all the surface Te atoms are passivated by H atoms. The QDQD distance in the QD assembly is about 14.78 . This QDQD distance in the QD-assembly is close to the length of capping linker molecules and the same for all sizes of QDs. It is worth mentioning here that we have also studied the QD-assembly where the QDs are connected through ester linkage and the corresponding results are shown in Fig. S1S3 and Tables S1, S2 of the ESI. We obtained a similar conclusion to that found in the amide linked QD assembly.

We have herein considered periodically ordered 1D linear chain, 2D hexagonal and square ordered assembled structures of different sizes by varying the number of QDs. We have modeled the following different systems: (i) 1D linear assembled structures of five different sizes containing two, three, four, five, and six QDs, respectively, (ii) 2D hexagonal ordered structures of two different sizes containing seven and sixteen QDs, respectively, and finally (iii) 2D square ordered structures of two different sizes containing four and sixteen QDs, respectively. It is worth mentioning here that, to study the hexagonal and square ordered assembly, we have first modeled the hexagonal and square ordered structures containing seven and four QDs, respectively. We then increase the number of QDs to get larger sized assembled structures. The representative optimized structures of amide linked 1D linear chain, 2D hexagonal and square ordered superstructures for one representative CdTe QD (Cd17Te17 QD) are shown in Fig. 1.

3. Results and discussion

The efficiency of photovoltaic devices strongly depends on their light harvesting properties as well as spatial charge separation which in turn reduces the electronhole recombination process or prolongs the carrier lifetime. In the present study, to achieve the maximum photoconversion efficiency in CdTe QD assembly systems, we have considered three different strategies: (i) the first one is increasing the size of the assembled superstructure, (ii) the second one is increasing the size of the parent QD in the assembly, and (iii) finally, through covalent linking of the assembled CdTe QD with a fullerene acceptor.

From Tables 1 and 2, it is seen that the band gap value decreases in the assembled QD as compared to the isolated single QD. The decrease in band gap in going from the individual QD to the assembly is in good agreement with the experimental studies.33,4749,62 The charge transfer interactions between the quantum dots results in a reduced band gap in the assembled QD as compared to the single QD. In our studied covalently linked assembled QDs (Fig. 1), the QDs are linked through amide linkages (QDCONHQDCONHQDCONHQD). The polarized molecular linking is the source of internal electric field along the chains. In recent years, Sobolewski et al.85 and Jankowska et al.86 have studied the polarized molecular assemblies for photovoltaic applications. They have shown that the polarization induced internal electric field in the molecular assembly can overcome the coulomb interaction of the exciton and dissociate the photogenerated electronhole pairs and drive them to opposite ends of molecularly assembled structures and thus make them potential candidates for photovoltaic applications. This type of phenomenon is quite different from the conventional photovoltaic effect in pn junction semiconductors. In pn junction based photovoltaic cells, the internal electric field is only present within the depletion layer at the interface and the photogenerated charge carriers are swept out of the depletion region and diffuse to the cathode or anode. The transport of charge carriers in pn junction devices is limited by diffusion. In contrast, in polarized ferroelectric materials, a permanent dipole moment and polarization induced electric field are present over the whole system, rather than just in the interfacial layer. In this case, the diffusion of charge carriers is not limited by any energy barrier at the interfacial junction. Once the system is exposed to sunlight, different excitations occur and electron and hole pairs are created. The calculated transition energies with oscillator strengths and the involved molecular orbitals of the assembled QD for a particular QD (Cd17Te17 QD) for the lowest 40 singlet excited states are listed in Tables S4 and S5 of the ESI. From this table, we can see that there may be transitions other than HOMO to LUMO such as HOMO1 to LUMO, HOMO to LUMO+1, etc. Among these transitions, the oscillator strength of some transitions may be small and for some transitions it may be large. A closer inspection of the spatial localization of the frontier orbitals such as HOMOn and LUMO+n with n being 0, 1, 2,, as shown in Fig. 3 and Fig. S4 of the ESI, reveals that the HOMOs and LUMOs are localized on particular QDs of the assembled superstructures. Due to the localization of the orbitals, photoexcitation occurs at a particular QD. The energies of these HOMOs and LUMOs are plotted in Fig. 4. The monotonic change of occupied and unoccupied orbitals localized on subsequent QDs creates an internal electric field. This electric field facilitates the dissociation of photogenerated charge carriers. Due to this electric potential gradient, orbitals having a higher (lower) energy than the LUMO (HOMO) are enriched with electrons (holes) and are ultimately transferred and localized on the LUMO (HOMO). The interaction of HOMOs and LUMOs localized on adjacent QDs provides a force to the holes and electrons for their conduction toward the terminal HOMO and LUMO, respectively. The force is unidirectional and the energy gradient for hole and electron charge carriers is in the opposite direction (Fig. 4). Noteworthily, the electric driving force governed by the energy difference between the terminal occupied and unoccupied orbitals (HOMOLUMO band gap) saturates at a constant value with an increase in the size of the assembled system for a fixed size of parent QD (Table 1). A similar type of phenomenon for a molecularly assembled cluster has already been reported by Sobolewski et al.85 Actually, when the number of QDs increases in the assembly, the change in energy of occupied orbitals and unoccupied orbitals with the size of the assembled system is small (Fig. 4) due to which the band gap saturates at a constant value.

The internal electric field which overcomes the coulomb interaction of electronhole pairs and governs the photoinduced charge separation is directly connected to the polarity of the system which is measured by the dipole moment of the system.85,86 We performed Mulliken population analysis to see the charge population on each QD and the charge population of each QD in assembled superstructures is shown in Fig. 5. We see that the assembled QD is strongly polarized. The HOMO state is electron populated and the LUMO state is electron depleted along with only minor contributions to other QDs. The dipole moment values along the chain of the assembled QD are shown in Table 3. The table shows that our studied systems are strongly polarized with high dipole moments and the average dipole moment increases with increasing the size of the assembled QD. Noteworthily, after a certain assembled superstructure size, the increase in the average dipole moment is small. Moreover, the collective polarity of the assembled QD may provide an important factor for separating the photoexcited charge carriers.

We have studied amide linked assembled QD superstructures where the amide linkages are orderly aligned (QDCONHQDCONHQDCONHQD). Another possibility may arise when the QDs are linked as QDCONHQDNHCOQDCONHQD (see Fig. S5 of the ESI). Notably, in this type of assembly, the amide linkages (CONH) are not orderly aligned unlike the previous ones we have studied. So, a natural question that arises is whether the dipole moment of this type of assembly is robust or not? Our results show reduced dipole moments as compared to the previous ones (Tables S6 and S7 of the ESI). So, the dipole moment is dependent on the orientation of the amide linkage. Therefore, the insight obtained in this study suggests experimentalists to fabricate an amide linked QD-assembly where the amide linkages (CONH) are orderly aligned and exhibit large dipole moments because the internal electric field, which facilitates photogenerated exciton dissociation and drives electrons and holes in opposite directions toward the cathode and anode, respectively, is dependent on the dipole moment of the system.

Therefore, from Fig. 3 and Fig. S4 of the ESI, we can see that the HOMO and LUMO states are located on two opposite ends of the assembled structures. This remains true irrespective of the size of the assembled structure, dimensionality (whether it is 1D chain or 2D hexagonal or square ordered) and the size of the parent QD as shown in Fig. 6 and Fig. S6 of the ESI. The understanding of spatial localization of the frontier orbitals gives a qualitative idea about the recombination dynamics of photoexcited charge carriers. The large separation of HOMO and LUMO states drastically hinders electronhole recombination and leads to longer carrier lifetimes. The carrier lifetime increases when the assembled superstructure size increases. Our theoretical findings are in line with the experimental observation of Talgorn et al.35 where authors have observed that the lifetime of mobile charge carriers in a QD assembly is larger than the exciton radiative lifetime of 17 nanoseconds. Thus, these systems when coupled to a suitable acceptor will be much more promising for application in solar cells.

As we have already discussed that the fullerene molecule acts as a good acceptor, we attached a fullerene molecule to the CdTe QD assembly. Mikroyannidis et al.87 have observed that hydrolysis of phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) to carboxylic acid (PCBA) makes it more soluble in organic solvents. We therefore attached the QD (where LUMO state of assembly is localized) to the COOH group end of PCBA through an amide linkage (Fig. 7). In the CdTe QD assemblyPCBA hybrid composite, the contribution of PCBA to light absorption is very small whereas the CdTe QD assembly generates a large number of excitons upon incident light. As PCBA has a low energy lying LUMO level, the photoexcited electrons of the CdTe QD assembly potentially transfer to the PCBA and show distinct charge separation, which is reflected from the HOMOLUMO charge densities and projected density of states (PDOS) as shown in Fig. 7 and 8, respectively, where the LUMO resides on PCBA and the HOMO resides on the CdTe QD. For a single QDfullerene composite, the LUMO (localized on fullerene) and HOMO (localized on QD) are located as neighbors as shown in Fig. 7a but for QD assemblyfullerene composites, the spatial charge separation increases with increasing system size as shown in Fig. 7b and c. This large spatial charge separation in the assembled QDfullerene composite causes a drastic reduction of the electronhole recombination rate and prolongs the carrier lifetime. This result shows that assembled QDfullerene composites are more appealing for solar energy harvesting devices as compared to the single QDfullerene composite system. It is worthwhile to mention here that eqn (1) depicts the maximum PCE of a pair of donoracceptor system, for our QD assemblyfullerene composite where the QD assembly acts as a donor and fullerene act as an acceptor. Eqn (1) basically depends on the donor band gap and energy offset between the donor and acceptor LUMO. However, eqn (1) does not take into account the effect of the large spatial separation which increases with the assembled system size. The large spatial charge separation reduces the electronhole recombination drastically. The electronhole recombination has a great role in controlling the efficiency. In the composite system [assembled CdTe QDfullerene], the electron of the donor [assembled QD] after getting photoexcited reaches the LUMO and then from this LUMO to the LUMO of the acceptor [fullerene] molecule. Now, as the LUMO of the assembled QD as well as the LUMO of the assembled QDfullerene composites are largely separated from their position of the HOMO the chance of electronhole recombination is less. As the spatial separation of the LUMO of both assembled QD and assembled QDfullerene hybrid and HOMOs increases with increasing size of the assembled QD, the chance of electronhole recombination is lower. Thus, from this spatial charge separation (which increases with system size), we qualitatively argued that, for the assembled QDfullerene composite because of the large spatial charge separation, the electronhole recombination dynamics will be slow and results in longer carrier lifetimes. Therefore, we strongly believe that the CdTe QD assemblyfullerene hybrid composite we proposed is a new entity in the realm of photovoltaic devices and thus deserves great attention for the design of efficient solar energy harvesting devices.

4. Conclusion

In summary, we have studied detailed electronic structures of covalently linked periodically-ordered finite 1D linear, 2D hexagonal and square ordered CdTe QD superstructures to understand the suitability of their potential application in photovoltaic devices. The efficiency of photovoltaic devices depends on their light harvesting properties as well as the spatial separation of photoexcited electrons and hole charge carriers which reduces the electronhole recombination and prolongs the carrier lifetime. Our results show that the HOMO and LUMO states are localized at two different ends of the assembled superstructures irrespective of the size of the parent QD. This result indicates a large spatial charge separation of photoexcited electrons and hole pairs which causes a longer carrier lifetime. We also studied the electronic structure of CdTe QD assemblyfullerene nanohybrids. In assembled CdTe QDfullerene hybrid nanocomposites, the photoexcited electrons of the QD assembly potentially transfer to the low energy lying LUMO of fullerene, thereby showing distinct charge separation. We found that the separation of the HOMO (localized on the QD) and LUMO (localized on fullerene) states increases upon increasing the size of the assembled QD, promising a novel feature to reduce the electronhole recombination effectively by increasing the assembled structure size. This result shows that assembled QDfullerene composites are more appealing for photovoltaic devices as compared to a single QDfullerene composite system. Our study reveals that such assembled QDfullerene inorganicorganic hybrid composites exhibit a high photoconversion efficiency (PCE) of about 19.3% which makes them competitive with other inorganicorganic hybrid and two-dimensional heterojunction solar cells. The seminal concept in the present study may open up a new avenue for designing efficient solar energy harvesting devices based on assembled QDs (assembly of nanocrystals of group IIVI, IIIV and IVVI semiconductors).
