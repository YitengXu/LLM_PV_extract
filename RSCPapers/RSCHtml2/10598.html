Abstract

Dry plasma reduction (DPR) is an excellent approach for easily, continuously, uniformly and stably hybridizing platinum nanoparticles (Pt-NPs) on a graphene-coated layer under atmospheric pressure without any toxic chemicals and at a low temperature. The Pt-NPs with a size of 0.5â€“4 nm and mostly 2 nm were stably and uniformly hybridized on the surface of reduced graphene oxide (RGO) after co-reduction of Pt precursor ions and GO to Pt atoms and RGO, respectively. XPS analysis also revealed a repair of structural damage on the basal plane of the graphene as well as chemical bonding between Pt-NPs and RGO after DPR. Thus, the Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrids applied to the counter electrode of dye-sensitized solar cells (DSCs) exhibited robust stability as well as ultrahigh electrochemical catalytic activity and conductivity using less than 1% of the Pt exploited for the Pt-sputtered counter electrodes. Thus, the Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid fabricated by DPR could be an excellent material for a robust and low-cost counter electrode for DSCs.

1. Introduction

A dye-sensitized solar cell (DSC) has drawn a lot of attentions due to its high conversion efficiency and low fabrication cost.1 The DSC's main components are the photo-anode, electrolyte and counter electrode (CE). Although the photo-anode has the greatest potential to improve the efficiency of the DSC, the CE also has an important role in reducing triiodide to iodide. Platinum (Pt), owing to its catalytic functionality, high conductivity and chemical stability in an acidâ€“base environment, has been known as one of the best materials used as a CE for DSCs.1,2 The materials used as the CE for DSCs are required to have high conductivity and excellent catalytic activity. However, both requirements are not easy to simultaneously satisfy. For instance, a Pt layer directly sputtered on a fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) glass substrate usually shows excellent conductivity, but also high charge transfer resistance due to the limited active surface area of the polycrystalline surface with internal defects,3 not to mention its high production cost using expensive vacuum processing and a Pt target. In other words, Pt nanoparticles (Pt-NPs) can provide high electro-catalytic activity owing to their large active area, but low electric conductivity because of their abundant grain boundaries and defects.4

Graphene is an emerging candidate material that can replace Pt owing to its 2D structure, high specific area, high electron mobility,5â€“8 and high electrochemical stability under prolonged potential cycling.6,7 However, graphene CEs usually require a large amount of graphene to achieve an acceptable level of catalytic activity.5,7 Thus, Pt-NPs supported on graphene, using only a small amount of Pt, can be a most reasonable alternative that can satisfy the inevitable requirements of low cost and long-term stability as well as excellent catalytic functionality and high electric conductivity. Many attempts have been made to combine graphene and Pt metals9â€“16 with a chemical or physical reducing agent. Pt-NPs/graphene CEs have been mostly fabricated by depositing Pt precursor ions on the graphene film/substrate and then the Pt precursor ions were reduced to Pt metal by chemical reducing agents such as hydrazine hydrate10 and sodium borohydride (NaBH4)11 or physical methods such as photo-reduction,10 thermal decomposition,12 hydrogen plasma17 or a combined chemical and physical reducing agent such as a microwave-assisted heating method in an ethylene glycol media.9 Recently, Pt-NPs were directly deposited on the graphene using the pulsed laser ablation method.18 The previous methods have some drawbacks such as a high reduction rate during chemical reduction,10,11 long reduction time during photo-reduction,10 long cooling time and agglomeration of NPs owing to high temperature reduction,12 expensive vacuum equipment and Pt targets,17,18 rendering it difficult to develop an economic, continuous process. Recently, we developed a new process of efficiently synthesizing supported Pt-NPs using dry plasma reduction (DPR) at near room temperature under atmospheric pressure.19 This method allows us to overcome the process restrictions previously mentioned-especially in a high temperature, low pressure, or toxic liquid environment. Furthermore, hydrogen radicals and electrons formed during the plasma reduction process20 can also reduce oxygen-containing functional groups on the surface of GO.21

Herein, we present a strategy to easily, continuously, uniformly and stably hybridize Pt-NPs on the surface of reduced graphene oxide (RGO) with simultaneous co-reduction of Pt precursor ions and GO to Pt atoms and RGO, respectively, through DPR. It is expected that the Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrids will show ultrahigh catalytic activity, conductivity and good long-term stability for regenerating iodide from triiodide at the CE of DSCs due to chemical bonding of Pt-NPs with graphene under atmospheric pressure plasma and physical bonding between the graphene sheet and FTO glass with the aid of a binder, which is composed of paste and removed after annealing.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthesis and characterization of Pt-NP/RGO nanohybrid materials

The synthesis of Pt-NPs hybridized on a GO-coated FTO glass substrate, which was started from the preparation of graphite flakes (shown in ESI Fig. S1â€ ) using a modified Hummers method,22 was achieved by using a DPR, as sketched in Fig. 1b. As the first step, a drop of precursor solution was loaded on the GO-coated FTO glass, followed by a complete drying process of solvent at 70 Â°C for 10 min. During this drying process, H2PtCl6Â·xH2O was partially reduced to Pt atoms through alcoholic reduction. In the second step, the remaining salts on the dried surface are further reduced to Pt-NPs through the irradiation of RF atmospheric pressure plasma with Ar gas under previously optimized conditions.19 At this step, RGO was also formed from GO through the reaction with electrons and hydrogen radicals,20 which are generated through the atomization reaction of the Pt precursor at the interface between the plasmas and Pt salt on the surface of the GO-coated electrode, with the oxygen containing functional groups on the surface of the GO-coated electrode.21 As shown in Fig. 1, the DPR can also be well adapted to develop an economic continuous process for immobilizing metal NPs on the surface of various substrates including thermoplastic films as well as GO-coated FTO glass substrates.

Scanning electron microscope (SEM) and transmission electron microscope (TEM) images of GO CE and Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid structures are shown in Fig. 2aâ€“c. The Pt-NPs are clearly visible and successfully immobilized on RGO (Fig. 2b). The reduction of GO to RGO will be confirmed by the XPS data below (Fig. 3). As can be seen in Fig. 2b, Pt NPs are favorably well dispersed on the surface of RGO without showing any agglomeration. The TEM image (Fig. 2c) further confirms the SEM analysis, showing that the Pt-NPs have a size in the range of 0.5â€“4 nm and mostly 2 nm, which are quite small and highly mono-dispersed. The average size of Pt-NPs formed on the surface of the Cu grid and FTO glass was 2.5 and 3 nm, respectively.19 The average size of the Pt-NPs on RGO is slightly larger than that of Pt-NPs on CVD-grown graphene (1.5 nm).24 The dependence of the NP size on the nature of the substrates could be due to the different electrostatic energy of the graphene.25 Other studies using chemical or physical methods reported that Pt-NPs grown on the surface of graphene had a size larger than 10 nm due to the high reduction rate of NaBH4,13 larger than 5 nm due to vitamin C reduction,14 and larger than 3 nm due to ethylene glycol under an ultrafast microwave-assisted method.9,15,16 In this study, Pt-NPs are not only uniform, but also well distributed on the surface of the RGO without any aggregation. A heavy element, Pt, is distinctly observed in Fig. 2c, where the lattice spacing is shown in the enlarged image, and the estimated lattice spacing is 1.96 Ã…, which coincides well with Pt {200}.20

Raman spectroscopy is a powerful tool to characterize carbonaceous materials, particularly for distinguishing ordered and disordered crystal structures of carbon.32,33 The Raman spectrum of the pristine natural graphite is well known. The spectrum displays a strong G line at 1582 cmâˆ’1, a weak D line at 1350 cmâˆ’1, a broad 2D line at 2690 cmâˆ’1, and a very weak 2G line at 3245 cmâˆ’1.32,33 The D band peak is attributed to the disordered structures, such as bond-angle disorders and asymmetric lattices in the graphite. The G band peak represents one of the fundamental Raman modes of sp2-bonded graphite. Fig. 4 shows the Raman spectra of the GO and Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid. The spectra are very similar. The discernible peaks can be indexed as a D-band (blue line) at 1340 cmâˆ’1, a G-band (purple line) at 1595 cmâˆ’1, and a Dâ€²-band (green line) at 1620 cmâˆ’1. Compared to pristine graphite, the D band of the GO and Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid are shifted upward to 1595 cmâˆ’1. Furthermore, the intensity of the D band is higher than that of the G band. These phenomena could be attributed to the significant decrease in the size of the in-plane sp2 domains due to oxidation and ultrasonic exfoliation, and the partially ordered graphite crystal structure of the graphene nanosheets.32,34 At the same time, a strong Dâ€² peak occurred, which can be assigned to the nonzero phonon density of the states above the G band. These phonons, which are usually Raman-inactive, can become active due to phonon confinement caused by the defects.33 We noted that the ID/IG intensity ratio of GO (2.35) is higher than that of the Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid (1.79). The lowering of the D to G peak ratios point out the increase in the average size of the sp2 domains upon reduction of the GO due to the loss of oxygen.26 This result has been supported by XPS analyses as aforementioned.

3.3. Stability of Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid materials

Most importantly, the Pt-NPs/RGO counter electrode shows excellent stability. The stability was confirmed by the reproducibility of the currentâ€“voltage curves over time for the redox processes at the Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid working electrode as shown in Fig. 7.20 No detachment of the Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid films from the FTO-glass was observed after the 1000th cycle20 probably due to a Ï€â€“Ï€ interaction within the RGO11 (XPS data in Fig. 3), and van der Waals force between the RGO and FTO glass substrate with the aid of binder from paste and chemical bonding between Pt and RGO via functional group on the RGO surface.42 These results confirm the trends observed for the impedance characteristics, supporting the validity of our interpretation.

4. Conclusions

In summary, a highly pure Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid has been successfully synthesized by dry plasma reduction at low temperature. TEM results showed Pt-NPs with a size range of 0.5â€“4 nm (mostly 2 nm) dispersed on the surface of RGO. The long-term stability of the Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid electrocatalyst for triiodide reduction was clearly improved by chemical binding between the RGO and Pt-NPs. The FF of the DSC with the Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid electrode was the highest among the three electrodes, due to the small internal resistance of the cells. The electrochemical impedance measurement of the DSCs also revealed that the charge-transfer resistance of the Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid-coated electrode was the smallest. Thus, we expect that the Pt-NPs/RGO nanohybrid-coated electrode, which shows long-term stability as well as excellent energy conversion efficiency for DSCs with a small amount of Pt, can be a robust and low-cost electrode for an energy conversion device such as fuel cells or energy storage devices such as the lithium ion battery and supercapacitor.
