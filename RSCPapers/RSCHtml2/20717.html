Abstract

Due to their chemical and electrical characteristics, such as metallic conductivity, redox-activity in transition metals, high hydrophilicity, and adjustable surface properties, MXenes are emerging as important contributors to oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), oxygen evolution reaction (OER), and hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) catalyst development. In this review, we summarize current advances in electrocatalysis using MXene-based composite materials for the ORR, OER, and HER, and their linking processes to theoretical models. Significant factors that affect the properties of MXene based electrocatalysts, including functional groups, conductivity, and interfacial design, are discussed. Recent advances in MXene material engineering and material technology are also discussed. Finally, we have discussed the current limitations of ORR, OER, and HER electrocatalysts based on MXene composites, obstacles to their future development, and recommendations for future research efforts.

1. Introduction

MXenes (transition metal carbides and nitrides) are new members of the rapidly expanding family of 2D materials, and the first known MXene was Ti3C2, which was produced by selective etching of Al from Ti3AlC2. MXenes have the general formula Mn+1XnTx (n = 1–3), where M is the early transition metal, X is carbon/nitrogen, and Tx represents surface terminations (O, OH, Cl, and F; x is the number of surface terminations). More than 30 MXene compounds are currently being synthesized, and many more are theoretically anticipated.1–4 Professor Yury Gogotsi and his Drexel University colleagues reported the very first work on extraordinary 2D MXenes, the first Ti3C2X,1 and subsequently the etching of Ti3AlC2 with hydrofluoric acid (HF), which introduced O, OH, and F functionalities on the surface. The relative stability of various surface terminations was demonstrated in this sequence: Ti3C2O2 > Ti3C2F2 > Ti3C2(OH)2.1,5 The general chemical formula of MXenes is given as Mn+1XnTx (n = 1–3), where M can be Ti, V, Nb, Mo, etc. X can be C or N, with surface groups such as OH, O, and F yielding potential 2D structures of different compositions, namely M2X, M3X2, M4X3 and M5X4 (ref. 2) (Fig. 1). Functional groups are often included in MXenes to provide hydrophilicity.

MXenes represent a new 2D-structure-based material family that has developed significantly over the past decade, in particular, they find applications in batteries, photonics, sensors, and CO2 reduction, and as electrocatalysts, etc.6–8 MXenes offer superior support because of their extremely large area, broad surface performance, and distinctive geometrical and electronic structures.8–11 The combination of conductivity (ions/electrons) and electrochemical reaction (oxidation/reduction) is important in battery technology, while electrocatalysis is influenced by thermodynamic, charge transfer, ohmic and concentration overpotentials. Conductivity affects transparent conducting films and thermoelectric devices, respectively, while photoconductivity has an effect on optoelectronic devices.5

Generally, MXenes are prepared by selective etching of MAX phases, and are considered as a family of ternary compounds consisting of a transition metal (M), an A-group element, and a carbon or nitrogen (X). Multifaceted flakes of MXenes are often generated by selective etching of the middle element A from a MAX phase (MAX becomes MX), such as the etching of Al from Ti3AlC2 as discussed above, using intercalation agents, e.g., LiF, etc. (Fig. 2).7 MAX-layered precursors provide the raw ingredients for MXenes, including Al, Ga, In, Tl, Si, Ge, Sn, Pb, P, and S (Fig. 3).5,12 MAX phases are a class of ternary nitrides and carbides that exist as nanolaminates. Mn+1AXn (n = 1–3) is the general formula for MAX phases, where M is a transition metal, A is a group A element, and X is either carbon or nitrogen. The n number may be 1, 2, or 3, and the phases are designated as MAX phases 211, 312, and 413. Layers of transition metal carbides or nitrides (Mn+1Xn) are interleaved with layers of A-element atoms to create them.10,11 Unlike certain others, the solid architecture and characteristics of MXenes, which are significantly proportional to the derived surface removal, are found to be M or X elements.10,11,13,14 The composition and structure of transition metal components and their electronic properties can be tuned by adjusting the ratio of transition metal elements to surface functional groups, resulting in a variable bandgap, extremely high electrical activity and charging density, and increased mechanical performance resistance.10,11,13,14 For example, most surface terminations of MXenes have a considerable amount of negative charge as a result of the electron transportation between the outer metal element and surface termination, allowing metal cations to bind to the surface of the material in question. In addition, targeted etching may remove the adjacent external metal atoms, resulting in the production of vulnerable metal void defects with a high capacity for reducing the number of metal ions binding and reducing them in solution.5,12,14,15 Extensive simulation and practical research demonstrated how the content and arrangement of surface terminations significantly impact the physiochemical properties of MXenes. The anions in the etchant produce the surface termination Tx of Mn+1CnTx MXenes when using the exposed A-site atoms of the MAX phase. Surface chemistry investigation of MXenes has accelerated and will probably increase further in the future.5,12

Electrocatalysis is essential in sustainable energy transformation since it can convert atmospheric gases (including water, carbon dioxide, and nitrogen) into value-added products using energy sources.16–18 Attempts have been made to investigate the fundamental electrocatalytic processes associated with the ORR, OER, HER, NRR, and CRR by various electrocatalysts such as heterostructured electrocatalysts,19 transition metal dichalcogenide (TMD) heterostructures,20,21 metal–metalloids,22 non-van der Waals 2D electrocatalysts,23 transition metal nitrides (TMN),24 metallenes25 and non-noble electrocatalysts.26,27 For example, defect-rich and ultrathin nitrogen-doped carbon nanosheets fabricated by self-foaming pyrolysis were used as multifunctional electrocatalysts for the ORR, OER, and HER.28 In another work, a MoS2-based HER catalyst was developed wherein the baseline plane was stimulated and tuned by the synthesis of strained sulfur vacancies, and this catalyst showed high intrinsic HER activity.29 Recently, MXenes have been studied for electrocatalysts in clean energy conversion, namely the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), oxygen evolution reaction (OER), and hydrogen evolution reaction (HER).17,30 This demonstrates that MXenes may be potential electrocatalysts in the aforementioned processes, replacing costly platinum-based and RuO2 catalysts.5,16 Among the major issues, adsorption, membrane separation, photocatalysis, and electrocatalysis systems are regarded to be practical and economically feasible remediation techniques that may be addressed by using MXenes on a massive scale.17,31 Mo2CTx MXene was the first electrocatalyst reported for the HER and thus opened the door for MXene-based electrocatalyst development for a wide variety of clean energy challenges.32 MXenes have a lot more advantages compared to other 2D layered structures. The first one is their bulk production in water and their non-toxic nature. Secondly, the high surface area and high electrical conductivity of these 2D materials promise applicability in electrocatalytic activities, including the ORR, OER, and HER.

It is undeniably true that the investigation on MXenes is still in its early stages despite more than a decade after their discovery. Their properties are still at the exploration stage and have a plethora of untapped potential yet to be realized. Because of the significant applications of these rapidly developing 2D materials, it is essential to conduct timely reviews of the most recent advances in order to expedite the research outcome. Even though several outstanding evaluations on diverse MXene-based fields have sprouted,33–38 a comprehensive description and assessment of the latest accomplishments in electrocatalysis continues to be a void in the knowledge base. This review will therefore focus on MXenes from the perspective of electrocatalysts. The present review begins with a general discussion of synthetic methodologies followed by highlighting the most recent advances in electrocatalysis technologies (ORR, OER, HER, NRR, and CRR). Throughout this review, we focused on emerging trends in MXene-based energy conversion technologies and their potential for growth in the years to come. International research communities from a wide range of fields, including material science, chemistry, and engineering, are expected to find this review very informative.

2. Synthesis strategies

Synthesis of MXenes has seen a resurgence of activity since 2011,1,4,39 where efforts have been made towards developing cost-effective and energy-efficient synthetic protocols for MXenes. Typically, MXenes are synthesized via fluoride etching, alkali treatment, electrochemical etching, and water-free etching, as reviewed in this section (Fig. 4).38 Usually the MAX phase is the starting precursor for MXene preparation; however, certain MXenes, such as Mo2CTx and Zr3C2Tx, were produced by etching the A-elements from the non-MAX phases Mo2Ga2C and Zr3Al3C5.40,41 The use of molten salts, which allow the A-element to be removed at high temperatures, is an alternate way of etching. After etching, the MXenes obtained were usually of high purity. Synthesis of Ti4N3 was accomplished by etching Ti4AlN3 with molten fluoride salt (Fig. 5).42 Aside from wet etching, chemical vapor deposition is another technique for MXene production. One advantage of this technique is that the 2D MXenes are left unterminated.43 To date, over 30 MXenes have been created using etching techniques on MAX phase precursors by removing the A-element.44 The structure of the MAX phase precursor and the etching technique govern the building block of MXene, Mn+1Xn concluded by specific functional groups.1,40,44 When alloying novel metals to the MAX phases, the synthesized MXenes are no longer restricted to MXenes such as Mo2C.1,40 Consequently, the expanded MXenes (MXenes created by alloying new metals) such as double-metal MXene (Mo2Ti) C2, (Cr2Ti) C2, etc. are essentially limitless.45,46 Density functional theory (DFT) was used to investigate the thermodynamic stability of the MAX phase with various compositions, and the results revealed the existence of stable M2AX phases, including solid solutions. The Pourbaix diagram and thermodynamic stability of MXenes under electrochemical circumstances were studied, and it was discovered that novel MXene materials might be synthesized by etching.47,48

Mo2CTx (where Tx refers to the surface terminations of O, OH, and F) MXene was created by selectively etching Ga atoms from a Mo2Ga2C crystal in HF solution to create a 2D organ-like molybdenum carbide. The hydrothermal technique was used to make MoS2 nanoflowers to decorate Mo2CTx MXene. This technique enhances the electrocatalyst's electronic conductivity and controls its electronic structure to boost intrinsic HER activity in basic solution.51 In another study, Co-based MOF (ZIF-67) crystals were formed on the 3D MXene hydrogel to create a 3D ZIF-67@MXene composite hydrogel. The electrostatic repulsive interactions between the nanosheets are destroyed by cross-linking Co2+ ions with the OH groups of MXene, resulting in a stable 3D MXene hydrogel.52

As a general technique for producing additional MXenes, the E-etching process was developed.53–55 Because V2CTx and Cr2CTx are difficult to synthesize under moderate circumstances and have the same M period as Ti, they were chosen as representatives.55 The efficient etching method was investigated for Ti2N (MXene), synthesized by selective etching of Al from a Ti2AlN precursor using a potassium fluoride and hydrochloric acid combination.56,57 In another study, ammonization at 600 °C transforms Mo2CTx and V2CTx carbide MXenes into 2D metal nitrides. Mo2N maintains the MXene structure while V2N changes into a mixed layer structure, including trigonal V2N and cubic VN.58,59 Beyond the MAX phases, the transition metals Zr and Hf are more likely to produce a distinct family of layered ternary and quaternary transition metal carbides with the formulas (MC)nAl3C2 and (MC)n[Al(Si)]4C3 (where M = Zr or Hf, n = 13). The Zr-containing MXene was recently produced by selective etching of AlC units from layered Zr3Al3C5.41,60,61

Traditional methods for synthesizing MXene quantum dots (MQDs), such as the widely used solvothermal and hydrothermal methods, are time-consuming and require high temperatures and hazardous chemicals. Recently Yeo et al. suggested a additive-free, room-temperature technique for delaminating multilayer MXene into monolayer MQDs, which takes advantage of the enormous mechanical force that occurs when the sample is nebulized by high-frequency acoustic waves.62 MXenes must still be built into structures with high ionic and electronic transport characteristics before being utilized as electrocatalysts.63 In this review, recently proposed synthesis strategies were classified based on the elemental group of M in MAX phases, such as IVB MXene, VB MXene, and VIB MXene synthesis strategies.

3. ORR electrocatalysts

Research and development of innovative clean energy conversions based on renewable sources are in great demand. Electrocatalysis, particularly the ORR in alkaline media, is a crucial building block for renewable energy systems. The slow kinetics of the ORR on the cathode restricts the fuel cell performance. Advanced transition metal carbides or nitrides (MXene) are a new class of 2D materials with excellent conductivity, strength, and corrosion resistance in an alkaline medium. MXenes have surface functional groups that can enhance the adsorption of metal particles by anchoring active sites.

3.1 MXene-based ORR electrocatalysts

Recently, Wang et al. reported a series of MXenes, including Ti3C2Tx, V2CTx, and Nb2CTx and investigated their electrochemical performance for H2O2 generation.65 All the three MXenes showed high selectivity and great potential for H2O2 electrogeneration. Interestingly, Nb2CTx MXene displayed high ORR activity and a high H2O2 production rate of 1.95 mol gcat−1 h−1 at 0.1 V (vs. RHE). However, the stability of Ti3C2Tx and V2CTx materials is poor due to the intrinsic dissolution property of Ti and V in alkaline media. Chenxi Xu et al. showed that loading Pd nanoparticles on a hybrid catalytic substrate consisting MXene (Ti3C2Tx) and carbon nanotubes improves the performance of the electrocatalyst. Compared to the Pd/C catalyst, this hybrid catalyst had increased durability and enhanced ORR activity. For the ORR, the mass-specific activity and specific activity of the Pd/Ti3C2Tx–CNT catalyst were 4.4 and 3.3 times higher, respectively, than those of Pd/C. The O2 → OOH* → O* → OH* → H2O (O* mechanism) is now the most generally recognized associative ORR mechanism. There is another route: O2 → OOH* → 2OH* → OH* → H2O (2OH* mechanism), where 2OH* replaces the intermediary O* in the O* mechanism. Because two OH* may bind to distinct active sites, the free energy of 2OH* is roughly equal to that of O*. According to the well-known scaling equation ΔG(O*) = 2ΔG(OH*), these two pathways are thermodynamically comparable for catalysts with near-continuous active sites.

For conventional catalysts, most studies addressed only the O* pathway, which is logical and comparable to the 2OH* approach. In recent years, a new potential area in heterogeneous catalysis has emerged: single-atom catalysts with isolated active sites. Because of its unique atomically scattered active sites, the reaction process on the single-atom catalyst may vary. The ORR has three surface intermediates, two of which (OOH* and OH*) are the same in O* and 2OH* processes69,70 (Fig. 11a and b).

4. OER electrocatalysts

The oxygen-evolution reaction (OER) is a fundamental process in renewable energy systems like rechargeable metal–air batteries, fuel cells, and water electrolysis.78,79 However, overcoming the high overpotential (theoretically, a potential equivalent to 1.23 V under standard conditions should suffice) and sluggish kinetics of this four-electron oxidation reaction is crucial for efficient, practical use of green electrochemical energy conversion and storage technologies.

The scientific community has been continuously investigating the development of robust, low-cost, stable, and efficient electrocatalysts for the OER.80–82 The electrocatalyst could be developed as the electrode itself or immobilized on the working electrode surface (sometimes in porous hosts or with binders) for superior reactant adsorption and easier intermediate transformations (OH*, OOH*, and O* intermediates).78,83

The theoretical voltages vs. RHE for Nb2CO2–Pd, Nb2CO2–Pt, Nb2CF2–Pd, and Nb2CF2–Pt were 1.79, 2.34, 1.68, and 2.20 V, respectively (Fig. 12). 5. HER electrocatalysts

Renewable energy sources are required to avoid fossil fuels and global warming effects in recent years. However, the low energy delivery efficiency of some renewable energy sources limits their applications. The conversion of these energy sources into value-added chemical fuels is a prospective solution to store and transport energy with safety. Hydrogen (H2) is considered one of the most promising solar fuels owing to its high gravimetric energy density (as liquid), relatively high abundance, and low pollution emission during consumption.88,99 Water splitting is an alternative technique to produce green hydrogen with high reaction selectivity by using electricity from sunlight.88,99 The HER is one of the half-reactions in water electrolysis, including hydrogen atom adsorption, proton reduction, and H2 desorption processes.100,101

The HER in the acidic and basic electrolytes has been found to follow four pathways:

H2O + e− → Hads Volmer reaction in acid solution

H+ + e− → Hads + OH− Volmer reaction in basic solution

Hads + Hads → H2 Tafel reaction

H+ + Hads + e− → H2 Heyrovsky reaction
The HER kinetics is influenced by either a Volmer–Tafel or Volmer–Heyrovsky mechanism depending on the catalytic surface and applied potential. There is a consensus that the HER always consists of two primary steps in acidic and alkaline electrolytes.99,102

The HER reaction is a multi-step process that involves adsorption, reduction, and desorption in both acidic and alkaline media. The term “Volmer step” refers to the process of hydrogen adsorption from a hydronium ion (under acidic circumstances) or a dissociated water molecule (under alkaline conditions). The Heyrovsky or Tafel process combines the adsorbed hydrogen (H*) to create molecular hydrogen. Under actual electrocatalytic circumstances, these routes rely on the electrode surface's inherent chemical and electrical characteristics. The Tafel reaction can reveal the rate-determining step of the reaction, and it may provide some valuable discernment of possible reaction pathways.103,104 Due to their exceptional properties, MXenes and MXene-based materials have been extensively used as electrocatalysts for the HER.

5.1 MXene-based HER electrocatalysts

Since hydrogen is released during the synthetic processes of MXenes and related materials, MXenes are thought to be efficient catalytic materials for the HER.105 In particular, Mo2CTx, a promising catalytic candidate for the HER, exhibits good stability in acidic medium during galvanostatic measurements.32 Computational studies suggest that the basal planes of Mo2CTx are catalytically more active, unlike in the case of MoS2, in which the edge sites of the 2H phase are active. The carbon nanoplating strategy was reported for efficiently stabilizing the MXenes against structural degradation caused by the spontaneous oxidation reaction.106,107 This strategy provides the platform for synthesizing MXene-based materials with fascinating structures and properties. 2D hierarchical MoS2/Ti3C2-MXene@C nanohybrids are fabricated with excellent electrical properties, structural stability, and reaction kinetics for electrochemical reactions by assembling carbon coated few-layered MoS2 nanoplates on the carbon-stabilized Ti3C2 MXene backbone. As a result, they exhibited exceptional electrochemical performance for Li storage with high capacities and an ultra-long cycle life of 3000 cycles at a high rate of 20 A g−1. Meanwhile, MoS2/Ti3C2-MXene@C nanohybrids are also highly active non-precious metal electrocatalyst for the HER with positive onset potential, low overpotential and stable a low pH. A three-dimensional interconnected ternary nanoarchitecture was constructed from Ti3C2Tx MXene, graphitic carbon nitride nanosheets and graphene using a convenient co-assembly process.108 The resulting nanoarchitecture creates numerous pore sites for fast transportation of electrolytes. Also, it exhibits an exceptional HER catalytic activity with a low onset potential of 38 mV and a Tafel slope of 76 mV dec−1 because of its large specific surface area, ultrathin nanolayers, optimized electronic structure, and high electric conductivity.

Due to the quantum confinement effect and metal–support interactions, single atom catalysts (SACs) can be ideal for electrochemical applications.108,109 To achieve desired catalytic activity and selectivity, SACs depend on the nature of the single metal atom and the solid supporting materials used to disperse the metal via possible interactions between them. Liu et al. reported a catalyst consisting of single-atom ruthenium (RuSA) sites supported on nitrogen (N) heteroatom-doped Ti3C2Tx MXene (N–Ti3C2Tx) through a coordinative interaction between RuSA and Ti3C2Tx.110 RuSA sites are stabilized via the formation of Ru–N bonds with N heteroatom dopants on the Ti3C2Tx MXene support, which remarkably enhances the electrocatalytic activity for the HER at various pH values. The reported RuSA–N–Ti3C2Tx catalyst exhibits high catalytic performance and stability with low overpotentials of 23, 27, and 81 mV to realize a current density of 10 mA cm−2 in 0.5 M H2SO4, 1 M KOH, and 1 M PBS electrolytic solutions. 5.2 Surface mechanisms for the HER by DFT

Density functional theory (DFT) calculations indicate that RuSA coordinated with N and S heteroatoms on the Ti3C2Tx MXene support is the active catalytic site for the HER. As shown in Fig. 17d, impressively, RuSA–N–S–Ti3C2Tx shows a satisfactory ΔGH* value of 0.08 eV, which is much closer to zero, highlighting the beneficial H adsorption–desorption and subsequent H2 production characteristics of RuSA–N–S–Ti3C2Tx. In addition, the total density of states analysis of RuSA–N–S–Ti3C2Tx confirms that the system possesses metallic characteristics with good electronic conductivity, which is favorable for the electrocatalytic HER.111

Kuznetsov et al. reported a new route for the synthesis and structural characterization of a solid solution phase of Mo2CTx:Co MXene with substitution of the Co atom on the molybdenum sites.116 The incorporation of a Co atom into a Mo2CTx matrix not only introduces an additional reaction site but also alters the binding energies of electrocatalytic reaction intermediates,122 thereby influencing the kinetics of the reaction. The favorable effect of cobalt substitution on the electronic structure and the redox properties of Mo2CTx:Co improves the HER catalytic activity compared to the unsubstituted Mo2CTx material. DFT calculations explain that even a small amount of Mo substituted by Co imparts a significant perturbation in the electronic structure of Mo2CTx:Co, in particular, affecting the hydrogen adsorption energies on the neighboring oxygen atoms of an oxygen-terminated Mo2CO2:Co surface, making these metallic sites more favorable for HER catalysis.

Zhang et al. prepared Mo2TiC2Tx MXene nanosheets by electrochemical exfoliation of Mo2TiC2Tx–VMo with abundant surface Mo vacancies, in which as-formed Mo vacancies can act as the anchoring sites for single Pt atoms (Fig. 18a).123 At the electrochemical exfoliation process, single Pt atoms are immobilized on the Mo vacancies and stabilized by forming covalent bonds with surrounding carbon atoms on the MXene. The resulting Mo2TiC2Tx–PtSA catalyst showed enhanced HER catalytic activity (30 and 77 mV at 10 and 100 mA cm−2) with excellent stability (10000 HER cycles) and increased 39.5-fold mass activity compared to the commercial Pt/C catalyst (Fig. 18b and c). As shown in Fig. 18d, the ΔGH* of Mo2TiC2O2 is concluded to be −0.19 eV, resulting in strong hydrogen adsorption on Mo–O active sites. In contrast, the free energy (ΔGH*) for hydrogen adsorption reveals that the single Pt atom site on Mo2TiC2O2–PtSA is a hydrogen generation active site with a low adsorption energy of −0.08 eV, which is even lower than that for commercial Pt (−0.10 eV).124 Niu et al. developed a novel one-pot synthesis approach to incorporate Pt single atoms into the Mo2C nanocrystals confined in a MOF-derived 3D porous carbon material.125 The as-formed Pt–Mo2C–C catalyst with 0.7 wt% Pt content exhibits higher electrocatalytic activity for the HER in 1 M KOH electrolyte solution, with an overpotential of 155 mV at 10 mA cm−2 and 7-fold higher mass activity than the commercial 20 wt% Pt/C catalyst. The electronic interaction between Pt single atoms and the Mo2C surface promotes the catalytic activity for the HER and strengthens hydrogen adsorption on the Mo2C surface. 6. Nitrogen reduction reaction (NRR)

The generation of ammonia (NH3) from nitrogen (N2) is a critical reaction, especially in light of the widespread usage of NH3 as a fertilizer, refrigerant, and feedstock.128 The classic Haber–Bosch process takes place at high temperatures (400–500 °C) and high pressures (10–30 MPa), consuming approximately 1–2% of global energy and causing approximately 1% of global energy-related CO2 emissions due to natural gas reforming.129 The electrochemical N2 reduction process, in which N2 combines with protons in an aqueous electrolyte, has garnered much attention during the last few years. It is believed that the electrochemical NRR, which makes use of renewable energy sources, is the most environmentally friendly and cost-effective method of producing “green ammonia”. In the NRR, a major challenge is the activation of the stable N2 triple bond, having a high bond energy of 941 kJ mol−1.130 Also, the strong interaction between the active intermediate N species and the catalyst may prevent the production of NH3. Hence the appropriate catalyst for the NRR should be designed to demonstrate the optimum interaction between N species and the catalyst. One more obstacle for the NRR is the competing HER, which can occur in acidic and alkaline environments.129,131,132 As a result, limiting the amount of hydrogen adsorption on the catalyst is also critical for improving catalytic performance.

6.1 MXene based nitrogen reduction reaction (NRR) electrocatalysts

Various MXenes (e.g., Mo2C, W2C,133 and Mo2TiC2 (ref. 134)) have been investigated for their potential as electrochemical NRRs, as shown in Table 5. The terminations in MXenes play a very important role in determining the stability of the catalyst under various operating conditions and the selectivity for the NRR over the HER. In particular, as compared to O-terminated and N-terminated M2CTx MXenes, H-terminated M2CTx MXenes exhibit greater stability at higher potentials than the other two.135 Also, F-terminated Mo2CTx MXene exhibits higher NRR activity and structural stability than O-terminated Mo2CTx MXene.136,137 In addition to the basal sites of MXenes, it has been established that the edge sites of MXenes catalyze the NRR. In order to manufacture MXenes with more edge sites, vertically aligned Ti3C2Tx nanosheets are hosted on a FeOOH nanosheet electrode.138 It has been found that the Ti atoms on the edge of Ti3C2Tx preferentially bind and activate N2, with a low activation energy barrier in the first hydrogenation phase. Compared to horizontally or randomly ordered MXene sheets, the HER activity of the Ti3C2Tx sheet catalyst is low, resulting in a higher NRR faradic efficiency than the horizontally or randomly ordered MXene sheets. Also, bulk metal nitrides, such as VN, have shown potential in the NRR;139,140 however, due to the poorly defined nitride structure of nitride catalysts, it is challenging to control the reaction rate and catalyst deactivation. Nitrides with well-defined MXene structures have the potential to be more stable catalysts and to provide uniform active sites that correlate more closely with the structural models derived from DFT calculations of NRR mechanisms. Nitride reaction rates are lower than those of carbides, and surface heteroatoms such as oxygen atoms play an important role in limiting catalyst deactivation compared to carbides.139 It is possible to alter the amount and diversity of surface terminations or vacancies on MXenes by modifying the synthesis or reaction conditions,141 which should open the door to designing and developing effective NRR catalysts. It is also possible to modify the catalytic activity and selectivity of MXenes by substituting or adsorbing single metal atoms on their surfaces. DFT calculations have been used to screen for the balance between hydrogenation of *N2 to *N2H and hydrogenation of *NH2 to *NH3 for several MXene-supported single metal atom catalysts.142–144 Among 3d–5d TM-based MXene catalysts, the Ru-substituted Mo2CO2 catalyst has the lowest limiting potential of −0.46 V, making it the most efficient.145 Using Ru-doped Mo2CTx MXene, Tan and colleagues discovered that it exhibits excellent faradic efficiency and high ammonia production.146

7. MXene based CO2 reduction reaction (CRR) electrocatalysts

The release of CO2 into the atmosphere due to fossil fuel combustion has prompted global climate change.153 Attempts are underway to develop potential methods for CO2 mitigation and conversion CO2 into fuel and value-added chemicals through the electrochemical CRR using renewable electricity.154–156 In principle, through a series of proton/electron transfer steps, CO2 could be transformed into carbon-based products such as carbon monoxide (CO), formic acid (HCOOH), formaldehyde (HCHO), methanol (CH3OH), and ethylene (C2H4). The overall general equation is as follows: mCO2 + n(H+ + e−) → products + xH2O.157 MXenes as CRR electrocatalysts are still in their infancy and under investigation using DFT.158–160 The computational investigation of the CRR to generate CH4 on 19 types of M2XO2 MXenes by Handoko and coworkers159 suggested that a favorable *HCOOH pathway primarily facilitated the CRR. As a result of the O(OH) groups preferring bonding with C in CO2, the formate (HCOO) intermediate forms preferentially on bare MXenes, whereas the carboxylate (HOCO) species forms on O/OH-terminated MXenes in the first hydrogenation step. According to She et al., CO2 reduction involves H-coordinated *HCOOH species on O-terminated MXene, as illustrated in Fig. 20. The C- and H-coordination of the intermediates alternates throughout the reaction pathway, avoiding adsorption of intermediates during the reaction.159 Off late, using HCOOH as the primary carbon-based product, a peak faradaic efficiency of more than 56% on Ti2CTx and a partial current density of up to −2.5 mA cm2 on Mo2CTx have been achieved.161 Furthermore, both experimental and theoretical studies demonstrate that increasing the number of F terminations on the MXenes can result in improved CRR performance.161 However, recent investigations of supporting platinum group metal (PGM) over bulk carbides162 and nitrides163 have demonstrated synergistic effects for the CRR. CRR experiments over MXenes have only demonstrated the two-electron mechanism for the generation of HCOOH, which is a limitation. The optimization of MXene surface terminations and the inclusion of supporting metals are required for improved intermediate stability to achieve larger CO2 reduction. Besides, numerous active sites could be required for the synthesis of C2+ products; as a result, the use of MXenes in conjunction with other CRR-active materials, such as Cu, should be investigated. It is also necessary to investigate the catalytic pathways of CO2 reduction on MXenes, particularly when utilizing in situ experimental approaches. Computational calculation, combined with experimental work in photocatalytic CO2 conversion,164,165 could allow the fabrication of efficient CRR electrocatalysts based on MXene faster.

8. Summary

The electrocatalytic comparison of MXenes and composite material catalysts suggests that MXenes are catalytically superior materials with/without single-atom catalyst sites. MXene-based materials provide superior capacity to establish strong contacts with various other components. MXene's efficiency could be improved upon incorporating metals as they have better charge carrier transport characteristics that inhibit the aggregation of active materials.166–170

The importance of active site density in understanding the catalytic activity for the ORR, OER, HER, NRR, and CRR has been overlooked for a long time. The neighborhood effects between nearby active sites have to be investigated in theoretical and experimental studies. The basis of theoretical research on heterogeneous catalysis is developing an appropriate catalyst model. DFT simulations, which are increasingly widely used, are useful for investigating active sites and their electronic structures and predicting reaction pathways. DFT calculations may provide somewhat likely trends, but quantitative predictions might suffer from substantial departures from experimental observations. Techniques at higher theoretical levels, such as wave function theory embedded in density functional theory (WFT-in-DFT), periodic MP2, periodic coupled cluster methods, and more realistic hydration models, are required to address the shortcomings.

Understanding the fundamental mechanism of the HOR is critical for designing high-performance HOR/HER catalysts. The HOR follows the Tafel–Volmer or Heyrovsky Volmer process in an acidic environment. H2 is absorbed on the catalysts' surface, resulting in H2 (Had). Had is then desorbed as a proton after the release of an electron. As a result, the hydrogen binding energy (HBE) characterizes the HOR activity.171,172 The various OH functions result in various catalyst design approaches. When OH is not adsorbed, the HOR activity is exclusively influenced by HBE, as it is in acid. On the other hand, a bifunctional catalyst with both H and OH adsorption sites is required to initiate the surface reaction. Recently, various metal catalysts were investigated and it was discovered that the metal with the highest OH adsorption is better for HOR activity.173 Recently, a series of transition metal (TM) SAs of 3d, 4d, and 5d TM, supported on 2D V2C with O termination (V2CO2), were investigated to expand the application of 2DMXene material related SAs into bi-functional materials, in particular for catalyzing the HOR/ORR on an alkaline polymer electrolyte fuel cell. Higher levels of HOR activity were observed in Sc–, Ti– and V–, Cr– and Mn–V2CO2. In addition, Mn– and Sc–V2CO2 may be attractive options for high catalytic activity, stability and low cost towards bifunctional HOR/ORR catalysis.174

Limited reports are available on the use of transition metal-based electrocatalysts towards the HOR, and utilization of MXenes is still in the developing phase.175–178 Since MXenes offer many potential advantages due to their synthetic tailoring according to specific applications, significant studies have been carried out pertaining to electrochemistry such as for the ORR, HER, OER, NRR, and CRR, although little emphasis is given to HOR. HOR applications using MXenes could offer a lot of scope in material design and structural modifications.

9. Conclusion and outlook

MXene catalysts are often more effective for electrochemical reactions with multiple transmission steps of protons/electrons such as the HER, ORR, OER, NRR, and CRR. We highlight and underline new developments in MXene catalysis-based energy conversion throughout this review, including the HER, the ORR, and its kinetics and prospects for new classes of catalysts.

This review also highlighted the most significant increase in product selectivity by stabilizing intermediates via cooperation with other active catalytic species. Additionally, most recent investigations into the connection between surface termination and catalytic performance, particularly in the case of electrocatalytic processes, have been evaluated. Additionally, for different electrocatalytic processes, the latest studies on DFT simulations have been mentioned to understand better the synergistic effects of MXenes and support metals or active components.

The recent research on the design of MXene and MXene-based nanocomposites for varied applications in electrocatalysis, such as the HER, ORR, OER, NRR, and CRR, is presented in this review. MXenes are a large family of 2D nanomaterials with remarkable properties and characteristics such as high metal conductivity, adjustable morphology, band gap, carrier anisotropic mobility, hydrophilicity, and large specific surface area with high mechanical and thermal stability, key aspects to inspire researchers for the rational design of MXene-based electrocatalysts. Advances have been made to modify the improved electrocatalytic properties of MXenes via boundary state, functional group and defect state control, single-atom loading, and heterojunction building. Additionally, enchanting is a key process for surface functional group modifications that can alter the intrinsic properties of MXenes and catalytic efficacy. So, more research is required in this area to get a better outcome from MXene as a catalyst without altering its inherent properties.

To address practical challenges, the following future research focuses are recommended in MXene-based materials to achieve high efficiency in electrocatalytic applications: a) covering the MXene surface with an oxidation-resistant layer to improve their stability, b) large-scale manufacturing with high reproducibility, c) investigating the boundary state, functional group and defect state, d) efficient single-atom loading and, e) impact and investigation of the 2D material confinement effect.

To investigate the electronic and structural characteristics of the supported catalysts under real electrocatalytic reaction circumstances, in situ methods should be used. The mechanisms of HER, ORR, and OER activity in 2D MXenes are elucidated using in situ XAS. The XANES study demonstrates that the electronic structures and changes in the oxidation states of the transition metals as a function of applied potentials affect the electrochemical characteristics and catalytic activity of the nitride MXenes.179

Numerous techniques need to be investigated for the structural changes and mechanism of action of designed MXenes, including transmission electron microscopy (TEM) with electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS), X-ray diffraction (XRD), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), and synchrotron radiation (SR)-based techniques.180

Other measurement methods can be developed to study the structure of materials in a variety of fields and the mechanisms underlying energy storage and conversion, including X-ray absorption fine structure (XAFS), synchrotron X-ray diffraction (SXRD), and synchrotron X-ray diffraction (SXRD) (SXPS).181 Due to its short-range ordered sensitivity and element-specificity, XAFS excels in detecting the interaction between atoms and their surroundings in solid, liquid, and gas phases. Meanwhile, different testing techniques are appropriate for diverse objectives and material circumstances, including transmission XAFS for bulk materials, magnetic XAFS for spin research, and in situ XAFS for the dynamic mechanism study. As a result, advanced characterization and theoretical calculations are required to facilitate the rational design of MXene-based catalysts. Detailed experimental mechanistic studies are required to better understand the working principle of MXene as a catalyst. This could be accomplished via a high-throughput screening strategy for a given application.
