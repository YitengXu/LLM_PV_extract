Abstract

The modeling of organic solar cells (OSCs) can provide a roadmap for their further improvement. Many OSC models have been proposed in recent years; however, the impact of the key intermediates from photons to electricityhot charge-transfer (CT) stateson the OSC efficiency is highly ambiguous. In this study, we suggest an analytical kinetic model for OSC that considers a two-step charge generation via hot CT states. This hot kinetic model allowed us to evaluate the impact of different material parameters on the OSC performance: the driving force for charge separation, optical bandgap, charge mobility, geminate recombination rate, thermalization rate, average electronhole separation distance in the CT state, dielectric permittivity, reorganization energy and charge delocalization. In contrast to a widespread trend of lowering the material bandgap, the model predicts that this approach is only efficient along with improvement of the other material properties. The most promising ways to increase the OSC performance are decreasing the reorganization energy, i.e., an energy change accompanying CT from the donor molecule to the acceptor, increasing the dielectric permittivity and charge delocalization. The model suggests that there are no fundamental limitations that can prevent achieving the OSC efficiency above 20%.

1. Introduction

Despite great progress in recent decades, the efficiencies of organic solar cells (OSCs) are still significantly lower compared to those of inorganic solar cells. Moreover, the record efficiency curve for OSC has been saturated for the last couple of years at 13%. As a result, a key issue is whether there is a fundamental limit for the OSC efficiency that has been already achieved, or there are ways to overcome the stagnation.

Modeling of the OSC operation can shed light on this issue. The variety of approaches to OSC modeling is discussed in several reviews, e.g., ref. 1. The models are based on the kinetic approach,25 thermodynamic concepts,69 and combined or semi-empirical assumptions.911 The main feature of the thermodynamic and semi-empirical models is the concept of the (quasi) equilibrium between the photons, excitons (tightly bound electronhole pairs formed after photon absorption), and free charges (electrons and holes). Kinetic models, on the contrary, assume that the equilibrium in the OSC is broken mainly due to the irreversibility of the photoinduced charge transfer process.3 Moreover, the kinetic models can take into account non-thermalized photogenerated charges, i.e., those whose energetic distribution cannot be described by a quasi-equilibrium Fermi distribution. Therefore, in the kinetic models, the equilibrium concepts such as the generalized Planck radiation law and Fermi levels splitting are inappropriate, and the OSC operation should be described by kinetic processes such as charge generation, thermalization, and recombination. Most of the OSC models are numerical because of the complexity of the processes involved in the cell operation. Nevertheless, analytical models can be very useful since they provide an opportunity to evaluate the impacts of various parameters on the OSC characteristics and formulate straightforward guidelines for OSC improvement.

Unlike inorganic semiconductors, photon absorption in organic semiconductors generates a bound electronhole pair, an exciton, instead of a pair of free charges. In a pristine organic semiconductor, the probability of exciton dissociation to free charges is low since the electronhole binding energy is high, as compared to thermal fluctuations. To efficiently generate free charges, the photoactive layer of the OSC is usually based on a type-II heterojunction formed by two organic semiconductors with different energies of the lowest unoccupied molecular orbitals (LUMOs) and the highest occupied molecular orbitals (HOMOs). One of these semiconductors is an electron donor, and the other is an electron acceptor. An exciton can reach the donoracceptor interface, where the energy difference between the LUMOs of the donor (EdLUMO) and acceptor (EaLUMO), commonly called the driving force, E = EdLUMO EaLUMO, can facilitate electron transfer from the donor molecule to the acceptor, resulting in a charge-transfer (CT) state.1215 An analogous mechanism works in the case of exciton generation in the acceptor phase, with the driving force for hole transfer determined by the differences in the HOMO energies between the donor and acceptor.16,17 In terms of classical electrostatics, a CT state is a Coulomb-bound pair of the electron at an acceptor molecule and the hole at the adjacent donor molecule. According to quantum mechanics, the CT states are excited states of charge-transfer complexes formed by the donor and acceptor molecules.12,14,1820 The CT states can further dissociate into free charges (charge separated state, CS) so that the electron and (or) hole move(s) away from the interface. Therefore, the process of exciton dissociation into free charges in OSC can be considered within a two-step charge generation model that is sketched in Fig. 1 for a blend of conjugated polymer (typical donor) with fullerene (typical acceptor).

Several models describing the generation of free charges via CT state formation and dissociation have been proposed lately.21,22 However, until recently23 these models did not take into account a manifold of different CT states (hot) above the lowest-energy one (cold). This manifold corresponds to different excited states of the donoracceptor complex and originates from mixing of different molecular orbitals of the donor and acceptor molecules.2426 Hot CT states were observed in time-resolved spectroscopic experiments by Jailaubekov et al.27 and Chen et al.,28 and were found to cover the energy range of at least 1 eV above the cold one. It was suggested that the hot CT states dissociate much more efficiently12,29 because they are more delocalized,30 and non-equilibrium charges generated from them are more mobile3133 due to their excess energy. It is worth noting that the thermalization rate within the hot CT state manifold was found to be comparable to the charge transfer rate and makes the dissociation of the hot CT state competitive with its thermalization.27,28 Large amounts of experimental data12,29,3436 and theoretical calculations37,38 support the impact of the excess energy on charge separation, and as a result, the cold CT state was considered not essential for free charge generation in many blends.39,40 However, in other systems, the cold CT state can dissociate as efficiently as the hot ones,12,41 and Vandewal et al. recently argued that the role of the hot CT states is strongly overestimated.42 Recent theoretical studies43 have confirmed that hot CT states are necessary intermediates for charge separation in some systems and are unnecessary in others. However, the authors concentrated only on delocalization of CT states, while the increase in the diffusion coefficient (mobility) of separating non-equilibrium charges from the hot CT states was not considered. Since the role of hot and cold CT states in OSC is a subject for debate (e.g., ref. 44), their impact on the OSC performance needs appropriate modeling.

In this work, we propose an analytical OSC model suggesting the kinetic, i.e. non-equilibrium, nature of the OSC operation. We focus on the process of the free charge generation via hot CT states. To describe the formation of a CT state from the exciton, we utilize the widely used Marcus model45,46 explaining CT from a donor molecule to an acceptor. To describe the CT state dissociation into free charges, we use the OnsagerBraun model47,48 (with the corrections suggested by Wojcik and Tachiya40) considering charge motion as a classical diffusion process. We assume that the excess energy (hotness) of a CT state is important for its dissociation, and modify the OnsagerBraun model to account for the excess energy. Charge transport and collection in OSC were modeled in detail elsewhere,8,49 and we describe them in an approximate way, neglecting space-charge and contact effects to keep the model analytical. The analytical character of the model allows us to evaluate the influence of various parameters such as the driving force, optical bandgap, dielectric permittivity, electronhole separation distance in the CT state, geminate recombination, thermalization rate, reorganization energy and charge delocalization on the OSC efficiency. In the last part of the manuscript, we compare our findings with the results of other models. Our approach is applicable both for polymer and small-molecule solar cells; moreover, it can be extended to hybrid solar cells, e.g., to some types of perovskite ones.

2.2. CT state dissociation

From the relaxed CT(i) state, several decay pathways are possible: it can dissociate into free charges (CS state) with rate constant kd, recombine with rate constant krec, or relax to a CT state with lower energy via internal conversion. The CS state can be interpreted as an electronhole pair with low binding energy comparable to the thermal fluctuation energy (kT). The CT state dissociation into the CS state is modeled using the OnsagerBraun approach as diffusion of an electron in the attractive field of the hole and an external electric field F. Recently, Tachiya and Wojcik48 showed that the original Braun approximation is not mathematically correct and suggested a correction for kd. Recent electromodulated photoluminescence measurements on vacuum-evaporated films50 indicated that the TachiyaWojcik approach reproduces the experimental data very well. This model is also in excellent correspondence with the Monte-Carlo simulations.50,51

The dissociation constant is proportional to the diffusion coefficient, and the latter is related to the electron e and hole h mobilities via the EinsteinSmoluchowski relation: D = (e + h)kT/e, where e is the elementary charge. The OnsagerBraun model, as well as its WojcikTachiya variant, considers an effective homogeneous medium conducting both electrons and holes. Donoracceptor phase separation in real OSCs can facilitate the free charge yield56 because it hinders electronhole encounters and suppresses charge recombination. However, an analytical model for CT state dissociation in phase-separated blends is a challenge. We roughly evaluated the impact of phase separation in ESI, Section S2.4 and found that it significantly improves the OSC performance for the low-efficiency blends, but plays a minor role in high-efficiency blends. Moreover, in the latter the donor and acceptor phases are strongly interpenetrated resulting in a large area of the donoracceptor interface. In this case, the assumption of homogeneous effective medium seems to be reasonable, and it is frequently used in OSC modeling (e.g., ref. 5759). For instance, in ref. 42 the results of the Monte-Carlo simulation of free charge generation, which take into account phase separation, are in line with those of the WojcikTachiya approach.40 We thus conducted all the modeling under the approximation of an effective homogeneous medium.

The excess energy of a hot CT state and the charge pair generated from it decreases rapidly (within the sub-nanosecond range), due to reorganization of the environment and downhill motion within the energy landscape induced by disorder.27,33 However, we initially neglected the charge thermalization assuming D(t) = Dhot = const to simplify the model, and then will consider it in Section 2.5. Note that the efficient increase of the electronhole distance as a result of the fast motion of non-equilibrium charges was frequently accounted for in OSC models via a phenomenological parameter of thermalization length that corresponds to the average electronhole distance after loss of the excess energy.54,60,61 In contrast, we consider the motion of non-equilibrium charges explicitly, and this allows us to estimate the impact of the driving force and the reorganization energy on the OSC performance.

2.3. Charge transport

The free charges (in the CS state) formed as a result of CT state dissociation are transported to the corresponding electrodes to produce the photocurrent, J. The transport and collection of free charges in OSC have been addressed in many studies (e.g., ref. 8 and 10), and we describe them in a very approximated way in order to focus on free charge generation and keep the model analytical. Specifically, we assume that the generated free charges are uniformly distributed within the active layer resulting in a uniform electric field (no space charge and band bending). The charges are assumed to be cold and much slower than the non-equilibrium ones, since their thermalization is much faster than their transport to the electrodes. The electric field promoting the motion of separated charges is F = (EHL/e V)/d, where EHL/e is the built-in voltage (Ohmic contacts are supposed), V is the cell voltage, and d is the cell thickness. Recombination of free charges, i.e. non-geminate recombination (red arrow from SC to GS in Fig. 2), is described by the Langevin model (e.g., ref. 62) with the rate constant knon = e/(0), where 0 is the vacuum permittivity, and is the dielectric permittivity of the active layer. The impact of the reduced bimolecular recombination coefficient63 is discussed in ESI, Section S2.3, and was found not to be significant for high-efficiency OSCs. Geminate recombination is already taken into account within the OnsagerBraun model for CT state dissociation.

2.5. Thermalization

The model formulated above does not consider downward electronic transitions within the manifold of the CT states (wavy arrow in Fig. 2), energy relaxation of non-equilibrium charges (hole in the donor phase and electron in the acceptor one) and their downhill motion within the disorder-induced energy landscape.27 To take this thermalization into account, we follow the approach by Arkhipov et al.52 and assume that the energy of the electronhole pair decreases as E(t) = E0 t, where E0 is the initial energy of the charge pair, i.e. the energy of the parent CT state, and describes the thermalization rate.

The E(t) dependence implies a time-dependent charge diffusion coefficient, D(t) in eqn (2). A decrease of the diffusion coefficient (charge mobility) was experimentally observed in several studies, e.g., in ref. 3133. However, the solution of a diffusion equation with a time-dependent coefficient is far beyond the scope of the current study. Fortunately, since the characteristic time of charge thermalization, = (E0 ECT0)/, is of the order 1011109 s,33,52 a negligibly low fraction of hot charges can reach electrodes. We thus divide the CT state dissociation into two sub-stages: hot, when the charges are not in equilibrium with the environment, and cold, when the charges are already thermalized. The electron/hole diffusion during the hot sub-stage prepares the charges for separation, providing them an opportunity to become far from each other after their excess energy is lost. If the charges are distant enough, they can be readily separated even if they are cold. Ultrafast (1012 s) long-range (5 nm) charge separation was observed recently for P3HT:PCBM31 and small molecule30 bulk-heterojunctions. 3. Results

3.1. Electric field and driving force

In the following sections (3.13.3 and 3.5), we neglect the thermalization of the charge pairs during CT state dissociation. In Section 3.4, we show that including thermalization does not affect the qualitative results of the model, but somewhat lowers the OSC efficiency values. We start with the effect of the applied electric field F and the excess energy W on the generation of free charges. Fig. 3 presents the calculated free charge yields from the states with different W as a function of F. For moderate fields, up to 108 V m1, the free charge yields are relatively low, indicating that these electric fields are too weak to force dissociation of the low-energy cold CT states. These results are in line with experimental observations of nearly field-independent CT state emission lifetime60,77 and polaron yield78 in the moderate electric field.47,54 For the typical thickness of the OSC active layer (100 nm), the external electric field is about 107 V m1. Therefore, the model suggests that the impact of the applied electric field on free charge generation in OSC is not significant. In contrast to the weak impact of F, the excess energy W strongly influences . A small change in W from 0.10.2 eV, for example, results in a nearly 10-fold increase in for E < 108 V m1.

Fig. 4b illustrates the impact of the driving force E = EdLUMO EaLUMO on the OSC efficiency for Eg = 2 eV and various charge mobilities. The driving force determines the average excess energy of the hot CT states, which plays the key role in charge separation (see Fig. 3), and hence strongly affects the OSC efficiency. For low E (<0.1 eV), which corresponds to a small energy difference between the acceptor and donor LUMOs, the efficiency is low since the charges in a CT state do not have enough excess energy to escape the mutual Coulomb attraction, resulting in low and hence low J. For high E (>0.75 eV), i.e. EaLUMO far below EdLUMO, the CT state easily dissociates into free charges, due to high excess energy, but the transport of these charges to the electrodes is inefficient due to the low built-in electric field decreasing the current and thus the efficiency. 3.2. Recombination rate and charge mobility

In our kinetic model, as in the OnsagerBraun model, the geminate recombination rate krec and charge mobility are interconnected, and only their combination = /krec determines the free charge yield.47 We discuss only the impact of charge mobility on , since also affects charge transport to electrodes. The impact of krec is addressed in ESI, Section S2.2. Also, the effect of the reduced Langevin recombination rate is investigated in ESI, Section S2.3.

Organic semiconductors have relatively low charge mobilities as compared to inorganic ones, and one could expect that an increase in the charge mobility could substantially increase the OSC performance. Fig. 5 presents the efficiency plots for different values. The mobility increase from 105 to 102 cm2 (V s)1 (typical mobilities in OSC materials) results in a more than 10-fold increase in . However, further increase by two orders of magnitude, up to 1 cm2 (V s)1, results in a less pronounced increase in efficiency, and the efficiency curve (see inset in Fig. 5) saturates at 20%. Therefore, we conclude that if the charge mobility is low, its impact is strong, whereas if the charge mobility exceeds 101102 cm2 (V s)1, its impact becomes less significant. This may explain why OSCs based on some conjugated polymers or small molecules with high (designed for OFETs) do not show remarkable . Moreover, modeling performed in ref. 82 predicts that an increase in the charge mobility above 0.1 cm2 (V s)1 results in a decrease in the OSC efficiency, due to decreased VOC.

4. Discussion

4.1. Are hot CT states necessary?

The proposed hot kinetic model distinguishes two general types of OSC materials: poor organic ones with low mobility ( < 0.01 cm2 (V s)1), low dielectric permittivity ( < 5) and high reorganization energy ( > 0.4), and good (inorganic-like) ones with high mobility ( > 0.1 cm2 (V s)1), high dielectric permittivity ( > 10) and low reorganization energy ( 0). The former type can be termed as CT-state-dissociation-limited, since dissociation of the cold CT state is hard, and charge separation occurs mainly via the hot CT states. Therefore, the excess energy, which strongly facilitates CT state dissociation (Fig. 3), determines the overall performance of these OSCs. The other type of OSC materials can be termed as CT-state-generation-limited, since the cold CT state can dissociate easily, and overall performance is determined by the rate of the CT state generation, i.e., absorption and exciton diffusion. In this case, the role of the excess energy is less pronounced. For the CT-state-dissociation-limited materials, the OSC efficiency is low, but improving one of the material electronic properties (, krec, or r0) can result in the significant increase of the OSC performance as shown in Section 3. For the CT-state-generation-limited ones, the OSC efficiency is significantly higher and less dependent on moderate variation of the material properties. Thus, the model describes both types of OSCs, i.e., those where hot CT states play a dominant role and those where the cold CT state dissociates efficiently. The model confirms, in accordance with the previous works and a recent report that appeared while the present work was under review,85 that while the excess energy can force CT state dissociation, it is not always necessary for high efficiency. It is much better to achieve easy dissociation of the cold CT state and hence to decrease the energy loss.

4.2. Optimal bandgap

Since the role of the excess energy depends on the material properties, the model predicts that the optimal bandgap Eg also depends on them. For OSCs based on the state-of-the-art materials, optimal Eg is in the range 1.51.8 eV. For instance, for a donoracceptor pair with = 0.5 eV and = 103 cm2 (V s)1 (e.g., P3HT:PCBM or F8BT:PCBM), the optimal bandgap is 1.75 eV. This is in contrast to inorganic solar cells, where the optimal bandgap should be about 1.34 eV, according to the ShockleyQuiesser model.79 The higher bandgap for OSCs is associated with the losses beyond the unavoidable radiative recombination. The general trend is: the better the blend (lower and krec, and larger , r0 and ), the lower the optimal bandgap, and the closer it is to that of inorganic solar cells. For example, Fig. 57 show the decrease in the optimal bandgap with the increase in , , and decrease in , respectively. For 0.1 eV, the optimal bandgap is about 1.31.4 eV, close to that predicted for inorganic semiconductors (Fig. 7). Therefore, the model suggests that the widely used approach for Eg lowering is efficient only with the improvement of the electronic properties. For instance, novel non-fullerene acceptors possess extended conjugated systems, which are expected to result in low and a less pronounced role of the excess energy. Efficient charge separation, despite the low driving force, was indeed reported for OSC with a non-fullerene acceptor in ref. 86. Thus, the Eg decrease seems to be promising for the development of well-performing OSCs based on these materials, e.g., the efficiency of more than 10% was achieved for the acceptor with Eg = 1.24 eV.87

Our conclusion that state-of-the art OSCs should have larger bandgaps than that predicted by the ShockleyQuiesser model is in agreement with many other models.1 For instance, the kinetic model suggested in ref. 57 also considers the role of the excess energy and uses the effective temperature concept, similar to eqn (3), to account for CT state hotness. Despite several significant differences between our model and that from ref. 57, both models show that the optimal bandgap for state-of-the-art OSC is in the range 1.51.8 eV. The efficiency plot predicted by our model for moderate-efficiency blends with maximal efficiency 10% (e.g., the black curve in Fig. 7) is close to that reported in ref. 11, and both suggest the optimal bandgap of 1.92.0 eV. Importantly, several thermodynamic models,9,88,89 in spite of the completely different approach, indicate the same trend: taking into account various losses (e.g., reorganization,90 strong CT-state binding,89 recombination due to low charge mobility,9 or other type of energy loss85) results in higher optimal Eg. For instance, the model based on the detailed balance approach90 suggests that the optimal Eg increases with and is between 1.5 and 2 eV for = 0.5 eV, in accordance with our findings.

Comparison of our two-step kinetic model with the kinetic model suggested in ref. 4 (that considers charge generation as a single process described by the Marcus formula) shows that for both, the reorganization energy has a large impact on the OSC efficiency. However, our model, first, predicts the lower efficiencies, and second, advises the use of the larger bandgaps. These two features result from the additional energy losses during the CT state dissociation, considered in our model and neglected in ref. 4.

Finally, we compare the predictions of our model with the reported OSC efficiencies. Fig. 10 presents experimental power conversion efficiencies of OSCs from a number of reports plotted vs. donor Eg (points). The best cell efficiencies are observed for materials with bandgaps in the range Eg 1.51.6 eV, which are close to the optimal bandgaps predicted by our model and significantly larger than that within the ShockleyQuisser model.

4.3. Pathways to boost photovoltaic efficiency

To boost the OSC efficiency, the energy loss should be decreased, i.e., the need for excess energy should be avoided. From the model, we suggest three promising approaches: decreasing the reorganization energy, improving intermolecular charge delocalization, and increasing the dielectric permittivity. All three pathways promise the ultimate OSC efficiency above 25%.

The particular importance of the reorganization energy, , is unexpected at a glance since the CT state formation, whose rate is determined by , is much faster (kM = 10121013 s1 in typical blends20,64) than the PL rate (kPL = 1091010 s170 for typical organic donors) and does not limit the charge yield. However, the reorganization energy also determines the average excess energy of the CT states: the CT states manifold is populated according to a Gaussian-like distribution with a maximum at EmaxCT = Eg (see eqn (1)), and for smaller values, hotter CT states are populated for given Eg. Since the CT state excess energy is crucial for its dissociation (see Fig. 3), the reorganization energy becomes important. The particular role of the reorganization energy is in agreement with the results from ref. 89 and 90, where the impact of the energy loss on was addressed using the detailed balance approach, and with the data from ref. 10, where the Monte-Carlo modeling based on the Marcus model was utilized. Although in ref. 94 moderate impact of variation (two-fold change in the charge separation rate) was reported, it stems from the narrow range of considered values (0.060.12 eV). For this range, we also observe moderate variation of the cell efficiency (ca. 20%), while variation in a wider range provides more than three-fold variation of the OSC efficiency (Fig. 7).

The overall reorganization energy (sum of the values for single donor and acceptor molecules) for typical OSC materials is high because of their softness and can be roughly estimated as 0.40.6 eV.46,95 Assuming = 0.5 eV, the model predicts the limit for the OSC efficiency of about 14% (Fig. 7, green line). If = 0.3 eV is achieved, the efficiency limit will rise above 17% (Fig. 7, violet line). Accordingly, ways to decrease the reorganization energy are of great interest and worth detailed studies. The reorganization energy is commonly divided into two terms: the inner-sphere term, i, which stems from the change in the molecule conformation after the CT event, and the outer-sphere term, o, which stems from the polarization of the environment. i usually dominates (at least for non-polar organic semiconductors such as oligoacenes96,97) and can be lowered by increasing the (intramolecular) electron delocalization and the molecular rigidity.98,99 In addition, recent reports show that the non-bonding character of HOMO and/or LUMO is beneficial for small i.100,101 Relatively small i values (0.10.2 eV) were reported for P3HT,102 fullerene,103 porphyrins and phtalocyanines,104 and condensed aromatics.99,105 Notably, the intramolecular reorganization energy of the phtalocyanine molecule was measured as low as 0.037 eV.104 Graphene nanoribbons, carbon nanotubes, graphene nanosheets and quantum dots should also show small i, due to rigidity and charge delocalization.105,106 The assessment of o is not straightforward. The calculations show that it is lower than 0.01 eV for oligoacenes,96,97 and one may expect that it is also low for other organic conjugated molecules without polar groups.107 However, o increases with the polarizability (and hence )45 and can be important for organic semiconductors with high . Therefore, the impact of can be more complicated than discussed above. It is worth mentioning that small is not a panacea, and the donor/acceptor pair with small will perform well in OSC only if other material properties (, krec, etc.) are appropriate (e.g.Fig. 5). For instance, OSCs based on phtalocyanines and condensed aromatics (e.g. perylene diimide), which have low , frequently show low efficiency, most probably due to small r0 and high recombination rate in the CT states (krec).108

The second way to boost the OSC performance is to enhance charge delocalization via improved crystallinity of the donor/acceptor domains. Charge delocalization affects several material properties as discussed above, the most important being the decrease of the reorganization energy. The optical data by Vandewal et al.42 show that in well-performing blends, can be as low as 0.2 eV (the half of the difference between CT state absorption and emission). This value is significantly lower than the sum of the typical reorganization energies of donor and acceptor molecules (0.4 eV), and we attribute this decrease to intermolecular charge delocalization. This explains the enhanced OSC performance109 and the lower role of the driving force110 for the blends with crystalline donor and/or acceptor, which are in line with observations that the cold CT state can dissociate efficiently in some blends.42 The deduced impact of delocalization is also in accordance with theoretical results obtained by Nan et al.,43 where the excess energy of the CT state was shown to play a significant role in charge separation for OSCs with low crystallinity of donor/acceptor, but was found to be unimportant in the blends with enhanced crystallinity.

The third factor limiting the solar cell efficiency, namely low , was mentioned in previous studies as an important source of worse performance of organic solar cells as compared to inorganic ones;111,112 an increase in was suggested as a promising route to improving the OSC efficiency (e.g., in ref. 10). Not surprisingly, in our model, high also provides an opportunity to achieve high power conversion efficiency. Note that for materials with large , low reorganization energy is not obligatory for high OSC efficiency, since in this case even cold CT states dissociate efficiently due to decreased (screened by ) electronhole attraction. To increase , polar groups can be attached to organic semiconductor molecules; values as high as 16.7 were reported for polymer donors,10,83 5.7 for fullerene derivatives,113 and 9.8 for non-fullerene acceptors.112 However, as mentioned above, increasing increases the outer-sphere reorganization energy, 0, which can decrease the cell efficiency as shown in Section 3.4. The impact of on the solar cell performance can therefore be overestimated, and a more detailed investigation of this issue is required, but this is beyond the current study. Note that the frequency dependence of the dielectric permittivity may also play a significant role.

Perovskite structures are interesting emerging photovoltaic materials that provide power conversion efficiencies higher than 20%,114 and should certainly be considered good solar cell materials. The photophysics of these materials is under active discussion,115 and the hot kinetic model can be applicable to these materials if the photoinduced charge transfer is essential in the perovskite photophysics. Perovskites show charge mobilities above 10 cm2 (V s)1,115 which are beneficial for photovoltaics according to our model. However, we assume that the main reason for their superior performance is high > 30,116,117 which seems to be related to the organic molecules with a large dipole moment embedded in the perovskite lattice.116 Interestingly, the reorganization energy was calculated to be rather high in these materials.118 However, high reorganization energy can be overwhelmed by high as shown in Section 3.3 (see Fig. 6), since in this case the electrons and holes in the excitons (or CT state) are weakly bound and do not require excess energy to be separated.

The constructed model is first of all a guide for high-efficiency OSCs and addresses mainly the pathways to improve the OSC efficiency, rather than a detailed description of specific devices. To keep the analytic character of the model and its generality, we have to use a number of approximations and neglect factors that can be significant in some cases. Specifically, the charges are considered to be localized at molecules, and this can be generally not the case for the blends with high crystallinity of the donor and/or acceptor. While the increase of OSC efficiency due to intermolecular charge delocalization is roughly evaluated in Section 3.5, the more rigorous treatment is not straightforward.119 Further, the model could overestimate VOC and the fill factor, and hence the OSC efficiency for extremely high-performing materials with high mobility ( 1 cm2 V1 s1) and high dielectric permittivity ( > 10), since the Langevin expression for non-geminate recombination is valid for semiconductors with low charge mobility and may underestimate the recombination in high-mobility semiconductors. Neglect of charge diffusion and the assumption about the uniform distribution of charge and electric field within the active layer could also overestimate VOC and the fill factor. The current model could be improved by accounting for exciton diffusion, exciton delocalization, and possible long-range dissociation;53 there could also be explicit consideration of intermolecular charge delocalization,120,121 phase separation,56 energetic landscape and disorder,122,123 and more detailed treatment of charge transport and recombination (e.g., with a drift-diffusion model). However, all of this can hardly be done within an analytical model and numerical modeling is required, which is far beyond the current study.

4. Conclusions

The proposed hot kinetic model shows that rigid materials with low reorganization energy, high dielectric permittivity and pronounced charge delocalization are exclusively important for boosting the organic solar cell efficiency toward and above 20%. The model also predicts that for state-of-the-art OSC materials, the optimal bandgaps are in the range 1.51.8 eV, i.e., larger than that predicted by the ShockleyQuiesser model. If materials with lower reorganization energy, higher dielectric permittivity, and/or stronger intermolecular charge delocalization are developed, bandgap reduction will be a benefit.
