Abstract

Presently, supercapacitors have gained an important space in energy storage modules due to their extraordinarily high power density, although they lag behind the energy density of batteries and fuel cells. This review covers recent approaches to not only increase the power density, rate capability, cyclic stability, etc. of supercapacitors, but also to increase their energy density using hybrid architectures. Electrodes are the most important component of a supercapacitor cell, and thus this review primarily deals with the design of hybrid supercapacitor electrodes offering a high specific capacitance, together with the elucidation of the mechanisms involved therein. The electrode performance significantly depends on the available surface area, porosity and conductivity of the component materials, and thus nano-structuring of the electrode is an elegant approach, which is discussed in the subsections for 0-, 1-, 2-, 3-dimensional hybrid materials, including some miscellaneous hybrids. The fabrication of different hybrid materials using metal oxides, metal sulfides, carbon materials, etc. with conducting polymers such as polyaniline and polypyrrole and their characterization are delineated from the literature data. Here, we primarily focus on the mechanism of energy storage by non-faradic electrical double-layer capacitance and faradaic pseudo-capacitance, discussing the contributions of different component mechanisms towards the total capacitance. In the hybrids, the impact of the component concentration operating via different mechanisms for charge storage on their final electrochemical performance is discussed. The specific capacitance, volumetric capacitance, charge–discharge cycles, Ragone plot, etc. of hybrid supercapacitors are described. Besides household and heavy-duty applications, the state-of-the-art future applications of supercapacitors in robotics, renewable and sustainable energy devices, wearable and self-healing supercapacitors, and biotechnology and their challenges in real-world applications with the scope of future work are elucidated.

1. Introduction

The global energy consumption is increasing in an alarming rate together with remarkable development in electronics and of course population explosion.1,2 The energy consumption in a standard U.S. home has already doubled since 1980 (ref. 3) and the International Energy Agency (IEA) has projected that it will double again by 2022 and triple by 2030.4 This situation is becoming increasingly worse with the depleting level of stored fossil fuels.1 However, currently, the alternative avenues for energy generation from wind, solar power and other renewable resources are not good enough to cope up the ever-increasing energy demand. Furthermore, most of these energy generation/conversion routes are intermittent, and therefore to become an alternative to fossil fuels they require proper storage systems. This has triggered intensive research efforts in last three decades, which have resulted in the advent of modern electrochemical energy storage systems such as batteries and supercapacitors.5–8 Batteries have come a long way (∼2 centuries) since Volta's inventive discovery to modern day lithium-ion technology. Similarly capacitors also have traversed a long way from the discovery of electrophysical parallel plate electrolytic capacitors to the present electrochemical capacitors, ultracapacitors and supercapacitors.7 Commercial batteries can store much greater energy (energy density of ∼180 W h kg−1) compared to commercial supercapacitors (energy density of ∼10 W h kg−1).9,10 However, supercapacitors can deliver significantly higher power and exhibit much higher cycle life (in the order of millions) compared to batteries (cycle life in thousands only).10 Therefore, batteries are effective for applications requiring long-time energy supply. Conversely, a faster rate of charging/discharging enables supercapacitor applications, where a large amount of energy is required to be stored or delivered quickly. This includes energy harvesting in seaports,11 regenerative braking,12 pulse power in communication devices,13 power grids,14 application in the emergency door of airplanes (Airbus A380).10 In addition, the shorter time required for charging is convenient for applications in portable devices and hybrid vehicles.5

In battery electrodes, electrolyte ions intercalate through the channels or gaps present in the electrode material and become distributed throughout the bulk of the electrode. This is followed by faradaic charge transfer processes to generate the energy capacity (mA h kg−1). Thus, capacity development in battery electrodes, which is limited by solid-state ion diffusion, exhibits relatively poor kinetics; however, the use of the entire bulk of the electrode for charge storage leads to very high energy density.5,7 Conversely, capacitance generation in supercapacitors occurs either due to the formation of an electrical double layer at the electrode/electrolyte interface due to purely electrostatic interaction (electrical double layer capacitance, EDLC) or surface/near-surface faradaic charge transfer processes between the electrolyte ions and electrode materials (pseudocapacitance) (Scheme 1). Therefore, charge storage occurs very fast in supercapacitors (unlike battery electrodes) because they are not being limited by solid-state ion diffusion; however, due to the use of the electrode surface or near-surface only, their charge storage and energy density are rather poor. Nevertheless, supercapacitors, which are built with typically environmentally green components, exhibit very high charging/discharging rates and offer a nearly maintenance-free extraordinarily long life. These features promise their significant potential for future applications together with batteries; however, the energy density of supercapacitors needs significant improvement.

High conductivity and large exposed surface area together with desired porosity are the fundamental requirements of high-performance EDLC-based electrodes, where instantaneous polarization occurs at the electrode/electrolyte interface.10 This leads to very high rate of charging–discharging; however, given the practical aspects of electrode fabrication, it becomes difficult to maintain a high exposed electrode surface area in the packed cell configuration. Therefore, a high energy density cannot be achieved. In this respect, materials involving faradaic charge transfer processes are much more attractive given that they offer significantly higher capacity/capacitance, and hence high energy storage. However, these materials (metal oxides, phosphates, sulfides, nitrides, oxynitrides, etc.) are often associated with a phase change during charge storage/release or have poor conductivity, which affects the kinetics of the process.5,15 Increasing the conductivity of battery electrode materials by compositing with highly conducting carbon materials has already shortened the charging time in commercially available faster Li-ion batteries. Based on this, the claim of building a high energy supercapacitor without a higher charge/discharge time (in the order of 10 min or more) becomes less convincing, given that the principal difference between batteries and supercapacitors is the faster operation (charging/discharging) of the latter.16 A comparison of the specific energy density versus charging time for a commercial high-power Li+ ion battery and EDLC-based supercapacitor device is presented in Fig. 1(a). The plot shows that the specific energy density of Li+ ion battery remains constant during discharging for a timescale of >10 min; however, it sharply decreases for a shorter timescale (down to 10 s or less). The observed decrease in specific energy density on a shorter timescale is attributed to the various resistive losses originating from the sluggish ion and electronic conductivity in battery electrodes, which may create serious safety problems such as thermal runway during high-rate operation.5 Conversely, EDLC-based supercapacitors offer much inferior energy density, but it remains constant at various timescales of discharging. Thus, batteries are suitable for providing higher energy over a longer timescale. Alternatively, supercapacitors are best suited for high power applications on a lower timescale (<10 s). Pseudocapacitors are expected to provide their best performance within an interim time domain.

The high energy capacity of materials operating via surface or bulk faradaic processes is highly desirable to build high-energy devices. Therefore, the generation of hybrid materials as electrodes or fabrication of hybrid cell configurations consisting of faradaic and non-faradaic material components has become the most unique and obvious strategy for the development of high energy and high-power supercapacitor devices (i.e., going through the diagonal of the Ragone plot7 in Fig. 1(b)). A hybrid supercapacitor cell comprising two different electrodes operating via faradaic and non-faradaic processes (asymmetric electrode configuration) is a very good approach for having the combined features of high energy storage (faradaic electrode) and high rate (non-faradaic) in a single device. However, these devices often fail to reach their expected energy density level, particularly when battery electrodes are used, due to the significant mismatch in the rate of charging between non-faradaic (EDLC) and battery electrodes.7 Further advancement in this direction is the use of the double hybridization approach, i.e., using the asymmetric cell configuration, where one will be an EDLC-based electrode and the other should be and EDLC/battery or pseudocapacitor hybrid electrode (Fig. 2).7 The fabrication of a true EDLC/battery hybrid composites extracting the synergistic performance of each component should overcome the difficulty related to the possible lower rate of the faradaic component.

Accordingly, the advent of nanomaterials has been revolutionary, where besides providing increased surface area, nano-structuring of electrode materials offers decreased diffusion paths and restricts phase changes, which are conducive for achieving a high rate even in faradaic charge transfer-operated electrode materials. Furthermore, nano-structuring allows atomic-level or molecular-level connection between the components of a hybrid material, and hence, ensures exchange of their chemistry to result in improved synergistic properties. This helps the combination to behave as a truly hybrid material instead of a macroscopic composite. In the present review, we give a brief account of different hybrid supercapacitor systems consisting of nano-structured pure or hybrid materials as electrodes. A dimension-based classification of the nano-structured electrode materials is presented to clearly understand the dimensional effect on electrochemical properties of electrode materials, and hence consolidated performance of the entire supercapacitor cell. In addition, the mechanism for charge storage in different electrode types is discussed in detail. The distinctive features of battery and supercapacitor electrodes are presented clearly, which we believe should be helpful to reduce the unwarranted claims for newly developed faster battery electrodes as high energy density supercapacitor electrodes.16

2. Non-faradaic processes

The first patent based on the principle of electrochemical double layer capacitance was filed in 1957 by Becker;12 however, the concept of these capacitors was introduced much earlier by Hermann von Helmholtz in 1879.19 In the proposed model, when an electrified surface is placed in an electrolyte, an immobile oppositely charged ion layer is formed over the surface based on purely electrostatic interactions. The as-formed electrical double layer is responsible for energy storage following the principle of a typical parallel plate capacitor. In the early 1900s, Gouy and Chapman proposed the existence of a diffused layer of oppositely charged ions instead of a surface-adsorbed immobile layer, considering counter-balancing the thermal energy (diffusion) of the ions with electrostatic interactions.20–22 The ion concentration changes exponentially over the diffused double-layer thickness. However, this model considers the ions as point charges, and therefore they have no limitation in approaching the surface.23 Furthermore, this model fails in the case of a highly charged surface or high electrolyte concentration. In 1920, Stern combined these two models and proposed an electrical double layer (EDL) comprising two different layers of charges, where the first is a stationery layer of de-solvated ions adsorbed as close as possible to the surface (Stern Layer, SL) and the second is a diffused layer (DL) of solvated ions nearest to the surface.20,23 In 1957, Grahame further refined this model by dividing it into two sub-regions: (i) inner Helmholtz plane (IHP), which passes through the center of specifically adsorbed ions and (ii) outer Helmholtz plane (OHP), passing through the center of the solvated ions closest to the surface (Fig. 3).24 The double-layer capacitance as described by Helmholtz may be presented as follows:10

Organic solvents typically allow a higher operational voltage (up to 2.7 V in a symmetric electrode system)10 compared to water (maximum 1.23 V due to the decomposition of water),26 and hence organic solvent-based electrolytes or ionic liquids are attracting increasing attention for application in commercial electrochemical capacitors.10 In the EDLC, charge adsorption/desorption during the charging/discharging cycles occur on the electrode surface purely by electrostatic interactions. This results in a significantly high rate capability and cyclic stability from EDLC-based devices, with the necessity of a higher specific surface area (SSA) for greater charge storage. Carbon-based materials produced from different sources and different techniques such as activated, templated and carbide-derived carbon,27 carbon fabrics, fibers, fullerenes,28,29 nanotubes,30 nano-onions,31 and nanohorns32 have been applied as electrodes.25,33 Qiu and coworkers prepared porous carbon materials by templating with MgO or ZnO nanostructures via the carbonization of coal-tar, petroleum pitch, etc.34–37 The improved capacitive performance of electrochemical capacitors over the typical parallel plate electrolytic capacitors stem from the increased electrode surface area ‘A’ and decreased electrode surface-electrolyte ion distance ‘d’ in eqn (i). In electrochemical capacitors, ‘d’ is typically in the range of 1–10 nm.7 Therefore, an increased SSA should be the key to achieving a higher capacitance. Activated carbons have received significant attention as electrode materials due to their large SSA, abundant porosity, conductivity and moderate production cost from naturally occurring carbonaceous precursors. However, it has been observed that despite having a very high SSA (∼3000 m2 g−1), activated carbons exhibit unexpectedly poor specific capacitance (<10 μF cm−2). This indicates that besides a high SSA, an increase in capacitance also depends on other parameters such as pore size distribution, pore shape/structure, accessibility of the pores by electrolyte ions, and conductivity of the electrode.38 Accordingly, initial research in this field was devoted to developing activated carbons having increased pore volume and SSA by refining the activation process. The impact of porosity or the pore size range of the electrode materials on the capacitance or rate capability has been critically addressed in the literature. Earlier studies suggested that mesoporous surfaces having pore sizes in the range of (2–5) nm, more than twice the size of the solvated ions, are preferable for achieving optimum capacitance and rate capability.10 It was further suggested that pore sizes in the sub-nanometer range (<0.5 nm) were inaccessible to hydrated/solvated ions39,40 and pore sizes less than 1 nm were too small to be accessed by organic electrolyte ions, which typically have sizes greater than 1 nm.41 However, even after using finely tuned mesoporous carbon electrode surfaces, appreciably high specific capacitances could not be achieved (100–120 F g−1 in organic electrolyte and 150–200 F g−1 in aqueous electrolyte).42,43 On the contrary, microporous carbon surfaces resulted in an appreciable enhancement in capacitance value.44 Experiments with carbon cloths having a combination of both mesopores (2–50 nm) and micropores (<2 nm) resulted in appreciably high capacitance.45–47 An activated coal-based microporous (<1.5 nm) material resulted in a high capacitance value of 120 F g−1 or 80 F cm−3 using organic electrolytes.48–50 Carbide-derived carbon (CDC) having an exclusively microporous nature (0.6 nm to 1.1 nm) exhibited about 50–80% increase in capacitance value,51 which was stable (with respect to current density variation), compared to that of conventional activated carbons39,40,52 using organic electrolytes [1 M Et4NBF4 in acetonitrile] having sizes 1.3 and 1.16 nm for solvated cations and anions, respectively. The variation in capacitance with pore size in CDC exhibited that the capacitance value gradually decreased as the average pore size approached 1 nm. However, upon a further decrease in pore size, the capacitance value sharply increased and shows the maximum near a pore size close to the electrolyte ion size [Fig. 4(i)].51,53,54 These results definitely indicate that partial or complete removal of the solvent shell over the ions must have occurred for them to access the micropores.55 Chmiola and coworkers showed the linear dependence of change of capacitance with (1/b) (here ‘b’ is the pore radius) as the sub-nanometer pore size range is approached.51 This result indicates that as the pore size decreases, the distance between the ion and carbon surface (d) decreases, accounting for the increase in capacitance. In another very interesting study on CDC as an electrode material with a solvent-free ionic liquid [EMI+TFSI−] as the electrolyte (having an ion size of 0.7 nm for both the cation and anion), the maximum capacitance value was observed at around a pore size of 0.7 nm [Fig. 4(i)].56,57 This further ensures that micropores having sub-nanometer sizes yield the maximum capacitance when accessed by completely desolvated ions. In a very interesting work, Kaskel and coworkers reported high energy storage capacity from an asymmetric capacitor cell composed of size-selective microporous carbon (w < 0.9 nm) in one electrode and ordered mesoporous carbon (w > 2 nm) in the other.58 The microporous electrode allowed electrosorption of only the small-sized BF4− anions, excluding the large-sized tetrabutylammonium cation (TBA+) or tetrapropylammonium (TPA+) cations, while the mesoporous electrode allowed the sorption of both cations and anions. Thus, the designed asymmetric supercapacitor showed unidirectional charging with a high rectification ratio (RR = 12), representing a novel capacitor analogue of semiconductor-based diodes (“CAPode”).

Besides improving the SSA, heteroatom doping on carbon-based materials has resulted in a significant improvement in electrochemical properties, which has been ascribed to the improved electrical conductivity and increase in active sites on the carbon surface.64–71 Oxygen grafting on the carbon surface has been reported to increase the specific capacitance due to the involvement of faradaic charge transfer processes. However, this strategy is detrimental to the cyclic stability.10,64 The principal advantages of EDL-based capacitors are their high rate performance (faster charge/discharge) and tremendous cyclic stability (in the order of millions!), both of which originate from reversible electrostatic charge adsorption/desorption.10 However, despite these advantages, EDLC-based capacitors significantly lag behind batteries with respect to their energy storage ability, where energy storage occurs by faradaic (redox) electron transfer processes. Commercial EDLC-based charge storage devices offer energy densities in the range of 3–6 W h kg−1.12

3. Faradaic processes

Faradaic processes refer to an alternative mode where energy storage occurs due to charge transfer originating from redox reactions when electrolyte ions contact the electrode material at their interfaces.5,7,15,18 Therefore, this mode is fundamentally electrochemical in nature, in contrast to the electrophysical charge storage observed in electrolytic capacitors or similar mechanisms observed in the previous section.7 This mode of charge storage is typically observed in various commercialized rechargeable (secondary) metal-ion batteries. In lithium-ion (Li+) batteries, the insertion of Li+ ions in the bulk electrode materials such as Li4Ti5O12 and LiFeO4 leads to a redox reaction, and hence charge transfer.5,7,15 The charge storage in battery electrodes occur within the bulk of the electrode material instead of only the electrode surface as in double layer capacitors. This leads to remarkably higher charge storage capacity for batteries compared to double-layer capacitors. The insertion of ions within the battery electrodes generate strain, which is subsequently relieved by volume expansion to open up the crystallographic path. This is often accompanied by a crystallographic phase change in battery electrodes during the charging/discharging cycles. Therefore, ion insertion in battery electrodes is limited by semi-infinite diffusion originating from the slow solid-state diffusion of the charge-compensating ions (i.e., Li+ and Na+ ions) in the electrode material and non-martensitic phase transformation of the electrode materials.15 This has emerged as one of the principal shortcomings of battery electrodes, leading to poor charge/discharge kinetics compared to double-layer capacitors. The cyclic voltammograms of battery electrodes show well-separated (>0.1 to 0.2 V) intense peaks corresponding to the oxidation and reduction reactions, unlike double-layer capacitance, where instantaneous polarization makes the current (I) and hence capacitance (C) constant for the entire scanned voltage range [following C = (dV/dt)I].5,10,16 The phase change is further apparent from the presence of a long plateau (at the redox reaction potential) in the constant current charge/discharge experiment of battery electrodes, unlike the constantly sloping charge/discharge plot in capacitors (Fig. 7).5,15 The difference in the rate performance between battery and capacitive processes is apparent from the following relation of the current response (i) with the scan rate during CV analysis:

This further suggests the exploration of electrode materials operating via surface or bulk charge storage, not limited by the solid-state diffusion of the charge-compensating ions.

The principle of intercalation pseudocapacitance is particularly very similar to that of battery electrodes; however, in case of the former, the kinetics of ion/electron transport is much faster and not diffusion controlled, which is apparent from their proportionality with scan rate (ν). Another distinctive feature of pseudocapacitors is their constantly sloping discharge curve without the appearance of plateaus (Fig. 7(c) and (d)), which is typically observed in battery electrodes. The above discussion makes it apparent that besides using only the exposed surface area (‘outer surface’), the pseudocapacitance phenomenon may also use the ‘inner surface’, and hence the bulk electrode material, without showing diffusion rate limitations. Therefore, pseudocapacitors store much higher energy than EDLC electrodes both on account of faradaic charge transfer and that involving the bulk of the electrode material. Similarly, the absence of diffusion limitations guarantee a much-improved rate performance than batteries. The characteristic features of pseudocapacitors that distinguish from battery electrodes are as follows: (1) quasi-rectangular CV response or presence of closely separated broad oxidation and reduction peaks (surface redox); (2) the current response is proportional to the scan speed (ν); (3) steadily sloping discharge curve (instead of plateaus in batteries with little hysteresis); and (4) impedance plot with a phase angle of 90° or slight deviation (less).5,16 Understanding the nature of energy storage by a particular electrode material (either battery or pseudocapacitive electrode) is of great significance to eliminate unwarranted claims regarding the energy storage or power ability of a newly developed electrode material.16 The charge storage ability in capacitors is expressed as specific capacitance after dividing the capacitance value (expressed in Farad) by mass, area or volume of the electrode. Conversely, due to the existence of a plateau region in the discharge curve, the capacitance (ΔQ/ΔE) of battery electrodes cannot be determined in every stage. Hence, their charge storage ability is expressed as capacity (mA h g−1). The energy density in capacitors is measured using eqn (iii), and in the case of batteries, it should be done by integrating the area under the specific capacity vs. potential plot. It should be further mentioned here that the calculation of the energy density of batteries following eqn (iii) may involve up to an order of error.91 To achieve an acceptable cycle life, relatively thick electrodes with a high mass loading should be used during the fabrication of supercapacitor cells. However, this may deplete the conductivity and affect the nano-structuring of the electrode materials due to agglomeration. In this case, the application of a lower scan speed may result in a higher specific capacitance and higher energy density. However, these claims may be inappropriate given that supercapacitor devices are particularly designed to have rapid charging (within minutes if not seconds)/discharging rates. The relevance of this comes from the fact that modern advanced lithium batteries may be charged in just over 10 min.5,7,16,91

Conductivity, porosity and nanostructure are three very important features, irrespective of faradaic or non-faradaic mode of electrode operation. Improved electrode conductivity helps instantaneous polarization across the electrode surface, which permits greater access of the electrolyte ions within a short time, resulting in an improved energy and power density together with a rectangular CV curve in non-faradaic processes. Similarly, higher electrode conductivity results in improved conduction of the transferred charges during faradaic processes. This is particularly significant during pseudocapacitive processes, which show electron transfer to the surface or near-surface active centers by the electrolyte ions. In the case of high electrode conductivity, both the surface and near-surface charge storage should be sufficiently fast. The poor electrical conductivity of MnO2 (depending on its crystal structure, the conductivity of MnO2 ranges from 10−7 to 10−3 S cm−1)5,76 restricts the charge storage to only a thin layer at the electrode/electrolyte interface. Thus, the gravimetric specific capacitance of MnO2 or Mn3O4 (spinel structure) electrodes show a much lower value than the theoretically predicted specific capacitance when the electrodes are fabricated with a practically usable thickness.92,93 This limits the emergence of MnO2-based electrodes as a viable alternative to the appreciably conducting RuO2 (conductivity 104 S cm−1 for bulk single crystal)5-based electrodes, despite the much lower cost and environmentally benign nature of the former. Incorporating MnO2 into a graphene scaffold or compositing with highly conducting CNTs improves the electrode conductivity; however, despite the improvement in rate capability, there is a decrease in the gravimetric capacitance due to the mass of the carbon materials used.94 faradaic charge transfer processes benefit from electrode porosity in two ways: (1) greater access of the charge transfer active sites and (2) easier ion conduction through the electrode. Ghodbane and coworkers demonstrated the impact of wide and interconnected crystallographic pathways using MnO2 electrodes with various dimensions (1D, 2D and 3D).76 Their work also indicated that an increase in the BET surface area of the electrode has a marginal effect on its charge storage properties, whereas ion conduction exerts a very important effect. In the very interesting and detailed work by Brezesinski, Dunn, Tolbert and coworkers, they demonstrated that the development of porosity results in the development of significant electrochemical properties in otherwise poor pseudocapacitive ceria (CeO2) electrodes.95 In their work, mesoporous CeO2 with crystalline pore walls was generated by polymer templating with poly(ethylene-co-butylene)-b-poly(ethylene oxide) (also known as KLE). Generally, when an electrode material is brought into contact with an electrolyte, the following phenomenon may occur: (a) the electrolyte may adsorb electrostatically on the electrode surface, (b) ions may react chemically with the active sites in the bulk electrode with concomitant electron transfer after intercalation into the channels or interlayer spaces, which may or may not be associated with a phase change (as in battery or intercalation pseudocapacitive materials), and (c) ions may be adsorbed electrochemically on the electrode surface, accompanied by charge transfer (surface redox processes in pseudocapacitors). Thus, energy storage by a particular electrode material usually involves of the simultaneous operation of different charge storage mechanisms. For example, conducting polymer electrodes with a large surface area show a combination of both double-layer capacitance and pseudocapacitance. Similarly, heteroatom-doped or functionalized carbon materials usually show improved specific capacitance due to the simultaneous operation of charge transfer capacitance (by the functional groups or heteroatom centers) and double-layer capacitance.26 Therefore, it is very important to analyze the various charge transfer contributions present in single-component or multi-component (composite) electrode materials for better understanding of the charge storage mechanism, and subsequent application of this knowledge during electrode fabrication. According to Conway,96 the current response including both diffusion-controlled and non-diffusion-controlled processes may be represented as follows:

 
i(V) = k1ν + k2ν½(xi)
where k1 and k2 represent the contributions from the capacitive and diffusion-controlled components. Trasatti and coworkers97 reported the relation of total capacitance with the contribution to surface and diffusion-controlled processes as follows:
 
Q = Qν=infinity + constant (ν−½)(xii)

The capacitance originating from surface processes should be independent of the sweep rate, whereas the diffusion-controlled capacitance should vary with ν−½. This analysis was then applied to estimate the capacitance contribution of a thin NiCo2O4 coating on Ti with an interlayer of RuO2. The capacitance originating from the surface processes could be found from the Y-axis intercept of the total capacitance vs. ν−½ plot. The result showed about 62% contribution from the surface processes towards the total capacitance of this electrode material.98 The surface processes here refer to the complete outer surface charge storage processes originating from non-faradaic double layer capacitance and surface redox processes.

To further separate the total capacitance into double-layer and pseudocapacitance components, impedance analysis is of significant value. The distinction between these components may be achieved by analyzing the impedance data over a wide range of frequencies [0.1 Hz to 100 kHz] and then fitting the data with the appropriate equivalent circuit model. A vertical line (phase angle 90°) in the lower frequency region of the Nyquist plot usually refers to ideal capacitive behavior, which can be well fitted in a circuit model for simple capacitors.5 However, for pseudocapacitors, the plot often deviates from a vertical nature, and a phase angle of less than 90° is often encountered. In this case, fitting of the data in a circuit model with a constant phase element (ZCPE) is better than fitting in a simple capacitor model. ZCPE may be represented by the following equation:

 
ZCPE = [B(iω)n]−1(xiii)
where B and n (0 < n < 1) are the frequency independent proportionality constants,99ω is the frequency, and n tends to 1 for ideal capacitors and shows a value of 0.5 for diffusion-controlled processes.100 Brezesinski, Dunn, Tolbert and coworkers successfully deconvoluted various charge storage components in mesoporous CeO2 in a non-aqueous electrolyte, i.e., LiClO4 in propylene carbonate solvent.95 The sweep rate-dependent charge storage analysis from CV indicated a uniform contribution from the capacitive processes, irrespective of the sweep rate (0.5 mV s−1 or 10 mV s−1). However, charge storage by intercalation processes shows a significant sweep rate dependence, having a much higher value at a lower sweep rate. Here, it may be noted that charge storage capacitive processes include both the effects of the electrostatic double layer and surface redox processes. Conversely, the impedance result for the same cell configuration may be best fitted with the circuit model introducing a constant phase element. Here, the non-faradaic current is parallel with the charge transfer resistance (RF), which is again in series with the current due to faradaic charge transfer processes. This analysis separately gives the capacitance due to double layer and pseudocapacitance, where the latter shows significant voltage dependence for mesoporous CeO2 electrodes (Fig. 10). The major contributor in the total capacitance is the pseudocapacitance, and the latter shows significant voltage dependence up to 2.5 V compared to double-layer capacitance. This analysis led them to conclude that the development of mesoporosity in CeO2 electrodes generates significant intercalation pseudocapacitance, resulting in a higher total specific capacitance compared to non-porous CeO2, which gives a much poorer pseudocapacitive response. A very interesting comparison of the impedance analysis between mesoporous CeO2 and hydrous RuO2 shows almost comparable vertical lines in the Nyquist plot for the latter in the entire voltage region of 0.2 V to 1.2 V.5 This voltage independent impedance behavior of hydrous RuO2 electrodes indicates the operation of entirely surface charge storage processes (both faradaic and non-faradaic) instead of intercalation charge storage.

3.1 Nanostructured electrodes

Nano-structuring of electrode materials is gradually increasing with improved significance in the case of both batteries and supercapacitors. All the desirable features such as energy storage, power density and cyclic stability of energy storage systems (such as batteries and supercapacitors) benefit from nano-structuring. The reduced domain sizes in nano-structured electrodes help increase the surface area, increase the access of the electrode surface by electrolyte ions, reduce the diffusion length, and hence increase the capacitance even under a high applied potential, i.e., increased rate capability and shorter diffusion length enhance the electrode surface utilization, thus reducing cyclic degradation.101,102 The use of nano-structured and highly conducting carbon-based electrodes (hybrid systems) has already begun in commercial lithium-ion batteries to increase their charge/discharge rate, while retaining high energy density.10 Nano-structuring blurs the distinction in the operation of batteries and pseudocapacitor electrodes. The above discussion makes it apparent that the main distinction between pseudocapacitive and battery electrodes lies in their rate performance. The slower charge/discharge rate in battery electrodes originate from the sluggish solid-state ion diffusion within the electrodes and associated phase change. In contrast, nano-structuring of battery electrodes increases the surface redox processes and also suppresses the phase change phenomenon.5 Therefore, nano-structured battery electrodes become operationally similar to pseudocapacitive electrodes. Simon and Dunn gave the name ‘extrinsic’ pseudocapacitors to battery electrode materials that behave as pseudocapacitors upon nano-structuring. The LiCoO2 electrode is an example of an extrinsic pseudocapacitor, where the battery-like plateau region in the charge/discharge plot gradually disappears upon a decrease in domain size, and finally results in a constantly sloping curve (typical of capacitive charge storage) when the domain size becomes 6 nm (Fig. 11).103 The improved kinetics of charge storage/delivery may also be apparent with an increase in the ‘b’ value in eqn (viii) upon nano-structuring, which finally tends towards b = 1. However, care should be exercised that only based on the value of ‘b’ (i.e., b = 1), but also one should not be convinced of the pseudocapacitive nature of a typical battery electrode material unless other characteristic capacitive features are not met.16 In this regard, it may be mentioned that lithium ion insertion in the Li4Ti5O12 electrode to fully lithiated Li7Ti5O12 suffers very little electrode volume change (∼0.2%), and thus any strain, exhibiting an improved rate of ion diffusion. Despite the rate improvement, the other features of this electrode are similar to that of the battery electrode.5 Therefore, an increase in the value of ‘b’ for improved rate battery materials should be considered as an effect.18 Conversely, ‘intrinsic’ pseudocapacitors retain their characteristic features irrespective of their size in the bulk state and nano-structured state. A few examples of intrinsic pseudocapacitors are MnO2, RuO2 and Nb2O5 electrodes.5 Nano-structuring of EDLC or intrinsically pseudocapacitive electrode materials is very effective considering the increase in surface area and decrease in diffusion path, which boost both the energy storage and rate capability of the electrodes. Particularly, in pseudocapacitance, which is a surface/near-surface phenomenon, nano-structuring helps to use the materials underneath the near-surface domain, and hence increases the energy storage along with power density. This may be observed is Fig. 7(c), where upon going to the nano domains from the bulk stage, the charge storage efficiency of the pseudocapacitive electrode materials increases. However, it is notable that although a significant improvement in the rate performance occurs, the coulombic efficiency decreases in the case of battery electrodes after nano-structuring (Fig. 7(d)). Therefore, unique strategies need to be designed for the kinetic improvement of battery electrodes retaining their intercalation bulk storage. A possible strategy is to widen the crystallographic pathways for ion transport, increasing the conductivity of the intercalation channels by conductive carbon or metal coating or formation of a composite with highly conducting EDLC or pseudocapacitive (surface active) materials.

4. Hybrid electrochemical capacitors (HEC)

Hybridization of different types of materials is archetypical of materials science; however, it should also be noted that the hybridization of materials should allow proper exchange of the chemistries between various components rather than a macroscopic mixture. Therefore, prudent design of the nanostructure or microstructure of various components should be done to extract their optimum performance. Electrochemical capacitors having different electrode configurations with respect to mass, volume and material type are called asymmetric electrode systems, which are also a type of hybrid capacitor.15 Hybrid electrochemical capacitors having individual electrodes following different energy storage mechanisms such as EDLC//pseudocapacitive, EDLC//battery electrode are very effective with improved electrochemical performances given that these devices allow the realization of improved kinetics of EDLC materials, in addition to improved energy storage performance of pseudocapacitive or battery electrode materials.6,7 Representative CV and GCD plots of similar hybrid electrochemical capacitors are presented in Fig. 12, where the pseudocapacitive WO3 electrode against KI-reduced GO (capacitive) (Fig. 12(a)–(c))105 and Fe3O4–C battery electrode against capacitive CNT electrode (Fig. 12(d)–(f))106 are used. Asymmetric electrode devices are also attractive for having an increased operational cell voltage, combining the different voltage windows of the component electrodes (Fig. 12), and minimized self-discharging of the capacitor cell. The potential window of an asymmetric supercapacitor is given by the equation E = E0 + ΔE1 + ΔE2 = 1/F(ωβ − ωα)NA + ΔE1 + ΔE2, where ωα and ωβ are the work functions of the positive and negative electrodes, respectively, NA is Avogadro's number, F stands for 1 Faraday (96485 coulomb), and ΔE1 and ΔE2 are the surface dipole potentials of the positive and negative electrodes, respectively. Therefore, the selection of a proper electrode material in an asymmetric cell having the largest difference in work function (typically found in metal oxide electrodes) and proper charge balance of the positive and negative electrodes should allow extension of the voltage window.16,107 The electrolyte used poses another concern in achieving extended cell potential considering its electro-degradation. Aqueous electrolytes are desirable considering their low cost, high specific heat, high conductivity and environmentally benign character compared to organic- and ionic liquid-based electrolytes having associated environmental concerns and viscosity issues restricting ionic mobility despite offering much higher potential windows (∼3.6–4 V). The major difficulty associated with aqueous-based electrolytes is that water splits into oxygen (O2) at roughly 1 V (vs. Ag/AgCl electrode or 1.23 V vs. SHE)26 and hydrogen (H2) at ∼−0.3 V (vs. Ag/AgCl or −0.08 V vs. SHE). Therefore, only a limited potential range (0–1.2 V) can be employed for the operation of aqueous-based electrolyte supercapacitors.108 This problem may be alleviated by the suitable choice of electrodes having an overpotential for the evolution of either or both oxygen and hydrogen gases in an asymmetric supercapacitor cell.10,107,108 For example, the classic lead–acid battery, which works in a voltage window of ∼2.2 V in concentrated H2SO4 or supercapacitor cell having a carbon-based negative electrode and MnO2-based positive electrode in neutral aqueous Na2SO4 operating in the potential window of ∼2 V.108Table 1 summarizes the examples of some other faradaic material-based electrodes showing a similar water decomposition overpotential in the asymmetric cell configuration together with their improved electrochemical performances.

In addition to the two electrodes, the electrolyte residing inside the separator and inside the electrode material layers is the most important electrochemical cell (ES) component. The specific requirements for an electrolyte are wide voltage window, high electrochemical stability, high ionic concentration, low solvated ionic radius, low resistivity, low volatility, low viscosity, low cost, low toxicity, and accessibility at high purity.102 These electrolytes can be classified into three categories as follows: (i) aqueous electrolyte, (ii) organic electrolyte, and (iii) ionic liquids (ILs). An important disadvantage of aqueous electrolytes is their small voltage window, e.g., ∼1.2 V, which is much lower than that of organic electrolytes, e.g., ∼3.5 V. ILs can exist in liquid form at desired temperatures having low vapor pressure, high chemical and thermal stability, low flammability, conductivity of ∼10 mS cm−1, and wide voltage window of ∼4.5 V. Here it is necessary to mention that the use of a gel/polymer electrolyte eliminates the necessity for a separator and enhances the mechanical properties of the device. The chemical inertness and compatibility of the electrolytes with the electrodes are also important characteristics for developing efficient electrochemical cells.119

Besides the necessity of the increased cell operating voltage, the other concern associated with electrochemical capacitors is their self-discharging property, which severely limits their electrochemical performance.120 Self-discharging refers to the spontaneous loss of cell potential (and hence stored energy) over a period when kept in open circuit condition.121,122 There are three possible mechanisms proposed to account for self-discharging in supercapacitor devices as follows: (1) ohmic leakage, which takes place through the internal resistance present in the device, (2) charge redistribution through the diffusion of ions observed at the electrode surface, and (3) faradaic charge transfer reactions caused by local over charging or due to impurities.122,123 Several methods have been used to suppress the self-discharging of supercapacitors, which include tailoring of the electrode,124 introducing additives to the electrolyte125–127 and using ion-exchange membranes or chemically active separation materials, which can trap impurities.123,126 The rate of self-charging in batteries is much lower than that in supercapacitors, where in the case of the former, ions are intercalated within the bulk of the electrode materials, whereas in the latter, electrostatically surface-adsorbed ions can redistribute via diffusion. Therefore, as expected, in asymmetric supercapacitors utilizing intercalation or conversion electrodes, the problem of self-discharging should be minimized. A remarkable development in this respect was achieved by Zhi and coworkers in the form of a Zn-ion hybrid capacitor, where the Zn metal was typically used as the anode and MXene (Ti3C2) or TiN as the cathode (Fig. 13).128,129 In this regard, they proposed a very interesting ‘static electricity-immune mechanism’, which refers to breaking the electrostatic adsorption, i.e., the conversion of Zn2+ ions into electroneutral Zn atoms, and subsequently is anchors tightly on the anodic surface during charging, which will not transfer to the cathodic region under the open circuit state due to the ‘broken’ electrostatic adsorption imposed by SO42− ions (Fig. 13(b)).128 Thus, the mechanism proposed warrants general implication for other similar hybrid capacitors having different metal anodes. In another very interesting report, they demonstrated the role of the anion (SO42−) in improving the electrochemical and anti-self-discharging property employing TiN as the cathode.129 Here they have proposed a double-lock mechanism over charge redistribution between the electrodes given that Zn would not spontaneously convert to Zn2+ ions (anodic lock) and the strong adsorption interaction between the TiN and SO42− ions imposes another lock (cathodic lock) to inhibit the self-diffusion behaviour of the anions towards the anodic region under open circuit condition (Fig. 13(c) and (d)). Therefore, a balance in charge storage/release kinetics of two different electrodes is desirable. In this case, the best strategy should be to use an EDLC and battery electrode material hybrid (composite) in a single electrode against an EDLC-based counter electrode (i.e., a double hybrid system) (Fig. 2).7 However, to realize full utilization of this strategy, the composite components should have proper contact, otherwise during charging, due to the faster kinetics of the EDLC component, the potential will rise fast, and thus the relatively sluggish faradaic charge storage component would not be able to use its full charge storage capacity. Nano-structuring of composite components may be very effective in this case. In a well-connected composite electrode containing EDLC and battery materials, in the initial stage, charge storage should occur in the EDLC component until the redox potential for the faradaic component is reached, and then charge storage of the battery electrode material should occur, maintaining a steady potential. After the charge storage capacity of the battery material is exhausted, charging of the EDLC component should occur again to reach the fully charged cell potential.7 Therefore, it should be commented here that composite electrodes having an EDLC component with faradaic component in one electrode should be more effective compared to another EDLC electrode during cell construction [Fig. 14(ii)].136 This was well demonstrated by Naoi and coworkers, where an Li4Ti5O12 nanoparticle-anchored carbon nanofiber (CNF) electrode137 against an activated carbon electrode (double hybridization approach) resulted in extraordinary energy storage capacity at very high rates.138 The conductivity of the non-faradaic components above their redox potential becomes very poor;95 therefore, it is apparent from the above discussion that in composites of only non-faradaic components, complete charging of the various components may not be possible. Simultaneously, the issue of reduced conductivity for any of the composite components may also hamper the rate performance of the device, particularly in the initial stages of discharging. Furthermore, in nano-structured hybrid electrode materials composed of EDLC and faradaic components, electrostatically stored energy in the EDLC component should enhance the electron transfer in the faradaic component. Thus, the synergistic effect between the composite components would help enhance the electron transfer reaction at higher rates in the faradaic component compared to purely faradaic electrodes (battery or pseudocapacitor electrodes individually).

4.1 0D nano structured materials

0D nano-structures refer particularly to spherical particles (solid or hollow) having dimensions constrained to the nanoscale (1–100 nm). 0D nano-structures offer several advantages, which originate from their constrained nature and spherical shapes, for example, activated carbon (AC) shows a large surface area (up to 3000 m2 g−1), wide pore size range (micro, meso and macropores), low density, high surface to volume ratio (in hollow 0D nano structures), shortened pathways for mass and charge transportation, and of course, low degree of agglomeration.6 Carbon materials and transition metal oxides (oxides of Mn, Ni, and Fe) have been widely fabricated as 0D nano-structured electrode materials. A fascinating example is the nanoporous Au/MnO2 supercapacitor electrode, which was fabricated by growing nano-crystalline MnO2 grains (∼5 nm) within the nanopores created by de-alloying Ag65Au35 using HNO3. The remarkably high specific capacitance of ∼1145 F g−1 at 50 mV s−1 was attributed to the faradaic reactions by the MnO2 nanoparticles, where nano-porous gold contributed via improved mass and charge transport.145 Double-shelled NiO nanospheres exhibited a remarkably high specific capacitance of 612.5 F g−1 [Fig. 15(i)].146 The core shell 0D nano-structures offer considerable advantages of enhanced electrical conductivity, low agglomeration, robust chemical and mechanical stability, etc.147–150 Zhao and coworkers coated PANI on hollow carbon spheres, which showed an improved specific capacitance of 525 F g−1 with the optimized PANI content [Fig. 15(ii)].147 They reported the formation of an MnO2 shell over graphitic hollow carbon spheres prepared by heating graphitic hollow carbon spheres with KMnO4 solution at 70 °C. The synthesized core–shell nanostructure electrode exhibited a specific capacitance of 190 F g−1 at 0.1 A g−1 with 64 wt% of MnO2 [Fig. 15(ii)].148 Chatterjee and coworkers reported the synthesis of covalently attached core–shell carbon nanodot@PANI particles, where a uniform and nanostructured coating of PANI chains was created using oligomer-initiated controlled polymerization (OICP) of aniline on the surface of carbon nanodots [Fig. 15(iii)].151 The covalently attached nanocomposite exhibited a very high specific capacitance of 945 F g−1 with cyclic stability of 92% after 2000 charging/discharging cycles. The observed improved electrochemical properties were attributed to the non-agglomerated discrete core–shell morphology and covalently attached PANI chains on the CND surface. 4.2 1D nanostructured materials

1D nanostructured materials (both homo- and hetero-structures) have been extensively applied as supercapacitor electrode materials due to their superior chemical and physical properties.158–163 The longitudinal axis of these materials is beneficial for mass and charge transport, and furthermore, they retain the benefits of 0D nanostructures in two nanoscale dimensions. Nano-rods/nano-pillars, nano-wires/nano-fibers, nano-ribbons and nano-tubes (hollow) are examples of some 1D nanostructured materials. Different template-assisted and template-free methods such as hydrothermal synthesis, chemical vapor deposition (CVD) and electrodeposition have been employed for the synthesis of these materials.6 The excellent electrochemical properties of these materials originate from their distinct morphological characteristics, for example, nano-wires with high aspect ratio (length/diameter) possess a very high surface area. Conversely, nano rods/nano pillars possess a relatively poor surface area (aspect ratio < 10), but vertically aligned nano-pillars on conducting surfaces show lower chances of collapse on neighboring nanostructures, and hence, allow better percolation of ions, which increases the access of the electrode surface by ions. Thomas and coworkers reported a very interesting template-assisted synthetic strategy for the fabrication of well-ordered carbon nanopillars.164 They used a less time-consuming and cost-effective spin on nanoimprinting (SNAP) technique, where a solution of polyacrylonitrile (PAN) was initially fabricated into arrays of nanopillars after curing in a template and peeling off the polymer from the template. In the subsequent stage, stabilization and carbonization resulted in well-ordered carbon nano-pillars. In the beautiful nanostructure (Fig. 16) of carbon nano-pillar arrays, the height and diameter of the individual nano-pillars were ∼95 nm and ∼200 nm, respectively. The remarkable enhancement in the specific capacitance of the nano pillar arrays (3.4 mF cm−2) in comparison to only ∼0.05 mF cm−2 for a planar carbon film showed the effectiveness of the nano-structured morphology. It is worth mentioning that the benefit of these developed nanostructures is not only restricted to the improved electrochemical properties of the homo-structures, given that further improvement may be achieved by coating these nanostructures with suitable pseudocapacitive or battery electrode materials. A similar approach was reported by Thomas and coworkers, where AuPd sputtered on SNAP generated PAN nano-pillars, on which MnO2 was conformally coated to have AuPd@MnO2 core–shell nano-pillars, exhibiting a much-improved specific capacitance (603 F g−1) with more than two-fold increase compared to that of the AuPd–MnO2 planer electrode.165 Nanowires with a high aspect ratio offer a large surface area for charge storage and efficient pathway for charge transportation. Li and coworkers synthesized PANI nanowires via electrodeposition on an anodized alumina oxide (AAO) template.166 The synthesized PANI nanowires exhibited a very high specific capacitance of 1142 F g−1 at a current density of 5 A g−1, with 95% cyclic stability (500 cycles). Nanotubes offer several advantages over solid 1D nano-structures considering their increased surface area and low mass utilization, which normally results in the production of a high gravimetric capacitance. Furthermore, conduction in nanotubes occur via ballistic transport, which means charge carriers get a high mean free path without scattering. Therefore, no Joule heating takes place, which is a genuine problem in other conductors.8 Nanotube-based core–shell hetero-structures have been widely used as electrode materials. Currently, among the different combinations of core–shell hetero-structures: (1) EDLC@pseudocapacitor and (2) pseudocapacitor@pseudocapacitor have attracted major attention.6,7 The EDLC core offers several advantages in these materials owing to its high cyclic stability, and hence robust backbone structure. Also, the high conductivity of EDLC materials offers better electronic conduction, in addition to the high specific capacitance of the pseudocapacitive shell materials, which may offset the poor specific capacitance of the EDLC backbone, hence giving appreciably high energy storage by the heterostructure electrode. Conversely, pseudocapacitive@pseudocapacitive core–shell materials offer a significantly high specific capacitance. CNTs have been mostly used as the EDLC core material in the type (1) heterostructure. CNT@PPy-MnO2, CNT@PEDOT–MnO2 and CNT@PANI–MnO2 with moderate to appreciably high specific capacitance were well reported in the literature by Zhai and coworkers.170–172 Ajayan and coworkers reported that CNT@MnO2 showed appreciable improvement in specific capacitance (∼2 times) compared to pure MnO2 and remarkable mechanical and cyclic stability even after 1000 cycles, indicating its robust nature.173,174

Similarly, the reported type-2 heterostructures include Co3O4@MnO2,175 CoO@NiHON,176 Co3O4@NiO,177 CoO@TiO2,178 and Co3O4@PEDOT–MnO2 (ref. 179) as electrodes. Evidently, the combinations using pseudocapacitor/pseudocapacitor or pseudocapacitor/battery electrodes result in appreciably high charge storage. Liu and coworkers reported an improved specific capacitance of ∼1500 F g−1 with the rate performance of ∼67% retention at 90 mA cm−2 using an NiCo2O4@MnO2 electrode.180 This enhancement is apparently due to the NiCo2O4 battery electrode material and the rate performance contributed from the synergistic effect of the pseudocapacitive MnO2 shell. Metal oxide electrodes such as V2O5, MnO2, and Co3O4 suffer from the problem of dissolution in the electrolyte solution, which gradually erodes the electrochemically active electrode material, resulting in poor cyclic and mechanical stability of the electrodes. Thus, to alleviate this problem, a conductive polymer coating over the metal oxide electrodes is very effective. The conductive polymer shell here plays a dual role as follows: (1) contributes to the electrochemical performance of the electrode by redox charge transfer reactions and increased conductivity and (2) protects the metal oxide electrode materials from dissolution/de-lamination. Wu and coworkers, in a very interesting work, prepared a V2O5@PPy core–shell nano structured electrode by coating PPy over V2O5 nanoribbons. Subsequently, they fabricated a hybrid supercapacitor cell with the V2O5@PPy anode against activated carbon (AC), which demonstrated only 5% capacitance fading after 10000 cycles compared to that of 17.5% under similar conditions using the V2O5 electrode without PPy coating.181 There are many reports in which conducting polymers other than PPy were also used for the same purpose such as MnO2@PEDOT–PSS,182,183 Co3O4@PANI,184 and V2O5@PEDOT–MnO2.185 The combination of 0D and 1D nanomaterials has also attracted significant attention as supercapacitor electrode materials. Gu and coworkers reported the synthesis of a composite of vertically aligned CNT arrays with MnO2 nanoflowers via electrochemical deposition. The MnO2/CNT nanocomposite showed an appreciably high specific capacitance of 199 F g−1 with only 3% capacitance fading after 20000 cycles.186 Lee and coworkers loaded well-dispersed MnO2 nanoparticles onto PEDOT nanowires.187 The MnO2/PEDOT composite exhibited a specific capacitance of ∼250 F g−1 and a remarkable rate capability of ∼80% from 5 to 25 mA cm−2. Now, among the carbon-based 0D nanomaterials, GQDs exhibit novel physical properties, including 3D nanoconfinement, good carrier mobility, and electrical conductivity, which are beneficial for energy storage-related applications. Similarly, abundant edge defects or opportunity for better surface grafting are beneficial during the formation of heterostructures. Yan and coworkers reported the fabrication of a hybrid (asymmetric) micro-supercapacitor with improved charge storage and rate capability using graphene quantum dots (GQDs) as the negative electrode and PANI fibers as the positive electrode. The same electrode combination was also used for the fabrication of an all-solid-state asymmetric supercapacitor containing H3PO4–PVA gel electrolyte, which exhibited desirable electrochemical properties.188 In another very interesting work, Malik and coworkers reported the synthesis of a GQD/PANI nanocomposite electrode, where GQDs were used as a dopant during the oxidative polymerization of aniline (Fig. 17(a)).189 The in situ-formed nanocomposite material exhibited beautiful PANI hollow nanofibers (nanotubes) having a distinct inner and outer diameter (Fig. 17(b) and (c)). The synthesized nanocomposite material exhibited an excellent specific capacitance of ∼1044 F g−1 with ∼20% capacitance fading after 3000 capacitive charge/discharge cycles. Tang and coworkers synthesized an Ni(OH)2/AC/CNT composite via a microwave-assisted method and used this as a positive electrode in front of an AC negative electrode, which allowed a wide potential window of 1.6 V, resulting in an appreciably high energy density of 32.3 W h kg−1 at a power density of 504.8 W kg−1 with 83.5% capacitance retention after 1000 cycles.190 Similarly, Das and coworkers191 established an asymmetric supercapacitor device with an energy density as high as 85.7 W h kg−1 at a power density of 11.2 kW kg−1 employing NiO–CNT as the positive electrodes and AC as the negative electrode. The utility of CNT-based supercapacitors is further apparent from the exceptionally high energy and power density reported in some systems. Shao-Horn and coworkers reported oxidized CNT as an electrode material, achieving an energy density of ∼200 W h kg−1 and power density of ∼10 kW kg−1.192 In another report, a CNT/PANI free-standing electrode delivered high energy and power density values of 131 W h kg−1 and 62.5 kW kg−1, respectively.193

4.3 2D nanostructured materials

Improved rate capability and cyclic stability, which are the signature electrochemical properties of supercapacitors, require a decrease in diffusion length, increased conductivity of the electrode material and increased surface area. These properties also increase the usage of the electrode surface, which is responsible for an improvement in energy storage property. The requirement of an increased electrode–electrolyte contact area for improved supercapacitor electrode performance is likely to be best satisfied with 2D nanomaterials, which offer only surface area (i.e., no bulk volume). Graphene nanosheets (GNSs) have been the most successful carbon-based 2D nanomaterial owing to their high theoretical surface area (∼2630 m2 g−1), conductivity, high charge carrier mobility, and mechanical and chemical stability.6 Furthermore, graphene sheets possess intrinsically structured accessible pores and interlayer spaces, which allow better accessibility to ions.194,195 The use of transition metal oxides and chalcogenides such as RuO2, MnO2, NiO, Co2O3, V2O5, MoO2, MoS2, and WS2 in the form of 2D electrodes is the main focus for the development of high-performance hybrid supercapacitors. Kang and coworkers demonstrated the suitability of 2D MnO2 (2 nm thick) as a highly stable cathode, which exhibited a specific capacitance as high as 774 F g−1 for up to 10000 cycles196 in Ca(NO3)2–SiO2 gel electrolyte. Similarly, RuO2 thin film electrodes showed a high specific capacitance of 730 F g−1.197,198 In a very interesting report, Dai and coworkers synthesized a capacitor-like RuO2-graphene electrode and battery-like Ni(OH)2 nanoplate attached to graphene sheets as electrodes. Subsequently, these electrodes were combined to fabricate an asymmetric supercapacitor cell to achieve attractive energy and power densities.199 Wei and coworkers reported a facile method for the preparation of a high-performance graphene–MnO2 electrode, exhibiting 310 F g−1 at 2 mV s−1 (228 F g−1 at 500 mV s−1) and 95% cyclic stability even after 15000 cycles.200 Nandi and coworkers reported the synthesis of a hydrogen tungsten oxide-decorated reduced graphene oxide (HxWO3@rGO) composite using WS2 and GO as precursor materials. The synthesized electrode material exhibited a high aerial capacitance of 409 mF cm−2 at 1 mA cm−2, which retained 61.4% of its specific capacitance at a current density of 20 mA cm−2. The synthesized hybrid material was subsequently drop-casted onto a graphite layer coated on Scotch tape to design a flexible supercapacitor having a volumetric capacitance of 3.56 F cm−3 with an ultra-high energy density of 1.6 mW h cm−3 at a power density of 1469.43 mW cm−3.201 In another report, the same group developed a facile and new synthetic method for the formation of a 2D WO3 electrode material (flower-like aggregated nano-sized WO3 plates) from WS2 as the precursor material (Fig. 18). The synthesized electrode material established its superiority as a negative electrode material by showing a high range of electrochemical properties such as specific capacitance of 508 F g−1 at 1 A g−1 and 97% capacitance stability after 1000 cycles. The synthesized material was also used as a negative electrode in front of a mildly reduced GO positive electrode in an asymmetric supercapacitor cell, which exhibited a high energy density of 218.3 mW h cm−2 and exceptionally high power density of 17500 mW cm−2.105 In this respect, a comparison of 2D GO sheets with 1D CNTs may be very interesting, given that the latter also offer similar properties such as high surface area and conductivity; however, it has appreciably high contact resistance with the current collector substrate unlike the former. Hybrid 2D electrodes prepared by combining MoS2 or WS2 with rGO exhibited encouraging the energy density of 63 W h kg−1 or ∼50 W h kg−1, respectively, with minimal loss in capacitance (<10%) even after 1000 cycles.202 In the MoS2–rGO composite, the components were found to remain covalently attached due to the formation of an Mo–O–C bond. This may explain the very high cyclic stability of this electrode material. The similar benefit of covalently attached composite components was also reported by Baek and coworkers or Xie and coworkers in covalently attached PANI–rGO or PANI–GO composites.203,204 In another very interesting work, Han, Wei and coworkers reported the controlled growth of an ordered array of PANI nanocylinders on GO sheets. The composite was formed in situ during the oxidative polymerization of aniline, where control of the growth of the PANI nanocylinders was achieved only when a rather lower concentration of aniline [<0.05 (M)] was used (Fig. 19).205 The synthesized composite material showed an appreciably high specific capacitance of 555 F g−1 in a symmetric electrode configuration together with high cyclic stability (92% capacitance retention after 2000 cycles). The improved electrochemical properties were attributed to the formation of an ordered nano-structured PANI and low interfacial contact resistance at the PANI/GO interface due to the good contact between the 2D GO sheet surface and p-linked PANI chains.

4.4. 3D nanostructured materials

3D nanostructured materials have attracted significant interest as supercapacitor electrodes owing to their high surface area (increased charge storage), interconnected pore structure facilitating fast and efficient access by the electrode ions (conducive both for increase in charge storage and delivery), and high mechanical strength (greater cyclic stability). Increased energy delivery and cyclic stability are the signature features of supercapacitors, which coupled with the development of higher energy density should allow them to compete with commercial batteries or even to replace them. Greater cyclic stability and energy density require the fabrication of thicker electrodes with a high mass loading held by a non-conducting adhesive and use of supplementary conducting fillers. However, this mode of fabrication depletes the kinetic performance of the resulting electrodes. Agglomeration of the active material during the fabrication of thick electrodes deteriorates the electrochemical performance of the nano-structured electrode materials. This issue has been well addressed in the literature, for example, a nano-structured MoO2 supercapacitor electrode was shown to undergo a decrease in ‘b’ value towards b = 0.5 when a higher MoO2 mass loading was employed.212 In this respect, electrodes fabricated using 3D nanostructured materials with increased mechanical stability and interconnected porosity should be very effective. Metal foam and various carbon-based 3D porous materials are typically used as templates for the active material or as active material themselves in this respect. Metal foams are attractive considering their high conductivity and mechanical strength, but they possess a higher density and lower surface area compared to carbon-based 3D materials. Highly porous and conductive Ni foam has been used for the deposition of various carbon-based EDLC (graphene, CNT, etc.) and pseudocapacitive materials such as metal oxides and conductive polymers.213–218 In a very interesting report, bimetallic Co/Ni hydroxide precursors were deposited on Ni foam, and subsequently pyrolysed to form NixCo(1−x) oxides. Different spinel arrangements of Ni or Co were tuned just by managing the Co/Ni ratio in the precursor material. Considering that the spinel structure itself has a porous 3D microstructure, coupled with the 3D nanostructured Ni foam, the fabricated material should have high ionic/electronic conductivity. This was well reflected in the remarkably high specific capacitance of 1523 F g−1 at 2 A g−1 and ∼95% capacitance retention after 1000 cycles. The hybrid electrochemical cell fabricated using this as a positive electrode against activated carbon as the negative electrode delivered an energy density of 36.46 W h kg−1 at a power density of 142 W kg−1.214 In another work, electrode fabrication was carried out by growing well-aligned mesoporous CoO nanowires on Ni foam, which were subsequently coated with a thin layer of PPy. This electrode exhibited an extremely high specific capacitance of 2223 F g−1, good rate capability and tremendous cyclic stability with 99.8% capacitance retention after 2000 cycles. This hybrid material was used as a positive electrode in front of an activated carbon film as the negative electrode, which resulted in a very high energy density (∼43.5 W h kg−1) and maximum power density of ∼5500 W h kg−1 at 11.8 W h kg−1 and outstanding cyclic stability (∼20000 cycles with 91.5% capacitance retention).218 Ni foam was also used for the deposition of graphene/CNT followed by the deposition of RuO2 nanoparticles on CNT. The hierarchical porous structure allowed efficient access of the electrode materials by the electrolyte ions and provided the high energy and power density of 39.28 W h kg−1 and 128.01 kW kg−1 with remarkably high cyclic stability (106% retention after 8100 cycles).213 The high surface area and interconnected architecture of 3D carbon materials result in a good conductive network, and therefore they have been widely applied for fabrication of hybrid electrodes via the deposition of pseudocapacitive materials. Mesoporous carbon, carbon fibers, carbon cloth and carbon aerogels are various examples of 3D carbon materials. There are several reports in which pseudocapacitive materials such as MnO2 and PANI have been deposited on mesoporous carbon (MC) to form binary or ternary composites, exhibiting improved electrochemical performances.219–223 Li and coworkers deposited needle-like PANI nanostructures on MC, which resulted improved electrochemical performance with the optimal PANI loading (517 F g−1 with 60% w/w PANI deposition).222 The specific capacitance was further improved to 695 F g−1 in the PANI–CMK-3–MnO2 ternary composite, where PANI and MnO2 were co-electrodeposited on MC.223 Xia and coworkers grew whisker-like PANI nanostructures on the surface of MC, showing a specific capacitance as high as 900 F g−1 (5% capacitance fading after 3000 cycles). The observed electrochemical properties were attributed to the generation of well-controlled nano-sized domains, resulting in a low diffusion path for electrolyte ions.101 Electrodes fabricated by coating carbon fibers (diameter of several micrometers) with PANI/PEDOT or deposition of PEDOT nanoflowers exhibited significantly improved electrochemical properties.224,225 Similarly, carbon cloths, which are composed of networks of carbon fibers and widely used in the field of free standing/conducting substrate electrodes, were coated with a well-ordered PPy nanowire array, resulting in a high specific capacitance of 699 F g−1 (1 A g−1) with an excellent rate capability and 81.5% capacitance retention at 20 A g−1.226 Zhao and coworkers fabricated a conducting polymer–porous carbon composite electrode by depositing a thin layer of PANI on the surface of 3D highly ordered macroporous carbon (3DOM carbon) [Fig. 21(i)]. The 3DOM carbon skeleton favored the diffusion of the electrolyte and prevents the agglomeration of PANI.227 In a very interesting synthetic approach, Chen and coworkers prepared a 3D interconnected nanotubular graphene/PPy composite by incorporating PPy in highly conductive and stable nanotubular graphene.228 The nanotubular graphene was synthesized via the chemical vapor deposition (CVD) technique using nanoporous Ni (np-Ni), and subsequently a thin layer of PPy was electrodeposited on the 3D core–shell np-Ni@graphene. Finally, the np-Ni template was etched by acid [Fig. 21(ii)]. The composite electrode showed a maximum energy density of 21.6 W h kg−1 at a power density of 32.7 kW kg−1. Graphene aerogel (GA) has received significant attention as an electrode material owing to its open 3D network structure, which restricts the aggregation of graphene nanosheets and facilitates effective electrolyte transportation. Although the structure of GA is conducive for greater access to the electrochemically active sites by electrolyte ions compared to pristine graphene nanosheets, the conductivity of GA is lower than that of pristine graphene due to the formation of defect sites during the oxidation–reduction processes.229 Thus, to alleviate this problem, Thomas and coworkers reported a Pd nanoparticle-incorporated GA electrode, which showed reduced electrical resistivity and improved specific capacitance compared to pristine GA.230 An improvement in conductance was also attempted by doping graphene nanosheets with boron or nitrogen atoms.64 In a very unique approach, 3D macroporous electrodes composed of chemically modified graphene (CMG) were fabricated using colloidal polystyrene particles as a sacrificial template. A thin layer of MnO2 was deposited on the porous electrode to further improve its capacitance. The use of CMG helped to retain high electrical conductivity together with favorable ionic transport within the electrode material. A hybrid (asymmetric) supercapacitor cell was fabricated using MnO2/CMG as the positive electrode in front of a negative CMG electrode, which exhibited a high energy density of 44 W h kg−1 and power density of 25 kW kg−1 with excellent cycle life.231 A hybrid material composed of graphene nanosheets with vertically aligned carbon nanotubes (VA-CNT) was explored as a supercapacitor electrode material considering their controlled pore size and distribution and high conductivity.232–235 Dai and coworkers prepared a VA-CNT–graphene hybrid material via the intercalated growth of CNT into thermally expanded highly ordered pyrolytic graphite (Fig. 22). The as-prepared VA-CNT–graphene was further coated with the battery material Ni(OH)2. This composite exhibited a very high specific capacitance and charging rate capability of 1065 F g−1 at a current density of 22.1 A g−1.233 In another report, Cheng and coworkers coated PANI nanocones onto a graphene/CNT composite and assembled this ternary composite electrode in front of a graphene/CNT electrode to achieve a high specific energy density.235 Liu and coworkers prepared a 3D GNR–CNT–PANI ternary hybrid material via the in situ polymerization of aniline on a GNR–CNT hybrid. A symmetrical two-electrode configuration was fabricated with PVA/H3PO4 gel electrolyte, resulting in a high specific capacitance of 890 F g−1 with 89% capacitance retention after 1000 cycles.236 The excellent capacitance reported in these works was attributed to the large surface area of 3D graphene/CNT network structure together with increased electrical conductivity due to the presence of CNTs.

4.5 Miscellaneous electrode configurations

Besides the abovementioned strategies, numerous approaches have been reported where electroactive materials of various dimensions were logically integrated to develop novel electrode materials with much improved performances. Metal oxide/hydroxide electrodes occupy the middle ground, given that they offer higher energy density (combination of surface and intercalation charge storage) compared to carbon-based electrodes and better cyclic stability than bulk conducting polymer electrodes. Hybrid electrodes obtained by the combination of battery-type oxide/hydroxide materials and conducting polymers have exhibited improved performances due to the synergistic effects of their components. Conductive polymers provide a highly conducting medium and prevent the agglomeration or restacking of metal oxide/hydroxide particles through the space steric hindrance and electrostatic effect, which allow the metal oxide/hydroxide particles to be uniformly dispersed in the conducting polymer matrix.26,237 This effect is particularly valuable for battery-like oxide/hydroxide materials, where a continuous phase change occurs during repetitive charging/discharging cycles and unwanted agglomeration may lead to permanent passivation of the materials. Conversely, interaction with metal oxides/hydroxides leads to nanoconfinement of the polymer domains, and hence buffers the volume change during repeated redox cycles, leading to an increase in cycle life. MoS2 offers excellent capacitive properties owing to its double-layer charge storage and availability of various oxidation state of Mo, between the +2 and +6 states. Liu and coworkers grew vertically aligned PANI nanowire arrays from the internal and external surfaces of 3D tubular MoS2, which served as both the active material for electrochemical reaction and provided a framework to provide pathways for ion insertion–extraction. The as-prepared MoS2/PANI composite exhibited an appreciable high specific capacitance of 552 F g−1 together with excellent rate capability, where 82% of the specific capacitance was retained even after increasing the current density from 0.5 A g−1 to 30 A g−1.238 Conversely, Xu and coworkers prepared an MoS2/PANI@C hierarchical nanocomposite by directly depositing PANI on an MoS2 monolayer, which was subsequently coated with carbon (Fig. 23).239 The high electrical conductivity, surface area and active electrochemical surface of the octahedral 1T phase of MoS2 make it an ideal candidate for the synthesis of nanocomposites with conductive polymers. The prepared nanocomposite exhibited an enhanced specific capacitance of 678 F g−1 and remarkable cyclic stability of 80% capacitance retention even after 10000 repetitive charge/discharge cycles. The improved electrochemical properties were attributed to the multiple synergistic effects among PANI, MoS2 and highly the conductive carbon coating. Zhao and coworkers reported the in situ generation of PANI–NiO nanostructures on Ni foam as a binder-free electrode, which achieved a specific capacitance of 2565 F g−1 at 1 A g−1 and maintained 70% of the capacitance even after an increase in current density by 10 times.240 Hu and coworkers fabricated a porous NiO/Ni(OH)2 (PNC) composite encapsulated in PEDOT nanoflowers on metal wires (Cu–Ni alloy) via an electrochemical route. Here, the PEDOT coating provided high conductivity and structural stability to the composite material. This 3D flower-like nanostructured electrode achieved a high specific capacitance of 404.1 mF cm−2 at a current density of 4 mA cm−2 with cyclic stability and about 82% capacitance retention after 1000 cycles. Furthermore, a fiber-shaped all-solid-state asymmetric supercapacitor was fabricated with the as-prepared NiO/Ni(OH)2/PEDOT composite as the positive electrode and ordered mesoporous carbon (CMK-3) as the negative electrode with a suitable polymer gel as the electrolyte. The cell delivered a high output voltage of 1.5 V, high specific capacitance of 31.6 mF cm−2 and high specific energy density of 0.011 mW h cm−2.118 Li and coworkers fabricated a high-performance asymmetric supercapacitor with highly ordered 3Dα-Fe2O3@PANI core–shell nanowire arrays as the anode against PANI nanorod–carbon cloth as the cathode. The supercapacitor cell exhibited a high volumetric capacitance of 2.02 mF cm−2 and excellent cycle life with ∼96% capacitance retention even after 10000 cycles.241 Similarly, well-aligned CoO@PPy nanowire arrays grown on 3D nickel foam electrode exhibited a very high specific capacitance of 2223 F g−1 and outstanding cycle stability in a suitable asymmetric supercapacitor module.242 The improved electrochemical properties were attributed to the shortened ion diffusion channel and highly conducting nanowire arrays. Cheng and coworkers prepared 3D NiCo2O4@PPy coaxial nanowire arrays on carbon textile, where NiCo2O4 behaved as a highly capacitive core and PPy a highly conductive shell (Fig. 24). A flexible asymmetric supercapacitor device was successfully fabricated using the prepared NiCo2O4@PPy as the positive electrode and activated carbon as the negative electrode, which exhibited a high energy density of 58.8 W h kg−1 at 365 W kg−1 and outstanding cycle life with ∼83% capacitance retention after 5000 cycles.116 Given that pseudocapacitance is a near-surface phenomenon, the access of the electrolyte ions to the active centers is more important than having a higher surface area of the electrode materials as in EDLC. This fact was nicely demonstrated by three different nanostructured NiO electrodes including flower like, slice like and particle like.243 It was observed that despite its minimum surface area and large pore structure, NiO having a flower-like nanostructure exhibited the best electrochemical properties, which was attributed to the porous and 3D network structure of the electrode, facilitating the access of the electrolyte ions. Similarly, an improved performance from urchin-like Co3O4 and MnO2 electrodes was also reported.244,245 Chatterjee and coworkers recently reported the synthesis of GO-g-PANI, where covalently attached PANI chains were grown from the surface of GO via the oligomer-initiated controlled polymerization (OICP) of aniline (Fig. 25).246 The morphological analysis indicated the development of well-aligned vertical PANI nanorods on the surface of GO. This work demonstrated that based on the nature of the electrode casting solvent, an interesting urchin-like morphology of GO-g-PANI may be produced, which was attributed to the interplay among various supramolecular interactions. Electrochemical analysis indicated a significant improvement in energy storage and other electrochemical features when a highly macroporous and urchin-like open 3D morphology compared to PANI nanorods was grown the 2D GO morphology. Despite its insignificant surface area, the remarkable improvement in the electrochemical properties of the urchin-like GO-g-PANI electrodes was attributed to the improved access of the electrolyte ions to the electrochemically active sites. Improved intercalation faradaic processes were further apparent from the broad peaks in the CV curve and analysis of the scan speed-dependent specific capacitance.

5. Applications

5.1 General applications ranging from household to heavy duty

As discussed previously, high power deliverability and maintenance-free long life are the principal advantages of supercapacitors compared to batteries, whereas poor energy density is their main drawback. Accordingly, applications requiring a higher energy burst within a shorter period with repetitive cycles of operations suit supercapacitors more than batteries. Currently, different applications ranging from products with easy operation to heavy duty operation increasingly rely on supercapacitors (Fig. 26).17,141 Devices requiring an intermittent supply of high power but relatively low energy and frequent charging such as the cordless screwdriver developed by Coleman Company Inc. and power drills developed by DeWalt Industrial Tool Company use supercapacitors only for their functioning.141,247 It is undeniable that supercapacitor-driven cordless screwdrivers can only perform with half the lifetime compared to their rechargeable battery-driven analogue, but their charging requires only 90 s and they can retain the charge when not in use.248 Similarly, supercapacitors are used in digital cameras to provide flashlights, and recently Sam Beck (Blueshift) developed portable speakers with an in-built supercapacitor assembly, which exhibited a playback time of up to 6 h on full volume after being charged for only ∼5 min.249 A hybrid supercapacitor containing an asymmetric electrode configuration with a lead oxide positive electrode and an activated carbon negative electrode250 (energy density ∼ 20 W h kg−1) is used for storing off peak electricity from the utility grid at night, when it is abundant and low cost, for supplying at the time of shortage (and costlier), which is usually in the day time. Supercapacitors are also effective for use in heavy hybrid vehicles such as city transit buses requiring stop and go driving250 and trash trucks, which can experience as many as a thousand start/stop cycles a day. Aowei Technology Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China) developed e-buses run by Ni(OH)2-activated carbon hybrid supercapacitors, which require a charging time of only 90 s for a distance range of 7.9 km attained at a maximum and average speed of 44.8 km h−1 and 22 km h−1, respectively, with an acceleration of (0–40) km h−1 in 16.5 s. Hybrid supercapacitors are also used in the emergency door operation and eviction slide operation in the Airbus A380 jumbo jet.10 The requirement of high current in a short duration during the charging of electric/hybrid electric vehicles is supplied most suitably by hybrid supercapacitors. Examples of automobiles using hybrid supercapacitors are Toyota and Mazda.141,251 Supercapacitors perform multi-tasking operation such as ignition, start up, security, transmission and lighting in automobiles driven by an internal combustion engine. In addition, compatibility with respect to the life span between hybrid supercapacitors and thermal motors compared to lead–acid batteries lowers the maintenance cost. The ability for quick storage and faster cycling rate of supercapacitors make them useful in regenerative braking (in heavy vehicles) and capturing and regeneration of energy during load lowering and raising the next load, respectively, in rubber tired gantry cranes used for loading-unloading of container ships at major seaports in the Far East.252 In these applications, supercapacitors capture the energy that would otherwise be wasted as heat either during repetitious up and down movement of heavy shipping containers (in rubber tired gantry cranes) or during stopping of large vehicles (buses, trucks, trams, etc.) or during a halt time while the engine is running. The captured energy (converted in electrical energy) stored in hybrid supercapacitors is subsequently provided for further operation of diesel engines such as load raising, ignition or speeding up. This results in up to 30% energy saving.

5.2 Wearable and self-healing supercapacitors

Besides offering high specific capacitance and improved delamination property, the structural flexibility and appreciable conductivity of conducting polymer electrodes make them suitable for the construction of wearable or stretchable hybrid supercapacitor devices.26,237 There are several reports in this regard; however, it often becomes difficult to achieve multiple stretchability with these devices while retaining their electrochemical properties. This may be attributed to the interlayer slippage, delamination and crease formation of typical 3-layer supercapacitors under deformation. Zhi and coworkers indicated that in most cases these failures originate from the poor elasticity in part of the typically used hydrogel-based electrolyte layers.253 Accordingly, they fabricated a highly elastic double network (DN) hydrogel composed of agar/hydrophobic polyacrylamide (HPAAM). Here, the H-bonded agar gel formed the first network and the hydrophobically associated polyacrylamide gel formed the second network. The electrode layers were composed of a pure polypyrrole (PPy) film, and its conductivity was increased by annealing (Fig. 27(a) and (b)). In this all-polymer supercapacitor, the PPy layers remained tightly adhered to the gel electrolyte layers possibly by H-bonding interactions between pyrrole ‘N’ and H-bond donor groups of the agar gel. The fabricated supercapacitor showed a high capacitance of 79.7 mF cm−2 (from 138.3 mF cm−2 initially) even after 1000 stretching cycles, suffering only 10.2% residual deformation of the device (Fig. 27(b)). In another very interesting work, the same group fabricated a self-healing stretchable supercapacitor device, where the electrolyte layer used was a supramolecular hydrogel containing two types of β-CD host–guest inclusion complexes (Fig. 27(c)).254 The β-CD moieties were anchored on polyacrylic acid (PAA) and silk fibroin chains. Moreover, the PPy electrode film interacted by H-bonding and covalent interactions with the amino acid moieties (Lys and Tyr) present in the silk fibroin, and thus a device having an all-in-one configuration could have been created, avoiding laminated multilayer configuration. The device showed a remarkable self-healing efficiency of ∼95.8% even after 30 cutting/healing cycles and an aerial capacitance of 0.37 F cm−2 with a volumetric energy density of 0.082 mW h cm−3, which were retained by over 85.2% even after 5 cut/healing cycles (Fig. 27(d)).

5.3 The state-of-the-art future applications of supercapacitors

5.3.1 Powering small robots. The complementary features of supercapacitors and lithium-ion batteries make their hybrid power system suitable for powering robots. The advantages of supercapacitors include their significantly higher power density (10000 W kg−1) compared to lithium-ion batteries (2000–3000 W kg−1), remarkably high cyclic stability (>105 cycles) and ability to store regenerative energy when a mobile robot is braking or going downhill. Small robots or micro-swarm robots may be powered by supercapacitors only while charging with renewable solar energy source. Artal and coworkers reported an autonomous mobile robot “MBot” (serving logistic application), which was converted from a Pb–acid or lithium-ion battery to a supercapacitor as the power source (Fig. 28).255 Furthermore, stretchable and wearable supercapacitors developed from graphene, carbon nanotubes (CNT) or hydrogel-based electrodes have emerged as a more efficient power storage solution that can be used to power soft robots.256

5.3.2. In soft-robotics. The high stretchability, elasticity (elastic moduli in the range of 1 kPa to 100 kPa or more), high conductivity and transparency of hydrogels make them ideal candidates in ionotronic devices as artificial muscles, artificial skins, artificial axons, etc. for application in wearable electronics for humans and robots and implantable human–machine interfaces.257 The basic construction of these devices is similar, where the hydrogel electrode layers remain insulated by a stretchable and transparent elastomeric layer in a sandwich configuration. The hydrogel electrodes (soaked with electrolyte ions) further remain in contact with the metal surface, which connects either to an external power source or load. In this configuration, electrical double layers (EDL) are formed in the metal–hydrogel interface and hydrogel–elastomer interfaces and these capacitors remain in series (Fig. 29). In the case of artificial muscles, as a voltage is applied from external sources, movement of electrons/ions starts towards or away from the interfaces, and hence, at a higher voltage, significant polarization occurs in the hydrogel–elastomer interfaces, which exerts electrostatic attraction, leading to squeezing of the elastomeric actuator. This further increases the surface area of the elastomer, and hence, the capacitance across the hydrogel–elastomer interfaces. A similar mechanism operates in the case of a transparent loudspeaker (iTunes) (Fig. 29(a)). In the case of artificial skins, the hydrogel layers remain connected to a capacitive meter via a metallic wire and whenever the dielectric layer is stretched or pressure applied to it, the total capacitance of the system changes, which is reflected in the meter. Thus, it works as a self-powered pressure sensor. This hydrogel-based ionotronic artificial skins can sense single touch events, while remaining functional in the deformed state,258 and can sense multiple touches and also self-heal (Fig. 29(b)).259–261 Conversely, in the case of artificial axons, two hydrogel layers remain separated by a similar elastomeric layer. Here, one hydrogel layer acts as the input part remaining connected with the external power source, whereas the other layer acts as the output part, remaining connected to load Z (Fig. 29(c)). In the case where a time-dependent signal is applied, the artificial axon can transmit the signal from the input part to the output part. The highly stretchable, transparent262 artificial axon remains functional despite being stretched several times to its original length, which makes artificial axons applicable as interconnects for wearable, implantable devices and soft robots.263,264

5.4 Supercapacitors in renewable and sustainable energy devices

Clean and renewable energy sources such as solar, wind and tidal energies are expected to be used in a large scale in the future. However, due to their intermittent nature, corresponding power generators are required to be coupled with energy storage devices such as batteries/supercapacitors to ensure a continuous and uninterrupted power supply in an integrated standalone power supply system. Modern batteries, which are capable of large-scale energy storage, are still the first choice as main source of electricity supply in electric vehicles (EV).265 However, despite their high charge density, high current discharge and energy efficiency, batteries suffer with unique problems such as short cycling life and poor power density.266 These drawbacks make them unsuitable in an integrated device with the above-mentioned energy generation systems, given that these applications require frequent charging/discharging cycles and undergo high voltage fluctuations.267,268 Thus, the introduction of battery–supercapacitor hybrid energy systems should be very effective for these applications given that supercapacitors can provide the required dynamic response. Supercapacitor–battery combinations or supercapacitors alone fed by solar cells or other energy generation systems such as fuel cells have already received a number of applications in powering electric vehicles and wireless sensors in medical applications to measure blood pressure, echocardiograms, oxygen measurement, etc.269–271 Furthermore, supercapacitors have also been combined with diesel engines during heavy duty applications to increase the efficiency and life of these devices. In all of these combined devices, supercapacitors are sized to meet the peak power demand, and thus help to reduce the size of diesel engines or batteries to operate at an average power level. Thus, these hybrid operating systems substantially reduce emissions and increase the fuel efficiency or life (in case of batteries) of these devices.17 Supercapacitors are further useful for storing regenerative braking energy, which eliminates the exposure of batteries to the peak current. In addition, supercapacitors can be fully charged in 1–10 s, whereas the latest version of lithium-ion batteries are charged up to 70% of their full capacity in the order of minutes.266 In a very interesting work, Hou, Lu and coworkers reported an integrated system composed of an Si/H solar cell as an energy conversion device, NiCo2O4‖AC battery–supercapacitor hybrid cell as an energy storage device and light-emitting diodes as the energy utilization device (Fig. 30). The designed battery–supercapacitor hybrid cell showed an energy density of 16.6 W h kg−1, power density of 7285 W kg−1, long-term stability of 100% capacitance retention even after 15000 cycles and low self-discharge. The designed hybrid cell could be charged to 1.6 V in 1 s by the solar cell and performed well as a reliable source for powering the LEDs. The integrated system showed an overall efficiency of 8.1% with a storage efficiency of 74.24%.272 The combination of solar energy with supercapacitors as energy storage units was used to provide eco-friendly street lighting (LED lamps) in Japan by three Japanese companies, Nippon-Chemi-Con, in 2010.273

5.5 Challenges of supercapacitors in real-world applications

Despite the above-stated wide range of applications, there are various challenges that restrict the real-world application of supercapacitors. The major technical problem with supercapacitors is their much lower energy density compared to batteries, which restricts their application as single energy storage devices capable of prolonged energy supply in various applications. The lower operating voltage of a single supercapacitor cell requires the series combination of a large number of cells. This leads to a giant volume to meet the energy output, which limits their practical application.272 The relatively high rate of self-discharge (discussed previously) is another very important concern for supercapacitors. The current–voltage proportionality is a major drawback of supercapacitors, unlike batteries, which leads to a steady decrease in the potential of the former whenever energy is delivered.121 Furthermore, the use of a large number of series-connected supercapacitor cells or application of organic/ionic electrolytes for increasing the operative voltage window increases the cost/performance ratio together with environmental concerns, which also work against their real-world applications. Besides these inherent technical problems, Zhao and coworkers mentioned several other challenges related to an electrical parameter model, consistency detection and industrial standard,274 which are required to be addressed by collective efforts prior to achieving the desired performance of supercapacitors in real-world applications.

6. Conclusion and outlook

In bridging the gap between batteries and traditional capacitors, supercapacitors play a crucial role because battery technologies have some drawbacks of increasingly punitive performance for industrial application, where higher power density is required. The delivery of power ranging from small-scale energy consumption to the industrial scale is achieved through appropriate hybrid supercapacitors, which can be varied by changing their electrode materials, electrolytes, and configuration with different nanoparticles and conducting polymers and also by changing their configuration from symmetric to asymmetric. Certainly, it is not possible to achieve all the required properties in a single material, and hence significant research has been documented on conducting polymer nanocomposites to improve both the power density and energy density. The dependence of the hybrid supercapacitor performance on the surface area, porosity and conductivity of its component materials was discussed, where nanostructured metallic oxides, sulphides and carbon nanomaterials play an important role in hybrid supercapacitor electrodes. The review covered all the 0-, 1-, 2- and 3-dimensional nanomaterials and their hybrids for improving the hybrid supercapacitor performance. The charge storage mechanism was discussed for both the faradaic (pseudo capacitance) and non-faradaic (EDLC) mechanisms based on cyclic voltammetry (CV) data analysis both for the symmetric and asymmetric configuration. Also, this review discussed the capacitive property compared to the battery property present in nanohybrids based on the analysis of CV data.

Hybrid supercapacitors are now widely used for powering portable devices to industrial power devices. The main market of supercapacitors in the coming decades will be transportation, including hybrid electric vehicles and metro trains. Also, there is the prime possibility that supercapacitors can be applied in self-power robots, self-power sensors, biotechnology, renewable and sustainable energy storage devices, etc. A hybrid energy storage system overcomes the drawbacks of each single energy storage device. Thus, future focus should be intensified on developing hybrid supercapacitors using battery-like electrodes and pseudocapacitive electrodes given that they afford high capacitance, high energy density and high longevity. For the high specific power of supercapacitors, coupling supercapacitors with batteries can reduce the strain on the battery pack and improve the acceleration and hill climbing performance. The internal serial hybrid of a battery electrode with a supercapacitor electrode (BSH) may yield high energy, high power, safety, and environmental-friendliness, and would be an attractive energy supplier for future devices. BSH devices can simultaneously have high energy and power densities, and thus they are necessary to power future portable/smart/wireless electronics or optoelectronics. Thus, future research should aim to in-depth study developing multifunctional BSHs having flexible, stretchable, bendable, and transparent properties with sensing functions to the ambient environment. To date, only a few flexible Li-ion and alkaline BSH devices have been reported, and their performance mechanism has not yet been fully explored. Further, for high longevity, the charging of hybrid supercapacitors by solar cells in the day, and the subsequent discharge of current in the dark is another important area of future research. Solar cell hybrid supercapacitor devices will be able to run portable electronics, light LEDs, etc. for a long time. Thus, the scope of future research on hybrid supercapacitors is to improve both fundamental and technological perspectives to meet the different power demands in diverse fields in a greener way. To accomplish these goals, not only extensive experimental research on hybrid supercapacitors, but also theoretical studies relating to modeling and simulation are highly desirable as future work. This computational research can afford guidance for the selection of electrode materials and electrolytes, and for designing ideal cell architectures to cope with the required need.
