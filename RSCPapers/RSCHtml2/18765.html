Optoelectronic performance of perovskite Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) based on high-throughput screening and first-principles calculations

Abstract

Developing novel lead-free perovskite materials with suitable bandgaps and superior thermal stability is crucial to boost their applications in next-generation photovoltaic technologies. High throughput screening combined with the first principles method can accurately and effectively screen out promising perovskites. Herein, we select two lead-free all-inorganic halide double perovskite materials Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) from 1026 compounds with the criteria including appropriate structure factors, positive decomposition energies, and suitable direct bandgaps. We investigated the thermal and mechanical stability, geometric and electronic structures, photoelectric properties, and defect formation energies for both perovskites Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In). They can exhibit excellent structural formability and stability through the analysis of structure factors, elastic constants, and stable chemical potential regions. In addition, we investigate the defect effects of Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) on the photovoltaic performance by evaluating the defect formation energies and transition energy levels. Based on the HSE06 functional, we calculated the energy band structures of these two compounds and demonstrate the direct bandgaps of 1.69 eV (HSE06) and 2.16 eV (HSE06) for Cs2KGaI6 and Cs2KInI6, respectively. Moreover, we predicted excellent spectroscopic limited maximum efficiencies (SLMEs) of these two perovskites with high light absorption coefficients (around 105 cm1), for instance, the SLME of Cs2KGaI6 can reach as high as 28.39%.

1. Introduction

In recent years, perovskite materials, which feature high power conversion efficiency (PCE) and low-cost manufacturability, deserve intensive attention as the next-generation solar cells.13 Based on their superior photoelectric properties, the reported PCE of perovskite solar cells has reached as high as 25.7%.2,4 However, the structural instability of hybrid perovskite materials seriously retards their practical applications in photoelectric devices.5 It is desired to develop novel all-inorganic perovskites with excellent thermal stability and photoelectric properties (such as ideal direct bandgaps, large absorption coefficients, and high carrier mobilities).68

Various designing strategies for all-inorganic perovskites have been proposed recently. For instance, it is routine to replace the Pb2+ cations with bivalent cations such as Ge2+ and Sn2+. Compared with the lead cation, other bivalent cations can be easily oxidized to tetravalent ones in perovskites.9,10 The trivalent or tetravalent metal cations (such as Bi3+, Sb3+, Sn4+, and Ti4+) can also be employed as the substitutes, while those generated compounds have the general formula Cs3M(III)2X9 or Cs2M(IV)X6 (M(III)/M(IV) represents trivalent or tetravalent metal cations, and X denotes the halogen atom) to maintain the charge neutrality.11,12 However, these trivalent or tetravalent cation-based compounds often display inferior photoelectric properties due to the isolated network of metal halide octahedral units.13 The heterovalent two-metal substitution is another usually-used strategy, in which two Pb2+ cations in perovskites can be replaced by a pair of monovalent and trivalent cations.14 With the general formula A2M(I)M(III)X6, the double perovskites have been regarded as stable and environment-friendly compounds, such as the Bi and Ag-based lead-free double perovskites.15,16 Some of these double perovskites have intrinsic indirect or large band gaps, which are unfavorable for thin film photovoltaics.17,18 Therefore, the screening of novel double perovskite materials with outstanding optoelectronic performance and structural stability can accelerate their applications in the next-generation photovoltaic technologies.

In this work, we aimed to screen lead-free perovskites with good thermal stability and promising performance from as many as 1026 compounds based on the high-throughput screening,19 where the criteria included appropriate octahedral structure factors and suitable direct bandgaps. Two double perovskites Cs2KGaI6 and Cs2KInI6 were selected and theoretically investigated based on the first-principles methods with different functionals (e.g. PBE, PBE + SOC, HSE06, and HSE06 + SOC).20,21 We evaluated the structural stability of Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In), by calculating the structure factors (tolerance factor and octahedral factor),22 elastic constants, and stable chemical potential regions.23 Furthermore, we calculated the defect formation energies and transition energy levels to investigate the performance dependence of Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) on defect effects.24 Finally, we can demonstrate the outstanding optoelectronic performance of these two perovskites by analyzing the electronic structures, light absorption coefficients, and spectroscopic limited maximum efficiencies.18

2. Computational methods

Our simulations were performed based on the density functional theory (DFT)20 with the Vienna Ab Initio Simulation Package (VASP).23 The initial crystal structures of 1026 perovskites with the space group Fmm17,2529 were derived from the Materials Project database.30 Based on the high-throughput screening, we selected two perovskites Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) and adopted the PerdewBurkeErnzerhof (PBE)20 functional in the generalized gradient approximation (GGA)31 to optimize their geometrical structures. The MonkhorstPack k-point grid was set to 3 3 3 for the geometric optimization and 6 6 6 for the self-consistent calculations.32 The convergence threshold of self-consistent field iteration was set to 106 eV, and the residual force on the atoms was set to less than 0.01 eV 1. The projector augmented wave (PAW)33 pseudo-potential was employed with the cut-off energy of 520 eV. Considering the usually underestimated bandgaps with the PBE31 functional, we adopted the PBE + SOC (spinorbit coupling),31 HeydScuseriaErnzerhof (HSE06), and HSE06 + SOC21 functional to calculate the band structures in our investigations. Moreover, we systematically studied the defects of perovskite Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) based on the 2 1 1 supercell by using the PBE-GGA31 method.

As shown in previous investigations,4143 we can analyze three independent elastic constants (C11, C12, and C44) to illustrate the mechanical stability of compounds. According to the BornHuang stability criterion,44 the structure is mechanically stable when the following conditions are satisfied: C11 > 0, C44 > 0, C11 C12 > 0, and C11 + 2C12 > 0.44 The calculated elastic constants of Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) are shown in Table S3 (ESI) and demonstrate that they are mechanically stable with satisfied BornHuang stability conditions. Moreover, the calculated Pugh's ratio (B/G) and Poisson's ratio (v) of Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) are larger than 1.75 and 0.26 respectively. This indicates that they are ductile materials with great application potential in foldable photovoltaic materials.45

The defects and stability-related characteristics of perovskites are critical for their applications in photovoltaic absorbers.46 It is desirable to synthesize these compounds in controlled chemical conditions to avoid performance-limiting defects. Totally, we took into account 20 different types of inherent point defects, including 4 vacancies (VCs, VK, VM, and VI), 4 interstitials (Csi, Ki, Mi, and Ii), 6 cation-on-cation antisites (CsK, CsM, KCs, KM, MCs, and MK), 3 anion-on-cation antisites (CsI, KI, and MI) and 3 anion-on-cation antisites (ICs, IK and IM).49

In different chemical conditions corresponding to the points A/C (Ga/In-rich, I-poor) and B/D (Ga/In-poor, I-rich) in Fig. 2, there are various dominant donor-type and acceptor-type defects for these two compounds. For Cs2KgaI6, the dominant donor-type defects include Gai, Ki, and Csi, while the acceptor-type defects are CsGa, Ii, IGa, KGa, and VCs in the Ga-rich and I-poor environment, as shown in Fig. S4(a) (ESI). In the Ga-poor and I-rich environment, the dominant donor-type defects include Gai, KI, and KCs, while the acceptor-type defects are CsGa, KGa, Ii, VK, and VCs, as shown in Fig. S4(b) (ESI). For Cs2KInI6, in the In-rich and I-poor environment, the dominant donor-type defects include Ini, Csi, and Ki, while the acceptor-type defects are IIn, CsIn, ICs, Ii, and IK, as shown in Fig. S4(c) (ESI). In the Ga-poor and I-rich environments, the dominant donor-type defects include KCs, KI, Ini, and Csi, while the acceptor-type defects are KIn, VK, IIn, CsIn, and Ii, as shown in Fig. S4(d) (ESI).46

In addition, we find that there exist some types of defects for both compounds. For instance, in either M-rich or M-poor chemical conditions, the donor-type defects include interstitial defects Mi and Csi, while the accepter-type defects include substitution defects involving the M element such as CsM, IM, and KM. The formation of defects can be controlled by tuning chemical conditions. For instance, when the chemical condition changed from the Ga-rich and I-poor environment to Ga-poor and I-rich ones, the formation energies of Ki and Csi can be obviously increased.

The PBE bandgaps are consistent with the database.30 Moreover, as shown in Fig. 3, the band structures based on the HSE06 functional are close to ones with the HSE06 + SOC functional. To further study the interatomic bonding and charge transfer, we performed the electron localization function (ELF) analysis for the [110] crystallographic plane of Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) (see Fig. 4).57,58 Based on a second-order Taylor expansion of sphere average pair density,59 ELF can be used to visualize atomic shell structures, the distribution of bond pairs and electron lone pairs in molecules, and thus to monitor the change in electron distribution during the formation and breaking of the bonds.60 The smaller ELF value (0) implies that the electron has an ultra-low density between two atoms with a typical ionic bond character.57 In contrast, when the ELF approaches 1, the electrons are localized between two atoms at a high density, which indicates the existence of an ideal covalent bond.52,57

Our ELF calculations can show the bonding properties between the metal cations (e.g., Cs+, K+, and M3+) and the I clearly.61 As shown in Fig. 4, the electron sharing behavior between M and I ion centers indicates the more covalent bond between them.61 The lower ELF values between K+, Cs+, and other ions indicate ionic interactions between K, Cs, and other ions, similar to the ones between Cs, K, and [MI6] units.58 These convinced us that Cs and K atoms generally act as charge donors. The coupling between M and I ions is obviously stronger than between K and I ions, which resulted in larger KI bond lengths, as shown in Table 1.62

In addition, we also performed the Bader charge analysis60,62 as shown in Fig. 4. Positive Bader charges indicate atoms donate charges in the charge transfer, while the negative charge indicates atoms accept charges.57 This further proves that Cs and K atoms act as charge donors. Previous investigations demonstrated that the enhanced charge transfer in perovskites implies good stability. Therefore, the Bader charges in the charge transfer of Cs2KInI6 are slightly larger than the ones of the other compound, which indicates slightly better stability of Cs2KInI6. These conclusions are consistent with our calculations of the formation energies, as shown in Table S4 (ESI).62

The SLME18,67,68 is a commonly-used tool to assess the performance of photovoltaic devices. Compared with the ShockleyQueisser limit,69,70 the SLME takes into account not only the energy band structures but also the active film thicknesses and absorption coefficients.18 As shown in Fig. 5(b), the SLME of these compounds can achieve 28.39% (with a film thickness of 530 nm) for Cs2KGaI6 and 19.05% (480 nm) for Cs2KInI6, respectively.

4. Conclusions

From 1026 compounds, we delicately selected perovskite Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) based on the criteria including appropriate structure factors, positive decomposition energies, and suitable direct bandgaps. Furthermore, we theoretically studied the thermal stability, photoelectric properties, and defect effects of these two unexplored perovskites by using the first-principles methods. We assessed the structural stability of these compounds based on the calculations of structure factors, decomposition energies, and stable chemical potential regions. With the HSE06 functional, we calculated the electronic structures and light absorption rates and show that the direct bandgaps of Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) are 1.69 eV and 2.16 eV, respectively. The light absorption rates of these compounds can approach as high as around 105 cm1, while the SLME of Cs2KGaI6 can achieve 28.39% with a film thickness of around 530 nm. By comprehensively analyzing the electronic and optical properties, we can propose that Cs2KMI6 (M = Ga, In) can exhibit superior photovoltaic performance with proper bandgaps, large light absorption rates, and high theoretical efficiencies. Based on the ELF and Bader charge analysis, we conclude that the KI bond exhibits a covalent character, while Mhalogen and CsI bonds are ionic. In addition, the calculated effective masses of these compounds are relatively small and thus facilitate charge carrier transport. These optoelectronic properties based on the first-principles methods can be integrated into the numerical models to predict the currentvoltage curves in the future.71,72 In addition, we predict different types of deep-level defects, which can lead to traps and recombination centers, resulting in device performance degradation. These theoretical results can contribute to the development and design of new lead-free double perovskites for highly efficient optoelectronic devices, and the defect engineering to tune their optoelectronic performance.
