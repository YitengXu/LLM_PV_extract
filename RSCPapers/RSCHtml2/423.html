Abstract

1. Introduction

The energy crisis and the increasing atmospheric concentration of carbon dioxide (CO2) due to overexploitation of fossil fuels are recognized to be the two major problems in the foreseeable future.1 Solar photocatalytic conversion of CO2 with H2O to hydrocarbon fuels has sparked a new sustainable development path since it would help to reduce the atmospheric CO2 level and partly fulfill energy demands.2 The chemical conversion of CO2 to industrially beneficial compounds is advantageous in terms of Green and Sustainable Chemistry because CO2 is an inexpensive, nontoxic and abundant C1 feedstock. However, the conversion of CO2 with H2O, two very stable molecules accompanied by a large positive change in the Gibbs free energy, is one of the biggest challenges in chemistry.3 Therefore, the design and synthesis of efficient photocatalysts to enable the reduction of CO2 for the production of fuels and chemicals has attracted much attention and is a desirable goal.4

The solar photocatalytic conversion of CO2 and H2O to produce fuels and chemicals can be realized by using active photocatalysts. Since the pioneering studies by Inoue and Fujishima, et al.,5 many studies have been devoted to the semiconductor-based photocatalytic reduction of CO2 with H2O and the pace has increased enormously in recent years.6 In this heterogeneous process, the photocatalyst is usually a hybrid semiconductor material which absorbs light, separates the photogenerated charges, transports them to the surface and provides active sites for the catalytic reaction.7 Among the various semiconductor photocatalysts, titanium dioxide (TiO2) has been the most common and promising photocatalyst for solar-fuel production so far, which is owing to its cheapness, availability, photostability and nontoxicity.8 The photoactivity of TiO2 derives from oxidizing holes and reducing electrons produced by exciting TiO2 at wavelengths shorter than its ultraviolet absorption edge. Therefore, the photocatalytic activity of pure TiO2 for the CO2 reduction under visible light irradiation is not high enough for practical application due to the drawbacks of the wide band gap, the low absorption efficiency of solar irradiation and the easy charge recombination.9

The design and development of efficient photocatalysts is a critical issue in photocatalytic CO2 reduction, which involves light absorption, photogenerated charge transfer/separation, subsequent catalysis, etc. On the one hand, for improving the light harvesting, the spherical voids can increase the absorption efficiency of sunlight due to the increase of the distance of the light path by enhancing random light scattering.10 Because of a periodic dielectric contrast in the length scale of the wavelength of light, photonic crystal (PC) materials offer a great capability of controlling and manipulating the flow of incident light. Recently, the unique optical performance of PCs for light harvesting has attracted strong interest in photo-catalysis and photoelectrochemistry.11 For example, three-dimensionally ordered macroporous (3DOM) TiO2 was constructed and demonstrated super performances for photodegradation of organic pollutants12 and hydrogen evolution.13 However, the photocatalytic efficiency of PCs with a single component for CO2 reduction is not good enough due to the low photogenerated charge transfer/separation efficiency.

On the other hand, many strategies have been developed to improve photocatalysis efficiency, including loading of metal,14 coupling plasmon resonance,15 doping with ions,16 reducing the particle size and crystal facet of photocatalysts,17etc. Among these tactics, constructing TiO2-based heterostructures has been demonstrated effective for improving photocatalytic efficiency through promoting the photogenerated charge separation, harvesting visible light and facilitating CO2 activation.18 Various types of second-component semiconductors of TiO2-based heterostructures have been reported, such as CdS,19 CeO2 (ref. 20), ZrO2 (ref. 21) and so on. On this basis, an electron transfer mediator (Pt,22 Au,23 and graphene oxide24) embedded in the heterostructure interface can strengthen the interface charge transfer and result in a vectorial electron transfer via a Z-scheme system,25 which can efficiently improve photogenerated charge separation and photocatalytic activity.26 For all the above prominent advantages, the construction of three-component heterostructures combined with a 3DOM structure is expected to bring excellent performance and powerful platforms for photocatalytic CO2 reduction.

Herein, we first report practical fabrication of a visible light responsive all-solid-state three-component Z-scheme system with the coreâ€“shell structured Pt@CdS nanoparticles (NPs) on the 3DOM TiO2 support (3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2) via the gas bubbling-assisted membrane reduction-precipitation (GBMR/P) method. Our design and versatile synthetic approach can control the thickness of the CdS shell over Pt core NPs at the nanoscale via the adjustable molar ratio of Cd/Pt. The 3DOM structure can enhance the light-harvesting efficiency. And the all-solid-state Z-scheme system with the CdS(shell)â€“Pt(core)â€“TiO2(support) nanojunction is favourable for improving the separation of photogenerated electrons and holes due to the vectorial electron transfer of TiO2 â†’ Pt â†’ CdS. This three-component system exhibits high photocatalytic activity for CO2 reduction to CH4 under simulated solar irradiation, far exceeding those of the single- and two-component systems, as a result of vectorial electron transfer driven by the two-step excitation of TiO2 and CdS. It suggests that 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalysts are an excellent system for photocatalytic reactions or surface chemical processes that occur at the interfaces of the reactant and catalyst.

3.2 Photocatalytic activity for CO2 reduction under simulated solar irradiation

The measurements for photocatalytic reduction of CO2 with H2O were carried out in a gas-closed circulation system with a continuous-flow mode under simulated light irradiation. CO2 and water vapor sealed in the system, and samples were continually taken from the reaction cell at given intervals (40 min) for quantitative analysis based on the external standard by using the calibration curve. Commercial P25 was selected as the reference catalyst in photoreduction of CO2. With a mixture of CO2 and H2O vapor passing through the reactor, the background test was first performed. No CO2 conversion products were detected under light in the absence of photocatalysts. Moreover, without light no hydrocarbon was detected even if there is a great amount of efficient catalysts. The background tests were also performed using a mixture of pure N2 and H2O vapor as the feed gas with 3DOM TiO2 and Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalysts in the reactor under simulated light irradiation, and again, no formation of carbon-containing products were observed. In addition, the analyses of 13C isotopic tracing via GC-MS for the products of photocatalytic 13CO2 reduction over the 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalyst after 20 h irradiation were carried out and the results are shown in Fig. S10.â€  It shows that the peak with an m/z of 17 could be recognized as 13CH4, and the peak with an m/z of 29 could be attributed to 13CO. It indicates that the C element in the product of CO and CH4 comes from the CO2 reactant. Therefore, we assert that the conversion products were derived from CO2 in the feed gas rather than the photo-decomposition of residual organics in the catalysts. According to the tests, we can learn that the process of CO2 reduction requires both photocatalysts and simulated light irradiation.

The photocatalytic reduction of CO2 with H2O over the studied catalysts provided CO and CH4 as two main carbon products, and the formation of H2 and O2 was also observed at the same time. 3.3 Discussion on the photocatalytic mechanism of 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalysts for CO2 reduction with H2O

During photocatalytic CO2 reduction with H2O vapors under simulated solar irradiation, CO, CH4, H2 and O2 were produced by CO2 reduction and H2O oxidation. In general, several intermediates in the photocatalytic process of CO2 reduction with H2O are radical species, whose recombination at different stages partially accounts for the number of possible pathways and final products. The exact order and mechanistic details of each subsequent step have not been fully elucidated, but three full pathways detailing the conversion of CO2 to CH4 have been proposed in the literature. In the following discussion, they are referred to as (1) the formaldehyde pathway, (2) the carbene pathway, and (3) the glyoxal pathway on account of their unique intermediate.9 For co-catalysts with coupling semiconductors with metal NPs, they have been shown to be beneficial for the performance of photocatalysts because of, among other factors, improved charge separation, facilitated CO2 activation, and the provision of catalytic active sites for the reduction process. In a typical realization of this concept, the nanostructured semiconductor is surface-decorated with metal or metal oxide NPs. This architecture is utilized in almost all of the most efficient photocatalytic systems. It is a convenient setup which allows the catalytic process to be isolated from the light-harvesting and charge-separation functions of the photocatalyst.

Moreover, it is well-known that the design of photocatalysts for CO2 reduction to CO and CH4 must meet the energy band theory, which is based on the relative positions of the conduction band (CB), valence band (VB) and oxidation potentials. In general, photogenerated electrons can be consumed effectively, if the reduction potential of the reaction is lower than the CB-potential of the semiconductor. Based on the results of the formation rates of H2, CO and CH4 (Table 3) and the nanostructure of 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalysts (Fig. 3), the enhanced photocatalytic activity for CO2 reduction can be ascribed to their unique structures with a variety of favorable properties. On the one hand, the 3DOM structure plays an important role for improving photocatalytic activity. 3DOM TiO2 shows higher activity for CO2 reduction than the commercial P25. 3DOM structure enhancement on the photocatalytic activity for CO2 reduction could be easily understood due to improving the light-harvesting efficiency.11b On the other hand, the introduction of Pt@CdS coreâ€“shell NPs in the CdSâ€“Ptâ€“TiO2 nanojunction can effectively extend the spectral response from UV to visible area owing to the CdS-photosensitization and improve separation of photogenerated charge carriers.23 According to the results of UV-Vis DRS, 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalysts show the super absorption efficiency for simulated solar irradiation due to the synergistic effect of Pt@CdS coreâ€“shell NPs and the 3DOM TiO2 support. 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalysts consist of isolated photochemical system 1 (Pt/TiO2), photochemical system 2 (CdS/Pt) and the electron-transfer system (Pt). After the absorption of photons, both TiO2 and CdS are excited to produce electronâ€“hole pairs. For the isolated Pt/TiO2 photochemical system, the generated electrons located at the CB of TiO2 flow into Pt, i.e., electron transfer from TiO2 to Pt. It is attributed to the different Fermi levels between Pt and the conduction band edge of TiO2 and the formation of Schottky barrier at the interface of Pt and TiO2. The VB-holes of TiO2 with a strong oxidation power oxidize H2O to O2. Combining the results of XPS and catalytic performance, the electron conduction ability of Pt NPs improves with increasing amounts of Pt&+ species which have stronger ability to capture the electron from the CB of TiO2 than metallic Pt species. For the isolated CdS/Pt photochemical system, the VB-holes of CdS could recombine with electrons derived from the Pt sensor because of the formation of the inner electric field with abundant electrons over Pt nanoparticles, i.e., the electron transfer from Pt to CdS. And the CB-electrons of CdS would be used for CO2 reduction rather than transferred to Pt because of the increase in their surface population in the Pt@CdS coreâ€“shell structure. Thus, the vectorial electron transfer of TiO2 â†’ Pt â†’ CdS should occur as a result of excitation of both TiO2 and CdS under the conditions for CO2 reduction. In other words, the 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalyst is an all-solid-state Z-scheme system with the CdSâ€“Ptâ€“TiO2 nanojunction. In addition, the weight ratios of CdS/TiO2 can adjust the amounts of photogenerated carriers in TiO2 and CdS. The 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2-1 catalyst shows high catalytic activity and stability for the photocatalytic reduction of CO2 with H2O. It suggests that the amounts of the photogenerated electrons in TiO2 and the photogenerated holes in CdS may achieve an optimization balance for neutralization.

In order to specify the reduction sites of Pt@CdS/TiO2, the Ag photodeposition experiment was carried out by irradiation with the same light source of the CO2 reduction test for 30 min, and the results are shown in Fig. 9. It indicates that Ag particles are supported on the surface of the CdS shell. If the electron transfer from CdS to TiO2 rapidly takes place through their contact with Pt, Ag NPs should be photodeposited on TiO2. However, HRTEM images demonstrated that Ag nanoparticles are deposited almost selectively on the surface of the CdS shell layer and formation of the Ag photodeposited on Pt@CdS/TiO2 (Ag/Pt@CdS/TiO2). It indicates that CdS acts as the reduction sites of Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalysts, and the back electron transfer from CdS to TiO2 is a minor path, which strongly supports the vectorial electron transfer in the all-solid-state CdSâ€“Ptâ€“TiO2 Z-scheme system. The Z-scheme system simultaneously generates VB-holes (TiO2) with a strong oxidation power and CB-electrons (CdS) with a strong reduction power, which explains the high formation rates of CH4 for CO2 reduction.

On the basis of the above discussion, the mechanism for the photocatalytic reduction of CO2 with H2O over 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalysts was proposed and it is shown in Scheme 1. Firstly, the 3DOM structure can improve the light-harvesting efficiency, which is an important factor to enhance photocatalytic performance for CO2 reduction. Secondly, the vectorial electron transfer of TiO2 â†’ Pt â†’ CdS over the all-solid-state Z-scheme system benefits the separation of photogenerated electrons and holes, which can increase the formation rates and selectivity of CH4 for CO2 reduction. Under simulated solar irradiation, the electronâ€“hole pairs are generated on the surface of both the TiO2 support and CdS shell. The generated electrons in the CB of TiO2 flow into Pt NPs, and the electron further transfers to the CdS shell and quenches with the VB-holes of CdS, which achieves the high separation efficiency of photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs in the CdSâ€“Ptâ€“TiO2 nanojunction system. Finally, the VB-holes of the TiO2 support can oxidize H2O gathered on the surface of the catalyst and produce O2 and H+ protons. O2 will desorb from the surface of catalysts to the gas, and H+ protons may still stay in the absorbed H2O film on the surface of catalysts, and they will migrate and gather to the surface of CdS due to the electrostatic gravity. Finally, the abundant CB-electrons of the CdS shell with abundant H+ protons would be used for CO2 reduction and give priority to generating CH4. Therefore, 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalysts, which take both advantages of the high light-harvesting efficiency by 3DOM supports and the high separation of photogenerated electrons and holes by the all-solid-state Z-scheme system with the CdSâ€“Ptâ€“TiO2 nanojunction, showed super photocatalytic performance for CO2 reduction into CH4 under simulated solar irradiation.

4. Conclusions

We demonstrated a strategy for preparing the photocatalyst that consists of the coreâ€“shell-structured Pt@CdS NPs uniformly dispersed on the 3DOM TiO2 support via the GBMR/P method. The macropore structures of 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalysts are highly ordered and interconnected to each other by small pore windows. The 3DOM structure can improve the light-harvesting efficiency. The coreâ€“shell structured Pt@CdS NPs are highly dispersed on the inner walls of uniform macropores. The nanolayer thickness of the CdS shell over Pt NPs can be controlled at the nanoscale by the Cd/Pt molar ratio. The all-solid-state Z-scheme system with the CdS(shell)â€“Pt(core)â€“TiO2(support) nanojunction is favourable for the separation of photogenerated electrons and holes due to the vectorial electron transfer of TiO2 â†’ Pt â†’ CdS. 3DOM Pt@CdS/TiO2 catalysts, which take both advantages of the high light-harvesting efficiency and the high separation of photogenerated electrons and holes, exhibited super photocatalytic performance for CO2 reduction with H2O to CH4 under simulated solar irradiation. Our design and versatile synthetic approach can potentially be extended to other nanoparticle cores with different compositions, sizes and shapes and to other shell compositions on the surface of the 3DOM support. The fabrication of the all-solid-state Z-scheme system on the 3DOM oxide surface is expected to throw new light on the design of highly efficient photocatalysts for CO2 reduction to hydrocarbon.
