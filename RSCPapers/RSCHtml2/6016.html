Abstract

MXene is an emerging two-dimensional (2D) material whose properties have been well-evaluated to cater to a wide range of applications. However, there are challenges in using MXenes that may impede their further use, such as aggregation, long shelf-life stability, oxidation, thermal and mechanical properties, etc. Thus, material developments with MXenes have shown great promise in solving these limitations. In this review, the challenges and research progress in the material development of MXenes towards coreâ€“shell structures and their applications are addressed. Particularly, the preparation techniques and mechanisms involving the successful attachment of MXenes as a core or shell are evaluated and discussed, along with the discussion of the challenges and solutions that arise from using MXenes. Additionally, key findings, evaluations, and applications of the MXene coreâ€“shell in energy storage and conversion are studied and assessed. The review ends with the future direction of utilizing and functionalizing MXenes in coreâ€“shell structures in expanding their development toward hybrid coreâ€“shell structures for prospective applications.

Introduction

Ever since Yury Gogotsi and his group developed MXene,1 there has been great research interest and progress in the synthesis and application development of MXene.2â€“5 MXenes are a class of two-dimensional (2D) transition metal carbides, carbonitrides, and nitrides originating from their MAX phases consisting of Mn+1AXn layers, where M is an early transition metal, A is mainly a group IIIA or IVA element, X is C, and/or N, and n = 1, 2, or 3 (ref. 6) (Fig. 1). The extraction of the A-group layers through selective etching results in the retention of an accordion-like morphology, thus resulting in the preservation of few-layer Mn+1Xn which are known as MXenes. As a newly found material, various combinations of MXenes with transition metals to carbides, nitrides, and carbonitrides have been sought in the last decade.7 The blooming research opportunity in developing MXenes not only sought their probable hybrid combinations,8,9 but also aided better functionalities, understanding of their properties, and further evaluations toward suitable applications.10,11 For instance, MXenes have attracted tremendous interest for use in energy storage systems12,13 which have been traditionally explored by using metal-based,14 carbon-based,15 polymer-based,16 and hybrid metal/carbon/polymer-based materials.17,18

Scientific studies of electrode materials such as carbon nanotubes, graphene oxide (GO), metal chalcogenides, metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs), metal phosphides, high-entropy alloys, and layered hydroxides, and their progress in transiting towards hybrid materials and oriented structures have been significant and well investigated throughout the years.19â€“27 For example, transition metal oxides,28â€“32 sulfides,33â€“36 porous carbon,37 GO,38 and reduced GO (rGO) are well-known 2D materials which have been utilized and evaluated in various applications.39â€“41 2D structures are desirable as they can act as protective shells or coatings, and also act as substrates to further grow nanomaterials.42,43 Aside from these functions, certain 2D structures are admirable for their good functional properties that are needed when preparing nanomaterials, hybrids, or composites. For instance, GO has shown exceptional solubility and stability properties in dispersing in different solvents compared to rGO.44 This makes GO an ideal candidate when preparing hybrid materials using wet-chemical synthesis. Yet GO has poor conductivity compared with rGO.45 Similar to graphene, MXenes offer a 2D structure with superior conductivity as high as 20000 S cmâˆ’1 without losing their good water dispersibility.46 However, being 2D materials like graphene, MXenes are also susceptible to aggregation and stacking due to the strong influence of hydrogen bonding and van der Waals forces. This may restrict the surface of MXenes from being fully utilized, especially in applications that require efficient interface utilization such as adsorption, catalysis, and energy storage and harvesting systems.47,48 Thus, as part of the advancement in studying MXene, surface modification and functionalization,49 incorporation of bi-functional materials as fillers and spacers,50 application as liquid-like inks,51 and transformation to a 3D structure52,53 are some of the approaches that may ultimately prevent MXene sheet stacking and agglomeration. Another challenging property of MXene is its inclination towards oxidation and thermal stability due to the presence of hydroxyl groups which may limit its further use in high-temperature applications. One effective approach is the defect passivation of MXenes with organic/inorganic ligands in preventing oxidation.55 Another effective approach is to coat MXene with an oxidation-resistant material that will safeguard MXenes from coming into contact with air. Coreâ€“shell structures promote the effective protection of inner materials by coating them with a secondary functional material while having a synergistic effect which has also shown success in supercapacitors,56 drug-delivery,57 membranes,58etc. There have been reports that MXenes as the core with secondary materials as the protective shell have exhibited better oxidation stability compared to MXene alone.59,60 Other strategies for improving the oxidation and thermal stability of MXene are discussed in the next section. Thus, the emergence of MXenes incorporated as either a core or shell will have great potential towards several applications.

In this paper, we reviewed the challenges, material development, and research progress on MXenes incorporated into coreâ€“shell structures. Throughout this review, we have discussed the challenges encountered and the evaluated solutions when using MXenes. The anchoring mechanisms, such as interactions and bond formations, of MXenes adhering to coreâ€“shell structures have also been evaluated. Different structures of the coreâ€“shell, such as fiber, encapsulated, yolkâ€“shell, hollow, and sandwiched structures, have been discussed. Moreover, the incentives of using MXene in coreâ€“shell structures while maintaining its good properties and improving its challenging properties have been evaluated in energy storage and conversion, and other applications. Addressing the challenges of MXene

As summarized in Fig. 2, MXene provides a wide range of properties that can cater to a lot of applications. From their highly conductive nature to having a 2D structure, MXenes have been an ideal candidate for energy storage, catalysis, etc. However, the limitations anchored to MXene, such as stacking and aggregation, also hinder its full utilization in other applications. Even for wet-solution synthesis and preparation of hybrid materials incorporating MXenes, challenges arise to the colloidal suspension and storage of exfoliated MXenes. In this section, we discuss the common challenges encountered with using MXene and evaluate studies that have been providing solutions to address such limitations.

Stacking and aggregation

The common challenge associated with MXene is aggregation. Having a 2D structure like graphene, it is not a secret that MXene tends to self-aggregate due to the presence of strong van der Waals forces.61 The lateral surface area ratio between two precursor materials is essential in determining which kind of coreâ€“shell structure may be formed. Even for the 2D structure, for instance, single-layer GO has two fundamental geometries for interaction, edge-to-edge and face-to-face,62 wherein different sizes of sheets play a factor in how they assemble, coagulate, and aggregate.

Ultrasonication has shown great promise in effectively delaminating, separating, and exfoliating MXene sheets, and hindering stable interlayer bonds from forming again.63 Long-time sonication can greatly yield high production and reduce MXene's lateral flake size, but the final product may be more defective and less porous.7,64 Thus, much attention has been focused on this dramatic aspect of MXenes wherein engineering their structures has been investigated. The specific surface areas of 1D and 2D nanostructures are generally larger than those of their zero-dimensional nanoparticle counterparts, which supports applications utilizing large surface areas. For electrode materials, hierarchical structures, such as nanorods,65 nanowires,31 nanoflakes,36 and nanoneedles,66 have played roles in material integrity especially when subjecting the electrodes to long cycle tests.27,67 To avoid aggregation and restacking, preparation techniques to assemble MXenes are shown in Fig. 3 including 3D hierarchical structures,68,69 hybrids and nanoparticle/ion decoration,70â€“72 and surface functionalization.73 Such a restructuring of MXene may avoid restacking and aggregation by physically placing MXene sheets far from each other, thus utilizing the high surface area of 2D MXene.

Another main drawback of MXene is related to its thermal and oxidative stability at a given range of temperatures. The oxidative degradation of MXene is affected by many factors. For instance, MXene flakes with different lateral sizes are also factored into their lifetime wherein smaller flakes tend to degrade faster than larger flakes.76 The degradation is relative to the more exposed defective sites and compounded exposure of edges for small-sized flakes compared to large flakes.7,77 Iqbal et al.77 evaluated the governing factors affecting the properties of MXene which includes the quality of the parent MAX phase, concentration and type of acid etching agents, mechanical treatments such as ultrasonication, pH, temperature, storage media, etc. For instance, the etchant HF concentration plays a critical role during MAX phase exfoliation, wherein not only Al is etched, but also the inclusion of some Ti atoms takes place.78 In MXenes, atomic defects can occur at both the X site and M site in the form of interstitial, removal or replacement of an atom.79 The strong concentration of HF causes Ti vacancies and defects, which serve as nucleation sites for the oxidation of MXenes. Defective sites are prone to oxidation reactions in the presence of water and/or oxygen, thus, deteriorating and degrading the intrinsic properties of MXenes.11 Similarly, the exposure of such defects to thermal treatment will eventually lead to the different transformations of MXene as shown in Fig. 4a and b. Fig. 4c shows the different approaches to improving the oxidation state of MXenes, wherein a lot of factors have to be considered even at the MAX phase exfoliation stage. The improvement in oxidation stability can also be addressed by passivating the defect using organic and/or inorganic ligands. This strategy blocks the reactivity of water molecules and dissolved oxygen to oxidize MXene sheets.55,80 Another strategy is by incorporating polymers into MXene, which not only improves the oxidation stability but also provides flexibility, high mechanical strength, and suppression of restacking and oxidation of MXene nanosheets.81,82

Exfoliated MXene in a colloidal suspension and its storage

MXenes tend to be hydrophilic due to the presence of water-loving polar surface terminations which makes them easy to prepare in an aqueous solution. Preparing solutions with MXenes is highly important since most of the material preparation includes wet chemical synthesis. However, suspending MXene in aerated aqueous dispersions can initiate the oxidation and degradation of delaminated MXene colloidal solutions.76 Thus, approaches to preparing a stable colloidal suspension containing MXene are explored. For instance, polar solvents showed effectivity in producing stable colloids, with the best MXene dispersion in N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone, N,N-dimethylformamide, propylene carbonate, dimethyl sulfoxide, and ethanol.83 Such stability in polar solvents can withstand aggregation and/or sedimentation of MXene sheets, support a high MXene concentration in colloidal solution, and provide shelf stability over a long time. In order not to expose MXenes to an oxygen-rich environment, storing MXene in sealed containers with argon followed by refrigeration extends the lifetime of MXene colloidal solutions.76 Additionally, the advantage of MXene stored as a colloidal solution is its ease of being redispersed and readiness for processing. On the other hand, under dry conditions, MXenes can maintain their conductivity for three weeks, while a significant drop in conductivity was observed when stored under humid conditions.84

Large-scale synthesis

Synthesis of MXene hugely deals with exfoliation of their MAX phases, wherein high amounts of acids, such as HF and HCl, are essential to selectively remove the reactive group IIIA or IVA elements, and completely oxidize each layer. Thus, exploration of safe, low-cost, and reliable procedures for the large-scale synthesis of MXenes should be carried out. For instance, green synthesis,85 fluorine-free synthesis,86,87 and using low-cost and environmentally friendly precursors88 were some of the methods evaluated to produce MXenes in a shorter time and with high purity, better oxidation properties, and prolonged shelf life. Nevertheless, the consistency and replicability of MXene's admirable properties should be considered when the production is translated into large-scale synthesis.

Preparation of coreâ€“shell structures

The fabrication methods of coreâ€“shell structures start with the choice of a core and a shell. One of the most commonly used cores is silica, having a spherical structure that can be evenly coated with a secondary material, such as metal-based materials.101 Coreâ€“shell structures have been well evaluated in advanced batteries,102 electrocatalysts,103 pharmaceutical and biomedical applications,104 sensing devices,105 and semiconductors.106 The benefit of engineering porous shells in coreâ€“shell structures is to permit free ion access in and out of the core107 that can be utilized in controlled catalytic reactions and targeted drug release activities.108

Due to their flat structure and sheet-like morphology, MXenes are one of the good candidates to be used as shells. For 2D materials such as sheets and flakes which are commonly used for shells, the formation of the coreâ€“shell is hypothesized as shown in Fig. 6. Considering a spherical substrate with a large diameter (d) acting as a core, 2D materials with lateral dimensions (l) smaller than the core prioritize covering the surface of the core until it is completely covered (case I, d > l). As a result, alongside the bonding mechanisms and forces involved in adhesion, the 2D sheets or flakes surround the core evenly, thus forming the typical coreâ€“shell structure. When the lateral size of 2D materials is in excess of the core's diameter (d < l), it is possible that the spherical structures can be embedded, encapsulated, or sandwiched between large 2D materials (case II). Still, other factors may influence and contribute to the shell or sandwich structure formation such as the concentration of MXene used, lateral size and thinness of MXene, etc.109 Likewise, aside from the typical wet-solution synthesis, hydrothermal method, template-assisted method, and spray drying process, methods of preparation that may induce preferred arrangements are also shown in Table 1. Mechanisms involved in anchoring MXenes in coreâ€“shell structures

Systematic studies have been conducted to explain the formation and growth of structures attributed to MXene. For instance, controllable atomistic design of 2D carbides and nitrides based on double transition-metal MXenes has been evaluated to bring different behaviors (thermoelectric, mechanical, magnetic, optical, catalytic, or electrochemical).129,130 For two or more structures, the mechanism of MXenes involved in anchoring from one structure to another is of significant importance. It is a common notion that layer-by-layer self-assembly of 2D structures can be governed by driving forces such as electrostatic interaction, hydrogen bonding, coordination bonding, Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking, and hydrophobic interaction.131 Thus, understanding the properties of MXenes and their adhering and bonding capabilities is essential in realizing their placement in coreâ€“shell structures.

Electrostatic interactions

Electrostatic interactions are common mechanisms usually generated by the attraction and repulsion between charged molecules. He et al.132 explained the interaction of Ti3C2 MXene in coreâ€“shell 2D structures. First, bulk Ti3AlC2 was selectively etched with HF to produce Ti3C2 MXene (Fig. 8a). Then, a Ti3C2 MXene quantum dots (TCQDs) were prepared from Ti3C2 MXene and further functionalized through amino-hydrothermal treatment requiring passivation in ammonia solution followed by the hydrothermal reaction. This treatment produces primary and secondary amine groups through nucleophilic substitution upon the reaction between ammonia and epoxy groups. Coupled with extensive sonication, functionalized TCQDs with a lateral size of 3 nm were successfully prepared. Finally, a TiO2/C3N4/Ti3C2 MXene composite was prepared by electrostatic interactions between the TCQDs on C3N4. The electrostatic potentials were further evaluated using density functional theory to determine the work functions and charge densities between TCQDs and TiO2/C3N4 (Fig. 8b). With C3N4 having a higher Fermi level, C3N4 was compelled to transfer electrons to TCQDs and TiO2 upon satisfying their alignment. This generated an interfacial built-in electric field between C3N4 and TiO2 and between C3N4 and TCQDs. Thus, TCQDs were attached and anchored to TiO2/C3N4 coreâ€“shell structures through effective electrostatic interactions.

Hydrogen bonding

The main driving force between MXene sheets is governed by its strong hydrogen bond interactions and van der Waals forces between layers.72 Due to the strong van der Waals forces, MXene is prone to aggregation and restacking which may limit the full exposure of its functional surface.135 Strategies are implemented not only to enhance the interfacial adhesion of MXene but also to improve its overall mechanical properties, such as better tensile strength and stiffness of MXene sheets.136 Thus, hydrogen bonding is utilized to deliver enhanced mechanical performance in MXenes incorporated in hydrogels137 and composites.138,139

For coreâ€“shell structures, hydrogen bonding has shown effective anchoring of MXene towards a secondary material. Li et al.140 studied the self-assembly of the coreâ€“shell based on graphene and hollow MXene spheres. Initially, Ti3C2Tx/polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) composite spheres were prepared using PMMA spheres and Ti3C2Tx flakes in an aqueous dispersion. Fig. 9a shows the schematic interaction between Ti3C2Tx and PMMA to form composite spheres. After mixing and centrifuging, the interaction involved hydrogen bonding and van der Waals forces between PMMA and Ti3C2Tx. This permitted MXenes to portray a spherical structure, ensuring their wrapping around spherical PMMA as a template. GO is then wrapped around Ti3C2Tx/PMMA composite spheres which are also formed through hydrogen bonding. A hollow spherical MXene enclosed with RGO was successfully prepared and remained tightly attached after removing PMMA spheres through vacuum freezing and thermal treatment (Fig. 9b). Zhao et al.141 evaluated the hydrogen bonding formed in self-assembled coreâ€“shell polydopamine (PDA)@MXene. The MXene colloidal solution and PDA suspension showed stability in water due to their hydrophilic and electrostatic repulsive properties. When combining MXene colloidal solution with the PDA suspension, aggregates formed and appeared at the bottom of the vial while a clear supernatant was observed, suggesting that MXene and PDA have strong interaction. The well-exfoliated ultrathin MXene sheets had shown a wide range of size distribution ranging from approximately a hundred nanometers to microns. This allowed small-size MXene sheets to form hydrogen bonds on the surface of PDA, forming coreâ€“shell microspheres, while large-size sheets linked the coreâ€“shell microspheres simultaneously.

Yolkâ€“shell and hollow structures

Yolkâ€“shell and hollow structures are unique structures with tailored physicochemical properties which can be applied in versatile applications. Yolkâ€“shell structures are composed of an interior core and a hollow shell, creating a void space in between them. On the other hand, hollow structures are composed of a shell with a defined thickness and lack the core part. Many cases showed the preparation of hollow structures in three steps. Primarily in these first two steps, it resembles the formation of any coreâ€“shell mechanism retaining the two main structures of the core and shell. Future directions in research

For the past few years, research and development have catered to the exploration and applicability of MXene towards other metalâ€“carbon/nitride combinations, and further investigations in various scientific fields. Likewise, tremendous efforts have also been devoted to studying their advantageous properties and determining their vulnerabilities which helps in fully understanding how MXenes work. Coreâ€“shell structures have shown effective integration of MXenes, not only to share their beneficial properties but also, to address their limitations. In this review, we first highlighted the efficient assembly and attachment mechanisms of MXenes in coreâ€“shell, encapsulated, yolkâ€“shell, hollow, and sandwiched structures. We have also evaluated the encountered limitations of MXenes and the approaches to address these concerns. In summary, challenges arose in the mechanical stability of MXene, particularly, its susceptibility toward oxidation, and the possibility of aggregating and stacking.

Thus, the future direction of utilizing MXenes is summarized below:

â€¢ Utilizing metallic elements from MXene to grow secondary compounds in situ. In this case, reforming the structure of the core and shell with stronger interaction/bonds while maintaining the same metal core can be utilized. Like MOFs,203 the metal core may be further used to derive another compound which may have beneficial properties, while maintaining MXene's structure and properties.

â€¢ In addressing the aggregation and stacking challenges of MXenes, there are three approaches summarized in Fig. 3. First, the preparation of hierarchical structures is not only limited to coreâ€“shell structures but also includes tuning their morphology into 1D, 2D and 3D structures. Encapsulation of integrated electrodes and hierarchical dimensional structures, such as nanoflakes, nanorods, nanowires, etc., using MXene as an outer shell has yet to be discovered. Second, its integration with other materials to enhance its mechanical integrity, oxidation stability, etc. as an outer shell should be investigated. Avenues in using cheap carbon materials, such as coal and biomass,115,204 can be utilized as well. Third, functionalization and surface passivation of MXenes can be researched and experimented to achieve stronger bonds with the core or shell. Likewise, oxygen-bonds to bridge the metal core of MXene towards another metal-containing shell and vice versa can be utilized to form stronger bonds.

â€¢ When evaluating MXenes as shells, the edge-to-edge and face-to-face placement of the sheets should be investigated. Ways to control the lateral area of 2D MXene, such as size reduction, can be investigated to allow maximized interface handling when used as a shell.

â€¢ Theoretical and computational studies have great opportunities to predict and satisfy experimental studies on coreâ€“shell anchorage. This may aid in better understanding and prediction of functional group terminations of MXene shifting towards other chalcogenide and non-oxide/hydroxide/fluoride groups which can be a new avenue for material development.
