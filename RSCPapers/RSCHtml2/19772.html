Abstract

Molybdenum oxides have emerged as an important entity for optoelectronics due to their wide band gap and carrier-selective transportation property. However, there is little progress in the development of high-performance molybdenum oxide based photodetectors. Herein, a fast response and high detectivity (âˆ¼1.27 Ã— 1013 Jones) photodetector based on the asymmetric heterojunction of MoO3âˆ’x/Si with a nanoporous pyramid array (NPPA) Si structure was reported. The device shows a broad linear dynamic range (LDR) over 116.6 dB and bandwidth over 50 kHz. Furthermore, the temperature-dependence of photoresponse was systematically investigated to probe the carrier transportation within the heterojunction. A model is proposed in which carrier diffusion dominates the photoresponse performance. Finally, the temperature-resistant NPPA based heterojunction photodetector is successfully integrated into a visible light communication system and its application as an optical signal receiver for transmitting texts is demonstrated. These findings suggest that our transition metal oxide based asymmetric heterojunction photodetectors have great application potential in optical communication.

1. Introduction

Photodetectors (PDs) can convert optical signal into electrical signal, which is a critical component for imaging and optical communication applications. Especially, PDs with intelligent characteristics1â€“5 are important for other optoelectronic nano-systems. Most of the commercially available photodetectors are currently based on a pâ€“iâ€“n device architecture, which usually needs high temperature treatment, doping, and complicated manufacturing processes. Recently, due to their unique optoelectronic and structural properties,6â€“8 high-efficient photodetectors based on mixed-dimensional heterojunctions have been extensively studied as an alternative to traditional semiconductors in photodetection. Typical configurations of heterojunctions include quantum dots/graphene (0D/2D),9 CNT/ZnO coreâ€“shell (1D/1D),10 van der Waals heterostructures (2D/2D),11 and carbon nanotube/graphene (1D/2D).12 Such mixed-dimensional heterostructures herald opportunities for investigating fundamental nanoscience and developing nanoscale optoelectronic applications. However, limited by weak light absorption, low carrier mobility, and incompatibility with commercial silicon electronics, most of the reported photodetectors exhibit low response speed under bias, along with low photoelectrical conversion efficiency (PCE), hampering the practical applications in optical communication systems. Hence, the fabrication of a heterojunction by combing low-dimensional materials with traditional bulk semiconductors is one promising strategy for constructing high-performance optoelectronic devices. For example, the fabricated hybrid graphene/silicon heterojunction PD shows a bandwidth of 40 GHz.13 Additionally, Jie's group reported a solution-processed 3D RGO-MoS2/Si heterojunction PD showing an ultra-broadband photoresponse (350 nm to 4.3 Î¼m).14

One promising way to enhance the photodetecting performance is by manipulating the geometry of heterojunctions for higher light-absorption efficiency.10,15 By optimizing the geometry of the heterostructure, both the efficiencies of light-trapping and carrier-collection are expected to be largely improved. Additionally, the use of dopant-free asymmetric heterocontacts with transition metal oxides (TMOs) was reported to be another effective way of improving the PCE of n-type silicon heterojunctions,16,17 because molybdenum oxide shows weak Coulomb scattering and high hole-mobility.18,19 The reported maximum PCE of MoO3âˆ’x/n-Si solar cells20 has already surpassed their silicon homojunction counterpart.21 It is interesting to note that TMO serves as a bi-functional layer that can effectively passivate the Si surface while selectively conducting holes.22 Molybdenum oxides have been successfully applied in various applications, such as solar cells,21â€“23 photodetectors,24 and light emitting diodes.25 However, PDs based on molybdenum oxides have shown little progress due to their lower than expected photoresponsivity and response speed. Furthermore, the reported response speed ranges from ten Î¼s to hundreds of ms, along with a narrow bandwidth, which greatly limited their application in optical communication. It has been reported that PDs based on MoO3 nanobelts show a typical response speed of a few minutes. The reason is usually ascribed to the low carrier concentration, low carrier separation efficiency, and high rate of surface recombination.24,26 It was reported that hierarchical MoO3âˆ’x/n-Si heterojunctions with photovoltaic mode show promising potential in high-speed photodetectors.27 Unfortunately, the hierarchical structure exhibits poor mechanical properties as Si nanowires can be easily damaged under stress. It is of great importance to design more unique architectures with robust structures, and further demonstrate the practical applications of the unique heterojunction photodetector. Visible light communication is becoming an alternative choice for indoor wireless technology by offering low cost, unregulated bandwidth and ubiquitous infrastructure support. Most heterojunction PDs exhibit a low response speed from micro-seconds to milliseconds, hampering their practical application in optical communication systems. Therefore, the fabrication of heterojunction PDs with a faster response speed is important for optical communication applications.28

Herein, we report a novel nanoporous pyramid Si array based high performance photodetector. The temperature dependence of the photoresponse characteristics of the heterojunction is investigated in detail. The novel heterojunction with a NPPA structure exhibits both high absorption and robust mechanical properties. In addition, most of the previous works overlooked the carrier transportation behaviour within the heterojunction.27,29â€“32 A carrier transportation mechanism within the heterojunction has been proposed, and the dependence of a built-in electric field and concentration gradient are found to mainly account for the performance enhancement of the PDs. Finally, we successfully demonstrate the use of our PDs in an optical communication system to transmit texts.

2. Experimental

Materials and characterization

N-Type (100) double polished mono-crystalline Si wafers (resistivity of 1.5â€“2.6 Î© cm) with a thickness of 450 Â± 10 Î¼m were ultrasonically cleaned in acetone, ethanol, and deionized water in sequence. Then, the substrate was cleaned with O2 plasma for 10 minutes. The typical pyramid structure was obtained on the clean Si wafer by immersing it in 0.2 wt% sodium hydroxide and 10 wt% ethanol at 80 Â°C for 30 min. Then, the substrate was immersed in dilute hydrochloric acid to remove the residual alkali and cleaned with deionized water. Si pyramids coated with a nanoporous structure were fabricated by immersing the Si pyramid substrate into a solution of 2.4 M hydrofluoric acid (HF) and 0.01 M silver nitrate (AgNO3) for 10 min at room temperature. Then, the sample was immersed in a solution of HNO3 (30% w/w) to remove all the residual sliver particles on the surface of the Si and cleaned with deionized water. The surface morphology of the Si structure and cross-sectional structure of the device were characterized using a scanning electron microscope (Zeiss Ultra 55). Reflection spectroscopy was undertaken using Elight's QE-R system, with BaTiO3 as a standard sample.

Device fabrication

The CH3-terminated Si surface was processed in a glove box. The Si substrates are firstly immersed in an aqueous solution of superfluous phosphorus pentachloride (PCl5) in chlorobenzene (CB) at 100 Â°C for 1 h. Then, the sample was cleaned with CB and tetrahydrofuran (THF) and immersed in a solution of CH3MgCl (1 M) in THF at 80 Â°C for 8 h. The CH3-terminated surface was formed after the sample was rinsed with THF and methyl alcohol. Afterward, an Al (100 nm) top electrode was deposited on the back of the Si substrate by thermal evaporation. A MoO3âˆ’x thin film was deposited on the surface of the Si structure by thermal evaporation at a pressure of 5 Ã— 10âˆ’6 mbar. The thickness was monitored by a crystal oscillator, and the optimized thickness is 10 nm. Finally, silver grids with a thickness of 100 nm were deposited on the surface of the MoO3âˆ’x thin film by thermal evaporation as the top electrode through a shadow mask. The effective device area was determined to be 0.3 cm2.

Photoresponse measurement

The self-powered characteristics were first measured by a Keithley 2400 source meter under the condition of AM 1.5 G using a solar simulator (Abet Technologies). Enlitech's QE-R system calibrated by a standard Si detector S10-14010 (300â€“1100 nm) was used to measure the EQE of all heterojunction PDs. The transient photoresponse behaviour of the photodetector was measured using the photo-induced open-circuit voltage decay method under a QE-R system or pulsed laser. Responsivity of the photodetector for the broad band spectrum was measured by a QE-R system with a light chopper (f = 133 Hz). The reversibly switched photoresponse characteristic of the photodetector was measured under 650 nm (laser, 118 mW cmâˆ’2). The laser was turned on/off regularly by a function generator. The output current was recorded and read by a Keithley 2601A source meter. Photoresponse speeds of the PDs were evaluated by combining a pulse laser and a digital oscilloscope (Tektronix TDS 2012C). The temperature was tested by a cryogenic test system that consisted of a liquid nitrogen thermostat and temperature controller (Homemade, TC202, with accuracy of +0.1 Co).

Fig. 4c shows the dependence of photoresponse times on the light intensity, and two characteristic regions can be found according to the different trends of rise and fall times under different light intensities. In region I, the rise time (Ï„r) rapidly decreases from 30 Î¼s to 1 Î¼s with the increase of power density from 3.9 mW cmâˆ’2 to 118 mW cmâˆ’2. However, when the light intensity further increases over 118 mW cmâˆ’2, the rise time tends to saturate at âˆ¼1 Î¼s. It is well known that the rise time is largely dependent on the carrier separation/transportation processes. Since the interfacial passivation of the MoO3âˆ’x/Si heterojunction remains the same (CH3 passivation), the carrier transportation process should dominate the rise time. To better illustrate the carrier transportation characteristic within the heterojunction region, we analyzed the two typical layers (drifting layer and diffusing layer) individually. Considering the doping concentration (ND) of silicon is about 2 Ã— 1015 cmâˆ’3 and the thickness of the depletion layer (W) near the heterojunction can be estimated to be âˆ¼230 nm, the drifting time of the hole is about 2 ps. The detailed calculations are shown in the ESI,â€  Calculations. Since the thickness of silicon is a few hundreds of micrometers, the electron diffusion process in silicon plays a dominant role in the photoresponse. Based on the equation of carrier diffusion,

Â 
F = âˆ’Dn(dn/dx)(4)
the flux of the photocarrier (F) is determined by the carrier concentration gradient dn/dx.
Â 
Dn = Î¼n(KT/q)(5)
where Dn is the diffusion coefficient (constant at room temperature) and defined by the Einstein relation, k is the Boltzmann constant, T is Kelvin temperature, q is the absolute value of electron charge, and Î¼n is the electron mobility. This clearly indicates that the higher the photocarrier gradient dn/dx, the higher the photocarrier flux. As the photoresponse time depends on the carrier diffusion process, a higher photocarrier flux corresponds to a faster response speed. However, there exists a saturated carrier concentration under critical light intensity. This accounts for the stable rise time when the light intensity is larger than 118 mW cmâˆ’2. On the other hand, the fall time depends on the recombination rate of the photocarrier. In region I, the fall time decreases from 104 Î¼s to 41 Î¼s with the increase of power density from 3.9 mW cmâˆ’2 to 118 mW cmâˆ’2. This process is consistent with the tendency that rise time decreased as the light intensity increased. It is reasonable to believe that the fall time becomes shorter with the increase of light intensity (region I). The fall time is largely dependent on the carrier recombination process, which is sensitive to the interfacial defect states. The higher the light intensity, the larger the amount of recombination centers located within the band-gap. However, in region II, the fall time slows down with a further increase in light intensity. Since the number of defect states remains saturated, the higher concentration of photocarrier would need a longer time to recombine.

Temperature dependence of the MoO3âˆ’x/NPPA Si heterojunction photodetector

Detailed measurements can be found in the Experiment section and Fig. S4 (ESIâ€ ). For an ideal diode, the ideality factor (n) and barrier height (Î¦B) should not be influenced by temperature. Nonetheless, these values are highly temperature dependent for a non-ideal heterojunction, which contributes to the combined effects of leakage current and series resistance. In Fig. 5d, the reverse saturation current at âˆ’1 V bias at different temperatures is plotted. It can be clearly seen that the temperature has a remarkable influence on the dark current. With the increase of temperature, the dark current shows a linear increase. However, the photocurrent exhibits the opposite dependence, namely, it is insensitive to temperature. It was reported that the dark current is determined by the number of thermally activated carriers. Under the assistance of temperature (thermal energy), more carriers would surpass the Schottky barrier, resulting in the increase of dark current under a bias of âˆ’1 V. The photocurrent is mainly ascribed to the photo-excited carriers, which largely depend on the wavelength of light. This accounts for the insensitive dependence of photocurrent on temperature.

To better understand the carrier transportation mechanism within the heterojunction, a series of rise/fall times under varied temperature was measured. As shown in Fig. 5e, the response time is highly sensitive to the temperature. Two regions can be found according to the different dependence trend of rise and fall times on the temperature. It can be seen that the rise time first increases in the range of 78 K to 225 K, then it decreases almost linearly with a further increase of the temperature. However, in the carrier recombination process, the fall time first shows a slow increase from 78 K to 200 K, and then sharply decreases when the temperature further increases from 200 K to 320 K, then it remains relatively stable when the temperature exceeds 320 K. The same trend of temperature-dependent photoresponse characteristics can also be found in the planar and pyramid heterojunctions (as shown in Fig. S5b, ESI,â€  and Fig. 5c).

The mechanism accounting for the temperature dependence of photoresponse characteristics is illustrated schematically in Fig. 5f. The carrier transportation length consists of two parts: a carrier drifting layer and diffusion layer. The rise time of PDs depends on the transportation of photo-generated carriers, which involves the separation, transportation, and the extraction of carriers to the electrodes. Given that the carrier mobility in ultrathin molybdenum oxide (âˆ¼10 nm) is larger than 1100 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1,57 the transportation of carriers from the MoO3âˆ’x layer to the Ag electrode should be negligible. Therefore, electron transportation determines the overall photoresponse speed of the heterojunctions. It is estimated that an electron needs only a few picoseconds to pass the drifting layer under the built-in electric field (Eb). However, by assumption of weak surface recombination in silicon, it needs up to 73 Î¼s to cross the diffusion layer, which is quite close to the reported value for n-type silicon with the same doping level (see Calculation methods, ESIâ€ ).

According to the classical theory of semiconductors, both impurity scattering and lattice scattering affect the carrier transportation. Given the weak doping of silicon wafer (2 Ã— 1015 cmâˆ’3), it is proposed that lattice scattering becomes dominant in the low temperature range. The lower the temperature, the weaker the lattice scattering, corresponding to the higher electron mobility that accounts for the faster rise time at lower temperature. However, when temperature rises up to >200 K, the rise time becomes faster and the underlying reason is the enhanced built-in electric field (Ebi). As mentioned above, the barrier height of the heterojunction increases with the increase of temperature (Fig. 5c). The larger the built-in electric field, the greater the electron velocity would be accelerated before arriving at the diffusion layer. The energized electrons (hot electrons) contribute to the faster diffusion of carriers, further resulting in a shorter rise time with the increase of temperature.

On the other hand, the fall time depends on the recombination rate of carriers. In the low temperature region (78â€“200 K), the photo-generated electrons show less sensitivity to defect scattering, thus exhibiting a relatively stable fall time.

There is a large fluctuation of the photovoltage in the low temperature region for heterojunctions with planar, pyramid and NPPA structures (Fig. S5, ESIâ€ ). However, when the temperature exceeds 200 K, the defect scattering effect becomes dominant and impurity centers are thermally activated, and this causes the increase of recombination probability, which accounts for the sharp decrease of fall time. It is worth noting that the fall time becomes stable with a further increase of temperature (320â€“420 K), which results from the saturated density of defect centers. Additionally, the MoO3âˆ’x/NPPA Si heterojunction photodetector shows excellent temperature resistance after the low and high temperature measurements. As shown in Fig. S5d (ESIâ€ ), the photodetecting performance of the heterojunction photodetector can recover its original level after the temperature cycling process, which indicates the high stability of this novel heterojunction photodetector.

Furthermore, we integrated the as-fabricated MoO3âˆ’x/NPPA Si heterojunction photodetector into a visible light communication (VLC) system as a light signal receiver. It is well-known that VLC is an evolving technology for forthcoming high data rate wireless communication, holding the promise of high-speed, high energy-saving, interference immunity and high-security characteristics. Meanwhile, VLC shows superiority of light condition matching, since it avoids the use of focus light in a thin fiber. Herein, a prototype VLC system integrated with our MoO3âˆ’x/NPPA Si photodetector as light receiver was built. Contrary to a commercial photodetector (light receiver), the present MoO3âˆ’x/NPPA Si photodetector is self-powered and does not need external power. As shown in Fig. 6a, data flow from a computer was transferred into high and low voltage levels using a driver. Then, the voltage level was used to switch the laser (660 nm) to output a modulated light, which can be received by the MoO3âˆ’x/NPPA Si photodetector. The output photovoltage was firstly amplified with a preamplifier and comparator and then input to a driver, which transferred the voltage signal into data that were received by a computer. 4. Conclusions

In summary, we demonstrated a high performance heterojunction photodetector with a unique nanoporous pyramid Si structure. The unique NPPA Si structure shows excellent light trapping and carrier collection efficiencies. The obtained heterojunction photodetector exhibits high detectivity, fast response speed, and an excellent linear dynamic range of 116.6 dB. Additionally, the carrier transportation characteristics within the heterojunction were investigated at different temperatures. The results indicate that the interfacial barrier height can be largely tuned by the temperature. A carrier transportation model is proposed in which carrier diffusion dominates the photoresponse characteristics, and the concentration gradient of the minority carrier in silicon determines the photoresponse time. Moreover, the MoO3âˆ’x/NPPA Si photodetector shows high resistance to low/high temperature cycling measurements. Finally, we successfully integrated the MoO3âˆ’x/NPPA Si photodetector into a visible light communication system and demonstrated the first transition metal oxide based prototype self-powered signal receiver. Our results suggest that the high speed and stable MoO3âˆ’x/NPPA Si photodetector may have great potential in optical communication applications.
