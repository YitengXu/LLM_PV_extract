Abstract

A new di(isopropoxy)boryl â€“B(OiPr)2 tellurophene precursor is described, from which several previously inaccessible phosphorescent borylated tellurophenes are formed via exchange of the â€“OiPr groups. One such tellurophene Mes(iPrO)B-Te-6-B(OiPr)Mes, bearing a sterically encumbered mesityl (Mes) substituent at each boron center, exhibits bright yellow-orange phosphorescence in the solid state at room temperature and in the presence of the known quencher O2. Furthermore, Suzukiâ€“Miyaura cross-coupling between the newly prepared borylated tellurophenes and the test substrate 2-bromothiophene was examined with the pre-catalyst Cl(XPhos)Pd(aminobiphenyl). While more electron deficient boryl groups such as catecholatoboryl (â€“Bcat) yield significant protodeboronation in place of productive Câ€“C bond formation, efficient formation of the desired thiophene-capped tellurophene thienyl-Te-6-thienyl was noted from tellurophenes bearing the readily accessible pinacolatoboryl (â€“Bpin) and 1,8-naphthalenediaminatoboryl (â€“Bdan) functional groups. These findings open the door for the efficient synthesis of aryl tellurophenes and polytellurophenes via the ubiquitous Suzukiâ€“Miyaura coupling of borylated tellurophenes, which was previously hampered by protodeboronation.

Introduction

Borylated compounds represent indispensable building blocks towards a variety of functional materials, ranging from fine chemicals to Ï€-conjugated materials for optoelectronic applications. Most often, these borylated precursors are amenable to Suzukiâ€“Miyaura cross coupling, arguably the most popular method for forming C(sp2)â€“C(sp2) linkages.1 This synthetic method offers many advantages over existing coupling reactions, including high functional group tolerance and water stability.

The electron-accepting ability of the three-coordinate boron centers found within boryl substituents â€“BR2 also enables their use as colorimetric anion sensors, and as acceptor units within luminescent compounds for OLED and bioimaging applications.2 The introduction of boryl-substituents to thiophenes has also led to an interesting class of fluorescent molecules and polymers, with air- and moisture-stability being a hallmark of these materials.3 A commonly employed route to attach boryl groups onto Ï€-conjugated heterocycles involves iridium-catalyzed Câ€“H functionalization,4â€“6 while metal-free approaches are now emerging as viable alternatives.7 However, the preparation of borylated congeners of thiophenes, such as the heavy element-containing selenophenes (Se) and tellurophenes (Te), pose additional challenges.8 In particular, the enhanced reactivity of tellurophenes means that pre-existing routes to ring functionalization can lead to competitive oxidation at the electron-rich Te atom or even ring-cleavage in some instances.9â€“11

Recently, the study of tellurophenes has undergone a renaissance, spurred by their advantageous properties for organic photovoltaics (OPVs),12â€“20 organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs),21â€“25 and in organic field effect transistors (OFETs).15,16,26â€“31 Of particular note, OPVs featuring a tellurophene-based triplet exciton acceptor unit led to power conversion efficiencies of up to 7.5% (Chart 1), and opens a path to simplified (layered) device architectures.19 Moreover, a few years ago our group uncovered efficient room temperature phosphorescence from a series of 2,5-borylated tellurophenes in the solid state (Chart 1), including the difficult to achieve retention of triplet state-mediated emission in the presence of O2 (a known quencher of phosphorescence).21 Lastly, our group and others have demonstrated the ability of tellurophenes to undergo polymerization via Suzukiâ€“Miyaura cross-coupling, although more often between difficult to prepare halogenated tellurophenes and aryl boronic acids.13,28,32,33 In this regard, stable and synthetically accessible Bpin-functionalized tellurophenes (Bpin = pinacolatoboryl) are promising substrates for cross-coupling, however one potential drawback is their propensity to undergo base-promoted protodeboronation.34

In this paper we introduce new boryl (â€“BR2) groups at tellurophenes to yield phosphorescence and reinvestigate ways to suppress protodeboronation during ring functionalization via Suzukiâ€“Miyaura coupling. The new derivatized tellurophenes are obtained via a general boryl group forming protocol starting from reactive â€“B(OiPr)2 substituents. The resulting products bearing boron-bound catecholato (cat), tert-butyl catecholato (tBucat) and 1,8-napthalenediaminato (dan) groups were previously inaccessible under the same conditions used to synthesize Bpin-functionalized tellurophenes. The products of the successful condensation chemistry were studied by single-crystal X-ray crystallography and where possible, solid state luminescence measurements. Finally, their use in high yielding Suzukiâ€“Miyaura cross-coupling reactions is also demonstrated.

Postulating that the absence of a boryl ring chelate in (iPrO)2B-Te-6-B(OiPr)2 would lead to enhanced reactivity of the â€“B(OiPr)2 groups, this compound was combined with various potentially chelating nucleophiles, as well as the Grignard reagent MesMgBr. Addition of the less nucleophilic diol catechol (1,2-dihydroxybenzene) to (iPrO)2B-Te-6-B(OiPr)2 also led to boryl-substitution to yield the new tellurophene catB-Te-6-Bcat within one hour (Scheme 2). Previous work has shown that a decrease in phosphorescence quantum yield can occur if close intermolecular Teâ‹¯Te contacts are found in the solid state (i.e. below the sum of the van der Waals radii of 4.2 Ã…).21,46 The rational is that close contacts facilitate tripletâ€“triplet annihilation, leading to quenching of phosphorescence. Encouragingly, we found that the closest Teâ‹¯Te contact in the solid (crystalline) state was 4.932(1) Ã… in tBucatB-Te-6-BtBucat, while all other tellurophenes show Teâ‹¯Te separations >5.0 Ã…. Although faint emission was observed for solid state samples of catB-Te-6-Bcat, tBucatB-Te-6-BtBucat, and danB-Te-6-Bdan at 77 K (Fig. S3 in the ESIâ€ ), only Mes(iPrO)B-Te-6-B(OiPr)Mes showed bright emission at room temperature. The much brighter emission observed for frozen solutions in MeTHF in place of cold (77 K) pure solid samples for the â€“Bcat, â€“BtBucat and â€“Bdan analogues, suggests that some self quenching might be occurring in the pure bulk phase, where closer contacts in the amorphous solids (vs. in crystals) might enable self-quenching to occur. Nevertheless, the observed luminescence at 77 K (both in solid state and in dilute solution) indicates that the energy requirements for phosphorescence, i.e. close excited state singlet (Sn) and triplet (Tn) energy levels, have been met. The observation of the brightest solid state emission for Mes(iPrO)B-Te-6-B(OiPr)Mes could be due to favorable solid state packing/morphology effects, since a restriction of molecular motion, a limit of close intermolecular contacts, and a reduction of diffusion of O2 through the solid can all lead to less efficient non-radiative decay (i.e. less quenching of emission). In addition, Mes(iPrO)B-Te-6-B(OiPr)Mes shows very weak phosphorescence in THF (under N2), which becomes much more intense in the solid state. In line with prior studies,21 this tellurophene shows aggregation-induced (enhanced) emission, wherein restriction of intramolecular motion in the solid/aggregated state suppresses non-radiative decay pathways, leading to enhanced photoluminescence.47

We also investigated the ability of our new borylated tellurophenes to act as reagents in Suzukiâ€“Miyaura cross-coupling. Although pinB-Te-6-Bpin has previously been used in Suzukiâ€“Miyaura cross-coupling to synthesize aryl-functionalized tellurophenes25 as well as low molecular weight copolymers,13 protodeboronation remained a continuous problem, preventing access to high molecular weight polymers. In these prior Suzukiâ€“Miyaura coupling reactions, common pre-catalysts such as Pd(OAc)2 or Pd2dba3 were used. In 2010, Buchwald and co-workers reported the one-pot synthesis of an active palladium pre-catalyst featuring a 2-aminobiphenyl ligand, Cl(XPhos)Pd(aminobiphenyl), that is activated under mild conditions (40 Â°C, reaction time â‰¤2 h, catalyst loading of 2 mol%).49 This pre-catalyst was effective at limiting protodeboronation during the Suzukiâ€“Miyaura cross-coupling reactions of furan- and thiophene-containing boronic acids.49 We were curious if this catalytic system was also suitable for electron-rich tellurophene substrates, which are even more prone to protodeboronation.34 We began by examining the coupling of pinB-Te-6-Bpin with two equivalents of 2-bromothiophene using Cl(XPhos)Pd(aminobiphenyl) as the pre-catalyst (Scheme 3). To our delight, the cross-coupling reaction successfully formed the known compound thienyl-Te-6-thienyl, with no detectable amounts of the known protodeboronated tellurophenes H-Te-6-H or pinB-Te-6-H.34 Although slightly elevated temperatures and catalyst loadings were required, this reaction still proceeds under mild conditions (including 7 mol% catalyst loadings at 50 Â°C for 16 h) giving a very high isolated yield (91%) of thienyl-Te-6-thienyl. In comparison, the use of Pd(OAc)2 or Pd2dba3 as pre-catalysts gave yields lower than 50%.34

Conclusions

In summary, a new di(isopropoxy)borate-functionalized tellurophene (iPrO)2B-Te-6-B(OiPr)2 has been synthesized and used to yield new borylated tellurophenes viaiOPr group exchange. This method was employed to synthesize four previously inaccessible tellurophenes: catB-Te-6-Bcat, tBucatB-Te-6-BtBucat, danB-Te-6-Bdan and the mesityl-functionalized product Mes(iPrO)B-Te-6-B(OiPr)Mes. Mes(iPrO)B-Te-6-B(OiPr)Mes yields bright yellow-orange phosphorescence with a respectable quantum yield of 15% in the solid state and in air, which are both conditions that typically quench phosphorescence. Finally, the performance of borylated tellurophenes in Suzukiâ€“Miyaura cross coupling reactions was investigated using the pre-catalyst and Cl(XPhos)Pd(aminobiphenyl), leading to mild reaction conditions and much less protodeboronation when tellurophenes bearing â€“Bpin and â€“Bdan groups are used as substrates. Future work will involve using the improved functionalization/cross-coupling strategy described here to gain access to high molecular weight polytellurophenes with small HOMOâ€“LUMO gaps for solar cell applications.
