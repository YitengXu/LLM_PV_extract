Efficient Nd3+ sensitized Yb3+ emission and infrared-to-visible energy conversion in gallium nano-garnets

Abstract

Nanocrystalline RE3Ga5O12 (RE = Gd, Y and Lu) garnets co-doped with 1 mol% of Nd3+ and 10 mol% of Yb3+ ions have been synthesized via a sol–gel method and their structural and luminescence properties studied. Upon laser excitation at 805 nm, an intense near-infrared emission from Yb3+ ions at 1025 nm is observed, which is due to efficient Nd3+ → Yb3+ energy transfer with a probability of about 90%. Lifetimes of the 4G7/2 and the 4F3/2 emitting levels of Nd3+ ions and the 2F5/2 excited level of Yb3+ ions have been evaluated upon pulsed laser excitations. The non-exponential nature of the decay curves has been studied to understand the multipolar interaction between Nd3+ and/or Yb3+ ions using the Inokuti–Hirayama model. Moreover, under 970 nm laser excitation, green upconverted luminescence is observed in Nd3+–Yb3+ co-doped RE3Ga5O12 nano-garnets, which can be related to both phonon-assisted excited-state absorption and energy transfer upconversion mechanisms. The results show that the as-synthesized nano-garnets could be useful for infrared detectors, solar energy converters and green phosphor applications.

1. Introduction

Trivalent rare earth (RE3+) ion doped crystals with emissions at around 1 μm have been attracting great attention for their potential applications in continuous wave and pulsed lasers, scintillators, optical bistability, solar energy harvesting in c-Si solar cells, optical sensors, etc.1–4 To obtain 1 μm emission, the trivalent ytterbium (Yb3+) ion is the most favorable with a series of advantages over other RE3+ ions such as longer lifetime, the absence of cross-relaxation or excited-state absorption, low quantum defect that leads to reduction in thermal load, wide absorption and emission bands that are necessary to generate ultra-short laser-pulses through a mode-locking technique, etc.4 The rich and unique energy level structure of RE3+ ions permits effective spectral modification of the emission through energy transfer between neighbouring RE3+ ions for enhancing the efficiency of solar cells.5 Moreover, Yb3+ emission can still be enhanced by sensitizing with appropriate RE3+ ion with optimized doping concentration through energy transfer processes. Among different RE3+ ions, Nd3+ ion is good sensitizer with intense absorption bands in UV-visible-NIR regions with variety of low cost pumping options and characterized by a fast non-radiative relaxation decays to the 4F3/2 emitting level, whose luminescence gives rise to different emission bands in the near-infrared range from 890 to 1400 nm, quite favorable for absorption of the Yb3+ ions.4,6

In this context, the selection of suitable host matrix to achieve efficient luminescence at around 1 μm through energy transfer has become a subject of interest. Even though RE3+ doped fluoride crystals are efficient luminescent materials due to their low phonon energy, high quantum yield and long radiative lifetimes, they exhibit less phase stability compared to oxide crystals.7–10 For example, Eu3+-doped YF3 nanocrystals transformed from orthorhombic to hexagonal phase when pressure is increased from ambient (0 GPa) to 12 GPa.10 But oxide crystals have been proved as thermally, chemically and mechanically stable materials.11,12 In particular, garnets are the best host matrices among oxide crystals due to their high solubility of RE3+ ions, wide transparency from UV to mid-IR and relatively low-energy phonons. Explicitly, gallium garnets are best hosts for RE3+ ions compared to aluminium garnets since replacement of Al3+ by Ga3+ ions in the RE3Al5O12 (RE = Gd, Y and Lu) crystals would decrease its band gap and thus might reduce defect states through anti-sites by lowering the conduction band below to the defect states which results in efficient luminescence.13–16 Moreover, lattice dynamics and ab initio studies of structural, elastic, and vibrational properties of gallium nano-garnets under high pressure indicate that these nano-garnets are mechanically stable up to about 90 GPa and then transformed to amorphous state beyond this pressure.17,18 It was also reported that Ho3+ doped gallium nano-garnets emits more intense emission compared to Ho3+:Y3Al5O12 (YAG) nano-garnets.19 Hence, it is interesting to investigate the structural and luminescence properties of Nd3+–Yb3+ co-doped gallium nano-garnets.

Structural and luminescence properties of Nd3+–Yb3+ ions doped Gd3Ga5O12 (GGG), YAG, Sc2O3, Y2O3, YAlO3, Al4B2O9, and YAl3(BO3)4 crystals have been reported.20–27 Jia et al., observed the Nd3+ sensitized Yb3+ emission at around 1025 nm due to energy transfer process from Nd3+ to Yb3+ in GGG single crystal grown by Czochralski technique.20 Lupei et al., studied the effect of Yb3+ ion concentration on Nd3+ emission and Nd3+ → Yb3+ energy transfer process in YAG, Y2O3, and Sc2O3 transparent ceramics synthesized by solid state ceramic technique.21–23 It is known that Czochralski technique is a quite costly, time consuming, and requires high temperature. In addition, the control of composition is not an easy task when growing single crystals. On the other side, solid state ceramic technique involves repeated grinding, sintering under vacuum condition, more time consuming, and high temperature. The synthesis technique that has to be adopted in the present investigation is simple, low cost, lower reaction temperature, easier composition control and environmentally benign and successfully prepared the GGG, YGG, and LuGG nano-garnets in a single phase cubic structure.

Maia et al. reported the structural and luminescence properties of Nd3+/Yb3+ doped Al4B2O9 nanocrystals.26 Rai et al., studied the upconversion luminescence in Nd3+ doped Ga10Ge25S65 glass.28 In spite of all these studies, there are no reports on Yb3+ ion sensitised Nd3+ emission i.e., infrared to visible energy conversion in gallium nano-garnets. Upconversion inorganic nanocrystals have technological applications such as fluorescent labels for sensitive detection of bio-molecules, photochemical reaction, bio-imaging, optical encoding, display technologies, etc.29–32

Hence, the present investigation aims to study on Nd3+ sensitized Yb3+ emission, as well as Yb3+ sensitized Nd3+ upconversion luminescence in gallium nano-garnets. The effect of Yb3+ co-doping on the structural, optical and luminescence properties of Nd3+:RE3Ga5O12 nano-garnets (RE = Gd, Y and Lu) have been studied. Luminescent decay curves for 4G7/2 and 4F3/2 levels of Nd3+ ions and 2F5/2 level of Yb3+ ions are analyzed by the Inokuti–Hirayama model to know the kind of multipolar interaction responsible for energy transfer between Nd3+ and/or Yb3+ ions. The Nd3+ → Yb3+ energy transfer efficiency for intense emission at 1025 nm has been evaluated. Upon 970 nm laser excitation, intense green upconverted luminescence is observed to the naked eye and the mechanisms involved in the upconversion process has been analysed by time-resolved spectroscopy.

2. Experimental details

The RE3−x−yNdxYbyGa5O12 (where RE = Gd, Y, and Lu; x = 0.01 and y = 0.1; labelled as GGG1Nd10Yb, YGG1Nd10Yb, and LuGG1Nd10Yb, respectively) nano-garnets were synthesized via sol–gel method.19 X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were measured on a diffractometer (PANalytical X'Pert Pro) using CuKα1 radiation. High resolution transmission electron micrographs were measured (JEOL microscope 200 kV) to know morphology of synthesized samples. The diffuse reflectance spectra were measured by using a spectrophotometer (Cary 5000). The luminescence spectra were measured by exciting with cw tunable Ti:sapphire laser (Spectra Physics 3900S) pumped by a 10 W cw Ar+ laser (Spectra Physics 2060-10 Beamlock). The emission was focused onto a 0.32 m monochromator (Jobin Yvon Triax 320) coupled with thermoelectric photomultipliers (Hamamatsu R5108 and H10330B-75). The luminescence decay curves were obtained by exciting with a pulsed (10 ns) optical parametric oscillator (OPO) (EKSPLA/NT342/3/UVE) using a digital storage oscilloscope (LeCroy WS424) coupled to the detection system. The temporal evolution of green luminescence was measured with the OPO by exciting at 970 nm. All the spectra were corrected for the spectral response of the equipment and were carried out at room temperature.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Structural and morphological properties

The powder XRD patterns of GGG1Nd10Yb, YGG1Nd10Yb, and LuGG1Nd10Yb nano-garnets, shown in Fig. 1, reveal that all of the reflections in the profiles are well-indexed to a single phase cubic structure belonging to Iad space group (no. 230, Z = 8). The diffraction data were refined by Rietveld fitting using the FULLPROF program.33,34 Table 1 shows the unit cell parameters as well as the corresponding reliability factors (χ2, Rp, Rwp, and Rexp), which make clear the goodness of the fitting, for co-doped nano-garnets, along with those for Nd3+ singly doped nano-garnets. Only small differences are found in amplitudes of a few peaks (see Fig. 1). Different features can be outlined from these data: (a) the crystalline structures of the gallium nano-garnets are very similar for 1 mol% Nd3+ single-doped or 1Nd3+–10Yb3+ co-doped and also quite close to those of their bulk counterparts (for example, a = 12.19 Å for bulk LuGG); (b) the decrease in unit cell parameter when the size of the RE3+ ion decreases (Gd3+ > Y3+ > Lu3+) is due to the lanthanide's contraction; and (c) upon 10 mol% Yb3+ doping into the GGG1Nd, YGG1Nd and LuGG1Nd nano-garnets, one can observe that the cell parameters decrease with respect to the corresponding ones for GGG1Nd and YGG1Nd, respectively, due to the fact that the ionic radius of Yb3+ (0.99 Å) is smaller than the ionic radii of Gd3+ (1.05 Å) and Y3+ (1.02 Å). However, in the case of LuGG1Nd10Yb a slight increase of the cell parameters with respect to those for LuGG1Nd takes place due to the fact that the ionic radius of Yb3+ is slightly larger than Lu3+ (0.98 Å).

By using the Scherrer's formula,6 the average crystallite sizes of doped nano-garnets have been calculated and are found to be around 35 nm. In order to obtain nanoparticles in the form of pure garnet structure, the sol–gel synthesis needs thermal treatments that gives rise to particles of sizes not smaller than this value. When the size of the nanoparticles is very short (<5 nm) it is quite typical to observe distortions of the crystalline structure due to the high surface-to-volume ratio, which further affect the vibrational and optical properties of the material, but that disappear when the size increases.35

The structural, vibrational and optical properties of nano-garnets are similar to their bulk counterparts,6 which may open new and interesting applications such as ultra-fast lasers, solar energy harvesters, infrared detectors and phosphors with increased resolution with respect to conventional bulk materials.1–4 The differences in the ionic radii of Gd3+ (1.05 Å), Y3+ (1.02 Å) and Lu3+ (0.98 Å) ions have a dramatic effect on the cubic structure of the garnet by reducing the volume of the unit cell by 5%, an effect clearly observed in the shift of diffraction peaks to higher Bragg's angles when passing from Gd3+ to Lu3+. Similar kind of effect was also observed in Nd3+ single-doped REGG nano-garnets.6

The morphology of these nano-garnets has been studied by high resolution transmission microscopy (HRTEM) and their micrograph is shown in Fig. 2 for YGG1Nd10Yb nano-garnets. As can be seen, the nano-garnets have been grown in the form of spheres with an average size of around 35–40 nm. The average size of the nano-garnets is well agreed with the crystallite size obtained from XRD technique. Similar results are observed for GGG1Nd10Yb and LuGG1Nd10Yb nano-garnets.

3.2. Diffuse reflectance spectra

Diffuse reflectance spectra were measured in the optical range from 400 to 1050 nm in order to identify the partial energy levels and local structure of optically active ions in the doped garnets [see Fig. 3(i) and (ii)]. The observed peaks between 400 and 900 nm are due to intra-configurational 4f3–4f3 electronic transitions originating from the 4I9/2 ground state to different excited levels of Nd3+ ion,6 whereas the peaks between 900 and 1050 nm are due to 4f13–4f13 transitions of Yb3+ ions originating from different Stark levels of 2F7/2 ground state to those of the single 2F5/2 excited state of Yb3+ ion [see Fig. 3(ii)]. Transitions of Nd3+ and Yb3+ ions are assigned according to the well-known Dieke's diagram.36 The sharp peak profiles are indicative of incorporation of Nd3+ and Yb3+ ions in the nano-garnet structure occupying the dodecahedral site of the RE3+ (=Gd3+, Y3+ or Lu3+) ions with a D2 point symmetry. Fig. 4 shows the partial energy level diagram of Nd3+ and Yb3+ ions, together with radiative and non-radiative transitions.

When analyzing the crystal-field interaction it is worth noting that the D2 local symmetry at the Nd3+ and Yb3+ sites in garnets is often described as an orthorhombic distortion of a main cubic symmetry. Thus, two contributions must be taken into account when describing the crystal-field interaction: a cubic symmetry and a superimposed orthorhombic symmetry component that accounts for the real D2 symmetry at the Nd3+ and Yb3+ sites. Using this description, the crystal-field symmetry splits the ground 2F7/2 multiplet into four levels and 2F5/2 level into three levels (see Fig. 4).4 As can be seen from Fig. 3(i), the relative intensities and the fine-structure of the bands are similar for the three nano-garnets, but with slight differences in the energies of the fine structure of peaks due to changes in the bond distances and angles, and hence in the crystal-field strength, around Nd3+ and Yb3+ ions, similar to Nd3+ single-doped nano-garnets.6 These effects can be ascribed to chemical pressure around Nd3+ and Yb3+ ions, i.e. the smaller the ionic radius of the RE3+ ion the larger the crystal-field strength. In the case of the garnet structure, the increase of the crystal-field magnitude gives rise to a decrease of the orthorhombic crystal-field contribution.6 In addition, as shown in Fig. 3(ii), Yb3+ absorption peaks are slightly shifted towards lower energy region upon reducing the size of RE3+ (=Gd3+, Y3+ and Lu3+) ion.

3.3. Luminescence

The luminescence spectra of Nd3+–Yb3+ co-doped REGG nano-garnets upon pumping Nd3+ ions with 805 nm laser excitation in resonance with the 4I9/2 → (2H9/2, 4F5/2) transition were measured and compared with Nd3+ single-doped nano-garnets (see Fig. 5). After a fast non-radiative de-excitation to the 4F3/2 emitting level, the 1 mol% Nd3+ doped REGG nano-garnets exhibit three main NIR bands in the 860–960 nm range, associated with the 4F3/2 → 4I9/2 transition, in the 1050–1130 nm range, to the 4F3/2 → 4I11/2 transition, and in the 1310–1430 nm range, related to the 4F3/2 → 4I13/2 transition.6 On the other side, co-doped nano-garnets exhibit an intense NIR band around 1025 nm associated with the 2F5/2 → 2F7/2 transition of Yb3+ ions due to phonon-assisted energy transfer (PAET) from Nd3+ ions to Yb3+ ions through the (4F3/2; 2F7/2) → (4I9/2,11/2; 2F5/2) channel (see Fig. 5). The quasi-resonant overlapping of Nd3+: 4F3/2 → 4I9/2 emission and Yb3+: 2F7/2 → 2F5/2 absorption is shown in Fig. 6.
Fig. 5 Luminescence spectra of 1Nd3+ singly doped (black) and 1Nd3+–10Yb3+ co-doped (red) (a) GGG, (b) YGG and (c) LuGG nano-garnets (λexc = 805 nm).

Fig. 6 Overlapping of Nd3+ emission (4F3/2 → 4I9/2; solid line) and Yb3+ absorption (2F7/2 → 2F5/2; dotted line) spectra of Nd3+/Yb3+ doped (a) GGG, (b) YGG and (c) LuGG nano-garnets.

The crystal-field splitting between 2F5/2 and 2F7/2 levels of Yb3+ in Nd3+–Yb3+:REGG nano-garnets (1000 cm−1) is larger than in Yb3+:YAG crystal (932 cm−1), which favours for 1025 nm emission through 2F5/2(5) → 2F7/2(2) transition, while the 2F5/2(5) → 2F7/2(1) transition is diminished by a very strong re-absorption due to the overlapping of the absorption peaks. The higher absorption cross-section of Nd3+ ions at 805 nm in garnets (8.3 × 10−20 cm2) is an indication for higher pumping efficiency of Yb3+ ions.20 This sensitization was observed in materials: YAG nanocrystalline ceramics, YAlO3 and YAl3(BO3)4 crystals.21,25,27 The disappearance of Nd3+ emission in 10 mol% Yb3+ co-doped nano-garnets and the intense emission from the latter ion represents efficient energy transfer from Nd3+ to Yb3+ ions. However, Yb3+ → Nd3+ energy back transfer may not be possible because of weak overlapping between Yb3+ emission and Nd3+ absorption bands, which would need the absorption of a phonon.21

3.5. Upconversion

Upconversion (UC) is an optical process that converts two or more low energy photons into a high-energy photon, and this can be obtained by either excited state absorption (ESA) and/or energy transfer upconversion (ETU) processes. Under 970 nm laser excitation, intense green (520–560 nm), weak red (630–680 nm) and NIR (730–820 nm) upconverted emissions are observed corresponds to the 4G7/2 → 4I9/2, 4G7/2 → 4I13/2 and 2H9/2 → 4I9/2 transitions, respectively (see Fig. 10). To analyze the UC processes, the upconverted emission intensities were recorded as a function of pump power. The upconverted emission intensity (IUP) follows the relation: IUP ∝ (IIR)n, where ‘IIR’ is the IR excitation intensity, ‘n’ is the number of IR photons absorbed per emitted photon. As shown in Fig. 11, the slope values are found to deviate from expected value of 2, which could be due to infinitely large UC rates.44

Fig. 11 Pump power dependence of the green, red and NIR up-converted emission intensities of Nd3+ ions in (a) GGG1Nd10Yb, (b) YGG1Nd10Yb and (c) LuGG1Nd10Yb nano-garnets (nG, nR and nNIR represents slope for green, red and NIR emissions, respectively).

The upconverted emission from Nd3+ ions and a simple schematic representation of Yb3+ to Nd3+ energy transfer processes are depicted in Fig. 4. Following the absorption of 970 nm pump photon, the Yb3+ ions are excited from the 2F7/2 ground state to the 2F5/2 excited level, then non-resonantly transfers its energy (ETU1) to the Nd3+ ions and excites them from the 4I9/2 level to the 4F3/2 one through phonon-assisted energy transfer. The Nd3+ ions are consequently and resonantly excited to 2G9/2 state via ETU2, and then decay non-radiatively to the 4G7/2 level. This in turn leads to the intense green (4G7/2 → 4I9/2), weak red (4G7/2 → 4I13/2) and NIR (2H9/2 → 4I9/2) transitions.

There is another possible channel to populate 4G7/2 level of Nd3+ ions via ESA mechanism. A 970 nm laser photon may excite the Nd3+ ions from 4I9/2 to 4F3/2 level through phonon assisted energy transfer. Consequently, within the same laser pulse, Nd3+ ion absorbs a second photon and reaches the 2G9/2 level. As shown in Fig. 4, the 4G7/2, (4F7/2, 4S3/2) and (4F5/2, 2H9/2) levels are populated through multiphonon relaxation process and emit intense green, weak red and NIR emissions from the respective emitting levels. This can be understood by analyzing the ETU/ESA contributions, the involved metastable levels, and the dynamics of the green emitting level.

Luminescence decay curves for green emission (4G7/2 level) of Nd3+ ion in all three co-doped nano-garnets have also been measured under resonant excitation to evaluate ESA/ETU contributions by comparing temporal evolution of green emission under 970 nm excitation. Moreover, the τ0 of 4G7/2 (Nd3+) level is found to be around 430, 365 and 330 μs in 1Nd3+–10Yb3+-doped YGG, GGG and LuGG nano-garnets, respectively, measured under resonant excitation at 488 nm by monitoring the emission at 537 nm. The decay curves are also well-fitted to IH model for S = 6 (not shown in figure) and the intrinsic lifetime of 4G7/2 level and energy transfer parameters are determined and are given in Table 2. There were only few reports found on evaluation of 4G7/2 level lifetime that include Nd3+ singly doped KPb2Cl5 and KPb2Br5 crystals having 55 and 63 μs, respectively.45,46

3.6. Dynamics

In order to estimate the ESA/ETU contributions involved in obtaining UC green emission, the temporal evolution of the upconverted green emission is measured at 970 nm and compared with decay curves measured under 488 nm excitation, resonant to the 4I9/2 → 4G7/2 transition (see Fig. 12). If the temporal evolution of the green UC luminescence is exactly the same as the luminescence decay curve obtained at resonant excitation, the observed green UC emission should be due to ESA mechanism.47 As can be seen from Fig. 12, the observed green UC luminescence is not only due to ESA but also due to other mechanisms. Apart from the ESA mechanism, energy transfer processes between nearby Nd3+ or Yb3+ ions in the ground (4I9/2) and intermediate (4F3/2 and 2G9/2) states could take place if the ETU mechanism dominates the UC process. To calculate quantitative values of ESA and ETU contributions in green UC emission, the temporal evolution of the upconverted green intensity is superimposed on the resonantly excited Stokes luminescence decay curve and calculated the area under these curves. The ESA contribution is evaluated by estimating the part of decay area over total area of temporal evolution, and the rest of value is ETU's part.47 The ESA & ETU's contributions are found as 17 & 83% for GGG1Nd10Yb, 16 & 84% for YGG1Nd10Yb and 22 & 78% for LuGG1Nd10Yb nano-garnets, respectively, indicating that the mechanisms are quite similar for the studied nano-garnets.
Fig. 12 Temporal evolution of the green up-converted luminescence of Nd3+ ions in (a) GGG1Nd10Yb, (b) YGG1Nd10Yb and (c) LuGG1Nd10Yb nano-garnets under 970 nm excitation. The luminescence decay curves of the green emission (dotted) under direct excitation to the 4G7/2 level at 488 nm are also included for comparison.

4. Conclusions

The RE3Ga5O12 (RE = Gd, Y and Lu) nano-garnets co-doped with 1Nd3+–10Yb3+ ions are synthesized in single phase of garnet structure with average crystallite size of about 35 nm. Upon exciting the Nd3+ ions at 805 nm, an intense near infrared emission from the Yb3+ ions is observed at around 1025 nm. The lifetime of 4F3/2 (Nd3+) level decreases drastically upon co-doping with 10 mol% of Yb3+ ions with 1 mol% of Nd3+ ions, which is due to efficient Nd3+ → Yb3+ energy transfer process. Moreover, lifetimes of the 4G7/2 (Nd3+) and the 2F5/2 (Yb3+) levels are evaluated. These decay curves are well-fitted to the Inokuti–Hirayama model for S = 6, indicating that interaction between Nd3+ and/or Yb3+ ions is of dipole–dipole. The upconversion luminescence of 1Nd3+–10Yb3+ co-doped RE3Ga5O12 nano-garnets exhibits intense green emission due to both phonon assisted excited state absorption and energy transfer upconversion mechanisms. These results indicate that these gallium-based nano-garnets could find applications in ultra-fast lasers, solar energy converters and phosphors.
