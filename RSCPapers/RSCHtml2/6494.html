Abstract

A novel benzodithiophene-substituted benzodithiophene (crossed-BDT) with an enlarged conjugated skeleton was designed and synthesized as an electron-rich unit for the construction of donorâ€“acceptor copolymers. Different acceptors, including 4,7-di(thiophen-2-yl)-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (DTBT) and 4,7-di(thiophen-2-yl)-5,6-difluoro-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (DTffBT) were used as electron-deficient units for the target copolymers to investigate the effect of the inclusion of two fluorine atoms on the acceptor unit of the polymer. The crossed-BDT and DTBT based polymers show high open circuit voltages (Voc) of over 0.9 V with the decent power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 3.74%. The crossed-BDT and DTffBT based polymers show nearly 1 V (0.95 V) Voc due to the fluorine induced low-lying HOMO energy level. The 0.9â€“0.95 V is an impressive Voc result for a 1.7 eV band gap (Eg) polymer. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report about a crossed conjugated benzodithiophene building block design, and this building block is effective in improving the Voc of low band gap DBT polymers.

1. Introduction

Recently there has been an increasing interest in the development of polymer solar cells (PSCs) due to the potential for the fabrication of light-weight, large-area, and flexible light-harvesting devices through low-cost solution processing.1â€“5 In recent years, tremendous progress has been made in the field of PSCs, and the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of PSCs has exceeded 10%.6 One of the key components of PSCs is the active layer, which determines the solar light harvest and the open-circuit voltage (Voc) of the device. Because fullerenes possess excellent isotropic electron-transporting properties and a low-lying lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) level that facilitates exciton separation, they have been frequently used as electron acceptors in PSCs.7â€“11 Therefore, new donor polymers should be designed to match the LUMO energy levels of the fullerene derivatives.12â€“16 In this regard, donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) copolymers have been widely designed and synthesized due to their low band gaps and efficient optical absorption.17,18 However, the acceptor units, generally introduced in Dâ€“A copolymers, will influence the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) level evidently and then influence the Voc of PSCs devices. Therefore, researchers concentrate on the design and synthesis of materials with low-lying HOMO levels. For some reported highly efficient materials, which do not exhibit very low-lying HOMO levels, researchers could use many methods to optimize their short circuit current density (Jsc), including using the additive DIO3,13 and thermal treatment.19,20 However, there are relatively less methods to improve the Voc of PSCs based on this type of donor material.

Among the reported efficient low band gap polymers, benzodithiophene (BDT) based copolymers have attracted interest as electron donors in the PSCs field due to its rigidity, coplanarity by fusing a benzene with two flanking thiophene units, high hole mobility and highly conjugated structures.21â€“24 Recently, many BDT copolymers with different conjugated units showed promising photovoltaic properties with high PCEs of more than 8%.25â€“28 As structural modifications on BDT, two dimensional (2-D) conjugated structures have been used to enhance intermolecular Ï€â€“Ï€ interactions, which can be beneficial in improving the photovoltaic properties of conjugated polymers.29â€“33 For example, the replacement of the alkoxy moiety by an alkylthienyl has been used to improve the photovoltaic properties of BDT-based polymers.31,32 However, some well-known efficient BDT-based polymers did not exhibit high enough Voc, such as the polymers using BDT as the donor units and 4,7-di(thiophen-2-yl)-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (DTBT) as the acceptor units, which show the Voc of 0.6â€“0.82 V.34â€“39 As we know, attaching fluorine (F) atoms to the conjugated backbone is an available approach to improve the Voc and as a result, the Voc of polymers could be improved (0.76â€“0.90 V).40â€“42 In addition, the introduction of large side groups into the BDT unit is another strategy to improve the Voc.43,44 In the middle of 2012, Stefan et al. reported the bithiophene-substituted BDT unit with extended conjugated side groups, which could be used as a donor and it exhibited a high Voc of around 1 V.44 Recently, our group also considered this strategy to improve the Voc, which could make the Voc reach up to 0.8â€“0.91 V in BDT and DTBT backboned polymers.45â€“47 In summary, with years of effort, polymers based on the BDT donor unit and DTBT acceptor unit finally showed the Voc of 0.6â€“0.91 V.

In this work, we designed and synthesized a novel benzodithiophene-substituted benzodithiophene (crossed-BDT) as a donor building block for Dâ€“A low band gap DBT polymers. Two crossed-BDT based copolymers (Scheme 1) with two different acceptor units, 4,7-di(thiophen-2-yl)-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (DTBT) and 4,7-di(thiophen-2-yl)-5,6-difluoro-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (DTffBT), were synthesized by Stille polycondensation. As a result, the polymers show the Voc of 0.9â€“0.95 V, which was very high for the same backbone polymers reported in the literature, whereas the PCE of the polymers were relatively decent. Thus, we believe that the crossed-BDT building block is an excellent donor unit which could availably improve the Voc of BDT-based polymers with low band gaps and high efficiency. This will help researchers to design more efficient novel BDT materials with low band gaps and high Voc.

2.5. Quantum mechanical calculations

The electron density of states distribution of these two polymers was calculated using the density functional theory (DFT) method. All DFT calculations were performed using Gaussian 09 (A.02) employing the hybrid B3LYP exchangeâ€“correlation functional with a split valence 6-31G* basis set. The blend ratio of 1:2 was found to give the best results for both polymers. PC71BM were also used as an acceptor with the device configuration ITO/PEDOT:PSS/polymers:PC71BM (1:2)/Ca/Al to optimize the performance of device. However, the photovoltaic properties of the devices did not improve. Finally, the best solar cells based on PCBDT-DTBT showed a PCE of 3.74% with a Voc of 0.91 V, a Jsc of 7.54 mA cmâˆ’2, and an FF of 54.56% and PCBDT-DTffBT showed a PCE of 1.14% with a Voc of 0.94 V, a Jsc of 2.62 mA cmâˆ’2, and an FF of 46.13% under the same conditions. It was noted that these two polymers give high Voc PSC devices due to their lower-lying HOMO energy level, which agree well with our expectations. Side substituted groups could affect the Voc of the polymer solar cells. In particular, bulky conjugated side groups will lower the HOMO levels of polymers to improve the Voc, as the Voc is related to the energy difference between the HOMO of the polymer and the LUMO of the acceptor.43,44 Unfortunately, the PCEs of the two polymers were moderate, which was attributed to their poor solubility. Furthermore, the large side groups connected to the crossed-BDT units could affect the intermolecular packing to cause the low Jsc. This could be another reason for the unsatisfactory PCEs. 3. Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully designed and synthesized a novel highly-conjugated crossed benzodithiophene (crossed-BDT) as an electron-rich unit for the construction of two donorâ€“acceptor copolymers. The BT unit, as an acceptor, was also modified with non-fluorinated and fluorinated moieties. A decrease in the HOMO level and a net blue-shifted absorption of the fluorinated polymer compared with the non-fluorinated polymer is observed. These two copolymers with a crossed-BDT structure in their backbone showed the desired deep HOMO energy levels. The crossed-BDT donor can markedly improve the Voc of polymers with the DTBT backbone, which show the Voc of 0.9â€“0.95 V. Therefore, this will provide some reference to improve the Voc of low band gap BDT material based PSC devices. To improve the PCE, further work on making long branched alkyl substituted polymers with the same backbone is underway.

4. Experimental

4.1. Materials

All starting reagents were obtained commercially as analytical reagents and were used directly without any purification. Toluene, ether and THF were purified by vacuum distillation under nitrogen prior to use. 4,8-Bis[(2-ethylhexyl)oxy]benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene, 4,7-bis(5-bromo-2-thienyl)-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole and 4,7-bis(5-bromo-2-thienyl)-5,6-difluoro-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole were synthesized as reported in the literature.54â€“56

4.2. Instruments and measurements

Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectra were obtained using a Bruker AVANCE-III 600 spectrometer. High resolution mass spectra (MS) were recorded under APCI mode using a Bruker Maxis UHRTOF spectrometer. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) and thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) measurements were performed using a STA-409 at a heating rate of 10 Â°C minâˆ’1. UV-Vis absorption spectra were measured with a Hitachi U-4100 spectrophotometer. The polymer films on quartz, which were used for absorption spectral measurement, were prepared by spin-coating from their chloroform solutions. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) was performed using a CHI660D electrochemical workstation with a glassy carbon working electrode, a saturated calomel reference electrode (SCE) and a platinum wire counter electrode at a scan rate of 100 mV sâˆ’1. Tetrabutylammonium phosphorus hexafluoride (Bu4NPF6, 0.1 M) in acetonitrile was used as the supporting electrolyte. Surface morphological characterizations of the films were done by a tapping-mode atomic force microscope (AFM, Agilent 5400). X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were recorded using a Bruker D8 Advance.

4.3. Fabrication and characterization of polymer solar cells

Photovoltaic devices were fabricated on pre-patterned indium tin oxide (ITO) coated glass substrates with a layered structure of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/donor:acceptor/Ca (10 nm)/Al (100 nm). The ITO coated glass substrates were cleaned in an ultrasonic bath in acetone, toluene, methanol and isopropyl alcohol, sequentially. Then, they were oxygen plasma treated for 20 min, spin-coated with PEDOT:PSS at 5000 rpm, and dried under argon for 20 min at 120 Â°C. Subsequently, the active layer was spin-coated from different blend weight ratios of donor (12 mg mLâˆ’1) and PC61BM in a deoxygenated anhydrous chloroform solution on the ITO/PEDOT:PSS substrate. The active layer thickness was about 100 nm, which was measured by a Dektak 150 profilometer. Finally, Ca (10 nm) and aluminum (100 nm) were thermally evaporated at a vacuum of âˆ¼2 Ã— 10âˆ’4 Pa on top of the active layer. The active area of the solar cell devices was 0.1 cm2. The current densityâ€“voltage (Jâ€“V) characteristics were measured with a Keithley 2420 source measurement unit under simulated 100 mW cmâˆ’2 (AM 1.5 G) irradiation from a Newport solar simulator. Light intensity was calibrated with a standard silicon solar cell. The external quantum efficiencies (EQE) of the solar cells were analyzed using a certified Newport incident photon conversion efficiency (IPCE) measurement system.
