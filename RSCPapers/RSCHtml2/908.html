Abstract

There is an urgent need for cleaner production processes for chemicals. An efficient and promising alternative for such reactions is heterogeneous photocatalysis, which works on the principle of converting (visible) light, including solar energy, into chemical energy. To that end, properly designed semiconductor based photocatalysts are necessary to trigger the photocatalytic reactions. Many commonly used photocatalysts have too large bandgaps (3â€“3.4 eV) to use visible light and a too low surface area for efficient production. Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) have emerged as an encouraging class of materials for photocatalytic applications due to their (i) large surface area and porosity that facilitate adsorption towards chemicals, (ii) tunable crystallinity and optical and electronic properties for efficient light absorption in the visible region, (iii) tunable composition and functionality that make them versatile photocatalysts for a wide range of reactions, and (iv) facile development of composites with other semiconductors to produce Z-scheme heterojunctions that can effectively suppress the recombination of photogenerated charges. Ongoing research has started focusing on the judicious construction of Z-scheme heterojunctions in MOFs, to mimic natural photosynthesis, such that the MOF photocatalysts have higher light harvesting capacity, spatially separated reductive and oxidative active sites, and well-preserved redox ability. This review provides a concise compilation of the recent progress in the development and applications of MOF-based Z-scheme photocatalysts, their advanced characterization, and future perspectives for further advancements.

Introduction

Energy scarcity, also caused by geopolitics, and environmental pollution are two critical issues that have pushed the world into a state of concern in recent years.1 Therefore, it is increasingly urgent to develop new technologies to create new energy sources and combat environmental pollution. Solar energy or visible light utilization technology is recognized as one of the most influential and promising technologies for splitting water into clean fuel H2 and O2, reducing greenhouse gases such as CO2 and degrading water pollutants, under the illumination of (sun)light under ambient conditions.2 The development of efficient semiconductor photocatalysts to improve light conversion efficiency and expand the range of applications has become an important issue in terms of both scientific research and societal impact. Though photolysis of water has been of continued research interest in the past, it was only in 1972 that Honda and Fujishima first developed a working model for â€œphotosensitized electrolytic oxidation of waterâ€. They showed that it is possible to decompose water in an electrochemical cell by visible light irradiation using a titanium oxide anode.3 Since then, an enormous amount of research has been conducted on photocatalysts to mitigate their low efficiency and pave the way for efficient practical applications. Before venturing into the drawbacks of photocatalysis that need to be addressed, let us understand the process of photocatalysis. Photocatalysis mainly consists of the following steps: (i) absorption of light by the semiconductor-type photocatalyst, (ii) formation of photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs, (iii) migration or recombination of the photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs, (iv) adsorption of reactants on/inside the pores of the semiconductor photocatalyst, followed by desorption of the products, and (v) redox reactions on the active sites of the photocatalyst driven by the photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs. Though the photocatalytic pathway may seem straightforward, it is not the case. The critical concern for low photocatalytic activity is the recombination of the photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs. When an electron gets excited by light irradiation, it jumps from the valence band (VB) to the conduction band (CB). But there is a strong coulombic force that acts between the electron in the CB and the hole generated in the VB. Therefore, the electron tends to fall back from the CB back to the VB and recombine with the hole, generating useless heat energy in the process. To prevent this fall back/recombination of electronâ€“hole pairs, it is necessary to design photocatalysts rationally, such that the photocatalysts achieve the following properties: (i) absorption of light in the visible region preferably, (ii) redox band edges (VB and CB) in appropriate energy levels for driving the photocatalytic reaction, (iii) generation of a large quantity of charge carriers upon excitation, (iv) high mobility of the charge carriers and low recombination rate, and (v) chemical and photo stability.4

Currently, TiO2 semiconductors have been proven to be one of the most used photocatalysts for water splitting and oxidative decomposition of many organic compounds. However, due to its wide band gap of 3.2 eV, TiO2 can only be excited by ultraviolet or near-ultraviolet radiation, which accounts for only 4% of the incident solar spectrum on Earth.5 Therefore, the development of visible light driven photocatalysts is the future trend. So far, although a lot of efforts have been made in preparing different visible light photocatalysts, the short lifetimes of photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs and the short lifetimes of electronâ€“hole pairs with limited absorption of visible light still hinder their practical applications.

To overcome the serious drawbacks of fast charge recombination and the limited visible-light absorption of semiconductor photocatalysts, many strategies have been developed in the past few decades and the most widely used one is to develop photocatalytic heterojunctions.2,6â€“9 Conventional photocatalytic heterojunctions are divided into three categories, such as Type I, Type II, and Type III, and only the type II has a positive effect in photocatalysis (Fig. 1a), because it can effectively separate photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs spatially through a band arrangement between two semiconductors. Under the irradiation of light, photogenerated electrons in the conduction band (CB) of semiconductor 1 will migrate to the CB of semiconductor 2, while photogenerated holes in the valence band (VB) of semiconductor 2 will transfer to the VB of semiconductor 1. Since the photogenerated electrons and holes accumulate on semiconductor 2 and semiconductor 1, respectively, the spatial separation of electrons and holes can be achieved by such heterojunction photocatalysts to enhance photocatalytic activity. Briefly, the reduction and oxidation reactions of type II heterojunction photocatalysts occur in semiconductor 2 with lower reduction potential and semiconductor 1 with lower oxidation potential, respectively. Therefore, the ensuing problem is that the redox ability of type II heterojunction photocatalysts will be greatly reduced. Therefore, it is necessary to develop new heterostructure photocatalytic systems to overcome these problems.7,8

The emergence of the Z-scheme heterojunction photocatalytic system (Fig. 1b) has provided a powerful solution to the above restriction6,7,10â€“15 because it can simultaneously meet the above requirements, i.e., effectively enhance the light absorption capacity while making the CB potential more negative and the VB potential more positive. This system is a promising strategy for photocatalyst improvement. Unlike conventional type II heterojunction photocatalysts, the reaction in the Z-scheme system is driven by photogenerated electrons (in the CB of PS I) which can maintain a high reduction capacity, and photogenerated holes (in the VB of PS II), which can maintain a high oxidation capacity. From a physical point of view, the charge carrier transfer by Z-scheme photocatalysts is simple. With their unique properties, Z-scheme photocatalysts are promising for various applications and have therefore been intensively studied. Even so, most of the current Z-scheme heterojunction photocatalysts are based on materials with low specific surface area, such as ZnO, TiO2 and CdS-based materials,15,16 which constrains the number of available catalytic sites.

With the aim to construct optimal Z-scheme heterojunction photocatalysts, the excellent structure of MOFs17,18 has come to the forefront. MOFs are a new class of materials that are crystalline and highly porous. They are constructed from inorganic nodes such as metal ions or clusters (secondary building units, SBUs) which are connected to organic ligands by coordination bonds to form an infinite array with a specific topology. MOFs can possess surface area up to 10000 m2 gâˆ’1 and pore size of up to 3â€“100 Ã…. MOFs have shown promising results in applications such as gas storage, catalysis, luminescence, and photovoltaic cells. In 2005, Widom and Divosi demonstrated by theoretical calculations that IRMOFs are equivalent to semiconductors with large band gaps. The HOMOâ€“LUMO bands are dependent on the Csp2 state of the organic ligands. The HOMO band of the MOFs are primarily constituted of the organic ligands. In the presence of light irradiation, the ligands in the HOMO harvest light and get photoexcited, further activating the metal nodes/clusters via the linker-to-metal charge transfer (LCMT) mechanism. Such structureâ€“property configurations have made MOFs lucrative photocatalysts of the next generation. Experimentally, the MOFs have also shown favourable results in photocatalysis owing to the following: (i) highly crystalline structure that can promote charge transfer from catalytic active sites to the surface adsorbed reactant molecules; (ii) high porosity which allows the loading of enhanced number of catalytic active sites as well as mass transport of reactants and products during photocatalysis; (iii) the tunable pore size of MOFs which can be exploited to use them as customized photocatalysts for reactions with different reactant and product sizes, thus increasing the selectivity of reactions; (iv) the facile modulation of the electronic and optical structure of MOFs by changing their metal cluster or the organic ligands, which can configure their band gaps and band edges, such that theoretical band gaps can extend from 1.0 to 5.5 eV, enough to overcome the potential barrier of a wide range of photocatalytic reactions;19 (v) the high content of heteroatoms in the ligands of MOFs, which permits the chemical functionalization of various photoactive molecules/materials, which in turn enables them to form heterojunction hybrids (composites) in a Z-scheme configuration. Principles and methodologies of Z-scheme heterojunctions

Classifications of Z-scheme heterojunctions

During natural photosynthesis, the electron transfer between photosystem I and II follows a â€œZâ€ pathway, and hence the name Z-scheme. In the case of Z-scheme heterojunction-based photocatalysts that essentially mimic the natural photosynthesis process, the photosystems are replaced by two semiconductors, and hence this process is called artificial photosynthesis (Fig. 3).

The first pioneering work on Z-scheme photocatalysts was done by Bard et al. in 1979.26 Since then, the Z-scheme photocatalysts have gone through a massive evolution as shown in Fig. 4. The first generation photocatalysts (Fig. 5a) as developed by Bard et al. constitute the liquid-phase Z-scheme photocatalysts, where the two semiconductors are connected by a shuttle redox mediator (Fe3+/Fe2+ and IO3âˆ’/Iâˆ’) that act as the charge transfer pathway. However, their applications are limited by the reversible electron mediators and their implementation in liquid systems only. The second generation all-solid-state-Z-scheme photocatalysts (Fig. 5b) by Tada et al. have the two semiconductors connected by metal nanoparticles such as Ag and Au, thus resulting in lower ohmic resistance and faster charge carrier transfer.27 To mitigate the high cost of noble metals, current research has also focused on using transition metals such as Fe, Co, and Cu as the charge carrier medium. Among the metal-free mediators, metal-free carbon nanotubes, carbon quantum dots,28 and carbon nitrides29 have also shown promising results. The third generation direct Z-scheme photocatalysts (Fig. 5c) developed by Yu et al. have the two semiconductors in direct contact at the interface. Synthesis methods of MOF-based Z-scheme heterojunctions

As discussed previously, MOFs have become an attractive class of materials for development of Z-scheme photocatalysts owing to their crystallinity, tunable metal clusters, photoactive ligands with appropriate redox potentials, extremely high surface area and porosity that exposes the active sites, and free functional groups for chemical hybridization with other semiconductors to form Z-scheme configurations. As it is possible to choose the inorganic and organic composition at the initial stages of synthesizing MOFs, unique properties, tailored to the photocatalytic reactions, can be obtained. Among the various methods for developing Z-scheme photocatalysts using MOFs, some principal ones are (i) hydro/solvothermal, (ii) impregnation/precipitation, and (iii) electro/photo-deposition. The versatile and facile hydro/solvothermal method generally follows encapsulation of the pre-synthesized nanoparticles in a solution of MOF components or vice versa. Judicious usage of temperature and solvents allows the MOF to grow tightly around the nanoparticles. Benzoic acid functionalized g-C3N4/NH2-MIL-125(Ti) was synthesized solvothermally by adding a dispersion of benzoic acid functionalized g-C3N4/dimethylformide in a solution containing the MOF reagents like 2-aminoterephthalic acid and tetra-n-butyl titanate.34 By subjecting the mixture to heating at 150 Â°C for 72 h, the MOFs supposedly wrapped intimately around the benzoic acid functionalized g-C3N4, thus providing a Z-scheme photocatalyst. Alternately, the MOF can be treated as a core and other photoactive materials can be grown as a shell, as in hydrothermally synthesized UiO-66-(COOH)2/ZnIn2S4/MoS2. After the synthesis of pristine UiO-66-(COOH)2, it was mixed in a salt solution containing indium chloride tetrahydrate (InCl3Â·4H2O), zinc chloride (ZnCl2), sodium molybdate dihydrate (Na2MoO4Â·2H2O) and excess thioacetamide (C2H5NS). The mixture was subjected to heating at 165 Â°C for 24 h, followed by purification by centrifugation.35 The one-pot method and high degree of control in the pre-fabricated materials allow the hydro/solvothermal method to be the most common method for the design of Z-scheme photocatalysts. However, it is energy-intensive, difficult to control the final product, and requires high usage of toxic solvents for dissolution.

The impregnation method requires the addition of MOF powder in a required concentration of the desired nanoparticles, which thereby form covalent interactions with the MOF support.32,36 As a rule of thumb, such impregnations are carried out by introducing nanoparticles equivalent to the pore volume of MOFs. Since MOFs have high pore volume, development of impregnation-based Z-scheme photocatalysts is easier. For example, to synthesize phosphotungstic acid (HPW)/UIO-66 or MOS2 dots/MIL-101(Fe), it is required to introduce a required pore volume amount of HPW or MoS2 solution and stir them with the MOF, followed by drying. This simple approach has a high degree of control in the amount of impregnated nanoparticles, provides heterojunctions and transfer of charge carriers via the covalent interactions, and is less energy-intensive. However, it is difficult to replicate in materials which do not dissolve easily and form a solution, or crystallize to larger size than the pore size of the support.

To obtain homogeneous growth of ultra-small nanoparticles at the MOF/MOF-derived substrates, electrodeposition of the metal-salt via tuning the potential has become the chosen method. Electrodeposition of copper using Cu(OAc)2 on MOF-derived In2O3@InP can be achieved in seconds.33 Briefly, in a three-electrode system, In-MIL-68 derived In2O3@InP was used as the working electrode, platinum wire as the counter electrode, and calomel as the reference electrode. The electrolyte was composed of copper acetate (Cu(OAc)2) solution. By applying a potential of 4 V, copper nanoparticles were deposited on the In2O3@InP in 200 s. The electrodeposition method allows the deposition of ultra-small nanoparticles with better interfacial contacts and offers an extremely fast synthesis process. However, this method still needs to be explored more to understand its versatility and optimization parameters.

In a unique example, Zheng et al. prepared Ag/NH2-MIL-125(Ti)/CdS by impregnation and photo-deposition. To a certain quantity of NH2-MIL-125(Ti) MOF dispersion in water, a predefined concentration of AgNO3 was added and the mixture was irradiated for 2.5 h under a 350 W Xenon lamp to photo-reduce AgNO3 to Ag. The prepared Ag/NH2-MIL-125(Ti) was dispersed in water with the addition of thiourea and cadmium acetate and heated for 60 Â°C for 1 h (also called chemical bath deposition), to obtain Ag/NH2-MIL-125(Ti)/CdS.37 Another example of such a photo-deposition method is the synthesis of the Ti-MOF/Ag/NiFe LDH Z-scheme photocatalyst.38 Just as in the previous example, NH2-MIL-125(Ti) was used and represented as Ti-MOF. To a certain quantity of Ti-MOF dispersed in N,N-dimethylformamide and water, Ni(NO3)2Â·6H2O, Fe(NO3)3Â·9H2O and urea were added followed by heating at 120 Â°C for 12 h in an autoclave. The Ti-MOF/NiFe LDH thus obtained was dispersed in water and AgNO3 was added and the mixture was irradiated under a 300 W Xenon lamp (180 mW cmâˆ’2, Î» > 300 nm) to photo-reduce AgNO3 to Ag. This work further explored how the radiation time and concentration of AgNO3 affect the photo-deposition process. An optimized concentration of 0.2 g Lâˆ’1 and an irradiation time of 30 min were considered as the best photoreduction conditions for conversion of AgNO3 to Ag. Lower irradiation time or concentration was unable to produce enough Ag nanoparticles, while higher irradiation time or concentration caused excessive Ag deposition or large sized Ag nanoparticles. Both of these blocked the light from reaching Ti-MOF and NiFe LDH, resulting in poor light absorption ability. Though Ag nanoparticles facilitated the charge transfer in Ti-MOF/Ag/NiFe LDH, the higher surface coverage of Ag acted as recombination centres, resulting in a lower plasmon effect.

MOF-based Z-scheme catalysts for photocatalytic hydrogen evolution

The photocatalytic hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) from water splitting is considered to be one of the best green solutions to generate clean hydrogen that can replace the polluting fossil fuels. The photocatalyst absorbs solar energy and splits water into hydrogen via a four-electron or two-electron process. Prior to MOFs, materials such as cadmium sulphide (CdS), zinc indium sulphide (ZnIn2S4, ZIS), and phosphotungstic acid (HPW) have shown promising activity towards HER. However, their limitations have hindered the scope of their commercialization. Developing Z-scheme heterojunctions of such materials with MOFs has provided a new outlook in the photocatalytic field. CdS is regarded as an excellent semiconductor for photocatalytic hydrogen generation due to its suitable band position and visible light absorption capacity. However, CdS-based semiconductors have drawbacks such as susceptibility to agglomeration, low surface area, and rapid recombination of carriers.39 To mitigate the downsides of CdS, they have been hybridized with MOFs to develop composites such as CdS/MIL-53(Fe),39 CdS/Ni-MOF,40 and CdS/NH2-MIL-125(Ti).41 The decoration of CdS on the outer surface or micropores of these MOFs caused intimate contact that benefitted interfacial charge transfer. On the other hand, using Fe- or Ti-based MOFs provided the advantage of metal-oxo centres which are highly responsive to light irradiation and facilitate the linker-to-metal charge transfer of photogenerated electrons. In the case of CdS/NH2-MIL-125(Ti), the bandgaps of CdS and NH2-MIL-125(Ti) are computed to be 2.40 eV and 2.66 eV respectively. But the hybridization caused strong contact interfaces and electron transfer between CdS and NH2-MIL-125(Ti) that led to the bandgap of 2.50 eV in the composite CdS/NH2-MIL-125(Ti). Also, the CBs of CdS and NH2-MIL-125(Ti) were calculated to be -0.76 eV and +0.66 eV respectively. Combining these data with electron spin resonance (ESR), it was deduced that the Z-scheme heterojunction improved the separation efficiency of the charge carriers by recombination of electrons of the CB of NH2-MIL-125(Ti) and holes of the VB of CdS as shown in Fig. 8(aâ€“c). Therefore, a large amount of effective electrons in the CB of CdS could participate in the reduction of H+ to hydrogen, while the holes in the VB of the MOF enabled the oxidation of water with sacrificial agents.41 A similar mechanism occurred in CdS/MIL-53(Fe) and CdS/Ni-MOF, where due to the difference in the Fermi levels of CdS and MOFs, the electrons and holes redistributed to create a Z-scheme electron transmission pathway.40 The Z-scheme improved the HER in the composites by 3â€“10 times (âˆ¼2334 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1â€“6.62mmol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1) compared to that of pure CdS. Further improvements in HER were achieved by exploiting the synergism of Z-scheme interfaces. By tuning the functional group in Î±-NiS-Î²-NiS/CdS/UiO-66, the heterojunction could be transformed from Type-I to Z-scheme. Additionally, the loading of Î±-NiS-Î²-NiS on CdS improved the light absorption ability and concentration of active sites. However, the optical shielding effect of these binary materials limited their HER activity. Therefore, loading them on NH2-UiO-66 changed the photoelectron pathway via an in-built electric field and improved the separation efficiency.42 To reduce the bandgap of CdS, further research has been conducted on developing Cd-based semiconductors with ternary metal sulphides such as CdLa2S4 and CdxZn1âˆ’xS. However, they still suffer from the issue of agglomeration and low surface area of CdS. Therefore, loading them on MOFs to achieve CdLa2S4/MIL-88A(Fe) or CdxZn1âˆ’xS/Ni-MOF-74 has improved their photocatalytic HER activity. In spite of CdLa2S4 featuring a bandgap of 2.1 eV, it was difficult to form a Z-scheme heterojunction when it was combined with TiO2 or g-C3N4. Finally, MIL-88A(Fe) as the host provided a suitable energy level similar to CdLa2S4 to develop the Z-scheme CdLa2S4/MIL-88A(Fe), that promoted the separation of photoexcited charge carriers. The formed solidâ€“solid heterointerface between the materials allows higher transfer and separation efficiency of photoinduced carriers, that could be verified from the higher photocurrent intensity.43 Apart from the similar band positions to form the Z-scheme, the spatial structure formed by the metal-sulfide nanoparticles and MOFs also contributes to the suppression of the agglomeration of the nanoparticles. Another attractive material for visible light HER is the hexagonal ZnIn2S4 (ZIS). ZIS is a typical layered transition metal chalcogenide that is composed of stacked Sâ€“Znâ€“Sâ€“Inâ€“Sâ€“Inâ€“S layers. It exhibits a bandgap of 2.06â€“2.85 eV, and is generally cheap and less toxic. However, pure ZnIn2S4 suffers from poor photocatalytic activity due to its low migration ability and poor separation efficiency of the photogenerated carriers.35 To extract the maximum activity from ZIS, it is presumed that directional synthesis of hollow structures would provide a large amount of exposed photocatalytic sites. Hence, MIL-68(In) rods composed of coordinating In3+ ions with organic ligand 2-aminoterephthalic acid were used as templates to grow ZIS as the outer shell to ultimately form a channel architecture. Based on Kubelkaâ€“Munk conversion and Mottâ€“Schottky analyses, the CBs of ZIS and MIL-68(In) were deduced as âˆ’0.40 eV and +0.74 eV respectively. The CB of MIL-68(In) and the unchanged EPR signal of photo-induced electrons before and after light irradiation over pristine MIL-68(In) proved that a Type-II heterojunction would be impossible. If electrons flowed from ZIS to MIL-68(In) and holes moved from MIL-68(In) to ZIS, then it was impossible for the collected electrons on the CB of MIL-68(In) to be used for HER. Therefore, it was only the interfacial connection between ZIS and MIL-68(In) that encouraged the transfer and recombination of photogenerated electrons in the CB of MIL-68(In) and holes in the VB of ZIS, thus establishing the Z-scheme.45 To improve the water stability of ZIS/MOF composites, 2D ZIS nanosheets have been combined with spindle shaped NH2-MIL-88B(Fe), that has a compatible bandgap of 2.5 eV. It is to be noted that Fe-MOFs generally suffer from limited photo absorption and poor charge separation; therefore, NH2-MIL-88B(Fe) had been modified by defect engineering via doping with monodentate ligand pyrrole 2-carboxylic acid (PDC). The defective MOF not only had an expanded light absorption range but also a lower band gap. The defect was characterized by the enhanced EPR signal intensity of core@shell ZIS/defective NH2-MIL-88B(Fe). The 502.4 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1 photocatalytic hydrogen production (âˆ¼2.69 times that of pristine ZIS) is attributed to two factors. Firstly, the unsaturated Fe clusters that are formed after doping with defect ligands, give rise to ligand vacancies. The unsaturated sites impart higher photo-absorption and charge separation. Secondly, the ligand doping bolsters the Z-scheme interface by promoting additional charge transfer, separation of carriers, and shortening the electronâ€“hole pair transmission distance.48 To further shorten the charge carrier distance at the interface, mediators with lower densities, small size and metal-like conductivity for efficient electron transfer are required. Therefore, a novel all-solid-state Z-scheme NH2-MIL-125(Ti)/Ti3C2 MXene quantum dots/ZnIn2S4 (Ti-MOF/QDs/ZIS) photocatalyst was developed. MXene, as a new type of 2D layered material with metallic conductivity and outstanding chemical stability, can also create Schottky barriers when combined with semiconductors and enhance the photo-carrier separation efficiency. When such powerful MXenes are reduced to 0D QDs, they exhibit exceptional photochemical properties as electron mediators due to the confinement effect. Under light irradiation, the photogenerated electrons in the CB from NH2-MIL-125(Ti) transfer to recombine with the holes in the VB of ZIS, via the Ti3C2 QD electron mediator. The presence of the mediator improves the fast transfer of electrons and provides a Z-scheme interface. On the other hand, the electron density in the CB of ZIS improves because of this mechanism and increases the photocatalytic hydrogen production to 2931.9 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1 (âˆ¼12.7 times that of ZIS).49 Introducing cocatalysts is another way to manipulate the electron density in the CB and improve the HER activity in photocatalysis. In a 3D hierarchical UiO-66-(COOH)2/MoS2/ZIS microarchitecture, the Z-scheme transfer mode was established by transfer of photogenerated electrons from UiO-66-(COOH)2 to ZIS due to their internal field from the mismatched Fermi level. Thereafter, the MoS2 co-catalyst accepts the electrons from the CB of ZIS, thus improving the photocarrier separation efficiency. Finally, these electrons from MoS2 transfer to H+ to produce H2. It should be noted that this photocatalyst uses the environmentally friendly and more water stable UiO-66-(COOH)2 instead of UIO-66. Due to the stability of the host MOF, the overall photocatalyst was able to endure 5 cycles of reactions without any evident agglomeration of the ZIS.35

Other photoactive materials like bismuth oxyiodine (BiOI) with a very low bandgap of 1.7â€“1.9 eV or polyoxometalate (HPW) with very high oxidizing power have also been coupled with MOFs to create Z-scheme photocatalysts (BiOI@NU-1000 and HPW@UIO-66).32,50 Such engineered photocatalysts provide better photostability to the core nanoparticles as well as increase the molecular diffusion of the reactants and products owing to the high surface area and porosity of the MOFs.

Since metals are toxic, cause pollution, and are expensive, carbonaceous materials have lately gained attraction to be integrated with MOFs. Among such photoactive metal-free carbon materials is graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4), with an impressive band gap of 2.7 eV. g-C3N4 is a 2D layered photocatalyst and is considered as an n-type semiconductor; it has an adjustable electronic structure and photochemical stability. However, the fast recombination of photogenerated carriers and low surface area limit its applications.47 Though homogeneous distribution of ZIF-8 on g-C3N4 provides better separation efficiency and prolonged charge carrier lifetime, it is more logical to use mediators for stronger interactions between the heterojunctions.51 With that in mind, C3N4 nanosheets have been loaded on NH2-UiO-66 (NH2-UiO-66/N-CN)52 or boron-doped g-C3N4 on cobalt porphyrin metal organic frameworks (HBCNN/CoPMOF),46 as shown in Fig. 8(dâ€“f). Such heterointerfaces are formed by Ï€â‹¯Ï€ interactions between the conjugate structure of N-CN and the benzene rings of ligands in NH2-UiO-66. In the case of HBCNN/CoPMOF, interfacial coordinative interactions of N-Co-N are formed between the N of HBCNN and partially oxidized Co centres, which give rise to a pseudo-gap and improve the charge separation efficiency. Further, to extend the interfacial contact, ternary Z-scheme novel catalysts like Î±-SnWO4/UIO-66(NH2)/g-C3N4 have been developed, as shown in Fig. 8(gâ€“i). The ternary structure not only increases the interfacial surface area but also suppresses recombination of charge carriers. Due to the Fermi level shift, a built-in electric field is formed on either side of UIO-66(NH2) between the n-type g-C3N4 and p-type Î±-SnWO4, constructing a double Z-scheme, that favours spatial charge separation and inhibits the recombination.47

MOF-based Z-scheme catalysts for carbon dioxide reduction (CO2-RR)

Owing to the temperature rise due to high CO2 concentration, it is imperative that CO2 capture and its conversion to useful chemicals should be a priority research domain. However, CO2-RR is an uphill reaction due to the following issues: Firstly the strong CO bond (âˆ¼750 kJ molâˆ’1) requires huge energy to dissociate. Secondly, photoreduction of CO2 is a complicated multi-electron pathway along with proton assistance. Thirdly, the HER occurs in a similar potential range to CO2-RR and is energetically more favourable than CO2-RR, thus bringing upon issue of selectivity. In mitigating the well-known issues of low surface area and poor separation efficiency, MOF-based Z-scheme photocatalysts for CO2-RR have shown promising results. As mentioned previously, the metal sulphide CdS semiconductor has an extended absorption capacity and favourable CB position for CO2-RR. However, to prevent its agglomeration and leaching, a Z-scheme photocatalyst has been developed by loading it in the matrix of UIO-66-NH2. UIO-66 NH2 possesses a high surface area for CO2 capture and CdS dispersion, visible light absorption capacity, and chemical stability to prevent the agglomeration of CdS as well as improve the separation efficiency. Due to the difference in the Fermi levels of the two materials, their band alignment provides a built-in electric field. Upon irradiation by light, the photogenerated electrons of UIO-66-NH2 and holes of CdS recombine, thus leaving a high concentration of electrons in the CB of CdS (âˆ’0.98 V) to reduce the CO2-RR. On the other hand, the holes in the VB of UIO-66-NH2 (2.19 eV) participate in the water reaction to release protons.53 Similarly, rare earth CeCO3OH is also integrated in UIO-66-NH2, to form a Z-scheme via the covalent bonding between CO32âˆ’ of CeCO3OH and Zrâ€“O of the MOF. Apart from the band alignment where the CB of UIO-66-NH2 takes part in CO2-RR, the presence of Ce3+/Ce4+ also improves the electron transfer and provides additional catalytic active sites.54

Since HER strongly competes with CO2-RR, the suppression of HER should also be considered while engineering the photocatalysts. In CuO/Ag/UiO-66, Ag acts as a bridge to promote charge transfer between the CO2-RR active site of CuO and the MOF, thus improving separation efficiency at the Z-scheme interface, as shown in Fig. 9(aâ€“c). Additionally, Ag also acts as a co-catalyst which has stronger adsorption capacity for the *H protons. Eventually, these *H protons combine with the adjacent *OCO adsorbed on CuO, to form *OCOH, and generate formic acid (HCOOH) with 63 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1 yield and 95.9% selectivity. Therefore, it can be seen that the cocatalyst induced MOF-Z scheme can promote the generation of a two-electron pathway and tune the product selectivity by controlling reaction intermediates.55 In the other instance, Pt was used as the bridge in Cu2O/Pt/NH2-MIL-125(Ti). In this case, owing to the large Fermi level of Pt nanoparticles, the photogenerated electrons of the CB of the MOF migrated via the Pt electron mediator and recombined with the holes of the VB of Cu2O, thus ensuring high separation efficiency. The electron of the active site of Cu2O converted CO2 photocatalytically to methanol (CH3OH) and ethanol (CH3CH2OH) with a yield of 434.46 and 718.47 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1 respectively. Additionally, the Schottky barrier formed between the Pt nanoparticles and the MOF prevented the complexation of the photogenerated carriers and improved their lifetime.56

Striving towards metal-free photocatalysts, there have been research studies where instead of using metal-based electron mediators, carbonaceous mediators like reduced graphene oxide (r-GO) and g-C3N4 have been successfully integrated in Z-schemes. Aiming towards the same, photocatalysts such as O-defective ZnO/r-GO/NH2-UIO-66 and g-C3N4/NH2-MIL 125 (Ti)/r-GO have been developed. The introduction of oxygen defects in ZnO reduces the bandgap from 3.7 eV to 2.98 eV. This enables it to form a band aligned Z-scheme photocatalyst with compatible band positions with NH2-UIO-66. Upon irradiation, r-GO is able to transfer the electrons quickly from the CB of O-ZnO to the VB of NH2-UIO-66, thus increasing the electron concentration in the CB of the catalytic centre of the MOF, as well as preventing recombination of photogenerated carriers.57 Similarly, in g-C3N4/NH2-MIL 125 (Ti)/r-GO, r-GO acts as the solid mediator (Fig. 9(dâ€“g)) to ensure suppression of recombination and thereby improve the lifetime of catalytically active electron density in the CB of g-C3N4, that can reduce CO2 to CO and CH4.58

MOF-based Z-scheme catalysts for pollutant degradation

Photocatalytic oxygen evolution reaction (OER) requires a potential drive of 1.23 eV and a four electron transfer process, which is too high for most photocatalysts to achieve, in spite of their compatible band positions. Further, the cleavage of the Oâ€“H bond and formation of the Oâ€“O bond has a high overpotential of âˆ¼700 mV, that hinders the OER.61 Therefore, most Z-scheme photocatalysts for OER investigations are still in nascent stages and pristine MOF-based Z-schemes are practically unavailable. In contrast, the photo-oxidation power of MOF-Z-scheme photocatalysts has been widely studied for pollutant degradation, where the pollutants themselves act as sacrificial agents and lower the thermodynamic potential for the oxidation reaction. With increasing pollutant concentrations of dyes, pesticides, and drugs in wastewater, the ecological environment is disrupted. Conventional wastewater treatments, such as adsorption, coagulation and ultrafiltration, can remove most water pollutants. However, azo-dyes at a low concentration and non-biodegradable antibiotics are difficult to remove using traditional purification techniques. Hence, photocatalysis as an advanced oxidation process can offer a cheap and versatile solution with high efficiency in this domain.
Antibiotics degradation. In recent years, the resistance of antibiotics to bio-degradation has posed a serious problem, with the concentration of ciprofloxacin being 31.5 and 2.5 mg Lâˆ’1 in wastewater and surface water respectively.62 In the previous examples, it has already been shown that loading low band-gap metal oxides or metal sulphides in MOFs with high surface area and light absorption range is a general way of engineering heterojunctions. Photocatalysts for antibiotics degradation are no exceptions. Therefore, hybrids such as V2O5/NH2-MIL-101(Fe),63 Fe2O3/MIL-101(Cr),64 and SnS2/MIL88B(Fe)65 have shown some promising progress, achieving almost 80â€“100% degradation of tetracycline, carbamazepine, or ibuprofen, in the presence of scavengers. The n-type metal oxide or sulphide semiconductors possess low surface area and are prone to agglomeration and thus low stability. Dispersion in the high surface area MOF structures enables them to provide a larger number of active catalytic sites and improve the stability of the photocatalyst. On the other hand, the MOFs with their porosity and metal clusters are promising materials for reactant diffusion and additional catalytic sites. However, their low band gap and poor charge separation efficiency can be mitigated by coupling with said metal oxides or sulphides. To overcome the problem of limited photo-efficiency of the metal oxides, perovskites have come into being.

Perovskite integrated MOFs form stable Z-scheme photocatalysts for removal of such toxic pharmaceuticals. Perovskite metal oxides (ABO3) have a nearly cubic structure with A-ion (alkaline earth metal) in the centre and B-ions (3d, 4d, 5d transition metal) on the corners of the lattice, with the A-cations in 12-fold coordination with the anions and the B-cations in 6-fold coordination.66 The bismuth based perovskite Bi2WO6 typically acts as an n-type semiconductor with a bandgap of 2.75 eV enabled by the conducive migration of photogenerated carriers within its layers of (WO4)2âˆ’ and (Bi2O2)2+. However, the maximum light absorption wavelength of a single Bi2WO6 is only about 460 nm, which implies that it still cannot make full use of solar energy.67 Further, the recombination rate is too high. Hence, integrating it with NH2-UIO-66 forms a Z-scheme interface where photogenerated carriers can transfer via the Biâ€“Wâ€“Oâ€“Nâ€“Câ€“Oâ€“Zr network, that eventually broadens the spectral range and suppresses the recombination rate. Meanwhile, the holes in the VB of Bi2WO6 participate spontaneously in the direct oxidation of ciprofloxacin or oxidize water, thereby generating hydroxyl radicals, that further degrade ciprofloxacin. SPR is a phenomenon where the electrons in the metal surface layer are excited by photons of incident light with a certain angle of incidence, and then propagate parallel to the metal surface. Subsequently, SPR can expand the optical response range to the visible region. Dye and other pollutant degradation. Apart from antibiotics, the other organic pollutants that constitute a major threat to wastewater and cause scarcity of drinking water are the organic dyes from textile industries, phenol and bisphenols from plastic industries, insecticides like diazonin, and aromatic hydrocarbons like o-xylene. These pollutants have been proved to be carcinogenic and life threatening and have been listed as contaminants of emerging concern by the United states Environmental Protection Agency.78 It has been observed that these contaminants are in concentrations significantly higher than expected, and/or their risk to human and environmental health in ways that may not be fully understood. Therefore, fast and green methodologies to degrade them are necessary. The mentioned pollutants also follow the same mechanism of photocatalytic degradation as the antibiotics discussed previously. Hence, in this section we will mainly highlight the Z-scheme photocatalysts that have not been already mentioned.

Constructing Z-scheme heterojunctions with low bandgap photoactive materials has already been discussed earlier. Some examples include (i) narrow-bandgap (âˆ¼2.3 eV) silver-based nanoparticles (Ag, AgCl, Ag3PO4); (ii) semiconductor like metal sulphides and perovskites (MOS2, ZIS), with a bandgap of 1.9â€“2.6 eV and (iii) bismuth-based oxyhalide semiconductors with a bandgap of 1.8 eV. They perform similarly as in HER or antibiotic degradation reaction pathways. Instead in this section we will focus on some exclusive types of Z-scheme photocatalysts that have been explored for dye and phenol degradation exclusively. In the 2D/3D Z-scheme heterojunction formed in BiOI/MIL-53(Fe), the Z-scheme is formed via interface coupling when the FeIII of the MOF binds to Bi through covalent interactions. The composite exhibits a high performance in degrading phenol, bisphenol, and methylene blue, i.e. 67â€“98%, which is âˆ¼20â€“30% higher than its pristine counterparts. Characterization of MOF-based Z-scheme heterojunctions

The in-depth characterization of (MOF-based) Z-scheme heterojunctions and the associated charge transfer mechanism is challenging and still under development. The current methods for this include (i) XPS, (ii) photoluminescence (PL), (iii) electrochemical measurements like photocurrent, Nyquist plot, and Mottâ€“Schottky plot and (iv) electron spin resonance (ESR) measurements. Since it is assumed that photoemission spectra are proportional to the valence band density of states, VB maxima can be estimated from XPS spectra.84 XPS can be used to validate the formation of Z-scheme heterojunctions and estimation of the VB and CB in MOF-based Z-scheme photocatalysts.35,38,85 PL spectroscopy in a non-destructive characterization method. Here, a material is excited with light of certain energy such that it is higher than its energy bandgap, and consequently the electrons within the material move into excited states. While coming down from this excited state to equilibrium, the energy is released by emitting photons. Hence, PL spectroscopy is used to study separation/recombination of photogenerated charge carriers. A low PL intensity is generated due to the high separation of charge carriers, while a high PL intensity reflects the fast recombination of charge carriers. Time-resolved photoluminescence spectroscopy (TRPL) can supplement the information about the lifetime of excitons and dynamics of photogenerated charge carriers from the resulting decay curves. Higher lifetime of charge carriers is reflective of their better separation and longer residence time to participate in reactions. In the case of characterization by time dependent transient photocurrent response, the semiconductor of interest is sandwiched between two extracting electrodes and excited by light; the photogenerated charge carriers are extracted by the electrodes, resulting in a current response. In the â€œlight onâ€ position, the photogenerated charge carriers build-up, while in the â€œlight offâ€ position, the charge carriers decay. The high intensity of photocurrent is directly proportional to the charge build-up and reflects on the quantity of excitons that can actively participate in the photocatalysis. However the decay time of the charge carriers should also be taken into account. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy is a powerful and versatile tool to investigate the optoelectronic properties and pathways of photogenerated charge carriers. Mottâ€“Schottky spectroscopy provides valuable insights into flat band potential estimation and the number of available charge carriers upon excitation. This supplements the data estimated from XPS and PL spectra. The formation of n-type or p-type heterojunctions at the interfaces and the consequent charge transfer directions can be inferred from Mottâ€“Schottky plots. However, due to the limitations of porosity and electrolyteâ€“electrode interfacial interactions, Mottâ€“Schottky plots need to be carefully interpreted. The Nyquist plot indicates the resistance to charge transfer at the interface of the photocatalyst and the reactant medium. The equivalent series resistance modelled from the Nyquist plot provides additional comprehension towards the built-in electric field formed by the charge carriers, their diffusion lengths, and charge transfer mechanism.

For example, XPS has been used to probe into the formation of Z-scheme heterojunctions and estimation of the VB and CB in Ti-MOF/Ag/NiFe LDH.38 In Fig. 12(aâ€“d), it could be seen that when comparing XPS spectra of Ti-MOF, Ti-MOF/NiFe LDH and Ti-MOF/Ag/NiFe LDH, the Ti 2p peaks of Ti-MOF/Ag/NiFe LDH are shifted to lower binding energy, while the Ni 2p and Fe 2p peaks are shifted slightly to higher binding energy, implying close heterointerfacial contact between Ti-MOF and NiFe LDH via metallic Ag. Recent developments beyond the MOF-based Z-scheme

Despite the attractive features possessed by MOF-based Z-scheme heterojunction photocatalysts, it is to be noted that the metal reserves of the world are depleting and hence metal-free materials need to be developed that can provide similar and/or better functionalities than MOFs. Additionally, the sustainable production of MOFs at low price is still challenging. The optimization of physical and chemical structures of MOF-based Z-scheme photocatalysts needs further improvement in terms of both engineering and theoretical modelling for perfect band alignment. We propose the following for future research advancements: (i) metal-free covalent organic frameworks (COFs), (ii) S-scheme photocatalyst, and (iii) photoelectrocatalytic configuration.

Covalent organic frameworks (COFs) have emerged as the next generation metal-free photocatalyst materials due to their versatile structure, morphology, high porosity, crystallinity, polymeric network, ease of obtaining desired bandgap and band edges by selecting starting monomers, low density, and facile surface modifications. Their unique and multifunctional properties have been explored in photocatalytic reactions like Câ€“S coupling, HER, hydrogen peroxide production, and alcohol oxidation.86â€“89 Zhang et al. have developed a series of ambient light Z-scheme photocatalysts by integrating semiconductor type metal oxide nanoparticles like TiO2 and Feâ€“TiO2 with COFs. The composites showed 95% efficient photocatalytic degradation of methylene blue and high recyclability owing to the high charge separation and transfer via the covalent bonds between TiO2 and COF.90 Adopting a similar approach, Lan et al. developed polyarylether COFs and connected the semiconductors TiO2, Bi2WO6, and Î±-Fe2O3via covalent bonds (Fig. 16). The fast charge carrier transfer from the semiconductor to the COF network via the covalent coupling resulted in electron accumulation in the cyano/pyridine moieties of the COF for CO2 reduction and holes in the semiconductor for H2O oxidation. COF-318-TiO2 exhibited the highest CO production rate of 69.67 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1, which is about six times higher than that of the pure COF-318 and TiO2 individually.92 Direct Z-scheme photocatalyst SnS2/CTF possessed high separation efficiency and strong reducing potential for photoinduced electrons, thus reducing CO2 to CO and CH4 at 123.6 and 43.4 mmol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1 respectively.91

The S-scheme (Step-scheme) photocatalyst is a novel idea proposed by Xu et al. in 2020 (Fig. 17). Previous discussions show that the Z-scheme is formed between two semiconductors when their interfaces come in contact with each other and their respective Fermi levels try to equilibrate. Under light irradiation, the electrons transfer from the CB of semiconductor 2 to the VB of semiconductor 1. Contrarily, in the S-scheme, the difference in the Fermi energy levels of the semiconductors promotes band bending. Under irradiation, the charge carriers move via the bent edges similarly as in the Z-scheme. The combination of the built-in electric field, band-bending, and recombination of the useless electrons and holes drives the charge transfer favourably and enhances the redox capacity of the S-scheme photocatalysts.8

In continuation with the band bending feature of photocatalytic systems, the photoelectrocatalytic (PEC) configuration has also shown promising performance. PECs operate on the same concept as Z-scheme photocatalysis by mimicking natural photosynthesis. However, the main difference lies in the fact that an external electric field can be applied (Fig. 19a). The application of an external bias allows continual extraction and transfer of electrons from the photoanode to photocathode via the external circuit. Therefore, the recombination rate is highly suppressed, and the separation of oxidation and reduction in separate zones improves their quantum yield and faradaic efficiency. PECs allow more relaxation in the design and band edge alignment of photocatalysts, compared to Z-scheme photocatalysts. Core@shell type ZnO@Au@ZIF-67, Ni(OH)2/ZIF-8/ZnO/NF, and Fe2O3/NH2-MIL-101(Fe) show high photoanode current density at 1.23 VRHE of 1.9 mA cmâˆ’2, 1.95 mA cmâˆ’2, and 2.27 mA cmâˆ’2 respectively (Fig. 19b), in the visible or AM1.5G illumination range.96â€“98 These reports suggest that besides exposing the metal cluster sites for better photocatalytic activity and stimulation by visible light, the MOF thin shell is crucial to promote charge separation by providing a cascade band level.

Conclusions

In this review, we have presented an overview of the current research trends that show the emergence of MOF-based Z-scheme heterojunctions for photocatalytic sustainable pathways for production of clean energy and value-added chemicals and degradation of pollutants, e.g. in HER, CO2-RR, and pollutant and dye degradation. The unique properties of MOFs owing to their topological versatility have made them attractive alternatives in photocatalysis. Through logical choice of ligands and metal clusters, it is possible to design MOFs tailored for specific reaction potentials. The most used MOFs to date are MIL-53, MIL-68, MIL-125-NH2, and UIO-66-NH2, since their ligand network can be excited within the visible range and they have metal clusters like Ti and Fe that are active towards photocatalysis. Undoubtedly, MOFs will continue to trigger future research; however, achieving the Z-scheme photocatalytic configuration alone in MOFs is still an uphill task. Therefore, the promising strategies require hybridization of MOFs with semiconductor like metal/metal oxide/metal sulfide nanoparticles, that act as the oxidation or reduction catalyst, depending on the VB and CB of the two systems. To meet the challenges of a global scale sustainable energy system by Z-scheme photocatalysts, more exploration should be done on the front of COFs, S-scheme, and photoelectrocatalytic configurations. These new technologies when combined with MOFs have the promising aspects of mitigating the low photocatalytic stability of MOFs, rigidity arising due to required band alignment, and recombination of photogenerated charges.

Future perspectives

For artificial photosynthetic Z-schemes to be translated to industrialization, multicomponent system modulation of photoactive materials is a necessary technique. The evolution of MOF-based Z-scheme photocatalysts is still in a growing stage, but they possess wide potential to remedy the environmental problems. Especially, in reactions such as HER, CO2-RR, and pollution degradation, the high adsorptivity of MOFs and their hierarchical porosity can improve the molecular diffusion and reaction rates to a higher limit than could be comprehended using metal oxide/phosphate/sulphide nanoparticles. For instance, the selective adsorption of MOF towards CO2 in a mixture of other gases tends to be very high, thus making them suitable materials for CO2-RR photocatalysis. Therefore, versatile functionalization of MOF ligands needs to be investigated to achieve better hydrophilicity, pollutant uptake, and stronger covalent bonding between MOFs and other binary/ternary materials. These can lead to efficient charge separation, light absorption in the visible range, higher product yield, and photostability. Better understanding of the cause of the heterojunction and the chemical configuration of the heterojunctions should be deduced by careful characterization of Mottâ€“Schottky and Nyquist plots, taking into consideration the altering environments of the reactions. Since MOFs have a complex framework, advanced characterization techniques like EXAFS need to be employed to understand the exact loading of the second material and their bonding nature. Since Z-scheme heterojunctions can alter the reaction pathway, more in situ DRIFTS, FTIR or GC-MS characterization of the intermediates should be carried out to evaluate the correct pathway and utilize this knowledge in developing multi-functional MOF-based Z-scheme photocatalysts. Further, the mechanistic reaction pathways of Z-scheme photocatalytic systems require more pertinent explanations subjected to suitable characterisation techniques. It is also important to identify the reactive radical species that actually participate in the reactions. With the aid of EPR and ESR, such radicals and intermediates can be identified. But caution should be applied during refining the spectrum and data interpretation from such analyses. Whenever possible, post-reaction characterization should be carried out to confirm the stability of materials and understand the cause of catalyst destruction. Future research should also focus more on full cell reactions and limit the use of scavengers to understand the rate limiting step in the realistic situation. That would provide more insights into the logical engineering of MOF-based Z-scheme photocatalysts. Additionally, steps must be taken to mould such Z-scheme photocatalysts into beads or tubes, that are viable for industrial reactors. Various start-ups like NOVOMOF AG have already sharpened their techniques to compact certain MOFs for industrial purposes. More emphasis should be given to check if the lab-scale MOF-based photocatalysts can sustain the moulding processes and still retain their photostability. Additionally, theoretical modelling is an important tool which not only provides structural understanding of the band structures of MOFs, but also helps in deducing reaction pathways. An all-inclusive effort from theoretical, chemical, and engineering fields is necessary to tackle the challenges in the pathway of advancing MOF-based Z-scheme photocatalysts.
