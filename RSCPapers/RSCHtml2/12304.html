A review on recent advances in polymer and peptide hydrogels

Sanjoy Mondal† , Sujoy Das† and Arun K. Nandi *
Polymer Science Unit, School of Materials Sciences, Indian Association for the Cultivation of Science, Jadavpur, Kolkata 700032, India. E-mail: psuakn@iacs.res.in

First published on 8th January 2020

Abstract

In this review, we focus on the very recent developments on the use of the stimuli responsive properties of polymer hydrogels for targeted drug delivery, tissue engineering, and biosensing utilizing their different optoelectronic properties. Besides, the stimuli-responsive hydrogels, the conducting polymer hydrogels are discussed, with specific attention to the energy generation and storage behavior of the xerogel derived from the hydrogel. The electronic and ionic conducting gels have been discussed that have applications in various electronic devices, e.g., organic field effect transistors, soft robotics, ionic skins, and sensors. The properties of polymer hybrid gels containing carbon nanomaterials have been exemplified here giving attention to applications in supercapacitors, dye sensitized solar cells, photocurrent switching, etc. Recent trends in the properties and applications of some natural polymer gels to produce thermal and acoustic insulating materials, drug delivery vehicles, self-healing material, tissue engineering, etc., are discussed. Besides the polymer gels, peptide gels of different dipeptides, tripeptides, oligopeptides, polypeptides, cyclic peptides, etc., are discussed, giving attention mainly to biosensing, bioimaging, and drug delivery applications. The properties of peptide-based hybrid hydrogels with polymers, nanoparticles, nucleotides, fullerene, etc., are discussed, giving specific attention to drug delivery, cell culture, bio-sensing, and bioimaging properties. Thus, the present review delineates, in short, the preparation, properties, and applications of different polymer and peptide hydrogels prepared in the past few years.

1. Introduction

Gels are intermediate state of matter between solid and liquid states, and are an important class of soft matter. It is difficult to define a gel but it is easy to realise as it does not flow when the containing tube of the gel is inverted. It contains a three-dimensional fibrillar percolation network structure and in between, a large amount of solvent remains trapped. Mechanically, it behaves as a solid and it is viscoelastic in nature.1–3 The gels may be classified as organogel and hydrogel depending on the nature of the solvent trapped in the gel. Organic solvents are trapped in organogels but in hydrogels, water remains trapped as it acts as the gelation medium. Organogels are very useful, particularly for producing porous materials and here, both small molecules and long chain polymers are used. From the application point of view, hydrogels have great importance because of their significant use in the biomedical field. Though small molecular hydrogels are now growing overwhelmingly because of their diversity in structure, large abundance, and varieties in properties showing applications in diverse fields, however, there a large number of reviews are already available in this area.4–7 So, the main thrust of this review is on polymer and peptide hydrogels because in recent times, there has been tremendous growth in this important area of research with respect to their applications in diverse fields, especially in energy and biomedical fields.

The area of polymer gels has a long history dealing with their structure, origin of gel formation, and physical and mechanical properties8–13 but even now it is an interesting area of research, particularly, finding applications in drug delivery by utilizing the stimuli-responsive and biocompatible properties of the polymers. The polymer gels can be classified as thermo-reversible and thermo-irreversible. In the former, there exists physical cross linking via interchain non-covalent interaction and in the latter, they are chemically crosslinked through a crosslinker; however, both the gels have found different applications. Compared to the small molecular gels, polymer gels are much superior because of their very good mechanical properties contributed by the long covalent chain of the polymer. Here, we would like to discuss about the present progress in both thermoreversible and thermoirreversible gels in diverse fields, e.g., in biomedical (targeted drug delivery, adhesion, and proliferation of cells, tissue engineering, etc.), conductivity, photocurrent, energy generation and storage devices, and dielectric and optoelectronic materials.

Peptide based hydrogels made from supramolecular approach have gained significant attention in recent years. Like polymer hydrogels, the peptide hydrogels have a three-dimensional fibrillar network structure. The fibrills are associated with α-helices, β-sheets, vesicles, micelles, fibers, ribbons, tapes, tubes, and coiled morphologies. Peptide-based hydrogels are prepared by using simple heating–cooling, sonication, and by adjusting the pH of the solutions. The peptide-based hydrogels exhibit multi-stimuli responsive behavior in the presence of external stimuli that have been given great attention to researchers because of their biocompatibility and biodegradibility. So, the peptide hydrogels have now been widely used in different biomedical applications such as drug delivery, protein separation, biosensors, tissue engineering, and wound healing applications due to their low toxicity. Thus, the research on peptide hydrogels is growing profoundly and in this review, glimpses of peptide hydrogels are also included along with polymer hydrogels.

2. Polymer hydrogels

Polymer hydrogels can be produced in different ways and depending on their source, preparation procedure, and properties, the polymer hydrogels can be classified into three categories, e.g., (i) cross-linking polymer hydrogel, (ii) conducting polymer hydrogel, and (iii) natural polymer hydrogel, and we will discuss them in the following sections.

2.1 Cross-linking polymer hydrogel

Polymeric hydrogels have three dimensional fibrillar networks, cross-linked through chemical or physical interactions and are able to entrap a substantial amount of water within its matrix. Polymer hydrogels can be classified into chemical and physical hydrogels based on their cross-linking mechanism. Chemical or permanent hydrogels are made by covalent crosslinking of polymer chains whereas physical crosslinking hydrogel are not permanent in nature, as they are formed via hydrogen bonding, entangled chains, hydrophobic interaction, crystalline interaction, host–guest mechanism, etc., giving them a reversible character. Another category is the double network hydrogel, which is made by the amalgamation of chemical and physical crosslinking because of electrostatic interactions. Recently, they have been widely employed due to their versatile properties and according to the method of preparation, the polymer gels can be classified into three categories (Scheme 1). Chemical crosslinking polymer hydrogels (CCPH) are formed by cross-linking through covalent bonds, resulting in CCPH that can resist mechanical strain. On the other hand, physical crosslinked polymer hydrogels (PCPH) are produced by non-covalent forces, crystallization, and molecular entanglement, resulting in weak mechanical properties, thus imparting viscoelastic behavior. Hybrid double network polymer hydrogels (HDNPH) contain two interpenetrating polymer networks; one is a strongly covalently cross-linked polymer network and the other is a loosely crosslinked physical network that approves energy dissipation at large strains. So, these hydrogels display very high mechanical strength and toughness when compared to individual CCPH and PCPH systems. There is no unique experimental technique to characterize the different types of crosslinking present in the hydrogel. Mainly, NMR and FTIR spectra characterize the chemical crosslinking while physical crosslinking is understood from the FTIR, UV-vis, and WAXS data.

2.1.1 Chemically cross-linked polymer hydrogels (CCPH). Chemically cross-linked polymer hydrogels (CCPH) are synthesized by applying a cross-linker through covalent interactions to interconnect two polymer chains. The covalent bonds in CCPH are stable at ambient conditions owing to their rigid and irreversible nature.14 Most common cross-linkers, such as epichlorohydrin, glutaraldehyde, and polyaldehydes are used to prepare CCPH networks of various natural and synthetic polymers. The CCPH have superior mechanical properties with good solvent resistance, which cause brittleness and make them suitable for various engineering applications. In 1960, Wichterle and Lim introduced the first polymeric hydrogels by crosslinking 2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate (HEMA) in the presence of a suitable crosslinking agent (e.g., ethylene glycol dimethacrylate) for biomedical applications.15 Park and co-workers synthesized reaction-induced poly(N-isopropylacrylamide) (PNIPAAM) hydrogel in the presence of N,N′-methylenebis(acrylamide) (BIS, a chemical cross-linker) and N,N,N′,N′-tetramethyletyhlenediamine (TEMEDA) (an accelerator) by chemical gelation process.16 The gelation process was monitored through time-resolved Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), principal component analysis, and two dimensional correlation spectroscopy (2D COS) below and above the LCST of PNIPAAM aqueous solution (Fig. 1a). The spectroscopic evidence yields the specific time spans in the two-step reaction process: the first-phase shows the formation of linear and branched random NiPAAm co-polymers and the second-step shows the cross-linking of the macroscopic PNiPAAm networks. Furthermore, the 2D COS spectroscopy of the first and second step processes have produced highly superposed spectra in amide I and amide II areas into the fundamental spectra containing the superposed spectra. However, utilizing the same method, Su et al. reported a poly(N-isopropylacrylamide-co-2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate) (PNIPAM-co-HEMA) permanent hydrogel showing micro dynamic mechanism of the volume phase transition behavior.17 During the phase transition, water molecules are expelled out from the hydrogel network. Perturbation correlation moving window (PCMW2D) spectra shows that the phase transition occurs through two processes (I and II) upon heating and the temperature regions of the processes I and II are 21.8–31.4 °C and 31.4–36.5 °C, respectively. In a follow-up work, Xu et al. showed potential in the drug delivery application of poly(acrylic acid)-co-poly-(N-isopropylacrylamide) with macromolecular cross-linker poly-(L-lactide)-co-polyethyleneglycol-co-poly(L-lactide) dimethacrylates based chemical hydrogel. It was noticed that the hydrogel is biodegradable and pH-responsive in nature.18 The hydrogel shows maximum swelling at pH 7.4 and is non-toxic to the cell. Utilizing the latter property, doxorubicin (DOX) was incorporated within the hydrogel matrix and its release property was studied at different pH. At pH 7.4, it showed maximum release efficiency and the DOX loaded hydrogel also destroyed the HeLa cells.

In another eye-catching work for the drug delivery purpose, Das and co-workers prepared a chemically cross-linked dextrin and poly(acrylic acid) (c-Dxt/pAA) hydrogel using N,N′-methylenebis(acrylamide) (BIS) as the cross-linker via Michael-type addition reaction (Fig. 1b).19 The crosslinking density of the c-Dxt/pAA hydrogel was measured using the peak intensity of sp3 carbon protons and was observed to be 52.26%. The hydrogel showed significant temperature and pH sensitivity. Moreover, this hydrogel provided high storage modulus (G′) and has shown non-cytotoxic behavior in human mesenchymal stem cells. The biodegradability studies confirm that Dxt/pAA is biodegradable in nature. The in vitro release studies of ornidazole and ciprofloxacin drugs demonstrate that the hydrogel can release both hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs, and exhibits sustained release of both the drugs. Using a different strategy, Wang et al. reported natural polysaccharide chitosan-based pH responsive hydrogel for different drug delivery. They prepared a chemical hydrogel using chitosan cross-linked poly(acrylic acid) and maleic anhydride as the chemical cross-linker between chitosan and poly(acrylic acid). The hydrogel exhibited sustained release of amoxicillin and meloxicam drugs. The rate of drug release has increased with increase in pH and the release data, fitted by Korsemeyer–Peppas and Weibull models, demonstrated that the release process follows the combination of hydrogel relaxation and drug diffusion processes.20 In another work, highly swellable, stretchable, low creep, non-cytotoxic chemically cross-linked hydrogel was reported by Ganguly and co-workers, who prepared the nano-composite psyllium-g-poly(acrylic acid-co-sodium acrylate)/cloisite hydrogel. The hydrogel is viscoelastic and thixotropic in nature. The mechanical property of the hydrogel was enhanced by mixing with clay nanosheets and the random distribution of clay nanosheets in the hydrogel increased the G′ and loading–unloading cycle measurement.21 This property served the basis for developing a drug delivery system that causes sustained release of drugs. Although the study of cyclodextrin based hydrogels exhibits significant progress in the last two decades, some challenges for the biomedical system still continue. For example, Roy and co-workers prepared a pH responsive chemically crosslinked β-cyclodextrin and N-vinyl pyrollidone (cl-β-CD/pVP) hydrogel via radical polymerization, in the presence of diurethane dimethacrylate (DUDMA) (as crosslinker).22 Different types of cl-β-CD/pVP hydrogels were produced from varying monomer and initiator concentrations having different crosslinking densities, variable gel strength, and different % swelling ratio. The swelling behavior of the gel was measured at acidic and biological pH, and the equilibrium swelling ratio was found to be higher at biological pH. The hydrogel showed different mechanical properties at different pH and at lower pH, it exhibited higher strength because of the lower equilibrium swelling ratio. The hydrogel is non-toxic and biodegradable in nature. Due to its good biocompatibility, amoxicillin was incorporated into the hydrogel, which shows sustained release of amoxicillin. For stimuli responsive hydrogel, Su and co-workers reported the chemically crosslinked temperature sensitive poly(N-vinylcaprolactam-co-hydroxyethyl methacrylate) (PVCL-co-EMA) hydrogel in the presence of a BIS cross-linker, via free radical polymerization.23 The combined temperature-dependent FTIR spectra with scaling moving window two dimensional (scaling-MW2D) correlation spectroscopy was used to explore the volume phase transition (VPT) mechanism of the hydrogel on heating and showed a distinct VPT nature. Because of PHEMA incorporation into the PVCL hydrogel networks, the scaling-MW2D spectra exhibited that the phase transition of the hydrogel is separated into two steps (I and II), and the two steps are at 25.0–32.3 °C and 32.3–46.8 °C, respectively.

The CCPHs are not homogeneous and contain regions of low water swelling dispersed within high swelling regions; the former is produced from high crosslink density, while the latter is produced from low crosslink density. The cross-linking does not take place isotropically because of viscosity and steric factor, which hinder the movement of reactants, thus restricting the cross-linking reaction at equal intervals of the main chain. Due to variation in the cross-link density, different size pores are present in the CCPH gels and drug molecules of different sizes can be embedded in CCPHs. Further, the degree of swelling varies with temperature, so the embedded drug can be released by varying the temperature and pH, which usually occurs during illness of the body. Most importantly, it is the better mechanical property of the CCPH gel compared to the physically crosslinked polymer hydrogel (PCPH) that facilitates the dealing of the drug incorporated gel for its use. PCPHs also have multi-porosity, so drugs of different sizes can also be accommodated here. The incorporation of drugs into CCPH is difficult as only water-soluble drugs can be incorporated via the diffusion process. On the other hand, physically cross-linked polymer hydrogels (PCPH) can be easily used to incorporate both hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs due to their thermoreversible and thixotropic character, and in the following section, we shall discuss the recent advances in PCPHs.

The individual properties of the polymers are very important for their use in specific organs and different CCPHs would be useful for different organs. Undoubtedly, biodegradation is an important part for these gels, which carries the drug through the stomach and small intestine, then to the respective organs. So, different degradation mechanism of CCPH takes place at different parts of the body and in most cases, enzymatic degradation occurs. For example, Guar gum, a polysaccharide gel, is proven to be the best suited for colonic drug delivery as here the hydrolytic cleavage of the glycosidic bonds occurs via enzymatic degradation.24 In the following section, we shall discuss the recent advances in PCPHs.

2.1.2 Physically cross-linked polymer hydrogel (PCPH). Physically cross-linked polymer hydrogels are formed through non-covalent supramolecular interactions such as hydrogen bonding, hydrophobic interactions, and crystallite formation between polymer chains. These hydrogels are usually reversible (by temperature, pressure, or other stimuli) but they are sufficient to cause insolubility of the hydrogels in aqueous media. They are produced by making solutions at high temperature or by sonication and then cooling to room temperature, where the gel is produced on standing. Physically cross-linked polymer hydrogels (PCPH) are usually obtained from natural polysaccharides, poly(vinyl alcohol), poly(ethylene glycol), poly(N-isopropylacrylamide), poly(acrylic acid), poly(vinyl imidazole), etc. These stimuli responsive hydrogels are very useful in drug delivery, cell culture, tissue engineering, 3D-printing, etc.

Physical hydrogels with outstanding mechanical performance were developed by Li et al. with tuneable isotropic/anisotropic structure, consisting of polyacrylamide (PAAm) and poly(dodecyl glyceryl itaconate) (PDGI).25 PDGI acts as a physical crosslinking group in PAAm/PDGI hydrogel via hydrogen bond formation. Here, PDGI produces a lamellar double layer structure and PAAm chains adsorb over the bilayer structure of PDGI through hydrogen bonding. Also, the hydrogel exhibited isotropic/anisotropic swelling in water and DMSO, and the hydrogels displayed high tensile strength. Cyclic tensile loading/unloading studies exhibited the mechanical properties recovery greater than 90% within 30 min at 20 °C. Sun and co-workers synthesized a physical polyampholyte hydrogel via one step random copolymerization of an anionic sodium p-styrenesulphonate (NaSS) monomer and a cationic 3-(methacryloylamino)propyl-trimethylammonium chloride (MPTC) monomer in the presence of 2-oxoglutaric acid that acts as a UV initiator.26 The polyampholyte hydrogels exhibited elasticity with tunable mechanical properties over wide ranges by using different ionic combinations. Also, the hydrogels showed 100% self-recovery and therefore, had high fatigue resistance.

Zhang and co-workers produced a physical double network hydrogel containing an amphiphilic triblock copolymer consisting of a hydrophobic unit at the end blocks of poly(butyl methacrylate) (PBMA) and at the middle hydrophilic block unit of poly(methacrylic acid) (PMAA) with polyacrylamide (PAAm).27 The –NH2 groups of PAAm form hydrogen bonds with the –COOH groups on the PMAA to produce a supramolecular physical network. This special combination in this hydrogel is responsible for high mechanical performance with high toughness, fatigue resistance, and stiffness. In another work, Hu and co-workers produced physically cross-linked hydrogels of poly(acrylamide-co-acrylic acid) (PAm-co-Ac) with clay nano-sheet and Fe3+ ions as the cross-linkers (Fig. 2a).28 The hydrogel exhibited good and tuneable mechanical properties by varying the molar ratio of the Ac/Am monomer with clay and Fe3+ ion concentration. It exhibited excellent mechanical properties with a high tensile strength of 3.52 MPa, good fracture strain of 2113%, elastic modulus of 0.637 MPa, and toughness of 49.11 MJ m3. This hydrogel showed hysteresis behavior during loading–unloading processes. In a similar strategy, Liang et al. a reported poly(acrylamide-co-acrylic acid)/Na alginate/Fe3+ hydrogel via the formation of dual cross-linking hydrogen bonds and ionic interactions (Fig. 2b).29 This hydrogel possessed high elastic modulus (24.6 MPa), tensile strength (10.4 MPa), compression strength (44 MPa), and toughness (4800 J m−2). The self-recovery of the hydrogel, studied at room temperature, showed good self-recovery at 100% strain.

Zhang and co-workers reported a triblock copolymer based thermo-reversible hydrogel having a dominant poly(ethylene oxide) block and terminal poly(N-isopropylacrylamide) (PNIPAm) block with ureido pyrimidinone (UPy) groups incorporated randomly (Fig. 3a).30 UPy moiety acts as the supramolecular cross-linker between different triblock copolymer (Fig. 3b). On increasing the temperature above the lower critical solution temperature (LCST), the PNIPAm part in the triblock copolymer becomes dehydrated producing a micellar structure. Consequently, the hydrophobic UPy moiety causes dimerization to grip the polymer chains, thus producing hydrogen bonded cross-linking side. This supramolecular hydrogel exhibits attractive self-healing, shear-thinning, thermo-reversible, and injectable properties. Zhong and co-workers produced physically cross-linked hydrogels of acrylic acid (AA) with vinyl silica nanoparticles (VSNPs) and Fe3+ ions.31 Herein, hydrogen bonding and Fe3+ ions mediated ionic interactions act as physical cross-linking between the PAA polymer chains, yielding ionic nanocomposite physical hydrogels. This hydrogel exhibits excellent mechanical properties showing tensile strength of 860 kPa and 2300% elongation at break. Also, the hydrogels exhibit first-rate self-healing properties and the healed hydrogel can be stretched to about fifteen times of its original length. Li and co-workers prepared a thermo-responsive hydrogel having a cellulose nanocrystal surface-functionalized by temperature-responsive (PNiPAM) polymer through RAFT polymerization.32 They utilized the thermo-responsive hydrogel as a growth component for cancer spheroids (CSs) at 37 °C and studied the release property of the hydrogel at the temperature below the LCST of PNIPAM. The hydrogel is also useful in drug screening. In another work, Guo et al. reported physical hydrogels having telechelic block-like copolymers with corresponding 2-ureido-4[1H]-pyrimidinone (UPy) groups encapsulated within hydrophobic PEG domains.33 The amphiphilic hydrogel network has high water content due to the high PEG weight segment within the polymers. The hydrogels possess good mechanical strength and pliability upon deformation, which is responsible for UPy–UPy interactions within the hydrophobic nanoscale pockets that maintain the network despite extensive water content. Also, this hydrogel exhibits shape memory behavior.

Cyclodextrins (CDs) based host–guest interaction between CDs and guest moieties via inclusion complexation have inspired the construction of new supramolecular physical hydrogels. Various homopolymers such as poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO) or polyethylene glycol (PEG), poly(ε-caprolactone) (PCL), and poly(propylene oxide) (PPO) have been studied for the synthesis of CD-based poly(pseudo)rotaxanes.34 In addition to polymer chains, lots of small guest moieties can also be encapsulated in the CD cavities through host–guest interaction, forming a physical supramolecular hydrogel. These hydrogels always exhibit thixotropic property and have attracted widespread attention for biological applications. Bian and co-workers fabricated a host–guest macromer (HGM) based supramolecular hydrogel.35 The HGM hydrogel was prepared via host–guest complexation between adamantine (AD) functionalized hyaluronic acid (ADxHA) as guest polymers and monoacrylated β-cyclodextrins (mono-Ac-βCD) as host monomers. The hydrogels are robust and are easily self-healable because of the reversible host–guest interaction. They showed good protection for the encapsulated stem cells in the compression remoulding process, suggesting that they can easily carry the therapeutic cells and can adapt to the nearby tissues very fast. Similarly, Burdick et al. demonstrated a novel hyaluronic acid (HA) based thixotropic and reversible hydrogel,36 where HA is functionalized either with β-CD or with adamantanes and forms the hydrogel upon complexation. The HA hydrogels are injectable in nature and are pronounced for biomedical applications, such as in an injection system with cells or therapeutic agents in vivo and also in 3D-printing “bio-ink”.

2.1.3 Hybrid double network polymer hydrogels (HDNPH). Hybrid double network polymer hydrogels (HDNPH) have two networks. The “first network” in DNPH is generally rigid and is tightly crosslinked through covalent bonds.37 On the other hand, the “second network” is generally soft and is loosely crosslinked through supramolecular interactions (such as hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, coordination interactions, etc.) (Scheme 1). HDNP hydrogels have large water content (ca. 90 wt%), and high mechanical strength and toughness.38 These HDNP hydrogel network structures and properties can be tuned by varying the first network along with the second network. Fig. 4 provides some typical examples of HDNP hydrogels with different types of crosslinkers. Sun and co-workers reported three different stretchable HDNP hydrogels through covalently and ionically crosslinked networks (Fig. 4a–c). BIS acts as the covalent crosslinker for polyacrylamide chains and divalent cations Ca2+ acts as an ionic crosslinker of alginate chains through electrostatic interaction.39 The polyacrylamide-alginate was stretched >20 times its original length without rupture. Guo et al. prepared a thermoplastic double network hydrogel (TPG) via in situ photo polymerization of acrylamide monomer (AAm) in agarose hydrogel matrix in the presence of a little amount of BIS and 2-hydroxy-4′-(2-hydroxyethoxy)-2-methylpropiophenone as the crosslinker and the photoinitiator, respectively (Fig. 4d).40 The TPGs exhibit high mechanical strength with transformable arbitrary 3D-shapes, including a helix that is twisted by the heating–cooling process (Fig. 4e and f). The gels exhibit impressive affinity for the adhesion and proliferation of cells, thus showing potential applications in tissue engineering.

In another important work, Le and co-workers introduced a triple shape memory DN hydrogel obtained from AAm polymerized in the presence of supramolecular network-phenylboronic acid-grafted alginate (Alg–PBA) and PVA. Afterwards, the hydrogel was dipped into an alkaline solution to introduce reversible PBA–diol ester bonds into the Alg–PBA–PVA gel network.41 Ca2+ ions can chelate the alginate chain via an egg-box-like structure. So, Ca2+ in the Agl–PBA–PVA network acts as a “switching segment” to fix the temporary shape. When the hydrogel is dipped into K2CO3 solution, the Ca2+ ions are extracted by CO32− and the hydrogel recovers to its original shape. The dynamic PBA–diol ester bonds and supramolecular interactions of Ca2+-alginate in Agl–PBA–PVA gels do not disturb each other, and easily form a reversible triple shaped memory. In another work, Zhao and co-workers introduced double crosslinked (DC) cellulose hydrogels in the presence of epichlorohydrin as the chemical crosslinker and anhydroglucose unit.42 This hydrogel exhibits higher mechanical property than that of pure cellulose hydrogels. The flexibility and elasticity are studied by compression experiment that demonstrates the recovery of DC hydrogels to their original shape without any estrangement.

In a similar strategy, Chen et al. prepared a hybrid chemical and physical crosslinked DN gel by agar and polyacrylamide (PAM).43 This agar/PAM DN hydrogel was prepared by covalently crosslinked PAM chains and by physical crosslinking of agar. The agar/PAM hydrogel displayed high tensile rupture strength of 10 MPa, fracture tensile strain of 2000%, large toughness of 500–1000 J m−2, and excellent mechanical recoverability (within 10 min). However, the chemically linked DN gels and agar/PAM DN gels exhibit different necking behavior, stress/strain, no stable necking platform, and simultaneous necking. Ouyang et al. reported dual cross-linking biomaterials with hyaluronic acid (HA) based host–guest and covalent cross-linking. β-Cyclodextrin (CD) as host and adamantane (Ad) as guest moieties are separately coupled to HA to produce two different HA based precursors that make a supramolecular assembly on mixing (Fig. 5a).44 Then, to increase the rigidity of the supramolecular host–guest hydrogel, they established that the methacrylate group acts as a photo cross-linker to the macromers. The rheological properties show that the hydrogels are injectable in nature and are good materials for bioinks in 3D printable applications. Time sweep experiment shows greater G′ and G′′ values (∼1000 Pa) observed in the host–guest HA hydrogel (Fig. 5b). The viscosity of the supramolecular cross-linked hydrogel is higher (Fig. 5c) and it breaks down at high strain and quickly recovers the mechanical strength at low strain (Fig. 5d). The advantage of the dual supramolecular and covalent cross-linking hydrogel is that it acts as a good material for 3D printable structure. Azevedo and co-workers reported another double cross-linked hydrogel by covalent crosslinking of genipin (GP) as a cytocompatible chemical crosslinker of chitosan free amine groups with non-covalent coordination bonds between the catechol groups present in polysaccharide deacetylated chitin functionalized with catechol groups (DOPA-CHT) and Fe3+ ions.45 The hydrogels provide a maximum compressive strength of 1.05 ± 0.19 MPa, without deformation until 90% of deformation (E = 28.50 ± 2.09 kPa). The biocompatibility of these hydrogels is proved from the proliferation of seeding ATDC5 cells over the surface of the hydrogels. After 7 days of incubation, the cell viability is good for these hydrogels, as proved by the number of living cells (∼91%). 2.2.1 Ionic conducting polymer hydrogel. Ionic conducting polymer hydrogels have great potential for many advanced applications because of the combination of interesting properties of both hydrogels and ionic conductors. In these ionic hydrogels, the polymer network makes the hydrogel stretchable and the mobile ion accompanying the water molecules makes the ionic hydrogel conducting.49–55 The ionic hydrogel based devices function through a hybrid circuit of mobile electrons and mobile ions. The moderate conductivity (e.g., conductivity σ = 0.1 to 10 S m−1) of these hydrogels has made them promising materials for next-generation actuators, bioelectronic interfaces, artificial muscles, artificial skins, artificial axons, liquid crystal devices, energy generators, soft robotics, etc. Also, the ionic gels offer exceptional performance as gate dielectrics in organic field effect transistors (OFETs). Keplinger et al. synthesized polyacrylamide (PAAm) based ionic, highly stretchable, fully transparent hydrogels by varying the concentration of NaCl for actuators (Fig. 6a and b). An impressive ionic conductivity of 10 S cm−1 was obtained for the hydrogels. The resistivity of the hydrogel increased when the hydrogel was stretched. They produced a transparent loudspeaker using the hydrogel that produced sound across the entire audible range, from 20 Hz to 20 kHz. The loudspeaker is located in front of a laptop, which plays music videos (Fig. 6c and d). The laptop screen was visible through the loudspeaker. Pharr and co-workers prepared a stretchable dielectric elastomer composed of acrylic acid hydrogel.57 They showed that the stretch at rupture and fracture produced energy using the hydrogel of different heights at different stretch-rates. Parida and co-workers reported triboelectric nanogenerators (TENGs) fabricated using stretchable, transparent ionically conductive hydrogel consisting of poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA) polymer network and sodium tetraborate.58 The electrical double layer (EDL) in the hydrogel improved the energy-harvesting performance significantly. Lei and co-workers reported a skin made with ionic hydrogel composed of amorphous calcium carbonate (ACC) nanoparticles, polyacrylic acid (PAA), and alginate chains.63 Herein, ACC nanoparticles act as physical crosslinker of PAA and alginate chains and make the gel highly robust. 2.2.2 Conducting polymer hydrogels. Conductive polymers are organic polymers that can conduct electricity through polymer chains via π-electron conjugation. Herein, the atoms in the chain of the conducting polymer are connected through conjugated unsaturated bonding causing delocalization of electrons through the conjugated bond of the polymer. Some examples of important conjugated conducting polymers are polyacetylene, polypyrrole, polyaniline, and polythiophene. The electrical conductivity of these conducting polymers (10−6 to 101 S cm−1) is higher than that of insulating polymers and lower than that of typical metallic conductivity. The conductivity of the conducting polymer can be tuned by changing the dopant and the dopant concentration added externally. Also, conductive polymers exhibit unique mechanical and optical properties due to their curbed dimensions at the nanoscale level. Gels have a cross-linked network structure that shows intermediate properties between soft and hard matter exhibiting viscoelastic nature in the material. Motivated by the 3D structural features and easy synthetic approaches of supramolecular gels, conductive polymer hydrogel (CPHGs) with 3D structural features were recently prepared by crosslinking the polymer chains using molecules with multiple functional groups. Conductive polymers are organic polymers that conduct electricity through the polymer chains by π-electron conjugation. Herein, the atoms in the conducting polymer are connected through unsaturated bonding, i.e., by making conjugated bonds; then, delocalization of electron may occur through the conjugated bond of the polymer. This kind of polymer is termed as a conducting polymer. Several examples of conjugated conducting polymers are polyacetylene, polypyrrole, polyaniline, and polythiophene. The electrical conductivity of this conducting polymer (10−6 to 101 S cm−1) is higher than that of other insulator polymers and lower than that of a typical metallic conductor. The conductivity of the conducting polymer can be tuned by changing the dopant and the dopant ratio. In addition, conducting polymers exhibit unique mechanical and optical properties due to their curbed dimensions at the nanoscale level. Gels have a cross-linked network structure that shows intermediate properties between soft and hard, exhibiting viscoelastic nature. Motivated by the 3D structural features and easy synthetic approaches of supramolecular gels, conductive polymer hydrogel (CPHGs) with 3D structural features have recently been prepared by crosslinking the polymer chains using molecules with multiple functional groups.69–72 Conducting polymer hydrogels are gels, which contain a conducting polymer along with a supporting multifunctional dopant as well as a gelator. Polyaniline, polypyrrole, polythiophene, or poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) represent the conducting moiety in the conducting polymer hydrogels. CPHGs with 3D nanostructure show great potential in a wide range of applications, such as energy storage/conversion, solar cell, hydrogen evolution reaction, and bioelectronics. Mainly, conductive polymer polyaniline (PANI), polypyrrole (PPy), polythiophene (PTh), and poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) (PEDOT) based conductive polymer hydrogels are widely studied (Fig. 8) and are discussed here.

The most common property among the polyaniline, polypyrrole, and polythiophene based hydrogels is that they are all electronically conducting, showing conductivity in the semiconductor region. The advantage in the gel from the pristine polymer is that the three dimensional network structure helps to increase the conductivity in the gel state due to flow of charges both in longitudinal and lateral directions. However, in all the conducting polymers, doping by foreign substances increases the conductivity both in the gel and xerogel states, and the nature of dopants varies from polymer to polymer as in their pristine states. For example, polyaniline is doped very well by organic or inorganic acids, polythiophene is well doped by keeping the gel over iodine vapour, mixing with LiClO4, etc., and polypyrrole can be doped with biotin, methyl orange, or carbon nanotubes (CNTs). It is necessary to mention here that no single dopant can dope all the conducting polymers and hence also their gels. Also, the most common property in the gels of conducting polymers is their chromic response but there is a difference in their absorption peak positions, which attributes different colours to different conducting polymer gels. Also, the fluorescence property of the gels of the three conducting polymers are different; only polythiophene gels exhibit good fluorescence but there are scarce reports on the fluorescence property of polyaniline and polypyrrole gels.


2.2.2.1 Polyaniline based conducting hydrogels. PANI hydrogels consist of conductive PANI polymer, non-conductive dopant, and gelator components yielding a conductive gel matrix. These are often engineered by adding the conductive polymer PANI to the gel matrix or by polymerizing ANI monomer within a non-conducting gel matrix. The in situ polymerization of ANI to PANI hydrogel is usually called as a dual network gel. Herein, the second network of PANI chains is produced inside a pre-formed polymer gel network. Recently, we have shown that small molecules with multiple crosslinking sites can act as gelators, dopants, as well as crosslinkers to conductive polymer chains, leading to 3D nanostructure conductive polymer gels. In 1992, MacDiarmid and co-workers first reported PANI based gels.73 They showed that a concentrated solution (20 wt%) of PANI in N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) formed gels with aging time and the gelation time decreased with increasing molecular weight.

Bao and co-workers produced a PANI hydrogel using phytic acid as the gelling agent as well as the dopant. Phytic acid has six acid groups that are capable of interacting with more than one PANI chain, thus forming a crosslinking hydrogel network.74 The hydrogels show dendritic coral-like nanofibers possessing high surface area and 3D inter-connected pores. The phytic acid/PANI hydrogel exhibits high conductivity of 0.11 S cm−1. Owing to their high conductivity and large surface area, the hydrogels have potential for application as supercapacitor materials with high specific capacitance (∼480 F g−1), brilliant rate capability, and cyclic stability (∼83% capacitance retention after 10000 cycles). Also, the PANI hydrogels act as glucose oxidase sensors with quick response time (∼0.3 s) and high sensitivity (∼16.7 μA mM−1).

Shi and co-workers reported a thermo-responsive PNIPAM/PANI hydrogel in the presence of phytic acid as dopant as well as crosslinker of PANI chains.75 The hydrogels are prepared by in situ polymerization technique and exhibit good electrical conductivity (0.8 S m−1) because of the development of a 3D conductive polymer network that acts as better transporting path for electrons. In a very exciting work, Li et al. utilized a supramolecular strategy for the preparation of conductive polyaniline–poly(vinyl alcohol) hydrogel (PPH) through dynamic boronate bonding.76 Herein, boronic acid groups introduced into PANI by copolymerization of aniline (ANI), 3-aminophenylboronic acid (ABA) monomer, and boronic acid serve as cross-linker groups to PVA and PANI (Fig. 9a and b). The PPH hydrogel is mechanically robust and exhibited Young's modulus of 27.9 MPa, tensile strength of 5.3 MPa, and 250% elongation at break. The PPH shows high specific capacitance value of 928 F g−1. Also, the hydrogel based flexible solid-state supercapacitor yields a large capacitance (306 mF cm−2 and 153 F g−1) and high energy density of 13.6 W h kg−1. Furthermore, the robustness of the solid state supercapacitor device was analysed by 1000 mechanical folding cycles and exhibited 100% capacitance retention.

The porous structure and ultrathin fibrillar network of the PANI hydrogels was tacitly used as a supercapacitor electrode material, exhibiting a high specific capacitance of 636 F g−1. A stretchable and transparent conducting hydrogel based on PANI with α-CD functionalised polyacrylamide was reported by Hao and co-workers.79 They showed that the α-CD–PAAm/PANI hydrogel is highly stretchable, flexible, and can withstand twisting in random direction, thus also exhibiting an impressive conductivity of 0.39 S cm−1. Sodium alginate–PANI (PANI–SA) hydrogel was prepared via in situ polymerization for producing supercapacitor.80 The reinforced PANI–SA hydrogel exhibits good conductivity of ∼10−3 S cm−1 and it shows an impressive compressive strength of ∼41 kPa. The PANI–SA hydrogel does not contain any adhesive and conducting filler but it is directly used as an electrode material for supercapacitors and shows moderate specific capacitance value (252 F g−1 at 0.5 A g). In a very exciting work, Luo and co-workers prepared PANI/graphene and PANI/N-doped graphene hydrogel through the hydrothermal process.81 The morphology of the polyaniline/graphene hydrogel is tunable by the addition of p-phenylenediamine (PPD) and endowed PANI/N-doped graphene hydrogel with dendritic to long fibrillar networks. Compressive strain–stress studies reveal that PANI/N-doped graphene hydrogel has higher stress value (75.8 kPa) compared to the PANI/graphene hydrogel (9.6 kPa). Also, these hydrogel-based supercapacitors exhibit a maximum specific capacitance value of 610 F g−1.

For bio-electronics purpose, Bettinger et al. reported a robust electronic-ionic conductive PANI hydrogel, which was fabricated in a biologically derived heparin gel framework.82 Methacrylate groups act as photo-cross-linker in heparin and the heparin hydrogel was made via photo-cross-linking of heparin-methacrylate (Hep-MA) under UV irradiation (λ = 365 nm). The Hep-MA/PANI hydrogel displayed storage moduli (G′) = 900 ± 100 Pa and very low impedance value Z = 4.17 Ω at 1 kHz. The hydrogel supported the adjunct proliferation and differentiation of C2C12 murine myoblasts deprived of any surface treatments. Also, Hep-MA/PANI hydrogel is good for electronically active bio-interface material with the mechanical properties of soft tissues collected from excitable cells. Zhao et al. reported an injectable, antibacterial, electrically conductive hydrogel using quaternized chitosan (QCS) grafted PANI with oxidized dextran as the chemical crosslinker.83 QCS was prepared by the reaction of chitosan with glycidyltrimethylammonium chloride in acetic acid medium. The hydrogel is biodegradable in nature and exhibited impressive conductivity of 15.7 × 10−4 S cm−1. The injectability of the gel was examined through in vivo subcutaneous injection on a Sprague Dawley rat and the antibacterial activity was verified by employing antibacterial assay using Escherichia coli and Staphylococcus aureus in vitro.

Duan and co-workers prepared a stretchable and force-sensitive electrically conductive chitosan/polyacrylamide/polyaniline hybrid (MC) hydrogel. First, microspheres of chitosan (CSM) were synthesized via the emulsion process and they were dipped into acrylamide and BIS solution for swelling. The hydrogel exhibits good potential for removing organic pollutants through adsorptive preconcentration followed by photocatalytic oxidation. First, pollutants are adsorbed on the 3D hierarchical nanostructure, then the pollutants are in situ oxidized through photocatalysis. Similarly, Jiang et al., prepared a PANI/TiO2 nano-composite hydrogel for photocatalytic degradation.86 They proposed a mechanism of adsorption-enrichment and in situ photocatalytic degradation by synergistic interaction between the components.

Various nanomaterials including nanocarbons and metal nanoparticles may be incorporated in conductive polymer-based hydrogels. Nanocarbon materials such as graphene oxide (GO), porous carbon, and carbon nanotube (CNT) forming assembly with PANI to produce conductive hydrogels have been successfully demonstrated. These gels have high surface area and excellent electrical conductivity, which are contributed by the carbon material. Zou and co-workers reported robust dual crosslinked functionalized graphene/polyaniline stiff hydrogels (DN-PGH/PANI) through the polymerization of aniline and graphene (PGH) hydrogel matrix.87 PANI exhibits two types of morphologies: nanodot protrusions and nanofibers, where PANI nanodot protrusions are strongly anchored to graphene and thus, PANI nanofibers form crosslinks with graphene sheets. Also, the formation of AgNPs significantly enhances the specific capacitance to 646 F g−1 and photocurrent to 56 mA. The FP2Ag xerogel based device provides faster rise and decay in time-dependent photo-illumination compared to that of the FP xerogel based device. In the FP hydrogel, PANI exists in the doped radical cation emeraldine salt (ES) form, which helps in the adsorption of anionic pollutants.94 The FP xerogel could selectively absorb Cr2O72−(VI) and anionic dyes (rose Bengal, eosine yellow, and methyl orange) from water, while displaying low adsorption capacities to cationic (Pb(II), Hg(II), rhodamine B, methylene blue, and bismark brown Y) and neutral pollutants (neutral red). The selectivity in absorption behavior is due to the electrostatic attraction between the anion and the positively charged doped PANI.

Recently, PANI based hydrogel has attracted growing attention for conversion into functional biosensors.1–6,98–103 Zhai and co-workers prepared Pt nanoparticles (PtNPs)–polyaniline (PANI) hydrogel heterostructures for the detection of glucose enzyme sensor.4 The hydrogel and PtNPs nanostructures have synergistic interaction between PANI hydrogel and Pt nanoparticle, which is catalysed by high density immobilization of the enzyme. 2.2.2.2 Polypyrrole based conducting hydrogels. Polypyrrole (PPy) is rigid and insoluble because of the delocalised π-electrons in the polymeric chains; therefore, the synthesis of PPy based conductive hydrogel remains challenging. PPy hydrogels are not as common as PANI hydrogel. In 2013, Wei et al. reported a PPy hydrogel using methyl orange (MO) as the dopant and FeCl3, Fe2(SO4)3, and Fe(NO3)3 as the oxidants under static in situ polymerization of pyrrole and obtained self-assembly of nanotubes.104 The electrical conductivity of the hydrogel varies by ∼1 order with the shrinking–swelling cycles. In the shrinkage process, the PPy chains come closer, thereby smoothing electron transfer, resulting in increase in conductivity (1.7 × 10−2 S cm−1). But in the swelled condition, the PPy chains become separated and the conductivity decreases to 8.2 × 10−3 S cm−1.

In a very exciting work, Deng and co-workers reported a conductive self-healing hydrogel consisting of β-cyclodextrin (β-CD), N-isopropylacrylamide (NIPAM), CNT, and nanostructured polypyrrole (PPY).105 The self-healing hydrogels were synthesized by copolymerization of NIPAM, acryloyl-β-cyclodextrin (AC-β-CD), and (poly-(ethylene glycol)-b-poly(propylene glycol)-b-poly(ethylene glycol) (PF127))/CNT, and then subsequent pyrrole polymerization inside the gel matrix (Fig. 13a and b). The poly(NIPAM-co-β-CD)/CNT/PPY hydrogel exhibited a high conductivity value of 34.9 S m−1. The self-healing properties of the hydrogel were observed from the rheological experiment (Fig. 13c) and the excellent self-healing ability is presented in Fig. 13d and e. The hydrogel exhibited fast response rates towards temperature and near infrared (NIR) light. They also prepared a hydrogel based electrical circuit for bulky pressure-dependent sensors and self-healable electronic device. Also, it was observed that L929 cells and C2C12 cells possessed higher proliferation rate after three days when cultured on poly(NIPAM-co-β-CD)/CNT/PPY hydrogel. The synthesis of PPy hydrogels using PAM/chitosan (CS) hydrogel framework as molecular templates yielded the PPy–PAM/CS hydrogel that exhibited high electrical conductivity, excellent mechanical properties, and good biocompatibility.106 The hydrogel displayed a compressive strength of 6.5 MPa, high compressive modulus of 136 MPa, stretchability >250%, and a fracture energy of 12000 J m−2. The conductivity of the hydrogel increased when PPy was grown in situ, showing the highest value to be ∼0.3 S cm−1. The hydrogel has good biocompatibility and electroactivity. Also, this hydrogel is capable of propagating electrical signals into cell/tissue and subsequently improves the cell activity.

Recently, Yu and co-workers reported some PPy based hydrogels for various applications. In 2015, they synthesized a self-healing metal–ligand supramolecule with a conductive polypyrrole hydrogel.107 They prepared a supramolecular metallo-organic gel with cubic architecture consisting of 2,2′:6′,2′′-terpyridine (tpy) as the organic ligand and Zn(II) as the gluing element. Then, phytic acid doped PPy was introduced in the metal organic gel via reversible sol–gel transition, to produce hybrid metal organic conducting polymer (PPy/G-Zn-tpy) hybrid hydrogel. The gel exhibited high electrical conductivity of 12 S cm−1. Also, the hydrogel-based self-healable electronic circuit showed good potential applications in various fields such as self-healing electronics, artificial skins, and soft robotics. Also, they introduced a supramolecular strategy to synthesize different morphologies of PPy conducting hydrogels using a dopant copper phthalocyanine-3,4′,4′′,4′′′-tetrasulfonic acid tetrasodium salt (CuPcTs).108 Herein, CuPcTs acts as both dopant and gelling agent. The morphology of the CuPcTs–PPy hydrogel exhibited interconnected nanofibers and the fibre diameter increased with decreasing CuPcTs concentration. The CuPcTs–PPy hydrogel displayed a conductivity value of 7.8 S cm−1, which is higher than that of pristine PPy. Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of the CuPcTs–PPy hydrogel exhibits a specific capacitance of ∼400 F g−1 at 0.2 A g−1, which is higher than that of pristine PPy (∼232 F g−1). The CuPcTs/PPy hydrogel hybrid with commercial lithium iron phosphate (C-LFP) particles were used as model cathode materials to obtain a hybrid inorganic–organic hydrogel matrix for high performance Li+ ion hydrogel suitable for high performance Li-ion battery.109 Uniform coating of C-LFP particle with PPy prevents C-LFP aggregation, enabling full access to each particle. Similarly, Fe3O4 nanoparticle hybrid CuPcTs–PPy hydrogel also acts as a good material for Li-ion battery.110

Chen et al. synthesized a stretchable, fast self-healing, and adhesive conductive hydrogel by in situ polymerization of pyrrole in a supramolecular polymer matrix consisting of poly(ethylene glycol)methyl ether methacrylate (PEGMA), and 2-ureido-4[1H]-pyrimidinone (UPy) group modified 2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate (HEMA-UPy) cross-linked by multiple hydrogen-bonding to obtain an elastic network.111 In an eye-catching work, Park et al. reported a self-healing and thermoplastic conducting hydrogel prepared by linear polysaccharide derivative of agarose with PPy. Agarose itself produces reversible hydrogel by varying the temperature.112 Pyrrole monomer and copper(II) chloride (an oxidizing agent) were mixed with molten agarose matrix (∼40 °C). Then, on decreasing the temperature of the mixed molten agarose to 25 °C, concomitant gelation of agarose and homogeneous polymerization of pyrrole monomer inside the agarose hydrogel matrix resulted in the formation of the PPy/agarose (APy) hydrogel. The APy hydrogel displayed dc-conductivity of ∼1.95 × 10−1 S cm−1 at a pyrrole concentration of 450 mM. The thermoplastic property of the APy hydrogel persisted till the pyrrole concentration of 150 mM. The APy hydrogel exhibited a Young's modulus of 27–46 kPa, which is closer to that of human skin of 5 kPa, thus permitting the hydrogel to be directly painted on human skin and used as stretchable and bendable bioelectrode.

Chen et al. synthesised a strong and elastic conducting polypyrrole (PPy) hydrogel using PVA, SDS, and APS as the oxidants under static reaction conditions.113 The PPy hydrogel had a spongy biphasic microstructure that was simultaneously produced during the hydrogel formation. It had a high tensile strength of 2 MPa, good stretchability (elongation at break 500%), and high elasticity. The conductivity of the hydrogel was 0.8 S cm−1. The PVA/PPy hydrogel was utilized as a stretchable supercapacitor (SSC) electrode material. The PPy hydrogel-based SSC provided an areal capacitance of 950 mF cm−2 at a current density of 1.6 mA cm−2. 96% of the capacitance of this SSC device was retained when elongated to 100% strain and 81% after 500 stress–strain cycles. In another eye-catching work, Ding and co-workers reported a multifunctional hybrid conductive hydrogel based on viscoelastic PVA–borax (PB) gel matrix and nanostructured cellulose nanofibers (CNFs)–PPy complexes. The CNF–PPy complexes were prepared via static polymerization of pyrrole monomer over the surface of CNF templates, which were well-dispersed on the PB hydrogel network to obtain homogeneous CNF–PPy/PB hybrid hydrogels (Fig. 14a–d).114 Herein, the CNF–PPy network tangled with PVA chains through hydrogen bonds and reversible cross-links with borate ions. The stress–strain experiment revealed that the CNF–PPy/PB hydrogel displayed maximum storage modulus of ∼0.1 MPa and high compression stress (∼22 MPa). The CNF–PPy/PB hydrogel provides maximum conductivity of 3.65 ± 0.08 S m−1 and shows maximum specific capacitance of 236.9 F g−1. Also, the CNF–PPy/PB hydrogel demonstrated excellent biocompatibility, pH sensitivity, plasticity, and fast self-healing ability without additional external stimuli.

2.2.2.3 Polythiophene based hydrogel. Thiophene monomer can be modified at α and β positions by hydrophilic groups to make it water-soluble. The chemical structures of water-soluble polythiophenes used to fabricate conductive hydrogels are presented in Fig. 15. In 2000, Chen and co-workers first reported a polythiophene-based hydrogel by chemically cross-linking poly(3-thiophene acetic acid) (PTAA) in aqueous medium.115 Herein, adipoyl dihydrazide was used as the chemical crosslinker of the functional carboxylic groups of PTAA and N,N′-dicyclohexylcarbodiimide (DCC) as the condensation agent to produce the 3D network of the hydrogel. The swollen hydrogel exhibited a conductivity 7 × 10−4 S cm−1 and the conductivity of the hydrogel increased to 10−2 S cm−1 when the hydrogel was doped with HClO4. In a follow up study, Mawad et al. reported a chemically crosslinked PTAA hydrogel using 1,1′-carbonyldiimidazole (DCI) as the crosslinking agent.116 The advantage of DCI is its waste products, which are soluble in water and are easily washed from the hydrogel network. The swelling ratio and the mechanical properties of the hydrogel could be tuned by changing the crosslinker ratio to the monomeric unit of PTAA. Single component PTAA hydrogel exhibited high conductivity of ∼10−2 S cm−1 and good electroactivity in physiological conditions and the hydrogel was tested as a scaffold for tissue engineering.

PEDOT based hydrogel is of great interest because of its high conductivity and good stability in aqueous medium, and versatility of side chain functionalization. To this effect, it has been utilized for the construction of conductive hydrogels. Dai et al. utilized supramolecular self-assembly for the fabrication of poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) (PEDOT)–poly(styrenesulfonate) (PSS) based conducting hydrogel. EDOT and SS monomers were polymerized in aqueous medium in the presence of Fe3+ ions, which act both as oxidant and ionic crosslinker to produce the hydrogel.117 The AFM images demonstrated that the assembly of spherical grains in the hydrogel originated from physical crosslinking of the PEDOT–PSS particles. The hydrogel provided a conductivity of 4.1 × 10−3 S cm−1 in the swollen state but the conductivity increased in the dry compressed state to a value of 4.3 S cm−1. In another work, alkoxysulfonate functionalised PEDOT based hydrogel was fabricated via one-step polymerization of sodium 4-(2,3-dihydrothieno[3,4-b][1,4]dioxin-2-yl)-methoxybutane-1-sulfonate (EDOT-S) monomer in the presence of oxidant FeCl3 or APS.118 The SEM images demonstrated that the interior 3D structure of the PEDOT-S hydrogels could be controlled depending on the oxidant and its ratio. The gels showed a maximum conductivity of 102 S cm−1, and the conductivity depended on the oxidant type and concentration of the monomer. The authors described the mechanism as follows: in aqueous medium, amphiphilic EDOT-S exists in micellar form, which upon polymerization fuse together to form lamellar sheets budding into a 3D hydrogel network. In a splendid work, Mawad and co-workers synthesized a conducting hydrogel based on N-(3-aminopropyl)methacrylamide hydrochloride functionalised PEDOT co-polymerized with acrylic acid (AA) in the presence of poly(ethylene glycol)diacrylate (PEG-DA) as a crosslinker.119 The hydrogel based film exhibited good electrochemical peaks at both anodic and cathodic regions, indicating that the electron transfer ability of PEDOT was not hindered by functionalization with carboxylic group. The swelling studies revealed that the freeze-dried xerogel exhibited incredible water uptake after 48 h (∼5000%). Also, the hydrogel scaffolds were used as hydrated 3D structures for proliferation, adhesion, and differentiation of C2C12 cells.

Yao et al. reported that pure acid treated PEDOT:PSS (AT-PEDOT:PSS) hydrogels could be synthesized by adding concentrated H2SO4 into PEDOT:PSS aqueous solution, followed by thermal treatment.120 The AT-PEDOT:PSS hydrogels displayed high electrical conductivity of 8.8 S cm−1 with over 95% water content. Air dried fibers were made by the AT-PEDOT:PSS hydrogel and were utilized in current free collector fiber-shaped solid supercapacitor electrode. This fibre supercapacitor shows high volumetric capacitance of 202 F cm−3 at 0.54 A cm−3 with high-rate performance. It also delivers brilliant electrochemical stability and good flexibility towards energy-storage devices. In an interesting work, the strategy of green method was applied to synthesize stretchable supercapacitors using harmless conductive polymer hydrogels of PEDOT:PSS/PVA/PMAA.121 The hydrogel possessed large stretchability, excellent conductivity (3.1 S cm−1), as well as good air stability. Using this hydrogel as current collector and electrode material for stretchable and flexible supercapaciator, the device provided a specific capacitance of 7.38 mF cm−2 at 10 mV s−1, and displayed 82% capacitance retention after 2000 charge–discharge cycles examined at a current density of 10 mA cm−2. The excellent mechanical properties were observed when the device was stretched up to 100% strain without disturbing the electrochemical performance.

The preparation of tough, stretchable, robust double conducting hydrogels was carried out by simply soaking preformed PEDOT–PVA hydrogel in sulfosuccinic acid (SA) aqueous medium.122 The attachment of SA/PVA/PEDOT in the hydrogel greatly enhanced the mechanical properties and conductivity of the gels due to its high ionic strength and enriched ionic functional groups. The SA/PVA/PEDOT containing gels had high tensile stress ∼2.5 MPa, elongation >600%, and maximum conductivity of ∼25 S cm−1. The self-healing properties of the gels were examined through a rheological experiment that displayed fast self-healing property with the aid of hot water. The SA/PVA/PEDOT hydrogel was utilized for the fabrication of a strain sensor device.

In an innovative strategy, Xu and co-workers reported physical conducting hydrogel based on poly(ethylene glycol)–peptide and PEDOT:PSS (Fig. 16a and b).123 Remarkably, the non-covalent crosslinking peptide-based hydrogel possessed injectable as well as self-healing properties. Cyclic voltammetry studies showed that the hydrogel is electro active in the anodic as well as in the cathodic region. Also, the hydrogel exhibited low impedance value of 35 Ω.

2.2.2.4 Hydrogels with combined ionic and electronic conductivity. There are also some reports on the combined ionic and electronic conductivity in the hydrogels.128 Manthiram and his coworkers produced randomly stacked holey graphene anodes for lithium ion batteries, exhibiting high charge–discharge rate because of the large surface area of the anode that was readily accessible to the electrolyte, thus reducing the electronic and ionic transport length and facilitating a rapid charge–discharge even at high current density.129 Wang et al. produced a three-dimensional porous LiFePO4 cathode material modified with a nitrogen-doped graphene aerogel for high-power lithium ion batteries. The cyclic voltammetry results showed symmetric and sharp redox peaks, suggesting that the redox kinetics were enhanced due to the improved conductivity (electronic and ionic) and the reduced diffusion length. This yielded ultrahigh power density of 8.6 kW kg−1 with high energy density of 4 9 W h kg−1.130

Pan et al. showed that the PANI hydrogels with phytic acid gelator having 3D hierarchical porous nanostructure exhibited very high conductivity of 0.11 S cm−1 and showed superior performance in electrochemical devices. Basically, it involved both ionic and electronic transport processes at the electrode and the electrolyte interface. For conducting polymers, the pseudocapacitance arises from faradaic reactions (doping and undoping) occurring close to the surface of the active electrode materials. This 3D hierarchical hydrogel afforded a short diffusion path for the ions to access the electron conducting surface of PANI, thus improving the electrochemical performance. The system yielded a specific capacitance of ∼480 F g−1 at a current density of 0.2 A g−1 showing excellent rate performance with only ∼7% capacitance loss when the current density was increased by a factor of 10, indicating an exceptional rate capability for high power performance. This high rate performance was attributed to the facile electronic and ionic transport due to the hierarchical conducting network.131 Kalupson et al. fabricated symmetric carbon nanotube capacitors using highly ionically conducting poly(vinyl alcohol)-based hydrogel membranes absorbing sulfuric acid. The system behaved as a supercapacitor, showing high power density (1040 kW kg−1) and excellent cyclic stability (>10000 cycles) with good coulombic efficiency and energy density. This may be attributed to both the electronic and ionic conductivities present in the system.132

2.3 Natural polymer hydrogels

Natural polymers are the most popular biopolymers, crucial ingredients of marine animals, green plants, bacteria, algae, etc. Sustainability, biocompatibility, biodegradability, and low cytotoxicity of the natural polymers provide large number of applications in the biomedical field. Most common natural polymers and polysaccharide, for e.g., cellulose, chitosan (a chitin derivative), and hyaluronic acid (HA), are used for hydrogel preparation. Cellulose, having β-(1–4) glycosidic bonds between D-glucose units (Fig. 19a),133 provides a linear chain syndiotactic polymer with hydroxyl groups present in an equatorial position. Cellulose with stiff rod-like conformational arrangement gives crystalline fibrous materials. Cellulose based hydrogels can be prepared by two different methodologies: (a) cellulose-based matrices and (b) nanocellulose incorporating into composites.134,135 Nowadays, cellulose based-hydrogels are perfect materials for tissue engineering applications because of their intrinsic properties such as biocompatibility, non-toxicity, porous microstructure, and good mechanical properties.

Lin et al. prepared amorphous alginate-oxidized nanocellulose hydrogel in the presence of Ca2+ as the ionic crosslinking agent.136 Carboxyl groups over nanocellulose surface were introduced by chemical oxidization through 2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidine-1-oxyl radical (TEMPO). By freeze-drying, the hydrogel sponges were prepared and these sponges showed great potential as thermal and acoustic insulating materials, kinetic energy absorbers, reinforcing platforms, and scaffolds for nanocomposites. Using a similar method, Syverud and co-workers reported a cellulose nanofibrillar (CNF) hydrogel using TEMPO mediated oxidation and fibrillation.137 Herein, a number of carboxyl and aldehyde groups were introduced over the CNF surfaces during this process. The aldehyde groups easily formed crosslinks with ethylenediamine and hexamethylenediamine producing elastic hydrogels. The CNF based hydrogel can be applicable in tissue engineering. A ionic-covalent cross-linked CNFs hydrogel was synthesized using oxidised CNFs reinforced covalent polyacrylamide (PAAm) with multivalent cations (Ca2+, Zn2+, Al3+, and Ce3+) (Fig. 19b and c).138 The multivalent cations induced the formation of porous networks of nanofibrils by attracting repulsive negative charges on CNF and the UV-vis spectra is presented in Fig. 19d. The ionic hydrogels provide good stiffness (Young's modulus 257 kPa) and toughness (fracture toughness 386 kJ m−3) properties.

In an eye-catching work, Yang and co-workers reported cellulose nanocrystals (CNCs) skeleton induced covalently cross-linked polyacrylamide (PAAm) composite hydrogel.139 The synergistic interaction between the CNC skeleton and the elastic PAAm hydrogel provided a tensile stress of 181 kPa, compressive strength of 1.01 MPa, and 1392% elongation at break with high breaking energy (2.82 kJ m−2). Also, loading–unloading cycles (after eight cycles) reveal that the hydrogels showed more than 70% recovery. Maleimide-functionalized CNCs based hydrogels were prepared by effective cross-linking of gelatin and chondroitin sulfate, which formed stiffer networks with lower swelling ratios.140 The nanocomposites provided high strength, modulus, and toughness because of the good interfacial adhesion and stress transfer from the chemical linkage between the polymeric matrix and nanofillers. For tissue-engineering purposes, bacterial cellulose (BC) can be used as a transplant replacement tissue for skin.141 BC with hierarchical structure facilitates the healing process. Biological effects of the top and bottom sides were examined by BC film and the in vitro experiments suggested that the cells provided better viability and proliferation rate on the bottom side than on the top side. The BC membrane was used as a carrier for controlled release of berberine hydrochloride and berberine sulphate.142 The release curve was analysed through Ritger–Peppas model showing free diffusion as the most prominent mechanism of drug release. In another work, BC hydrogel was used for various strain rate hardening, softening, or strain-rate insensitivity.143 BC nanofibers are haphazardly spread in a fibrous cover; few fibres act as cross-linkers to interconnect the fibrous layers, developing a multi-layered network structure. These hydrogels provide high crystallinity of 60–90%. Liu and co-workers reported a stimuli responsive cellulose hydrogel prepared from cellulose acetoacetate (CAA) and cystamine dihydrochloride (CYS).144 The hydrogel exhibited reversible sol–gel transition on varying the pH and redox triggers.

Chitosan (CS) is a linear polysaccharide consisting of randomly present β-(1–4)-linked D-glucosamine and N-acetyl-D-glucosamine units, and is synthesized by the exhaustive deacetylation of chitin. Chitosan is the second most abundant natural polymer on earth after cellulose. The physical properties of chitosan can be tuned by varying the molecular weight of the precursor, deacetylation, and degree of depolymerization. The ionic character of chitosan helps to produce hydrogels via electrostatic interactions.145 Sun et al. reported supramolecular hydrogels consisting of biopolymer chitosan with a variety of metal ions (specifically, Ag+, Cu2+, Co2+, Ni2+, Zn2+, and Cd2+) (Fig. 19e–g).146 They demonstrated a series of transparent hydrogels within a few seconds via supramolecular complexation. Chitosan (CS) with Ag+ ions forms CS–Ag hydrogel that exhibits distinct phase transitions behavior under external stimuli, such as pH, cations, anions, neutral species, and chemical redox reactions. Ionotropic chitosan metal hydrogels was synthesized by the in situ formation of colloidal metal salts in chitosan solution.147 Herein, chitosan, present in the polycationic state, attracted the negatively charged surface of the metal salts, thus forming gel networks. They used Al3+, Cu2+, Fe3+, Cr3+, V2+, Ni2+ and the rare-earths such as Ce4+, Pr3+, Nd3+, Y3+, Sm3+,Eu3+, La3+, Gd3+, and Tb3+ based colloidal metal salts for the gelation purpose. These hydrogels were found to be self-healing in nature, and the Fe3+ based gel showed antimicrobial nature and low cytotoxicity. Similarly, Wahid et al. fabricated a novel type of carboxymethyl chitosan supramolecular hydrogel through cross-linking with metal ions (Ag+, Cu2+, and Zn2+).148 Simple mixing of chitosan in aqueous solution with metallic salt solutions at an appropriate pH formed the hydrogels within a few seconds. The antibacterial activities of the hydrogels were examined through Staphylococcus aureus and Escherichia coli by diffusion method, and they showed outstanding antibacterial activities. CS hydrogel has been used in many drug delivery systems, present in the unionized amine state at the alkaline/neutral pH condition, whereas anionic polyelectrolyte exists in the ionized state. To overcome this difficulty, modified chitosan such as quaternized chitosan (QCS) was used.149 QCS was synthesized by reacting chitosan with 3-chloro-2-hydroxypropyltrimethylammonium chloride and urea in alkaline solutions. QCS with poly(acrylic acid) (PAA) based polyelectrolyte complex hydrogels was made by in situ polymerization of the AA monomer. The mechanical properties of the hydrogels were precisely tuned from the soft and elastic to stiff and viscoelastic by varying the PAA concentration. Hu and co-workers reported a dual network of N-carboxymethyl chitosan and alginate hydrogels, prepared by both divalent chelation and electrostatic interaction.150 The hydrogel provided wound healing and cell proliferation via epidermal growth factor. Jing et al. synthesized a chitosan/GO/polydopamine based conducting hydrogel by oxidizing dopamine and simultaneous reduction of GO.151 The hydrogel provided three times more adhesive strength than chitosan–dopamine based hydrogel. Also, the cell viability results proved that the hydrogel improved the proliferation and cell viability of human embryonic stem cell-derived fibroblasts and myocardial cells. Chen and co-workers synthesized a new microporous chitosan hydrogel, which depended upon the self-crosslinking reactions between maleimide and amine on the modified chitosan even below the freezing point.152 The chitosan based hydrogel exhibited good potential as a pollutant remover. Yu and co-workers fabricated a sponge-like composite hydrogel by the combination of chitosan, reduced graphene oxide (RGO), and montmorillonite clay.153 The resulting hydrogel provided good potential for the removal of hexavalent chromium ions (Cr(VI)). The Cr(VI) ion sorption of the hydrogels is greatly pH dependent and was maximum at pH = 2 and maximum Cr(VI) uptake by the hydrogel was 87.03 mg g−1 at 288 K.

Hyaluronic acid (HA) is an immunoneutral polysaccharide that consists of alternating units of a repeating disaccharide, β-1,4-D-glucuronic acid-β-1,3-N-acetyl-D-glucosamine.154 HA can be modified in different ways to alter the physical properties of the newly modified HA, which gives hydrophobicity and biological activity. Shin and co-workers reported a catechol-modified HA (HA–CA) hydrogel prepared via oxidative crosslinking, which provides good biocompatibility and tissue-adhesive properties. The HA–CA hydrogel has been applied to immobilize hydrogels in vivo.155 HA can also from hydrogels by harnessing non-covalent associations that define supramolecular assemblies. For example, water-soluble monofunctionalized cucurbit[6]uril–hyaluronic acid (CB[6]–HA), guest moieties diaminohexane conjugated HA (DAH–HA), and drug conjugated CB[6] (drug-CB[6]) crosslink through host–guest interactions forming the hydrogel network.156 The hydrogels possess good potential in controlled drug delivery for cartilage regeneration and several other tissue engineering applications.

3. Hydrogels of functionalized amino acid derivatives

The development of amino acid based functional soft materials using a supramolecular approach has gained significant attention in the recent years.157 In particular, supramolecular hydrogels derived from amino acids and its derivatives through non-covalent interactions have been widely used as biomaterials in the fields of drug delivery, tissue engineering, biosensors, protein separation, and wound healing applications because of their low toxicity, biocompatibility, and biodegradability.158–160 Biocompatibility is defined as the relationship between a material and the organism so that it neither produces undesirable effects but generate appropriate beneficial cellular or tissue response, thus optimising the clinically relevant performance of the therapy.161 The standard values of biocompatibility of materials have been evaluated by the international standard ISO 10993 series. Biodegradability is the ability of substances (organic, polymers, and materials) to be broken down into simpler substances by bacteria or other living organisms, thus avoiding pollution. Toxicity is the degree to which a chemical substance or a particular mixture of substances can damage an organism. The adverse effect can vary from a minor side effect to a major threat to life or survival of the patient. Toxicity can be acute, subchronic, and chronic. Acute toxicity involves harmful effects in an organism through a single or short-term exposure. Subchronic toxicity is the ability of a toxic substance to cause effects for more than one year but less than the lifetime of the exposed organism. Chronic toxicity is the ability of a substance to cause harmful effects over an extended period.162,163 Low toxicity means toxicity much below acute toxicity, which varies from substance to substance and also with their concentration in the medium. Toxicity is a dose dependent parameter and recently, Dixit has proposed “Drug Toxicity Index” (DTI), where the author has provided mechanistic insights and has predicted outcomes clinically.164 Toxicity can be measured by its effects on the target. The lethal dose of toxin, radiation, and pathogen are measured by using median lethal dose (LD50) and lethal concentration, 50% (LC50). Duffus has also proposed different terms and the lethal dose used in toxicology.165

The non-proteinaceous aromatic groups are incorporated into the peptide/amino acid derivatives to enhance the intermolecular interactions and thus, increase the gelation stability. For example, fluorenylmethyloxycarbonyl (Fmoc) acts as N-terminus protecting group for peptide synthesis and the presence of Fmoc in the amino acid/peptide framework enhances π–π stacking as well as the self-assembly process. Recently, the development of supramolecular hydrogels from Fmoc-amino acid derivatives has received great attention due to their increasing applications in numerous fields of material and biochemical sciences.166–171 Fmoc-Phe-DAP molecular systems spontaneously and rapidly form self-assembly in aqueous medium upon addition of physiologically relevant concentrations of sodium chloride leading to the formation of hydrogels.172 The reported hydrogels exhibited stable mechanical property, robust shear-thinning behavior and drug release profiles, which demonstrate that these hydrogels could be used as injectable materials for delivering the drug in vivo. The functionalization of amino acids by suitable π-conjugated aromatic chromophores results in the formation of bio-functional materials for bio-sensing and imaging applications. In this perspective, Lin and co-workers developed a series of biocompatible pyrene–amino acid conjugate hydrogelators. They reported that the combination of two different oppositely charged derivatives of pyrene-capped amino acids provided mechanically strong and biocompatible hydrogels.173 Reddy et al. reported pyrene and fluorenylmethoxycarbonyl functionalized L-lysine based supramolecular ambidextrous gels (acting as donor) exhibiting charge transfer (CT) behavior when mixed with an electron acceptor such as 2,4,7-trinitro-9-fluorenone (TNF).174D-Biotin is a vitamin (B7) playing a crucial role in the cell cycle, various biochemical processes, and in metabolic reactions. Shanmugam and co-workers developed biocytin based supramolecular hydrogelator from the amidation product of D-biotin and L-lysine. They reported that the hydrogel has a specific bactericidal activity against E. coli and showed a favorable cell proliferation.175 The improvement in gelation properties by incorporation of metal cations has recently gained immense interest due to their impressive properties and control over self-assembly process by alternating metal–ligand coordination. For example, Basak et al. reported a phenylalanine based amphiphile system 1 (Fig. 20a–c), which formed a hydrogel in a phosphate buffer solution. On addition of metal cations such as Co2+ and Ni2+ to the gel matrix, dramatical enhancement in the thermal and mechanical stability was noticed.176

3.1 Peptide hydrogels

Peptide-based supramolecular gelators form self-assembled fibrillar network obeying 3D percolation model produced through non-covalent interactions and thereafter, the developed structures lead to gel formation by encapsulating solvents.177,178 The nanofibres with 3D network structures of peptides are associated with α-helices, β-sheets, vesicles, micelles, fibers, ribbons, tapes, tubes, and coil-like morphologies.179–181 Peptide based hydrogels are very important due to their biocompatibility and low toxicity, thus innovating different applications in biomedical science. They are prepared by simple heating–cooling, sonication, and by adjusting the pH of the solutions. Besides, the peptide hydrogels are also prepared by adding a suitable salt to the peptide solutions at high pH182,183 and by performing enzymatic reaction on the precursor of the peptide gelator.184 The peptide-based hydrogels can exhibit multi-stimuli responsive behavior in the presence of external stimuli, for e.g., temperature, pH, ions, enzymes, strain, light, redox, and ultrasound, which makes them prominent candidates in different research fields.185,186 The most important features of the self-assembled peptide hydrogels are their non-toxicity and stability under physiological conditions, which make them ideal for a variety of biological187–190 and non-biological applications.191 Recently, short peptide based hydrogels have become very attractive in this respect due to their inherent biocompatibility, tunable bioactivity, structural diversity, and easy and cost-effective synthesis, and could offer a myriad of potential uses in biomedical, biochemical, biotechnological, and electrical applications.192–197 Depending on the peptide linkages (amide bonds), the short peptides can be categorized into dipeptides, tripeptides, tetrapeptides, and so on. The di or tripeptides have advantages due to their low-cost and ease of preparation, relative to longer peptides or more elaborate peptide derivatives. In peptides, as the sequence increases from di- to tri- to oligo- and polypeptides, the number of peptide (–CONH–) linkages increase, thus facilitating the formation of more intermolecular hydrogen bonding in the system. This changes the physical properties of the peptides including the mechanical properties, which find applications in different systems. Also, depending on the nature of amino acids in the peptide, the hydrophobicity and hydrophilicity vary, thus affecting the gelation properties. Thus, drug loading and release behaviors of the peptide hydrogels depend both on the sequence length and chemical nature of the sequence present in the system. Herein, we are going to briefly discuss about the preparation, properties, and uses of short peptides to polypeptide based hydrogels, chronologically.
3.1.1 Dipeptide hydrogels. Dipeptide is the shortest peptide in the class of short peptides. Dipeptide based hydrogels are rapidly gaining popularity due to their low-cost and ease of synthesis through solid phase peptide synthesis by using bio-relevant amino acid residues, and can be used as potential candidates for drug delivery, tissue engineering, cell scaffolding, as well as non-biological applications.198,199 From structural investigations, it has been resolved that the dipeptide-like short peptide hydrogelators typically require presence of additional aromatic groups in order to enhance the gel formation as well as the mechanical properties.200–204 The hydrogels showing thixotropic as well as self-healing properties can be used as injectable materials for sustainable drug carriers to the target site. In this regard, Banerjee and co-workers developed a dipeptide based thixotropic injectable hydrogel by modifying the molecular structure, which exhibited antibacterial activity against Gram negative bacteria Escherichia coli and Pseudomonas aeruginosa.205 The authors also reported that the hydrogel exhibited stability towards proteolytic enzymes present inside the cells and showed negligible toxicity towards human erythrocytes and human fibroblast cells. A class of Amoc (9-anthracenemethoxycarbonyl)-capped dipeptide-based biocompatible, self-healable, and injectable hydrogels has been developed by Das and co-workers.206,207 The gelator molecules (2 and 3) presented in Fig. 21 showed excellent gelation ability due to the presence of suitable aromatic groups responding to π–π stacking and hydrophobic interactions. At pH 7.4, the gelator molecules form self-supported hydrogels, showing antibacterial activity against Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria. They reported that hydrogel 2 showed antibacterial activity towards Gram-positive bacteria at higher gelator concentrations, whereas hydrogel 3 exhibited antibacterial activity against both Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria. At lower gelator 2 concentrations, there is a marginal positive impact on cell viability and proliferation. But gelator 3 at higher concentrations does not exert any toxicity on the cell viability. Ferreira and co-workers reported that dehydrodipeptides containing Naproxen N-capped tryptophan formed hydrogels by peptide self-assembly via hydrogen-bonding and π–π stacking interactions.208 The authors revealed from the fluorescence and Förster resonance energy transfer experiments that the loaded drug in the hydrogel matrix was located in a hydrophobic environment associated with the peptide fibers, indicating that this hydrogel is a good candidate for the drug-nanocarrier. The in situ detection of cellular biomolecules by using peptide based bio-interface has practical significance in cell function, pathology, physiology, and toxicology. Lian et al. constructed a smart bio-interface by using Fmoc-diphenylalanine nanofibrous hydrogel for enzyme-based electrochemical biosensor and cell monitoring applications.209 Again, a 3D culture model-based cellular biosensing system was reported by Lian et al. by immobilizing living cells and enzymes as sensing elements into a dipeptide-derived hydrogel matrix through simple one-pot self-assembly for the detection of superoxide anion (O2˙−).210

3.1.2 Tripeptide hydrogels. Tripeptide is a class of short peptides that may be N-terminal protected211 or N-terminal non-protected tri-amide building blocks that has been recognized as biocompatible soft material212 and has potential uses in biomedical and sensing applications.213 Herein, the contribution of the hydrophobic part such as aromatic or poly-aromatics groups or other π-conjugated systems attached into peptide based molecular building block for efficient gel formation by π–π-stacking interactions is discussed.214 The presence of hydrophobic part in the tripeptide frameworks has a great importance in the gel formation mechanism. Lin and colleagues reported the structural properties’ correlation of synthesized naphthalimide capped tripeptides hydrogelators.215 Weiss and coworkers designed and synthesized a series of Fmoc protected tripeptide based supramolecular systems containing β-amino acid in order to develop proteolytically stable, thermoreversible, and pH-sensitive hydrogels. The authors proposed that the tripeptides form long nanofibrils by self-assembly, which are interconnected, leading to the formation of gels with 3-D network structures. They also reported that the peptide hydrogels exhibited proteolytic stability against proteinase K enzyme in vitro.216 Yang et al. reported that Fmoc protected phenylalanine–phenylalanine–cysteine tripeptide forms micelles through self-assembly at higher pH, leading to the formation of a viscous solution, which spontaneously transforms into a stable self-supporting, thixotropic, printable hydrogel with time.217
3.1.3 Tetrapeptide hydrogels. Like the above short peptides, tetrapeptides also form self-assembled hydrogels with 3-D nanostructure morphologies through non-covalent interactions, which can be widely used in various applications.218–220 Li and co-workers synthesized Nap-protected tetrapeptide hydrogels for ophthalmic drug delivery.221 In this case, a commercial eye drop, diclofenac sodium (DIC), is loaded into the hydrogel network by simple physical mixing and it is rapidly released from the gel matrix within 24 hours. The authors reported from an ocular tolerance test that after a single instillation of the DIC-loaded Nap-GFFY hydrogel, no eye irritation occurs. They also reported that the synthesized Nap-GFFY hydrogel has no toxic effect against different cell lines after incubation for 24 hours. Zhao et al. reported a tetrapeptide based supramolecular hydrogel serving as a novel vaccine delivery system.222 When protein molecules are encapsulated in the hydrogel network, then the mechanical property of the hydrogel is enhanced up to 15-fold and there also occurs a conformational transition from β-sheet to α-helix structure. The resulting protein–peptide hydrogels enhances antigen uptake capacity and moderately promotes dendritic cell (DC) maturation in vitro. Interestingly, this peptide hydrogel can promote antigen specific antibody creation in vivo and splenocyte proliferation ex vivo.
3.1.4 Pentapeptide hydrogels. The construction of functional hydrogel based biomaterials from pentapeptide building blocks by supramolecular approach have attracted great interest in biomedical and chemical fields of applications.223 Lampe and co-workers developed stimuli-responsive pentapeptide based hydrogels with nanofiber network structures for tissue engineering.224 They reported that the mechanical properties of the pentapeptide hydrogels changed due to variations in the peptide sequences, peptide concentration, and pH. The authors also reported that the peptides can form sequence-dependent fibrillar morphologies via self-assembly. The hydrogels also exhibited shear-thinning and self-healing properties induced by the facile formation of β-sheet structures. For the tunable structural and mechanical properties of these supramolecular pentapeptide scaffolds, these hydrogel systems can be made suitable injectable materials for tissue engineering and cell delivery applications. Later on, Lampe and colleagues developed an injectable biomaterial hydrogel based on the pentapeptide scaffold that exhibited shear-thinning and rapid self-healing behavior.225 They reported that the hydrogels possessed nanofiber morphology that can serve as a cell carrier for cell transplantation.
3.1.5 Hexapeptide hydrogels. Hexapeptide is a family member of oligopeptides that form supramolecular hydrogels similar to that of other peptides and have been shown considerable attention in various biomedical applications due to their multifunctionality, biocompatibility, and biodegradability properties.226,227 A hexapeptide based supramolecular self-assembling hydrogel with nanofiber morphology was developed by Qi et al.228 Interestingly, this nanofibrous hydrogel transformed into well-organized nanofibers with an antiparallel β-sheet structure in the presence of chemotherapeutic drugs such as doxorubicin (Dox) (Fig. 22a). The authors found that when the Dox loaded hydrogel was injected at the tumor site of mice, it inhibited the re-growth of the cancer cell at the primary tumor site and reduced distant metastasis. Zhong and coworkers developed a fluorescent hydrogel containing a fluorescent moiety and a hexapeptide unit for accurate and reversible measurement of pH.229

3.1.6 Oligo-peptide hydrogels. Oligo-peptides are short chain peptides produced by connecting more than two and less than twenty peptide linkages. We have already discussed a number of oligopeptides including dipeptides, tripeptides, tetrapeptides, pentapeptides, and hexapeptides; herein, we will briefly discuss the rest of the oligopeptides. Recently, oligopeptide-based materials have gained great interest for a variety of biomedical and technological applications due to their interesting properties such as piezoelectric activity, high optical non-linearity, nanoscale wettability, electrochemical behavior, magnetic susceptibility, and luminescence properties. Like other supramolecular systems, oligopeptide based materials can also form self-assembly through non-covalent interactions and can lead to the formation of gels with 3-D network, showing a variety of nanostructures (including β-sheets, helical, or β-hairpin-based fibrils and nanotubes).230–235 The self-assembly as well as supramolecular architecture based on the peptide fragment is highly dependent on the peptide sequence, which comes from the composition of amino acids.236 It is well known that, for the formation of gels, various non-covalent interactions are required to construct the self-assembled 3-D structure; beyond that, a perfect balance between the hydrophobicity and hydrophilicity has to be maintained for designing oligopeptide based gelators. The hydrogels obtained from oligopeptides can be utilized as nanoparticles, drug and cells carrier, vaccine nodule, catalysts, biosensors, and biomaterials with piezoelectric and hybrid biodegradable materials for bioelectronics.237–241

“Cancer” is one of most traumatic diseases amongst all kinds of known diseases. Till now, researchers have being trying to finding the right way to cure cancer. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), cancer is a serious menace to the health of humans and animals worldwide. Cancer cells are unusually and uncontrollably divided without any limitations and can attack adjacent normal cells and tissues, thus hampering their normal physical activities. Chemotherapy is known as the most effective method for treatment of cancer. Various anti-cancer drugs have been developed and are used specifically for the treatment of cancer by monitoring the type of cancer. Therefore, it is very important to choose the delivery systems for chemotherapeutics agents that can selectively deliver drugs to the cancer sites. From this perspective, a number of different drug carrier systems such as nanoparticles, vesicles, micelles, and nanofibers have been developed. Furthermore, a number of drug carrier systems have been constructed, which can exhibit stimuli responsive behavior in the presence of external stimuli such as pH and temperature. These types of drug carrier systems play an important role for delivering the chemotherapeutic agents to the specific target site of the tumor in the presence of stimuli. In general, the surroundings of the cancer cells site are more acidic (low pH) in comparison to that of the normal cells site. Therefore, pH responsive hydrogels, which form gels at neutral or basic pH but convert into sol at lower pH, play an important role in delivering anti-cancer drugs to the specific site of cancer cells. Karavasili et al. established an oligopeptide hydrogel as a vehicle for simultaneous delivery of Doxorubicin and Curcumin drug for the treatment of head and neck cancer.242 Paclitaxel (PTX) is an important anti-cancer hydrophobic drug that belongs to the taxane family and has been widely used for a variety of cancer treatments. Raza et al. developed a pH responsive octapeptide hydrogel for the carrier of anti-cancer drug such as Paclitaxel.243 The peptide molecules form a stable hydrogel with high drug loading capacity at pH 7.4 (Fig. 22b). The authors found that this peptide hydrogel transforms into a sol and sustainably releases PTX at the tumor site of mice at pH 5.5, and it efficiently enhances tumor inhibition.

MAX8 is an oligopeptide molecule containing twenty amino acid residues, which exhibit self-assembly behavior. The MAX8 molecule is associated with alternative hydrophilic and hydrophobic amino acid residues, which promote β-strand formation. The motif VDPPT is known as an important part of MAX8 and forms β-turn. The β-strands of MAX8 molecule form β-hairpin with the help of β-turn.244 β-Hairpin peptide hydrogels is known as a vehicle for therapeutic drug delivery due to their biocompatible nature.245,246 Langhans and co-workers reported a β-hairpin hydrogel obtained from MAX8 oligopeptide, bearing unique shear thinning properties and it exhibited immediate re-healing after the removal of mechanical forces, making this hydrogel an excellent material for injectable drug delivery vehicle at the localized injury site.247 Pochan and co-workers248 developed a MAX8 β-hairpin peptide hydrogel for delivering a hydrophobic drug vincristine for the purpose of chemotherapy.

The cyclic peptide (CDPs) is a class of oligopeptide, generally formed by head-to-tail intramolecular cyclization of the corresponding linear dipeptide residues.249,250 They are universally found in nature ranging from bacteria to human with different biological functions. One of the most important properties of their structural rigidity is due to cyclic conformation, which significantly enhances the resistance power towards the enzymatic hydrolysis and physiological stability than that of their linear counterparts. CDP derivatives are able to form gels in many organic and aqueous solvents via self-assembly by non-covalent interactions with the help of physical stimuli such as temperature, shear force, and pH, as reported by Govindaraju and co-workers.251 Due to the unique physical and chemical properties, CDPs can be used as important candidates for controlled drug delivery and other biological applications (Fig. 23A and B).

3.1.7 Polypeptide hydrogels. The polypeptide is a long chain peptide motif, containing 20–50 amide linkages. Depending on the sequence of amino acid residues in polypeptides, different types of secondary structures such as α-helix, β-sheet, and random coil are obtained by supramolecular assembly formation.257 These type of secondary structures of polypeptide also exhibit structural transitions in the presence of external stimuli such as light, pH, and temperature.258 Again, the incorporation of external motifs or ligands in polypeptides have also exhibited a structural transition.259 Due to biocompatibility, tunable physical, mechanical, architectural, and multiple functionalities, the polypeptides play a significant role in the construction of supramolecular assembled hydrogels for differential applications.260–265 Wooley and co-workers developed a multi-responsive polypeptide hydrogel for delivering non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs.266 Hartgerink et al. reported the self-assembled nanofibrillar structure of multidomain peptide that transforms it into an anisotropic hydrogel string with modest shear stress by the incorporation of the amino acid DOPA (3,4-dihydroxyphenylalanine).267

The polypeptide hydrogels with similar properties such as extra cellular matrix (ECM) have been widely investigated in cell culture and tissue engineering.268 Arginylglycylaspartic acid (RGD) is a short peptide motif commonly used for cell adhesion to ECM.269 Chen and co-workers developed a novel poly(L-glutamic acid)-based injectable hydrogel fabricated by RGD (Fig. 24A and B).270 They showed that the incorporation of RGD motif in the polypeptide framework increased the cell adhesion and cell proliferation by developing cell–matrix interactions. The authors also reported that the conjugation of RGD with the network by disulfide bonds helped to control the bioactivity of the hydrogel by tuning the RGD concentration. For targeted drug delivery and tumor chemo-photothermal therapy, a hybrid nanogel matrix was developed by Liu and coworkers.271 When gold nanorods were coated with a triblock polypeptide, then a stable biocompatible hydrogel was obtained by self-assembly. Again, to promote cellular uptake on the surface of the hybrid nanogels, the authors introduced arginine–glycine–aspartic acid motif into the polypeptide. Lee and his group developed a series of multi-stimuli responsive bioresorbable block copolymers based polypeptide hydrogels by ring-opening polymerization for controlled delivery of the therapeutic protein.272 Olsen and co-workers reported a homopolypeptide obtained from the polymerization of L-proline and it formed a supramolecular hydrogel with left helical structural morphology.273 Peptoid or poly-N-substituted glycine is a member of the peptomimetic family and is developed for different biomedical applications. In peptoid moieties, nitrogen atoms are engaged by alkyl substitution. Like normal peptides, peptoids do not show secondary structures due to the lack of amide hydrogen. A series of copolypeptiod based thermo-reversible injectable hydrogels were prepared via ring opening polymerization by Zhang and coworkers.274 The hydrogels exhibit less cytotoxic effect on human adipose derived stem cells and also show bioactivity by inducing chondrogenesis of human adipose derived stem cells. The encapsulation of water-soluble enzyme into the hydrogel matrix by sol–gel transition demonstrated its practical utility in tissue engineering and in other biological fields.

3.2 Hydrogels of peptide amphiphiles

Peptide amphiphile is a class of synthetic biomaterials in which hydrophilic and hydrophobic amino acids are arranged in an alternative manner and it has been widely used in biomedical applications.276–279 The peptide amphiphilicity is responsible for the creation of secondary structures, which subsequently self-assemble into 3-D networks with different morphological structures such as fibrils, nanotube, nanofibers, and β-sheets.280–283 The incorporation of acidic, basic, and charged motifs into the peptide amphiphilic residues is carried out in order to enhance the self-assembly formation, followed by the gelation process.284 pH and thermo-responsive anionic hydrogel derived from the acidic amphiphilic scaffold exhibits shear-thinning and thixotropic behavior, reported by Schneider and coworkers.285 Rapaport et al. reported the effect of cationic, anionic, and zwitterionic β-sheet amphiphilic peptides on blood clotting/coagulation.286 The 3-D entangled network of amphiphilic hydrogels, which mimics the extracellular matrix (ECM), have been widely used as potential drug carriers.287,288 Fu et al. reported the three-dimensional networks of nanofibers formed by self-assembly of peptide amphiphiles that can be used as a biomimetic hydrogel scaffold for tissue engineering.289 A series of multi-stimuli responsive injectable peptide amphiphilic hydrogels with β-sheets and nanofibrous network structures were developed by Mehra et al.290 In this case, the authors synthesized hydrogel nanoparticles from amphiphilic peptides, which can deliver anticancer drugs such as 5-fluoro uracil/doxorubicin at physiological conditions. Guler et al. reported a supramolecular nanofibrous injectable hydrogel from the peptide amphiphile, which have the ability to encapsulate and slowly release the anti-cancer (DOX) drug (Fig. 25a–e).291 In this case, the non-cytotoxicity and encapsulation strategy of nanofiber gels increased the activity of the drug molecules on cellular viability. Ballet and co-workers developed a new class of amphipathic peptide hydrogelators consisting of mixed α/β-peptide form transparent self-supporting gels with good shear-thinning behavior after dissolution in water or phosphate buffer.292 The authors also enhanced the proteolytic stability of the mixed α/β-peptide gelators by incorporation of β3-homoamino acid residues for the purpose of tissue engineering and drug delivery applications. Besides the drug delivery ability to the target site, the peptide amphiphilic hydrogels exhibited antibacterial activity against different types of bacteria.293 Wan et al. prepared a series of supramolecular hydrogels from cationic peptide amphiphiles by ring opening polymerization and post-modification strategy.294 Interestingly, by incorporation of lysine residues into peptide amphiphile motif, the resulting hydrogel showed pH responsive behavior and antibacterial activity. The controlled release of H2S gas from the peptide amphiphilic based hydrogel network was reported by Matson and coworkers.295 Roy and coworkers fabricated stimuli responsive amphiphilic peptide hydrogels containing histidine scaffold.296 Herein, the authors observed that the hydrogels exhibit gel to sol transition by incorporation of various metal ions (Cu2+, Ni2+, and Co2+) at physiological pH and thereafter, the resulting peptide–metal ion complex is shifted to the gel phase at lower pH (pH 3). 3.3 Advantages and disadvantages of peptide hydrogels

The advantages of peptide hydrogels are: the hydrogel environment can protect the cells and the drug, and the peptide may be modified with the cell adhesion ligands. The drug loaded gel can be injected into the body as a liquid due to thixotropic properties that may form gels at body temperature/pH and the peptide molecules are usually biocompatible with very low toxicity. The disadvantages of peptide hydrogels are that they are difficult to handle as they are mechanically weak. These gels also find some difficulty in loading drugs and cells, and then to crosslink in vitro as a prefabricated matrix.

So, mechanically strong peptide hydrogels with thixotropic properties can be an agreeable solution and it can be made by adding nanofillers or by using long chain peptides where there would be a large number of H-bonding interactions with the –CONH– groups. The incorporation of aromatic moieties in the peptide chain may improve the mechanical properties due to π-stacking interaction; however, care should be taken to keep the toxicity low and the final hydrogel should be biocompatible.

Further, employing synthetic end-capping groups (e.g., naphthalene, fluorenylmethyloxycarbonyl, anthracene, etc.) would play a prominent role in improving the mechanical and gelation properties of the small peptides. Peptide based hybrid, co-assembled, metal coordinated hydrogels also remedy the above difficulties and we will discuss it in the next sections.

4. Protein hydrogels

Proteins are bio-macromolecules composed of one or more polypeptide residues, which are essential building blocks of all living organisms. Each and every protein residue has a unique feature together with a new functional property and the most of the proteins available in the environment are synthesized by nature itself. Nowadays, a variety of artificial proteins are being prepared synthetically by varying the amino acid sequence in the long chains of peptide for different biomedical applications. Recent research greatly focuses on the development of biocompatible and biodegradable protein hydrogels because of their increasing significance in the field of biomaterials for their biomedical applications in therapeutic delivery and tissue engineering.372–382 The physically crosslinked networks are developed by protein domains, which lead to stimuli responsive behavior of protein-hydrogels383,384 in the presence of external stimuli such as, pH, redox, light, and enzyme, which has drawn considerable attention in research fields in recent times.

Nature-derived protein hydrogels (extracted from nature) have been widely studied in the chemical and biochemical fields.385–387 Soy protein isolate (SPI), the most abundant nature-derived plant protein, can form a hydrogel and is widely used as a promising material for biotechnological and biomedical applications. Dutta and co-workers established the structure–property relationship of photo-cross-linked SPI and SPI/silk fibroin (SF) hybrid hydrogels.388 Liu et al. fabricated a protein composite hydrogel obtained from a functional component polyethyleneimine (PEI) and soy protein isolate (SPI) for the selective removal of copper ion from aqueous solution.389

A shear-thinning injectable protein hydrogel with excellent encapsulation and long-term delivery efficacy was developed by Huang and coworkers for cell transplantation. The prepared hydrogel was able to protect from cell membrane damage during syringe flow and its crosslinking network was formed in situ, providing a reinforcing network to improve cell retention.390 Fibroin is a nature-derived insoluble protein, available in silk extracted from cocoons of Bombyx mori and other moths, having potential uses in biocompatible and biodegradable hydrogels for biomedical applications.391 Tannic acid is a biocompatible natural plant-derived polyphenol molecule and has antibacterial, antioxidative, and anti-inflammatory activities. Li and coworkers developed a multifunctional hybrid hydrogel from silk fibroin protein and tannic acid.392 They reported that the protein–hybrid hydrogel was produced from hydrogen bonding, hydrophobic interactions, and π–π stacking interactions. The resulting hydrogel exhibited a number of advantageous properties such as shear thinning, thixotropic, and good cell adhesive properties. More importantly, due to functionalization with tannic acid, the hybrid hydrogel scaffolds showed antibacterial activity towards both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria in vitro. Highly biocompatible and injectable hybrid hydrogels with inherent antibacterial and antioxidant activity are also beneficial for improving wound healing. Cha and colleagues developed a nature-derived, mechanically stable, silk-like protein hydrogel extracted from sea anemone. The authors reported that this protein hydrogel showed adequate swelling ability and microstructure, which are useful for facilitating mass transport and cell proliferation.393

Hydrogels made from coiled-coil proteins are reversible; they can be disassembled and reassembled rapidly by external stimuli such as temperature or mechanical stress. Rapp et al. reported the use of fluorescence recovery after photobleaching (FRAP) to characterize the interplay between strand exchange and chain mobility in associative protein hydrogels.394 The hydrogels were obtained from an engineered triblock protein (designated “PEP”) having two identical coiled-coil domains (“P”) at the C- and N- termini, flanking a water-soluble midblock (“E”) having elastin-like polypeptide repeats (Fig. 40A and B). The P domain is obtained from the N-terminal fragment of rat cartilage oligomeric matrix protein (COMP), which produces homopentameric coiled-coil bundles. The reversible assemblies of PEP produced from the association of P domains are converted into optically transparent, physically cross-linked networks. The viscoelastic nature of PEP networks is similar to that of other assembled networks due to the association of coiled-coil domains. Herein, the authors used FRAP for determining the diffusion coefficients and equilibrium binding constants of fluorescently labeled PEP chains present in the PEP hydrogels. They observed that the reversible network association reduced the mobility of PEP chains significantly. They also demonstrated that the mobility of the tracer chain was highly sensitive to structural changes in the coiled-coil end blocks.

4.1 Protein based polymer hydrogels

Proteins have also been conjugated to larger synthetic polymeric backbones in order to create a wider range of supramolecular hybrid materials and nanostructures, including protein–polymer hybrid hydrogels, fibrils, spherulities, and micells. PEG is an attractive component in hydrogels and has been extensively investigated for 3D cell culture, and is widely used in large number of clinical and pharmaceutical applications.395 Keratin is a family of natural fibrous protein mainly found in wool, hair, horn, nail, hooves of mammals, and bird feathers. Keratin comprises peptide sequences and secondary structures that interact with the surface of the cell receptors to support cell adhesion, differentiation, and maturation. Jabbari and co-workers reported an injectable and photo-polymerizable hydrogel from keratin, extracted from poultry feather, for the encapsulation and delivery of stem cells in tissue regeneration.396

5. Summary and scope of future work

So far, we have portrayed a broad picture on recent advances on polymer and peptide hydrogels developed in the past few years. The progress in polymer hydrogels is discussed by classifying them into three categories, for e.g., (i) cross-linking polymer hydrogel, (ii) conducting polymer hydrogel and (iii) natural polymer hydrogel. The crosslinking polymer hydrogels are discussed in the subheading of chemically cross-linked polymer hydrogel, formed by covalent crosslinking; physically cross-linked polymer hydrogel formed by non-covalent interactions and hybrid double network polymer hydrogels, where the hybrid gels, are formed both by chemical and physical crosslinking processes among the components. All these types of gels have different applications both from the biomedical and technological viewpoints such as shape memory, 3D printing, and self-healing. The conducting gels are classified mainly as ion and electronic conducting gels and the recent progress in the gels are summarized with concrete examples of different classes of conducting gels, extending their applications in ionic skin, energy harvester on different stimuli, artificial skin sensor, blood pressure sensor, photovoltaics, supercapacitor, Li+ ion battery, polymer robotics, etc. In natural polymer hydrogels, mainly the gelation behavior of various carbohydrate polymers, for e.g., cellulose and chitosan, in the presence of different solvents, ions, nanoparticles, etc., are discussed, extending their applications in targeted drug delivery, tissue engineering, cartilage regeneration, cell adhesion, etc.

In peptide hydrogels, we have discussed the recent advances in the gelation properties of different peptides, for e.g., dipeptide, tri-, tetra-, penta-, hexa-, oligo, and polypeptides. Apart from their preparation procedures, the properties, their uses in biomedical fields, for e.g., drug delivery, tissue engineering, biosensors, protein separation, and wound healing are delineated very well. In peptide-based hybrid hydrogels, in the presence of polymer, oligonucleotide, amino acids, fullerene, metallic nanoparticles, etc., are discussed, showing applications in drug delivery, photoacoustic imaging, catalyst, photodynamic therapy, etc. In the co-assembled hybrid hydrogels having two different peptides, fluorophores are tagged with the peptide, extending their uses in cell imaging, bone tissue engineering, etc. Due to the good biocompatibility of peptides, different drugs are loaded by covalent/noncovalent bonding and released at the targeted area by different stimuli. The peptide hydrogels are used for the detection of enzymes in biological fluids including blood and cell lysates. Thus, in short, the peptide hydrogels have found enormous practical applications in biomedical field.

While there is already a substantial progress in polymer gels, there is still enough room for future developments. In polymer gels, new research is required to develop polymer solar cells with high efficiency using different nanoparticles as acceptor and polymers as donor, vis-a-vis, efficient polymeric supercapacitor of xerogels derived from the hydrogels as in the hydrogel, intimate mixing of the components takes place. Hybrid polymer hydrogels may be produced with two different polymers using in situ polymerization techniques, which can generate new properties of the hydrogels that are not present in single polymer hydrogels, with an expectation of high mechanical and stimuli-responsive property. In polymer hydrogels, LMWGs can be used to act as concomitant gelators, importing both stimuli-responsiveness with good mechanical property. These hydrogels could act as suitable candidates for biomedical and electronic applications. Different carbon nanomaterials, e.g., carbon nanotubes, carbon dots, graphene, etc., can be introduced into the polymer gels to enhance their mechanical and optoelectronic properties as the above nano-materials can reinforce the properties of the polymer hydrogels for large surface area and synergistic interaction between the components. The conductivity and capacitance properties of such hybrid systems are expected to increase significantly. Besides, the hybrid hydrogels can be used for drug delivery and also for cell imaging and tissue engineering applications. Also, the applications of the conducting hybrid hydrogels can be extended to produce artificial skin, soft robotics, tissue generation, 3D printing, etc.

In peptide hydrogels, there is immense scope for future work as they have low toxicity and biocompatibility. Special attention can be given to peptide-based hybrid hydrogels. The mixing of different water-soluble polymers with the peptide hydrogels would not only improve the mechanical properties but also enhance the stimuli responsivity, optoelectronic property, etc. Recently, there have been many newly discovered water-soluble conducting polymers and making hybrid hydrogels of these polymers with the peptide hydrogels would impart new optoelectronic properties, which would be suitable for monitoring different biological processes. Apart from mixing with the polymer, metallic and carbon nanoparticles can be mixed to produce hybrid peptide gels having synergistic properties. The optical property of the nanoparticles together with the biocompatibility of peptides can be used in practical applications, particularly for cell imaging, tissue engineering, and cell growth apart from drug delivery applications. As far as our intuitions are concerned, hybrid polymer or hybrid peptide gels would certainly become the most important area of material science, promising its use as soft materials in both biomedical and technological applications.
