Abstract

Graphdiyne (GDY), which possess sp- and sp2-hybridized carbon and Dirac cones, offers unique physical and chemical properties, including an adjustable intrinsic bandgap, excellent charge carrier transfer efficiency, and superior conductivity compared to other carbon allotropes. These exceptional qualities of GDY and its derivatives have been successfully used in a variety of fields, including catalysis, energy, environmental protection, and biological applications. Herein, we focus on the potential application of GDY and its derivatives in the biomedical domain, including biosensing, biological protection, cancer therapy, and antibacterial agents, demonstrating how the biomimetic behavior of these materials can be a step forward in bridging the gap between nature and applications. Considering the excellent biocompatibility, solubility and selectivity of GDY and its derived materials, they have shown great potential as biosensing and bio-imaging materials. The unusual combination of properties in GDY has been used in biological applications such as OFFON DNA detection and enzymatic sensing, where GDY has a greater adsorption capacity than graphene and other 2D materials, resulting in increased sensitivity. GDY and its derivatives have also been used in cancer treatment due to their high doxorubicin (DOX) loading capacity (using-stacking) and photothermal conversion ability, and radiation protection since their initial biological use. The poor biodegradation rate of graphene demands the search for new nanomaterials. Accordingly, GDY has better biocompatibility and bio-safety than other 2D nanomaterials, especially graphene and its oxide, due to its absence of aggregation in the physiological environment. Thus, GDY-based nanomaterials have become promising candidates as bio-delivery carriers. Besides, GDY and GDY-based materials have also shown interesting applications in the fields of cell-culture, cell-growth and tissue engineering. Herein, we present a comprehensive review on the applications of GDY and its derivatives as biomedical materials, followed by their future perspectives. This review will provide an outlook for the application of graphene and its derivatives and may open up new horizons to inspire broader interests across various disciplines. Finally, the future prospects for GDY-based materials are examined for their potential biological use.

1. Introduction

Carbon and its compounds are some of the most ubiquitous elements on Earth, which are found in both minerals and organic substances.16 Carbon, being a unique element with four valence electrons, has a variety of methods to create carboncarbon bonds, allowing it to form a variety of allotropes with various structures.1,720 There are three hybrid states for carbon atoms, i.e., sp, sp2, and sp3. Carbon networks with sp2- or sp3-hybridized carbon atoms make up natural and manufactured carbon compounds. The benefit of sp-hybridized carbon atoms with a triple bond is that they have a linear and conjugated structure; hence, they can form new carbon allotropes.7,9,11,13,16,2133 In the last decade, numerous carbon-based nanomaterials, including zero-dimensional (0D), i.e., fullerene,34 one-dimensional (1D), i.e., carbon nanotubes,17 and two-dimensional (2D), i.e., graphene7,35 have been investigated due to the advances in science and technology (Fig. 1(a)). Therefore, there has been a significant increase in the number of reports published on carbon-based materials for different applications, e.g., small molecule delivery, gene therapy, chemotherapy, phototherapy, biosensing, and regenerative medicine.17

Currently, the most important members of the carbon family in the nanotechnological world are in the 2D form, especially since the discovery of graphene.32 Recently, 2D nanomaterials have been discovered to have unique functional features, which are related to their shape, surface area to mass ratio, and improved physicochemical qualities (electrochemical, mechanical, and physical properties).79,14,17,31,34,35,3742 Therefore, 2D materials have attracted significant attention for their potential use, ranging from basic to present/future nanotechnology applications,42i.e., electronics, energy storage/conversion, electro(opto-)-catalysis,43 and biological application (i.e., biosensors, chemotherapy, radiation protection, and germicidal agent).17,34,44

Over the years, researchers have been focused on the search for new carbon allotrope materials considering the enormous application potential of 2D all-carbon materials. Regarding carbon-based 2D materials, Baughman et al.45 presented a planar carbon material including sp2- and sp-hybridized carbon atoms in 1987. Later, in 2004, the discovery of a material combining the features of 2D materials with carbon compounds, namely graphene, was reported, which has attracted significant nano-technological interest on both an industrial and laboratory level.31 Further study was performed on the members of the graphene family, such as graphyne, graphdiyne (GDY), graphone, and graphene, which have recently been proposed as novel 2D graphene derivatives. Graphene has been employed in biomedical applications such as biosensors, bioimaging, antimicrobial agents, cancer treatment, biological protection, antibacterial agents and tissue engineering; however, there are some limitations in its application.46 Therefore carbon-based 2D materials, e.g., graphene, have been functionalized via chemical and physical processes to modify the characteristics of graphene to achieve novel industrial applications. The effect of functionalization of the size, shape, band gap, doping level, chemical functional groups, etc. on graphene has been studied, but the required properties for particular applications were not achieved.34 Therefore, a new carbon derivative, GDY, was further discovered and studied regarding its science and application. GDY, an allotrope of graphene with a honeycomb structure, is a 2D carbon allotrope.

GDY was discovered by Li et al. in 2010 using hexa-ethynyl-benzene in a homo-coupling process.47 GDY is a graphene derivative that has been hydrogenated. Cross-coupling on copper foil was used to create GDY, a novel 2D graphitic carbon material.48 GDY is the first synthetic carbon-based nanomaterial composed of benzene rings with sp2-hybridized carbon atoms and acetenyl groups with sp-hybridized carbon atoms. GDY is made up of benzene rings and carboncarbon triple bonds, together with sp- and sp2-hybridized carbon allotrope assembly layers, which form its large-conjugation architecture. The uniformly distributed pores, massive conjugation, and rich surface chemistry of the GDY are due to its linear sp-carbon atoms and planar sp2-carbon atoms (Fig. 1(b)). GDY has showed tremendous potential as a photoelectric material in nano-electrics, catalysis, and energy storage since its initial synthesis. GDY has been reported to have potential use in a variety of fields, including energy,44 catalysis,49 and the environment, due to its unusual features.50 Furthermore, GDY and its derivatives have been used in biosensing, bioimaging, chemotherapy, and radiation protection since its initial biological use in 2016.51

Recent needs in biomedical applications have been addressed thanks to the rapid growth of nanotechnology, which is an efficient solution to unresolved difficulties. Regarding the basic biological reactions of the superior 2D nanomaterials, a comprehensive assessment in terms of toxicity.17,35,5263 In fact, for biological applications such as drug administration, PTT, biomarker monitoring, and tissue engineering, graphene has been investigated as a nanomaterial.64 However, due to the poor biodegradation rate of graphene, transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs), carbon nitride, boron nitride, and black phosphorous (BP) have been used for biological applications instead.65 Furthermore, preliminary bio-safety evaluations suggest that these new materials are less hazardous than graphene.66 Consequently, the carbon allotrope GDY was investigated for numerous biological applications. Although, GDY is similar to graphene, more cautious and rigorous research is needed to determine its potential for biomedical applications. GDY-based 2D nanomaterials outperform other carbon-based materials in many biological applications in terms of activity and durability. The unusual combination of properties in GDY has been used in biological applications such as OFFON DNA detection and enzymatic glucose sensing, where GDY has a greater adsorption capacity than graphene and other 2D materials, resulting in increased sensitivity.34 Furthermore, GDY-based materials have been manufactured and used in numerous property-related biomedical applications due to the outstanding success of graphene. For cancer therapy, GDY also has a high doxorubicin (DOX) loading capacity (via stacking) and photo-thermal conversion ability. Clearly, while designing GDY and its derivatives for drug delivery, tissue engineering, and bio-molecular sensing, its long-term negative health effects must be considered (Fig. 2).

2. Toxicity and biocompatibility of GDY and GDY-based materials

To optimize the biological applications of GDY with the lowest hazards to the environment and human safety, complete material characterization and mechanical toxic impact studies are required for its safe design and manufacture. Due to the advantage of having a high degree of tunability, GDY and its derivatives are envisioned to be feasible to achieve the required features for particular applications.7,67 However, there are no reports on the use of GDY and its derivatives as antibacterial agents to date. In general, the theranostic use of any nanomaterial requires extreme caution to ensure that its toxicities are thoroughly investigated. Currently, GDY is being researched for use in drug administration, as a material for the fabrication of biosensors, and as a possible antibacterial agent. The toxicity and biocompatibility of GDY have attracted increasing interest in its future applications given that the number of biomedical uses of GDY has increased. However, only a few studies have investigated the toxicity of GDY in vitro and in vivo. Unfortunately, there are even less investigations on GDY and its composites in the literature. Considering that GDY materials have a large surface area, they can be predicted to be effective sorbents for a range of small molecule solutes in physiological fluids. In the case of molecules with poor solubility, partial hydrophobicity, or positive charge (for the usual case of negatively charged GDY-based materials), molecules with conjugated bonds, which impart planarity and allow interactions with graphenic carbon surfaces, are preferable for adsorption on carbon surfaces. Thus, this may result in micronutrient deficiency as one of the biological repercussions of GDY-based materials. Consequently, we sought to summarize the knowledge on the toxicity of GDY to provide basic knowledge on the biocompatibility of GDY-based materials. GDY-oxide (GDYO) and GO, which have comparable sizes and shapes, were examined in terms of physicochemical characteristics and cytotoxicity by Zheng et al.68 In H2O, GO was quite stable, but in saline solution, it rapidly aggregated. Conversely, GDY oxide (GDYO) was substantially more stable in H2O and other saline solutions, which may be due to its intermolecular hydrogen bonding and acetylenic bonds linking carboxyl groups in the GDYO. The findings revealed that GO prefers to stick on the cell membrane, causing membrane ruffles and the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), which eventually led to cytotoxicity and death. In contrast, GDYO no effect on cell viability and produced less apoptosis. When utilized in a bio-system, GDYO appears to be safer and more biocompatible than GO. GDYO has been shown to produce in vivo mutagenesis in mice when administered intravenously at a dosage of 4 mg kg1 day1 for 5 days. The toxicity and biocompatibility of modified GDY nanoparticles (NPs) have been examined in several studies. When the concentration of GDY was less than 100 g mL169 or 40 g mL1, the cells treated with PEG- or BSA-modified GDY maintained more than 90% viability.70 After GDY was given intravenously at a dosage of 10 mg GDY per kg body weight, the main tissues of the mice exhibited no evident damage. Meanwhile, the adsorbed proteins on the GDY sheets exhibited minimal structural alterations at the molecular level, which may most likely be related to the heterogeneous energy distribution on the surface of GDY, which is made up of different types of carbon atoms. The unique shape of GDY causes proteins to be adsorbed point-to-point on its surface rather than face-to-face, which has a significant impact on the structure of proteins.

Compared to other 2D materials, recent findings show that GDY has bio-safety advantages. However, computer modeling projected that membrane insertion and phospholipid extraction cause cytotoxicity in graphyne, a carbon-based polymer of the GDY family with just one carbon triple bond between nearby benzene rings.44 This finding highlights the potential cytotoxicity of GDY and its family. Consequently, numerous type of toxicity and the bio-compatibility of GDY, such as its bio-distribution, hemolysis effect, cell membrane interface and protein interaction, should be further researched. GDY, like other pure carbon-based compounds, is difficult to track in vivo, although this may be overcome utilizing a variety of approaches.71 However, the potential of GDY to disrupt membranes makes it likely a candidate for antibacterial action. The above-mentioned findings highlight the critical importance of the surface physicochemical properties of the GDY and GDY-based materials in their interactions with bio-molecules and cells (Fig. 3). This review will provide an in-depth overview of GDY, while also encouraging the development and implementation of this innovative carbon material.72

3. Biomedical applications of GDY and GDY-based materials

Considering its numerous unique properties, GDY materials have recently attracted significant attention and have been used as highly sensitive and selective platform for the detection of a variety of targets. Consequently, the following sections highlight the application of GDY-based sensors in a variety of fields.

3.1. Biosensors

Targeted biomarkers necessitate the development of highly selective and sensitive biosensors in the biomedical field due to their extremely low concentrations in clinical blood samples. Biosensors are devices that provide a quantifiable signal to detect biological substances and events. Biosensors may be used to detect or sense bio-molecules, which are significant for healthcare, environmental, and security applications. A chemical sensor is a device that turns information about the presence of a chemical species into an analytically usable signal, either quantitatively or semi-quantitatively. Sensors usually include two structural elements, namely, a receptor and a transducer. Any organic or inorganic substance with a particular interaction with one analyte or a collection of analytes can be used as a receptor. A bio-molecule serves as the recognition element in biosensors. The transducer, which turns chemical information into a quantifiable signal, is the second critical component of the sensing platform. GDY-based biosensors are covered in this section (for example, field-effect transistors, electrochemical, fluorescent, and colorimetric biosensors, and gas sensors).
3.1.1. GDY for the detection of nucleic acid. 2D materials have been employed as deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) sensors based on the distinct interaction forces between 2D materials and single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) or double-stranded DNA (dsDNA). This is due to their high surface area and great adsorption capabilities.46,65,73 These 2D materials are capable of the sensitive detection of DNA because of their excellent fluorescence quenching of dye-labeled ssDNA through the fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) process. GDY was originally employed as a DNA sensor based on a similar idea in 2016 given that it is a unique 2D carbon-based material with a large-conjugated surface.51 The probe employed was a 5-carboxyfluorescein (FAM)-labeled ssDNA, which was engineered to be complementary to the target DNA. The fluorescence intensity of FAM was effectively muted (by around 97%) when GDY or its oxide was added to the probe ssDNA-containing solution owing to the FRET caused by the strong contacts between GDY and ssDNA. The probe ssDNA was hybridized with the target DNA, forming dsDNA when the target ssDNA was introduced in the mixture, reducing its interaction with GDY. Consequently, the fluorescence of FAM was restored and could be identified. This GDY-based sensor is more sensitive and has a lower limit of detection (LOD) than other carbon-based sensors. Recently, GDY nanosheets (NSs) have been used to successfully perform the multiplexed real-time fluorescence detection of DNA.74 The GDY-based sensor was shown to be more sensitive (with an LOD of 251012 m) and faster (nearly immediate response) than existing carbon-based and other 2D materials. Furthermore, simulation calculations were performed to better understand the mechanism of the superior performance of the GDY-based DNA sensor compared to graphene. The benefits of GDY for the detection of DNA, such as the greater adsorption energy of FAM on GDY and its greater electron-accepting capacity are mostly owing to the existence of triple bonds in GDY according to the computational results.

MicroRNA is commonly deregulated in numerous forms of human malignancies. Thus, the ultra-sensitive and affordable detection of microRNA is of tremendous value for cancer diagnosis and treatment. Using the inherent reducibility of GDY, (NH4)2MoS4 was reduced to MoS2, generating MoS2GDY complex, which is photoactive material, according to Xiaohua Li et al.75 The broad band-gap MoS2 and the narrow band-gap GDY were combined in MoS2GDY. The inherently narrow band-gap of GDY increased the light absorption due to the above-mentioned combination.

Moreover, MoS2 decreased the electronhole recombination, resulting in a robust photo-electrochemical (PEC) signal. The mass ratio of GDY to (NH4)2MoS4 affected the PEC signal of MoS2GDY. At a ratio of 1:25 MoS2GDY, the best PEC performance was observed. Subsequently, a PEC assay for the detection of microRNA-141 was fabricated using MoS2GDY with a mass ratio of 1:25. A dual-modified probe linking bio-barcode Au-NPs and the hybridization chain reaction technique was created to achieve reduced background noise and a strong PEC signal. On one side, the probe was tagged with MoS2GDY, while on the other, it was labeled with biotin. The intensity of the PEC was also influenced by the microRNA-141 concentration. The PEC assay had a linear range of 1.0 1017 mol L1 to 1.0 109 mol L1 and a low LOD of 3.3 1018 mol L1 under ideal conditions (i.e., 0.2 V and pH 7.5). This assay was used to determine microRNA-141 in complicated biological matrices with great effectiveness (Fig. 4). Jing Xiao et al.76 used surface chemistry to modify the adsorption affinity of ssDNA and dsDNA on Tremella-like GDY. The effectiveness of GDY to extinguish fluorescence at various oxidation levels was compared. The nonspecific displacement of DNA probes on GDY was investigated. GDY with a low oxidation degree had a higher adsorption affinity and capacity for both ssDNA and dsDNA at the same concentrations than GDY with a high oxidation degree. Furthermore, the DNA adsorbed on the low-oxidized GDY was more resistant to displacement by other DNAs. Protein has a significant contact with various GDY and was capable of dislodging the DNA probes from GDY. Based on these findings, an optimal GDY with the right oxidation degree was used as a scavenger of redundant ssDNA fluorescent probe in an enzyme-assisted amplification system for the sensitive detection of ochratoxin (OTA), demonstrating its strong surface affinity for ssDNA and low affinity for dsDNA. These results show good GDY-mediated DNA adsorption with fluorescence sensing in a complex system using a logical method (Fig. 5).

Fan Chang et al.78 reported a simple method for the controllable synthesis of a novel few layered 2D GDY NS with a thickness of 0.9 nm via an electrochemical lithium intercalation strategy, which had a prominent fluorescence quenching effect. The precise and early detection of Mycobacterium tuberculosis is of great significance for clinical diagnosis and cure of tuberculosis. Accordingly, the few-layered GDY NS with strong adsorptivity for ssDNA as a fluorescent sensing platform has excellent specificity, multiplicity, and very high sensitivity (LOD as low as 25 pM). Thus, this sensing platform can be further applied for the detection of Mycobacterium tuberculosis in clinical samples and the recognition of drug-resistant mutants with a low signal to noise ratio.

3.1.2. GDY for the detection of small molecules. Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) is a key mediator in many biological processes. Thus, its detection is critical for physiological system monitoring. The electrochemical method79 and color method80 are examples of the traditional methods for the detection of H2O2. For the first time, Zhuang et al.81 employed GDYO as a substrate for the in situ production of Prussian-Blue (PB) nanoparticles, which were utilized to detect H2O2. After 50 continuous cycles of cyclic voltammetry (CV), the PB/GDYO composite showed substantially greater stability than PB on GO. PB/GDYO had sensitivity to H2O2 roughly 3.3-times that of PB/GO. The heterogeneous electron distribution is thought to play a role in the stability of PB throughout the electrochemical cycles. Another important metric for evaluating the human body is glucose concentration, which must be measured quantitatively in the case of disorders such as diabetes mellitus. Enzyme-based color approaches,82 fluorescence approaches,83 and electrochemical detection are the most extensively used methods for glucose measurement.84 In a comprehensive glucose detection procedure, traditional enzymatic techniques involve two phases. The first involves the generation of H2O2, where glucose is first oxidized by oxygen with the aid of glucose oxidases. Secondly, with the aid of peroxidase, a chromogenic agent is oxidized by H2O2 to produce a visible and observable color response. Therefore, a GDY-based glucose sensor was created, which merged these two procedures into a single-step glucose detection process.85 This glucose sensor underwent two stages of development. Initially, ferrous ions (Fe2+) were adsorbed on GDY sheets to create an FeGDY composite. The enhanced peroxidase activity of FeGDY was attributed to the catalytic activity of the adsorbed Fe2+ ions. To make the final product, glucose oxidase was adsorbed on the FeGDY. The composite was created by combining the peroxidase activity of Fe2+ with the enzymatic activity of glucose oxidase, which could be utilized to detect glucose quickly. Three components of the function of GDY were examined in this study. Firstly, compared to graphene, GDY has a higher adsorption capacity for Fe2+, which was supported by both experimental data and computational simulation. Secondly, GDY preserved the adsorbed Fe2+ in its original form, which may have been easily oxidized by dissolved oxygen without protection. Thirdly, the secondary structure of the adsorbed protein was unaffected by GDY. Specifically, for the adsorption of enzymes or proteins, the heterogeneous electron and energy distribution and pore structure of GDY likely caused the protein to have point contact rather than entire contact on its surface. The acetylenic linkages, which can bind strongly with metal ions and have a low reduction potential, are most likely responsible for the benefits of GDY. However, to date, GDY has not been reported as a platform for immobilizing metal ions or enzymes. By immobilizing ferrous ions and glucose oxidase on GDY sheets, Jiaming Liu et al.86 reported a GDY-based composite having dual-enzyme activity. GDY had a high adsorption capacity and maintained the strong catalytic activity of the ferrous ions. Compared to graphene, the ferrous ions chose to bind between the surrounding two CC triple bonds of GDY with a lower adsorption energy (5.64 eV and 1.69 eV, respectively). Meanwhile, GDY could adsorb glucose oxidase while having no discernible effect on the structure or catalytic function of the enzyme. The as-prepared composite was successful in detecting blood glucose in a single step. This research sheds light on the immobilization of ions and enzymes by 2D materials. Lingxia Wu et al.87 investigated as-prepared GDY as a very appealing matrix for the immobilization of tyrosinase (a model enzyme) to develop a mediator-free GDY-based biosensor for the fast detection of bisphenol (Fig. 6). The GDY-based tyrosinase biosensor exhibited a linear response in the range of 1.0 107 to 3.5 106 mol L1, with a high sensitivity of 2990.8 mA cm2 M1 and a low detection limit of 24 nmol L1. The reported GDY-based tyrosinase biosensor outperformed the CNT- and graphene-based biosensors in terms of analytical performance for the detection of BPA. The remarkable performance of this biosensor can be attributed to the strong chemical interactions between GDY and BPA, which can enrich the available BPA concentration on the electrode for reaction with tyrosinase. The reliable GDY-based tyrosinase biosensor was successfully employed to detect BPA in drinking bottles and tap water. GDY has been shown to be a potent electrochemical enzyme biosensor platform for the immobilization of bio-molecules (as recognition elements) and biosensor manufacturing, and it has a wide range of applications in biomedical detection and environmental analysis.

Solid-state ion-selective electrodes (ISEs) are powerful tools for monitoring the dynamic change of ions at physiological concentration ranges. However, the unintentional accumulation of an aqueous layer at the ion selective membrane/solid contact interface compromises the potential stability of ISEs, limiting their in vivo application. Lijun Zhao et al.88 used model solid contacts and ISEs such as MnO2 and potassium ISE (K+-ISE), respectively, to show for the first time that GDYO can improve the potential stability of solid contact-based ISEs. These findings implied that the intrinsic structural and hydrophobic properties of GDYO are important in preventing and stabilizing the establishment of a water layer. The K+-ISE, which used GDYOMnO2 as the solid contact, had high short-term potential stability and selectivity, allowing reliable K+ sensing at the animal level. The GDYO-based technique may be used for a variety of the ISEs and does not require any complex processing steps, opening up new possibilities for in vivo ion detection and sensing. Qiang Bai et al.89 investigated a unique sensing technique based on the synergistic impact of an inner filter and photo-induced electron transfer. They developed a new nano-sensor based on a newly synthesized nanomaterial and exhibited sensitive and selective detection of both Fe3+ ions and AA in real clinical samples. For the first time, fluorescent GDYO-QDs were synthesized using a simple ultrasonic approach and characterized using a variety of methods, revealing a strong blue-green emission with a quantum yield of 14.6%. Fe3+ effectively quenches the emission, which is then recovered by AA. Based on this unique characteristic, we created off/on fluorescent nanosensors. These nanosensors could detect Fe3+ at concentrations as low as 95 nmol L1 and has a dynamic range of 0.25 to 200 mol L1. In the range of 10500 mol L1, the LOD of AA was 2.5 mol L1. 3.2. GDY as humidity/damp sensors

Humidity is a critical indication in the biomedical field regarding the medical environment and respiratory monitoring. Carbon-based compounds outperform other materials as humidity sensors due to their hydrophobic structure and the water-adsorbing capability of their hydrophilic functional groups.90 The humidity sensing in carbon-based materials is based on measuring the change in conductivity caused by water adsorption, which is attributed to the hydrogen bonds generated between water and hydrophilic functional groups on the carbon-based materials. Due to its distinctive 2D flakes, GO has been shown to have a quick response time to humidity.91,92 Recently. GDYO has been proven to work effectively for ultrafast humidity sensing.93 GDYO had an ultrafast humidity response with an extraordinary response speed (7 ms), which was three-times faster than that of GO with the same thickness and O/C ratio, according to Hailong Yan et al.87 For the first time, they evaluated the rapid humidity response of GDYO, which is thought to be due to its unusual carbon hybridization, featuring acetylenic bonds with stronger electron-withdrawing capability than ethylenic bonds in GO, resulting in a faster water binding rate. This unique structure-based trait enables the development of novel GDO-based humidity sensors with ultrafast response times, great selectivity against other gas molecules, and high sensitivity. These characteristics substantiate the ability of the sensor to properly track changes in human and hypoxic rat respiration rates. This study not only exhibits the unrivaled humidity sensing capabilities of GDYO, but it also sheds light on the structureproperty relationship of 2D carbon materials. In a humid environment, GDYO was shown to be more responsive than GO, with a bigger change in conductivity and a shorter reaction time (12 ms for the GDYO and 34 ms for GO). A pH titration technique was used to test the acid dissociation constant (pKa) of GO and GDYO to better understand the underlying cause for the superior performance of GDYO. GDYO has a lower pKa value for each functional group than GO, resulting in improved water solubility and a lower water saturation vapor pressure. The hydrophilic functional groups of GDYO (e.g., phenylpropiolic acid or Ph-C-C-COOH) formed hydrogen bonds with water much easier than that of GO (e.g., benzoic acid or Ph-COOH) due to the high electron-withdrawing characteristics of the sp-hybridized carbon atoms in GDY. Thus, GDYO was shown to be practical for sensitive respiratory monitoring based on these good features.

3.3. GDY as ROS for biotherapeutics

The production of mitochondrial ROS occurs both in physiological and pathological conditions. The bimodal actions of ROS have both beneficial and negative effects. The beneficial aspects of ROS are related to their effects on the redox state of cells and the important role that some ROS play in signaling cascades. The negative effects of ROS are related their excess chemical moieties, which are caused by the excessive production or insufficient actions of endogenous antioxidants. There are three major modes of action for antioxidants as follows: (i) antioxidants that directly scavenge ROS already formed; (ii) antioxidants that inhibit the formation of ROS from their cellular sources; and (iii) antioxidants that remove or repair the damage or modifications caused by ROS. However, excessive levels of ROS production become pathological and may lead to mitochondrial and cell apoptosis through the activation of the apoptosome protein complex. An increase in ROS oxidative stress acts as a bactericide, which damages bacterial DNA, RNA and proteins.
3.3.1. GDY photothermal ROS oxidative stress as a bactericide. Antibiotic resistance is becoming a great threat, necessitating the discovery of new antibacterial agents. Bacterial infections are posing an unprecedented threat to the public health worldwide, particularly with the advent of antibiotic-resistant bacteria resulting from drug overuse or misuse. The advent and global spread of antibiotic-resistant bacterial strains has sparked interest from academic labs to clinical trials. Thus, finding novel antibacterial medicines that work differently than standard antibiotics to address pathogen-associated disorders is imperative. Antibacterial nanomaterials, unlike regular antibiotics, do not promote bacterial resistance given that they not only work through many antibacterial pathways simultaneously but also have high membrane penetration due to their tiny size. To date, a variety of sophisticated nanomaterials, such as metals and metal oxides (MoS2, MXenes, and carbon nanomaterials) have shown tremendous promise in the treatment of bacterial infections. Carbon NPs (e.g., carbon nanotubes, graphene, and GO) are capable of regulating and battling bacterial infections, and this have become some of the most prominent antibacterial nanomaterials to successfully destroy pathogenic bacteria. However, a systematic and comprehensive review of the literature showed that these carbon nanomaterials cannot create enough ROS to combat dangerous bacteria because the required electronhole pairs are insufficient during the photo-catalytic sterilization process. The interaction of GDY-based nanomaterials with living creatures is unavoidable and vital from their point of synthesis until their disposal. 3.3.2. GDY for chemotherapy. Cancer is one the most challenging diseases to treat globally. Many factors contribute to the significant rise in cancer death rates, including an aging population, smoking, stress, and lack of physical activity. Furthermore, due to variables such as inadequate bioavailability and inadequate targeting of chemotherapeutics, achieving adequate anticancer therapy remains difficult. This leads to inadequate treatment results, significant recurrence risks, and unpleasant side effects, and traditional therapies such as surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy are unable to satisfy the clinical need. Thus, new combination therapies with great efficiency are required to increase the survival rate of cancer patients. Accordingly, nanostructure materials are getting significant attention as cancer theranostic agents due to the advancement of nano-biotechnology. In anticancer therapy, GDY and GDYO have been intensively investigated as drug carriers, biomolecule sensors, and cellular imaging agents. In recent years, 2D material-based cancer detection and therapy have received great interest. Many 2D materials have achieved success in applications such as in vivo tumor imaging, drug administration, and photo-thermal treatment (PTT) due to their unique 2D planar structure and physicochemical features.65 PTT involves the use of a light-absorbing therapeutic chemicals to create heat in the tumor location in the presence of light irradiation, causing permanent damage to tumor cells.

GDY is very hydrophobic, but GDYO has oxygen-containing hydrophilic groups on its surface. This special surface chemistry allows for stacking interactions and electrostatic interactions with other surrounding molecules. Drugs can be physically and chemically bound to the surface of GDY and GDY-based materials, allowing drug delivery applications. The superior light absorption property of photo-thermal agents allows them to efficiently convert light irradiation into thermal energy using a low power density laser, minimizing the negative side effect of injuring normal tissue in the irradiation region. Thus, GDY has recently been employed as a photothermal and contrast agent in PTT and in vivo tumor imaging due to its high photothermal conversion efficiency.97 GDY was PEGylated before use to increase its biocompatibility. GDYPEG exhibited extensive optical absorption from the UV to NIR bands, similar to other 2D materials. GDYPEG exhibited a mass extinction coefficient of 10.1 L g1 cm1 under 808 nm laser irradiation, which was around three-times that of GO.98 GDYPEG exhibited a photothermal conversion efficiency of 42%, which was greater than other traditional PTT agents.99 GDYPEG was used for both in vitro and in vivo PTT, producing an effective antitumor impact at the cellular level and in a xenograft model due to its superior qualities. GDYPEG was also employed as a contrast agent for in vivo tumor tissue imaging using photoacoustic imaging (PAI).97 Due to their large surface area, 2D materials make excellent drug carriers, allowing a wide range of medications to be loaded on their surface.100 Graphene and its derivatives have been widely employed for very effective drug loading and delivery due to their sp2-hybridized carbon atoms and huge conjugated surface.46 GDY, a 2D carbon-based material with sp- and sp2-hybridized carbon atoms, may be much more effective for drug delivery. Our team recently developed a GDY-based drug delivery platform, which was employed in cancer treatment combining PTT and chemotherapy.44 GDY was used in this study to treat cancer in two ways, as a photothermal agent and as an anticancer medication carrier. Briefly, ultrasonication and stirring were used to load DOX, a typical chemotherapeutic medication with an aromatic structure, on GDY NSs, where stacking and electrostatic interactions were the primary interactions between GDY and DOX. The loading capacity of GDY NSs was around 38%, which was higher than the virgin graphene NSs.101 Under acidic circumstances, more DOX was released from GDY/DOX, especially under NIR irradiation, according to the drug-release experiment. GDY/DOX showed a high antitumor efficacy under NIR irradiation both in vitro and in vivo by combining the high photothermal conversion efficiency of GDY with the cytotoxicity of DOX.

In comparison to biological enzymes, nanocatalysts have raised several concerns in the biomedical field given that their catalytic activity is durable and efficient even under extreme conditions. However, during biological applications, the key problems are the stability of both their physical form and catalytic activity. Jiaming liu et al.102 developed a method for fabricating a stable nanocatalyst, in which GDY was used as a template for immobilizing catalytic palladium NPs (Pd-NPs) on the surface of GDY. They developed a high-activity, stable GDY-immobilized and Pd-NP-based oxygen generator, which successfully attenuated tumor hypoxia and delayed tumor development. GDY, in contrast to other carbon-based nanomaterials, absorbs metal atoms primarily through its oxygen-containing functional groups and structural defects,103,104 and can easily attach and distribute a huge quantity of ultra-small metal atoms due to its sp-hybridized carbon atoms and heterogeneous electron distribution on its surface.105 The as-prepared Pd-NPs/GDY demonstrated significant catalytic activity in degrading H2O2 to H2O and O2 compared to the commercial Pd/C. Furthermore, during the catalytic reactions, Pd-NPs/GDY had substantially greater stability. This nanocomposite, which has greater dispersibility and chemical stability, retained more than 80% of its initial activity even after 50 reaction cycles, outperforming Pd-NPs, Pd-NPs/GO, and commercial Pd/C. This nano-platform effectively serves as a long-term oxygen nanoreactor for cancer therapy using hypoxia. 3.3.3. GDY free radical scavenging activity for radiation protection. ROS, such as H2O2, superoxide anion (O2), singlet oxygen (1O2), and hydroxyl radical (OH), are unstable chemicals, which can damage biomolecules and cause cellular necrosis and apoptosis. Thus, many efforts have been made to use these compounds in cancer treatment.113,114 In fact, much effort has focused on the exploitation of ROS-mediated cancer treatment techniques (e.g., radio-, sonodynamic, and photodynamic therapy), which offer various benefits over traditional surgical and chemotherapeutic techniques, such as noninvasiveness, high selectivity, and minimal side effects. However, the insufficient oxygen supply in the hypoxic tumor tissues substantially limits the therapeutic efficacy of these treatments in solid tumors.115 Consequently, the development of oxygen-independent, ROS-mediated oncotherapy is critical. Unlike other carbon-based materials, GDY has a unique sp-hybridized carbon atom-containing structure, which allows significant reduction. GDY was shown to have a low reduction potential and be capable of reducing palladium ions in situ to generate Pd NPs, indicating that it may act as both a carrier and a reductant.116 Xie et al.70 devised a bovine serum albumin (BSA)-modified GDY for scavenging the free radicals created during X-ray irradiation based on the reducibility of GDY. The extremely active O2 and OH were cleared by the GDYBSA NPs, demonstrating their great free radical scavenging capabilities. GDYBSA NPs in cells could minimize DNA damage caused by X-ray radiation and remove intracellular ROS, while the in vivo experiments indicated that GDYBSA NPs could protect key organs from X-ray-induced damage. The latter was validated by measuring the DNA damage in the bone marrow and two critical biomarkers of radiation-induced harm in key organs (superoxide dismutase and malondialdehyde levels). For effective cancer therapy, Fenton reaction-mediated oncotherapy employs iron ions to catalytically transform endogenous H2O2 into hydroxyl radicals, the most reactive oxygen species known in biology. Due to the higher amounts of H2O2 (0.11 103 m) in the tumor microenvironment compared to normal tissue, Fenton chemistry provides the benefits of excellent selectivity and fewer adverse effects compared to other ROS-mediated cancer therapies. A tumor-targeting iron sponge (TTIS) nanocomposite based on GDYO, which has a high affinity for iron, was explored by Huan Min et al.117 To enable tumor photoacoustic and magnetic resonance imaging, TTIS can be decorated with a tumor-targeting polymer and allowed to accumulate in tumor tissue. TTIS is an effective PTT agent because of its high photothermal conversion efficiency (37.5%). Furthermore, the heat generated during the PTT process can speed up the release of iron ions from TTIS, while also increasing the efficiency of the Fenton reaction, allowing a combination PTT and Fenton reaction-mediated cancer treatment. This study offers a GDYO-based iron sponge with improved anticancer properties thanks to PTT and Fenton chemistry. Fig. 14(a) depicts the putative anticancer mechanism of TTIS. We investigated the hemolytic activity of TTIS and TNIS to determine their biocompatibility before examining their anticancer effects in vivo. After 4 h of exposure to TNIS or TTIS, hemolysis increased slowly and dose dependently. Below 100 g mL1 TNIS and TTIS caused negligible hemolysis. Therefore, 10 mg kg1 was chosen as the dosage for the subsequent experiments in mice. 4T1 xenografted mice were randomly divided into 6 treatment groups (saline, TNIS, TTIS, saline + laser, TNIS + laser, and TTIS + laser), which were injected every other day for 3 treatments (Fig. 14(b)). The temperatures of the tumor tissue of the mice that were exposed to laser irradiation were monitored 6 h after each injection. Fig. 14(c and d) show that the mice given TNIS + laser irradiation had significantly higher temperatures (T = 15.8 C) than the mice given saline injection (T = 8.6 C). Under the same intensity of laser irradiation, the mice injected with TTIS demonstrated an even larger rise in tumor temperature (T = 19.8 C). The body weights of the mice were monitored every other day during therapy to explore potentially unfavorable side effects of the treatment with TTIS or TNIS. No significant changes in body weight were observed throughout the course of the therapy (Fig. 14(e)), indicating that the therapy was generally safe. Critically, the tumor volume was measured to assess the therapeutic benefits of the various formulations (Fig. 14(f)). Tumor development was rather quick in both the saline and saline + laser groups, but tumor development was somewhat slowed in the TTIS alone group, confirming the Fenton reaction-mediated cancer therapy effects. In the mice treated with TTIS + laser, tumor inhibition significantly increased (80%), showing a combination anticancer impact of PTT and the Fenton reaction. The mice treated with TTIS + laser had greater tumor suppression than those treated with TNIS laser, which can be ascribed to the improved tumor targeting of TTIS. The mice were euthanized at the conclusion of the therapy, and the tumors were removed and weighed (Fig. 14(g and h)). The average tumor weights matched the in vivo volume measurements, with the TTIS + laser group being lighter than the TTIS and TNIS + laser groups. Most of the tumor cells in the TTIS + laser group were destroyed according to the H&E staining of the tumor sections obtained from the mice at the conclusion of therapy (Fig. 14(i)). The tumors from the mice treated with TTIS, TNIS, or TNIS + laser showed some cell damage, whereas that from the mice treated with saline or saline + laser showed no evident cell damage. The TUNEL staining results were consistent with the H&E test findings, demonstrating the improved anticancer impact of TTIS, which is based on a combination of PTT and Fenton reactions.

4. Conclusions and perspectives

GDY and its derivatives have shown considerable potential in the biological field in recent years due to their unique structures and remarkable performances. The properties and benefits of GDY are at the forefront of materials design for a wide range of biological applications, including cell proliferation, differentiation/maturation, drug delivery, and anticancer therapy. Various survivability experiments on human cells in vitro and on mice in vivo have yielded extremely encouraging results, indicating that GDY-based materials are appropriate for real-world applications. For a number of applications, GDY has been shown to be a suitable substrate for loading medicines, metal ions, proteins, and nanocatalysts. The excellent photothermal conversion efficiency of GDY broadens its usefulness in tumor eradication by producing hyperthermia. The capacity of GDY to scavenge ROS and monitor humidity is enhanced by its sp-hybridized carbon atoms. However, the application of GDY in the biological field is still uncommon. The essential features of GDY are crucial to its anticipated medicinal applications. GDY is a promising substrate and carrier with a wide range of applications. The triple carbon bonds of GDY are possible active sites, which may interact with other ligands covalently or non-covalently. For its biological use, GDY may covalently bond with targeted molecules, tracking molecules, and biocompatible molecules. One-bond and two-bonds make up the acetylenic bond, with the electron pair of each bond acting as an electron donor to coordinate with transition metal atoms with vacant orbitals. Simulation calculations showed that GDY, a carbon-based material with a high fraction of acetylenic linkages and natural nanopores, has substantial capacity for the adsorption of metal atom. The ion carrying and in situ catalytic properties of GDY have been tested in the lab. Consequently, we believe that GDY will be a suitable substrate for immobilizing a large number of metal atoms and the in situ creation of nanocatalysts, which can be employed as stable and high-performance biosensors. Meanwhile, because of its capacity to adsorb metal ions, GDY can be used as a drug carrier for loading platinum medicines. In addition, GDY can be utilized to detect and eliminate heavy metal ions. Secondly, GDY can be covalently functionalized through addition reactions between its acetylenic bonds and unsaturated ligand groups, a process known as click chemistry. The addition of azides and alkynes is a prototypical click chemistry reaction. Thirdly, the hydrophobic and conjugated surface of GDY, like many other 2D materials, may non-covalently load numerous functional molecules for disease diagnostics and therapy, such as drug and gene loading via hydrophobic interaction and stacking. The inherent nanopores of GDY make it a suitable device for biological applications. DNA sequencing is one of its possible uses. Simulation computing was also used to investigate the binding force between a nucleobase and GDY. The results of the calculations indicated that the distinct nucleobase binding strengths on GDY follow the order of G > A > C > T > U. Thus, GDY can be a potential choice for DNA sequencing based on its outstanding qualities indicated above and other computational predictions. Furthermore, GDY can be developed as a nanopore-based electrochemical device for single-molecule detection based on a similar idea. However, the biological application of GDY is still uncommon in many domains compared to other carbon-based nanomaterials such as graphene and carbon nanotubes. There are still several obstacles to overcome, which restrict the future applicability of GDY. Firstly, single layers of GDY NSs are challenging to maintain in solution because of the high contact between them. Multilayered GDY NSs cover a portion of the acetylenic bonds, lowering the fraction of sp-hybridized carbon atoms accessible and limiting the benefits of GDY. Secondly, the large-scale manufacture of GDY is a difficulty that must be solved. To achieve the large-scale manufacture and wider use of GDY, its present synthetic techniques need to be enhanced. Thirdly, techniques for identifying the acetylenic bonds of GDY, particularly for distinguishing GDY from other carbon-based compounds in biological systems, are currently scarce. GDY, like other carbon-based nanomaterials such as graphene and carbon nanotubes, is grappling with the problem of translating laboratory discoveries into therapeutic application. The therapeutic uses of GDY-based nanomaterials are limited by two major limitations. The biotoxicity and metabolism of GDY in vivo are the first considerations for its clinical use. Despite the fact that certain research suggests that GDY and its derivatives are safer than other carbon-based materials, the long-term biotoxicity of GDY is still unknown. Furthermore, the bio-distribution, metabolism, and excretion of GDY-based materials are critical in their practical use. The buildup of GDY in different organs and its excretion time, like other 2D carbon-based nanomaterials such as graphene, are likely connected to its long-term biotoxicity. Consequently, rigorous research on the biotoxicity and metabolism of GDY is critical. Secondly, the large-scale clinical use of GDY-based materials requires the mass manufacturing of homogenous GDY-based materials. Specifically, the production of both pristine GDY and its reprocessed derivatives on a large scale is required for their clinical application. Meanwhile, GDY-based products should have similar physicochemical properties in different batches, such as shape and solubility. Consequently, developing large-scale and robust manufacturing procedures is critical. In conclusion, GDY and its derivatives appear to be great materials for biomedical applications; however, several issues have to be resolved. We believe that GDY has a lot of promise for future applications because of its unique performances and features.
