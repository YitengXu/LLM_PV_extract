Abstract

Plasmon-enhanced harvesting of photons has contributed to the photochemical conversion and storage of solar energy. However, high dependence on noble metals and weak coupling in heterostructures constrain the progress towards sustainable plasmonic enhancement. Here earth-abundant Ti is studied to achieve the plasmonic enhancement of catalytic activity in a solar-driven heterostructure Ti/TiO2âˆ’x. The heterostructure was fabricated by engineering an intense coupling of a surface-etched Ti metal and a gradient-based TiO2âˆ’x dielectric via diffusion doping. Ti/TiO2âˆ’x exhibits a highly resonant light absorption band associated with surface plasmon resonances that exhibit strong near-field enhancement (NFE) and hot electron injection effects. In a photoelectrochemical system, intense interaction of the resonant plasmons with a vicinal TiO2âˆ’x dielectric accelerates the transfer of solar energy to charge carriers for plasmon-enhanced water splitting reactions. Moreover, the plasmonic Ti/TiO2âˆ’x structure presents sustained enhanced redox activities over 100 h. The intense coupling by gradient doping offers an effective approach to enable the plasmon resonances of Ti excited by visible light. The Ti-based plasmonic heterostructure potentially opens an alternative avenue towards sustainable plasmon-enhanced catalysis.

Introduction

Plasmon resonances provide an exciting path to advance catalytic conversion and storage of solar to chemical energy.1â€“5 The plasmons in metals (typically Au and Ag) can harvest photons from visible to infrared light by tuning their morphology.6,7 Hetero-coupling the plasmonic metals with semiconductors as plasmonic heterostructures is widely used to enhance the absorption of solar energy to drive chemical reactions through hot carrier injection, near-field enhancement and/or light scattering effects.3,8 However, the intrinsic features of the plasmonic heterostructures limit their further development for highly efficient coupling of plasmons with solar light and yields of energetic charge carriers, such as the weak coupling,9â€“11 dependence on noble metals and complexity of fabrication. Nowadays, non-noble metals are attracting high research interests due to their abundance and superior physicochemical properties,12â€“15 but only a few non-noble materials can support plasmon resonances (SPRs) in the visible light region and overcome the challenges which include chemical stability, controllability and reliable synthesis.12

In principle, metal Ti interfacing with a suitable dielectric environment, such as titanium oxides, can activate SPRs.16â€“19 The plasmonic energy could then be transferred to the dielectric of titanium oxides for applications. Since the first successful trial of TiO2 to split water under ultraviolet light in 1972 by Fujishima and Honda,20 a variety of strategies have been explored to enhance visible light driven catalytic activities,21 such as the plasmonic noble metals hetero-coupled with TiO2.22 The earth abundant Ti could function as an alternative plasmonic metal for sustainable enhancement. However, excitation of plasmon resonances of Ti in the visible light region and extraction of the plasmonic energy have not been achieved and basically studied.

Herein, a gradient-based heterostructure is proposed as a promising path by engineering a metal coupled with a gradient-based dielectric. Diffusion doping23 is employed to inject foreign atoms in the surface region of a metal, forming a gradient-based dielectric intensely coupling with the underneath metal. Variations of the dopant stoichiometry affect the distribution of hetero-atoms and further the states and density of free carriers.24 Therefore, it is possible to manipulate the dielectric properties at the interface between a metal and a dielectric via the diffusion doping, enabling the excitation of SPRs by visible light.

A gradient-based heterostructure Ti/TiO2âˆ’x is fabricated via a thermal diffusion doping process,25 using non-noble metal Ti foils with micro- and/or nano-prisms on the surface created by the chemical etching process. The prisms were designed to increase the aspect ratio of nanostructured surface edges for high density of hot spots and provide active sites for reactions. Excitation of SPRs of Ti and the transfer mechanism of the plasmonic energy to chemical molecules through the gradient dielectric were both investigated experimentally and theoretically. Our findings show that the SPRs of Ti are excited by visible light. The gradient-based heterostructure Ti/TiO2âˆ’x presents a strong resonant light absorption band associated with the SPRs that exhibit both NFE and hot electron generation effects. An intense interaction of SPRs between a plasmonic Ti metal and a gradient-based TiO2âˆ’x dielectric accelerates the conversion of solar to chemical energy. After the coating of the Ni thin film, the plasmonic heterostructure possesses long-term stability over 100 hours in the photoelectrochemical environment. The gradient-based heterostructure represents a novel approach to enable the SPRs of Ti excited in the visible light spectrum for sustainable plasmon-enhanced catalysis and photochemical applications.

Energy-dispersive X-ray (EDX) element analysis shows that Ti and O are distributed homogeneously on the top-surface with the atomic ratio close to 1:2 (Fig. 2a and b), forming a thin-film dielectric of TiO2 on the top surface. Dedicated line-scan EDX along the cross-section from the top into the deep surface layer discloses that the oxygen content gradually decreases from the top surface to the inner space, while the Ti content shows opposite tendency. The oxygen concentration shows a decreasing amount down to about distribution around 500 nm underneath the top surface. The cross-section revealed the formation of a varied-stoichiometry of titanium oxides from the top TiO2 dielectric thin film on the surface via a transition region TiO2âˆ’x (0 < x < 2) to underlying pure Ti in the bulk.25 This profile forms a quasi-sandwich configuration of the top dielectric film, middle transition region, and bottom original metal.26 Gradient variation of the dopant in the transition region constructs heterogeneous junctions. The X-ray diffraction pattern taken from these samples shows broadened peaks of Ti and rutile TiO2 phases (Fig. S2, ESIâ€ ), indicating the presence of a transition-phase state such as amorphous/crystalline. The gradient-based transition-phase state agrees with the possible formation of Ti and O with gradient distributions, denoted as TiO2âˆ’x (0 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 2). The variation affects the stoichiometry of hetero-atoms and therefore the states and density of free charge carriers, benefiting the transition region with a favoured variety of dielectric constants. For example, mixed-phase titanium oxides27 and vacancies28 of O and Ti can be specially formed to create proper interfacing between the TiO2 dielectric and Ti metal.

Optical properties

Fig. 3a shows the absorption spectra of bulk Ti foil and Ti/TiO2âˆ’x. Both spectra show a basic spectrum valley at around 350 nm. Remarkably, the Ti/TiO2âˆ’x heterostructure has a strong resonant absorption peak in the visible light range. According to the Kâ€“M function, the quality factor of the resonant absorption band is estimated to be about 8 which is close to the value of âˆ¼9 for gold nanoparticles.24 This factor is defined by the ratio of the resonant peak energy to the FWHM (full width at half maximum) energy. For both specimens, absorption with small amplitude in the ultra-violet light range is assigned to an intrinsic absorption of TiO2 due to the native oxidation layer on Ti and the TiO2 on the surface of Ti/TiO2âˆ’x.26Fig. 3b shows a dark field image of Ti/TiO2âˆ’x exhibiting a distinct glowing on the surface due to scattering and free-space decaying on the periphery.29 This was not observed in the Ti foil (Fig. S3aâ€“c, ESIâ€ ). The orange-yellow glowing color is consistent with the light wavelength of the absorption peak, indicating the possible excitation of plasmon resonances. Moreover, the resonant absorption band is even repeatable and controllable with a regular red-shift by variation of oxidation time (Fig. S4, ESIâ€ ). The redshift is due to alteration of the dielectric properties, such as thickness and optical constant, resulting in the transformation of electromagnetic field interaction between the dielectric and the metal substrate. The results are strongly related to the excitation of SPRs due to appropriate momentum of dielectric couples of photons into the surface plasmon of the Ti metal substrate, ruling out the possibility that the absorption peak is induced by defects associated with oxygen vacancies.30

In general, the refractive index of the surrounding medium has an impact on the intensity and position of plasmon-induced light absorption peaks.31 The Ti/TiO2âˆ’x structure was immersed in different solvents, air, 0.5 M Na2SO4 and 1 M NaOH aqueous solutions with refractive indices of 1, 1.3338 and 1.3358, respectively. Fig. 3c shows the absorption spectra of Ti/TiO2âˆ’x under the influence of different solvents. The absorption band shows a blue shift with increasing refractive index, as expected from the characteristic of resonant plasmons.32 The reversible blueshift could be explained by a negative charging in the solution, thereby causing a change in charge carrier density in surface dielectrics.33

To further explore plasmon resonances in Ti/TiO2âˆ’x and relevance of the resonant light absorption band with surface plasmons, we have employed finite-element-method based calculations in the frequency-domain to extract the spatial electric field distribution in an approximated 3-dimensional model (Fig. S5, ESIâ€ ). The spatial field distribution (Fig. 3d and Fig. S6â€“S10, ESIâ€ ) shows the maximum of electric field enhancement that is localized in the region between the TiO2 nanoparticle and Ti following the trend of respective wavelengths, due to the polarization of surface plasmons in the Ti metal substrate with the presence of TiO2 on the top.34 The electric field enhancement spectra disclose two distinctive enhancement bands (Fig. 3e and f). One is at around 290 nm and the other is at about 430 nm. The enhanced band in the gap is dominated close to the bottom surface of the TiO2 particle, becoming more pronounced when the gradient decreases the thickness of the TiO2 dielectric film. In contrast, an enhanced field band becomes more pronounced at the interface between the Ti metal and TiO2 film dielectric (Fig. S6â€“S10, ESIâ€ ), when the gradient increases the thickness of the TiO2 dielectric film in the gap. The enhanced field distribution verifies that the surface dielectric particles promote the excitation of localized surface plasmonic resonances (LSPRs) by coupling light into the metal, while the gradient increasing the thickness of the TiO2 film in the gap enables confined excitation of SPRs in the interface between the dielectric and the metal substrate. Hybrid coupling of both excitations results in an enhanced resonant light absorption band in the visible light range (Fig. 3e and f).

The absorption valley at around 350 nm and the peak band close to 450 nm in the absorption enhancement spectra (Fig. 3e and f) both correlate with the field enhancement bands. In addition, the absorption peaks in the absorption enhancement spectra show a slight red shift with a gradient increasing the thickness of the TiO2 dielectric film, corresponding to the red shift of the experimental resonant absorption peaks. With the adjustment of the parameters, the calculated absorption band agrees with the positions of resonant absorption peaks present in the experimental absorption spectra. The calculated results further support the excitations of SPPs and LSPRs in Ti/TiO2âˆ’x. The resonant plasmons exhibit strong near-field effects interacting with surface mass through the transition region between the Ti metal and gradient-based dielectric for applications.

Plasmon-enhanced photoactivities for photoelectrochemical application

To investigate the effect of resonant plasmons on the energy transformation in Ti/TiO2âˆ’x, a photoelectrochemical system was employed, in which Ti/TiO2âˆ’x was used as a working electrode. Ti/TiO2âˆ’x immersed in liquid electrolyte forms a close and firm contacting interface. The built-in potential was characterized by open-circuit potential (Voc) under simulated solar light illumination (Fig. 4a). The saturation potential in the Voc corresponds to a steady-state distribution of the carriers. A large numerical difference between the Voc under the illumination and in the dark, Î”Voc = âˆ’350 mV, clearly indicates that the polarizability of electron and hole carriers upon excitation has been maintained, while back-recombination processes are retarded dramatically.35â€“37 In KOH solution, the positive carriers move towards an electrode/electrolyte interface, while the negative carriers move to the opposite direction. The negative tangent slope in Mottâ€“Schottky curves (Fig. S11a, ESIâ€ ) reveals an n-type conductivity of Ti/TiO2âˆ’x both in the dark and under light. Therefore, the balanced distribution of carriers in bulk shifts the Voc to a more negative value upon excitation. Finally, a high plasmonic photovoltage of 350 mV is established across the heterojunction. Indeed, for example in a p-type photoelectrode, a positive shift caused by light irradiation was observed in Voc.37 With the high plasmonic photovoltage, Ti/TiO2âˆ’x can not only generate electronâ€“hole pairs but also obtain a large number of separated charge carriers for photoelectron-chemical reactions.5

Giving an appropriate thermodynamic potential, the separated charge carriers can activate photoelectrochemical reactions to generate current. Ti/TiO2âˆ’x as a photoanode shows an enhanced photocurrent that is reproducible and timely responds to the periodic solar light illumination (Fig. 4b). When the light illumination is shielded, the anodic current drops immediately to a steady state without undergoing any attenuation which would be manifested by transient spikes. Spikes are usually observed due to bulk or surface recombination.35,38 The absence of spikes indicates that the ultrafast recombination process is inhibited. The continuous current response of the chemical reaction versus sweeping potential was also evaluated by linear sweep voltammetry (Fig. 4c). Upon exposure to simulated sunlight, the obtained current is amplified by a factor of 210 at a bias of 0.23 V compared to the case when exposed to the dark. A 200-fold enhancement is also obtained in the Na2SO4 electrolyte (Fig. S11b, ESIâ€ ). In addition, the anodic current shows a nearly linear response to different light power illumination, which is consistent with the expected OER driven by the hole carriers (Fig. S11c, ESIâ€ ).

Incident photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE) spectra were measured to clarify the dependence of the OER on the solar light at a specific energy. Interestingly, an enhancement peak is located at 340 nm (blue line, Fig. 4d). The entire IPCE spectrum shows a high conversion efficiency region below 400 nm (Fig. S12a, ESIâ€ ), which is fitted to a Gaussian function resulting in three enhanced peaks (Fig. S12b, ESIâ€ ). The ultra-high IPCE value activated by high-energy photons (blue line, Fig. 4d) is magnified by approximately 400%. A comparable enhancement is also attained in the second specimen, where the increase occurs at 350 nm (Fig. 4e). Between them (Fig. 4d and e), the shift of the enhancement peak is obvious, due to the difference of the maximum electric field varied by plasmon resonances, similar to the shift of absorption resonance peaks (the insets in Fig. 4d and e). The offset between the enhanced peaks is also obtained in the Ti foil and Ni-coated Ti foil with annealing (Fig. S13, ESIâ€ ) with cut-off edges at 350 nm (Fig. 4f), strongly indicating that the photons directly excited charge carriers through the surface TiO2 generating the photocurrent. The coating of Ni was used to protect the surface from corrosion.39,40 The result suggests that the OER activity is mainly caused by the hole carriers that are instantly excited by high energetic photons. In addition, compared to the Ti foil without Ni (Fig. 4f), the achieved ultra-high conversion efficiencies (Fig. 4d and e), benefiting from the introduction of a gradient-based dielectric, disclose that the direct photonic transition is enhanced by the localized electromagnetic field. This can be explained by the mechanism of plasmon-enhanced photonic energy transfer.11 The absorption edge of TiO2âˆ’x at 350 nm spectrally overlaps with the amplified near-field band, resulting in an increased light absorption that promoted the photonic transitions.

After deposition of a 2 nm Ni thin film (Fig. S14, ESIâ€ ) followed by annealing, we investigated the energy transfer process in the second specimen Ti/TiO2âˆ’x (Fig. S15, ESIâ€ ). The derived IPCE spectrum highlights two distinct regions with opposite polarities (Fig. 5a): one peak is at 360 nm and the other negative peak is at 500 nm. The peak at 360 nm with a rational offset fits the enhancement profile but has a lower amplitude compared to the specimen without the Ni-coating (Fig. 4e). The adverse enhancement peak indicates that the proton reduction reactions were activated under the light irradiation. Moreover, the IPCE curve faithfully follows the plasmon resonance absorption spectrum in the visible light region (Fig. 5b). This match confirms that the resonant absorption band present in the experimental absorption spectra originates from the excitation of SPRs. Furthermore, the reduction reactions are driven through the hot electron injection mechanism,11,41 so that Ti/TiO2âˆ’x can utilize the plasmonic energy of visible light below the absorption band edge of TiO2âˆ’x. Therefore, the plasmonic Ti/TiO2âˆ’x possesses two distinct mechanisms, one is the enhanced photonic energy transfer of ultra-violet light by a localized electric field, and the other is hot electron generation by plasmonic energy of visible light.

We then investigated the stability of the Ni-coated Ti/TiO2âˆ’x as a photoanode in the environment of photo-electrochemical system. The anode maintains the OER activity for over 100 hours (Fig. 5d) under the simulated solar light illumination, although cyclic voltammograms taken in the times show a slight change after 64 hours of a continuous test in aqueous KOH solution (Fig. 5c). The UV-Vis absorption spectra show no deviation in the resonance intensity of surface plasmons after 106 hours. A control third specimen of Ti/TiO2âˆ’x without coating Ni was measured under the identical conditions (Fig. S16a, ESIâ€ ). Apparent degradation of the light absorbance intensity can be observed in the UV-vis spectra (Fig. S16b, ESIâ€ ) after âˆ¼25 hours. Clearly, the corrosion is suppressed to a large extent by coating Ni as a protection layer. As a result, the long-term and stable resonant plasmons and transfer of photonic to chemical energy are ultimately obtained.

To examine the origin of the charge carriers, we performed a density functional theory (DFT) based calculation of electronic properties on a doped Ti model (Fig. S17, ESIâ€ ). The density of states (DOS) predicts an initial energy distribution of the carriers over energy bands close to the Fermi level (Fig. S17aâ€“c, ESIâ€ ). Increase in the oxygen concentration leads to stronger hybridization in s, p and d orbitals close to the Fermi level, which could provide local interband and intraband transitions of the charge carriers.42,43 Also, the gap between the O2p and Ti3d energy levels tends to decrease, which allows a higher possibility of interband transitions by high-energy photons.44 After heavy doping (Fig. S17c, ESIâ€ ), the energy gap between O2p and Ti3d orbitals shows an onset threshold of âˆ¼3.54 eV (350 nm), which correlates with the enhancement peak at 350 nm in the IPCE spectra. Thus, the charge carriers could be generated from both the interband and intraband transitions by the localized electromagnetic field enhancement. The interband transitions dominate the top surface positions while the intraband transitions are mainly below the surface region.

With the generated charge carriers, the mechanism for the separation and transportation of charge carriers is understood through a proposed schematic (Fig. S18, ESIâ€ ) of the SPRs of the Ti metal intensely coupling with the gradient-based TiO2âˆ’x dielectrics.45 Their built-in band bending can suppress an undesirable recombination process while allowing charge carriers at low energy states to cross and be collected.46,47 Ti/TiO2âˆ’x holds a gradient profile of oxygen elements in the surface region of Ti (Fig. S18a, ESIâ€ ), forming a slight band bending (Fig. S18b, ESIâ€ ). Upon the excitation of SPRs, the enhanced local electric field forms a deeper band bending. This is in line with a photovoltage of âˆ¼350 mV observed when subjected to the alkaline solution. The excited charge carriers tend to recombine, but the pulling force of a built-in electric field promotes the transfer electrons and holes, and accumulate and inhabit in the opposite directions away from the Fermi level (Fig. S18c, ESIâ€ ). With appropriate thermodynamic potential, the hole carriers are beneficial to the reaction sites involved in the OER, while the hot electrons injected into the conduction band of titanium oxides participate in the HER.

Conclusions

In summary, a solar-driven plasmonic heterostructure Ti/TiO2âˆ’x is fabricated via the diffusion doping process on earth-abundant Ti. The resonant plasmons of Ti intensely coupling with a gradient-based TiO2âˆ’x dielectric confine light to a resonant absorption band and exhibit an enhanced local electric field and hot electron generation effects. The excitation and quality of the SPRs in Ti/TiO2âˆ’x are adjustable in the visible light range by tuning the thermal doping parameters. The strong interaction between plasmonic Ti and the gradient-based TiO2âˆ’x dielectric enhances the conversion of solar energy to charge carriers through the plasmon-enhanced photonic energy transfer and the hot electron injection mechanisms. By adding a protective Ni thin film, a more robust plasmon resonance in Ti/TiO2âˆ’x is achieved in a chemical environment that maintains long-lasting plasmon-enhanced activity over 100 hours.

The gradient-based heterostructure Ti/TiO2âˆ’x represents a conceptually different approach to excite the SPRs by visible light and extract the plasmonic energy, using a thermal doping process to construct an intense coupling of Ti metal and the gradient-based TiO2âˆ’x dielectric. The Ti-based plasmonic structure with gradient doping could act as a platform to provide strong plasmon resonances in the visible light range and contribute to the conversion and storage of solar energy for sustainable plasmon-enhanced catalysis.

Materials, fabrication and characterization

Titanium foils (Ti foils, L: 20 mm Ã— W: 14 mm Ã— T: 0.5 mm, 99.99% purity) were cut out from a Ti sheet, which was purchased from Baoji Titanium Industry Co., Ltd. Scanning electron microscope images with energy-dispersive X-ray analysis (Hitachi SU 8230), interferometry maps (Wyko NT9100) and atomic force microscope images (AFM XE-200) were used to confirm the morphology, surface roughness, and surface micro-prisms, respectively. FEFD calculations

Finite element frequency domain (FEFD) simulations were performed using the commercially available COMSOL RF module.49,50 An approximated simulation model was constructed by using Ti metal foil as a substrate, and the TiO2 thin film with TiO2 nanoparticles in an ambient air atmosphere on the substrate. The Ti and TiO2 were modelled respectively using the full frequency-dependent dielectric functions. A uniform plane transverse electric (TE)-polarized electromagnetic wave was incident on the model. The optical constants for the materials, TiO2 and Ti, were taken from ref. 51 and 52, respectively. Perfectly matched layers (PMLs) were used in all boundaries to absorb the outgoing waves and simulate an open space around the model. The local field and absorption enhancement were both computed to investigate the nature of the constructed model.
