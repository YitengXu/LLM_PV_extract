Perovskite photodetectors and their application in artificial photonic synapses

Abstract

Organicinorganic hybrid perovskites exhibit superior optoelectrical properties and have been widely used in photodetectors. Perovskite photodetectors with excellent detectivity have great potential for developing artificial photonic synapses which can merge data transmission and storage. They are highly desired for next generation neuromorphic computing. The recent progress of perovskite photodetectors and their application in artificial photonic synapses are summarized in this review. Firstly, the key performance parameters of photodetectors are briefly introduced. Secondly, the recent research progress of photodetectors including photoconductors, photodiodes, and phototransistors is summarized. Finally, the applications of perovskite photodetectors in artificial photonic synapses in recent years are highlighted. All these demonstrate the great potential of perovskite photonic synapses for the development of artificial intelligence.

1. Introduction

Photodetectors (PDs) can detect optical signals by converting optical signals into electrical signals, and have been developed and applied in image sensing, artificial neural networks, optical communication, health care monitoring, military defense, etc.14 Semiconductors are the key functional layers of PDs, which can absorb incident light and generate carriers (electron and hole pairs).5 Under an external electric field, the electron and hole pairs are extracted and collected by the electrodes, and photocurrent will be generated. The absorption coefficient and exciton separation efficiency of semiconductors determine the performance of PDs. The commonly used semiconductor materials include inorganic materials, organic materials and organicinorganic hybrid perovskites.6 Inorganic materials include Ge, Si, and IIIV compound semiconductors that have been fully investigated, but they are usually fabricated with high temperature, high vacuum, and high cost, which are restrictions for flexible large-area imaging systems.7 Organic materials have the advantages of solution processability, low cost and high mechanical flexibility, and have become promising candidates for PDs. Perovskite materials have the above advantages of organic materials and have become famous due to their large absorption coefficients, direct and adjustable bandgaps, ambipolar charge transport, large carrier mobilities and long carrier diffusion.813 In 2009, Kojima et al. first introduced the perovskite material methyl ammonium lead iodide (MAPbI3) as a sensitizer in dye-sensitized solar cells, achieving a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 3.8%.14 Afterward, Park et al. adopted MAPbI3 nanoparticles as a light absorbing layer in solid-state mesoscopic perovskite solar cells with a PCE of over 9%.15 Since then, the PCE of perovskite solar cells has rapidly increased to a certified value of 25.5% during the past ten years.16 Besides solar cells, great progress has been made in photodetectors (PDs), light emitting diodes, optically pumped lasers and other optoelectronic devices.1719 The remarkable optoelectronic properties and low temperature solution processability of halide perovskites make them a research hotspot for low-cost PDs with high performance.

Commonly, three-dimensional (3D) perovskites have a typical formula of ABX3 (Fig. 1a),2022 where A represents a monovalent cation (MA+ = CH3NH3+; FA+ = CH(NH2)2+; Cs+), located at the eight corners of the cubic crystal; B is a divalent metal cation (Pb2+, Sn2+, Cu2+, Ge2+, or Mn2+), located at the centre of the body; and X is a monovalent halide anion (I, Br or Cl), located at the six face centres. By changing the halide or cation composition of perovskites, the bandgap can be tuned, so that the absorption of the perovskites can be adjusted from the ultraviolet (UV) to near infrared (NIR) region (Fig. 1d).6,23 MAPbI3 perovskites with a bandgap of around 1.5 eV exhibit broad absorption spectra from 300 to 800 nm. Meanwhile, MAPbI3 perovskites have high absorption coefficients, which make them attractive as active layers in PDs. Using Br/Cl to replace the halide anion of I, the energy bandgap of MAPbBr3/MAPbCl3 perovskites can be adjusted to 2.2 and 2.88 eV.2426 Using FA to replace the organic cation of MA, the energy bandgap of FAPbIyBr3y perovskites can be adjusted down to 1.48 eV, with the absorption edges starting from 840 nm.27 A lead free MASnI3 perovskite has a narrow bandgap (1.21.4 eV) and an extended absorption spectrum to 950 nm.28 However, the excessively large size of the A cations cant maintain the symmetry of the 3D perovskites. Big cations such as BA (C4H9NH3) and PEA (C6H5CH2CH2NH3) usually form 2D layer-structured perovskites with a chemical formula of (RNH3)2(MA)n1BnX3n+1 (where R is a long carbon alkyl or aromatic moiety).29,30 The 3D cubic structures are sandwiched by 2D long chains through intermolecular forces; thus 2D perovskites generally present better resistance to moisture than 3D perovskites due to their long organic hydrophobic chains.31Fig. 1(b) shows different layered 2D perovskites BA2PbI4 (n = 1), BA2MAPb2I7 (n = 2) and BA2MA2Pb3I10 (n = 3). When n is equal to 1, the crystalline structure of BA2PbI4 turns out to be an ideal quantum well in which one inorganic layer is separated by one layer of organic chains. When n tends toward infinity, the structure approaches a 3D perovskite structure.32 3D and 2D perovskite films show excellent absorption and high device performance, while polycrystalline films present more grain boundaries and trap densities, and exhibit low carrier mobility. The grain boundaries of perovskites make it easy for them to decompose in water and oxygen environments, hindering their practical application in stable functional devices. Perovskite nanocrystals present fewer grain boundaries and low trap densities and demonstrate larger carrier mobility and higher stability in air. Perovskite nanocrystals include zero-dimensional (0D) spherical quantum dots, one-dimensional (1D) nanowires, and 2D nanoplatelets, which can be prepared via different fabrication methods.33,34Fig. 1c illustrates the formation process for different CsPbX3 (X = Cl, Br, I) nanocrystals; by adding various organic acid and amine ligands, the CsPbX3 nanocrystals can be transformed into spherical quantum dots, nanocubes or nanoplatelets with different mediators.34 CsPbX3 nanocrystals exhibit adjustable emission spectra in the entire visible spectrum. Perovskite nanocrystals can be dispersed in a mild organic solvent, which can be blended with organic materials that have better flexibility to fabricate hybrid PDs.

Major efforts have been devoted towards achieving high performance perovskite PDs, such as tuning the composition of perovskites, improving the perovskite film morphology, and combining perovskites with 2D layered semiconductors, organic semiconductors and other optoelectronic materials. To develop applications for perovskite detectors, a lot of work has been devoted to exploring the application of perovskite PDs in image sensing and artificial photonic synapses in the past five years.42,43 However, the applications of perovskite PDs in artificial photonic synapses have not been systematically summarized. This review intends to summarize the recent progress of perovskite PDs and their applications in photonic synapses, which are very important in information transfer, data storage and manufacture of artificial intelligence vision systems. First, the key performance parameters of PDs are briefly introduced. Second, the recent research progress of PDs including photoconductors, photodiodes, and phototransistors is summarized. Then, the applications of perovskite photodetectors in artificial photonic synapses in recent years are highlighted. Finally, current challenges and possible solutions of perovskite artificial photonic synapses are proposed.

3. Perovskite photodetectors

Perovskite PDs could be divided into photoconductors, photodiodes, and phototransistors according to their different working mechanisms as illustrated in Fig. 2. Photoconductors and phototransistors are lateral-type devices, while photodiodes are vertical-type devices. The common mechanism is based on the photoelectric effect, which means that perovskite active layers absorb incident photons with energy larger than the bandgap of the perovskites and subsequently generate electronhole pairs. Then, the electrons and holes are separated and collected by the electrodes. To achieve high performance PDs, different structure PDs with various compositions of halide perovskites and perovskite heterojunction devices have been studied.

3.1 Perovskite photoconductors

Photoconductors are the simplest metalsemiconductormetal configuration, including only perovskite absorbers and electrodes as shown in Fig. 2a. The devices are easily fabricated by depositing perovskite films on substrates with electrodes, or depositing perovskite films and symmetrical metal electrodes on a substrate. The photogenerated charge carriers are generated under light illumination, then directly dissociated into holes and electrons, and collected by the electrodes under bias, which usually results in large Iph, and high R and EQE (Fig. 2d).48 However, the longer distance between the electrodes (always greater than 1 m) will lead to a longer carrier transport time, which always leads to slower response speed and requires a higher driving voltage. In 2014, Hu et al. developed the first perovskite photoconductor using MAPbI3 material on flexible indium tin oxide (ITO) substrates, which has a broad spectrum from 250 to 800 nm.49 The device presented a high R of 3.49 A W1 and an EQE of 1.19 103% at 365 nm. By adjusting the morphology, perovskite films with large crystal grains and high crystallinity can be obtained, which can reduce the boundary defects of the film, improve charge transfer and enhance the response speed of the photoconductors.6062 By controlling perovskite crystallization, uniform and transparent MAPbI3 perovskite film networks were successfully synthesized on poly(ethylene terephthalate) (PET). The fabricated MAPbI3 perovskite film network-based photoconductor exhibited a normalized D* of 1.02 1012 Hz1/2 W1 (Jones).50 However, polycrystalline films have a large number of defects that lead to a slow response time. Perovskite single crystals are expected to achieve high performance perovskite PDs due to their large carrier mobility, fewer grain boundaries and low trap densities. Large area (1 cm2) MAPbBr3 single crystals were fabricated on ITO substrates with high crystallinity and exploited to build high performance PDs (Fig. 3a).51Fig. 3b displays the measured currentvoltage (IV) curves of the PD, revealing that photocurrent can be generated under light illumination. As the light intensity increases, the photocurrent increases. The MAPbBr3 photoconductor presented a remarkable R exceeding 4000 A W1 and a superior D* exceeding 1013 Jones (Fig. 3c).

2D layered perovskite (BA)n(MA)n1PbnI3n+1 single crystals with different n values were grown from the waterair interface by Wang et al.52 They studied the photoresponse of (BA)n(MA)n1PbnI3n+1 perovskites with different quantum well thicknesses. Both the dark current and the photocurrent decreased as the n value decreased. And the quasi-2D perovskite BAPbI4 single crystal device exhibited a significantly low Id of 6 1013 A, a big P value of about 104 and a fast response time of 1.7/3.9 s. The 3D perovskite single crystal device exhibited a high R of 38 A W1 and a superior D* of 1.6 1013 Jones among the 2D layered perovskites. The Sun group fabricated 2D perovskite (BA)2(MA)n1PbnX3n+1 anisotropic micro-wire arrays using a template-confined growth method (Fig. 3d).53 By adjusting the space and length of the PDMS template and the concentration of the perovskite precursor, nanowire arrays with different lengths, heights and widths can be obtained. The PD with good crystalline quality and uniform perovskite micro-wire arrays exhibited an ultralow Id of 1012 A, a high R of 3.5 A W1 (Fig. 3e), a remarkable D* of 1 1015 Jones and a fast response time of 4.1/3.3 ms (Fig. 3f). Besides, the device was successfully applied for high-performance polarization detection. The Sun group further fabricated a MAPbBr3 microwire array with a length larger than 2 mm.17 Upon exposing the MAPbBr3 microwire PD to air for 340 days, the PD can still maintain 96% of the initial photocurrent, indicating that the MAPbBr3 microwire PD has excellent stability in air. Zhang et al. fabricated a 2D PEA2PbBr4 single crystal array with a large-size of about 200 mm2 by controlling the evaporation process.38 The UV photoconductor exhibited an extremely low Id of about 1013 A, a large P value of about 105, a superior D* of 1.55 1013 Jones and a fast response time of 0.41/0.37 ms.

All-inorganic perovskites exhibit high photoluminescence quantum yields, narrow half-peak widths, and good thermal stability and have attracted widespread attention.63,64 Liu et al. fabricated the first all-inorganic perovskite CsPbBr3 nanoplatelet based photoconductor. The CsPbBr3 nanoplatelets have large lateral size, perfect crystallinity, no pinholes and superior stability. The CsPbBr3 nanoplatelet photoconductor showed a large R of 34 A W1 and a superior D* of 7.5 1012 Jones.63 Porous single-walled carbon nanostructures such as nanotubes (p-SWCNT) were incorporated with CsPbBr3 nanocrystals which acted as the active layer of PDs.57 By introducing the p-SWCNT in the CsPbBr3 crystal based photoconductor, the charge mobility and mechanical stability were enhanced. The photoconductor showed a high R of 41.0 A W1 with a specific D* of 1.67 1012 Jones. To increase the responsivity of photoconductors, large grain and high crystallinity -CsPbI3 perovskite nanowire arrays were successfully fabricated using an asymmetric-wettability topographical template (Fig. 3g).55 To obtain a stable -phase CsPbI3 perovskite, 10 wt% polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) was added to the perovskite precursor. PVP used as an additive can assist the growth of -phase CsPbI3 nanowires with large grains and low trap densities.65 The CsPbI3 PDs exhibited a remarkable R of 1294 A W1 (Fig. 3h), an excellent D* of 2.6 1014 Jones and a fast response time of 0.85/0.78 ms (Fig. 3i). Additionally, the PVP doped CsPbI3 PDs exhibited superior stability under light illumination, and the photocurrent could maintain 90% of the original value of the PD after 30 days, which presented long-term stability. New lead-free, all-inorganic and ambient stable perovskite CsBi3I10 films were synthesized.56 The CsBi3I10 photoconductor exhibited an obviously high R of 21.8 A W1 and an excellent D* of 1.93 1013 Jones upon 650 nm light illumination.

Combining perovskites with narrow-bandgap NIR organic polymers can broaden the absorption spectra covering the UV-vis-NIR region. An organic polymer (poly(diketopyrrolopyrroleterthiophene), PDPP3T) was combined with MAPbI3 to fabricate a photoconductor.59 The MAPbI3/PDPP3T heterojunction could facilitate carrier separation, and the Id of the hybrid PD was efficiently suppressed. The photoconductor achieved excellent responsivity in the UV-vis-NIR region with an R value of 0.15 A W1 under 835 nm light. Besides, the device exhibited a peak D* of 8.8 1010 Jones at 835 nm. Additionally, the PDPP3T layer could protect the MAPbI3 film from the intrusion of O2 and H2O, thereby improving the stability of the device.

3.2. Perovskite photodiodes

Perovskite photodiodes are photovoltaic-type detectors as illustrated in Fig. 2b. The perovskite generated electronhole pairs under light illumination, and electrons and holes are separated by the built-in electric field in the junction and collected by the electrodes, subsequently (Fig. 2e). Since the distance between the electrodes is determined by the active layers which is always lower than 1 m, photodiodes always have fast response times compared to photoconductors and phototransistors. For perovskite photodiodes, interfacial materials including hole transporting layers (HTLs) and electron transporting layers (ETLs) are always used to facilitate the separation and collection of charge carriers. Commonly used HTLs include organic polymer materials poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):poly(styrenesulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS) and poly(bis(4-phenyl)(2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)amine) (PTAA), inorganic oxides NiOx, Al2O3, and MoO3, organic small molecules Spiro, etc. Commonly used ETLs include inorganic oxides TiOx, SnO2, and ZnO and small organic molecules [6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM), C60, etc.

Yang et al. first fabricated a perovskite photodiode adopting MAPbI3xClx as a light absorbing material via a solution-process method as displayed in Fig. 4a.66 PEDOT:PSS was used as an HTL and PCBM was used as an ETL, which can facilitate charge extraction and improve photocurrent. However, the Id of the photodiode was very large because PCBM cant block hole injection under reverse bias. BCP and PFN were selected as cathode buffer layers, which can block holes and extract electrons, and the devices were marked as PD2 and PD3. Both the PD2 and PD3 devices presented lower Id and higher detectivity than PD1, and PD3 exhibited the lowest Id and highest detectivity (Fig. 4b). Finally, the PD3 device presented a superior EQE of 80%, and an excellent D* of 1014 Jones with a fast response time of 180 ns (Fig. 4c). Shen et al. fabricated a PD using PTAA and C60 as the HTL and ETL via a solution process method.67 The hydrophobic PTAA was conducive to forming perovskites with large grains and fewer grain boundaries.83 The C60 has higher electron mobility than PC61BM, which can improve the electron extraction capability of the PD. 30 nm C60 can completely cover the perovskite layer and C60 plays an important role in reducing the surface traps of the perovskite film, resulting in a low dark current, a large D* of 7.8 1012 Jones and an ultrafast response time of 1 ns. Large size (up to millimeters) MAPbBr3 and MAPbI3 thin single crystals were grown on PTAA to form vertically structured pin photodiodes.68 The hydrophobic PTAA allows the perovskite solution to be spread out quickly on the plane, so that thin single crystals of the perovskite can grow with lateral dimensions ranging from hundreds of microns to several millimeters. Due to the fewer traps and no grain boundary of the thin single crystals, the corresponding PDs showed ultralow Id and a superior specific D* of 1.5 1013 Jones with a fast response time of 100 ns. Quasi-two-dimensional (Q-2D) perovskite PEA2MAn1PbnI3n+1 films were adopted to fabricate photodiodes as displayed in Fig. 4d.37 The Q-2D perovskite exhibited a better crystal phase and environmental stability than the MAPbI3 perovskite. When n = 1, the PEA2PbI4 perovskite photodiode presented the lowest Id as illustrated in Fig. 4e. When n = 60, the Q-2D perovskite photodiode exhibited a low dark current density of 1.76 107 A cm2, an R of 0.53 A W1 (Fig. 4f), and a high D* of 2.20 1012 Jones.

To achieve broadband detection, low optical bandgap FASnI3 or MASnI3 perovskites were blended with MAPbI3 perovskites as light absorbing layers. Li et al. fabricated a broadband spectral (FASnI3)0.6(MAPbI3)0.4 perovskite photodiode.74 By adjusting the thickness of the perovskite films to 1000 nm, the (FASnI3)0.6(MAPbI3)0.4 photodiode showed enlarged grains, a suppressed Id of 3.9 nA, a superior EQE of above 65% and a remarkable D* of 1.1 1012 Jones in the NIR region. Xu et al. employed the ascorbic acid (AA) antioxidant additive to fabricate a MA0.5FA0.5Pb0.5Sn0.5I3 perovskite; the perovskite film showed a broad absorption spectrum from 350 to 1000 nm.75 The excellent antioxidant property of the AA additive can facilitate perovskite film formation and suppress the Sn oxidation, thus improving the stability of perovskite PDs. Another effective strategy to broaden the absorption spectra is to combine low bandgap polymers with perovskites.81,84,85 An organic bulk heterojunction of PTB7-Th:F8IC was spin-coated on MAPbI3 to form a perovskite photodiode (Fig. 4g).82 The MAPbI3/PTB7-Th:F8IC film shows stronger broader absorption from 400 to 1000 nm, leading to an EQE covering the NIR region with a peak of 54% at 850 nm (Fig. 4h). Owing to the excellent electron mobility of F8IC and the matched energy levels of F8IC and the MAPbI3 perovskite, the PD realized effective charge separation and fast charge transport, thereby achieving an ultrafast response time of 5.6ns (Fig. 4i).

3.3. Perovskite phototransistors

A phototransistor is a field effect transistor (FET) in the dark as shown in Fig. 2c. The phototransistor can be turned on and off by applying a gate voltage to modulate the number of carriers in the channel. The off-state current of the phototransistor is usually relatively low in the dark. Under light, the perovskite generates electronhole pairs; only one type of carrier can be modulated and can participate in channel conduction (Fig. 2f). The accumulated carriers can be swept from the source to drain under the applied gate voltage with low recombination; therefore, phototransistors usually show low Id, superior responsivity and outstanding detectivity. 4. Application of perovskite PDs in artificial photonic synapses

The human brain is the control center of the nervous system which contains about 1011 neurons connected by about 1015 synapses that have excellent computing capabilities, such as high parallelism, low power consumption, and fault tolerance, which can outperform in many computer tasks.108,109 Synapses are the junctions between adjacent neurons that are dedicated to signal transmission. The most important attribute of synapses is plasticity, which represents changes in synaptic weight that is responsible for learning and memory. Artificial synaptic devices that integrated information transmission and data storage can greatly enhance the calculation speed, which has attracted the widespread attention of researchers. Artificial synaptic devices can realize many synaptic functions, e.g. excitatory postsynaptic current (EPSC), inhibitory postsynaptic current (IPSC), paired-pulse facilitation (PPF), short-term potentiation (STP, STP is a short-term memory of synaptic connections lasting a few minutes or less), long-term potentiation (LTP, LTP is a long-term memory lasting for several hours or long), spike-timing dependent plasticity (STDP), and spike-rate dependent plasticity (SRDP).110112

Perovskite optoelectronic devices usually present significant hystereses, and are very precious for artificial synapses used to mimic habituation-based plasticity, sensory adaptation and artificial vision systems.113116 In 2016, Xu et al. first demonstrated MAPbBr3 perovskites as active materials to realize synaptic functions using electrical stimuli.116 Then, perovskite memristors are developed to obtain various synaptic behaviors using electrical stimuli.117 However, electrical stimulation synaptic devices usually have fixed coupling coefficients and slower neural operation speed, which cant further simulate the complex activities of real organisms.116,118,119 Besides, it's hard to realize the interconnection of multiple synaptic devices because of the need for additional electrodes to apply voltages at the same time. In contrast, light-stimulated synaptic devices have large bandwidths, low power consumption, and low crosstalk that can greatly increase the computing speed.107,120,121 Furthermore, photonic synaptic devices can also respond to electrical stimuli, which can realize the synergistic effect of light and electrical stimuli to achieve complex and outstanding multifunctional synaptic behaviors. So, many efforts have been made towards developing photonic synaptic devices.122124 Perovskites have exotic advantages attributed to their large absorption coefficients, solution processability, charge trapping effects and a variety of material components, which are very suitable for fabricating photonic synaptic devices. 5. Conclusion and outlook

In this review, the recent progress of perovskite photodetectors and their application in artificial photonic synapses are summarized and discussed. Perovskite photoconductors can be easily fabricated, and the performance of the photoconductors can be improved by controlling the morphology of the perovskite. Single crystal or low-dimensional nanostructure perovskites have fewer grain boundaries and low trap densities that are expected to fabricate high performance photodetectors with high responsivity and high detectivity. For example, large grain, good crystallinity and stable -CsPbI3 perovskite nanowire arrays were used as active layers to promote the responsivity of the device to 1294 A W1 with a superior detectivity of 2.6 1014 Jones.65 For perovskite photodiodes, the preparation process of the devices is relatively complicated. The commonly used strategy is to modify the interface of the device to reduce the contact barrier, which can passivate traps at the interface, improve charge separation and extraction efficiency, suppress dark current, and obtain a fast response time. For instance, the hydrophobic PTAA is conducive to the growth of perovskites with large grains, and high mobility C60 can passivate the surface traps of perovskites and guarantee fast electron extraction, resulting in a low dark current and a fast response time of 1 ns.83 Perovskite phototransistors can effectively promote the separation of charge carriers by gate voltage modulation, which usually present low dark current and high responsivity. Both 2D perovskite PEA2SnI4 and 3D perovskite FASnI3 phototransistors can achieve remarkable responsivity values up to 1 104 A W1. To further improve the responsivity and detectivity of photodetectors, high-mobility 2D materials (graphene, MoS2) or organic semiconductor materials are introduced to integrate with perovskites that produce the photogating effect and achieve high responsivity. In general, to realize a photodetector with wavelength selectivity, the bandgap of the perovskite can be tuned over a broad spectral range by adjusting the chemical composition. To obtain a broad spectral response range to the NIR region, narrow bandgap polymers are combined with perovskites. To improve the stability of 3D perovskite, all-inorganic perovskite, and quasi-2D perovskite detectors are developed. To reduce the toxicity of lead-based devices, many environmentally friendly lead-free perovskite materials have been developed.

Perovskite detectors have superior light responsivity, excellent detectivity, hysteresis characteristics and charge trapping effects that provide optical modulation of artificial photonic synapses. Current research on perovskite photonic synapses focuses on simulating the learning, forgetting, habit and visual memory behaviors of the human brain. The application of perovskite materials with different chemical compositions and structures in photonic synapses has been explored. Many synaptic functions have been realized, including EPSC, PPF, STP/LTP and STDP. Hao et al. reported a DPPDTT/CsPbBr3 QD SPT that realized the AND/OR logic function and can imitate human visual memory.130 He et al. reported a SPT with a structure of Si/SiO2/PVP:CsPbBr3 QDs/PVDT-10/Au, which certified the Pavlov classical conditioning experiment using all-light stimulation.131 Pradhan et al. demonstrated a grapheneperovskite ultrathin photonic synapse transistor, which can realize facial recognition.99 Our group developed a 2D perovskite/organic heterojunction (PEA2SnI4/Y6) ambipolar SPT which can work in dual-mode and realize the recognition of visible light and NIR light for the first time.132 However, perovskite photonic synapse devices still face huge challenges. (1) Most perovskite photonic synapse devices still have large power consumption and low processing efficiency. (2) There are fewer devices that can simulate light of different colors, and it is still a challenge to efficiently simulate NIR light. (3) Perovskite photonic synapses that can simulate weak light are very important to improve the recognition rate of images; however, there is still a lack of such devices to realize neuromorphic networks for image recognition. (4) It is difficult for a single perovskite photonic synaptic device to detect sensory information. To solve these problems, more synaptic devices with new materials and structures need to be explored. Combining perovskites with 2D materials, organic semiconductor materials, and other types of new materials can improve concurrent signal processing and realize more synaptic behaviors. It is necessary to design and integrate synaptic devices to process sensory information with large scale parallelism and ultralow energy consumption.
