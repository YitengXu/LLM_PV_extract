Abstract

Coupling electrochemical CO2 reduction with renewable energy sources is one of the best ways to minimize the carbon footprint in the atmosphere. Viable technology can be designed with the aid of an electrocatalyst which can be easily scalable and selectively reduces CO2 at lower overpotentials, i.e. ought to be energy efficient. In this context, we utilised the microwave polyol method to synthesis and systematically tune the oxygen vacancies of an SnO2â€“CNT composite by varying the particle size. The extent of the oxygen vacancy formed was confirmed with the help of Raman, XPS and EPR studies. The oxygen vacancy has the ability to lower the activation barrier for CO2Ë™âˆ’ formation. Oxygen vacancy variation has altered the onset potential and the selectivity towards formate production of SnO2â€“CNT composites. A distinction in the electrocatalytic activity may arise from the difference in CO2 adsorption and intermediate binding strength and desorption. Among all the catalysts, SnO2â€“CNT pH-11 stands out with the highest faraday and energy efficiency of 76 and 54% towards formate production at âˆ’0.77 V vs. RHE, respectively. This catalyst exhibits an excellent stability and produces 16 mM of formate with âˆ¼60% Faraday efficiency after a long term run of 10 hours.

1. Introduction

The primary source of energy consumption in today's scenario is fossil fuels through which an extensive amount of CO2 is produced as a by-product. The National Oceanic & Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) has reported that the global CO2 concentration in the atmosphere has increased from 387 ppm to 414 ppm in the time span of 10 years. This rising level of CO2 is causing adverse effects on the climate and leads to global warming.1 Hence it is necessary to prevent CO2 emission and also to reduce the CO2 level in the atmosphere. One of the best ways is to convert CO2 into a value added product by electrochemical means.2 Electrochemical reduction of carbon-dioxide can be carried out (i) under ambient temperature and pressure and (ii) product selectivity can be achieved by choosing an appropriate catalyst, potential and electrolyte. Among all the reduction products, formate is preferred over others as it is extensively utilized in the pharmaceutical, leather and steel industries.3 It is a less energy consuming reduction product and hence has a large scope for commercialization. It can be directly used in formic acid fuel cells4 and used as an H2 storage device.5 Since CO2 is a stable molecule, it possesses both thermodynamic and kinetic barriers and so most of the electrodes require a larger overpotential for direct reduction of carbon dioxide which decreases the conversion efficiency. Thus, it is essential to develop an electrocatalyst to make the reduction process industrially viable. An electrocatalyst ought to be selective and should offer a lower overpotential for the CO2 reduction reaction. Furthermore, it must be low cost, easily scalable and energy efficient. Numerous electrocatalysts like metals Sn,6 metal alloys Inâ€“Sn,7 metal oxides SnO28 and hetero atom doped materials (N-doped graphene9) are used for this purpose. Among all of these Sn has been extensively studied because of its abundance, nontoxic nature and low cost along with the fact that the presence of a native oxide layer can improve the selectivity towards formate.10,11 Further many structural modifications like creating size,12 core/shell structures,13 mesoporous,14 grain boundaries,15 structure engineering,16 defect engineering17etc. are employed to improve the efficiency.

Introducing defects especially oxygen vacancies on metal oxide lattices can tune the physical and chemical properties particularly the electronic bonding and band structures of the metal oxides. Due to such a modulation in the intrinsic properties they find wide application in conversion and energy storage devices.18 However, in recent times it has been found that the CO2 activation energy can be lowered with the help of oxygen vacancies and hence it has a profound effect on the catalytic activity of the electrochemical reduction of CO2. Shan Gao et al. proposed a model and correlated the atomic level oxygen vacancies with the carbon-dioxide reduction present in the Co3O4 material.19 Rahman Daiyan et al. revealed that the enhanced activity of m-SnO2 is due to the high oxygen vacancy defects and crystallinity which is enabled by the mesoporous support.20 Zhigang Geng et al. also depicted the enhanced electrochemical reduction of the ZnO nanosheets to CO due to deliberately created oxygen vacancies.21 They adopted a Lamellar inorganicâ€“organic hybrid intermediate strategy, H2 plasma treatment, and flame spray pyrolysis to create oxygen vacancies. Other typical strategies for creating vacancies include hydrogen reduction treatment, active metal reduction, electrochemical reduction, high energy particle bombardment, chemical reduction etc.18 Most of these methods are complicated and adopt sophisticated instruments as well as requiring harsh treatment. Hence, it is quite a demanding and challenging task to create defects in a controlled manner by employing a cost effective and scalable technique. Recently many reports emphasise the utilisation of microwave energy to create defects in the lattice structure of metal oxides. Chang et al. have created oxygen vacancies over TiO2 in a solution of EG and NaOH with microwave irradiation of 125 W for 2 min by using a microwave furnace.22 Similarly Kim et al.23 and Wang et al.24 utilised microwave irradiation to synthesise and introduce defects in grapheneâ€“SnO2 nano particles. Hence, microwaves have the potentiality to introduce defects on the lattice structure of metal oxides which can improve CO2 adsorption on catalytic surfaces. It should be understood here that an efficient electrocatalyst after adsorption of CO2 must aid electron transfer or proton migration through optimum cleavage of Câ€“O or Câ€“H bonds and facilitate the product to desorb from the surface without any hurdles. Meyer et al. demonstrated the dependence of SnO2 particle size loaded onto high conductive carbon supports like carbon and graphene with observed activity. They altered the size and demonstrated the dependence towards obtaining optimum binding energy to adsorb CO2 and desorb the product from the electrocatalyst surface.25 Further Arik Kar et al. demonstrated that the extent of oxygen vacancies can be affected by size and shape of SnO2.26 Considering these facts, we made an attempt to synthesise SnO2 and decorate it on acid-functionalised multiwalled carbon nanotubes (a-CNT) with systematic variation of oxygen vacancies by tuning SnO2 particle size with the aid of microwave irradiation. Multiwalled carbon nanotubes (CNT) have been utilized to increase surface area, prevent aggregation of metal oxides, and to enhance conductivity and charge transfer between metal oxide and CNT. The adopted polyol synthesis has its own advantage of controlling the particle size without the addition of any surfactant or capping agent along with uniform distribution of the nanoparticle over CNT thus preventing agglomeration.27 It is capable of solubilising almost all kinds of metal halides, nitrates salts and it is one of the best methods for the synthesis of nano-particles over other methods. Further microwave irradiation helped us to create oxygen vacancies and the methodology can be adopted for large scale synthesis as the reaction conditions can be easily optimised. Even though there are reports demonstrating SnO2â€“CNT as an electrocatalyst towards reduction of CO2, they were all based on different precursors28 or different loadings of SnO2,29 or by introducing heteroatoms on SnO230 or even by varying the functional group present on CNT31 but they required a high overpotential to reduce CO2 and their faraday efficiencies were quite low. Hence, this is the first report that demonstrates a simple protocol of a microwave assisted polyol method to synthesise SnO2â€“CNT and to embed defects on the SnO2 surface in a controlled manner by varying the size of SnO2. Further variation of oxygen vacancies in SnO2â€“CNT through size alteration towards reduction of carbon-dioxide particularly to formate was studied in detail by analysing the characteristics of the material, their reaction kinetics etc. This protocol can be adopted to create oxygen vacancies in other metal oxides as oxygen vacancies find a vast range of applications in sodium and potassium ion batteries, photo degradation of pollutants, photochemical CO2 reduction, as an electrocatalyst, supercapacitors, and dye sensitised solar cells.18

3.2. Microwave assisted formation of SnO2â€“CNT materials

Polyol synthesis has been used over years to synthesis metal nano particles where polyol itself acts as the solvent and reducing agent. For the synthesis of metal oxides, there must be the presence of water or hydroxyl group along with polyol.48 Here rapid synthesis of SnO2â€“CNT was achieved by hydrolysis followed by reduction of SnCl2 through the microwave polyol method. Ethylene glycol has been chosen as the solvent medium to absorb microwave radiation strongly as it possesses a higher tan Î´ value. The tan Î´ value refers to the ability of a substance to convert microwaves to localized heat.49 Further NaOH helps in ionic conduction and it is believed that dissolved oxygen in basic media helps in oxidizing Sn+2 to Sn+4.50 We propose the formation of SnO2â€“CNT through the following steps which is similar to the mechanism proposed by Ji-Young Kim group for RuO2 on CNT48 â€“ initially, ethylene glycol forms a chelation with Sn+2. During the microwave heating, Sn+2 undergoes hydrolysis in the presence of water and NaOH which in turn adsorbs on the CNT surface through electrostatic interactions with â€“CO and other oxygen functional group present on CNT with Sn2+.51 Subsequent microwave heating converts â€“Sn(OH)2 to SnO2 in basic medium. In this way the acid functionality present in CNT assisted the decoration of SnO2 on CNT. The formation mechanism is supported by FT-IR, XRD and Raman given in Fig. S4â€“S6.â€

3.3. Effect of synthetic procedure in tuning size and oxygen vacancies

A microwave frequency of 2.45 GHz is much lower to break a chemical bond. Thus microwaves effectively heat the material through dielectric heating. Dielectric heating occurs through dipolar polarisation and ionic conduction. Microwave synthesis provides rapid and homogenous heating by eliminating thermal gradients, lowering synthesis time and with low contents of by-products.49 In recent years microwaves have been effectively utilised to synthesis inorganic nano materials and their decoration over nano tubes.52 During microwave irradiation, the equilibrium which is present in the solution is disturbed thereby leading to the entropy driven desorption of lattice creating oxygen defective sites on the lattice of SnO2.53 A control experiment was performed by eliminating the microwave irradiation step during synthesis to understand the influence of microwaves in creating the oxygen vacancy. SnO2â€“CNT without microwave irradiation lacks oxygen vacancies which is confirmed from the O 1s XPS provided in Fig. S7.â€  Usually the populations of oxygen vacancies are varied by changing the temperature,54 synthetic atmosphere55 or time of exposure of gas46 during synthesis. In particular, the population of oxygen vacancies has a direct relation with the amount of reducing agent employed by adopting a chemical reduction process.56,57 Our case is very similar to that of a chemical reduction method. In our synthetic methodology, we kept all the parameters identical except NaOH and with an increase in the quantity of NaOH (i) the reducing power of ethylene glycol increases leading to the increased number of oxygen vacancies from SnO2â€“CNT pH-7 to SnO2â€“CNT pH-13 (ii) stronger alkalinity results which enhances the hydrolysis rate that is responsible for the increase in the particle size when we are raising the pH from 7 to 1358 and (iii) the increased hydrolysis rate increases the water concentration which has the ability to alter the tan Î´ value and it further alters the microwave absorbing capability of the mixture. Factors like microwave irradiation, tan Î´ value, amount of NaOH employed and size of SnO2 have in combination helped us to induce and tune the oxygen vacancy systematically in SnO2â€“CNT composites. This work describes a suitable synthetic procedure to form and decorate SnO2 particles over the surface of CNT with a tuneable size and oxygen vacancy in an environmentally benign method. We extended further to examine their catalytic performance towards CO2 reduction.

3.4. Electrocatalytic reduction of CO2 to formate

Electrocatalytic activities of all the synthesized catalysts were evaluated by cyclic voltammetry in an inert and CO2 atmosphere and are displayed in Fig. 4. From Fig. 4 it can be seen distinctly that the onset potential of the CO2 reduction reaction is slightly positive for SnO2â€“CNT pH-7, SnO2â€“CNT pH-9 and SnO2â€“CNT pH-11 when compared to N2 atmosphere which indicates that CO2 reduction starts prior to water reduction and also suggest that the ability of the adsorption of CO2 on the surface of the developed catalysts has been improved enormously. However, in the case of SnO2â€“CNT pH-13 and bare SnO2 there is no significant shift in the onset potential in N2 and CO2 atmosphere which reveals both water and CO2 compete with each other leading to a lower catalytic activity on these catalysts. Further, it is observed that the CO2 reduction current densities for all catalysts are comparatively larger than their respective current density observed for N2 solution. This strongly suggests the fact that in CO2 atmosphere the current contribution is from both the CO2 reduction and HER but in nitrogen atmosphere it is solely due to HER. The higher activity observed for all the SnO2â€“CNT composites as compared to SnO2 is due to improved CO2 adsorption, facile mass transport, improved electrical conductivity and enhanced charge transfer between SnO2 and CNT. Among them SnO2â€“CNT pH-11 exhibits a higher current density showcasing a better activity which may originate from the observed oxygen vacancy, size and crystallinity in the case of SnO2â€“CNT pH-11.

From the TGA results discussed in the earlier section, it is understood that the loading of SnO2 on CNT is different which might influence the difference catalytic activity. Hence it is important to assess the electrochemical active surface area (ECAS) to identify the loading effect of SnO2 on CNT on catalytic activity. Fig. 6(c) shows the calculated ECAS for all the catalysts by plotting a graph between Î”j vs. scan rate. The ECAS of SnO2â€“CNT pH-7, SnO2â€“CNT pH-9, SnO2â€“CNT pH-11 and SnO2â€“CNT pH-13 are 0.0654, 0.0308, 0.0271, 0.0151 and 0.00780 cm2. The SnO2â€“CNT composites exhibited 10-fold higher surface areas than pure SnO2. This is mainly due to the presence of large surface area CNT which can accommodate more SnO2 particles over the surface. Among the composites, it was found that there is a decrease in ECAS with an increase in particle size but it does not directly enhance the catalytic activity of CO2 reduction which may be entirely controlled by other intrinsic factors. After careful evaluation, we could infer that the catalytic behaviour of SnO2â€“CNT must definitely originate from the defect sites. In our previous discussion, the Raman spectra and the resolved O 1s spectrum of XPS and EPR show the presence of oxygen vacancies. The quantified data are shown in Fig. 3(e). There have been many previous reports insisting that the presence of oxygen vacancies in the catalyst may boost CO2 adsorption and activation. The high density oxygen vacant sites with trapped electrons present on the surface of Sn+4 will act as an active site for CO2 adsorption.17 These oxygen vacancies have the ability to stabilise the CO2Ë™âˆ’ anion radical making the reaction occur at a lower overpotential.19,20 It should be emphasised here from the previous literature that the stabilisation of the catalyst surface after radical formation would become a hurdle for further protonation. The particle size of SnO2 in SnO2â€“CNT pH-11 is 5 nm which may provide a moderate binding energy for both adsorption and desorption processes. The impact of the 5 nm particle size of SnO2 in tuning the binding energy was previously established by Meyer and group.25 Hence the superior catalytic activity of SnO2â€“CNT pH-11 may be due to the increased oxygen vacancies and 5 nm size of SnO2. In order to acquire further insight into their activity, reaction mechanism and the rate determining step, a Tafel plot was drawn for all the catalysts and is shown in Fig. 6(d). Even though there is a difference in the content of the oxygen vacancies, the catalysts SnO2â€“CNT pH-7, SnO2â€“CNT pH-9, SnO2â€“CNT pH-11 and SnO2â€“CNT pH-13 exhibited a Tafel slope of 133, 134, 126 and 150 mV decâˆ’1, respectively, which is very close to 118 mV decâˆ’1 suggesting that the first electron transfer to form the CO2Ë™âˆ’ radical intermediate is the rate determining step. Hence the presence of defects in SnO2â€“CNT does not alter the rate determining step but affects the formate production. It is understood that the formation of HCOOH over SnO2 surface occurs through two types of intermediates COOH* or OCHO*. There is a difference in the binding nature of these two intermediates. The COOH* binds through the C atom while OCHO* binds through the O atom over the catalyst surface which has the ability to affect the selectivity of the product. In our case, the presence of oxygen vacancies preferentially converts Ë™CO2 to the OCHO* intermediate that solely produces formate instead of CO and H2.62 The plausible mechanism for formate production is depicted in Scheme 1. Even though all the catalysts are exhibiting a similar mechanism, a lower Tafel value was achieved for SnO2â€“CNT pH-11 indicating the faster kinetics of CO2 reduction.20 Hence SnO2â€“CNT pH-11 is considered as the best catalyst in which HER is suppressed and is evident from the observed faraday efficiency for formate. 4. Conclusions

In this work, we successfully utilised microwave polyol synthesis to systematically tune the oxygen vacancies of SnO2â€“CNT composites by varying the particle size. Furthermore, the as-synthesised materials were evaluated for the electrochemical reduction of carbon dioxide. It was observed that there exists a direct correlation between variation of oxygen vacancies through size alteration and electrocatalytic reduction of carbon dioxide. Among the various composites, SnO2â€“CNT pH-11 exhibited a higher faraday efficiency of 76.33% with an energy efficiency of 54.4% at a lower potential of âˆ’0.77 V vs. RHE and high stability. The better activity of SnO2â€“CNT pH-11 originated from the combined factors of improved oxygen vacancies and 5 nm size of SnO2 which helped for CO2 reduction by offering a moderate binding energy towards both adsorption and desorption of intermediates. The synthetic methodology provides a simple protocol for creating defects which can be utilised for any other application.
