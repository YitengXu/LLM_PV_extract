Abstract

Electrocatalysis plays a vital role in energy conversion and storage in modern society. Localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) is a highly attractive approach to enhance the electrocatalytic activity and selectivity with solar energy. LSPR excitation can induce the transfer of hot electrons and holes, electromagnetic field enhancement, lattice heating, resonant energy transfer and scattering, in turn boosting a variety of electrocatalytic reactions. Although the LSPR-mediated electrocatalysis has been investigated, the underlying mechanism has not been well explained. Moreover, the efficiency is strongly dependent on the structure and composition of plasmonic metals. In this review, the currently proposed mechanisms for plasmon-mediated electrocatalysis are introduced and the preparation methods to design supported plasmonic nanostructures and related electrodes are summarized. In addition, we focus on the characterization strategies used for verifying and differentiating LSPR mechanisms involved at the electrochemical interface. Following that are highlights of representative examples of direct plasmonic metal-driven and indirect plasmon-enhanced electrocatalytic reactions. Finally, this review concludes with a discussion on the remaining challenges and future opportunities for coupling LSPR with electrocatalysis.

1. Introduction

In recent years, the worsening energy crisis and environmental issues have made the utilization of clean and renewable energy extremely important. By using renewable electricity generated from solar energy or wind, electrocatalysis could be a core technique for green synthesis under ambient conditions. It is also a promising approach to achieve zero-carbon emission for producing value-added chemicals and fuels,1–3 for example, hydrogen-energy and metal–air batteries involving the electrocatalytic HER and OER processes,4–6 hydrocarbons from greenhouse gas CO2 electrolysis,7,8 ammonia or urea by the electroreduction of N2,9,10etc. However, reactions can be limited by their sluggish kinetics, a high overpotential, and undesirable side-reactions. Ideally, the design of an electrochemical interface is the foundation of electrocatalytic synthesis. Various electrocatalysts have been engineered to promote reactions at the electrode/electrolyte interface.

In this scenario, localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) seems to be a powerful approach to improve the electrocatalytic performance and selectivity utilizing UV and visible-light irradiation. Briefly, the LSPR excitation of a metallic nanostructure could be visualized as the co-oscillation of its free conduction electrons coupled with the electromagnetic field of the incoming light. As a result of LSPR excitation, highly energetic electrons or hot holes, a strong electromagnetic field and heat dissipation are generated at the nanostructured surface. Therefore, a wide range of applications in many fields, including nanosensors11–13 and biomedicine and information technologies,14,15 can be opened. The application of the LSPR effect in catalysis is a relatively recent research field,16–20 especially electrocatalysis.21–23 The incorporation of these LSPR features into electrocatalysis has shown unprecedented activity. Using light irradiation, the key parameters of electrocatalysis could be manipulated by reducing the onset potential/overpotential (thermodynamics), accelerating the reaction kinetics or reducing the Tafel slope, lowering the activation energy, enhancing the current density and exchange current density, and altering the reaction path to inhibit undesirable side-products.

LSPR-mediated electrocatalysis relies strongly on the LSPR effect and the electrocatalytic activity of plasmonic electrocatalysts. The LSPR frequency and strength could be simply tuned by varying the size/shape,24,25 component,26 and the interparticle distance of the nanostructures.27,28 The LSPR effects can be encountered directly by the adjacent reactant species at the electrode/electrolyte interface, where pure plasmonic materials act as both light-absorbing and electrocatalytic sites. Alternatively, plasmonic materials can first interact with adjacent electroactive materials (e.g., Pt, Pd, metal oxide/hydroxide, MOF) and semiconductors, and then play an indirect role in the electrocatalytic reactions. The interface of a hybrid composite is critical for interaction of the plasmonic effects with adjacent species such as in the transfer of hot carriers. The size/shape and morphology of plasmonic metal nanostructures and supports could also be controlled to increase the electrochemical surface area, the density of active sites and even the electronic conductivity. Moreover, compared with single-component plasmonic metal nanostructures, the synergistic effect and electronic structures could be tailored for electrocatalytic reactions through alloy formation. Although progress has been achieved for this relatively recent and rapidly growing field, the current state-of-the art catalysts are insufficient in the terms of their current density, turnover frequency (TOF) and stability. The design of active plasmonic electrocatalysts is highly desirable to meet the requirements of renewable energy technology on a large scale.

Thus, we give an overview for the engineering of various LSPR-based nanostructures and electrodes. The preparation process and parameters that affect the obtained structure are presented for improving the activity of the designed materials. Exploiting the intrinsic mechanism for plasmonic-mediated electrocatalysis is still a challenge at the present stage and is important for plasmonic electrocatalyst design. So far, a variety of techniques that assist with experimental and theoretical simulations have been developed to reveal the underlying mechanisms. We highlight our efforts on the characterization techniques used to identify the LSPR effect and differentiate individual LSPR effects at electrochemical interfaces with in situ and ex situ spectroscopy, pairing LSPR with electrochemical methods. Based on the mechanisms of LSPR for electrocatalysis, selected cases for direct pure plasmon metal-driven electrocatalysis and indirect plasmon-enhanced electrocatalysis over hybrid systems are discussed, especially on how the LSPR effects interact with the reactant molecule, the electrocatalytically active species and semiconductors.

2. Physical understanding of LSPR for electrocatalysis

2.1 Fundamentals of LSPR

“Plasmon” describes the collective oscillation of conduction electrons in metals.29 When the electron oscillation is confined on a nanostructure, it is termed LSPR. The LSPR effect is established with nanoparticles that have a diameter much smaller than the wavelength of the light, where the particle can be described as an electric dipole: as shown in Fig. 1, with incoming light irradiation, the electromagnetic field of the light causes the free conduction electron density in the metal nanoparticle to polarize to one surface, creating a net charge difference at the nanoparticle surface (electric dipole). This dipole generates an electric field inside the nanoparticle opposite to that of the light, which in turn acts as a restoring force to drive the electrons returning to the equilibrium position. In this way the electrons oscillate in resonance with the light's frequency, referred to as localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) since it is confined to the nanoparticles and located at the surface.30,31 For non-spherical structures such as nanorods (Fig. 1b), the oscillation is either along or across the rod, corresponding to longitudinal resonance and transverse resonance, respectively.

2.2 Mechanism in plasmonic metal nanostructures for electrocatalysis

The LSPR excitation or evanescent wave excitation will quickly undergo radiative decay (i.e. scattering), that occurs by the re-emission of phonons, and non-radiative decay, which generates hot carriers on a time scale ranging from 1 fs to 100 fs.10,17,18,45–48 The three main physical mechanisms involved in LSPR-assisted electrochemistry are illustrated in Fig. 3A–C. In mechanism A, a high lattice temperature of the plasmonic material is induced by the relaxation of hot carriers via electron–electron (100 fs to 1 ps) and electron–phonon scattering (1 to 10 ps). The local electrolyte temperature slowly increases with this photothermal effect (heat dissipation, 100 ps to 10 ns), which has been utilized to enhance the reaction kinetics or current density in certain electrocatalytic reactions.49 Mechanism B involves a highly enhanced electromagnetic field, which could activate nearby species or generate vacancies in the conduction band of adjacent semiconductors.22,50 The finite-difference time-domain method (FDTD) is usually used to calculate the current enhancement for field-enhanced electrocatalytic activity. In mechanism C, the transfer of hot carriers occurs from the plasmonic nanostructure to an adjacent species, as shown in (Fig. 4C-1–C-6), which is critical for direct plasmon-driven electrocatalysis over pure plasmonic electrocatalysts and indirect plasmon-assisted electrocatalysis over electroactive species or semiconductors.

Hot carrier transfer over pure plasmonic electrocatalysts. Hot electrons transfer to the cathode via an external circuit, while the holes left on the plasmonic material are responsible for oxidation reactions (Fig. 4C-1). At negative potential bias for reduction reactions, hot holes are quenched faster than hot electrons, leading to the accumulation of electrons for reducing the reactants (Fig. 4C-2). For the plasmon-induced hot electron transfer at the interface between the plasmon material and the reactant adsorbate, several transition pathways have been proposed.35,51–53 There is indirect electron transfer via chemical interface scattering (100 fs to 10 ps) and direct electron transfer via chemical interface damping (5 to 100 fs) (Fig. 4, transfer path for C-2).35,51,54 In the indirect process, the decay of resonant plasmons creates hot electron–hole pairs in the metal. The hot electrons with enough energy then transfer to the adsorbate. In the direct process, electrons are resonantly transferred to an unoccupied adsorbate state without the formation of an excited electron distribution in the metal (Fig. 4, transfer path-i). The chemical bond formed by the interaction of the molecule with the metal could reduce the energy gap between the HOMO and LUMO for adsorbates, leading to direct intramolecular excitation from the HOMO to the LUMO (Fig. 4, transfer path-ii).55,56

Hot carrier transfer at the interface between a plasmonic material and electroactive species. Upon LSPR excitation, the hot electrons are swept into the external circuit while the transfer of the remaining hot holes leads to the formation of an active electrocatalyst with a high valence state for oxidation reactions (Fig. 4C-3). The hot electron transfer could induce a change in the charge density distribution and binding energy of the reaction intermediates for transition metallic species (Fig. 4C-4), accelerating the reactions. The transfer of hot electrons can also generate vacancies in transition metal electrocatalysts,57 confirmed by theoretical calculations and the X-ray absorption near edge structure (XANES) technique.
Hot carrier transfer at the interface between a plasmonic material and semiconductors. The electron transfer process at the interface between a plasmonic material and semiconductors is similar as that in the presence of reactants, including the indirect and direct pathways (Fig. 4, transfer path for C-4 and C-5). The hot electrons are transferred to or are generated in the conduction band of semiconductors to participate in reduction reactions, or are transferred to the external circuit, producing a photocurrent and increasing the separated holes for oxidation reactions (Fig. 4C-4 and C-5). It is proposed that direct transfer achieves a higher efficiency than the indirect route for injecting hot electrons to adjacent reactants or semiconductors.51,53 The Schottky barrier formed at the interface of a metal and a semiconductor could facilitate the hot electron transfer process by delaying their return to the plasmonic metal nanostructures (ps to sub-ns scale).35 The electrons transfer to the reactant species upon plasmon decay for p-type semiconductors with large bandgap structure (Fig. 4C-6), whereas holes transfer to the valence band of the semiconductor and then to the external circuit.

In addition, plasmon-induced resonant energy transfer (Fig. 4D) and the scattering effect (Fig. 4E) in some hybrid systems of plasmonic material/semiconductors play a role in electrocatalytic reactions. Plasmon resonant energy transfer occurs when the light absorption of a plasmonic metal overlaps with the absorption of the nearby semiconductor (few nm). The transfer of energy leads to the generation of electron–hole pairs in the semiconductor, extending the carrier creation to a longer wavelength and enhancing the photocurrent.58 The scattering effect is photo-emission toward the medium with a higher refractive index, namely the semiconductor in an aqueous electrolyte, which indeed enhances the light absorption of some semiconductors for electrocatalytic water splitting.59–61

Differentiation of the aforementioned effects will be illustrated in Section 4 that deals with characterization, and the details of how these effects assist in electrocatalysis will be described in Section 5 using selected cases.

3. Preparation methods for LSPR-based electrodes

The successful design of plasmonic materials is critically important for enhancing the electrocatalytic activity. For instance, the optimized particle size, shapes and composition of plasmonic nanostructure achieved high activity for PEC water splitting and the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) (Fig. 5).57,62,63 Au nanoparticles (NPs) with size of 5, 15 or 30 nm were fabricated via a chemical reduction strategy and then adsorbed onto the inner surface of TiO2 nanotube arrays via electrostatic interaction. There was a marked increase in the photocurrent for the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) in the presence of Au NPs and the PEC performance increased with the decrease of particle size (Fig. 5a), attributed to the enhanced transfer of hot carriers from LSPR-excited Au across the Au/TiO2 interface. A designed mixture of deposited Au NPs and adsorbed Au nanorods (NRs) on TiO2 nanowires exhibited enhanced activity for the OER over the entire UV-visible region, caused by the LSPR excitation of both the nanoparticle and nanorod structures (Fig. 5b). The alloy composition of the AgPt electrode was also simply manipulated to optimize the synergistic effect between the light absorber (plasmonic Ag) and the electroactive species (Pt), in turn enhancing the PEC ORR. The optimized AgPt30 gave a rapid and obvious response of suppressing peroxide with the light illumination, which corresponded with the enhanced current density (Fig. 5c).

3.1 Photo/chemical reduction

Pioneering work on photoreduction or photodeposition was reported by Clark and Vondjidis in 1965 and by Kraeutler and Bard in 1978.64,65 For the photodeposition of plasmonic material onto semiconductors, the conduction band position of the semiconductor should match with the redox potential of the plasmonic metal salt solution for photoreduction. Namely, the conduction band of the semiconductor must be more negative than the redox potential of the metal, while the valence band must be more positive than the hole scavengers.66 The most frequently used hole scavengers or sacrificial agents are H2O,67–73 MeOH,74–77 and EtOH.70,78–82 The metal salt precursor and the irradiation time have an influence on the morphology and particle size of the supported plasmonic metal nanostructures. A high precursor concentration and a long irradiation time always develop a large particle size. A larger particle size shows a lower electrocatalytic activity for water splitting over photodeposited Au NPs.67,83 Repeating the photodeposition step will make a difference, leading to larger Au NPs with a smaller particle number density, whereas smaller Au NPs with a larger particle number density are obtained via single-step photodeposition under UV light irradiation (Fig. 7a).68 In addition, the plasmonic nanostructures could be selectively photodeposited on immobilized semiconductor substrates.84–86 For example, needle-like Au nanostructures were control-deposited on a TiO2 thin film by altering the UV light intensity and the irradiation time.84 The extremely low UV light intensity could allow the photoreduction of Au3+ by photogenerated electrons from the TiO2 film surface while preventing the uncontrolled photoreduction of Au3+ in the solution or the formation of Au aggregates.

In the absence of a semiconductor, separated plasmonic bi-metal or alloy nanostructures could also be easily loaded via photoreduction to promote the catalytic activity. Ag NPs were deposited inside light-excited Au nanohole arrays using the LSPR effect (Fig. 7b).72 Supports with a high conductivity and specific surface area were chosen to facilitate catalytic reactions. Small AuPd and AuAg alloy NPs on graphene oxide (GO) and a ZnCr layered double hydroxide (LDH) were reported by Parida's group.80,87 For the formation of alloy NPs, they proposed that H+ from the HAuCl4 precursor could interact with the amine groups forming the positively charged ammonium ion (Fig. 7c). The negative ion (AuCl4−) first bonded to the positively charged support surface since Ag or Pd was in the form of positive ions (AgNO3 and PdCl2). Then Ag+ or Pd2+ ions attached to the negatively charged Au particles via metallic bonds, resulting in the formation of AuAg or AuPd bimetallic alloys. Furthermore, the first reduction of AuCl4− was decided by its high redox potential.

In chemical reduction, a reducing agent is required instead of the photogenerated electrons used in the photoreduction method. Strong (e.g. NaBH4,88–94 H295–97) and weak reducing agents (e.g. alcohols98–100 and HCHO101) with/without stabilizers have been widely investigated in engineering-supported Au, Ag and Cu nanostructures. Compared with NaBH4, using a weak reducing agent it is much easier to manipulate the obtained structure, e.g., interparticle distance,101 in turn tuning the plasmon effect on electrocatalysis. Seed-mediated chemical reduction is also widely applied in the design of various colloidal plasmonic metallic nanostructures in the absence of a support, and is the first step for assembling an LSPR-based electrode via a post-treatment strategy. Besides the electroactivity enhancement, alloy formation could inhibit the oxidation of plasmonic monometallic nanoparticles with low stability. Shiraishi's group fabricated the supported AuCu alloy (3.4 nm) via H2 reduction and found that the surface Cu atoms oxidized during the reaction could be reduced by plasmon-activated Au atoms, which maintained alloy effect and the high activity.97

3.2 Deposition–precipitation

Deposition–precipitation (DP) is a technique for depositing metal catalysts on supports by varying the pH of the precursor solution. A variety of precipitating bases (NaOH,102–105 Na2CO3,106,107 and urea108–112), pH values and calcination temperature have been investigated for the DP method.

Since the particle size is a key parameter that must be considered to guide the design of plasmonic electrocatalysts, pH values should be carefully controlled for the DP method. The pH exhibited a significant effect on the Au particle size at pH > 6 where the main Au complexes were transformed from AuCl4− to [Au(OH)nCl4−n]− (n = 1–3), utilizing NaOH as the precipitating base.113 Different from NaOH or Na2CO3 agents that were discovered by Haruta's group for preparing supported Au NPs,114,115 urea will decompose by heating (60–90 °C) and form NH4+ and OH−, i.e., CO(NH2)2 + 3H2O → CO2 + 2NH4+ + 2OH−, leading to the pH increase of the precursor solution. Then, gold or Cu precursors could be deposited as ammonia complexes.112,116

The DP processes are always followed by annealing to obtain a catalyst in the metallic state as well as to enhance the interaction between the support and the LSPR metal nanostructures.117 Strong interfaces by annealing could facilitate the plasmon-induced hot electron transfer at the plasmonic metal/semiconductor interface to achieve high-performance electrocatalysis.118 However, a high annealing temperature could cause the growth of LSPR metal NPs.102,108,119–121 Thus, to achieve a high activity for the plasmonic electrocatalysts, both parameters should be balanced. Manipulating the annealing conditions might be a good choice to increase the interface interaction while maintaining a similar particle size since some reports have shown that the growth of the Au particle size induced by an increase in the calcination temperature was not drastic, especially under an H2 atmosphere.120 The annealing process also has an influence on the position of the Au NPs.119,122 As shown in Fig. 8a, Au NPs were formed mainly on the rutile surface upon calcination at 200 °C and migrated to the anatase/rutile interface at high temperature (300–400 °C). When the calcination temperature was higher than 400 °C, larger Au NPs (>5 nm) were formed, reducing the catalytic activity.

Similar to the photo/chemical reduction strategy, alloy formation via DP also leads to a small particle size compared with the monometallic counterpart.111,121 The DP method usually achieves a smaller particle size than photodeposition.122 These three strategies were investigated for fabricating plasmonic Au–TiO2 catalysts.103 The photodeposition method developed a larger Au particle size (41 nm) than the DP method (10–16 nm, 200–600 °C). The photodeposition method also gave larger Au NPs than the chemical reduction method using NaBH4 (8.7 nm). As illustrated in Fig. 8b–d, the photo-reduction was incomplete, leading to a mixture of Au0/Au3+ with a relatively low Au content compared with the other two methods.

3.3 Electrodeposition

Electrodeposition is an easy, scalable, and inexpensive technique for the in situ controllable preparation of LSPR-based electrodes. Compared with other strategies, however, it is not favorable for small nanoparticle confinement, especially for nanostructures with sizes of a few nm to tens of nm. By simply tuning the applied potential/current, adjusting the precursor concentration, adding additives, or varying the substrates during electrodeposition, the surface morphology of plasmonic Au, Ag and Cu could be controlled.123–135

Although the formation mechanism for some metal nanostructure systems is still under investigation, a general trend can be summarized. The applied potential or current plays an important role both thermodynamically and kinetically. At a high overpotential/current, there are more nucleation sites and a faster deposition rate. Also, under equilibrium or near-equilibrium conditions, the nanostructure develops a simple morphology with a minimum surface energy. At more negative potentials or with a limited precursor concentration, the deposited nanostructures possess complicated shapes since mass transport and surface kinetics play major roles in determining the shapes. These complicated shapes possess a high density of catalytic sites (dendrites, 3D nanopores) that are favorable for electrocatalysis, such as electrodeposited plasmonic Ag dendrites for enhanced EC CO2 reduction.136 Recently, a hetero-nanoelectrode of highly ordered arrays of Au NRs and MoS2 quantum sheets was fabricated via sequential electrodeposition (Fig. 9a).118 The gap between the nanorods was adjusted (20 nm) to enable the scattering effect/reabsorption to improve the light absorption of Au–Au–MoS2. Hot electron injection from excited Au to the conduction band of MoS2 also lead to a broad and strong absorption in the visible-light range. Both effects result in a high performance in the plasmon-enhanced electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution reaction (HER).

3.4 Vapor deposition

Chemical vapor deposition (CVD) or physical vapor deposition (PVD) could easily produce a uniform film of plasmonic metal nanostructures, directly being applied in plasmon-enhanced electrocatalysis. 3 nm Au film was loaded on SrTiO3 substrate via e-beam electrodeposition by Misawa group for enhancing N2 fixation under visible-light irradiation.137–139 The light-excited Ag thin film cathode fabricated via this technique also exhibited high selectivity for electrochemical CO2 reduction.140,141

Uniformly dispersed plasmonic metal nanoparticles could be obtained via PVD and sequential annealing or an electrochemical process. As shown in Fig. 10a and b, Atwater and co-workers designed ∼8 nm plasmonic Au and Cu NPs on p-type semiconductors via electron-beam evaporation for LSPR-enhanced electrochemical CO2 reduction.142,143 A 1.5-nm-thick Au discontinuous film was initially deposited on a p-GaN substrate via PVD, followed by annealing at a controlled temperature and time to ensure the coalescence of the discontinuous film to ∼8 nm nanoparticles (Fig. 10a). The thickness of these deposited films will determine the final particle size and density. The plasmon-excited Au NPs with an optimized size and density could significantly enhance the electron mobility, exhibiting the highest applied bias photon-to-current efficiency for electrocatalytic water splitting.144

Recently, polystyrene beads were used as a template to manipulate the plasmon metal structures via vapor deposition. The plasmon-enhanced activity could be competitive with that on Pt electrodes which are known as the most active metal for the ORR.146

The PVD approach with the gas aggregation source (GAS) has also been utilized to produce various plasmonic nanoparticles, even alloy nanoparticles with controlled compositions. Faupel's group deposited plasmonic AuAg alloy NPs from custom-built multicomponent targets via GAS and enhanced the composition control using in operando optical emission spectroscopy.16,147,148 This will be a useful technique for preparing self-supported electrodes that are directly utilized in LSPR-enhanced electrocatalysis.

3.5 Post-treatment processes

The post-treatment or two-step process is favorable for fabricating LSPR-based electrodes with tunable sizes, morphologies and even multi-metallic nanostructures. So far, a great deal of research has been devoted to engineering LSPR-based electrodes via the two-step strategy for electrocatalytic reactions.57,149–162 In the first step, various plasmonic materials are usually fabricated via the seed-mediated chemical reduction method. The second step is assembly of the electrode through chemical/electrostatic adsorption or drop casting/spin coating, described as follows.
3.5.1 Chemical/electrostatic adsorption. Strong adsorption occurs when chemical bonds are formed between ligands and the LSPR-based metal, such as Au–S and Au–N bonds.163–166 A TiO2 nanowire array electrode was functionalized using mercaptopropionic acid ligands with the carboxylic acid group attached to TiO2 and the thiol group exposed outwards. This functionalized electrode is capable of adsorbing Au NRs via Au–S bonds,63 and effectively enhanced the PEC oxygen evolution reaction (Fig. 5b). 3-Aminopropyl-triethoxysilane (APTES) and 3-aminopropyl-trimethoxysilane (APTMS) are ligands well known for the fabrication of Au@SiO2 sandwich and core–shell structures via Au–N bonds.165–167 In addition, the thickness of the SiO2 shell could be tuned via the concentration of the precursor such as Na2SiO3/TEOS (Fig. 11a).168 The tunable thickness of this insulating layer plays an important role in plasmon-mediated electrocatalysis since a non-reactive shell is required to avoid the catalytic effects of plasmonic nanostructures or to block the transfer of hot carriers for mechanistic investigations.165,169

The positively charged ligands poly(allylamine hydrochloride) (PAH) and cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB), and the negatively charged ligands poly(sodium styrene sulfonate) (PSS) and polyacrylic acid (PAA) were used to functionalize plasmonic metal and supports for fabricating plasmonic catalysts.170,171 As shown in Fig. 11b,172 Au NPs on CNTs were successfully obtained via the electrostatic interaction between PAH and PSS. After functionalization, the charge state of the Au NPs@PAH determined via the zeta potential was +22.7 mV whereas it was −53.4 mV for CNTs@PSS (Fig. 11c). The light-excited Au NPs enhanced the activity for the methanol oxidation reaction (MOR) and there was an increment in the current density after removing the PAH-PSS linker layer through annealing, which indicated that the interface layers had a negative effect on the electrocatalytic activity. A small gap was also observed between the TiO2 nanowire and Au NRs for the presence of other linker molecules (e.g. mercaptopropionic acid63). However, the presence of Au NRs indeed assisted the charge transfer for the PEC OER. A similar phenomenon was observed for the insulating ethylenediamine spacer between Au NPs and glassy carbon, where the resistance of charge transfer across the insulating layer was not limited.154 Thus, the negative effect caused by the insulating linker molecule should be considered when engineering a plasmonic electrocatalyst via ligand absorption.

3.5.2 Other post-treatment methods. Various colloidal LSPR metal nanostructures could be post-treated for an electrode via self-assembly173,174 or spin coating/drop casting.175 Spin coating/drop casting is also utilized for supported LSPR catalysts,176–179 where a Nafion solution is sometimes added to promote adhesion.91,180,181 Generally, a slow evaporation rate is favorable for a uniform film via drop casting. For spin coating, the rotation speed could be adjusted to obtain a uniform film.

4. Characterization of plasmonic electrocatalysts for electrochemical interfaces

The characterization of plasmonic electrocatalysts and the interface for multi-metal nanostructures, a plasmonic metal/support, and an electrode/electrolyte plays a critical role in the design of the material and mechanistic investigations of plasmonic electrocatalysis.

LSPR metal nanostructures might be oxidized during electrocatalysis. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is a powerful measurement technique for identifying the oxidation states of elements. High-resolution TEM (HRTEM) is applied in analyzing individual planar fringes, crystal interfaces, interphase boundaries or crystallographic orientation relationships among different crystal phases.88,194 Early this year, using aberration-corrected environmental TEM, the in situ manipulation of the epitaxial rotation of Au NPs on TiO2 from S‖ to S= or the atomic configuration at the Au–TiO2 interface was achieved by changing the external environment (Fig. 13).195 We believe that greater achievements will be made for plasmon-mediated electrocatalysis with development of the technique at the electrode/electrolyte interface.

The temperature distribution map for a plasmonic nanostructure/electrolyte interface at different time points is also an effective technique for monitoring the photothermal effect. Szunerits's group mapped the temperature of the Au nanohole electrode at different time increments,146 from near 0 s to equilibrium (20 s). The results confirmed that the heating started near the light-excited plasmonic Au and then diffused to the surrounding medium (e.g., H2O), as shown in Fig. 14f, promoting the reaction rate. Halas's group observed a temperature upwards of 175 °C on the surface of Al2O3-suppored Al nanocrystals using high-resolution spatial and temporal mapping at a high light intensity (Fig. 14g).209 Through a comparison of this temperature profile with optical absorption and field enhancement, however, they found that this photothermal effect did not play a major role in the photocatalytic reverse water–gas shift reaction.

4.3 In situ XAS/XANES for the electron occupancy

In situ XAS or XANES is a powerful technique for tracking the electronic states of plasmonic electrocatalysts with and without illumination,50,57,206,210,211 offering further insight into the LSPR effects on the adjacent electroactive species or semiconductors via their interaction. For the designed plasmonic Ag–Pt bimetallic nanocages, the in situ XAS/XANES approach was used to offer a meaningful indication of Pt d-band character, which comprised mainly Pt5d states,57 since the Pt L3-edge absorption involved the transition from the 2p to 5d states. Under light illumination, AgPt with an optimized ratio showed the highest intensity (Fig. 15a), i.e., the presence of more vacancies in the 5d state and hot electrons transferred from plasmon-excited Ag to the Pt active center, leading to the highest performance in the EC ORR. The peak XANES intensity is also related to the electron occupancy of semiconductors and then the electron transferred to their conduction band could be determined (Fig. 15b). Using in situ XANES at the Zn K-edge (Fig. 15c) and DFT calculations, in addition to LSPR-induced hot electron transfer, it was proved that LSPR-induced field enhancement could modify the band structure at the Au/ZnO interface leading to the rapid transportation of photogenerated electron–hole pairs.50

4.4 Coupling LSPR with electrochemical methods

4.4.1 Electrochemistry to differentiate LSPR effects. Together with the above theoretical simulations, electrochemistry-related characterization has been utilized with the aim of determining the LSPR mechanism in electrocatalysis. Hot electron transfer was experimentally verified by plotting the photocurrent against the wavelength. As shown in Fig. 16a, comparing the LSPR absorption spectrum of Au NPs against the photocurrent plotted versus the wavelength, fitted to Fowler's law,50,212 it can be seen that LSPR can promote hot electron flow and that hot electron injection plays an important role in PEC water splitting.50 The LSPR-induced photocurrent was also compared with the quality factor, defined as Q = LSPR peak energy/line width, to illustrate the function of the local field enhancement.213,214 Large values of Q indicate a high local field enhancement. Huber's group measured the photocurrent and quality factor over different Au nanodot sizes for PEC water splitting (Fig. 16b).214 The direct proportional relationship indicated that the enhanced water splitting performance with the decreased Au nanodot size was induced by the local field enhancement as the quality factor increased. The wavelength-dependent IPCE measurements over engineered TiO2–Au@TiO2/Al2O3/Cu2O confirmed the scattering mechanism in the UV range and the plasmon-induced resonant energy transfer (PIRET) mechanism in the visible-near-IR range (Fig. 16c).60

Efforts have been made to differentiate the photothermal effect from other LSPR effects, which are usually realized through investigating the electrochemical parameters by elevating the electrolyte temperature, via external heating, to be equal to the temperature induced by the LSPR effect. In some reports, the photothermal effect contributes only to the current enhancement and not to the potential shift.152,172 For example, from cyclic voltammetry (CV) scans at different temperatures and the corresponding peak potential plotted against the peak current density, the thermal effect of plasmonic Ag only induced a current enhancement for the Pt-catalyzed MOR (Fig. 16d). They differentiated the photothermal effect from the non-thermal effect by comparing the results under light illumination, where both the enhanced current density and the potential shift were observed. Both the higher current and the potential shift induced by the photothermal effect were also observed in some reports.169 However, some reported that the photothermal effect had no influence on either the potential shift or the current density.215,216 By measuring the temperature near the Au/GCE surface and in the bulk electrolyte under different laser light intensities, Jain's group systematically investigated the photothermal effect on solution resistance, electron transfer resistance, current density and onset potential using the HER as the model reaction.154 They found that the photothermal effect contributed little to the current density enhancement or to the overpotential shift, but it indeed reduced the solution resistance. In addition, there are investigations on the activation energy derived from CV at different temperatures, utilizing the Arrhenius equation and kinetic data.146,217 Elevated temperatures in the dark could increase the reaction rate, which could be promoted further with light irradiation. Thus, a lower activation energy could be achieved with light compared with in the dark. Regarding the controversial results for different systems reported, more efforts should be made to reveal the underlying mechanism. Moreover, attention should be paid to how to keep the reaction conditions, such as the pressure (P) and gas volume (V), unchanged when elevating the electrolyte temperature.

There are also other examples of home-made electrochemical setups to differentiate LSPR effects.218 The individual role of LSPR effects is dependent on the designed system and could be discerned with the assistance of various electrochemical characterization methods, theoretical methods, in situ spectroscopy and other techniques.

4.4.2 In situ plasmonic spectroscopy integrated with electrochemistry. An interdisciplinary technique combining in situ plasmonic spectroscopy and electrochemistry is necessary for investigating the role of plasmonic nanostructures and the evolution of intermediates at plasmonic metal surfaces or electrode/electrolyte interfaces during reactions.141,219–224 The in situ local electrochemical current images were recorded using an LSPR-excited Au electrode and a charge coupled device (CCD) camera (Fig. 17a).221 This setup gave vivid optical images of the reaction evolution with different applied potentials (i–vi), indicating whether the reaction took place or when it achieved the maximum reaction current. With the assistance of in situ dark-field scattering spectroscopy measurements under potential control over an Au electrode (Fig. 17b),222 it was confirmed that the generated hot electrons were responsible for the plasmon-enhanced EC oxidation of glucose. The technique of in situ SERS spectra integrated with electrochemistry was established by Tian's group for plasmonic Ag electrode-driven hydrodehalogenation (Fig. 17c).223 Changes in the peaks using EC-SERS (i) gave information on C–Br bond cleavage and the formation of new bonds during the reaction. Time series EC-SERS spectral mapping showed that the plasmonic-mediated EC hydrodehalogenation reaction was accelerated at a shorter laser wavelength (ii–iii). In situ ATR-FTIR spectroscopy at different potentials under dark and light conditions was also developed recently and confirmed that light could assist the desorption of CO from the plasmonic Ag surface, facilitating EC CO2 reduction.141

5.1 Direct LSPR-driven electrocatalysis

LSPR metal alone could act as both a light absorbent and a catalytically active center for directly enhancing the electrocatalytic activity. Thus, the dimensions and the morphology of a free-standing plasmonic metallic electrode show a significant effect on the performance.227,228 Various conductive supports could also be utilized for loading LSPR metal nanostructures including carbon materials (glassy carbon, carbon, graphene, etc.), Ti film, FTO and ITO substrates. Employing pure LSPR metal as model system, the mechanism has been further investigated to identify the contributions of LSPR effects including photogenerated electrons, hot holes, local electromagnetic fields and photothermal effects to the electrocatalytic activity in the absence of a Schottky junction.
5.1.1 LSPR-enhanced electrocatalytic activity of pure plasmonic metal. Utilizing glucose oxidation as a model system, Xia's group demonstrated that plasmonic Au NPs on glassy carbon (GC) directly accelerated the electrocatalytic reaction upon LSPR excitation. They investigated the underlying mechanism via experiments on the light wavelength, the electrolyte pH and using dark-field scattering spectroscopy integrated with the electrochemistry technique.222 As illustrated in Fig. 18a and b, they proposed that photogenerated carriers (hot electrons) on LSPR-excited Au NPs either recombined with photogenerated holes (i), took part directly in electrochemical reduction reactions (ii), or transferred to the external circuit (iii). Appling a potential above the onset potential, (iii) occurred and generated a photocurrent. At this suitable voltage bias, the hot holes were driven to the surface of the Au NPs and those with a matching energy level could oxidize glucose, which inhibited the recombination of hot electrons and holes. The proposed plasmonic-enhanced mechanism was also successfully applied to other electrochemical oxidation reactions including the oxidation of ascorbic acid, ethanol, ethylene glycol, and glycerol. They also found that highly alkaline conditions enhanced the photoanodic current density due to the fast capture of hot holes by OH−. Kim and co-workers reported a synergistic effect between surface plasmon polariton (SPP) of an Au film and the LSPR effect of Au nanobipyramids for enhanced electrocatalytic glycerol oxidation enabled by a higher concentration of hot holes.160 The contributions of hot holes generated from interband transitions and around the Fermi level for the photooxidative dissolution of Au NRs were distinguished by Link, Landes and co-workers.229 The hot holes generated in the d-band of the Au NRs were able to overcome the higher activation barrier of electrochemical oxidation reactions, which is too high for the hot holes around the Femi level. Manipulating the multi-metallic component of LSPR metals is able to further enhance their electroactivity due to an efficient LSPR effect, a synergistic effect and an electronic effect between different LSPR metals.156,230

In addition to efficient hot carrier separation and a reduced activation energy (overpotential), Wang and co-workers demonstrated that the photothermal effect promoted the reaction kinetics of ascorbic acid oxidation with plasmonic Au nanostars upon LSPR excitaion.231 Regarding the thermal and non-thermal effects, various studies have been reported for quantifying these effects.218,232,233 Experimental and theoretical evidence has shown that non-thermal effects contributed significantly to the plasmonic-enhanced electrocatalytic reactions, far outweighing the photothermal effect.232,233 Recently, Lu and co-workers reported a new strategy to quantitatively evaluate the thermal and non-thermal effects (plasmonic electric effect and hot electrons) at an Ag NP electrode via a slow and rapid current response instead of using a conventional thermal camera and subtraction method, respectively (Fig. 18c).218 This hypothesis was based on the fact that it took a much longer time for photocurrent observation induced by the photothermal effect than by hot carriers. The specific contributions from thermal and non-thermal effects could be manipulated by applied potential (i). The rapid current response disappeared when the light was located at the electrode above the electrolyte level (ii), in agreement with the absence of non-thermal effects since hot electrons were unable to reach the reaction sites due to their short mean-free path. At a higher voltage bias, they observed a larger contribution of the thermal effect to the enhanced current. It was also reported that the photothermal effect of Ag nanostructures could be minimized by using graphene as a support for the plasmonic-enhanced ORR.151

5.1.2 LSPR-switched electrocatalytic reaction pathway. LSPR effects could not only enhance the sluggish electrochemical reactions but also increase the low selectivity by inhibiting undesirable intermediates or selectively interacting with adsorbed reactant species/desired intermediates. A plasmonic Ag film on a Ti/glass slide was reported to direct the selectivity of CO2 reduction.140 At a small applied potential (−0.6 V to −0.8 V vs. RHE), the light excitation promoted CO production while it suppressed the HER. At a large applied potential (−1.1 V vs. RHE), the light excitation enhanced the formation of CH3OH and HCOO−. CH3OH was only observed with light-excited Ag. The hot electrons generated were found to be responsible for the observed selectivity change rather than localized plasmonic heating. Kostecki and co-workers showed that hot electrons from LSPR-excited Ag were transferred to specific unoccupied molecular orbitals of adsorbed species using different applied potentials and light sources, resulting in the selective reduction of CO2. The hot electrons were injected into the LUMO of the adsorbed CO2 or COOH at short wavelengths (∼360 nm) and moderate potentials, enhancing the production of CO, whereas the hot carriers were injected into the LUMO of the adsorbed CO2, COH or CHO at longer wavelengths (>360 nm) and high potentials, promoting the formation of CH4, CH3OH or C2H5OH (Fig. 18d and e).145 The plasmonic Au nanohole array film on a Ti/glass slide for enhancing the ORR activity and switching the ORR pathway was also reported.146 It was demonstrated that the LSPR effect was stronger than the thermal effect, resulting in a lower activation energy. The reaction followed a direct four-electron pathway under light irradiation to reduce O2 to H2O, whereas under dark conditions there was a contribution of the two-electron side-reaction pathway forming the undesirable H2O2 intermediate (Fig. 18f). This plasmon-induced suppression of the two-electron pathway in the ORR was also verified for AgPt,57 the hybrid system of an LSPR metal-electrocatalytic material.
5.1.3 Volcano plots for plasmonic electrocatalysts. Volcano plots are widely explored for the electrocatalytic HER,234–236 ORR,235 OER,235 and CO2RR.237,238 The top position of a volcano plot indicates that the adsorption energy is neither too strong nor too weak for the reaction. As shown in Fig. 19a and b, Ag is located downhill of the volcano plot. With an increase in the laser intensity, it was found that the ORR activity of plasmonic Ag nanotriangles increased first and then decreased slightly.151 This could be explained using the volcano plot. With laser excitation, the O adsorption energy shifted to the optimized position due to the LSPR effect. When the laser power exceeded a certain degree, the activity would move over the peak of the volcano plot and then decline. The high performance of plasmonic Au–Pd–Pt active centers under light irradiation for the ORR was also induced by the shift of pure Pt toward the optimized scenario.239 As shown in Fig. 19c, results from DFT calculations indicate that the binding energy of OH* on the Pt surface decreases due to the injection of hot electrons, which is favorable for the ORR. Thus, volcano plots could provide a guide for developing novel multi-metallic nanostructures for plasmon-mediated electrocatalysis.

5.2 Indirect LSPR-enhanced electrocatalysis

5.2.1 LSPR-enhanced electrocatalytic reactions over electroactive species. LSPR effects have been applied in promoting the activity of electrocatalytic materials. Pt is a well-known active electrocatalyst for the HER, methanol and ethanol oxidation reactions (MOR and EOR), and the ORR etc. Plasmonic Au@AgPt urchin-like nanostructures were fabricated for the MOR.240 The localized heating and the decreased d-band center of the surface Pt was induced by the LSPR effect of plasmonic Au and Ag, leading to the superior MOR kinetics. In this reaction system, the negative effect of elevated temperature existed, promoting poisoning of the catalyst by the CO intermediate. The LSPR-excited hot holes injected to CO could weaken the adsorption energy of CO, reactivating the poisoned Pt active sites. The localized electric field from LSPR excitation could downshift the LUMO energy level of the CO intermediate, boosting the rate-limiting step (*CO + OH− → *COOH + e−) for the MOR under light irradiation. A threefold higher current density with light irradiation over that in the dark was also found for the MOR when utilizing Pt–Au nanodisks. Light-excited Au nanodisks were confirmed to be responsible for the enhanced electric current.241 In the ORR, Amal and co-workers optimized the Au–Pd–Pt NR nanostructures to maintain both the LSPR effect of the Au NRs and the ORR activity of Pt, where Pd served as the buffer layer.239 A 2.2-fold enhancement was observed with visible-light illumination. Through experimental and theoretical investigations such as single-particle PL studies, DFT calculations and FDTD simulations, they found that the enhanced activity of Pt was mainly caused by the transfer of hot carriers from the light-excited Au NR core to the Pt NP, resulting in the modification of its surface electronic structure and too-strong binding behavior to adsorbed intermediates such as O* and OH* (Fig. 19c). Recently, Lyutakov's group deposited the plasmon and catalytically active Au@Pt nanostructures on a proton exchange membrane to incorporate the plasmon-active membrane into the fuel cell for enhancing both the HOR and ORR half-reactions. Light-trigged enhancement of the photocurrent was observed.216

As is known, Pd is also an efficient catalytic material for various reactions. The mechanism for the LSPR-enhanced electrocatalytic properties of Pd active sites is similar to Pt active sites. Kim and co-workers designed a reduced graphene oxide (rGO) spacer between an Au NP core and a Pd NP shell.206 The rGO was proposed to reduce PdO, to enhance the conductivity and to transfer hot carriers from light-excited Au NPs to surface Pd NPs. A 1.9-fold and a 1.1-fold enhancement in the HER and OER under light illumination was achieved, respectively. Jain's group fabricated Pd–Ag alloy nanotubes/CNTs and combined the catalytic activity of Pd and the LSPR effects of Ag for the electrocatalytic MOR.152 An enhancement in the current density and a shift of the peak potential was observed for visible-light plasmonic response of Ag, whereas only the current density change was obtained by changing the electrolyte temperature. In addition to the function of photogenerated electrons and holes from LSPR-excited Pd–Ag, as shown in Fig. 20a, they also found that the LSPR excitation could lower the quasi-Fermi level of the Pd–Ag alloy, resulting in a decrease of the open-circuit potential (OCP).

The LSPR excitation could also enhance the generation of active species with a high valence for metal oxide/hydroxide species, further promoting electrocatalytic oxidation reactions (Fig. 20b–d). Ye and co-workers loaded Au NPs onto Ni(OH)2 nanosheets, achieving a fourfold enhancement in activity and a decrease in the overpotential (η10) from 330 mV to 270 mV under 532 nm laser irradiation.243 Besides the improved hot electron transfer from light-excited Au, as shown in Fig. 20b, the charge transfer from Ni(OH)2 to the hot holes of Au could promote the oxidation of inactive NiII to active NiIII/IV species, where the NiIII/IV sites capture electrons from OH−, markedly enhancing the OER activity. The strategy could be extended to the Au/CoO and Au/FeOOH systems. Other studies also confirmed the hot hole-facilitated oxidation of CoII to active CoIII/IV species for the OER using CoO NPs/Au NRs.245 The facilitated generation of active Mnn+ species from Mn4+ and electron attraction from OH− was also reported in Au/MnO2 nanosheets (Fig. 20c).242 IrO2 and RuO2 are among the best OER electrocatalysts. Camargo's group designed an Au@IrO2 nanoflower where the ultrathin IrO2 layer (1 nm) did not completely cover the Au branches.157 The hot holes generated from the light-excited Au core could activate Ir–O bonds by transferring it to its antibonding states, which played a dominant role in the enhanced OER activities.

The hot hole-facilitated generation of high-valence metal active sites was also observed for LSPR metal and metal–organic framework (MOF) hybrids in electrocatalytic oxidation reactions. For example, plasmon-induced hot holes (Au NPs) mediated the oxidative formation of CoIII/NiIII active species from CoII/NiII in ultrathin semiconductor-like Co/Ni–MOF nanosheets, promoting OER activity.246 For the OER, Xia and co-workers found a synergistic effect between the high-valence Ni active species and energy matching with Au NRs/NiCo–MOF.244 The hot electron transfer and electron transfer between high-valence Ni* and Au were via the same path as stated above while the electron could also transfer from Ni to Co centers via oxygen ligands, further facilitating the formation of high-valence Ni* (Fig. 20d, top). Moreover, the hot electrons and holes respectively transferred from light-excited Au NPs to the LUMO and HOMO of the NiCo–MOFs, resulting in the decrease of its energy level and better matching with the OH−/O2 potential (Fig. 20d, bottom). In the same year, Jiang and co-workers gave a first report on assembling Au NRs on CoFe MOF nanosheets for LSPR-enhanced HER.217 A dramatic decrease of the overpotential from 228 mV to 135 mV at 1 mA cm−2 and an increase of the current density from 1.5 mA cm−2 to 6 mA cm−2 at −0.24 V were obtained by switching the light on and off. As shown in Fig. 20e, the continuous injection of hot electrons induced by the LSPR-excited Au NRs could raise the Fermi level of the CoFe MOF, matching better with the energy level of the H2O/H2 potential, and reducing the activation energy for the HER.

5.2.2 LSPR-enhanced electrocatalytic reactions over semiconductors. The LSPR effects on electrocatalysis within LSPR metal/semiconductor systems have been widely investigated. Regarding semiconductors, for p-type semiconductors the majority carriers are holes, which move in the valence band and in the direction of conventional current flow, whereas for n-type semiconductors the majority carriers are electrons, which move in conduction band and against the direction of conventional current flow. Both LSPR metals and/or semiconductors could be excited by adjusting the light wavelength.

Tsai and co-workers loaded plasmonic Au NPs onto n-type ZnO nanorod arrays for the OER.50 Using small Au NPs (4.7 nm), a higher photocurrent was obtained (1.3 and 0.3 mA cm−2) than with pure ZnO nanorods (0.7 and 0 mA cm−2) under visible-light and solar simulation. They ascribed the enhanced separation of photogenerated electrons and holes to the injection of hot electrons from the light-excited Au NPs to ZnO and the creation of vacancies in the conduction band of ZnO by the plasmon-induced electromagnetic field (Fig. 21a). Several strategies were successfully adopted to reveal these effects. The photocurrent diverged from Fowler's law indicated the contribution of hot electrons from the LSPR effect of Au NPs, as shown in Fig. 16a. DOS and XANES investigations demonstrated that the LSPR-induced electromagnetic field could generate vacancies in the conduction band of the ZnO support (Fig. 15c), facilitating the formation of photogenerated electrons.50 In their work, small Au particles exhibited a better performance than big Au particles. A higher photocurrent was also achieved for other electrocatalytic reactions over LSPR metals that possessed a smaller particle size.62,214 Huber and co-workers demonstrated that the high performance of the smaller Au size was probably due to the greater number of charge carriers generated by the increased localized electric field, which is directly proportional to the quality factor.214 The LSPR effect of Au and Ag nanostructures on an n-type TiO2 film upon the electrochemistry was first investigated by some experts in the early 2000s.225,226,247–249 Employing electrochemical techniques, Wei and co-workers illustrated the electron transfer within plasmonic Au/TiO2 under visible-light irradiation (>515 nm) and discovered that the excited-state lifetimes of these transferred electrons were much longer than photogenerated electrons from UV-excited TiO2, which further enhanced HER activity.250 Recently, utilizing a series of molecules with various redox potentials and Au/TiO2 as the model system, Willets and co-workers successfully quantified the wavelength-dependent plasmonic hot carrier energy distributions at the Au/TiO2 interface, which facilitated the reactions.251

n-Type semiconductors with a low band gap, such as BiVO4,59,61,162 and α-Fe2O3,252,253 have also been applied in such a hybrid system for the LSPR-promoted OER at the semiconductor/electrolyte interface. Au@SiO2 nanostructures at different locations of Fe2O3 gave different enhanced photocurrents as a function of the wavelength (Fig. 21b). Utilizing aluminum nanoparticles as the plasmonic material, the Si@Al@α-Fe2O3 core@multishell structure was engineered by Yang's group (Fig. 21c). Compared with the core–shell design, this structure could ensure a uniform electromagnetic field enhancement induced by LSPR excitation over the entire nanowire electrode. Moreover, the enhanced photocurrent of Al NPs (50 nm) was comparable to Ag NPs with the same size, outperforming Au NPs, providing an inexpensive alternative plasmonic electrocatalyst to precious metals.

Excepting the LSPR effect, the catalytic effects of a plasmonic metal in such a hybrid system were confirmed by Abdi's group using a unique strategy.59 As shown in Fig. 21d, modifying the BiVO4 surface with Ag@SiO2 core–shell NPs, they found that the scattering effect was dominant for absorption enhancement of the OER. Adding H2O2 as a hole scavenger, they investigated the absorption enhancement, the catalytic enhancement and the total enhancement factor, where the catalytic enhancement was obtained by dividing the total enhancement factor by the absorption enhancement. The catalytic enhancement was significantly larger than the absorption enhancement.

p-Type semiconductors with a large band gap incapable of being excited under visible-light irradiation are able to act as hot hole acceptors and block electron transfer from plasmonic metals, promoting direct reduction reactions on an LSPR-excited metal surface. As shown in Fig. 21e, p-type NiOx blocks electron transfer while allowing hole transfer back to the Al electrode and then to the Pd anode via the external circuit. The hot electrons left on the Au NPs surface are directly transferred to H+ for the HER without a Schottky junction.254 The difference between such a p-type semiconductor with a large bandgap and n-type TiO2 with a large band gap is illustrated in Fig. 21e′. Atwater and co-workers chose a large band gap p-type semiconductor as a support, which served solely to collect hot holes from light-excited plasmonic Au or Cu, resulting in the accumulation of hot electrons on the metal surface for CO2 reduction (Fig. 21e′ and e′′). Upon plasmon excitation, the plasmonic photocathodes improved the CO2 selectivity and suppressed H2 production in an aqueous electrolyte. Enhanced CO selectivity was observed for the plasmonic Au/p-GaN photocathode, while both enhanced CO and HCOO− selectivity was observed for the Cu/p-NiO photocathode.142,143 In designing such a system (e.g. Cu/p-NiO), the valence band of the p-type semiconductor and the Fermi level of the plasmonic metal should be matched for the formation of a modest Schottky barrier (ΦB) of around 1 eV at the interface to allow hot-hole injection (Fig. 21e′′). Besides these systems, the plasmonic Ag nanodimer structure has also been deposited on p-GaP for the HER. The pH value of electrolyte showed an influence on the activity. The improved HER activity was confirmed with light irradiation, especially under neutral conditions.255

When the adsorption of the semiconductor overlaps with the LSPR absorption band of its supported metal nanostructure, a further increase in the visible-light absorption and catalytic activity can be observed. Such systems have been reported for Au@Cu2O,58 Ag/N doped-TiO2,208,256 and Au/TiO2 PCNTs.257 Photonic layers of semiconductors have also been applied to enhance the LSPR effect on electrocatalysis. The photonic band gap of TiO2 photonic crystals was tuned to overlap with the LSPR band of Au NPs, resulting in more hot electrons transferred from light-excited Au NPs to TiO2, in turn improving the water-splitting efficiency (Fig. 21f).67,98

It is also possible to tune reduction reactions at the cathodic electrode side with an LSPR-based electrode at anodic electrode side. Misawa and co-workers demonstrated that plasmon-induced charge separation promoted the OER and the subsequent N2 reduction reaction (NRR) in one system comprised of both the anode and cathode.137,138 Recently, they further constructed a two-electrode (Au/n-SrTiO3‖Zr) system for ammonia synthesis, as shown in Fig. 22.139 The electrons generated from the light-excited plasmonic Au NPs transferred to the conduction band of SrTiO3 and further to the Zr coil via an external circuit, facilitating the NRR to NH3.

6. Conclusions and outlook

The efficient utilization of solar energy is a promising route to relieve energy shortages and reduce environmental pollution. The LSPR effect of light-excited plasmonic metal nanostructures could produce electromagnetic field enhancement, create hot carriers, induce resonant energy transfer and the photothermal effect. For traditional electrocatalysis, therefore, LSPR could provide new possibilities not only to promote the activity but also to tune the reaction pathway and consequently improve the selectivity. Although progress has been achieved in LSPR-mediated electrocatalysis, the following items may be considered for future development.

(1) Materials design. In addition to the further improvement on the activity of currently available LSPR-based electrodes, either through structure/shape design or the synergistic effect of alloys, efforts should be made to explore new types of plasmonic metal nanostructures. For instance, cheap and stable non-noble alternative materials with the LSPR effect in the visible-light range are desirable for their large-scale applications. The undiscovered properties of novel plasmonic materials could probably enter a new regime. Moreover, future efforts should be devoted to the modification of preparation methods that aim to seek a facile and simple synthetic approach for constructing active LSPR-based electrodes on a large scale with a controllable size/shape and structure.

Careful materials design is required for certain hybrid plasmonic metals (Au, Al) and semiconductors (TiO2) under UV-Vis light irradiation since both negative and positive effects of LSPR exist.16,259–261 The negative effect comes from the reverse injection of electrons from the plasmonic metal to the conduction band of the semiconductor, promoting the recombination of UV-generated electron–hole pairs in TiO2. Although the positive effects of the LSPR play a dominant role over the negative effects, strategies should be adopted to further promote the positive effects, either through varying the intensity ratio of the Vis/UV light, engineering an interface Schottky barrier, or designing hybrid materials with sufficient LSPR energy to excite the semiconductor to generate electron–hole pairs through mechanism D in Fig. 4.

(2) Mechanistic investigations. A deep understanding of the mechanisms involved in LSPR-enhanced electrocatalysis is required and could provide grounds for the rational design of plasmonic electrocatalysts. Although a few attempts have been made to explain the LSPR-induced chemistry, further investigations are needed to achieve a detailed description of the roles of hot electrons, hot holes, and magnetic and photothermal effects during the electrocatalytic process. Developments in theoretical simulations and material characterization techniques, such as in situ spectroscopies together with electrochemical methods, will surely offer help for differentiating the contribution of LSPR effects while assisting in the design of efficient plasmonic electrocatalysts. Stable plasmonic metals with well-known properties are suitable materials for mechanistic investigation or proof-of-concept studies such as Au nanostructures.

(3) Ag and Cu nanostructures are different from plasmonic Au in that they are easily oxidized. In situ reduction seems to be a promising alternative for the rational design of plasmonic electrocatalysts. This could also create a large number of active sites for certain reactions, e.g., the in situ reduction of CuxO nanoparticles to Cu for LSPR-enhanced CO2 reduction. A multicomponent nanostructure, such as in alloy formation, is another solution that could inhibit the oxidation of Ag or Cu and provide new catalytically active centers.

(4) Exploiting new LSPR-mediated electrochemical processes. The application of LSPR in electrocatalysis is not limited to the above types of electrochemical reactions in Table 1. Seeking new types of electrocatalytic organic synthesis is in its exciting early stages. Moreover, exploiting the application of LSPR in other electrochemistry research fields is attractive. For example, a light-induced high-performance electrode for use in batteries was discovered last year.262

(5) Tuning the reaction pathway. The experimental and theoretical results show that the transfer of hot electrons and the reaction pathway could be tuned using different applied potentials and light sources. Selectivity is extremely important in organic synthesis. Precise control over the product selectivity via LSPR-induced electrochemistry is significant and challenging. To this end, future efforts on the exploration of multi-component plasmonic metals combined with variation of the potential and light wavelengths are expected.

(6) Practical applications. Encouraged by the present and continuing research efforts on efficiency improvement, we believe that there will be a widespread application of LSPR-mediated electrocatalysis for large-scale synthesis or transforming various compounds on an industrial scale. We also expect the development of controllable PEC devices integrated with LSPR effects for practical applications such as in renewable-energy storage.
