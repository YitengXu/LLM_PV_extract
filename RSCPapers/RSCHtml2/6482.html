Electrocatalyst design for the conversion of energy molecules: electronic state modulation and mass transport regulation

Abstract

Electrocatalytic conversions of energy molecules are involved in many energy conversion processes. Improving the activity of electrocatalysts is critical for increasing the efficiency of these energy conversion processes. However, the tailored design of highly active electrocatalysts for practical applications remains challenging. In this regard, we present an overview of the general design principles for efficient electrocatalysts and application of these principles in different electrocatalytic processes. Specifically, enhancing the intrinsic activity of electrocatalysts by electronic state modulation through heteroatom doping, vacancy introduction, interfacial electronic transfer and strain engineering is introduced. In addition, improving the apparent performance of electrocatalysts by mass transport regulation, which is realized by morphological and wettability control, is also discussed. Finally, enlightenment from these studies is summarized and perspectives for the future development of electrocatalysts are provided. The important progress highlighted in this work will provide solid foundations for the tailored design of electrocatalysts toward practical applications.

1. Introduction

Currently, fossil fuels account for more than 80% of the global energy supply; the heavy consumption significantly disrupts the ecological balance.1 In addition, the expected rapid increase of energy demand from 18 TW in 2014 to 24 TW in 20402 will accelerate the depletion of fossil fuels. In this situation, the development of sustainable and eco-friendly energy conversion devices is of great significance.36 Electrochemical conversion of energy molecules, such as H2, O2, N2 and H2O, plays an important role in eco-friendly energy conversion devices. Till now, considerable efforts have been invested in these electrochemical processes. However, there are still technical challenges to be overcome for the efficient utilization of these electrochemical processes. One of the primary challenges is to develop a high-performance electrocatalyst that efficiently catalyzes the conversion reaction of the energy molecules, such as the oxygen evolution reaction (OER), hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), nitrogen reduction reaction (NRR) and CO2 reduction reaction (CO2RR). For example, the electrochemical water splitting can be divided into the anodic OER and the cathodic HER with a theoretical decomposition voltage of 1.23 V.7 However, a high overpotential is required in practice.

In particular, the sluggish kinetics of the anodic OER, involving the simultaneous transfer of four electrons and four protons, significantly limits the overall efficiency of water splitting.79 Moreover, there is a strong pH effect on the OER reaction mechanism, which can be categorized into water oxidation under acidic or neutral conditions and hydroxide oxidation under highly alkaline conditions.10,11 Therefore, it is important to customize the OER catalyst to response various application conditions. Specifically, RuO2 and IrO2 exhibit excellent OER activity in both acidic and alkaline electrolytes, but they are unstable at high overpotential and will be oxidized to form RuO4 and IrO3 and dissolved in electrolytes, especially in acidic electrolytes because the participation of lattice oxygen may lead to structural collapse to accelerate the dissolution of Ru/Ir.1214 For neutral electrolytes, the presence of the polymeric water structure with strong hydrogen bonding limits the kinetics, while in the HER, there is a potential surface reconstruction in alkaline electrolytes caused by the adsorption of OH on the catalyst surface, resulting in misjudgment of the really active sites.

Even though different electrochemical catalytic reactions follow different mechanisms, the general design principles of electrocatalysts for different reactions have many common points. Improving the intrinsic activity of the electrocatalyst is an important yet challenging point for the design of an electrocatalyst. For example, commercial Pt/catalysts for the ORR have a much lower mass activity (0.23 A mgPGM1 at 0.9 V) than that required for practical applications (0.44 A mgPGM1 at 0.9 V).15 From a mechanistic point of view, the intrinsic activity of a catalyst mainly depends on the adsorption/desorption behaviour of the reaction intermediates on the active sites of electrocatalysts.16 The adsorption/desorption behaviour of the reaction intermediates on the electrocatalyst is dictated by the electronic states of the electrocatalyst. Therefore, modulation the electronic state of the electrocatalyst is the key for improving its intrinsic activity. Besides intrinsic activity, the mass transport capacity is another limiting factor for the performance of electrocatalysts.17 Nowadays, most of the catalysts are designed and evaluated at a low current density region (tens of mA cm2), where mass transport limitation is not prominent. However, in practical applications such as fuel cells and water electrolysis, the electrode should be able to be operated above 1 A cm2 under most of the working conditions, where mass transport loss is significant. Consequently, improving the mass transport capability of electrocatalysts is also critical for their performance. Since typical electrocatalytic reactions proceed at three-phase boundaries, appropriate design of electrocatalyst morphology and control over its interfacial wettability are also crucial for the performance of the electrocatalysts.

Considering the important roles of electronic state modulation and mass transport regulation in the design of high-performance electrocatalysts, we present an overview of recent efforts on the tailored design of advanced electrocatalysts. This review mainly focuses on metal based electrocatalysts because of their high potential for practical applications. Strategies allowing for the tuning of the electronic states of electrocatalysts, with an emphasis on strain engineering, are introduced. Approaches for enhancing the mass transport capabilities of the electrocatalysts are also introduced and discussed.

2. Intrinsic activity improvement by electronic status modulation

2.1. Intrinsic activity improvement by electronic status modulation

The intrinsic activity of the catalyst depends on the adsorption and desorption barriers of the reaction intermediates on the surface of the catalyst, which is controlled by the electronic structure of the active sites. It was proposed that the change of adsorption energy can be approximated by the change of KohnSham orbital energy, based on which the change in the adsorption energy of reaction intermediates on different catalysts can be calculated. According to the conclusion above, the NewnsAnderson model18 describing the interaction between the adsorbent and the surface of catalysts from the perspective of electronic interactions, is established.

Norskov et al. proposed a simplified model emphasizing the important role of the d-band center of the electrocatalyst in determining the adsorption behaviour of reaction intermediates (d-band center theory). It is stated that the bandwidth of the half-filled s and p-orbitals in the valence electron of transition metal is increased due to the uncertainty principle, which leads to a weak interaction between the s and p orbitals and the electrons of the adsorbents. As a result, the primary factor determining the adsorption of adsorbents is the d band orbital of the electrocatalysts. As adsorbents bind on the metal surface, bonding and antibonding orbitals are formed. The molecular orbitals of the adsorbent interact with the d bands of the metal and result in the splitting of the d band. The thus-formed antibonding orbitals will be lifted close to the Ef so that the electrons in the antibonding orbitals tend to transfer to the bonding orbitals, resulting in enhanced adsorption of the reaction intermediates on the metal surface. The number of electrons transferred to the bonding orbitals depends on the position of the d band center of the metal relative to Ef. The direct correlation between the d band center and adsorption energy of typical reaction intermediates is proved by combined theoretical and experimental analysis (Fig. 1a).19 Therefore, it is suggested that the position of the d band center of a metallic electrocatalyst is an important descriptor of its catalytic activity.

Modulation in the d-band center of the metal-based catalyst can be directly proved using different characterization studies. The ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy (UPS) is a kind of photoelectron spectroscopy that uses low-energy ultraviolet light (1641 eV) as the excitation source. By measuring the photoelectron energy generated by the ultraviolet excitation, the surface electronic structure, chemical state, composition and other information of the solid materials can be obtained. Sun et al. demonstrated the shift of the d band center of Ni in Ni2P to Ef after Fe-doping by UPS valence band characterization studies (Fig. 1c).20 Such a shift effectively regulated the adsorption and desorption capacities of the oxygen-containing intermediates (*O, *OH, and *OOH) on Ni2P. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) providing valence band spectra (VBS), reflecting the valence band information of solid materials, is another commonly-used technique for d-band center characterization. Wu et al. demonstrated the shift in d-band spectra of an Ag-based catalyst by alloying using XPS VBS (Fig. 1d).21

2.2. Strategy for d-band center modulation

The applicability of d band theory in the design of active electrocatalysts is demonstrated in many studies. Several approaches have been developed for the enhancement of the electrocatalytic performance of catalysts by modifying the d-band center of the electrocatalysts.
2.2.1. Heteroatom doping. Heteroatom doping is a facile and effective means to adjust the d-band center of the catalyst. In 2009, Dai et al. found that doping-induced charge redistribution of nitrogen-doped vertically aligned carbon nanotubes (VA-CNTs) facilitated the chemisorption of O2 and the electron transfer for ORR.22 Subsequently, they discovered that co-doping with an additional heteroatom could further improve the performance of ORR by studying the B and N co-doped VA-CNTs.23 Thereafter, the approach of heteroatom doping has been widely employed in the design of electrocatalysts.24 Improving the utilization and durability of precious metal atoms is important for the development of precious metal-based catalysts. Qi et al. used phosphorous doping to modify the electronic structure and stability of the Rh single-atom catalyst (PRuSACCo3O4/NF).25 Their results demonstrated that the introduction of P atoms could efficiently boost the charge transfer process at the grain boundary and thus promote the reaction kinetics. DFT calculations showed that the P-doping strategy facilitates electrons transfer among Rh atoms, which decreased the adsorption and desorption energy of various intermediates. With these advantages, the catalyst demonstrated outstanding alkaline overall water splitting activity and stability. Additionally, the P-doping method was employed to enhance the durability of MoNi alloy catalysts through triggering the electronic redistribution around the metal centers.26 DFT calculations confirmed that the reconstructed electronic structure optimized the bond strength of H* and NiMoP, which reduced the energy barrier of water splitting, as well as favored the structural stability of the PMoNi catalyst. To facilitate the NRR process and improve the faradaic efficiency, Hou et al. reported a viable strategy to manipulate the local spin state of isolated iron sites via S-coordinated doping (FeSA-NSC).27 The in situ analysis revealed that the incorporation of S induces the transition of spin-polarization configuration with the formation of a medium-spin-state of Fe. Theoretical calculations showed that the induction of S-doping lowered the energy barrier for *NNH intermediate formation and accelerated hydrogenation reaction kinetics. Tao et al. tuned the d-band center of Pd-based catalysts by boron doping.28 Their state density calculation showed that the d-band of Pd shifted to Ef after optimal boron doping due to the hybridization between the 3d orbital of Pd and the p-orbital of boron (Fig. 2a). Considering that the adsorption/desorption of proton in Pd-based catalysts is the rate-determining step in the HER, the shift of the d-band center towards Ef will cause a higher occupancy in the antibonding orbital between Pd and H+, thereby weakening the binding strength of hydrogen and facilitating the HER (Fig. 2b). Doping of B into Mo2C was also reported to modify the d-band center of Mo.29 In addition, B-doping enhances the hybridization of the Mo 4d orbital and N 2p orbital, which promotes the adsorption and activation of N2 on the catalysts and reduces the reaction barrier for the NRR (Fig. 2c and d). Other non-metallic heteroatoms such as N and P were also used as dopants for a metal-based catalyst to adjust the d-band center of the catalyst.30 The doped P species at the PtN3 regulates the 5d orbitals of Pt, inducing a left shift of the PDOS of the 5d orbitals and a deeper d-band center (Fig. 2f). In addition, the rate-determining step of the PtN was changed from the first protonation step (H2O + O2 + e + * OOH* + OH) to the fourth protonation step (OH* + e * + OH) after P-doping (Fig. 2g and h).

Doping of metallic heteroatoms (such as Fe, Ni, V and Zn) also modifies the d-band structure and tunes the surface properties of the catalyst.7,8,31 Wang et al. successfully adjusted the d-band center of Co4N via V-doped and prepared a highly HER active V-Co4N catalyst.32 The DFT analysis confirmed that the anti-bonding state was filled with more electrons after V-doping, which reduced the adsorption energy of H and promoted H2 desorption. Detailed characterization and DFT calculations identified that the Fe dopant synergistically optimized the electronic states of Co2, and the Co2 site nearest the Fe site has the highest activity.33 Sun et al. found that the doping of Fe into Ni2P enhanced the OER activity of Ni2P because the introduction of Fe lifted the d band-center of Ni (Fig. 2e).20 The use of noble metal atom doping to adjust the electronic state of the surrounding atoms was also proposed to improve the activity of the catalyst.3436 Lu et al. designed a surface-engineering strategy to dope Ru into the subsurface of the Co matrix as a HER catalyst.34 DFT results showed that Ru doping in the subsurface dramatically enhances the catalytic activity for the HER and the Ru doped into the subsurface was thermodynamically more stable than that the Ru alloyed on the surface.

2.2.2. Vacancy engineering. The introduction of vacancies into metal-baed catalysts is another viable approach to regulate the d-band center of the active component in the catalysts.79 Catalysts are typically designed with two vacancies: (1) anionic vacancies (non-metallic vacancies). Compared with cationic vacancies, anionic vacancies are easily obtained because of the lower formation energy.7 Qiao et al. successfully increased the onset potential of the HER in an alkaline electrolyte via introducing phosphorus vacancies (Pv).37 The DOS analysis disclosed that Pv can facilitate the electron enrichment of Ni and P atoms next to them. Luo et al. developed amorphous a-VSe2x nanosheets with Se vacancies for electrochemical NRR.38 The projected density of state (PDOS) analysis suggested that the unsaturated V atoms in the a-VSe2x had a shift in the d-band center (unsaturated V atoms) to Ef (Fig. 3a), and as a result the antibonding orbital between unsaturated V atoms of a-VSe2x and adsorbed intermediates was less occupied (Fig. 3a). In the scenario, the V-3d orbital of a-VSe2x could favourably interact with the N2-2p orbital to generate hybrid states that facilitate sufficient adsorption and activation of N2 (Fig. 3c). Therefore, a-VSe2x showed excellent NRR activity due to the modulation of the d-band center of the catalyst (Fig. 3b). Zhong et al. generated O-vacancy in Co3O4 through the introduction of low valence Ni2+.39 The electron redistribution resulting from vacancy introduction affords the Co atoms (around the vacancy site) higher valence charge numbers, indicating a shift in its d-band center toward Ef. Superior HER performance was demonstrated for the Co3O4 catalyst with a controlled vacancy structure (Fig. 3d). The strategy of d-band engineering via vacancy engineering is also demonstrated for perovskite-type catalysts. Han et al. investigated the effect of the oxygen vacancy concentration on catalytic activity without altering the surface area, physical properties and composition (Fig. 3e).40 The experimental result and DFT calculations indicated that the d-band center of Co in the catalyst was closer to the Ef and the p-orbital level of O with the increases in O-vacancy concentration. The shift in the d-band center of Co facilitated orbital hybridization with O and promoted the adsorption activation of reactants and intermediates, which ultimately improved the OER/ORR activity of the catalyst (Fig. 3f and g).

The cationic vacancy has a multifarious electron configuration, which is also conducive to alter the electronic structure of the catalyst.7 By comparing the introduction of O-vacancies and Co-vacancies, Dou et al. found that the Co-vacancies effectively reduced the overpotential of the OER.41 The theoretical calculations revealed that the Co vacancies seek to optimize the electronic states of Co2, and therefore the binding energy of OH* is dramatically decreased. Zhang et al. devised a facile chemical synthetic method via a one-pot solvothermal method in the presence of rhodium(III) acetylacetonate, oleylamine, oleic acid and formaldehyde.42 The DFT calculations verified that the ordered Rh vacancies in VBP-1 Rh(010) break the hollow site and make the adjacent Rh atoms more active, resulting in relatively more positive adsorption energy for H and leading to the enhanced adsorption of H2O molecules. In addition, vacancy engineering also plays a role in improving stability via adjusting the electronic states. Instability under high oxidation potentials is the key issue that limits the application of highly active layered double hydroxides (LDH). Waterhouse et al. revealed that the stability degradation of the catalyst resulted from the decrease in the number of active sites and undesirable phase segregation, with metal dissolution underpinning both these deactivation mechanisms. Fortunately, they effectively enhanced the LDH stability by introducing cationic vacancies.43 DFT calculations showed the strengthened binding energy between the metal and oxygen in the LDH sheets and the decreased lattice distortions after the introduction of cation-vacancies, achieving both high catalyst activity and stability during the OER. Zhang et al. demonstrated a simple wet-chemistry method to grow ultrathin feroxyhyte nanosheets with rich Fe vacancies on the Ni foam substrate as the water splitting catalyst.44 Significantly, the structural analysis and DFT simulation indicated that the iron cation vacancies not only led to increased electrical conductivity of the catalyst, but also strengthened the mechanical steadiness. In short, the design of the iron vacancies enabled it to endure the drastic gas evolution at an increased current density without active attenuation.

2.2.3. Interface electronic state modulation. In addition to heteroatomic doping and vacancy engineering, the construction of heterointerfaces enabling electronic interactions between the catalyst components could be also utilized to tune the position of the d-band center of the active component to improve the catalytic performance. This approach for d-band engineering is particularly effective for precious metal catalysts with low-cost supports. In our previous work, Au/ZrO2 composite was designed for NRR.45 As shown in Fig. 4a, electron transfer occurred at the interface between Au and ZrO2. The electron transfer propelled the shift of the d-band center of Au closer to Ef, which facilitates the adsorption and activation of N2 on the surface of the catalyst (Fig. 4b and c). In another example, Pt/MoS2 was developed. It was proposed that the strong synergy between S and Pt contributed to the formation of electronic coupling at the interface of Pt and MoS2 (Fig. 4d).46 The coupling effect has changed the relative position of the d-band center of Pt, thereby enhancing the HER performance of Pt (Fig. 4e). Chem et al. synthesized h-BN/Pd electrocatalyst with outstanding activity and long-term durability by confining Pd nanoparticles to an ultra-thin h-BN nanosheet support.47 The experimental and DFT calculations show that h-BN not only plays a role in inhibiting the agglomeration of Pd NPs and maximizing the exposure of the active sites, but also in shifting the d-band center of Pd away from Ef (Fig. 4g). This electronic modulation is the key to the enhancement in the ORR kinetics of h-BN/Pd (Fig. 4f). Organic ligands are a new type of material for the interfacial electronic modulation of electrocatalysts. Li et al. reported the tuning of the electronic states of bi/trimetallic layered hydroxides (LDH)/MOFs OER electrocatalyst by the assembly of carboxylate ligands on the electrocatalyst.48 The carboxyl groups around the metal (oxygen) hydroxide take a function to induce the redistribution of interface electrons (Fig. 4h), modulate the relative position of the d-band center (Fig. 4i) and optimize the adsorption of intermediates on the surface of the catalyst to dissociate the energy barrier. Significantly enhanced OER performance was demonstrated by the interfacial electronic modulation.

Furthermore, the formation of a hetero-structure is a viable approach to modulate the electronic state of the active sites due to the high electronic conductivity, low interfacial resistance and rapid electron transfer.7,49 In this respect, several catalysts with a hetero-structure were designed to enhance the catalytic activity. Recently, Co-based LDHs were regarded as one of the most efficient OER catalysts owing to their special morphologies, high catalytic activity and facile synthesis. However, the stability of the Co-based LDH remains to be enhanced. An effective strategy to improve the durability and catalytic activity of the Co-based LDH is to tune the local electronic structure by constructing heterojunctions.8,9 For example, Yang et al. designed a Co-LDH@ZIF-67 heterojunction to improve the activity and stability of Co-LDH.50 The DFT calculations indicated that the heterojunction interface was constructed by the O of CoLDH and N of the 2-methylimidazole ligand in ZIF-67. The heterojunction modulated the electronic structure of the active sites, which facilitated the breakage of the HOout bond and resulted in lower free energy in the OER. To reduce the amount of precious metal and accelerate alkaline water dissociation, Yan et al. developed a single-atom platinum immobilized NiO/Ni heterostructure (PtSANiO/Ni) as a HER catalyst.51 Among the catalyst, the single-atom-level dispersion fully improved the utilization of Pt, while the Pt single-atom coupled with the NiO/Ni heterostructure efficiently tailors the water dissociation energy and promotes H* conversion. For the OER, the current understanding is that the active oxygen species formed by the surface reconstruction during the OER process are the real active sites of the catalyst.52 Fisher et al. built an adaptive heterojunction via the OER-induced surface reconstruction, where NiOOH grows on delithiated LiNiO2.52 The in situ characterization and DFT calculations demonstrated that the formation of delithiated-LiNiO2/NiOOH heterojunction improves the rate of electron transfer and evolves the delithiated LiNiO2 with lithium vacancies that strengthen the stability of NiOO* species. Poor durability and sluggish charge transfer kinetics limited the development of large-scale hydrogen production under high current density-driven water splitting. Huang et al. constructed a sulfide-based metallic heterostructure (Mo2S3@NiMo3S4) to meet the industrial demand.53 The metallic Mo2S3 modulated by the heterostructure facilitated the charge transfer for fast Volmer H and Heyrovsky H2 generation. The constructed heterostructure realized an extraordinarily low cell voltage of 1.672 V at the current density of 1 A cm2, and remained stable after chronoamperometric test for over 100 h. In addition, the pH effect cannot be ignored during the HER process, for this catalyst. The development of electrocatalysts applicable in a wide pH range is of great significance for industrial applications. Yin et al. reported a PtCo@PtSn heterojunction catalyst with excellent HER performance in pH-universal.54 DFT simulations illustrate that the construction of the heterojunction tunes the surface electronic structure of Pt and decreases the PtH bond strength. The PtCo@PtSn heterojunction catalyst absorbed the advantages of Pt-based materials and heterojunctions to achieve exceptional HER activity, with overpotentials of 25 mV, 18 mV and 21 mV in 1.0 M KOH, 1.0 M phosphate-buffered saline and 0.5 M H2SO4, respectively.

Finally, the self-supported electrocatalysts are presently considered to be the most suitable catalyst form to increase the active loading, mass transfer and large current density activity because of enhanced kinetics and stability.55 At present, the most commonly used substrates applied in the self-supported catalysts are the conventional conductive substrates, such as those that are carbon based (carbon cloth, carbon fiber paper) and metal based (metal foam, metal foil).8,55,56 However, these carbon-based support materials are more surface-stable, resulting in a simple deposited rather than forming a solid interface, which increases the resistance of interface electron transfer. Wang et al. fabricated the active ingredient on the activated carbon cloth (ACC) via a hydrothermal method.57 Additionally, the active component of the self-supported catalyst was closely combined with the conductive substrate. Compared with carbon cloth, the ACC was equipped with a higher electrical conductivity of 4.2 S cm1, which facilitated the electron transfer during the electrode/electrolyte interface. This structure not only reduced the mutual coverage but also prevented the catalyst abscission during the HER process. Julio et.al reported a solution combustion methodology to produce self-supported electrocatalysts.58 A strong interfacial effect was formed between the active component (MNiO) and the substrate with the introduction of NF, which guided the MNiO to form a unique laminar structure and modified its electronic structure to enhance the electron activity. Therefore, the self-supported electrode exhibited outstanding alkaline oxygen evolution performance and excellent stability.59 Furthermore, the structure and surface morphology of the electrocatalysts are directly related to the resulting water splitting efficiency. Hence, the regulation of the structure and surface morphology of active species can effectively enhance the activity and stability of the self-supported catalysts. Du et al. found that the multidimensional integrated architecture can improve the performance of individual 1D or 2D nanocatalysts by fabricating a self-supported electrocatalyst of 1D MoO3 nanorods coupled with 2D nanosheets on NF.60 The unique nanostructure had the features of large surface area, more accessible active sites, rapid electron transfer and favorable gas bubbles escape.

2.2.4. Strain engineering. The alloying of Pt, Pb and other transition metal elements not only reduces the amounts of noble metals, but also generates the alloying effect, ligand effect, etc.6170 The aforementioned effects can effectively change the position of the center of the low d-band of the metal active site relative to the Ef, which can enhance the adsorptiondesorption behavior of the active sites for intermediates. More importantly, in the process of forming the alloy, the lattice parameters of transition metals or Pt, Pd, etc. noble metals change, resulting in the generation of strain.71 Density functional theory calculations show that strain changes the electronic structure of metal active sites, and appropriate tensile/compressive strain can further optimize the activity of metal-based catalysts.7279

Luo et al. prepared ultrathin PdMo bimetalenes via a one-pot method. Using molybdenum carbonyl as the metal precursor, the low-temperature pyrolysis of this precursor effectively promotes the formation of ultrathin structures with only 0.88 nm thickness. The shift of the characteristic XRD peaks indicates that Mo enters the lattice of Pd to form an alloy, which induces strain in the PdMo bimetal. Simulation results at room temperature display an average tensile strain of 0.95% in the PdMo bimetalene, and the bending geometry also contributes to the strain. The ORR performances of the PdMo bimetalene in an alkaline solution are shown below. The mass activity of the PdMo bimetalene/C at 0.9 V (vs. RHE) is 16.37 A g1, which is equivalent to 327.4 times that of commercial Pd/C. DFT theoretical calculations showed that a tensile strain of 1% enables the PdMo bimetalene to have the best O adsorption free energy. The relationship between oxygen adsorption energy and strain on the surface of metalenes was studied by comparing different PDMS (M: Cu, Ni, Mo, W), the result shown that the alloying effect plays the most important role in enhancing the ORR activity of PdMo bimetalenes. Moreover, the synergy of strain effect, quantum size effect and alloy effect makes the ORR activity of the PdMo bimetalene reach the best.80 In addition, the instability of platinum-group metals under a high overpotential was another vital problem. The coupling of adsorption heat energy of reactants and intermediates with surface mechanics is a research hotspot in the field of surface science and catalysis. 3. Apparent activity enhancement by mass transport regulation

As an inseparable process in electrocatalysis, mass transport is a common limiting factor for electrocatalytic reactions, especially at high current density.101103 Effective mass transport represents rapid absorption and desorption step at active sites, which not only require suitable adsorption energy between reactants and various reaction intermediates, but also splendid mass transfer channels.103105 Although numerous methods have been employed to exploit efficient catalysts, it is still a rigorous challenge to develop a catalyst with efficient mass transfer capability.106108 Considering the restrictions of mass transport in heterogeneous electrocatalysis, we review the kinetic mechanism and optimization strategy of mass transport in the design of electrocatalysts.

3.1. Mass transport kinetics

Due to the presence of mass transport limitation, the electrode reaction frequently shows the kinetic characteristics of the mass transport step, instead of the reaction kinetic characteristics.104,109 For example, the Tafel slope of HER catalyzed on some catalyst is 120 dec1 in an acidic medium, reflecting mass transfer limitation rather than the reaction kinetic limitation as the Volmer step must occur under acidic conditions.109 Therefore, the study of mass transport kinetics is beneficial to the development of high-performance electrocatalysts.104,110,111

For electrocatalytic reactions, there are three main forms of mass transport between the electrolyte and catalyst layer: convection, diffusion and electromigration.104,112 In the absence of external agitation, concentration gradient and temperature gradient are the main forces of convection and diffusion, while applied electric field causes electromigration of the dot particles.112 The mass transport rate can be expressed by the flow of the species studied through the unit cross-section in a unit time.104,113 As the electrocatalytic reaction progresses, the concentration difference of species i is formed between the electrode surface and electrolyte, which in turn produces a diffusion flow (JD,i). In addition to convection and diffusion triggered by concentration gradients, there is mass transport flow brought by electromigration (JE,i). 3.3. Strategies for boosting mass transport

3.3.1. Construction of mass transport channels. For nanostructured catalysts, interconnected and hierarchically structured pores are normally required for mass transport because a parochial channel is easily blocked by electrolyte, resulting in low accessibility of the active sites.130132 In contrast to the mature procedures of catalyst synthesis and functionalization, tailoring the mass transport channel in electrocatalysts remains challenging.133

Many electrocatalysts were synthesized by direct calcination of catalyst precursors and therefore there was a lack in control over the pore structures of the electrocatalysts.134138 Application of appropriate template materials in the synthesis is a straightforward method for the control of electrocatalyst morphology. An active non-metal ORR catalyst with three-dimensional ordered mass transfer channels (3DOM NS-C) was prepared in our previous study.111 Briefly, a new ionic liquid bearing an alkyne group has been synthesized and used as a precursor to prepare the 3DOM NS-C catalyst using a silica spheres template (average diameter of 300 nm). 3DOM NS-C exhibited rich micropores and macropores, which facilitate the diffusion of oxygen molecules in the catalyst layer. Due to the hierarchical pore structure, the 3DOM NS-C catalyst shows a more positive ORR onset potential and half-wave potential compared with the Pt/C catalyst. Li et al. prepared a honeycomb-like CoNxC electrocatalyst by using a polystyrene (PS) template with a size of 180 nm.130 With the introduction of PS, interconnected macropores were constructed in the catalyst. The honeycomb-like CoNxC catalyst (Honeycomb-600) exhibits a typical polyhedral morphology (Fig. 9a and b) with a particle size of about 0.84 m and abundant interconnected macropores of 180 nm throughout the crystal. It was shown that the splendid ORR activity mainly stemmed from the improved mass transport in the unique pore structure. Salt template is another commonly used template for the optimization of mass transfer in the electrocatalysts. Zhang et al. reported an ant-nest-like hierarchical porous ultrathin carbon nanosheet network as an efficient electrocatalyst by employing NaCl as a template.139 The electrocatalyst with an optimized porous feature exhibited excellent activity toward electrocatalytic conversion of polysulfide species. Although numerous research studies have shown that hierarchical macro-, meso-, and microporous structures can provide abundant mass transport channels and the accessibility of the active site, self-reliant strategies are momentous for multifarious catalysts and reactions due to the difference in conductivity and particle size, respectively.110,121,122,140,141

3.3.2. Electrocatalyst support engineering. Tuning the porous features of the electrocatalyst may alter the electronic status of the electrocatalysts, thereby deteriorating its catalytic performance. Engineering the mass transport features of the catalyst support allows for effective tuning in the mass transport features of the catalyst without affecting its intrinsic properties. Such supports can be mainly divided into two types. The first type of support is porous material composite strategies (e.g., carbon nanotubes, metalorganic frameworks and their derivatives).7,58,147,148 As highly ordered coordination polymers, metalorganic frameworks (MOFs) have emerged as ideal support/precursors for electrocatalysts because of the high surface area and rich pore structure, facilitating the exposure of active sites and expediting mass transfer.147 In addition, the MOF-derived catalysts will enhance the electronic conductivity of the MOF materials under the base of inheriting the porous structure. More advantageously, the combination of single-atom active sites with MOF-derived materials can further enhance the distribution density and the stability of active sites, which is shown by the research of Jiang et al. They fabricated a CO2RR catalyst of Fe and Ni single-atom decorated on MOF-derived N-doped carbon support via pyrolysis of MOFs (FeNi-NC).149 The MOF-derived N-doped carbon carrier endowed the catalyst with abundant pore structure and high specific surface area (595 m2 g1), which is favorable for mass transfer and adequate dispersion of Fe and Ni atoms. To further suppress the agglomeration of single-atom, they inserted SiO2 into the MOF materials and successfully synthesized the Fe implanted N-doped porous carbon (FeSA-NC) with high Fe loading (3.46%).150 In-depth study found that the Fe atom is bound by the N atoms in the MOF-derived substrate and anchored by the FeN4/SiO2 interfaces. These features afforded the catalyst with a high Fe loading of 3.46% and excellent stability. Compared with MOF-based materials, employing commercial nano-carbon materials (such as CNTs, nanorods) as the support is more conducive to clarify the role of each component during the reaction process because of the regular pore structure and single component.24 Moreover, the accessibility and stability of the active sites can be regulated by selecting a porous composite with different wetting properties. Shul et al. reported a nanofibrous oxidecarbon composite by an in situ method of carbon nanofiber (CNF) growth by highly dispersed Ni nanoparticles.151 The in situ evolution strategy endowed the catalyst with excellent stability, while the hydrophobic properties of the carbon nanofibers improve the full diffusion of oxygen on the catalyst surface. The Pt@NOMC-A catalyst exhibited excellent HER activity and a high Pt utilization efficiency. 3.3.3. Mass transport driven by interfacial wettability control. Most of the electrochemical catalysis processes are a multi-phase reaction, which normally involves the release or consumption of gases in electrolytes. It is recognized that the intimate contact of the reactants and the catalyst is beneficial for the utilization of active sites.170 Therefore, endowing the catalyst with the hydrophilicity or hydrophobicity on-demand can promote mass transport of the reaction.171

For electrochemical reactions using gas reactants (ORR, NRR, CO2RR, etc.), gas transport channels should be also introduced to avoid water flooding. Hence, hydrophobic catalysts need to be designed to facilitate gas transport, especially for NRR and CO2RR due to the facile competitive HER. The construction of a hydrophobic catalyst can be realized by modifying the active phase with organic phases.172175 Alternatively, high hydrophobicity of the catalyst can be achieved by integrating superficial structure with low surface energy into the catalyst phase.176180 To the aim of developing a catalyst for NRR with high NH3 yield and faradaic efficiency, He et al. prepared the hydrophobic-treated Au@MOF (HT Au@MOF) by modifying the catalyst with organosilicon. The XRD results indicated the organosilicon coating treatment did not affect the MOF framework in HT Au@MOF.178 The water droplet could be quickly absorbed on Au@MOF, while freely standing on the surface of HT Au@MOF with a large water contact angle of 154, proving that a hydrophobic coating is generated over the external surface of HT Au@MOF to repel the water (Fig. 11a). Relying on the hydrophobic characteristics, the competitive HER was effectively weakened, and N2 transfer efficiency was improved. In another example, Lai et al. reported burying selenium vacancy-rich ReSe2@carbonized bacterial cellulose (CBC) between two hydrophobic CBC layers and successfully prepared an NRR catalyst Vr-ReSe2@CBC (Fig. 11c).173 The CBC layers kept Vr-ReSe2@CBC in the hydrophobic state, thus impeding the HER process and facilitating the transport of humid N2 into the catalyst layer. This control in the catalyst wettability greatly enhanced its performance for NRR. To weaken the ion poisoning and facilitate O2 transfer on the ORR catalyst, Kumeda et al. evaluated the effect of hydrophobic cations of different alkyl chain lengths on the performance of single-crystal Pt catalyst. The results showed the TAA+ with longer alkyl chains promotes the ORR activity through facilitating O2 transfer by inhibiting water flow on the Pt(111) surface.180 Modification of oxygenpermeable oil (dimethylsiliconoil) onto the conventional Pt/C catalyst was demonstrated to enhance O2 transfer.181 Zhang et al. designed a supported electrocatalyst through in situ fabrication of Au nanoparticles located on hydrophobic carbon fiber paper (Au/o-CFP).182 The hydrophobic CFP surface facilitates efficient three-phase contact points for N2, electrolyte and Au NPs (Fig. 11d). Moreover, the surface of Au/o-CFP with a lower gas-bubble contact angle of 112 than the Au/i-CFP (hydrophilic surface of CFP) of 141.5, resulted in an aerophilic property with a relatively strong interaction toward N2 bubble. The hydrophobic interface provides sufficient contact between catalyst and N2 (Fig. 11d), resulting in enhanced NRR performance and suppressed HER performance.182

In contrast to the electrocatalytic reactions using a gaseous substance as the reactant, the hydrophilic surface characteristics are conducive to the rapid shedding of gas products and the full adsorption of electrolyte on the surface of the catalyst when the reactants are in the liquid phase, which promotes the efficiency of mass transport. Roughening of an intrinsic hydrophilic electrocatalyst may render the electrocatalyst superhydrophilic.183186 Representatively, Qiu et al. proposed a coordination strategy to enhance both surface roughness and oxygen species by controlling the atom-escape of metal Ni for self-assembling super-hydrophilic hydroxide FeNiOH. The catalyst exhibits a super-hydrophilic surface, which makes the water entry rapid thus the H2 and O2 are crowded out in time. These advantages enable the FeNiOH with considerable activity, yielding 50 mA cm2 at an overpotential of 236 and 196 mV for OER and HER, respectively.183 Cao et al. constructed a novel nanostructure of one-dimensional (1D) MoS2/Ni3S2 nanoarrays on NF (3D MoS2/Ni3S2/Ni) to enhance the roughness of the electrode.185 The wettability of the 3D MoS2/Ni3S2/Ni catalyst is tested, and the small droplet entered the 3D MoS2/Ni3S2/Ni as soon as it contacted the surface, suggesting the first-class surface wettability. Modifying the catalyst with a high surface energy substance is another route to superhydrophobicity.187190 The electronic conducting polymers (ECPs) have excellent charge transfer properties, but as the catalyst for HER is rarely reported because of their poor adsorption energy per water molecule, which hinders the Volmer step. To enhance the hydrophilic properties of PPy (one ECPs), Zheng et al. designed a hybridization strategy by adopting a good water affinity SiO2. After the formation of SiO2 shell on the core-layer Ppy, the contact angle significantly decreased from 82.4 to 27.4 (Fig. 11e), convincingly confirming that the PpySiO2HMS exhibits excellent water affinity than PpyHMS.187 Thus, the hydrophilic surface of PpySiO2HMS will display improved water dissociation kinetics. With the excellent surface character, the PpySiO2HMS exhibits a lower overpotential of 123 mV at 10 mA cm2 than 192 mV at 10 mA cm2 of PpyHMS (Fig. 11f). Chen et al. highlighted an F-anion surface engineering to enhance oxygen evolution and synthesised the OER catalyst FCoOOH/NF. Benefiting from stronger ionicity of weak metalF bonds, the dynamic migration of F-anions from inside to the outside can be observed and finally enriched on the surface.191 Combining with the improved electron transfer capacity and hydrophilic surface (Fig. 11g), FCoOOH/NF exhibits improved OER performance. Numerous reports have shown that the metal hydroxides are ideal OER catalysts as the unique electronic structure and the excellent hydrophilicity of hydroxide promote mass transport. Different from the above strategy, Zhang et al. studied the effects of different precursors on the surface hydrophilicity of the Co/FeC catalyst. The tannic acid (TA) and fulvic acid (FA) derived Co/FeC catalysts demonstrate greatly similar morphologies, as well as the performance optimization process of electrocatalytic OER. Notably, OER performances of the TA-derived Co/FeC catalyst surpass that of the FA-derived Co/FeC catalyst as the super-hydrophilic surface of TA-derived Co/FeC facilitates mass transport.192 Furthermore, Zhu et al. prepared a bifunctional catalyst Co3S4L with excellent activity for HER and OER by screening hydrophilic medium and precursors. The dispersion of Co3S4L (with the different concentrations of 10, 20, 30, 50 ppm) in water is homogenous and there is no precipitation after standing for 2 h, while the hydrophobic Co3S4B (prepared using hydrophobic precursors) cannot be dispersed well in water and precipitation at the bottom of glass bottles is observed, proving that the surface characteristics of the catalyst can be regulated by selecting appropriate precursors.193

4. Summary and outlook

Approaches enabling intrinsic activity improvement and mass transport facilitation of electrocatalysts are summarized. First, recent progress in improving the intrinsic activity of electrocatalysts by electronic structure regulation is shown. Guided by d-band centre theory, the intrinsic activity of electrocatalysts is effectively improved through heteroatom doping, vacancy engineering, interface design and strain engineering. In addition, related studies on enhancing the apparent performance of electrocatalysts by mass transport optimization are also presented. We show that the construction of well-defined transport channels and regulation of interfacial wettability are the key factors to facilitate mass transport because these strategies promote the diffusion of the reaction species on the surface of the catalysts. Despite the fact that important progress has been achieved in the development of advanced electrocatalysts, most of the electrocatalysts reported so far could not meet the performance requirements for industrial application. For the future development of high-performance electrocatalysts, the following suggestions are provided.

First of all, the development of advanced characterization methods for the evaluation of the electronic states and mass transfer capability is essential. As discussed above, the d-band center of metal-based catalysts, which is an important descriptor for the intrinsic activity of the electrocatalysts, could be characterized by UPS/XPS valence-band spectra. However, traditional characterization could only be implemented in the ex situ mode. It is known that the catalyst surface usually undergoes a dynamic reconstruction process during the electrocatalytic process, which leads to the change in the d-band centre. Therefore, the development of advanced characterization methods which could be used to monitor the dynamic changes in the d-band center of the electrocatalysts will be beneficial for the understanding of the catalytic mechanism and rational design of the electronic states of the electrocatalysts. The mass transport performance could be only determined indirectly by morphological characterization studies and impedance measurements. Direct and quantitative analytic approaches for the evaluation of mass transport capability are still lacking, which impedes the reasonable design of the mass transport characteristics of the catalysts.

Second, understanding of the coupling of electronic state modulation and mass transport regulation in the design of electrocatalysts should be deepened. An ideal electrocatalyst should possess balanced intrinsic activity and mass transport capability. However, most of the studies reported so far focus either on the electronic state modulation or mass transport regulation. In further studies, theoretical models that involve both the catalytic reaction process and mass transport process should be established to provide comprehensive guidelines for the design of electrocatalysts. In addition, research on how electronic state modulation and mass transport regulation affect each other should also be studied for the optimized design of the synthetic routes of electrocatalysts.

Finally, the research paradigm for the design of electrocatalysts should be updated with the state-of-art artificial intelligence technique. Traditional semi-empirical exploration in the design of electrocatalysts is laborious. Furthermore, the research output gained from different research studies is scattered, which makes establishment of general design principles for electrocatalysts difficult. The update of the research paradigm with artificial intelligence technique is expected to greatly improve the efficiency of electrocatalyst development.
