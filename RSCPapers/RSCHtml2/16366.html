Synthesis and characterization of 9,10-bis(2-phenyl-1,3,4-oxadiazole) derivatives of anthracene: Efficient n-type emitter for organic light-emitting diodes

Abstract

With a general aim to make anthracene derivatives multifunctional (n-type emitter) and also study their suitability as electron transport layers for organic light emitting diodes (OLED), and with a more specific interest to understand the charge transport and packing pattern in the solid state due to the rotating side rings, we report the synthesis and characterization of six novel molecules (510) in which the 9 and 10 positions of anthracene have been directly substituted by phenyloxadiazole groups. We have carried out detailed studies of these molecules including photophysical, electrochemical, electroluminescent studies and solid state structure determination through crystallographic techniques. The electron affinity is very high, around 3.13.2 eV, and the ionization potential is around 5.96.0 eV, comparable to the more commonly used electron transport electroluminescent layer Alq3. The studies reveal that the new molecules being reported by us, in addition to the high thermal stability, are quite efficient in a two layer unoptimized device with the device structure ITO/-NPD/510/LiF/Al and have an emission in pure green. They also show very high efficiency as electron transport layer in device structure ITO(120nm)/-NPD(30nm)/Ir(ppy)3 doped CBP(35nm)/BCP(6nm)/5(28nm)/Al(150nm). From these studies we conclude that the anthracene derivatives also have considerable potential as multifunctional layers and as electron transport layers in OLED.

Introduction

Organic molecules having semi conducting and emission properties are of immense interest due to their potential applications in opto-electronic devices such as organic light emitting diodes (OLED), field effect transistors (FET) and solar cells.1OLED in particular have attracted considerable attention after the first report by Tang and VanSlyke.2 The search for new and efficient emitting materials and charge transport layers remains as one of the most active areas in this field.3 While bringing out efficient devices is one challenge, understanding the interactions, properties due to substitution, geometry and packing of the molecules in the solid state is another.4 The latter would help us not only to improve the device efficiency but also give us more detailed insight into the mechanism of OLED.

The basic structure of the OLED which have been widely reported in the literature is multilayered constituting of a hole transport layer (HTL), an emitting layer (EML) and an electron transport layer (ETL) with each layer having an independent function.5 Very high device performances have been achieved with these.3c,6 It has been suggested that in the next generation of OLED the focus should be on thermally robust materials with improved multifunctional properties like, emission and charge transport in a single layer.7 The process of device fabrication then gets simplified due to the multifunctional properties.8 A device with a single organic material having a good emission, charge mobilities, i.e. both hole and electron, and thermal stabilities is difficult to achieve.7

Compared to emitting HTL, fewer emitting ETL have been reported.710 One good example of electron transporting emitting material is the well known green emitter Alq3 which is still being used as a good electron transporter since its first report in a bilayer device.2 Other metal chelates have also been investigated for electron transport properties after the success achieved with Alq3.9 Molecules containing aromatic heterocyclics (electron rich or electron deficient) like oxadiazoles, triazoles, quinoxalines, triazines, benzobisazoles, benzothiadiazoles, pyridine, quinoline, phenanthrolines, anthrazoline based materials have been synthesized and suggested as electron transport and hole blocking materials for electroluminescent (EL) devices.7,11,12 Emissive groups attached to charge transport moieties in a single molecule will not only help to improve charge injection but also favour cheaper fabrication of devices as fewer layers are necessary for coating on to the device. To work as an efficient ETL the molecule should possess high electron affinity (EA) to match with the high work function of the air stable cathodes.13Oxadiazole derivatives have been investigated as electron transport materials.7,112-(4-biphenyl)-5-(4-tert-butylphenyl)-1,3,4-oxadiazole (PBD) is a molecule with EA of 2.16 eV and ionization potential (IP) of 6.06 eV that has been exploited as an electron transport material.14 To increase the EA of these materials another oxadiazole molecule was appended and an EA value of 2.8 eV was achieved.7 The dimeric oxadiazoles also have an added advantage of higher glass transition temperature Tg.7 Changing the central phenylene moiety in PBD, which lies in between the two oxadiazoles, to ethylene and to pyridyl the EA increased.7b,11e At high electric fields electron mobilities of 2 105 cm2/Vs has been noted for PBD and related compounds.7a

Anthracene has been widely investigated as an emitting material for OLED due to its excellent photoluminescence and electrochemical stability.15 Many anthracene derivatives have been synthesized and characterized, with various donating groups and electron withdrawing groups symmetrically substituted at 9,10 or 2,6 positions, as emissive layers for blue and green/yellow emission.15i,j A direct substitution of either a strong electron withdrawing or electron donating groups at 9 and 10 positions changes the electron density on the anthracene ring due to conjugation and the emission peak is red shifted.16,15i On the other hand substitution of groups like alkyl or an aromatic ring with a negligible conjugation with the anthracene ring almost retains the large band gap of the unsubstituted anthracene which has an emission in the blue region.15ah,j,k Exploiting this, bulky groups have been attached to anthracene to induce non-planarity into the molecular structure which essentially prevents aggregation in the solid state without changing the emission characteristics. In addition there is also an increase in the luminescence quantum yields.17 Non-planarity also enables formation of pin hole free thin films.18 Ando et al. have investigated p-and n-type organic semiconductors containing an anthracene unit.15l The substitution of acceptor or donor at 2 and 6 positions gave rise to n- and p-type activity with the same mobility range. Yu et al. had synthesized and characterized anthracene derivatives with electron donating groups like diaminophenylnapthalene and diphenyldiamino at the 9 and 10 positions for green host EL materials.15i They obtained very high efficiencies in three-layered device structure. In a recent study Chien et al. had tested triphenylphosphine derivatives of anthracene as a multifunctional blue emitter.15f In their studies they appended two electron deficient triphenylphosphine oxide (TPO) groups to the 9th and 10th position of anthracene to make it an n-type emitter.

With an aim to achieve an efficient n-type emitter, we rationalized that appending the highly electron withdrawing oxadiazole moiety directly to the anthracene ring would give us not only an emitting layer but also an ETL with larger EA and this would increase the multifunctionality of the layer. In addition this would also increase the thermal stability of the molecule. In this paper we report the synthesis, structural characterization of a series of six new n-type organic emitters based on 9,10-bis(2-phenyl-1,3,4-oxadiazole) derivatives of anthracene (510). Investigation of the photophysical, thermal and electrochemical studies are presented. Solid state structure of two of the derivatives (5 and 10) were determined by single crystal X-ray crystallography while detailed understanding of the absorption/emission and reorganization energies of all the derivatives were obtained by theoretical studies. Simple bi-layer EL devices were fabricated for all these anthracene derivatives using 4,4-bis[N-(1-naphthyl-1)-N-phenyl-amino]-biphenyl (-NPD) as hole transport layer and the resultant electroluminescent properties analyzed. A three layer device with 5 as electron transport material and phosphorescent Iridium complex as the emitter was also fabricated and tested. These anthracene derivatives show promising applications as n-type emitters. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report of multifunctionality of anthracene derivatives as n-type green emitters for OLED.

Results and discussion

Synthesis and characterization

We have synthesized and characterized six 9,10-bis(2-phenyl-1,3,4-oxadiazole) derivatives of anthracene 510. The synthetic routes to these are illustrated in Schemes 1 and 2. The basic differences in the six derivatives lie in the substitution at the phenyl ring. In 5 there is no alkyl group attached to the phenyl ring (R1 = R2 = H), while 6 has a methyl group at the para and in 7 at the ortho position respectively. 8, 9,and 10 have methoxy, t-butyl and hexyl groups substituted at the para position respectively. 1 was prepared as per the published procedure.19 Subsequent bromination20 of 1 led to 9,10-dibromo product 2 with 81% yield. Conversion of the dibromo derivative to dinitrile 3 was achieved by Rosenmund-von Braun synthesis21 using cuprous cyanide. Freshly prepared Bu3SnN322 was used to obtain the ditetrazole intermediate 4. 4 was confirmed by negative-ion ESI mass spectrometry and the relevant peak observed at m/z425.2(M-1). In the final step 510 were obtained by treating 4 with corresponding acid chlorides and refluxing in pyridine solvent (5682%). All the anthracene derivatives were fully characterized by spectroscopic methods and elemental analysis. The details of the experimental characterization data are available in the ESI.

Molecular geometry and crystal structure

The crystal structures of 5 and 10 have been determined. The X-ray molecular structures are shown in Fig. 1. In both crystals there are two independent molecules of the anthracene derivative and each lie about the crystallographic inversion centre. The oxadiazole ring is symmetrically twisted with respect to the central anthracene ring with a twist angle of 79 in one molecule and 66 in the other molecule in 5. Similarly in 10 we find the corresponding angles to be 62 and 57. B3LYP/6-31G(d,p) reproduces the structures of these molecules, with an accuracy of 0.025 for bond lengths and 1 for bond angles. But the twist angles are a bit different as predicted by DFT for both molecules. This difference in the theoretical value and the experimentally determined one can be attributed to the intermolecular forces. The experimental bond length of the anthraceneoxadiazole connecting CC bond is around 1.48 which has a more single bond character (free rotation). Oxadiazole prefers to have conjugation with the phenyl ring in the ground state (oxadiazole-phenyl part is planar). This would also explain that 10 with a longer hexyl chain and thus decreased intermolecular interactions relatively has a smaller dihedral angle. Extending the single molecule DFT studies to the other molecules (6,7,8 and 9), we obtain essentially similar angles in the range 5253.

Conclusion

We have shown that 510 have considerable potential to act as n-type emitters and as electron transporting layers in OLED. Blending in anthracene's superior photophysical properties and oxadiazole's good electron transport properties into a single moiety has been achieved. The thermal robustness of the materials also increases as seen from the higher dissociation and melting temperatures (Td = 400461 C; Tm = 180296 C). The Tg (52131 C) are also very high. In the solid state, from the data obtained for 5 and 10, we infer that the stabilizing interactions in these derivatives are CH and . The electrochemical studies reveal that these derivatives have reversible reduction process and quasi reversible oxidation process. Theoretical studies reveal that the HOMO and the LUMO lie on the anthracene ring and the orbital energy values of these are in good agreement to the ones estimated from the cyclic voltametry studies. The absorption and emission of these derivatives lie in the range 3.083.11 eV and 2.392.42 eV respectively. Basically the major contribution to the absorption is a HOMOLUMO transition. High fluorescence is observed for all the derivatives (the quantum yields = 0.760.98). The EL performance of all these devices is good and can be attributed to the high electron affinity of these molecules (3.1 eV), which offers a low barrier for electron injection 0.6 eV from the cathode LiF/Al. The injection barrier is quite low in comparison to the injection barrier in the case of Alq3, where the EA is 2.7 eV, which is mostly used as an emissive material as well as an electron transport material. Due to the electron withdrawing nature of the oxadiazole ring, the central anthracene ring has only a partial conjugation with this group in the ground state, the twist angle between the two rings calculated without including any intermolecular interaction is in the range 5253. The electron density on the central ring is modified due to an increase in conjugation on excitation and hence the emission takes place in the green.

Based on the calculation it is seen that the conjugation between the rings in the ground state tends to increase in the excited state due to the charge transfer between the anthracene and oxadiazole. Thus there is a rotation of the oxadiazole ring on excitation with decrease in the twist angle between the anthracene and the oxadiazole ring. In the polaron hopping model regime, the charge transport takes place through formation of cations and anions. For the rotation of the rings, both in the formation of cation and anion, slightly larger reorganization energy is required for these derivatives when compared to the unsubstituted anthracene. In spite of the larger reorganization energy due to this rotation, efficient ET characteristics are seen, which could be attributed to the larger transfer integral between the neighboring molecules in the solid state.

In the un-optimized two layer device we find that the power efficiency, current efficiency and luminous intensity is reasonably good. The CIEx,y coordinates obtained for 6 (0.25, 0.71) lie in pure green according to Nation Television System Committee (NTSC) green color standard (0.21,0.71). Thus these molecules act as both ET and EM layers in a non-doped OLED device. Further studies of the compounds as ETL in a three layer device with Ir complex as the phosphorescent emitter, showed reasonably high efficiency when compared to the standard Alq3 as the ET layer.

Experimental

Measurements and instruments

Analytical Reagent grade reagents were used without further purification. 1H-NMR spectra were recorded on a Gemini (200 MHz) spectrometer and Bruker Avance (300 MHz) spectrometer in CDCl3 and 13C-NMR spectra on a Bruker Avance (300 MHz) spectrometer, both with TMS as standard. Mass spectra were obtained by using electrospray ionization iontrap mass spectrometry (Thermofinnigan, Sanzox, CA, USA) and fast atom bombardment (FAB) mass spectra were recorded on VG-AUTOSPEC spectrometer. Elemental analyses were performed using a Vario-EL elemental analyzer. UV-Visible absorption spectra were measured on a Jasco V-550 spectrophotometer. Computational methodology

The ground state geometries for all the molecules (including a model molecule) in this study were obtained at DFT/B3LYP/6-31G (d, p) level using the software G03.28 Where possible the crystal structures were taken as an initial guess for optimization. Frequencies were evaluated for the optimized geometries to ensure the structure obtained was a minimum on the potential energy surface. To get a deeper insight into the absorption spectrum, excitation energies were calculated using time dependent DFT RPA methodology at the B3LYP/6-31G (d, p) level. The vertical S0S1 vertical excitation energy obtained at B3LYP ground state geometry was taken to be the absorption maximum.29 To get a deeper insight into the emission, the first singlet excited state geometries were obtained with optimization using HF-CIS/6-31G (d,p) model chemistry. The vertical absorption at the excited state geometry was taken as the emission maxima.30
