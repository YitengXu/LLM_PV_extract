Abstract

Conjugated copolymers based on isoindigo/fluorinated isoindigo and bis(dialkylthienyl)benzodithiophene, PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID, were designed and synthesized for organic photovoltaic applications. The isoindigo fluorination made PBDTTâ€“FID show high PCE of 5.52% and 7.04% in its conventional and inverted PSCs, respectively.

Recently, tremendous efforts on low band gap (LBG) copolymers and their polymer solar cells (PSCs) have been devoted to improve the power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) from 1% to over 10%.1 Isoindigo (ID) was newly developed as an efficient acceptor block due to the strong electron-withdrawing character and large optical transition dipole of two lactam rings. Reynolds et al. reported the first ID-based photovoltaic oligomer, exhibiting a PCE of 1.76%.2 Andersson et al. synthesized a new ID-based copolymer, PTI-1, using thiophene as a donor block with designed side chains. PSCs fabricated from PTI-1 and PC71BM as the photoactive layer showed a PCE of 6.3%.3 Other ID-based copolymers containing different donor blocks, such as dithienosilole, benzodithiophene, naphthalene, anthracene and cyclopentadithiophene, were also synthesized and investigated later.4 The ID-based polymers reported so far exhibit broad absorption and the low-lying HOMO (âˆ’5.50 to âˆ’5.90 V) and LUMO (âˆ’3.70 to âˆ’4.00 V) with low band gaps ranging from 1.3 to 1.8 eV. In addition, they possess high carrier mobilities. Pei et al. reported ID-based conjugated polymers with hole mobility up to 1.06 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1.5 Bao et al. introduced siloxane-terminated solubilizing groups on the isoindigo skeleton for shortening the Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking distance, which improved the hole mobility to 2.48 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1.6 After this, Pei et al. reported another fluorinated ID-based polymer PFII2T with good ambient stability. The electron mobility increased from 10âˆ’2 to 0.43 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 with high hole mobility up to 1.85 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1.7

In this communication, we firstly reported the design and synthesis of the Dâ€“A photovoltaic copolymers of isoindigo (ID)/fluorinated isoindigo (FID) and bis(dialkylthienyl) benzodithiophene (BDTT), PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID, along with their detailed characterization. The BDTT unit is selected as a donor block because the thienyl-substitution is helpful to broaden the absorption band, lower the HOMO level and improve hole mobility of the related copolymers.1c Conventional PSCs fabricated from PBDTTâ€“ID/PBDTTâ€“FID and PC71BM as the photoactive layer showed PCEs of 4.76% and 5.52% with high Voc up to 0.94 V. Using an inverted device structure with a thin conjugated polyelectrolyte layer, PSCs based on PBDTTâ€“FID achieved an impressively high PCE of up to 7.04%.

The UV-vis absorption spectra of PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID in chloroform solution and in thin films are shown in Fig. 1a. Both copolymers exhibit a high energy absorption band due to Ï€â€“Ï€* transition and a low-energy band due to intramolecular charge transfer. The vibrational shoulders in the longer wavelength region are attributed to aggregation resulting from strong polymer chain packing even in solutions.1a,8 In chloroform solution, PBDTTâ€“ID showed broad absorptions with main peaks at 447, 649 and 679 nm. After introducing fluorine atoms, PBDTTâ€“FID exhibited a red-shifted absorption. A similar behavior was observed in the absorption spectra of the polymer films. The slightly broader peaks in the wavelength range of 500â€“800 nm showed different red-shifts of 5, 13 nm and 10, 23 nm for PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID, respectively. The reason can be explained by the somewhat formation of Ï€-stacked structures in the solid state. It is known that the fluorination can afford Fâ€“H, Fâ€“Ï€F and Fâ€“S interactions and efficiently enhance this aggregation of polymer backbones.1a,c,8 This phenomenon was also identified here by the broader absorption shape and pronounced shoulders of a low-energy absorption band of PBDTTâ€“FID. The onset absorption edges of the thin films were at 777 nm and 783 nm for PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID, which determined the corresponding optical bandgaps to be 1.60 and 1.58 eV, respectively. Both copolymers show low bandgaps with broad absorption bands, which is very important to harvest more solar photons in their PSCs.

The HOMO and LUMO levels of the copolymers were measured by cyclic voltammetry. As shown in Fig. 1b, the onset oxidation potentials of PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID are located at 1.12 V and 1.24 V, leading to the corresponding HOMO levels to be âˆ’5.52 eV and âˆ’5.64 eV, respectively. The deep HOMO levels indicate that both copolymers are air stable and have desired high Voc for PSCs.9 Isoindigo fluorination can effectively lower the HOMO level, and PBDTTâ€“FID exhibits a 0.12 eV deeper HOMO level than that of PBDTTâ€“ID, which is in agreement with our previous fluorinated copolymers.1c On the other hand, the onset reduction potentials of PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID are positioned at âˆ’0.55 eV and âˆ’0.42 eV, resulting in the corresponding LUMO levels to be âˆ’3.85 eV and âˆ’3.98 eV, respectively. PBDTTâ€“FID shows a deeper LUMO level than that of PBDTTâ€“ID, indicating the positive effect of fluorine atoms due to their strong electron-withdrawing properties. The LUMO energy levels of both copolymers are aligned with fullerene derivatives (Fig. S1â€ ), thus effective charge separation and transfer can be obtained.9 The electrochemical bandgaps (Eg) of PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID were calculated to be about 1.67 and 1.66 eV. In order to explore the electronic properties of PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID, molecular simulations (BDTTâ€“ID and BDTTâ€“FID) were performed on their trimers.10 As shown in Fig. S2,â€  the localization of HOMO distributed in the middle part of the conjugated molecular system, but mainly focused on the donor skeletons. From a HOMO â†’ LUMO transition, the electron clouds of LUMO localized on the part of the ID fragment, which indicated that there was significant charge-transfer character. This is consistent with the observed strong low-energy absorption band in Fig. 1a. The electronic features of the corresponding donor and acceptor have an important effect on the HOMOs and LUMOs of the copolymers. However, the introduced fluorine atoms had a very little influence on the HOMO and LUMO distributions. Compared with HOMO, HOMO âˆ’ 1 orbitals are mainly on the donor units, which implies that the internal charge transfer is possible. This transfer trend becomes more evident in BDTTâ€“FID with FID, which was also observed in LUMO + 1. The strong electron-withdrawing properties of fluorine atoms enhanced the electron deficient power of isoindigo, which would promote the charge transfer and result in a lower bandgap.

The hole mobilities of PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID were measured by a space charge limited current (SCLC) method (Fig. S3aâ€ ),11 and calculated to be 6.5 Ã— 10âˆ’4 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 and 3.2 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1, respectively. PBDTTâ€“FID exhibits one order higher hole mobility than PBDTTâ€“ID, which can be attributed to the fluorine substitution on isoindigo skeletons. This fluorination would reduce charge recombination and boost the long-lived charge separation, resulting in improved carrier transporting properties.12 As shown in Fig. S3b,â€  the distance between F and H (BDTTâ€“FID, 2.26 Ã…) is shorter than that between H and H (BDTTâ€“ID, 2.28 Ã…). The dihedral angle between isoindigo and thiophene for BDTTâ€“FID is calculated to be 16.64Â°, which is smaller than 19.62Â° determined for BDTTâ€“ID. The results indicated that PBDTTâ€“FID favoured a more planar conformation and better polymer packing, which enabled the electrons to be delocalized across the whole conjugated molecular system for higher carrier mobility.13

The photovoltaic properties were firstly investigated in conventional PSCs with a structure of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/copolymer:PC71BM/Ca/Al. The device performances were optimized by varying the weight ratios of blend, changing the thickness of the active layer, choosing the different solvent and additives. The optimized weight ratio between the copolymer and PC71BM is 1:2 (w/w). 1,8-Diiodooctane (DIO) was used as a processing additive to optimize the morphology of the active blends. All the active layers were processed with 3% (v/v) DIO in o-dichlorobenzene. The current densityâ€“voltage (Jâ€“V) curves are presented in Fig. 2a. The PBDTTâ€“ID device exhibited a PCE of 4.76% with a Voc of 0.88 V, a Jsc of 8.95 mA cmâˆ’2, and an FF of 0.60. The PCE is comparable to those values obtained for other reported ID-containing copolymers.4 When using PBDTTâ€“FID:PC71BM (1:2, w/w) as the active layer, the best performance was obtained with a Voc of 0.94 V, a Jsc of 9.62 mA cmâˆ’2, and an FF of 0.61, giving rise to a PCE of 5.52%. Since the Voc is proportional to the difference between the HOMO level of the polymer and the LUMO level of the PCBM,14 as expected, both PSCs showed relatively high Voc values due to the low-lying HOMO levels. The PBDTTâ€“FID device exhibited higher Voc, which can be attributed to the deeper HOMO energy level of PBDTTâ€“FID arising from the introduction of fluorine atoms on isoindigo skeletons. Although the FFs were almost the same for both PSCs, the PBDTTâ€“FID device also showed higher Jsc than the PBDTTâ€“ID device. The reasons for PBDTTâ€“FID possessing smaller bandgap and enhanced hole mobility have been discussed above. This was further verified by external quantum efficiency (EQE) (Fig. 2b) and surface morphology investigations by AFM (Fig. S4â€ ). In Fig. 2b, the PBDTTâ€“FID device displays wider curve shape and higher photoconversion efficiency with a peak EQE value of over 47%. This result matched well with the improved Jsc of the PBDTTâ€“FID device obtained from Jâ€“V measurements. The surface roughness measured from the AFM topographic image was 2.3 and 1.9 for the PBDTTâ€“ID blend and the PBDTTâ€“FID blend, respectively. The results indicated that the domain of the PC71BM rich phase of the PBDTTâ€“FID blend was more continuous and the domain size was smaller than that of the PBDTTâ€“ID blend. This morphological information can explain the relatively high Jsc and FF of the PBDTTâ€“FID device, which is in accordance with the EQE measurements.

To optimize the photovoltaic performance of PBDTTâ€“FID, inverted devices with structures ITO/ZnO/PBDTTâ€“FID:PC71BM (1:2)/MoO3/Ag and ITO/ZnO/PNFBr/PBDTTâ€“FID:PC71BM (1:2)/MoO3/Ag were fabricated. As shown in Fig. 2a, inverted PSCs without the PNFBr layer showed a Voc of 0.90 V, a Jsc of 10.16 mA cmâˆ’2, an FF of 0.62 and the corresponding PCE of 5.70%. After introducing a thin PNFBr layer, inverted PSCs showed a Voc of 0.92 V, a Jsc of 11.30 mA cmâˆ’2, a FF of 0.68 and a related PCE of 7.04%. To our knowledge, this is the highest PCE reported to date for a PSC fabricated using an ID-based polymer. Compared to the conventional device, the Voc of the inverted device is not changed so much, suggesting the presence of an ohmic contact between the active layer and the collecting electrode.15 However, the Jsc and FF have been increased to a relatively large extent, especially for the PSCs with the PNFBr layer, resulting in a higher PCE. The enhanced Jsc can be verified by the improved EQE response as shown in Fig. 2b, with increased maximum peak value up to about 53% (with PNFBr layer) for more efficient light harvest. The Jsc calculated here by integration of the EQE curve also agrees well with the Jsc obtained from the Jâ€“V measurement of the inverted device. The deposited PNFBr layer also played an important role in PCEs because it could suppress bimolecular recombination and reduce the barrier height between the hole collecting electrode and the active layer, which led to improved Jsc and FF values.16

In summary, we synthesized two Dâ€“A copolymers based on isoindigo (ID)/fluorinated isoindigo (FID) and bis(dialkylthienyl)benzodithiophene (BDTT), PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID, for organic photovoltaic applications. The fluorinated substitution made this type of copolymer prefer a more planar configuration arising from introduced Fâ€“H, Fâ€“S and Fâ€“Ï€F interactions. As expected, PBDTTâ€“FID has a smaller bandgap, lower energy levels and a better carrier mobility compared to PBDTTâ€“ID. Conventional PSCs fabricated from PBDTTâ€“ID and PBDTTâ€“FID blends with PC71BM (1:2) showed PCEs of 4.76% and 5.52% with high Voc up to 0.94 V. Using an inverted device structure, the PBDTTâ€“FID device with a thin conjugated polyelectrolyte layer achieved an impressively high PCE of 7.04%. The molecular engineering strategies toward both BDTT and ID blocks demonstrated in this work have been proved to be effective approaches to develop high performance LBG copolymers for future organic solar cell applications.
