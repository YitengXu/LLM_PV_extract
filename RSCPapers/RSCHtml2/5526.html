Abstract

To achieve highly efficient photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting, great efforts have been dedicated to develop various photoelectrode materials in pure water electrolytes. Recently, some studies are turning to hydrogen production from seawater or wastewater, to save valuable freshwater resources and make waste profitable. Here, we fabricate one-dimensional SnO2@BiVO4 coreâshell nanorod arrays decorated with cobalt-phosphate cocatalyst (1D SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi NRAs), which show efficient charge separation and transport properties due to the rational design of conducting core-layer and introduction of the cocatalyst. The as-obtained SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi photoanodes exhibit largely enhanced PEC water oxidation performance: the maximum photocurrent density of 2.63 mA cmâ2 at 1.23 V vs. RHE is 6.58 times higher than that of pristine BiVO4 films (â¼0.40 mA cmâ2). Moreover, the ternary photoanode exhibits promising PEC performance for urea oxidation, giving rise to a current density of 3.44 mA cmâ2 at 1.23 V vs. RHE in a neutral urea electrolyte. This work presents a sunlight-driven, eco-friendly and sustainable method to produce hydrogen and simultaneously treat urea-rich wastewater.

1. Introduction

Hydrogen, as a regenerative and eco-friendly fuel, has received a great deal of attention in the past decades because of its large potential in replacing conventional fossil fuels.1â4 Photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting into H2 and O2 using sunlight has been regarded as one of the most promising routes for the green production of hydrogen.5â9 To achieve highly efficient PEC water splitting, great efforts have been dedicated to develop various photoelectrode materials in pure water electrolytes. Recently, some studies have turned to hydrogen production from seawater or wastewater, given that very precious freshwater is being preserved for agriculture, industry and human needs.10â12 For instance, urea [CO(NH2)2] has been proposed as an alternative hydrogen source for its gravimetric hydrogen content of 6.7%. Additionally, millions of tons of human urine are produced, which is 500 times more than the market demand of 0.5 Mt per day.5 Therefore, sunlight-driven photoelectrochemical hydrogen releasing from urea could provide a sustainable solution for hydrogen production and new insights into the purification of wastewater.

Compared to the two-electron hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) is comparatively more complex because this reaction requires a 4eâ transfer process. Similarly, the urea oxidation reaction (UOR) has intrinsically more sluggish kinetics owing to a 6eâ transfer process and complicated gas evolution steps (N2 and CO2).13 Thus, the oxidation reaction of water or urea that occurs in the photoanode is a speed-limiting step in the PEC system.14 The monoclinic scheelite BiVO4 with a band gap of â¼2.4 eV exhibits high potential as a photoanode since it has a good capability to harvest visible light and a high theoretical solar-to-hydrogen conversion efficiency of 9.1%.15 However, the practical performance of pristine BiVO4 photoanodes always suffers from slow charge transport, poor charge separation and sluggish oxidation kinetics,16,17 which limit its overall quantum efficiency. To overcome these drawbacks, hybrid nanostructures based on BiVO4 have been constructed, combining with another semiconductor, conducting materials, as well as cocatalysts. However, defects and interfacial energy levels may exist at the new generated interface due to the hard match between the photocatalyst and the functional layer, which results in serious recombination during the charge separation and transport process.18,19 For instance, Li et al. found that the interfacial energetics between the semiconductor and cocatalysts play a critical role in their PEC performance by constructing a Schottky heterojunction photoanode.20 Therefore, a well-constructed and highly-matched hybrid based on a BiVO4 photoanode is still highly necessary for achieving an efficient PEC process.

In this work, we present the design and synthesis of a nanostructured photoanode based on an electron-capture-layer@photocatalyst@hole-capture-catalyst composite for a high performance PEC process. The BiVO4 is uniformly dispersed on the surface of SnO2 nanorod arrays (NRAs) by a seed-directed in situ growth method, which guarantees a highly matched interface between SnO2 and BiVO4 and thereby facilitates the charge transport. After further deposition of Co-Pi cocatalysts, the as-fabricated SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi photoanode exhibits a largely enhanced PEC water splitting performance with a maximum photocurrent density of 2.63 mA cmâ2 at 1.23 V vs. RHE, which is 6.58 times higher than that of pristine BiVO4 (â¼0.40 mA cmâ2). This value is also superior to most reported BiVO4-based photoanodes (most are below 2 mA cmâ2). Moreover, the as-prepared ternary photoanode has been demonstrated to show high PEC activity for urea oxidation, with an enhanced photocurrent density of 3.44 mA cmâ2 at 1.23 V vs. RHE. As far as we know, this is the first time that the PEC urea oxidation has been achieved in a neutral solution.

The SnO2 NRAs directly grown on FTO glass substrates were prepared by a modified low-temperature CVD method. In a typical experiment, 100 mg of SnCl2Â·2H2O was mixed with 1 g of ZnCl2 powder to form a mixture, which was then put into a ceramic crucible. 3.1 Preparation and characterization

3.2 PEC water splitting

The PEC measurements were achieved under AM 1.5 G illumination from the backside of the photoanodes in 0.1 M PBS (pH = 7). As shown in Fig. 3a, the photocurrents of all the BiVO4-based materials increases with anodic bias, implying the successful water oxidation on these photoelectrodes. The SnO2@BiVO4 coreâshell NRAs shows an obvious improved current density compared with the pristine BiVO4, which can be further enhanced by deposition of Co-Pi. To further study the photoresponse of these photoanodes, transient photocurrent measurements were carried out under chopped light illumination (Fig. 3b). The LSVs clearly demonstrate that the SnO2@BiVO4 NRAs photoanode exhibits a fast light response, which indicates the improvement in PEC performance after introducing the SnO2 core. It is worth mentioning that the decoration of the Co-Pi catalyst further accelerates the surface oxidation kinetics with a more prompt and reproducible photocurrent response. Moreover, the photoconversion efficiency (PCE) of each photoanode was calculated based on the currentâvoltage curve as a function of the applied voltage (Fig. 3c), where a much improved PCE of 0.6% at 0.8 V vs. RHE was obtained for SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi, tenfold that of pristine BiVO4 (0.06%). As reported previously, the recombination of generated charge carriers will lead to an anodic photocurrent gradually decreased to a steady state plateau.30 The chronoamperometry measurement under chopped light illumination gives a fast and reproducible photocurrent response in five cycles of switching light ON and OFF for the SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi photoanode (Fig. 3d). In contrast, the pristine BiVO4 photoanode shows a relatively steady current but with the lowest activity, while the SnO2@BiVO4 photoanode displays a huge enhancement in photocurrent density but suffers an obvious photocurrent spike. In order to improve the charge injection efficiency, the Co-Pi cocatalyst was introduced to accelerate the surface oxidation kinetics, then the spike disappears and the charge accumulation diminishes. This indicates that the ternary photoanode has suppressed electronâhole recombination as well as fast surface oxidation kinetics. The incident photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE) was measured to understand the relationship between the PEC activity and the wavelength of the incident light (Fig. 3e). Typically, the maximum IPCEs are obtained at 400 nm, which are 16.0%, 27.6% and 47.4% for BiVO4, SnO2@BiVO4 and the SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi photoanode respectively. The higher IPCE value of the SnO2@BiVO4 coreâshell heterostructure compared with pristine BiVO4 suggests fast charge separation and rapid electron transport of BiVO4 after introducing SnO2, which largely increases the solar energy conversion efficiency. The catalytic effect of Co-Pi further increased the IPCE value of the SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi photoanode, which is nearly three times higher than the IPCE of pristine BiVO4. Subsequently, the absorbed photon-to-current efficiency (APCE) of SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi at 400 nm is 53.4%, which is 2.9 times as high as that of pristine BiVO4 (Fig. 3f), revealing that absorbed photons can be utilized more efficiently due to the improved charge separation. The PEC performance of the ternary photoanode in this work is superior to most reported BiVO4-based photoanodes without sacrificial agents, as shown in Table S1.â 31 It is worth mentioning that the SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi photoanode has the highest IPCE efficiency at 400 nm in all reported photoanodes based on BiVO4/SnO2.

3.3 PEC urea oxidation

Urea is a major component (â¼2% mass fraction) of human/animal urine, and millions of tons are produced every day.32 However, human urine has been considered as wastewater that could raise environmental issues such as nitrate contamination of ground water.33 The performance of the SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi coreâshell NRAs photoanodes for PEC urea oxidation was measured in a neutral medium of nearly real urine solution containing 0.1 M PBS and 2% urea. To investigate the advantages of PEC urea oxidation with the contrast of electrochemical urea oxidation, the performance of BiVO4 and SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi for urea oxidation under light illumination was studied (Fig. 4a). Without illumination, both the pristine BiVO4 and SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi NRAs can hardly produce urea oxidation in a neutral electrolyte. Surprisingly, the onset potential of urea oxidation shifts to 0.2 V vs. RHE under light illumination, which is significantly lower than the potential required for urea electrolysis (1.6 V vs. RHE). In comparison to pristine BiVO4, SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi exhibits an even more negative onset potential and significantly excellent PEC performance for urea oxidation. Compared with the reported articles, PEC oxidation of urea can effectively convert solar energy into chemical energy and greatly reduce the overpotential compared with the electrochemical process. It is also noted that the SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi photoanode in this work exhibits the largest cathodic shift of the onset potential compared to other photoelectrodes reported in the literature (Table S2â ). Furthermore, the urea oxidation in a neutral solution in this work is mild, which is more environmentally friendly and safe than that of strong alkali solution. As shown in Fig. 4b, the 100-SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi NRAs exhibit the best PEC performance for urea oxidation, giving rise to a current density of 3.44 mA cmâ2 at 1.23 V vs. RHE. In the absence or presence of urea (Fig. 4c), both pristine BiVO4 and SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi have a higher photocurrent density for urea oxidation (3.44 and 0.84 mA cmâ2 for SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi and BiVO4 respectively) than that of water oxidation (2.63 and 0.40 mA cmâ2 for SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi and BiVO4 respectively) at 1.23 V vs. RHE. Moreover, both of the two photoanodes produce two stages of oxidation, which suggests that urea oxidation takes precedence over water oxidation, which is consistent with their oxidation electrode potentials. 3.4 Mechanism discussion

The photoluminescence (PL) behavior, originating from the recombination of photoinduced electronâhole pairs, can reflect the surface separation efficiency of charge carriers in semiconductors.35 As shown in Fig. 5a, the pristine BiVO4 photoanode displays rather strong PL emission peaks within the 520â545 nm region. In contrast, the PL intensity of SnO2@BiVO4 decreases significantly, and the SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi photoanode shows a further decline, which indicates suppressed recombination of photogenerated charge carriers with the incorporation of SnO2 and Co-Pi. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) studies were carried out to investigate the charge transport behavior in these photoanodes, as shown in Fig. 5b. The ternary SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi photoanode displays the smallest charge transfer resistance (corresponding to the semicircle diameter in the plot) among these three samples, indicating the fastest charge transport kinetics. Compared to pristine BiVO4, the resistance of SnO2@BiVO4 is obviously smaller than that of BiVO4, suggestive of the superior charge transport capability of the SnO2 nanorods. Moreover, the flat band potential is obtained by extrapolating the MottâSchottky plot to 1/C2 = 0 (Fig. 5c), which exhibits an evidently positive shift for SnO2@BiVO4, implying a decrease in the bending of band edges between SnO2 and BiVO4. This confirms the contribution of the SnO2 buffer layer to the improved charge separation. Based on the discussion above, we herein attempted to construct a model to clarify the enhanced PEC performance in the SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi NRAs system. As indicated in Fig. 5d, ordered 1D SnO2 NRAs were selected for the construction of a coreâshell structure as an appropriate charge acceptor to facilitate charge transport; a heterojunction formed with two n-type semiconductors accelerates the charge separation efficiency due to the favorable band bending between SnO2 and BiVO4; finally Co-Pi oxygen evolution catalyst (OEC) promotes the surface reactions.

Fig. 5e illustrates the mechanism of solar-driven hydrogen production from urea solution using a band energy diagram. Under light illumination, photoexcited electrons (eâ) and holes (h+) produced in BiVO4 occupy the conduction band (EC) and valence band (EV) respectively. For the pristine BiVO4 without the SnO2 buffer layer, the photogenerated electrons in the EC will reach the defect trapping states (DTS*) at the FTO/BiVO4 interfaces through path â , which may facilitate charge recombination and result in the degradation of PEC performance.27 In contrast, for the SnO2@BiVO4 photoanode with suitable thickness of the SnO2 layer, the DTS* located at the FTO/BiVO4 interface are fully passivated. Furthermore, the SnO2 buffer layer induces favorable band bending in the BiVO4 layer (Fig. 5c), which leads to efficient electron transport towards the FTO through path â¡ and effectively prohibits charge recombination. A schematic illustration of the band alignment of SnO2 and BiVO4 is shown in Fig. S7â  based on the MottâSchottky curves and ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy (UPS) analyses. The lower flat band of BiVO4 than that of SnO2 guarantees the electron transfer from BiVO4 to SnO2, which is consistent with the XPS results. In addition, the photogenerated holes in the EV for BiVO4 probably cannot tunnel through the SnO2 hole blocking layer. Instead, the highly dispersed Co-Pi can be oxidized by the holes to higher valence Co3+ or Co4+,36,37 which efficiently catalyze urea and water oxidation.

4. Conclusions

In summary, well-defined 1D SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi NRAs directly grown on a FTO substrate have been successfully fabricated, where the SnO2 serves as the electron acceptor to facilitate charge transport due to its extremely high intrinsic electron mobility. Favorable band bending and satisfactory hole blocking capability with a SnO2 buffer layer between BiVO4 and a FTO substrate enhanced the charge separation and reduced the chance of recombination. Finally, an efficient Co-Pi as a surface electrocatalyst is utilized to accelerate the sluggish kinetics. The resulting SnO2@BiVO4/Co-Pi photoanode exhibits largely enhanced performance of PEC water splitting and urea oxidation compared with the pristine BiVO4 photoanode. Promisingly, the successful demonstration of solar driven hydrogen releasing from neutral urea solution will provide a sustainable solution for hydrogen production and new insights into wastewater treatment.
