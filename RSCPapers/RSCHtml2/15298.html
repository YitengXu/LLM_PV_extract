Abstract

High purity green up-conversion (UC) materials have wide applications in fluorescent labeling, plastic recycling and displays, etc. However, Yb3+/Er3+-based UC materials exhibit poor color purity of green light, and their output color is normally unstable under a variable excitation power density, which hinders their application in the aforementioned areas. Herein, we report the highly stable and efficient pure green UC of rod-like Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ submicro-crystals synthesized via a newly developed ion-exchange modified (IEM) method. IEM-derived NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ monodisperse submicro-crystals present not only a high-purity green output color (due to the large intensity ratio of green to red, IGreen/IRed â 5) but also excellent color stability under varying conditions, such as excitation power density and doping concentration of Yb3+/Ho3+. More importantly, IEM arouses an enormously enhanced UC emission intensity of â¼3700-fold that of the precursor crystals (PC). The UC emission intensity of IEM Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ is â¼4 times stronger than that of its high temperature annealing (HTA) counterpart. The emission intensity of the single green band is â¼40% stronger than that of the IEM Î²-NaGdF4:20%Yb3+,2%Er3+ submicro-crystals. A proof-of-concept fluorescent labeling of clover patterns (15 Ã 12 mm) printed from NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ ink with stable and high purity green fluorescence is demonstrated under excitation with various power densities.

Introduction

Over the last few decades, trivalent lanthanide-activated anti-Stokes up-conversion (UC) materials, which convert low energy near-infrared (NIR) photon excitation into high energy photon emission, have been attracting increasing attention due to their wide applications in displays, solid-state lasers, phototherapy, fluorescent labels, and solar cell converters.1â11 Among them, Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ and Gd2O2S:Yb3+,Er3+ micro-crystals have been recognized as the most efficient green UC phosphors upon excitation of a 980 nm laser diode (LD).2,7,12 Recently, Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Er3+ micro-crystals have also been proposed as a promising alternative to Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ as an efficient green UC emitter, owing to its similar structure and low phonon energy.13,14

Yb3+/Er3+-Based UC materials show two comparable emission bands in the green (â¼540 nm) and red (â¼655 nm) regions, leading to low green color purity upon excitation with a 980 nm LD.15â17 For example, Guo et al. recently reported the pure red UC emission of Yb3+/Er3+-based UC materials, which was caused by the large intensity ratio of red to green.18,19 Unfortunately, the output color of Yb3+/Er3+-based UC materials strongly varies with excitation power density, due to the varying intensity ratio of green to red (IGreen/IRed).8,20â22 These features hinder their applications in many areas such as fluorescent labeling, plastic recycling and display technologies. High purity green UC emission is hard to realize for Yb3+/Er3+-based UC materials.

Yb3+/Ho3+-activated UC materials, which present similar UC emission properties to Yb3+/Er3+-activated UC materials, are an excellent alternative for obtaining efficient pure green UC emission due to the following two reasons.23â30 Firstly, they yield high purity green UC emission output color, owing to the large intensity ratio of IGreen/IRed > 4.23â30 Although there still exists a certain amount of red emission intensity, pure green output emission color can be observed owing to the rather low sensitivity of human eyes to red photons, which is â¼20 times weaker than that to green photons.31 Secondly, the pure green UC emission of the Yb3+/Ho3+ pair is highly stable under variable conditions, such as a variable excitation power density. However, they are rarely studied compared with Yb3+/Er3+-activated UC materials.32,33

In the last decade, with the fast development of various novel synthesis strategies, several novel synthesis methods for novel Î²-NaGdF4 micro-crystals have been proposed in the literature.34â36 In comparison with traditional high temperature solid state reaction method, the novel hydrothermal route can finely tailor the morphology and size of Î²-NaGdF4 micro-crystals. However, it does not exhibit the high UC emission efficiency of Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Er3+ due to the poor crystallinity and high concentration of OHâ. A high temperature thermal annealing (HTA) step is then required to recover the UC emission efficiency of Î²-NaGdF4 micro-crystals, and thus, the advantage of achieving controlled morphology and size from using the hydrothermal route is lost.37 The recovery of the intrinsic efficient UC emission for wet chemical-derived micro-crystals (such as Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ and Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Er3+) still lacks a perfect solution. Recently, we developed a new hydrothermal ion-exchange modified (IEM) method.15â17 By applying a second additional IEM process, the Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ micro-crystals not only maintain the well-controlled morphology and size of traditional hydrothermal method, but also yield a high UC emission efficiency.

In this work, we report the highly stable and efficient pure green UC emission of co-precipitation-derived Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ submicro-crystals via an IEM method. The as-prepared precursor crystals (PC) present faint UC emission under excitation with a 980 nm LD due to the poor crystallinity and the high concentration of OHâ. The IEM effectively recovers the intense pure green UC emission of the Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ submicro-crystals. The UC emission intensity of the Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ submicro-crystals is â¼4 times stronger than that of their HTA counterpart and is even â¼40% stronger than that of IEM Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Er3+ for a single green band. Importantly, the pure green UC emission color of Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ is highly stable upon varying the doping concentration of Yb3+ and Ho3+, and varying the excitation power density, which is of great importance in achieving stable performance under different application scenarios. Proof-of-concept stable and high purity green fluorescent labeling of printed clover patterns (15 Ã 12 mm) is demonstrated under excitation with various power densities.

Impressively, in Fig. 2a, IEM arouses enormously enhanced UC emission intensity by â¼3700-fold. Although the HTA method also strongly improves the UC emission performance of PC, its emission intensity is â¼4 times weaker than that of IEM Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+, as demonstrated in the inset of Fig. 2a under the irradiation of the 976 nm LD with a power density of 1.5 W cmâ2. The lower emission intensity of HTA than that of IEM Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ submicro-crystals may be ascribed to the low phase purity of Î²-NaGdF4, as indicated by the XRD results in Fig. 1a. According to our previous study,16,17 the ion-exchange process is a topotactic reaction involving the migration of Na+ and Fâ ions from the solution into the crystal. Then, an ion-redistribution process takes place. Our previous high resolution element mapping using energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) for the PC and IEM Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ crystals shows that all the element distributions are more homogeneous after IEM.16 The IEM-induced enormously enhanced UC emission intensity is mainly ascribed to the improved crystallinity of Î²-NaGdF4 (as indicated by XRD) and reduced OHâ groups (as indicated by FTIR).

Fig. 2b shows the decay curves of the UC emission of Ho3+ at 541 nm for PC, IEM and HTA Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ submicro-crystals. The decay lifetime Ï1/e (80 Î¼s) and rise time (12 Î¼s) of Ho3+ for PC are very short in accordance with its weak UC emission intensity. IEM leads to the strongly prolonged decay lifetime (799 Î¼s) and rise time (995 Î¼s) of Ho3+, both of which are about two times longer than that of HTA.

Fig. 2c shows FTIR spectra of PC, IEM and HTA Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ submicro-crystals. For PC, the strong absorption band of OHâ in 3000â3600 cmâ1 can be clearly identified, indicating a high OHâ concentration. Both the HTA and IEM methods can effectively reduce the OHâ concentration, as evidenced by the vanishing absorption band of OHâ.

The increased UC emission intensity, rise time and decay lifetime after IEM and HTA are ascribed to strongly reduced defect states. Based on our previous study,15â17 the local Ln clusters (such as Yb3+ clusters and Ho3+ clusters) and crystal structure defects (such as OHâ in Fig. 2c) in PC strongly block the energy transfer process from Yb3+ to Ho3+, which results in the fast dynamic equilibrium of energy transfer from Yb3+ to Ho3+ and leads to an abnormal fast rise process and short rise lifetime. Meanwhile, these defects also cause the fast radiation relaxation rate of Ho3+ and lead to the short decay lifetime in PC. After IEM and HTA, these defects are reduced to different extents, leading to strongly enhanced UC intensity. As indicated by the XRD results (Fig. 1a), HTA arouses phase separation and low phase purity, which quenches the UC emission. Consequently, compared with IEM, the UC emission intensity is weaker, and the rise time and decay lifetime are shorter for HTA. All these results in Fig. 2 present the advantages of the IEM method for increasing the crystal quality of Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ crystals by eliminating the defects and enhancing their UC emission efficiency.

Optimum doping concentration of Ho3+ and Yb3+

IEM Î²-NaGdF4 submicro-crystals doped with various amounts of Ho3+ and Yb3+ were synthesized to obtain their optimal doping concentrations, to realize the strongest UC emission, as shown in Fig. 3. The optimal doping concentrations of Ho3+ and Yb3+ were determined to be 0.5 and 20 mol%, respectively, as summarized in Fig. 3e. In a low doping level regime of Ho3+ (0.2â0.5 mol%) and Yb3+ (10â20 mol%), the increase of the doping concentrations of Ho3+ and Yb3+ leads to the strongly enhanced UC emission intensity for all bands (see Fig. 3aâd). For example, the UC emission intensity of Ho3+ increases by 1.3-fold and 2-fold, when the doping concentrations of Ho3+ and Yb3+ increase from 0.2 to 0.5 mol% and from 10 to 20 mol%, respectively. This is because the concentration quenching is not so serious. More Yb3+ ions could absorb and then transfer more energy to Ho3+ ions and more Ho3+ ions mean more emission centers. The strong concentration quenching effect can be observed for the higher doping concentration of Ho3+ (>0.5 mol%) and Yb3+ (>20 mol%).41â43 With the increase in Ho3+ doping concentration, the closer distance between Ho3+ ions can cause serious cross-relaxation process. Similarly, with higher Yb3+ doping concentration, the closer distance between Yb3+ ions also induces a cross-relaxation process between them. The much simpler energy level configuration of Yb3+ compared with Ho3+ leads to different concentration quenching levels. Interestingly, the UC emission intensity ratio of IGreen/IRed only slightly changes with varying Yb3+ and Ho3+ concentration, leading to the highly stable pure green UC emission output color of Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+. Consequently, the corresponding CIE-1931 chromaticity color coordinates remain constant at (0.28, 0.71) with varying Yb3+ and Ho3+ concentrations (see Fig. 3f).

UC emission mechanism

The UC emission mechanism of IEM Î²-NaGdF4:20%Yb3+,0.5%Ho3+ is proposed based on our results, as shown in Fig. 6. Upon irradiation with a 976 nm LD, Yb3+ ions are excited from the ground state of 2F7/2 to the excited state of 2F5/2. The successive two-step energy transfer from Yb3+ to Ho3+ sequentially populates the Ho3+:5I6 level and then the Ho3+:5F4/5S2 level, via Ho3+:5I8 + Yb3+:2F5/2 â Ho3+:5I6 + Yb3+:2F7/2 and Ho3+:5I6 + Yb3+:2F5/2 â Ho3+:5S2 + Yb3+:2F7/2. The following relaxation from 5F4/5S2 to 5I7 and to the ground state of 5I8 gives rise to the NIR (752 nm) and green (541 nm) emissions, respectively. The large branch ratio of 5F4/5S2 â 5I8 to 5F4/5S2 â 5I7 leads to the dominant green UC emission. Both multi-phonon non-radiative relaxation processes from Ho3+:5I6 to Ho3+:5I7 and Ho3+:5S2 to Ho3+:5F5 could contribute to the red UC emission at 645 nm. For the first kind of non-radiative relaxation from Ho3+:5I6 to Ho3+:5F5, the following relaxation from 5F5 to the ground state 5I8 energy level can directly induce the red UC emission. Meanwhile, for the multi-phonon non-radiative relaxation from Ho3+:5I6 to Ho3+:5I7, the second energy transfer from Yb3+ to Ho3+via Ho3+:5I7 + Yb3+:2F5/2 â Ho3+:5F5 + Yb3+:2F7/2 is needed to populate the Ho3+:5I7 level to the Ho3+:5F5 energy level, as shown in Fig. 6. The energy gaps (ÎE) between 5S2 and 5F5 (2850 cmâ1) and 5I6 and 5I7 (3500 cmâ1) are about 8 and 10 times bigger than the phonon energy of the Î²-NaGdF4 host (350 cmâ1), respectively.46,47 According to the energy gap law, the probability of multi-phonon-assisted relaxation between these levels is low. Thus, the large energy gap between these levels leads to the weak UC emission intensity in red. In addition, the mentioned energy transfer from Yb3+ to Ho3+via Ho3+:5I7 +Yb3+:2F5/2 â Ho3+:5F5 + Yb3+:2F7/2 is the step after the mentioned multi-phonon relaxation. Thus, its influence on the IGreen/IRred ratio when the Yb3+ concentration varies is limited by the low probability of multi-phonon relaxation between 5I6 and 5I7.

Conclusions

We report the highly stable and efficient pure green UC emission of rod-like Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ submicro-crystals, which were synthesized via an ion-exchange modification (IEM) method by using co-precipitation-derived Î²-NaGdF4 as precursor crystal (PC). PC yields faint UC emission, which is mainly attributed to the poor crystallinity (indicated by XRD) and the high concentration of OHâ (evidenced by FTIR). The IEM of PC arouses the growth of PC into bigger particles and improved crystallinity, leading to a strongly enhanced UC emission intensity of 3700-fold with respect to PC. Among the three individual UC emission bands (green, red and NIR) of Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+, the green band dominates the UC emission spectra for all samples, leading to a pure green output color. The optimal Ho3+ and Yb3+ doping concentrations are 0.5 mol% and 20 mol%, respectively. The UC emission intensity of the Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ submicro-crystals is â¼4 times stronger than that of their counterpart synthesized using high-temperature annealing (HTA). Furthermore, in the single green region, the emission of the Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ submicro-crystals is â¼40% stronger than that of IEM Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Er3+. Importantly, the pure green UC emission color of Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ is highly stable with both the doping concentrations of Yb3+ and Ho3+ and the excitation power density, which is of great importance for stable performance under different application scenarios, as we demonstrated for the UC fluorescent labeling application. All of these results suggest that the newly developed rod-like Î²-NaGdF4:Yb3+,Ho3+ submicro-crystals have wide applications in fluorescent labeling, plastic recycling and display technologies, as high purity green UC emitters.
