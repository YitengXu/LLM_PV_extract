Poly(3-hexylthiophene): synthetic methodologies and properties in bulk heterojunction solar cells

Abstract

Poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) remains of significant importance as a prototypical benchmark hole conductor material in Organic Photovoltaics (OPVs). In this review we discuss synthetic strategies to P3HT, particularly focusing on those leading to the regioregular form and discussing key physical and morphological properties. Finally, a survey and a brief discussion of P3HT performance in bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) OPVs are also provided.

Broader context

Over the past three decades organic photovoltaics (OPVs) has been identified as a fast growing technology with a large potential, as it holds the promise for cost-efficient, lightweight, (semi)-transparent, and mechanically flexible solar energy conversion platform. To date, head-to-tail (HT) regioregular poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) is one of the most studied materials for the bulk heterojunction (BHJ) structure. Many regioselective synthetic protocols to defect-free P3HT have been developed, enabling an extremely high degree of control over the molecular weight with narrow polydispersities, which remains the key to technological breakthroughs using polymeric materials in OPVs since batch-to-batch property variations are held to a minimum. Here we summarize recent progress on the chemical synthetic approaches to regioregular HT-P3HT, and attempt to merge current understandings of the influence of polymer regioregularity, molecular weight and polydispersity on its bulk properties and photovoltaic response. The well-defined structure of HT-P3HT avoided ambiguities in structure–properties correlations, thus allowing enhanced material properties and excellent BHJ OPVs performance. Importantly, all the HT-P3HT distinctive features steadily make it a relevant model to explore new controlled polymerization techniques, as well as to develop new advanced materials and to tune their performance in organic solar cells.

1. Introduction

Since the pivotal work of Tang,1 organic solar (or photovoltaic) cells (OSC or OPV cells) based on π-conjugated semiconductors have attracted considerable attention as a potential source of green, inexpensive, and renewable electrical energy.2 The technological possibilities offered by PV cells based on organic semiconductors are enormous since they can enable light and mechanically energy producing flexible devices which can be easily fabricated at low-temperatures by printing techniques. Furthermore, the versatility and access to a variety of chemical structures are possible thanks to recent advances in synthetic methodologies. Thus, organic semiconductors have several potential advantages compared to conventional inorganic materials.

Among the classes of π-conjugated polymers for OPV applications, polythiophenes3 (PTs) exhibit a unique combination of high environmental/thermal stability, electrical conductivity, processability, and the most synthetic versatility, which allows a wide range of properties to be accessed through facile ring modifications. Poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) belongs to this semiconductor family and is one of the most common hole conductors investigated for bulk heterojunction (BHJ) solar cells.4

The development of P3HT started from the synthesis of unsubstituted 2,5-polythiophene (2,5-PT). One of the first chemical syntheses of this polymer was reported in 1980 by two research groups.5,6 Yamamoto et al.5 described the nickel-catalyzed polycondensation of 2,5-dibromothiophene. This monomer was reacted with magnesium in THF, affording 2-magnesiobromo-5-bromothiophene, which in the presence of Ni(bipy)Cl2 (bipy = 2,2′-bipyridyl) produced 2,5-PT. Lin et al.6 described another example of a metal-catalyzed route to 2,5-PT, exploiting acetylacetonates of Ni, Pd, Co and Fe as catalysts. Unfortunately, 2,5-polythiophene is an insoluble and infusible material, which cannot be processed for implementation into (opto)electronic devices. In the quest for a soluble and processable (semi)conducting polythiophene, 3-alkylthiophenes were developed. The first environmentally stable and soluble poly(3-alkylthiophenes) (P3ATs) were synthesized by Elsenbaumer7 in 1985, using a method similar to that used to prepare 2,5-polythiophene.

However, polymerization reactions of asymmetric 3-hexylthiophene (3HT) may result in three different coupling sites along the polymer chain, i.e. 2,5′-(head-to-tail, HT), 2,2′-(head-to-head, HH) and 5,5′-(tail-to-tail, TT) (Fig. 1). Therefore, synthetic methodologies have been developed to control P3HT regiochemistry, which greatly affects several structural, electronic, and optical properties of this polymer family.3,4

Particularly, a structurally regioregular HT arrangement is greatly desirable, since several physicochemical properties are optimized for a high degree of regioregularity. For instance, regioregular HT-P3HT architecture is preorganized for three-dimensional self-assembly, resulting in efficient two-dimensional hole transport8 with high mobilities and current density.9–11 Self-organization in P3HT results in a lamellar structure with two-dimensional conjugated sheets formed by interchain stacking. Sirringhaus et al.8 suggested that the π–π stacking crystalline planes adopt two different orientations (i.e. parallel and normal to the film) depending on the P3HT regioregularity and processing conditions. Jiang et al.11 evaluated mobilities of charge carriers in films of P3HTs with HT regioregularities of 97%, 81%, 70% and 54% as a function of doping level. It was found that the difference in regioregularity of P3HT resulted in a large mobility difference, especially in the low-doping regime. At the highest doping levels of ∼20% the mobility values reached 0.4 and 0.01 cm2 V−1 s−1 for the film of P3HT 97% and P3HT 54%, respectively. These findings were ascribed to the greater order level and extent of stacking of π-conjugated thiophene rings in regioregular P3HT, as elucidated by electrochemical, ESR, UV-Vis-NIR, and X-ray diffraction measurements.

The aspect of regioregularity in P3HTs also plays an important role in their bandgap control. The random polymerization of 3-hexylthiophene leads to a large degree of HH coupling that decreases the effective conjugation in the chain and blue-shifts the absorption maxima, due to a large twist-distortion.3,12 Further, the steric hindrance induced by the randomly oriented side-chains in the regiorandom polymer may suppress the interaction of chains to such an extent that no residual features of stacking of the P3HT chains is observed anymore.3

Besides the HT regioregularity, P3HT molecular weight and polydispersity are also important parameters to control electronic, optical, electrochemical properties, and solid-state packing, modulating therefore the transport properties and getting broad UV-Vis absorption.8,13–16 For instance, Sirringhaus et al.8 first reported that the molecular weight has a considerable impact on the crystalline connectivity and ordering of pristine P3HT spin-cast films, thus influencing the charge carrier mobility of the polymer. More recently, McGehee and coworkers13 observed that varying the molecular weight of P3HT by one order of magnitude results in a four order of magnitude change in charge carrier mobility. The low mobility, low molecular-weight films revealed a highly ordered structure consisting of nanorods, whereas the high-mobility, high-molecular weight films had an isotropic nodule structure. An examination of this apparent contradiction showed that poor connectivity and insulating grain boundaries between misoriented neighboring crystals limits the charge carrier mobilities of as-spun low-molecular weight thin films. Modifying thin-film morphology by changing the casting conditions or thermally annealing the samples affected charge transport by 100× for a given molecular weight. AFM data revealed that when more time is allowed for polymer crystallization, the crystals preferentially orient with their insulating side chains normal to the substrate, thus reducing in-plane insulating boundaries and increasing the charge carrier mobilities. On the other hand, processing conditions had little influence on the high-molecular-weight films. This was ascribed to the fact that the chains are longer than the domain size and minimize the effects of the grain boundaries by bridging neighbouring grains. Generally, the best mobilities are obtained using relatively high molecular weight P3HT (Mn > 25 kDa by Gel Permeation Chromatography, GPC) under slow evaporating conditions on hydrophobic substrates.14

Further, a systematic study by Trznadel et al.15 correlating P3HT bulk properties to the molecular weight (Mn) and/or polydispersity index (PDI) underscored the electronic changes. For instance, the increased conjugation length by increasing Mn explains the red-shift of the solution as well as solid-state UV-vis/near IR λmax. Further, solid state spectra from the high-molecular weight fractions were found to develop a well-resolved vibronic structure compared to those from low-molecular weight fractions. Similarly, electrochemical behavior of regioregular P3HT was also found to strongly depend on its molecular weight. Thin-film cyclic voltammetry (CV) showed an oxidation at 0.74 V in all samples that decreased in intensity with increasing Mn. Additional oxidations at 0.58–0.60 and 0.97–1.00 V appeared when the degree of polymerization (DP) was as high as 26 and intensified with increasing Mn.

Zen et al.16 reported on the optical, structural and electrical characterization of thin-films prepared from different MW fractions of regioregular P3HT. They demonstrated that lowering the molecular weight of P3HTs dramatically decreases the field-effect mobility and this was mainly ascribed to the ability of high-molecular weight fractions to adopt a planar backbone conformation which allows a close interchain contact. Indeed, in order to support this hypothesis the authors investigated all fractions as a function of temperature. They observed a continuous mobility decrease at increasing temperatures as well as a pronounced thermochromic effect of the P3HT films occurring with the same trend. They concluded that at high temperatures the polymer chains adopted a more twisted conformation, causing a more hindered charge carrier transport. On this basis they proposed that the lower charge carrier mobility shown by short molecular weight fractions was mainly caused by a more twisted, disordered intrachain conformation. Further, their structural studies revealed that layers of the low molecular fractions exhibited an inhomogeneous morphology in which highly ordered crystallites are blended in a disordered matrix. Apparently these latter results were in contrast to the finding that layers prepared from the low molecular weight fractions exhibited low charge carrier mobilities as well as blue-shifted and unstructured UV-Vis spectra. The authors explained this discrepancy with the fact that the optical and transport properties of these layers were mainly determined by the disordered matrix.

All the above considerations clearly highlight that the regiocontrolled synthesis of P3HT having the desired molecular weight and polydispersity is crucial to enhance the material self-assembly ability and, therefore, its bulk properties. In general, the synthesis of P3HT could be achieved by either chemical or electrochemical methods. For the latter,17 a potential was applied to a solution containing the monomer and an electrolyte. Even though the electrochemical methods were promising, chemical polymerizations are the most explored strategies since they can afford highly regioregular polymer in excellent yields and large scale.

In this review article we summarize the most representative chemical synthetic approaches to regioregular head-to-tail P3HT along with its properties in BHJ OPVs. We restrict the discussion to P3HT homopolymer although several interesting studies in the area of block copolymers containing P3HT have been carried out.18 In Section 1 we present an overview of the nickel and palladium-based cross-coupling methodologies employing organometallic reagents as key intermediates, which stand as the most satisfactory regioselective synthetic routes to date. Section 2 summarizes chemical oxidative polymerization, typically yielding HT-enriched sequences. In Section 4, we focus on the influence of regioregularity, molecular weight, and polydispersities of P3HTs on the performance of organic photovoltaic devices. Next, we give a brief survey of representative papers related to efficient photovoltaic cells incorporating regioregular P3HT. We have chosen some references that cover only a small part of the countless studies carried out during the last decade. A comprehensive survey of all the papers devoted to the various photoconversion efficiencies is beyond the scope of this review.

Note that the different papers mentioned in the text are not in chronological order but are presented to provide key studies advancing the field.

2. Metal-assisted cross-coupling reactions

2.1. Nickel-catalyzed coupling polymerization

Polymerization to regioregular P3HT based on nickel-catalyzed Kumada cross-coupling was first developed by McCullough et al.19 One of the key features of this synthetic method (Scheme 1) was the regiospecific metallation of 2-bromo-3-hexylthiophene (1) with LDA (lithium diisopropylamide) in the 5-position to generate the corresponding organolithium at −78 °C (step i in 1→ 2, Scheme 1). Remarkably, quenching studies performed after treatment of 1 with LDA, and also after generation of the Grignard reagent 2 by transmetallation with MgBr2·Et2O (step ii in 1→ 2, Scheme 1) demonstrated that less than 1–2% of the undesired 2,5-exchanged intermediate 3 was produced. The polymerization was then carried out in situ by treatment of 2 with 0.5 mol% Ni(dppp)Cl2, (dppp = diphenylphosphino propane) leading to regioregular head-to-tail (HT) P3HT in a 36% yield. Indeed, 1H NMR analysis indicated that the degree of HT–HT regioregularity was approximately 98%. The average molecular weight for unfractionated P3HT prepared by this method was Mn = 12200, with a PDI ∼ 1.9. However, the polydispersity was reduced by subjecting the polymer to Soxhlet extractions using a variety of organic solvents.20
Scheme 1 Synthesis of regioregular P3HT by McCullough's method.19Reagents and conditions: i, LDA, THF, −40 °C, 40 min; ii, MgBr2.Et2O, −60 to −40 °C per 40 min, −40 to 5 °C per 20 min; iii, Ni(dppp)Cl2, −5 to 25 °C, 18 h.

This procedure was later modified21 by replacing magnesium bromide etherate with ZnCl2, which allowed for a greater solubility of the organometallic intermediate at −78 °C. The resulting regioregular P3HT was found22 to self-assemble into highly ordered crystalline arrays (lamellar-like structures) by X-ray studies as well as UV-Vis data, and afforded electrical conductivities of ∼150 S cm−1versus 0.1–20 S cm−1 for regioirregular poly alkylthiophenes when doped with iodine.

Shortly thereafter, Chen and Rieke23 introduced the regiocontrolled synthesis of P3HT mediated by highly reactive Rieke zinc (Zn*) (Scheme 2). Highly reactive zinc was found to undergo direct oxidative addition to 2,5-dibromo-3-hexylthiophene 4 (1.1:1, 4 h, −78 °C) in THF under argon, giving a quantitative yield of the regioisomer 2-(bromozincio)-5-bromothiophene 5 predominantly (up to 97% when cryogenic conditions are employed) and 5-bromo-2-(bromozincio)-3-hexylthiophene 6 as a very minor regioisomer. Significantly, no bis(bromozincio)thiophene was formed. The head-to-tail polymerization of the metalated intermediate 5 was achieved in quantitative yield by Negishi coupling using a catalytic amount of Ni(dppe)Cl2 ([1,2-bis(diphenylphosphino)ethane]nickel(II) chloride] (0.2 mol%) upon 4 h of refluxing at 67 °C (98.5 ± 1.5% of HT linkage). This procedure was slightly modified later23 by carrying out the polymerization at room temperature for 24 h, thus leading to 82% yield of regioregular HT-P3HT after fractionation of the crude polymer by Soxhlet extraction.


Scheme 2 Regiocontrolled synthesis of poly(3-hexylthiophene) by the Rieke method.23

Interestingly, it was found23 that the percentage of HT couplings is altered dramatically by the use of other nickel catalysts. Indeed, switching the ligand of the catalyst from dppe to a more labile ligand PPh3 (as Ni(PPh3)4) greatly reduced the regioregularity (65:35 HT: head-to-head, HH). Furthermore, the use of palladium catalysts (vide infra) led to a marked reduction of the regioregularity (70:30, Pd(dppe)Cl2) as well as to a completely regiorandom (Pd(PPh3)4) P3HT.

The polymerizations reported above, and described by McCullough and Rieke, utilize a metal-catalyzed cross coupling methodology (Scheme 3) which have been extensively investigated.24 The reaction is believed to usually involve three steps: (1) oxidative addition of an organic halide with a metal–ligand L catalyst (2) transmetallation (disproportionation) between the catalyst complex and a reactive organometallic reagent (i.e. organomagnesium, organozinc, organoboron, organoaluminum, organotin) to generate a diorganometallic complex, and (3) reductive elimination of the coupled product with regeneration of the metal–L catalyst. In the Rieke case,23 it has been demonstrated that the choice of the catalyst is a critical issue since the degree of P3HT regioregularity may depend on the metal as well as the ligands used in the catalysts. The authors23 speculated that the smaller ionic radius of Ni2+vs. Pd2+ along with the higher steric demands for bidentate dppe-ligand with respect to monodentate PPh3 indicate that the degree of regioregularity was controlled by the steric congestion at the reductive elimination step in the catalytic cycle of the transition-metal catalyzed coupling reactions.


Scheme 3 Catalytic cycle of transition metal-mediated cross-coupling reactions.

The regioregular P3HT prepared by the Rieke method had an average molecular weight Mw = 37680 and a Mn = 25500 with a polydispersity index of 1.48, after fractionation by Soxhlet extraction of the crude product.

A polydispersity as high as 3.63, corresponding to Mw = 15000 and Mn = 4130, was found for crude HT-P3HT. Interestingly, the regioregular HT-P3HT prepared using Rieke zinc showed the absorption maximum longer than regioregular HT-P3HT prepared via the McCullough method, both in solution (λmax = 456 nm vs. λmax = 442 nm, respectively) and as a thin-film (λmax = 526, 560, 610 nm vs. λmax = 504, 550, 600 nm, respectively).15 On the other hand, a similar polycrystalline, self-organized lamellar morphology with three-dimensional ordering of the polymer chains, was revealed by XRD and AFM studies. Also, conductivities of 10−6 to 10−7 S cm−1 for the neutral HT P3ATs (pressed pellet) and of ∼103 S cm−1 for I2 vapor heavily doped P3AT films25 have been found.

In 2001, a new economical synthesis of regioregular, head-to-tail P3HT was reported by McCullough and coworkers26 (Scheme 4). Treatment of 2,5-dibromo-3-hexylthiophene monomer 4 with one equivalent of an alkyl/vinyl Grignard reagent resulted in a magnesium–bromine exchange reaction, also referred to as Grignard metathesis (GRIM) method. The reaction proceeded with a moderate degree of regioselectivity leading to an ∼85:∼15 distribution of regiochemical isomers 2:3. This ratio was independent of the reaction time, temperature and Grignard reagent used. As shown in Scheme 4, treatment of the 2:3 mixture with a catalytic amount of Ni(dppp)Cl2 (0.5 mol%) in refluxing THF for 45 min afforded regioregular P3HT in high yield (71%, after fractionation of the crude polymer by Soxhlet extraction). Although the ratio of the desirable to undesirable isomers is higher for the GRIM method compared to the McCullough and Rieke methods, this method still afforded HT regioregular P3HT with high regioregularity of ∼99%. Such a high degree of regioregularity was rationalized by the authors in terms of a combination of a number of kinetic and thermodynamic effects arising from steric and electronic factors in the catalytic cycle. Note also that one strong advantage of this method is that the use of cryogenic temperatures as well as highly reactive metals is unnecessary.


Scheme 4 Synthesis of HT-P3HT by the Grignard metathesis (GRIM) method.26,27

Further, it is noteworthy to mention here that other 3-substituted thiophene monomers have also been quickly and easy polymerized in a regioregular fashion (HT > 99%) through GRIM metathesis,3,26,27 thus paving the way to the production of kilogram-scale high molecular weight regioregular 3-substituted polythiophenes (Mn = 20000–35000, PDI = 1.2–1.4), and promoting their use in the development of new technologies.

As already discussed, the generally accepted mechanism for cross-coupling reactions involves oxidative addition, transmetallation and reductive elimination (Scheme 3). Since the Ni-catalyzed polymerization is formally a polycondensation reaction, affording polymers with a high polydispersity and an uncontrolled molecular weight, it is generally accepted to proceed via a step-growth mechanism. However, in 2004 Yokozawa and coworkers28,29 proposed that the polymerization of Grignard-type halothiophene monomers with a nickel catalyst (i.e. McCullough and GRIM method) proceeds in a chain-growth fashion to obtain regioregular P3HT with well-controlled molecular weight and low polydispersity.

As shown in Scheme 5, the addition of 0.4 mol% of Ni(dppp)Cl2 into the THF solution of the 2-bromo-5-chloromagnesio-3-hexylthiophene 8, which is obtained by reaction of 2-bromo-3-hexyl-5-iodothiophene 7 with isopropylmagnesiumchloride (1 equivalent, 0 °C), and stirring at room temperature afforded regioregular HT P3HT (78% yield; HT > 98%, Mn = 31700, Mw/Mn = 1.36).


Scheme 5 Yokozawa's route for the synthesis of regioregular P3HT.28,29

It was found that the Mn values increased linearly with the monomer conversion, as well as to the feed ratio of monomer to the Ni catalyst, and that the PDIs were found to be 1.30–1.39 throughout the polymerization. All these findings indicate that the polymerization followed the chain-growth mechanism.

In accordance with Trznadel's results,15 the λmax of chain-growth synthesized HT P3HT increased as the Mn of HT P3HT increased. Note that the absorption maxima in solution UV-vis spectra (CHCl3) of HT P3HT with Mn = 21900 (Mw/Mn = 1.35) was 451 nm, which was almost identical with the value previously reported (λmax = 456 nm, when Mn = 25500 and Mw/Mn = 1.48) by Chen and Rieke.23

The chain-growth mechanism of the polymerization was also examined by a “monomer-addition” experiment. When a fresh feed of monomer 8 was added to the polymerization mixture, the gel permeation chromatography profile of the polymer clearly shifted toward the higher molecular weight region, while a narrow PDI (1.34) remained.

However, there was always a shoulder peak in the GPC chromatogram at a high molecular region, with a Mn ∼ 2× corresponding to the main peak. Later29 Yokozawa and coworkers found that this shoulder originated from the formation of a small amount of polymer with a higher molecular weight by quenching the polymerization mixture with water in the workup process, owing to a disproportionation of the active chain P3HT-Ni(II)Br (9), followed by reductive elimination of P3HT–P3HT (Scheme 6). The disproportionation of the P3HT–Ni(II)–Br complex was attributed to a change of the ligand of the P3HT–Ni(II)–Br complex when water was added into the reaction mixture. This side-reaction was avoided by quenching the polymerization mixture with 5 M HCl, which promoted protonolysis before disproportionation. P3HT with Mn = 28700 and PDI as low as 1.14 was obtained.


Scheme 6 Yokozawa's proposed pathway for the formation of high-molecular weight P3HT promoted by water during polymerization quenching.29

All these findings opened a new way to synthesize HT P3HT having a well-defined molecular weight, which may contribute to the development of electrical and optical devices with more controlled performance.

At the same time, McCullough and coworkers,30 through similar experiments, elucidated the mechanism of complementary systems using 2-bromo-5-chlorozinc-3-hexylthiophene 10 and 2-bromo-5-magnesiobromo-3-hexylthiophene 2. It was found that the McCullough and Grignard metathesis polymerization methods, respectively, not only proceeds via a chain-growth mechanism but they also exhibit living characteristics.

These systems allowed for the control of the molecular weight of the polymer as a function of reaction time and the amount of Ni catalyst, and conducting P3HT with predetermined molecular weights (Mn up to ∼11000 for ∼92–94% monomer conversion) and relatively narrow molecular weight distributions (PDI ∼ 1.2–1.5) were prepared. Note that for McCullough polymerization the optimal experimental conditions were [Monomer]0 = 0.075 mol L−1, [Monomer]0:[Ni(dppp)Cl2] = 57:1, and T = 25–30 °C, whereas for GRIM polymerization [Monomer]0 = 0.07 mol L−1 and [Ni(II)]0 = 1.5 × 10−3 mol L−1 were used, at T = 23–25 °C.

There are two proposed explanations for the living chain-growth nature of nickel-catalyzed cross-coupling polymerization of P3HT. Yokozawa and coworkers speculated that the oxidative addition of the polymer chain occurs selectively, since it is kinetically slower or thermodynamically less stable for the monomer than for the growing polymer chain. This was ascribed to the decreased electron density of the thiophene ring on the polymer chain.31 A different explanation from McCullough and coworkers32 involved the complexation of the nickel(0) with the thiophene growing chain, forming an associated pair which is supposed to limit polymerization to one end of the polymer chain. The McCullough's proposed mechanism is outlined in Scheme 7.


Scheme 7 McCullogh's proposed mechanism for the nickel-catalyzed cross-coupling polymerization.32

The first step in the mechanism is the reaction of two equivalents of 2-bromo-5-chlorozinc-3-hexylthiophene monomer 10, generated in situ from 2-bromo-3-hexylthiophene 1, with Ni(dppp)Cl2, yielding the bis-organonickel compound 11. Next, the reductive elimination of the latter resulted in the immediate formation of an associated pair [12·13] of nickel(0) (12) and the tail-to-tail aryl halide dimer 13. Dimer 13 undergoes fast oxidative addition to the nickel center, thus generating the new organonickel compound 14, in view of the already mentioned fact that the formation of the complex [12·13] eliminates the potential separation of 12 from 13. Subsequently, growth of the polymer chain occurs by insertion of one monomer at a time, and the Ni(dppp) moiety is always bound to the polymer chain as an end-group. According to the proposed mechanism, during the catalytic cycle only one defect, i.e. one tail-to-tail coupling, is generated per polymer chain. As further evidence, the addition of various Grignard reagents (R′MgX) at the end of the polymerization resulted in end-capping of HT P3HT with the R′ end group, thus supporting the fact that Ni(dppp)Cl2 acts as an initiator, rather than a catalyst.32

More recently, Hiorns et al.33 reported a simplified route to quasi-100% regioregular P3HT (37–48% yields after fractionation of the crude polymer by Soxhlet extraction) with predetermined molecular weights (Mn up to 33400) and polydispersities as low as 1.12, based on the GRIM polymerization of 2,5-dibromo-3-hexylthiophene 4 as the starting material (Scheme 8). Although the metal–halogen exchange product is a mixture of 17 and 18 with a ratio of ∼80:20, the polymerization still affords regioregular P3HT, as indicated by in-depth NMR characterization. Note that these findings correlate well with those reported by McCullough et al. (vide supra)26 as to the fact that the minority metalated isomer 18 (Scheme 8) does not participate in the polymerization mainly due to steric effects. Further, the authors33 speculated that the reaction mechanism was in accordance with the one proposed by McCullough and coworkers,32 and depicted in Scheme 7.

In a following contribution34 the same group reported the synthesis of a series of P3HTs with predetermined molecular weights (Mn ∼ 4500–280000) and varying polydispersities (∼1.1–2.1) by using the above method. The extremely high molecular weight P3HT (Mn ∼ 280000, PDI < 1.1) was prepared without resorting to fractionation, by carrying out the polymerization of monomer 2-bromo-5-chloromagnesio-3-hexyl-2-bromothiophene 17 in diphenylether at 40 °C ([monomer]:[Ni(dppp)Cl2] = 20000:1, 40% yield). The authors also fabricated photovoltaic devices based on P3HT–PCBM blend films, and tried to correlate the influence of molecular weights, polydispersities, and degree of conjugation of the P3HT on cell characteristics (vide infra).

A drawback of the above synthetic methodologies is that it is not possible to initiate the polymerization from an external moiety, which would be necessary for the synthesis of more complex architectures such as brushes and star/block copolymers. Different strategies towards the ex situ initiation have been reported by several groups.35–40

Within this context, Kiriy and coworkers36 developed a surface-initiated polycondensation of 2-bromo-5-chloromagnesio-3-hexyl-2-bromothiophene 17via a chain growth mechanism, allowing to achieve regioregular HT P3HT brushes from poly(4-bromostyrene)-coated silicon wafers (Scheme 9).

Very recently Lohwasser and Thelakkat50 identified all the factors relevant for Grignard metathesis polymerization toward 100% end group control and low polydispersity P3HT synthesis. Indeed, they studied in detail the active Grignard monomer formation of 2,5-dibromo-3-hexylthiophene 4 using t-BuMgCl, followed by polymerization using Ni(dppp)Cl2 as catalyst (Scheme 16).

Lohwasser and Thelakkat50 found that polymer chains with 100% H/Br end groups are obtained in the absence of unreacted/excess t-BuMgCl, which can be realized when using a high ratio of 2,5-dibromo-3-hexylthiophene 4 to t-BuMgCl (0.96–1 equiv.) and ensuring complete consumption during active Grignard monomer formation. It was demonstrated that when these prerequisites are not fulfilled, the amount of H/Br end groups decreased with the polymerization time. On the other hand, there was no noticeable effect of unreacted/excess t-BuMgCl on the PDIs.32 The full monomer conversion requires long reaction times when t-BuMgCl and 2,5-dibromo-3-hexylthiophene 4 are used at room temperature (∼20 h). To overcome this problem, the authors used LiCl as an additive to effectively accelerate the active monomer formation (∼4 h). Further, adding LiCl led to an increase in P3HT molecular weight without changing the monomer-to-catalyst ratio (26:1). The origin of this effect was ascribed to an increase in the rate of polymerization, as well as to the incorporation of a small amount of the undesired organomagnesium isomer (18, Scheme 16), which causes a slight decrease (<5%) of the regioregularity of P3HT. By the way, this was not found to affect the polymer charge carrier mobility (μsat = 2 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 s−1). Additionally, in contrast with precedent findings51 stating that there is no effect on the polydispersity when methanol is used as a quenching agent instead of hydrochloric acid, the authors demonstrated that methanol as a quenching agent causes chain–chain coupling via disproportionation and hence an increase in polydispersity. This result correlated well with the observation of Yokozawa and coworkers29 (vide supra) when they used water as a quenching agent instead of hydrochloric acid.

Very recently Higashihara et al.52 reported the first demonstration of the catalyst-transfer polycondensation for the synthesis of high MW regioregular P3HT using the dianion-type zincate complex, dilithium tetra(t-butyl)zincate (tBu4ZnLi2) (Scheme 17).

2.2. Palladium-catalyzed coupling polymerizations

Other methods employed for the synthesis of regioregular P3HT include palladium-catalyzed cross-coupling reactions. Iraqi and Barker53 have investigated the synthesis of regioregular P3HT (HT > 96%) through the Stille reaction, using 3-hexyl-2-iodo-5-(tributylstannyl)thiophene 27 (Scheme 18). This intermediate is air and moisture stable, thus it can be purified from undesired residual starting materials. 3-Hexyl-2-iodo-5-(tributylstannyl)thiophene 27 was synthesized by selective iodination of 3-hexylthiophene 28 at the 2-position using iodine with mercury(II)oxide in benzene, followed by selective lithiation of the resulting 2-iodo-3-hexyl-thiophene 29 at the 5-position with lithium diisopropylamide (LDA) and reaction with tri-n-butyltin chloride at low temperatures. Polymerization reactions were carried out in different solvents at reflux temperature, and in the presence of Pd(PPh3)4 (3 mol%) as a catalyst. Molecular weights of regioregular P3HT prepared by this method were Mn = 10000–16000 with PDI = 1.2–1.4 after purification by Soxhlet extraction. Note that the rate of these polymerizations was found to be slower (∼10–50% yield) than that of polymerization reactions involving 2-bromo-3-hexyl-5-bromomagnesiothiophene (2, Scheme 1) or 2-bromo-3-hexyl-5-bromozinciothiophene (5, Scheme 2) intermediates, where reactions occur at room temperature or lower and where higher molecular mass materials were obtained. This result was ascribed to a lower rate of halogen exchange between tin and palladium, as compared to that between magnesium and nickel or zinc and nickel.

Higgins and coworkers54 investigated a range of Pd catalysts with bulky, electron-rich phosphane ligands for the synthesis of regioregular P3HT by the Suzuki reaction (Scheme 19). Such complexes were previously found55 to be highly active for a range of cross-coupling reactions, including Suzuki couplings. Indeed, although Suzuki couplings have been used to prepare oligothiophenes56 polymerizations have proven more difficult to be carried out successfully. Where thiophene boronates have been used up to then, deboronation was a problem, representing the termination of polymerization by aryl group transfer from the PPh3 ligands employed.57

The authors54 selected as monomer 2-(5-bromo-4-n-hexyl-thiophen-2-yl)-4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-[1,3,2]dioxaborolane 30 (Scheme 19). Treatment of 2-bromo-3-hexylthiophene 1 with one equivalent of (pin)B–B(pin) (pin = pinacolato) in the presence of a catalytic amount (5 mol%) of [Ir2(μ-Cl)2(η4-COD)2] (COD = 1,5-cyclooctadiene) and 4,4′-(tBu)2-2,2′-bipyridine (10 mol%) in refluxing THF for 16 h gave 30 in 97% yield. Note that the use of an Ir-catalyst avoided the use of organolithium reagents or strong bases, thus widening the range of accessible functionalized poly alkylthiophenes.

When the polymerization of 30 was carried out in the presence of [Pd(tBu3P)2], generated in situ from [Pd2(dba)3] (1.5 mol%) (dba = dibenzylidene acetone) and the ligand (t-Bu3P, 4.5 mol%), the highest P3HT average molecular weight was Mw = 11100 without Soxhlet fractionation (PDI = 3.30). Although the polymer was obtained in moderate yield (50%), the regioregularity was high (97%). The CH2Cl2 fraction (20% yield, Mw = 11400, PDI = 1.14, HT = 95%) and the hexane fraction (35% yield, Mw = 5100, PDI = 1.20, HT = 87%) were also polymeric.

Subsequently Bo and coworkers59 reported a novel efficient zero valent catalyst precursor, tris[tri(2-thienyl)phosphine] palladium (Pd(PTh3)3), and demonstrated that it was superior to the classically used Pd(Ph3)4 for Suzuki–Miyaura coupling polymerization of thiophene-based substrates. Monomers 3-hexyl-5-iodothiophene-2-boronic pinacol ester 34 and 3-hexyl-5-bromothiophene-2-boronic pinacol ester 35 were used for the polymerization. (Scheme 20).

The reactions were carried out in a refluxing biphasic mixture of aqueous NaHCO3/THF or aqueous NaHCO3/THF/toluene with approximately 1.2 mol% of Pd(PPh3)4 or Pd(PTh3)3 as a catalyst precursor (3 days). The head-to-tail regioregularity of this P3HT was over 97%, determined by 1H-NMR. The authors found that the use of Pd(PTh3)3 instead of Pd(PPh3)4 as a catalyst precursor can significantly increase the yields and the molecular weights of the polymers. In particular, the combination of iodo-monomer 34 and Pd(PTh3)3 as a catalyst precursor gave a yield of 72% and a Mw = 26000 for P3HT. Note that these results are comparable to those reported by Higgins and coworkers54 (vide supra) on the polymerization of 3-hexyl-5-bromothiophene-2-boronic pinacol ester 30 with tBu3P, (o-biphenyl)PtBu231, and Buchwald's type ligands 32, 33 (Fig. 2), whereas the synthesis of PTh3 was much simpler than the preparation of Buchwald's type ligands themselves. The high activity of tri(2-thienyl)phosphine ligand PTh3 in Suzuki–Miyaura coupling reactions of thiophene-based substrates was ascribed to the strong electron-donating ability of the thienyl rings.60 Indeed, the use of electron-rich compounds as the ligand facilitates the rate-determining oxidative addition61 in the catalytic cycle by increasing the electron density of the palladium intermediate.62 Further, the use of electron-rich compounds was supposed to suppress the main side-reactions such as deboronation of the thiophene-based monomers 34, 35 and afford high-molecular weight polymers.

Catalytic dehydrogenative coupling of heteroarenes with aryl halides63 (i.e. direct arylation) has undergone rapid development in recent years, and is becoming a viable alternative to traditional cross-coupling reactions. This class of cross-coupling processes, which does not require the preparation of organometallic reagents, is considered to be a benefit to polymer synthesis with respect to reduced waste generation and fewer reaction steps. Recently, Ozawa and co-workers64 applied this strategy to the synthesis of head-to-tail P3HT with high molecular weight (Mn up to 30600, PDI = 1.60) and high regioregularity (up to 98%) in an almost quantitative yield (99%).

Thus, polycondensation of 2-bromo-3-hexylthiophene (1) (Scheme 21) was carried out at ∼120 °C for 24–48 h in THF, in the presence of Cs2CO3 (1–2 equivalents) and a palladium catalyst (1–2 mol%) generated in situ from so-called Herrmann's catalyst 36 as a palladium source and a tertiary phosphine ligand. Complex 36, which is in turn derived from Pd(OAc)2 and P(o-tolyl)3 under heated conditions, has been proven to be a good catalyst precursor with high thermal stability.65

Among a variety of phosphine ligands, tris(o-Me2N)phosphine was found to be optimal, and the high ligand performance was ascribed to the bidentate coordinating ability of the o-Me2N groups, rather than steric or electronic effects. Interestingly, it was found that the head-to-tail regioregularity improved with increasing the molecular weight. The authors speculated that homocoupling reactions between C–H and C–H bonds and C–Br and C–Br bonds are operative to a considerable extent at the initial polymerization stage, while the cross-coupling reaction between C–Br and C–H bonds dominates at a later stage.

Very recently Yokozawa et al.66 investigated the t-BuPPd(Ph)Br (37)-catalyzed Suzuki–Miyaura coupling of 2-(4-hexyl-5-iodo-2-thienyl)-4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-1,3,2-dioxaborolane 34 (Scheme 22). Very recently, Koeckelberghs and coworkers68 proposed a universal chain-growth polymerization protocol for the synthesis of conjugated polymers. 3. Oxidative coupling polymerization

For the preparation of poly(3-hexylthiophene), oxidative coupling polymerizations, which does not require any leaving groups or the removal of the by-product from the resulting polymer, may have great advantages for large-scale preparation. Ueda and coworkers69 investigated the synthesis of regioregular HT P3HT by utilizing the simple oxidative coupling method. The polymerization was carried out using 3-hexylthiophene (3HT) in the presence of ferric chloride (III) (4 equivalents) as an oxidant in chloroform (Scheme 24, route a). It was found that by lowering the reaction temperature as well as the monomer concentration, the HT regioregularity increased. The effect of lowering the temperature was explained by an enhancement of selectivity in the oxidation between the monomer and the oligomer, whereas the lowering in concentration was supposed to effectively suppress the reaction between the oligomers. The best HT content of 89% was obtained when the temperature was kept at −45 °C for 72 h and the initial monomer concentration was 0.02 mol L−1 (62% yield, Mn = 38 × 10,3 PDI = 2). A longer polymerization time (200 h) quantitatively afforded a high molecular weight polymer (Mn = 68000) with a PDI = 1.9 and a HT content of 88%. After Soxhlet extraction with hexane, a regioregular polymer with HT content of 91% was obtained (86% yield).

4. BHJ photovoltaic cells incorporating regioregular P3HTs: influence of polymer regioregularity, molecular weight and polydispersity

P3HT is commonly considered a benchmark BHJ OPV hole conducting polymer.4 Power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) in excess of 5% for single-layer devices (see Fig. 3 for device structure and operation principle),71 and more than 6% in tandem cell configuration72 have been reported for regioregular P3HT when blended with 1-(3-methoxycarbonyl)propyl-1-phenyl[6,6]C61 (PCBM) as an acceptor, while the estimated theoretical potential is around 10%.73 These outstanding results were achieved despite a P3HT non-ideal bandgap (∼1.9 eV) that prevents effective spectral overlap with solar radiation. The reported operational lifetimes are in excess of 1000 hours.74 This is also the only semiconductor by which OPV panels have been fabricated in a large extent.
Fig. 3 Structure and operation principle of a BHJ OPV cell based on P3HT. (A) OPV cell material components and (B) energetics of the light absorption and charge collection processes involving P3HT as a donor material. In an OPV device the light passes through the transparent contact (typically indium tin oxide, ITO), and is absorbed in the active layer to generate hole–electron pairs (excitons), which dissociates into free charges at the interface between the donor and the acceptor interface. Holes and electrons flow in the donor (P3HT) and acceptor regions, respectively, and are collected at the electrodes, resulting in the generation of electrical power. Principal figures-of-merit include the power conversion efficiency (PCE), short circuit current (Jsc), open-circuit voltage (Voc), and fill factor (FF), defining, respectively, the ratio between the output device electrical energy vs. the input solar energy (Pin), the device current when no reverse bias is applied, the device voltage when no current flows through the cell, and the ratio between maximum power device and Jsc × Voc, respectively. PCEs of photovoltaic cells are calculated using the following equation: PCE = FF × Voc × Jsc/Pin.

To enhance the efficiency of OPV cells based on P3HT, several optimization strategies have been employed such as using solvent mixtures/additives, photoactive layer thermal annealing/film forming conditions, optical spacers, anode/cathode interfacial layers, and using different device architectures.4

For instance, Marks and coworkers have utilized a NiO inorganic interlayer to improve P3HT/PCBM-based cell efficiency.75 NiO is a transparent conducting p-type oxide, and considering the relevant energy levels for P3HT + PCBM cells indicates that a thin layer of NiO grown on the typically used indium tin oxide (ITO) anode could enhance hole extraction while blocking electron leakage. The highest PCEs reported at that time for optimized P3HT/PC61BM cells, using standard fabrication procedures, were 4.0% efficiency.76 Using the same fabrication procedure and optimal NiO thicknesses of 5–10 nm, the authors achieved record cell power efficiencies of 5.2%.

However, regioregularity of P3HT, molecular weight and polydispersity remain key parameters for solar cell performance. Indeed, phenomena occurring during the photovoltaic process (Fig. 3), such as the creation, the diffusion, and separation of excitons, as well as charge carrier transport, are governed by the active layer morphology and, therefore, by the phase segregation of P3HT/PCBM mixtures. The latter phenomenon, which depends on the self-organizing properties of P3HT, can be markedly influenced by its degree of regioregularity.3,8,77–81

For instance, Kim et al.77 clearly demonstrated the positive effect of the degree of regioregularity on the performance of polymer solar cells with P3HT:PCBM 1:1 weight ratio (PCEs up to 4.4%). The UV-vis absorption coefficient was observed to increase with increasing regioregularity, particularly on the longest wavelength shoulder formed by polymer interchain interactions. In the resulting solar cell, short circuit current Jsc showed positive correlation with regioregularity. The combination of increased absorption and charge transport, as the result of more organized solid-state packing, explained the performance improvement. A specific threshold for regioregularity (∼95%)77 seems to be necessary to give the highest Jsc and FF, mainly because of the better transport properties of highly regioregular P3HT.8

More recently, Urien et al.78 investigated two types of P3HTs with different degrees of regioregularity as active layers with PCBM in photovoltaic cells. The first one contained 77% HT couplings (Mn = 20400 g mol−1, PDI = 2.8), whereas the second sample had a high content of HT couplings, close to 97% (Mn = 20600 g mol−1, PDI = 1.2). They found that the best performance (Voc = 0.61 V, Isc = 4.56 mA cm−2, FF = 0.54, PCE = 1.49%) was exhibited by the devices based on high-regioregular P3HT (1:0.8 wt/wt). This was ascribed to its self-organizing properties, leading to greater absorption of sunlight and greater hole mobility.

On the other hand, it has recently been reported that a too-high regioregularity may be an issue. Sivula et al.79 have demonstrated that decreasing the regioregularity leads to polymer–PCBM blends with superior thermal stability but lowering the driving-force for the P3HT crystallization.

Further, Laquai and coworkers80 reported on charge carrier mobility measurements for blends of structural isomers of P3HT with very high regioregularity (>98%) as well as medium regioregularity (94%) with PCBM in annealed and non-annealed films to study the influence of charge carrier transport on photovoltaic efficiency. Upon blending the regioregular polymers with PCBM in chloroform, the hole mobilities were found to be typically reduced by one order of magnitude with respect to the corresponding pristine P3HTs, and they did not significantly change upon thermal annealing. Next, very high regioregularity led to a faster (μ ∼ 10−5 cm2 V−1 s−1) and almost balanced charge transport of the holes and electrons, and medium regioregularity resulted in lower (μ ∼ 10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1) and unbalanced charge transport in the blends. Despite the differences caused by the different regioregularities, solar cells prepared from both regioregular polymers in combination with PCBM showed comparable PCEs of about 2.5%, thus indicating that increasing the order and therefore charge carrier mobility in P3HT films by using very high regioregular polymers does not significantly help to improve the device efficiency.

Ebadian et al.81 investigated P3HT:PCBM OPVs made by two samples of P3HT with higher (98%) and lower (94%) regioregularity from the viewpoint of the figures-of-merit, along with their durability. They found that the devices based on high-regioregular P3HT initially have a better performance (Voc = 0.54 V, Jsc = 9.3 mA cm−2, FF = 53%, PCE = 2.5%) vs. those based on 94% HT P3HT (Voc = 0.54 V, Jsc = 8.8 mA cm−2, FF = 48%, PCE = 2.3%), but are more prone to degradation due to a tendency for micro-phase segregation of donor and acceptor domains, as confirmed by electron and optical microscopy. The authors speculated that the tendency in high regioregular samples for π-stacking8 pushes the PCBM out to form ever larger domains, thus decreasing the total heterojunction interface, essential for charge separation in the BHJ structure. In addition, this report demonstrated the importance of thermal annealing in correlation with the regioregularity of P3HT on the film morphology and charge mobilities. For devices made with the high regioregular P3HT, the short circuit current and open circuit voltage dropped with annealing to 8.0 mA cm−2 and 0.48 V, respectively, due to accelerated phase segregation. In contrast to the high-regioregular P3HT based OPV, the performance parameters of the low regioregular device remain substantially stable before and after annealing.

Like the effect of regioregularity, the influence of the polymer's MW is critical. In a detailed study Schilinsky et al.82 observed that the efficiency of P3HT–PCBM bulk heterojunction solar cells dramatically depend on the molecular weight of the donor polymer P3HT. The low molecular-weight fractions (Mn = 2200 g mol−1, PDI = 1.43 and 5600 g mol−1, PDI = 1.18) showed a pronounced decrease in the Jsc by a factor of 5–10 as to the high molecular weight P3HT based solar cell, accompanied by a reduction of the Voc and FF by 50%. PCE values exceeding 2.5% were found only for regioregular P3HT with a Mn > 10000. The well-established correlation between molecular weight and charge carrier mobility of regioregular P3HT,13 which is in turn based on the formation of polymer aggregates (vide supra), was considered responsible for the dramatic performance decrease of devices with low-molecular weight polymers. This was corroborated by the solid-state absorption spectra of the various P3HT fractions blended into PCBM. Composites from the higher molecular fractions showed absorption spectra with well-resolved vibrational structure, which was missing in those from low molecular weight fractions.

Hiorns et al.34 found that in order to attain the highest efficiencies, the photovoltaic cells based on P3HT–PCBM blend films had to be treated at a temperature appropriate to the polymer molecular weight, with larger molecular weight P3HT requiring a higher annealing temperature and longer annealing times. This variation was ascribed to the stronger degree of aggregation for high molecular weight P3HT polymer chains,13 which hinders phase separation and slows diffusion of PCBM throughout the film. Further, a more intense variation of the P3HT–PCBM films were found to induce the formation of larger PCBM crystallites, which degraded device performance and caused film-cracking. An ideal sample of P3HT (Mn = 14800) was found to include a mixture of both high and low molecular weight P3HT (i.e. high polydisperse sample, PDI = 2.1), where the low molecular weight polymer chains forms an amorphous matrix which facilitates the aggregation of high molecular weight P3HT into conductive fibrils at low temperatures. In such a way, the high-molecular weight P3HT was considered responsible for the enhanced electrical properties, whereas the small-molecular weight P3HT was used to improve processability. Thermal annealing at 120 °C of the above mentioned P3HT sample (30 min) led to an optimal power conversion efficiency of 3.4% using the cell structure of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/PCBM:P3HT 1:1/LiF/Al (Voc = 600 mV, Jsc = 9.1 mA cm−2 with FF = 63%).

Ma et al.83,84 investigated in detail the morphology and the associated performance of P3HT:PCBM bulk heterojunction solar cells using P3HT with different molecular weights. They found that devices made from P3HT with low molecular weight (A, Mn = 13000 g mol−1, PDI = 2.1) had a higher Isc than devices with higher MW P3HT (B, Mn = 62500 g mol−1, PDI = 2.46) despite the lower hole mobility.13 This was ascribed to a difference in blend film morphologies, all other parameters being kept constant, as also confirmed by transmission electron microscopy (TEM). For film A, the PCBM was uniformly dispersed throughout the film. On the other hand, for film B, the phase separation was so strong that large PCBM aggregates were clearly visible. Next, the authors investigated the evolution of film morphology and device efficiency after annealing, and found that after optimum annealing at 150 °C for 10 min PCBM in the A (low MW) films form aggregates on the nanometer length scale, which is generally favorable for charge separation, although the absence of obvious interconnections between the grains may limit the device performance. To form the proper phase segregation morphology, the high MW polymer required a higher annealing temperature, which implies a better thermal stability of the solar cell. Indeed, annealing the high MW films at 180 °C for 10 min lead to additional de-mixing, i.e. the P3HT rich grains became larger (∼20 nm). However, the grains were more likely to be interconnected by the long chains of high MW P3HT, thus leading to a better charge transport and, therefore, a better device performance. These findings correlate well with those reported by Kline et al.13 (vide supra). Taking all the results into account, the authors concluded that the best performance can be achieved by a “molecular weight controlled” material that is made of a mixture of P3HT with different molecular weights, thus leading to highly ordered crystalline regions formed by low molecular weight P3HT embedded in and interconnected by a high MW P3HT matrix.13,16 Following this idea, a series of devices with the structure of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/P3HT:PCBM 1:0.8/Al were fabricated using different ratios of high and low molecular weight P3HT. Devices with an optimized high/low ratio = 1:4 gave the best performance. Very recently Nicolet et al.85 reported a systematic approach to the study of the influence of P3HT molecular weight on the OPV performance through the mapping of the binary P3HT:PCBM blend phase diagram. They observed the presence of an eutectic composition,86 which shifts to a higher content of P3HT going towards lower molecular weight P3HTs (Mn = 60000 g mol−1, 25000 g mol−1, and 9600 g mol−1, respectively). This result was attributed to the molecular weight dependence of the P3HT crystallization behavior. The shift inherent to P3HT molecular weight was found to also be apparent in the photovoltaic performance, as the eutectic composition corresponded to the optimal photovoltaic properties, thus demonstrating that the best composition in P3HT and PCBM is not absolute, but depends on the P3HT MW. The authors also concluded that with a low disperse polymer (e.g. Mn = 25000 g mol−1, PDI = 1.1), the P3HT:PCBM blend has a larger tendency to phase segregate, leading to a segregation of the P3HT at the top interface. This was explained by the fact that the smallest chains of a polydisperse polymer are able to migrate more easily to the interface between the two constituents, thus decreasing their interfacial tension.87

5. Conclusions

To date, poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) is the most studied donor semiconductor for organic photovoltaic (OPV) cells owing to facile synthesis and unique electronic structure. Various power conversion efficiencies have been reported for P3HT-based OPV devices, up to approximately 6%. These outstanding results were achieved despite a P3HT non-ideal bandgap (∼1.9 eV) that prevents effective spectral overlap with solar radiation.

Regioregularity of P3HT is known3 to be one of the key parameters affecting the related solar cells performance, and critically depend on the regioselectivity of the synthetic approaches to conducting P3HT. The synthesis of regioregular P3HTs generates defect-free, structurally homogeneous, head-to-tail (HT) coupled polymers, which exhibit better self-assembly ability and, therefore, improved bulk properties as well as photovoltaic responses over the regiorandom counterparts. Many regioselective synthetic methods, including electrochemical and chemical polymerization, have been developed. A breakthrough for the preparation of regioregular P3HT has been reported by McCullough19 and Rieke,23 separately. From these works, high regioregular (>98%) HT P3HT has been achieved with reasonable molecular weight.

Since then, a strong chemistry effort has been required to make the regioregular P3HT evolve into an easy accessible system in which the synthesis can be carried out at room temperature and without using a highly reactive system, and is therefore suitable for scaling-up.

Intensive studies have also revealed that the regioregular polymerization of P3HT proceeds via a chain-growth mechanism and may also exhibit living characteristics. This feature allows for the precise control of the polymer molecular weight and polydispersity, which were found3 to be, in turn, critical parameters modulating polymer electronic, optical, electrochemical properties, and solid-state packing, therefore influencing the OPV device performance.

These advanced syntheses of regioregular P3HT have also been successful in producing a wide range of tailor-made functionalized polythiophenes for specific applications, and for the realization of well-defined block copolymers.
