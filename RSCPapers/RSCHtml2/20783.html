Abstract

We report a novel strategy to collect microporous carbon from disposable sugarcane waste for lithium ion battery (LIB) applications. First boiled in water and ethanol and then calcined, the sugarcane waste successfully transforms into microporous carbon, delivering a specific capacity of 311 mA h gâˆ’1 at 0.33C as a LIB anode material. For improved LIB performance, pyrrhotite-5T Fe1âˆ’xS nanoparticles were uniformly dispersed and robustly attached to the scaffold of the microporous carbon using a novel sulfurization method. A remarkably ultrahigh capacity of 1185 mA h gâˆ’1 (well beyond the theoretical value by 576 mA h gâˆ’1) was achieved after 200 charging/discharging cycles at a current density of 100 mA gâˆ’1, suggesting desirable synergetic effects between Fe1âˆ’xS and microporous carbon which lead to a shortened lithium ion transportation path, enhanced conductivity and effective prevention of polysulfide dissolution. Our approach opens a convenient route for mass-producing sustainable, superior LIB electrodes from natural wastes that can substitute commercial graphite.

Introduction

Alarmed by global warming and the energy crisis, intensive efforts have been devoted to developing sustainable energy technologies (solar, wind, geothermal and tidal energy).1 Among different energy storage systems for effectively storing intermittent natural energy, rechargeable lithium ion batteries are one of the most promising candidates.2â€“4 For example, they are particularly appealing for portable electronic devices and hybrid electric vehicles (HEVs) due to their high energy density (high voltage joined with high specific capacity, typically at 150 W h kgâˆ’1 and 650 W h lâˆ’1), high rate capability, and long lifespan.5â€“8 Nevertheless, the high price of LIBs largely restricts their wide application, particularly in HEVs, making the development of HEV-based green transportation unrealistic. In fact, how to effectively lower the manufacturing cost, enhance the safety, increase the lifetime, and improve the energy density are the major challenges of LIBs presented to scientists.9,10 Currently, the anode materials of commercial LIBs heavily rely on graphite, which possesses low energy capacities and mainly comes from natural graphite mines, imposing environmental stress and the threat of depleting the natural graphite reserves to meet the huge future demands from the electric vehicle and electricity grid industries. It is therefore an urgent task for scientists to discover new renewable electrode materials that are environment friendly, cheap and with nearly infinite abundance in nature.10,11

Sugarcane as a type of popular plant, is widely grown in tropical and subtropical regions. Brazil is the largest producer of sugarcane in the world, followed by the next five major producers of India, China, Thailand, Pakistan and Mexico. As estimated by the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations, sugarcane is cultivated on âˆ¼26.0 million hectares all over the world, in more than 90 countries, producing a worldwide harvest of 1.83 billion tons.12 It is noteworthy that âˆ¼60% of commercial sugar comes from sugarcane. Notably, after draining the juice, the sugarcane trunks are normally discarded, resulting in a huge amount of bio-waste (up to âˆ¼223 million tons per year) that needs to be disposed of, usually through either direct combustion or landfills for biodegradation, generating air pollution.13 The direct combustion treatment inevitably emits nitrogen oxides (NOX), carbon monoxide (CO), CO2, and sulfur oxides (SOX),14 which are main contributors to the haze weather seen recently in China.15 Landfill treatment could be a more suitable method of disposal, but it entails large economic consumption. Thus, a more convenient and environmentally friendly approach that can effectively convert huge amounts of disposable sugarcane waste into useful commercial products would be highly desirable. Note that nano-porous activated carbon16 and hierarchically porous carbon monoliths17 have been obtained from sugarcane waste for supercapacitor applications. Nonetheless, the application of sugarcane waste-derived microporous carbon for lithium-ion batteries has not been reported.

Herein, we report a new convenient mass-production strategy to convert sugarcane waste into microporous carbon for lithium-ion batteries. Following a simple boiling procedure in water and alcohol, the sugarcane waste was carbonized to be converted into microporous carbon (MC), which was then further coated with Fe1âˆ’xS nanoparticles through a novel and facile sulfurization method. When applied as LIB anode materials, the hierarchical Fe1âˆ’xS-microporous carbon (Fe1âˆ’xS-MC) nanocomposites thus obtained displayed superior electrochemical performance with ultrahigh specific capacities and excellent cycling stability (1185 mA gâˆ’1 after 200 cycles), representing the best electrochemical performance among all the iron sulfide-based anode materials reported to date. The success in converting disposable sugarcane into advanced carbon-based materials opens a new route to substitute graphite as electrode materials, unveiling the great potential values of bio-wastes for practical applications.

Experimental

Synthesis of Fe1âˆ’xS-MC nanocomposites

A schematic illustration of the synthesis process of the Fe1âˆ’xS-MC nanocomposites is shown in Fig. 1. Sugarcane trunks disposed of from a juice extractor machine were collected from a fruit store in Sham Shui Po, Hong Kong. The sugarcane trunks were cut into pieces and boiled in de-ionized (DI) water and ethanol for 6 h, to remove the sugar contents. During each cooking process, the solution was once replaced with a fresh solution. After being dried in an oven at 90 Â°C, the sample was carbonized at 1000 Â°C in Ar with a flow rate of 30 sccm for 4 h, and allowed to cool naturally. Microporous carbon (MC) was then obtained. For surface functionalization, the MC was treated in a mixture of H2SO4 (98%) and HNO3 (70%) (3:1 by volume) under constant stirring at 110 Â°C for 15 min. The reaction mixture was then filtered and the precipitate was washed with DI water several times.

An aqueous suspension of MC was produced by ultrasonicating 20 mg MC in DI water for 10 min. In a 50 ml Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave, âˆ¼40 ml of the suspension and 0.8 g ferric citrate (FeC6H5O7) were added. The precursor was then mixed with sulfur powder and subsequently thermally treated under the protection of Ar flow (flow rate: 30 sccm) to generate the final product of Fe1âˆ’xS-MC. A Raman spectrum collected from the MC sample (Fig. 2g) displayed two strong peaks at 1585 and 1346 cmâˆ’1. To examine its capability in lithium storage, the acid-treated MC was assembled into a coin-type half-cell, in which Li metal foils were used as the counter electrode. The galvanostatic discharge/charge test was carried out at a current density of 0.33C (1C = 372 mA gâˆ’1) between 0 and 3 V vs. Li+/Li. The discharge/charge voltage profile for the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 20th, 50th, 60th cycles are disclosed in Fig. 5a. Different from the long plateau after 0.5 V observed for graphite in the literature,35 the first discharge profile of MC presents a short voltage plateau at âˆ¼1.45 V, and then a long slope starting at âˆ¼1 V, which is similar to the voltage profile behavior of functionalized graphene nanoribbons previously reported,34 suggesting that more than one lithium storage mechanism was involved. The MC delivers a specific discharge capacity of 552 mA h gâˆ’1 and a specific charge capacity of 324 mA h gâˆ’1 in these initial processes. The irreversible capacity of 228 mA h gâˆ’1 could be caused by the formation of a solid electrolyte interface (SEI) layer on the surface of MC.36 Because Li+ is quite active in the first cycle which induces electrode instability, only 80â€“90% lithium was recovered in the first extraction process.35 Furthermore, an SEI layer may be formed during the first cycle due to the electrolyte decomposition that passivates the electrode, by allowing only the transportation of ions (e.g., Li+) but not electrons.37,38 After the first cycle, the charging curves of the 2nd, 3rd and 20th cycles well overlapped with each other, whereas the 60th cycle delivered an increased charging capacity. A further long-term cycling test (Fig. 5b) shows that the discharge capacity of MC did not show any decline beyond the second cycle (300 mA h gâˆ’1) and reached 311 mA h gâˆ’1 after 120 cycles. Excellent Coulombic efficiency was also observed for MC after the first 5 cycles (efficiency of 58.5% for the 1st cycle) with an average value of over 99.4%. These observations on MC represent a very attractive LIB performance among the carbon materials reported to date, cf., the initial values of hard carbon anodes are usually below 55% due to the formation of a thick SEI layer.39 Notably, the specific capability (311 mA h gâˆ’1) delivered by MC at 0.33C is much higher than that of commercial graphite (normally at 150â€“200 mA h gâˆ’1 at 0.33C).40 Previous research has pointed out that the major drawback of using graphite as LIB anode materials is its low theoretical capacity of 372 mA h gâˆ’1, calculated assuming that the LiC6 compound is formed for lithium storage.35 For MC studied here, the observed high lithium storage capability is possibly due to other lithium storage mechanisms, e.g., surface Li storage.41 Researchers have proposed that the formation of lithium multilayers on a carbon surface is an intermediate state between lithium intercalation and reduction to metallic lithium, which can be imaged as lithium plating onto the carbon surface at the electrode potential close to that of elemental lithium, resulting in additional lithium ions being converted into metallic lithium.41,42

In spite of the encouraging battery performance achieved, the energy density of the sugarcane waste-derived MC is still far from meeting the ever-increasing demand in the mobile electronics industry and for large-scale applications. In order to boost the energy density, different strategies can be implemented, such as integration with functional metal oxides (Fe3O4, CuO2, TiO2, SnO2, etc.),43â€“46 doping with active elements (B, N and S),47â€“49 and tailoring the surface functional groups.34 Herein, we utilize a novel sulfurization method to grow pyrrhotite-5T Fe1âˆ’xS nanoparticles on MC to boost its energy density. Our interest in the Fe1âˆ’xS modification strategy is based on the fact that sulfides possess high theoretical capacities, and are non-toxic and cost effective.50,51 Nonetheless, sulfide suffers a large volume change upon lithium uptake and release, and the formation of the insulating polysulfide Li2Sx (1 < x < 8)52 which is soluble in the liquid electrolyte and can gradually migrate to the cathode, causing severe loss of the active materials and poor cycling stability. Furthermore, the dissolved polysulfides can also deteriorate the conductivity of the electrolyte by forming an insulating layer outside the electrode, leading to capacity degradation.53,54 Recently, it was found that reducing the particle size of sulfides or wrapping them with a thin carbon layer could be an effective strategy in alleviating the dissolution problem of polysulfide.51,55,56 Here, a novel type of nanocomposite comprising iron sulfide nanoparticles and 3D MC is very promising for achieving high-performance LIBs.

The cycling performance of the Fe1âˆ’xS-MC nanocomposite was tested at a current density of 100 mA gâˆ’1 (Fig. 6a). Unlike the decay behaviors commonly observed for FeS-related electrodes in previous studies,57 Fe1âˆ’xS-MC delivers a stable capability in the first 100 cycles, which keeps increasing in the following cycles. In the first cycle, Fe1âˆ’xS-MC delivers an initial discharge capacity of 1094 mA h gâˆ’1 and a charge capacity of 787 mA h gâˆ’1, leading to a Coulombic efficiency of 72%. This great discharging/charging capacity is much higher than the theoretical value (609 mA h gâˆ’1 for FeS) and is possibly due to the formation of the specific SEI layer upon decomposition of the electrolyte solvent.43,58,59 Note that extra high capacity has been widely observed in transition metal oxide electrodes, which has been suggested to be due to the formation of a gel-like organic layer on the surface of the porous architectures.55,60 In the second cycle, Fe1âˆ’xS-MC delivers a discharge capacity of 788 mA h gâˆ’1 and charge capacity of 745 mA h gâˆ’1, showing a quickly increased Coulombic efficiency to 94%. After 200 cycles, the capacity gradually increases to 1185 mA h gâˆ’1, which is more than 3 times that of commercially used graphite (372 mA h gâˆ’1). To the best of our knowledge, this is the highest value among the iron sulfide-based materials reported to date (Fig. 7 and Table S1â€ ).50,51,57,61â€“65 The SEM image of Fe1âˆ’xS-MC nanocomposites after cycling at 100 mA gâˆ’1 for âˆ¼200 cycles (Fig. S8â€ ) reveals a varied morphology due to the formation of an SEI layer on the surface. However, remarkably, the Fe1âˆ’xS particles were still decorated on MC even after extended cycling with Li+ uptake and release, indicating the helpful role of MC in preserving the sulfide materials during cycling.

For comparison, the cycling performance of pure Fe1âˆ’xS electrodes was tested at 100 mA gâˆ’1, which showed poor cyclability (Fig. 6a). Although the initial discharge capacity (1219 mA h gâˆ’1) was higher than that of Fe1âˆ’xS-MC, it kept decreasing in the first 20 cycles, and quickly dropped to 235 mA h gâˆ’1 after 60 cycles. The poor cycling stability of the pure Fe1âˆ’xS particles is possibly due to the formation of undesirable polysulfide from Fe1âˆ’xS, the volume expansion of the sulfide, and the aggregation of the nanoparticles during the continuous Li+ uptake and release process. This result confirms the important role of MC, which helps alleviate the sulfide aggregation, prevent polysulfide dissolution, and increase the conductivity of the whole composite.

In order to evaluate their capability for high power density LIBs, the Fe1âˆ’xS-MC nanocomposites were galvanostatically tested at various current densities from 100 mA gâˆ’1 to 8000 mA gâˆ’1 (Fig. 6c). Excellent rate capability was observed for Fe1âˆ’xS-MC: the discharge capacities were 776, 728, 715, 669, 562 and 361 mA h gâˆ’1 at 100, 300, 500, 1000, 4000 and 8000 mA gâˆ’1, respectively. For comparison, the rate performance of pure Fe1âˆ’xS particles delivered discharge capacities of 826, 773, 735, 650, 408 and 232 mA h gâˆ’1 at 100, 300, 500, 1000, 4000 and 8000 mA gâˆ’1, respectively. It can be seen, that although the Fe1âˆ’xS particle electrode delivered higher specific capacities than Fe1âˆ’xS-MC at low current densities (100 and 300 mA gâˆ’1), it showed significantly lower specific capacities with much worse stability at high current densities (1000, 4000 and 8000 mA gâˆ’1). The cycling performances of Fe1âˆ’xS-MC and pure Fe1âˆ’xS at the high current density of 300 mA gâˆ’1 were further investigated (Fig. 6d). The capacity of Fe1âˆ’xS-MC decreased in the initial 10 cycles and then became stabilized afterwards, delivering a discharge capacity of 941, 662 and 630 mA h gâˆ’1 at the 1st, 10th and 40th cycle, respectively. On the other hand, with the capacity measured to be 959, 720 and 333 mA h gâˆ’1 at the 1st, 10th and 40th cycle, respectively, pure Fe1âˆ’xS delivered a slightly higher capacity than Fe1âˆ’xS-MC in the initial 20 cycles, but its performance deteriorated quickly afterwards, in good agreement with the cycling performance at 100 mA gâˆ’1 shown in Fig. 6a. The largely improved rate and cycling performance of Fe1âˆ’xS-MC is mainly attributed to the synergetic effects of the Fe1âˆ’xS nanostructures and the hierarchical MC framework. The well dispersed Fe1âˆ’xS nanoparticles are more tolerant to the large volume change associated with the lithium uptake and release process. The strong bonding between the Fe1âˆ’xS nanoparticles and the MC substrate may help alleviate the formation of the insulated polysulfide Li2Sx.50 Meanwhile, MC serves as a flexible and robust conductive scaffold that better accommodates the strain induced during the lithiation/delithiation process while maintaining good electrical contact with the Fe1âˆ’xS nanoparticles. Furthermore, by evenly dispersing the Fe1âˆ’xS nanoparticles on MC, self-aggregation of the Fe1âˆ’xS nanoparticles can be effectively restricted during the cycling process, while easy accessibility by the electrolyte is ensured with the active materials present in a 3D open hierarchical architecture with a large work surface area.

Conclusion

In summary, a scalable method has been introduced for conveniently transforming sugarcane waste into hierarchical microporous carbon (MC) for lithium-ion battery applications. The microporous carbon thus obtained exhibits a comparable storage capability to reduced graphene oxide, far better than the current commercial graphite. To further improve the lithium storage capability, Fe1âˆ’xS nanoparticles were deposited on the microporous carbon scaffold robustly, using a novel sulfurization method. The as-prepared hierarchical composite electrode of Fe1âˆ’xS-MC delivers excellent cycling stability, good rate retention, and a capacity well beyond the theoretical value. The superior performance of Fe1âˆ’xS-MC can be ascribed to the synergetic effects from the high flexibility and high conduction of the MC scaffolds, and the nanosized Fe1âˆ’xS. This work demonstrates the great potential for using biological waste as a rich recyclable resource for supplying sustainable and cost-effective solutions to energy challenges.
