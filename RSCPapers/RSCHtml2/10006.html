Abstract

To pave the way for InGaN-on-Si integrated photovoltaics, uniform and close-packed n-GaN/(Al)InGaN/p-GaN nanowire (NW) arrays with a âˆ¼0.29 Î¼m thick absorption segment of âˆ¼2.35 eV energy bandgap were fabricated on a Si substrate using Ti-mask selective area growth (SAG) in a molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) chamber. Instead of using thick and insulting buffer layers, this SAG process was realized by employing a 3 nm AlN/GaN: Ge buffer layer to facilitate electrical and thermal conduction between NWs and Si. Scanning transmission electron microscopy and high-resolution electron energy loss spectroscopy mapping revealed the discontinuities of AlN and the embedments of GaN:Ge which contribute to a negligible resistance of the NWs-on-Si interface. AlInGaN active segment exhibits coreâ€“shell structures, which suppress nonradiative surface recombination at NW surfaces. Working of AlInGaN coreâ€“shell NW solar cells was demonstrated with improved open-circuit voltage (Voc) and higher energy conversion efficiency (Î·) than those reported for InGaN NW solar cells. Stable output characteristics including the Voc of 1.41 V and Î· of 2.46% were obtained under 30-Sun illuminations. Such NWs-on-Si devices use Si substrate as the bottom electrode. With a low series resistance of âˆ¼1 Î©, this work paves the way to monolithically integrate MBE-SAG III-nitride devices and Si-based electronics, such as Si solar cells and CMOS devices.

1. Introduction

The monolithic integration of InGaN devices on Si is a desired approach to make full-solar-spectrum InGaN/Si multiple-band devices. Si solar cells (SCs) with a bandgap of âˆ¼1.1 eV are ideally suited as the bottom junction of double-junction photovoltaics (PVs) or double-band photosynthesis.1 InGaN alloy can exhibit a tunable energy bandgap from âˆ¼0.7 to 3.4 eV. Theoretical works report that such double-band PV devices exhibit an energy conversion efficiency (Î·) up to âˆ¼31% under 1-sun air-mass (AM) 1.5G illumination and higher under concentrated sunlight.2,3 Using III-nitride growth temperature lower than 900 Â°C,4 molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) is a good candidate technique for growing III-nitride alloy on industrial Si SCs.

The epitaxy of GaN-based crystals on a Si substrate and/or Si SCs can significantly lower manufacturing cost5 by eliminating the need for expensive substrates and additional integration processes such as mechanical stacking6 and wafer bonding.7,8 GaN-based planar heterostructures on Si exhibit large defect densities which limit their carrier lifetime and device efficiencies. The fundamental cause is the large lattice mismatch (âˆ¼17%) between GaN and Si. GaN-on-Si heterostructures have been widely explored using different growth methods, such as metalâ€“organic chemical vapor deposition (MOCVD)9â€“11 and MBE.12â€“14 In the earlier works of MOCVD III-nitride planar heterostructures,15,16 the thickness of the AlN buffer layer was generally 200â€“300 nm or more. However, such thick buffer layers are generally not desirable in nitride MBE growth, due to its slower growth rate.

It also remains difficult to realize efficient In-rich InGaN planar heterostructure on lattice-matched substrates, due to phase separation,17 presence of large defect densities,18 large strain-induced polarization field, the quantum-confined Stark effect (QCSE),19 and relatively poor p-type conduction.20 High-efficiency InGaN SCs generally require a thick absorption segment21 with high indium composition (â‰³25%)22,23 corresponding to a green spectral range or longer wavelengths. Recently, significant progress has been made in growing In-rich InGaN nanowire (NW) heterostructures exhibiting effective strain relaxation.24 However, the self-organized process of spontaneously grown InGaN NWs leads to relatively large fluctuations in NW diameter, length, and position, which cause spatial composition variations, diameter-dependent variation in dopant incorporation and random gaps between NWs. Such variations degrade the material, optical and electrical properties, such as the spectral intensity and linewidth, current densityâ€“voltage (Jâ€“V) characteristics and device efficiency. The selective area growth (SAG) technique enables the epitaxy of uniform GaN-based NW arrays with precisely controlled dimensions and position,25â€“27 which is promising to grow thick In-rich InGaN for PV applications.28â€“30

SAG techniques have been recently used in MOCVD for GaN-on-Si microLED technology, such as Î¼m-scale III-nitride heterostructures31 and NWs.32 NW SAG techniques have been used in MBE to realize monolithic, full-color-tunable InGaN microLED pixels on GaN/sapphire in one single-step growth.27 However, different from MOCVD-based SAG-techniques on Si, MBE-based SAG techniques encounter a challenge to control the NW size on Si12,14,33,34 without a buffer layer.

Different from spontaneously grown NWs1 in MBE, AlN/GaN buffer layers (â‰³40 nm) are generally used for III-nitride MBE SAG on Si.12,33,34 However, such buffer layers act as a barrier through which tunneling probability decreases dramatically with its barrier height.35 A thinner AlN layer exhibits a smaller barrier height, which was determined to be âˆ¼6 eV, 1.67 eV and 0.78 eV for 60 nm-thick,36 7.4 nm-thick35 and 0.7 nm-thick35 AlN layers, respectively. Using the n-Si substrate as the n-contact, A.-L. Bavencove et al. characterized vertically-aligned InGaN/GaN core/shell wire LED with AlN buffer layer in the electrical path.37 Such devices exhibit an estimated series resistance (Rs) of âˆ¼10 Î©.37 As such, there have been no reports of MBE-based SAG techniques to use a Si substrate as the electrical contact, due to either the usage of buffer layers (â‰³40 nm) with a large sbarrier height or difficulty in controlling NW size on Si. The resistance of such buffer layers is a bottleneck in using MBE-based SAG techniques to realize NWs-on-Si device applications, such as NWs-on-Si vertical field-effect transistor (FETs)38 and InGaN/Si multiple-junction PVs.

For nanostructured materials and devices, such as Si nanocrystals,39 GaAs NWs,40 asymmetric nanoporous gold nanostructures,41 perovskite film SCs42 and Inâ€“Gaâ€“Znâ€“O film transistors,43 their electrical, optical and/or chemical characteristics can be largely affected by defect properties of surfaces or interfacial regions. The commonly reported axial IIIâ€“V NWs suffer from large nonradiative surface recombination (NSR) associated with the presence of surface states, hence exhibiting short carrier lifetime.40,44 The NSR effect leads to low fill factor (FF),45,46 large leakage current45,47,48and Voc46,49 in NW SCs as well as poor carrier injection efficiency in NW LEDs.53 Various approaches have been used to suppress NSR. With multiple thin AlGaN layers incorporated in the NW active region, the efficiency and output power of InGaN NW LEDs can be significantly improved.50,51 By employing MOCVD coaxial coreâ€“shell structure, n-GaN/i-InGaN/p-GaN single-NW SC exhibits improved Voc and FF, but still limited in Jsc (0.39 mA cmâˆ’2) and Î· (up to âˆ¼0.19%) under 1-sun illumination.10,52 Taking advantages of CdS surface passivation, spontaneously grown InN NW array SCs exhibit improvements in Voc, FF, Î· and a reduction in the reverse leakage current.45 In our previous study, Al incorporation during the self-organized growth of a âˆ¼70 nm-thick InGaN segment leads to a coreâ€“shell passivation structure and hence improved LED output.53 However, the incorporation of multiple AlGaN layers and creating large barriers for carrier collections are not suitable for PV applications. In this work, Al incorporation in the SAG process of a relatively thick InGaN (âˆ¼0.29 Î¼m) segment was investigated for its effects on PV applications.

The PV output of single-junction InGaN-based NW SCs lag behind that of single-junction InGaN-based planar SCs. On GaN/sapphire and freestanding GaN substrates, Voc of 1.62â€“2.23 V, Jsc of 1.59â€“1.82 mA cmâˆ’2, FF of 52â€“61%, Î· of 0.98â€“1.41% were obtained from n-GaN/InGaN/p-GaN SCs grown by using MOCVD.54 Liou et al.23 have achieved Voc of 2.72â€“2.92 V, Jsc of 2.72â€“2.97 mA cmâˆ’2, FF of 61.51â€“74.89%, Î· of 4.97â€“5.95% from InGaN/GaN MQWs SCs grown on a SiCN/Si(1 1 1) substrate by using MOCVD. Such a large gap between NW SCs and planar SCs are attributed to a large NSR of NWs, a relatively small packing fraction55 of spontaneously grown NWs, challenges for uniform device fabrication and other issues. This work is dedicated to developing an approach of making uniform, close-packed (Al)InGaN NW-array SCs to improve the PV characteristics of NW SCs as well as paving the way for monolithic InGaN/Si multi-junction SCs.

Uniform and close-packed (Al)InGaN quaternary NW arrays were grown on a Si substrate by employing a 3 nm-thick AlN/GaN:Ge buffer layer using selective area growth. Ti-mask SAG process of such green-spectral-range pâ€“iâ€“n NW arrays was conducted using radio frequency (RF) plasma-assisted (PA) MBE. With the spontaneously formed the coreâ€“shell structure in AlInGaN segment,53 NWs were precisely and vertically aligned for a high packing fraction. The resulting NW SCs exhibits Voc of 1.10 V, Jsc of 3.12 mA cmâˆ’2, FF of 0.659 and Î· of 2.27% under 1-sun AM 1.5G illumination. Al incorporation suppresses NSR of SAG InGaN NWs and enhances the photoluminescence (PL) and PV characteristics. This work demonstrates SAG NWs-on-Si PV applications operated under 1-sun and concentrated illuminations. Under 30-sun illumination, NW SCs exhibit stable and repeatable output characteristics with the benefits of using the Si substrate as an electrical contact.

2. Experimental

2.1 Selective area growth of NW arrays on Si

Fig. 1a schematically shows SAG pâ€“iâ€“n NW heterostructures on patterned n-Si substrate. AlN has been widely adopted as the seed layer for GaN or InGaN hetroepitaxy on Si due to its relatively small thermal expansion coefficient and well-suppressed interfacial reactions with Si. 2.3 Device fabrication and photovoltaic characterization

NWs-on-Si device fabrication. After dipping NWs-on-Si samples in BOE, Ti (20 nm)/Au (100 nm) were deposited onto the backside of the n-Si substrate to serve as n-metal contacts. After p-contact (Ni/Au/ITO) layers lift-off, p-contact only forms in SiOx openings. 3. Results and discussion

3.1 Characteristics of conductive NWs-on-Si interface

Vertical-type devices with top and bottom electrodes enable large device density on a single chip. Thousands of vertical-type components, such as vertically-aligned pâ€“iâ€“n single SAG NWs,24 can be deposited and fabricated at the same time. The manufacturing process of such chips reduces the time and cost for production and test process. Such devices with conductive substrates facilitate efficient carrier collection/injection.24,56 The SAG NW-on-Si approach enables such a manufacturing process with scaling advantages over lateral devices.57 Another advantage of SAG NW-on-Si structure is a straightforward fabrication of metal contact at the backside of Si substrates. In contrast, devices on high-resistance substrates generally require a side electrode. The side-electrode fabrication includes selective-area etching active materials down to the bottom n-GaN or n-AlGaN layer and deposition of contact next to active regions.38,58

In order to make the resistance of the AlN/GaN:Ge buffer layer negligible, a very thin (a few nm) AlN/GaN:Ge buffer layer was used in this SAG work. As shown schematically in Fig. 1a, on the exposed AlN/GaN:Ge buffer layer, nanoholes are firstly filled to form n-GaN nucleation sites for the subsequent epitaxy of pâ€“iâ€“n NW heterostructures. As shown in Fig. 1b, the HAADF-STEM image justifies the schematic illustration of the NWs-on-Si interface. This STEM study was conducted on a sample with a 100 nm thickness along the electron beam direction. The thickness of the ultra-thin AlN/GaN:Ge buffer layer between Ti mask and Si substrate was measured to be 3 nm. A high-resolution HAADF-STEM image of the NW-on-Si interface is shown in Fig. S2 (ESIâ€ ).

As shown in Fig. 2, Al, Ga, N, Si and Ti elemental distributions at the NWs-on-Si interfacial region were characterized by using high-resolution EELS mapping. As shown in Fig. 2b and d, N and Ga distributions clearly present the profiles of NW bottom segments. The sharp and smooth interface of Si distribution (Fig. 2a) justifies the extremely high precision and sensitivity of EELS mapping. At the 3 nm thick NWs-on-Si interface, both Ga and Al distribution are discontinuous, in contrast to the continuous N distribution. At the interface, the discontinuities (dark or intensity close to zero) in Al elemental distribution indicate the existence of large number of pits and voids in the AlN layer, considering that the pixel size of EELS mapping (âˆ¼0.2 nm) is much smaller than the Al discontinuities (maximum size: âˆ¼10 nm). The subsequent epitaxy of the GaN:Ge layer also shows discontinuities (maximum size: âˆ¼16 nm), such as in positions 1, 2, 5, 6, 7, and 8 (marked with white dashed lines in Fig. 2d and e), where Ga shows no visible intensity, while Al shows strong intensity. Importantly, the growth of GaN:Ge takes place in some pits and voids of the AlN layer, such as positions 3, 4 and 9 (marked with red dashed lines in Fig. 2d and e), where Ga shows a relatively strong intensity, while Al shows discontinuities. In addition, it is highly possible that Ga, Ge, and Al interdiffuse during high-temperature NW growth, resulting in the formation of AlGaN:Ge, GaN:Ge, Ti and AlGaN:Ge fill in structural vacancies of the AlN layer, facilitating carrier transport and carrier tunneling through the buffer layer. Similar approaches to filling the vias in the AlN layer with highly conductive n-AlGaN have been reported to facilitate carrier transport through AlN.57

3.2 SAG process of NWs-on-Si

The presence of a weak but noticeable N K intensity in the Ti mask layer (Fig. 2b) indicates the formation of TiNx in the 3 min nitridation process prior to NW epitaxy and during NW epitaxy. However, the resulting Ti mask layer is a Ti-majority Ti/TiNx alloy because N K distribution is weak and relatively limited in the Ti mask region. This observation reveals the nature of the SAG alloy mask layer, which is critical for atom sticking and NW selectivity. In addition, there is almost no Ga signal (Fig. 2d) on the mask layer, indicating good selectivity during the NWs-on-Si SAG process.

The n-GaN nucleation site, which is confined and shaped by nanohole of Ti mask, can be clearly observed at NW bottoms, shown in Fig. 1b and Fig. 2. That bottom-most region exhibits a smaller diameter due to the restriction of nanohole. Once n-GaN grows beyond Ti mask, NW diameter increases with a lateral growth rate.

On the patterned Si substrate, n-GaN/(Al)InGaN/p-GaN NW arrays were grown with controllable NW diameter and positions. Such pâ€“iâ€“n (Al)InGaN NW consists of âˆ¼0.49 Î¼m GaN:Si bottom segment, âˆ¼0.29 Î¼m (Al)InGaN active segment and âˆ¼0.15 Î¼m GaN:Mg top segment. Taking advantage of the SAG flexibility of controlling nanohole diameter and periodicity, the NW array-packing fraction can be increased to be nearly unity to improve light absorption. With careful tuning of MBE parameters and growth duration of the n-GaN segment, a high packing fraction was obtained for the AlInGaN NW array without coalescing between neighboring NWs, as shown in Fig. 3a. The SAG NW array exhibits Ga-polarity based on the observation of semipolar top facets and hexagonal pyramid morphology at the top. As a result of Ga-polarity crystal structure, the nano-pyramid features at the top of SAG NWs can further improve light harvesting.60 For those NW arrays, the nanohole-array pattern on Ti maskhad a hexagonal opening with a 102 nm side length (a diameter of 204 nm) and the array was arranged in a triangular lattice with a 320 nm periodicity.

To obtain uniform and closed-packed AlInGaN NW arrays with high crystal quality, it is essential to grow uniform GaN:Si NW segments with perfect SAG selectivity. This requires optimized MBE parameters (III/V ratio, Tsub and Ga flux) and well-controlled pattern parameters (nanohole size and periodicity). In the case that undesired GaN:Si grows on the Ti mask, the subsequent AlInGaN and GaN:Mg continue growing on those undesired GaN. Those undesired crystals may grow and combine with neighboring SAG NWs, leading to coalescence and shadowing effect.

To study the effects of Al incorporation into the SAG InGaN active region on the performance of NWs-on-Si SCs, MBE parameters of (Al)InGaN segments were varied, as shown in Table 1, for NWs A, B, C and D. Their MBE parameters of GaN:Si and GaN:Mg segments were kept the same among these growths. The desorption of In atoms is much stronger compared than that of Al and Ga atoms.61 To enhance In incorporation in (Al)InGaN segments, Tsubstrate was reduced to 700â€“715 Â°C and the N2 flow rate was increased to 1.5 sccm. The adopted III/V ratio for the (Al)InGaN segment was much lower than that for n-GaN and p-GaN segments.

3.3 Characteristics of SAG AlInGaN coreâ€“shell structure

PL emissions of NWs A, B, C and D are shown in Fig. 3b. Summarized in Table 1, their peak emission wavelength (Î»PL) varies in the range of 513.8â€“527.9 nm, corresponding to the photon energy of 2.349â€“2.413 eV. With increasing Al BEP and increasing Tsubstrate, Al content of (Al)InGaN segment increases, leading to the blueshift of peak wavelength. PL full-width-half-maximum (FWHM) varies in the range of 32.4â€“42.9 nm, corresponding to 146â€“200 meV. Such FWHM is smaller than that reported for self-organized green InGaN NW array and InGaN quantum wells (QWs) epilayers.18,19,58 The reported relatively broader emissions (âˆ¼58â€“87 nm (ref. 50 and 62)) of self-organized InGaN NW array are resulting from inhomogeneous In distribution in each NW, and compositional variations due to fluctuations in NW diameter and position, which is also a challenge to improve the packing fraction. Grown on GaN/sapphire, SAG green InGaN/GaN QW NW arrays with a thin InGaN segment (âˆ¼50 nm) exhibits narrow spectral linewidths, such as linewidths of âˆ¼27 nm (135 meV)26 with Î»PL of 506 nm and linewidths of 31.5â€“37.5 nm (ref. 25) with Î»PL of 529.1â€“543.3 nm. In the current study of the SAG AlInGaN NW array on the Si substrate, Al incorporation and thick active segment (âˆ¼5 times thicker) do not increase the linewidth significantly, compared to those linewidths of 31.5â€“37.5 nm (ref. 25) with Î»PL of 529.1â€“543.3 nm.

A two-dimensional In-deficient shell (AlGaN shell) was formed surrounding the In-rich core in the AlInGaN segment of SAG NWs, as shown in Fig. S5 (ESIâ€ ). The underlying formation mechanism is caused by both low In incorporation63 at the lateral surfaces (m-plane) and strong In desorption. Under relatively high Tsubstrate, In atoms experience much stronger thermal desorption compared to Ga and Al atoms.61 At NW growth front (polar c-plane), direct impingent and diffusive In adatoms can immediately compensate the desorbed In atom. However, the In supply to the lateral surfaces is limited due to the shadowing effect of neighboring NWs.61,64 The spontaneous formation of an AlGaN shell surrounding In-rich core is also attributed to smaller Al atom diffusion length compared to that of Ga and In atoms.44 Importantly, such a large-bandgap AlGaN shell provides radial surface passivation for In-rich core to suppress non-radiative surface recombination.53

Variations of Î»PL, PL FWHM, and integrated PL peak intensity versus Ï• are summarized in Fig. 4aâ€“c, respectively. 4. Conclusions

Uniform and close-packed pâ€“iâ€“n GaN-based nanowire arrays were selective-area grown on a patterned Si substrates with a negligible-resistance AlN/GaN:Ge buffer layer, enabling SAG GaN-based NWs-on-Si devices with a relatively low series resistance of âˆ¼1 Î©. The results on selective area epitaxy and NW devices demonstrate that such a 3 nm-thick AlN/GaN:Ge buffer layer maintains the effectiveness and flexibility of the SAG technique to grow a uniform NW array with controlled dimensions and position on the Si substrate. High-precision HAADF-STEM and EELS-STEM characterizations of NWs-on-Si interface support the SAG NW architecture. Ga, Al and Ti elemental distributions explain the negligible resistance of the AlN/GaN:Ge buffer layer. During the selective area epitaxy of AlInGaN segments, In-rich core is surrounded by a spontaneously formed AlGaN shell, which can effectively suppress the nonradiative surface recombination and improve NW optical and photovoltaic characteristics. Such a coreâ€“shell structure, obtained by using proper Al corporation during the MBE process, improves Voc from 0.34 to 1.10 V, FF from 0.385 to 0.659 and Î· from 0.585% to 2.27% under 1-Sun illumination. The light-generated current density of AlInGaN NWs SC does not increase proportionally with illumination intensity and it is mainly associated with the blueshift of the absorption edge with increasing photon flux density. The use of a relatively thick absorption segment (âˆ¼0.29 Î¼m), AlInGaN coreâ€“shell structure, and Si substrate significantly increase the device stability and performance for high-power photovoltaics without cooling, by absorbing high-intensity illumination, minimizing nonradiative recombination, and enhancing the heat dissipation. The SAG NWs-on-Si structure paves the way to monolithically integrate SAG III-nitride/Si integrated electronics by using MBE, such as InGaN/Si tandem solar cells and CMOS-compatible III-nitride on-chip electronics.
