Structural modulation of phenothiazine and coumarin based derivatives for high performance dye sensitized solar cells: a theoretical study

Abstract

A series of dyes with the DA architecture has been designed and studied for dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs). We have used phenothiazine (PTZ) and coumarin (COU) derivatives as the donor unit and benzopyrrole (BTZ) and 2-methyl-2H-isoindole-1,3-(3aH,7aH)-diene (IND) as the acceptor unit along with the azomethine group and thiophene ring as the -spacer unit. Three electron donating groups viz. CH3, NH2, and OH and four electron withdrawing groups viz. CF3, COCl, F and NO2 have been attached at the donor and the acceptor units respectively of the four unsubstituted dyes COUBTZ, PTZBTZ, COUIND and PTZIND. Density functional theory (DFT) and time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT) methods have been employed to investigate the structural, electronic and photochemical properties of these dyes. The study reveals that the unsubstituted dye PTZBTZ possesses the lowest value of HL. Our study also reveals that attachment of the NO2 group at the acceptor unit lowers the HL values of all the dye molecules. We have observed that the excited state oxidation potential (ESOP) of all the dyes lies above the conduction band of the TiO2 semiconducting surface. However, the ground state oxidation potential (GSOP) of most of the dyes belonging to the COUBTZ and COUIND groups lies below the redox potential of the I/I3 redox couple. The total reorganization energy (tot) values of the COUBTZ and COUIND groups of dyes are observed to be low compared to the other groups of dyes. The study of the charge transport properties of the dyes confirms that the designed dyes will act as electron transport materials. The absorption properties of the dyes show that the COUBTZ group of dyes possesses the maximum values of the absorption wavelength (max values) and attaching the NO2 group at the acceptor unit shifts the max values of all the dyes to the longer region. From the study of the electronic properties of the dyeTiO2 complexes it has been observed that the performance of the dyes has been enhanced compared to the isolated dye molecules.

1 Introduction

Since the last century, the world has been facing a huge energy crisis and as a result serious environmental issues have been raised.1 Extensive research has been going on in the field of clean and green renewable resources as an alternative source of energy.25 Silicon based first generation solar cells are getting attraction day by day as they have the highest power conversion efficiency (PCE). But, compared to the organic counterparts silicon based inorganic solar cells possess some limitations like their high cost, difficulties in the manufacturing process, less mechanical flexibility, etc.5,6 Cadmium telluride (CdTe) and copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS) based second generation solar cells have low cost of production and are efficient for the mentioned purpose, but due to their toxicity and unavailability their production has been shut down. In this regard, the third generation solar cells, dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), also known as Gratzel cells, have been attaining enormous attention.1 Academic and commercial interest has been focused on DSSCs due to their low cost of production, simple assembly technology and ease of fabrication, and wide availability of the materials, which are environment friendly.1,2,4,720

Wide and extensive research has been performed on DSSCs since Gratzel and co-workers reported a DSSC based on Ru-complex photosensitizers in 1991.2126 Reportedly, a DSSC based on a ruthenium (Rupolypyridyl) complex has a PCE up to 11%.8,12,18,2531 It has also been reported that organic dyes in DSSCs exhibit a PCE over 12%.3235 However, the higher cost of Ru metal and lower extinction coefficient (usually <2.50 104 M1 cm1 in the visible region) have limited the development of Ru based dyes.1,2,22 Therefore, the study of metal free sensitizers i.e., organic sensitizers for application in DSSCs has become a rising topic these days. However, certain factors have been proven to be disadvantageous for organic dyes as well, such as their relatively sharp and narrow absorption bands in the visible region, which are not suitable for harvesting solar energy.5,27 Secondly, the lifetimes of emission of their excited states are generally shorter than those of metal complexes (e.g., 0.45 ns).5,21,25 Thirdly, organic dyes are reportedly found to aggregate on the semiconductor surface.25,36 In spite of these difficulties, much improvement has been made on metal-free organic dye based DSSCs by several groups.

Organic dye sensitized solar cells consist of a donoracceptor (DA) moiety where a conjugated spacer covalently links the donor and the acceptor thereby allowing charge transfer from the donor to the acceptor through the bridge.2 The dye is held on the semiconducting surface (TiO2, SiO2, etc.) by anchoring groups like cyanoacrylic acid groups or carboxylic acid groups.37 A DSSC includes a counter conducting electrode, a photoanode and an electrolyte (usually a redox iodide/triiodide couple (I/I3)).23 In DSSCs the electron injection and the electron regeneration processes are two important steps. In these cells, the conduction band of the TiO2 semiconducting surface lies at 4.00 eV and the chemical potential of the I/I3 couple lies at 4.80 eV.38,39 The electron injection and the electron regeneration processes are possible only when the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of the sensitizer lies below the chemical potential of the redox couple and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the sensitizer lies above the conduction band of the semiconductor.

In the DA architecture of DSSCs, the most commonly used donors are triphenylamine, indoline, coumarin, bis-dimethylfluorenylamino, phenothiazine, etc. Commonly used spacers are methane chains, thiophene, furan, pyrrole, benzene, etc.1 The aromatic ring spacer segment lessens the reorganization energy, facilitate effective electron transfer and maintains the planarity of the skeleton, which reduces steric congestion, allowing more compact dye packing on the semiconducting surface.40 Electron withdrawing groups like cyanoacrylic acid and carboxylic acid groups act as good anchoring groups which are bonded covalently to the semiconductor. The carboxylic acid can bind to the semiconducting surface via a unidentate mode, chelating mode, and bridging bidentate mode.37,41

The photophysical properties of DSSCs can be tuned by allowing a shift in the HOMO and LUMO.2 The shift can be brought about by extending the -conjugation or by adding various substituents (electron donating and electron withdrawing groups) in the donor and acceptor. This kind of molecular engineering can bring about a red shift in the absorption spectra of organic dyes, thereby increasing the charge transfer efficiency.2 In this paper, we have chosen four parent donoracceptor (DA) complexes with a common -spacer. We have used phenothiazine (PTZ) and coumarin (COU) derivatives as the donor unit and benzopyrrole (BTZ) and 2-methyl-2H-isoindole-1,3-(3aH,7aH)-diene (IND) as the acceptor unit.21 The thiophene ring along with the azomethine unit is chosen as the -linker, while the anchoring group cyanoacrylic acid is functionalized over the semiconductor surface.6 Here, we have used TiO2 as the semiconductor surface. The four DA moieties selected are COUBTZ, PTZBTZ, COUIND and PTZIND. In order to tune the performance of DSSCs, three different electron donating groups viz. CH3, NH2, and OH and four different electron withdrawing groups viz. CF3, COCl, F and NO2 have been incorporated into the donor and acceptor moieties, respectively. The compounds having substituents CH3, NH2, OH, CF3, COCl, F, and NO2 are represented by the numbers 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 and 7, respectively. Sketches of the studied compounds have been provided in Fig. 1. The coordinates of the studied compounds have been provided in Table S1 (in the ESI).

2 Computational methodology

All the calculations have been carried out using the Gaussian 09 program package.42 The ground-state geometries of all the dyes have been optimized by employing the density functional theory (DFT) method. The optimized structures have been confirmed to be at the minimum-energy points by vibrational analysis. To validate the functionals used in our calculations we have performed test calculations with the dye DIA3 reported in the literature as it has structural similarity with our designed compounds.43,44 The test calculations have been performed using the DFT method with the functionals B3LYP, B3LYP-D3, CAM-B3LYP, M06-2X-D3, PBEPBE, and B97XD and the 6-31G(d,p) basis set. The optimized structure of the test compound has been presented in Fig. S1 (ESI) and the calculated results have been reported in Table S2 (in the ESI). From this table, it is observed that for the ground state calculations the results obtained using the functionals B3LYP and B3LYP-D3 are in better agreement with the experimentally reported values. On the other hand, for the excited state calculations the results obtained using the functionals CAM-B3LYP and B97XD are in better agreement with the experimentally reported values. However, to keep the computational cost under control we have performed the ground state and excited state calculations employing the functionals B3LYP and CAM-B3LYP, respectively. The plot of the UV-visible spectra of the test compound has been presented in Fig. S2 (in the ESI).

For the solvent phase calculations we have employed the conductor-like polarizable continuum model (CPCM) where tetrahydrofuran (THF) is chosen as a solvent.45 The charge state optimization, energy calculation of the neutral and charged state, and ionization potential (IP), electron affinity (EA), and reorganization energy () calculations have been carried out at the B3LYP-D3/6-31G(d,p) level of theory. For the calculation of electronic coupling matrix element (V) values the M06-2X-D3/6-31G(d,p) level has been employed because this functional includes dispersion correction and is considered suitable for the study of charge transfer properties.46

For the optimization of the dyeTiO2 complexes the split valence method has been used. The B3LYP/6-31G(d,p) level of theory has been employed for the atoms other than the Ti atom and for the Ti atom the B3LYP functional along with the LANL2DZ basis set has been employed.47

To examine the charge-transfer properties of the dyes, we have calculated the values of the ionization potential (IP) and electron affinity (EA) of the dyes, which affect the electron injection and hole generation process. The IP can be defined as the energy required to remove an electron from the neutral species of a molecule. Thus, a low IP value ensures easy removal of an electron, which in turn creates a hole easily. The EA can be defined as the difference between the energies of the neutral and anion species of a molecule in their lowest energy states.6,23,45 Thus, it is similar to the binding energy of an electron to the molecule. It has already been reported that the EA of the dye molecule allows a desensitizing process or causes recombination between the injected electrons and the oxidized dye species. If the EA value is high the removal of the conduction band electrons will be very difficult. Therefore, to transfer electrons efficiently to the semiconducting surface a small EA value is desirable for a dye sensitizer.23

To examine the process of spontaneous electron injection and dye regeneration we have calculated the oxidation potential of the studied dyes. The ground state oxidation potential (GSOP) is defined as the difference between the energies of the neutral and oxidized ground state of the dyes in solution. To obtain charge transportation the GSOP values of the dyes must lie below the redox potential of the I/I3 electrolyte. Similarly, the ESOP values must lie above the conduction band, ECB, of TiO2 to trigger spontaneous electron injection and dye regeneration.23 The calculated GSOP and ESOP values of the dyes have been provided in Fig. 5.

4.2 Reorganization energies, charge transfer integrals and charge transfer rates of the dyes

The reorganization energy () is an important parameter describing the charge transfer processes in organic molecules. We have calculated the reorganization energies for holes (h) and electrons (e) and their values have been reported in Table 4. From this table it is observed that the e values of the compounds are lower than the h values. This result implies that the designed molecules will act as electron transport materials. From Table 4 it is also observed that the COUBTZ group of dyes possesses the lowest value of e among all the groups of dyes. Moreover, it is observed that compounds COUBTZ-1 and COUIND-5 possess the minimum and maximum values of e, respectively. This can be explained with the help of the plot of the frontier molecular orbitals of these compounds provided in Fig. S3 (in the ESI). The working mechanism of DSSCs is that the dye molecule absorbs sunlight to produce an exciton (electronhole pair), which splits only after reaching the dyesemiconducting surface interface. The binding energy is the amount of energy needed to pull apart the hole and electron from an electronhole pair. The lower the binding energy, the easier will be the charge separation from the dye to the semiconducting surface.62,67 The exciton binding energy (Eb) can be described as Eb = HL E1, where E1 is the optical band gap, i.e. the energy required for S0 S1.68,69 We have provided the respective values of Eb in Table 7. From this table, it has been observed that the dyes COUBTZ-7, PTZBTZ-2, COUIND-4, and PTZIND-4 exhibit comparatively lower binding energy than the rest of the dyes, which in turn leads to facile charge separation. On the other hand, all unsubstituted parent dyeTiO2 complexes exhibit maximum binding energy. In other words the transfer of electrons from the dye to the semiconducting surface will be most difficult in these complexes.

7 Conclusion

In this paper, we have designed and studied a series of dyes based on the DA approach. We have performed a theoretical study on 32 compounds designed by incorporating four groups of dyes viz. COUBTZ, PTZBTZ, COUIND and PTZIND. In order to study their structural properties we have calculated the ground state dihedral angles of the compounds. The electronic properties viz. the g, e, and HL values of the compounds have also been envisaged. From the observed HL values of the compounds it is evident that the unsubstituted PTZBTZ dye possesses the lowest value of HL. Moreover, it is also noticed that the NO2 substituent lowers the HL values of all the compounds to the maximum extent. The obtained IP and EA values of the compounds suggest that the COUIND, PTZBTZ and PTZIND groups of dyes are effective candidates for the construction of DSSCs. However, the GSOP, ESOP, Greg, Ginj, LHC and Voc values of the compounds suggest that the COUBTZ and COUIND groups of dyes are more efficient candidates for the fabrication of DSSCs. From the values of the observed total reorganization energy, tot, it can be confirmed that the COUBTZ and COUIND groups of dyes are the best choice. Further, the charge transfer rate of the dyes reveals the electron transporting nature of our designed dyes. From the absorption properties of the dyes it has been observed that the COUBTZ group of dyes possesses the maximum value of max and substitution at the acceptor unit by the NO2 group shifts the max values of the compounds to the longer wavelength region. Thus, we can conclude that the COUBTZ and COUIND groups of dyes are more efficient candidates for the fabrication of DSSCs and substitution of the acceptor unit by the NO2 group improves the performance of the designed dyes.

From the study of the structural and electronic properties of the dyes anchored to the TiO2 surface it has been observed that the HL values of the dyes get decreased and the max values of the complexes undergo a red shift compared to the isolated dye molecules. Therefore, it can be concluded that upon anchoring the studied dyes to the TiO2 surface the performance of the designed dyes is improved.
