Abstract

The attractive theoretical specific capacity of silicon (Si) makes it a strong candidate for use in electrochemical energy storage materials. Si exists in organisms in the form of silicic acid or SiO2, which is converted into simple Si or SiOx (0 < x < 2) by a simple reduction reaction. The synthesis of nano/micro structure Si-based materials from biomass with a high Si content has become an ideal method for their conversion into functional materials. Thus, the recovery of value-added products such as rice husks (RHs) and diatoms for the development of a green and economical way for the valorization of biomass material wastes has gained significant attention. In this review, the synthesis of Si-based materials derived from biomass and their applications in the field of electrochemical energy storage were summarized and the effect of their morphology was emphasized. Finally, in view of the application prospects and challenges of the biomass-derived Si-based materials, appropriate improvement schemes were proposed and prospects for the future practical applications of these materials were discussed.

1. Introduction

Since the early 21st century, energy and environmental issues have become increasingly serious. Many countries have devoted their scientific and technological strength and funds towards vigorously developing green energy. As high-performance electrochemical technologies are being currently developed, energy storage materials with high efficiencies and high densities have become a hotspot among researchers.1–8 In addition, some phenomena such as the overgrowth of algae and the burning of RHs and wheat straw are very common. The improper use and treatment of a large number of biomass materials have aggravated resource depletion and environmental pollution problems. The development and efficient utilization of biomass and its derivatives have become a hot topic in biofuels, electrocatalysis, and other fields. Li et al.9 chose low-cost lignocellulosic biomass (corn fibers, cotton stalks, soybean hulls, and sugarcane bagasse) fibers to produce n-butanol through microbial fermentation, which is a potential alternative fuel. The nitrogen-doped graphene-like mesoporous nanosheets synthesized from okara have been proven to be non-enzymatic electrochemical biosensors for vitamin C.10 Fontes-Candia et al.11 prepared superabsorbent food packaging bioactive cellulose-based aerogels derived from A. donax waste biomass via a simple freeze-drying method. Meanwhile, a growing number of efforts have been concentrated on the effective utilization of raw biomass materials for the extant applications in energy storage.12 Biomass mainly consists of animals, plants and microorganisms (Scheme 1a).13 As the core element of organisms is carbon, organisms are commonly used as the carbon source (such as ants,14 bacteria,15 and wisteria pod16) after activation and applied in the field of electrochemical energy storage. Wang et al.17 reviewed the application of biomass-derived renewable carbon materials in electrochemical energy storage with a detailed classification of biomass; the relevant research has attracted sustained interest. For instance, the coconut shell-derived activated carbon–sulfur composite synthesized by Manoj et al.18 delivered high capacity and excellent cycling stability. As an advanced electrode material for supercapacitors (SCs), the 3D porous graphitic carbon material derived from bamboo char synthesized by Gong et al.19 exhibited an excellent electrochemical performance. Interestingly, research indicates that substantial amounts of Si are also generally stored in biomass materials; for example, the content of silica (SiO2) in RHs is nearly 20 wt%.3

Recently, Si has become a hot topic for researchers due to its two superior properties that are often neglected: a low working potential and high theoretical capacity (4200 mA h g−1).20,21 In addition, considerable natural abundance, environmental safety, and the above-mentioned advantages have improved the reliability of Si for use as an electrode material.22–24 Interestingly, the unique orderly, delicate and complex structures of organisms created by nature can be reproduced naturally but cannot be reproduced by the existing manufacturing technologies.25–28 Therefore, obtaining Si-based products will be a prevalent research trend.29 Using a biogenic SiO2 source instead of a traditional Si source to obtain pure Si and its derivative materials can significantly and comprehensively benefit the economy and environment.29

As an excellent semiconductor material, Si provides an ideal platform in the field of SCs. SiC nanomaterials have become promising electrode materials for SCs due to their high specific surface area and excellent thermal stability.30 Si nanowire arrays have a ultrahigh electrode surface area, which results in significant increase in capacitance density.31 Furthermore, core–shell SiNWs/PEDOT@PPy synthesized by Zhou et al.32 delivered specific capacitance of 106 mF cm−2. Si has also been used in LIB applications. However, in the process of alloying/dealloying Si with lithium, the equilibrium lithium–silicon alloy can achieve a maximum lithium–silicon concentration ratio (Li22Si5) when Si is completely lithified, which leads to the dramatic volume expansion of Si (>300%).33 Si is severely crushed by mechanical stress during charging and discharging, and the continuous formation of an unstable solid electrolyte interface (SEI)34–37 results in rapid capacity loss and poor cycling performance, thus greatly limiting the commercial applications of Si in LIBs.38,39 So far, a variety of measures have been taken to deal with these challenges. The first strategy involves adjusting the morphology of Si.40–43 Among these morphologies, some special and ingenious structures (such as porous structures and hollow structures) provide abundant voids, which can improve the mechanical strength, thus allowing these materials to adapt to volume changes.44 The natural and unique porous structures in biomass materials such as bamboo leaves, reed leaves, and RHs are highly desirable. Nano-crystallization can reduce the particle size, shorten the transport path of Li+ and electrons in the phase and alleviate the change in the absolute volume.45–49 Another effective strategy is to uniformly mix Si particles with active or inert materials.50–57 The formation of a composite means that the particles of the active materials are evenly dispersed in the lattice of inert or active materials that act either as a buffer, skeleton, dispersant, or conductive material. To maintain the integrity of the structure during charging and discharging, the composite should be devoted to ameliorating the mechanical strength and flexibility of the materials. Coatings can be used as soft media to flexibly adapt to volume collapse and improve the transfer motion performance.57 By coating a layer of carbon on the surface of the Si particles, the overall conductivity and particle dispersibility of Si can be improved. In addition, electrolytes cannot contact Si directly because of the carbon barrier.58–64 Furthermore, compared with ordinary carbon coatings, heteroatomic carbon materials have a better electron transfer rate. Due to the synergy between the heteroatoms and the carbon materials, the resulting heteroatomic carbon materials exhibit an improved SC electrochemical performance compared to single doped materials.65 In addition, compounding Si with metal oxides or performing the in situ growth of silicates on an SiO2/C substrate is also an effective method to improve the performance of Si-based materials.

To the best of our knowledge, only a few papers about Si-based materials for applications in energy storage have been published. For instance, Zhang et al.66 reviewed the recent progress in the preparation of porous Si and nano-Si for use as electrode materials in lithium-ion batteries using various Si precursors; they found that porous Si and nano-Si permit larger volume changes during rapid charge–discharge because of the advantages of pores and nanostructures. These Si precursors include bulk-sized Si (e.g., Si wafers and Si microparticles), SiO2 (e.g., natural plants, sand, etc.), gaseous Si-based sources (e.g., SiH4, Si2H6, etc.), liquid Si sources, and elemental Si. Shen67 reviewed the recent research progress in using RH-derived Si-based materials in energy and environmental functional materials from the perspective of synthesis and applications. RHs can be converted into biofuels by pyrolysis and gasification and can be further converted to renewable biofuels under the action of RH-derived catalysts. Similarly, Sun et al.68 emphatically introduced a unique three-dimensional layered porous structure of diatoms, summarized the development of the composite materials used in SCs, batteries, and solar cells, and introduced new concepts. 2. Common Si-containing organisms

Biomass materials have been used as renewable resources since a long time. To this day, converting organisms into thermal energy via combustion and transforming these organisms into biofuels using the reprocessing technology are two of the most common techniques. A growing number of efforts have been devoted to the development and utilization of biomass-based materials and their derivatives.65,69,70 Some previous studies have shown the potential of some renewable natural by-products, such as RHs, bamboo leaves, sugarcane, and sage, for use as electrical energy storage materials.12,71–74 First, the common feature of these organisms is that they are rich in Si. Furthermore, after simple treatments, such as low-temperature calcination, the biomass inherits the original three-dimensional and multi-level structure, thus yielding materials with rich layered pores.75 Most of the reactions used to extract biomass to yield Si-based materials avoid high temperatures, and the most common method is a metal reduction reaction. The so-called metal thermal reduction process uses reducing metals, such as Mg and Al, as reducing agents; because it uses a low process temperature and the by-products are easy to remove, this method is widely used in the reduction of SiO2.43,76,77

Bamboo is considered as a “natural Si reservoir”3 and mainly consists of leaves and stems (Fig. 1a). The distribution of SiO2 in bamboo is not uniform; the content of SiO2 in bamboo increases from the roots to the leaves. Due to the differences in the geographical environments and varieties of bamboo, the content of biogenetic SiO2 in dried bamboo leaves is approximately 13–41 w%.78 As shown in Fig. 1b, bamboo leaves have fascinating biological structures, including complex nanostructures; these nanostructures include parallel vascular and fibrous bundles and tightly arranged bamboo cells.79 The 3D mesoporous C–SiO2 architectures obtained by the simple calcination of bamboo leaves maintain the native micro/nanostructures inherited from the wrinkles on the surface of bamboo leaves.65 Bamboo leaves are used as Si sources to manufacture silicate composites, which exhibit excellent latent capacities in SCs. Bamboo charcoal is rich in minerals and has pores and a specific surface area of more than 300 m2 g−1; thus, it has high adsorption capacity. Bamboo charcoal materials exhibit attractive porous properties. Fig. 1c shows the apparent 3D ordered pore structure (with pore size distribution of 20–30 μm) associated with microtubules.3

RHs are one of the most common residues in agricultural production and are also a renewable biomass Si source preferred by researchers (Fig. 1d).80 Statistics show that 1/6th of the annual global rice production (where the annual global rice produced amounts to 60 billion tons) accounts for RH production, and the output is increasing each year.81,82 Additionally, SiO2 accounts for ∼20 wt% of RHs.82 Traditionally, rice husk is treated by incineration or landfilling, which occupy large areas of fields, release large amounts of greenhouse gases, and greatly burden natural agricultural environments.83 Moreover, rice absorbs silicic acid from soil and converts it into SiO2 during natural rice growth. Hydrated amorphous SiO2 grows uniformly on the cell wall through natural deposition.76,84Fig. 1e confirms that the natural nanoscale porous structure is well maintained in RH-derived SiO2.85

Bioorganisms such as diatoms and sponges are experts in the use of a variety of cellular and biochemical processes to produce biological and species-specific SiO2 nanostructures that use biopolymers in environmental conditions.86 Single-cell algae diatoms are one of the most prominent creations of bio-fabricated 3D microstructures and have ordered nanopore and nanostructure patterns.87 Diatomaceous earth is mainly composed of ancient remains and its chemical composition mainly includes SiO2, which can be represented as SiO2·nH2O. Similar to that of an RH cell wall, amorphous SiO2 is also a major component of a diatom's cell wall, conferring the cell wall with notable transport, mechanical, and catalytic properties.86,88 A diatom consists of a series of diatom cell membranes with different morphologies. A typical diatom structure is a hexagonal honeycomb structure (Fig. 1f) as well as a slender palisade-like structure.89 The phytoliths formed by SiO2 in these organisms function as a layer that protects against potential biological and abiotic threats. The leaf sheath of bagasse has significantly different morphologies; for example, some phytoliths are bifoliate, while others have porous fiber morphologies (Fig. 1g).90Fig. 1h shows the 3D microstructure of reed leaf-derived SiO2 associated with microtubes (where the diameter is ∼3.0 μm) obtained via simple calcination.43

3. Synthesis of Si and its derivatives derived from biomass

The extraction and recombination of most biomass Si-based materials go through three processes. First, Si oxides are extracted from the selected Si-rich biomass by means of calcining, ball-milling, microwaving, etc. During calcination, the biomass materials can be removed, while the required SiO2 can be retained. Calcination is carried out in a certain atmosphere, which depends on the target product. To obtain single Si or SiOx, the reaction is usually carried out in air or in an O2 atmosphere. Gu et al.95 found that at the same pyrolysis temperature, the purity levels of nano-SiO2 prepared in a CO2/O2 atmosphere were higher than those of the material prepared in an N2/O2 atmosphere. The size of SiO2 NPs increases as the calcination temperature increases. The results show that the particle size is affected by the tendency of the sample to agglomerate, which may occur due to the Brownian motion.96,97 On the one hand, when the temperature used to pyrolyze RHs increases, the carbon content decreases, which improves the purity of SiO2. On the other hand, a very high temperature is not conducive for breaking the Si–C bond; thus, some carbons are retained and accordingly, the proportion of SiO2 NPs is reduced.98 This leads to the collapse of the biomass structure and the dissolution of SiO2, thus affecting the morphology and properties of the resulting material. Therefore, the choice of temperature is of great importance. Furquan et al.99 developed and ameliorated a fast and effective (only five minutes) method to obtain high-purity nano Si powder by microwave heating a mixture of sand and magnesium at 800 °C. Furthermore, a carbon-coated Si NP-electrochemically stripped graphene nanocomposite film was prepared by rapid microwave irradiation.100 Sustainable microwave-assisted synthesis (MAS) has attracted considerable attention for synthesizing Si because it is environmentally friendly, highly efficient, and energy saving.90 High-energy ball milling has been widely used to process synthetic powders because of its advantages of simplicity and high efficiency. Additionally, the surface area and volume of samples are regulated during ball milling.101 The nano/micro-structured Si obtained after ball milling showed a better electrochemical performance.102 For the first time, Kashyout et al.103 achieved all the architectures of a nano-porous Si powder by combining alkaline anisotropic chemical etching and ball milling techniques. Beyond that, the surface coating thickness could be readily manipulated by controlling the milling duration.102

Si is also extracted from SiO2 by means of metal thermal reduction and the metal reductants are superior to carbon as they can prevent Si products from being contaminated by carbon-related impurities at lower temperatures. Low-temperature reduction is beneficial because it is easier to operate and the large surface area of SiO2 particles is retained, making the product responsive to the subsequent leaching treatments. The effectiveness of porous Si depends largely on its surface area, pore diameter, pore volume, crystallinity, and morphology. The thermal reduction of metals has attracted considerable attention because of its ability to synthesize porous Si directly from cheap precursors; Lai et al.104 found that magnesium still exhibited an excellent reduction performance at its melting point and at higher temperatures. On the contrary, Al could only effectively reduce the SiO2 NPs above the melting point and at higher structural temperatures, but the resulting particles retained better morphology than the particles achieved using Mg as the reductant. Calcium cannot reduce SiO2. Due to the excellent conservation of structural features by the metallothermic reduction of frustules,105–107 the synthesis of Si-based materials using this method was investigated.87,108 Magnesium and aluminum are inexpensive, effective, convenient, and efficient for removing the by-products of reduction; thus, these elements are usually chosen as reducing agents.109 After the magnesiothermic reduction of Si, the extraction of Si from the products requires the removal of by-products, including unreduced SiO2, MgO, and Mg2SiO4. Most of the magnesium compounds are removed by HCl; unrefined Mg2SiO4 and unreduced SiO2 can be removed by a mixture of HF and CH3COOH109 and then washed with water or an alkaline solvent to achieve the desired pH.

Finally, Si was doped by hydrothermal and smearing methods to obtain the target product and then, SiO2 could be directly compounded.

3.1 Synthesis of Si derived from biomass

3.1.1 Synthesis of Si derived from biomass. Porous Si is usually prepared by the template method. The internal pores of Si can reserve space for volume expansion and relieve the mechanical stress inside the material. Porous Si materials have micropores, mesopores, macropores, hollow structures, pore-grade pore structures, etc. The type and distribution of pores, porosity, and pore size have a great influence on the material properties.

The preparation of porous Si electrode materials by the magnesiothermic reduction of bio-SiO2 is universal. The magnesiothermic reduction of porous SiO2 is a common method used to prepare porous Si anode materials. Shi et al.110 used RHs as the Si source to prepare porous Si, which maintained the primary intrinsic and unique nanostructure of RHs. The morphologies of the synthesized Si products can vary by changing the ramp rate of magnesiothermic reduction. When the ramp rate decreases from 5 °C min−1 to 1 °C min−1, the obtained Si-JHR-1 material, which has a diameter of approximately 20 nm (Fig. 2b), maintains fairly good structural integrity inherited from SiO2-JHR and has higher purity compared to Si-JHR-5 (Fig. 2a). In addition, as shown in Fig. 2c, the HRTEM image depicts a crystal spacing consistent with the Si (111) plane. The sample size can be controlled by purposefully changing the calcination temperature. Azadeh et al.29 successfully synthesized nanostructured porous Si that was extracted from an RH precursor by means of calcination and a magnesium thermal reaction. Different sized intermediates were synthesized via regulating the temperature of calcination and the yield of Si was up to 90%, which was substantially higher than the yield produced by other SiO2 sources. When the temperature was increased to 900 °C, the surface of the SiO2 particles melted because of the presence of some metal oxides (Fig. 2d). Therefore, an RHA-700-3 material with a diameter of approximately 10 nm was chosen as the next reactant and was prepared at 700 °C (Fig. 2e). In addition to conventional calcination treatments, the microwave-assisted metallothermic reduction (MW-MR) process combines the advantages of the two individual processes; it was used to further promote the development of a 3D bonded porous Si network and obtain high-quality samples with an aperture of 50–80 nm and wall thickness of ∼23 nm. The MW-MR technology has been used by Praneetha et al.90 First, SiO2 was extracted from the biomass waste (e.g., RHs, bagasse, and bamboo stalks) by a rapid microwave technology (within 30 min) and then, a metallothermic reduction process was adopted to prepare Si at 650 °C. Reductive gases were not introduced in the whole reduction process. From the comparison shown in Fig. 2f and g, it is obvious that the commercial Si fragments prepared by laser thermal decomposition do not display porous structures and the size distribution is 100–200 nm. However, SiRH prepared by MW-MR maintains an obvious 3D interconnected uniform porous network structure. Fig. 2h shows the simple process of magnesio-milling used to obtain RH-derived Si with porous morphology (with a pore size of 50–200 nm), as synthesized by Cho et al.111 According to Fig. 2i and j, the size of SiRH synthesized by this process is larger than that obtained from conventional calcination-assisted metallothermic reduction processes, which use relatively high temperatures.

3.1.2 Synthesis of Si/carbon (Si/C) derived from biomass. Obviously, the organic carbon components of natural biomass containing Si are often destroyed in the process of crystallizing Si. Carbon-based materials can be flexible, have regular shapes, and are applicable in energy storage applications; thus, they are favored by researchers in the development and preparation of materials.112–115 Ordered mesoporous carbon has a channel width between 4 and 6 nm, a lower ion-transfer resistance, higher diffusion path (0.5–1 nm), and smaller particle size (1–2 nm) than activated carbon. Therefore, the carbon material obtained from either biomass or chemical sources can function as a modifier, which can be fully utilized in the modification of biomass-derived Si-based materials.116

The combination of Si and carbon can be divided into two parts. In one method, biomass is directly calcined with Si and the carbon elements derived from biomass materials. In the other method, these materials are prepared via coating and other means after successfully extracting Si from the biomass materials. Lin et al.116 reported the synthesis of a porous Si@C composite using bamboo leaves as both the Si and C sources via a simple baking treatment and molten salt strategy (Fig. 3a), which can be categorized into the first abovementioned category. Unlike other Si reactions, for instance, the reactions using high temperatures, the complete process was controlled at 450 °C, and the temperature (200 °C) during the molten AlCl3 reaction was stable enough to avoid the formation of highly stable SiC. Fig. 3b clearly exhibits the porous structure of micro-particles with an average pore size and pore volume of 7.2 nm and 0.065 cm3 g−1, respectively. Campbell et al.89 reported the simple synthesis of nano-silicon (nano-Si) via the efficient magnesiothermic reduction of SiO2-based frustules present in diatomaceous earth (diatomite, DE), and this step was followed by a CVC C-coating step to synthesize C-coated nano-Si; the first step in this process belongs to the second abovementioned category, while the carbon layer modification step belongs to the first category. Note that the larger Si NPs are covered with a well-defined amorphous oxide surface layer, as shown in Fig. 3c. The unique structure of the diatomite appears as a bright spot. Fig. 3d confirms that nano-Si retains the integrity of the frustule-like structure and remains orderly arranged in a hexagonal honeycomb shape with different-sized apertures. As can be seen in Fig. 3e, there is an obvious boundary between the carbon layer (with a thickness of ca. 5 nm) and the Si NP. The hollow structures are famous among researchers for their large surface areas119 and low densities. Additionally, these hollow structures are hard to obtain due to the difficulty in forming a stable interface. Gao et al.117 successfully synthesized Si hollow spheres via constructing a stable interface artificially derived from diatoms. Si nucleates gradually and grows on the carbon shell during reduction, thus forming core/shell nanostructures. Fig. 3f shows the hollow spheres with an Si–C–Si layer shell. Si hollow spheres with multiple layers can be obtained via a similar process (Fig. 3g) and this structure is depicted in Fig. 3h. Liu et al.44 reported the fabrication of a 3D porous Si-based anode derived from reed leaves via the traditional calcination-assisted magnesiothermic reduction process. Fig. 3i shows the folded and undulating microstructures of the calcined reed leaves. The inset shows the existence of abundant holes, and these holes occur because the region from which the biomass is obtained is rich in organisms that release large amounts of gases during calcination and remove the impurities by leaching with HCl. Additionally, Fig. 3j reveals that even the eventually as-prepared black carbon-coated Si retains the original skeleton morphology of the reed leaves. As can be seen in Fig. 3k, the 3D structure consists of 2D ordered nanosheets or nanonets with abundant pores. As shown in Fig. 3l, Wang et al.118 prepared SiO2 by the pyrolysis of dried bamboo leaves using a scalable method and then prepared ultrafine Si NPs (with a size of 5–8 nm) by the magnesiothermic reduction process using NaCl as the heat-clearing agent (to prevent SiO2 from melting and Si NPs from agglomerating at a high temperature); then, Si@C@RGO nanocomposites were prepared by further layer-by-layer assembly. Note that the Si NPs coated with carbon can be uniformly encapsulated in an RGO nanosheet network, as shown in Fig. 3m. Fig. 3n shows the encapsulation of the final product, which further confirms the successful formation of the Si@C/RGO nanocomposites.

3.2 Synthesis of monox (SiOx) derived from biomass

SiO2 was obtained by calcining the biomass in a specific atmosphere (N2, Ar, CO2, etc.) for the first time and then reacted with a reducing agent to form SiOx.

The preparation of this porous nanomaterial does not require a complicated process, which once again highlights the advantages of biomass materials. The images of SiO2 after milling show particles with sizes up to 5 μm, rectangular shapes, and cube-like structures. The milling technology is expected to enhance the active surface area (Fig. 5e).

3.3 Synthesis of silicate derived from biomass

Silicate is a type of compound composed of Si, oxygen, and other elements. Layered alkaline silicate is a kind of hydrated layer silicate composed of silicate layers having different thicknesses and charge-compensated alkaline cationic interlayers.125 Here, we introduced cobalt, nickel, zinc, and manganese silicates, explored the similarities and differences in their syntheses, and compared their morphologies.

As shown in Fig. 6a, Li et al.65 have successfully grown ultrathin Co2Si2O5(OH)4 microporous flakes having 3D architectures on mesoporous carbon frameworks by calcining and hydrothermally treating mesoporous carbon frameworks. In addition, nanoflowers assembled by ultrathin Co3Si2O5(OH)4 nanoflakes were uniformly grown on a 3D porous C–SiO2 substrate (Fig. 6b). The TEM images show that the nanoflakes have layered features. Additionally, the lamellar space between the layers is approximately 1.2 nm (Fig. 6c). Similarly, Wang et al.94 synthesized Ni3Si2O5(OH)4 with a layered structure by a similar calcination-assisted hydrothermal method using bamboo leaves as the precursor, as shown in Fig. 6d. Although both Co3Si2O5(OH)4 and Ni3Si2O5(OH)4 exhibit layered structures, Ni3Si2O5(OH)4 grows orderly on the base without clustering into flowers, and the thickness of the layers is larger than that of the Co3Si2O5(OH)4 layers (Fig. 6e). The TEM images show that C–NiSi-3 (where the Ni/Si ratio is 0.5/1) is composed of a hierarchical porous structure inherited from C–SiO2, in which the Ni3Si2O5(OH)4 layers uniformly grow on it (Fig. 6f). The length and width of the layers are about 150–250 nm and 130–230 nm, respectively. Zhang et al.75 explored the effect of the ratio of Zn to Si in the precursor on the species and morphology of the product by tuning the Zn/Si ratio. The results showed that no zinc silicate was found using a low Zn/Si ratio (Zn/Si = 0.5/1), while other metal zinc silicates and oxides were formed at higher Zn/Si ratios (Zn/Si = 3/1, 5/1). The peaks were in accordance with those of Zn4Si2O7(OH)2·H2O. The morphology changed from a small part of the flower-like structure to nanoparticles as the zinc ratio increased, and it was clear that Zn4Si2O7(OH)2·H2O NPs grew firmly and evenly on 3D porous C–SiO2 (Fig. 6g and h). Similarly, an Mn/Si ratio was selected by Wang et al.126 to synthesize zinc silicate-doped carbon (MnSi–C) derived from bamboo leaves. As shown in Fig. 6i, MnSiOx NPs are uniformly distributed on the substrate, and the size ranges from 100 to 200 nm. Furthermore, no obvious boundary appears between MnSiOx NPs and the substrate because of the evenly distributed Si element in the bamboo leaves (Fig. 6j).

3.4 Summary

Biomass materials have unique structures that are hard to replicate. These structures generally have multiple layers, porous structures, and large active surface areas. At the same time, these structures maintain stability and show excellent high temperature resistance. In addition, carbon, nitrogen, and other elements are abundant in biomass. The intricate and well-bedded nano-SiO2 frameworks were fabricated using a biosilicification process;123 the fabrication process is economical and environmentally friendly, while the cost to commercially prepare nanomaterials is high.128

4. Applications

The electrochemical energy storage technology refers to the energy conversion technology, which stores energy in the form of chemical energy and appropriately converts it to electrical energy. The most common electrochemical energy storage systems are lead–acid batteries, LIBs, and SCs.

The transformation of different forms of energy has always been an important part of science and technology. Electrochemical energy storage devices convert chemical energy to electrical energy. Scientists are looking for renewable resources with improved energy efficiency, for instance, solar, hydro, wind, and tidal energy to meet both economic and environmental needs. However, because renewable energy relies heavily on natural conditions and lacks stability, it is particularly important to develop energy storage systems that can store these renewable resources. Among different energy storage devices, the advantages of high efficiency, diversity, and flexibility make electrochemical energy storage devices one of the most promising devices at present.128 Secondary batteries and SCs can directly and reversibly interconvert chemical energy and electrical energy. When the electrochemical potentials of two materials are different, the electrochemical energy storage device composed of the two materials can be constructed theoretically.

4.1 Biomass Si-based materials for lithium-ion batteries

With high energy density, long cycle life, and no memory effect, LIBs have won a space in the field of portable electronic equipment and power tools and have gradually expanded to the fields of electric bicycles, electric cars, and new energy storage systems.129

It is difficult for single-phase motor materials and even nanoscale electrode materials to simultaneously achieve high capacity, long cycle life, and good rate performance. Therefore, many researchers have turned their attention to two-phase or multi-phase core–shell structured electrode materials. The existence of a carbon layer can not only improve the conductivity but also prevent agglomeration, thus improving the electrochemical stability; however, this carbon layer can also minimize the volume expansion of the internal active material.

Traditionally, elemental Si is often used in metallurgy and the semiconductor industry.76 The poor cycling stability of bulk Si is a universal problem, which hinders the practical application of Si as the electrode material in LIBs. In view of this drawback, some papers have proposed the idea of transforming bulk Si into nano-Si,130 which can enhance the satisfactory damage tolerance of bulk Si during the charging and discharging processes.131 Due to the shortening of the diffusion path, materials with large surface areas exhibit highly efficient charging and discharging rates, and the polarization phenomenon can also be significantly improved.132–135 In addition, NPs can withstand rapid relaxation and therefore are more resistant to fracture. Nonetheless, this also produces some negative effects. For example, it promotes the formation of a passivation layer and SEI layer on the electrode surface, which causes an increase in capacity; thus, some kind of compromise needs to be found.130,131 A study on the misfit stress energy of some de-lithiated particles showed that the 10 nm particles exhibited an excellent cycling performance due to their good fracture resistance.136,137 The nanocrystallization of biomass-derived Si-based materials is easy and requires low amounts of energy, and the nano-scale morphology inherits the fine and unique structure found in the natural precursor in contrast to that of traditional Si-based materials.

As a typical 2D material, graphene is mainly composed of single-layer carbon atoms, and it can carry objects much heavier than its own weight. The theoretical surface area of graphene is large enough to reach 2630 m2 g−1. In addition, graphene also has good electrical conductivity, which can reach 106 S m−1. It is precisely because of these properties that graphene shows a great potential for its use in preparing composite electrode materials.156 Kim et al.141 reported the influence of the ratio of c-SiRH to graphene on the electrochemical properties. As shown in Fig. 10c, graphene has a smooth surface and uniform size (∼15 μm). Fig. 10d and e show that the specific capacity increases as the c-SiRH content increases, while the capacity retention shows the opposite trend. It can be inferred that SiRH contributes to the specific capacity and graphene improves the stability.

Reduced graphene oxide (RGO) has a single atomic layer and is prepared by the chemical oxidation of graphene. RGO contains more oxygen-containing functional groups and is therefore more active than graphene; thus, RGO has extensive applications in materials science.157–159 RGO was introduced by Wang et al.118 to remedy the unsatisfactory rate capacity of bamboo leaf-derived ultrafine Si NPs. Clearly, the capacity of Si@C/RGO decreased on increasing the current density, but it was always higher than that of the bamboo leaf-derived ultrafine Si NPs. Furthermore, the reversible capacity reached the initial value after 60 cycles, thus showing satisfactory reversibility and rate capability, which can be assigned to the existence of RGO (Fig. 10f).

Yu et al.142 explored the electrochemical effects of introducing activators and RGO into Si-based materials derived from RHs. First, the introduction of ZnCl2 produced massive pores, thus effectively mitigating volume expansion (Fig. 10g). Although RH-P–Si/C exhibited large initial capacity, the capacity retention rate was relatively low due to the introduction of an unstable pore structure in the activation step (Fig. 10h). Second, the existence of RGO reduced Sbet and Vpore and increased the pore size of RH-Si/C/RGO. RH-P–Si/C/RGO showed improved initial capacity and more stable cycle performance compared to RH-P–Si/C.

Metal oxide materials have good discharge activities and their theoretical discharge capacities are 2–3 times that of commercial carbon materials; therefore, metal oxides have high research significance.108 Poor conductivity seriously affects the rate performance of Si-based materials. The introduction of appropriate but less active metal oxides can effectively prevent the formation of SEI films, improve the conductivity of materials, and reduce the volume change of active materials, thus ensuring the structural stability of the electrode materials to some extent.160,161 Al2O3 was used as a reductant, and the formation of an Al2O3 layer could make an Si-based electrode adapt to large volume changes. Fig. 10i shows that the carbon-coated pure Si is subjected to severe volume expansion (up to 150%). Fig. 10j shows the SEM image of Si–Al-7-3 after 100 cycles, which distinctively exhibits that the original cylindrical structure containing mesopores is maintained well in Si–Al-7-3 (Si/Al2O3 contains 30 wt% Al2O3). The volume expansion improved on increasing the Al2O3 content in the Si/Al2O3 composites. Furthermore, both Si–Al-7-3 and Si–Al-5-5 showed excellent cycling performances because Al2O3 functions as a buffer layer when the volume changes; in addition, the excellent mechanical properties of Al2O3 are favorable for preserving the integrity of the structure (Fig. 10k). C/SiOx with SiOx NPs that were evenly distributed in a carbon matrix, synthesized by Cui et al.,122 exhibited excellent initial specific capacity (3096.6 mA h g−1) and maintained the capacity of 1230 mA h g−1 after 200 cycles at 100 mA g−1. Al@PCRH showed enhanced cycling stability (1390 mA h g−1 after 260 cycles at 100 mA g−1) when SiOx was simultaneously coated with carbon and 3 nm amorphous Al2O3 layers, and both the materials exhibited excellent rate performances.

4.2 Biomass Si-based materials for supercapacitors (SCs)

Compared with the traditional electrochemical batteries, SCs can be discharged and charged indefinitely without memory effects. At the same time, SCs also have obvious drawbacks, which include low energy density.162,163 The energy density of high-end and commercially available SCs is only approximately 1/25th of a lithium battery. Compared with a traditional capacitor, SCs have the following two advantages: (1) the charging and discharging distance is small, usually at the nanometre level; thus, it shows high specific capacitance. (2) The electrode material can be made up of a 3D material with a high specific surface area, which is advantageous for large capacitance.

Ultrathin flakes are potential materials for preparing supercapacitor electrodes from the perspective of specific capacitance and cycle life.65 Interestingly, in recent years, biomass derivatives have appeared as potential renewable energy materials as precursors for the electrode materials.164,165 Moreover, the sieving effects of the electrolyte ions and capacitances show that the existence of a mesoporous structure is beneficial for the electrochemical performance, while the biomass itself has a natural multi-layer porous structure.166 There are many types of composite materials containing metal elements and SiO2,167 and they are widely used.168–172 For example, Bai et al.173 reported the fabrication of manganese (Mn) SiO2 drapes and further tested the electrochemical properties of these materials to explore their potential for use as energy storage materials. In addition, biomass is rich in a variety of elements, such as nitrogen and sulfur, and co-doped Si–C composites tend to have better capacitance due to possible Faraday induction. Transition metal elements are often used to improve the electrochemical properties.181–185 Transition metal oxide-based pseudo-capacitors exhibit higher specific capacitance because it is easier for these materials to provide various oxidation states in the effective redox charge transfer than carbon materials and conductive polymer materials. MnO2, which has high theoretical capacitance of ∼1370 F g−1, is a potential candidate for use in SCs owing to its satisfactory pseudo-capacitance. Zhang et al.175 showed the excellent electrochemical properties of hierarchical MnO2-modified diatomite synthesized by a one-pot method: the charge curve tended to be linear and to a certain extent was mirror symmetrical with the discharge curve. In addition, the hierarchical MnO2-modified diatomite exhibited an excellent rate performance. The initial capacity still remained 65% even at high current density. Similarly, Le et al.87 synthesized two types of Si-based manganese dioxide composites derived from diatoms with different electrochemical properties. As shown in Fig. 11b, the Si diatom@MnO2 nanosheets, with longer discharge times, have better electrochemical properties than the nanorods. This can be explained as follows: during the calcination of the nanosheets, the nanosheets fall off and aggregate, resulting in nanorods with a smaller specific surface area; the δ-MnO2 nanosheets are more widely spaced than the α-MnO2 nanorods.

Biomass silicates are usually synthesized via the hydrothermal decomposition of calcined bio-silicates and their respective metal salts, while silicates are mostly grown on porous biomass materials in the form of flakes, which protrude due to a larger specific surface area.

Layered cobalt and nickel compounds, such as Ni3Si2O5(OH)4, Co3O4 and NiO, have remarkable advantages, which include their fine redox reversibility and high activity; thus, they have gained significant attention in the field of pseudo-capacitors. Zhao et al.43 synthesized three-dimensional mesoporous Ni3Si2O5(OH)4 and Co3Si2O5(OH)4 derived from reeds. The diameters of the constituent tube-like structures were 3.1 μm and 4.5 μm, while the thicknesses of the tube wall were 300 nm and 320 nm. Furthermore, the porous structure shown in Fig. 11c provides the materials with a large specific surface area, which is propitious for ion diffusion and electron transport. P1 exhibits a smaller size and thinner tube thickness, leading to faster ion diffusion and shorter diffusion times compared to P2, thus showing excellent rate capability (Fig. 11d). Fig. 11e shows the electronic transport within the electrode and clearly shows that the resistance is due to the interface and the inherent low electronic conductivities of the active materials. Carbon coating is an effective measure to shorten the transmission path and reduce the resistance, and the nanoflakes of Ni3Si2O5(OH)4 (P1) can directly contact the carbon conductive agent, thus significantly reducing the interfacial resistance (Fig. 11f). It is well-known that heteroatom-doped carbon materials exhibit improved charge transfer rates due to synergistic effects compared with the Si-based materials single doped with elemental carbon.186,187 Zhang et al.126 proposed two mechanisms for the formation of manganese silicate, and the second mechanism was verified in their research (Fig. 11g). Although manganese silicate exhibits poor conductivity, the SC property of MnSi–C is better than that of SiO2–C, which can be mainly assigned to the fact that the SiO2–C substrate overcomes the problem of the poor conductivity of MnSi–C. Besides, the porous hierarchical structure of bamboo leaves is more conducive for the diffusion of electrolytes, and the formation of manganese silicate clusters effectively improves the specific capacitance (Fig. 11h).

4.3 Other applications

Solid polymer electrolytes, as an indispensable part, have attracted increasing attention in electrochemical energy storage. A PEO (polyethylene oxide)-based polymer electrolyte, which easily forms complexes with lithium salts, is conducive for high carrier mobility and good stability of chemical properties. Hence, it was chosen as the polymer electrolyte membrane by Dissanayake et al.188 There are two techniques to improve ionic conductivity. One is the addition of plasticizers with low molecular weights to form plasticized polymer electrolytes; the other is the introduction of dispersed nano-sized inorganic ceramic filler particles into polymer dielectrics to obtain nano-composite solid polymer electrolytes,189 which was adopted here. When SiO2 is used as the filler to prepare an electrolyte, it also provides an improved electrochemical performance at room temperature so that the ionic conductivity of the electrolyte increases by about 12 times. Furthermore, the enhancement in conductivity can mainly be attributed to the ions in the polymer electrolyte and the O2− and OH− groups on the surface of fillers interact with Li+ and provide abundant active sites for Li+ migration through transient H bonding. Dissanayake also discussed the influence of the amount of nano-fillers and found that due to the existence of the nano-fillers, the conductivity significantly improved; the samples with 10 wt% nano-SiO2 filler performed the best among the three samples (5%, 10%, and 15%). The ionic conductivity of the PEO-based polymer electrolytes improved by incorporating nano-inert fillers190,191 such as the industrial grade powders of Al2O3, TiO2, and SiO2. Due to the addition of the RHA nano-SiO2 filler, the conductivity exhibited an enhancement from 9.77 × 10−6 to 1.24 × 10−4 S cm−1 at 30 °C, which compared favourably in performance with the commercial nano-SiO2 filler; therefore, it can be considered as a substitute for several commercial nano-inert fillers.

4.4 Summary

Fig. 12a shows a variety of the abovementioned morphologies, which exert a tremendous impact on the electrochemical performance of materials. 1D nanomaterials have a direct current channel because of their singular structure; thus, the ion diffusion path is significantly reduced as the charging and discharging time is effectively shortened. In addition, this simple structure effectively limits mechanical degradation and alleviates volume expansion.192,193 Nanotubes and nanorods are common 1D materials.194–197 The hollow structure of the nanotubes endows these materials with considerable specific surface areas and excellent electrochemical performances.198,199 In addition, for highly conductive CNTs, it is easy to form a 3D staggered network, thus providing enough ion channels to facilitate the effective transport of active particles.200 For example, CNTs were introduced to construct 3D cross-linked networks with Si nanospheres, and these composites exhibited an enhanced electrochemical performance.80 1D materials can be obtained by the transformation of 2D materials. For instance, the Si-diatom@MnO2 nanorods were obtained from Si-diatom@MnO2via annealing in air and the same substances with different morphologies demonstrated differences in their electrochemical performances.87

Nanosheet structures have the advantages of having high mechanical flexibility and large specific surface area, which significantly increases the number of active sites.201–204 2D nanosheet materials are chemically or physically connected to form a 3D layered structure. This unique 3D structure combines the advantages of improved mechanical properties, fast ion diffusion, and fast charge transfer in channels.205 Additionally, the thickness of the sheet also affects the properties of the material. Electrons are constrained in ultrathin nanosheets, resulting in the materials having remarkable electronic properties and large specific surface areas due to their lateral sizes; these properties are extremely attractive for applications in SCs.206,207 The layered structure, which is attributed to silicates, can be seen in Fig. 6. Nanospheres have regular morphologies and excellent mechanical properties.208–211 The size of the SNCC spheres is much larger than that of the Si/C/Si nanospheres. This size difference occurs because the SNCC spheres (3.2 ± 0.8 μm) consist of RH-derived Si NPs (∼50 nm) intertwined with CNTs,212 while the Si/C/Si nanospheres are directly reduced from diatom-derived SiO2 nanospheres via an etching-reduction/nucleation-growth process.117 Additionally, the hollow structure is at the risk of structural collapse though it simultaneously provides a larger specific surface area. The carbon layer is introduced for protection to maintain the structural integrity.117 The effects of the calcination temperature and heating rate on the morphology of Si NPs were studied by Liu et al.76 and Shi et al.110 The results indicate that the specific surface area and pore volume of the RH-derived Si NPs decrease with the increase in the heating rate. In particular, Cho et al.111 further evaluated the effect of reaction volume on the feasibility of reducing SiO2, and it was obvious that the attrition-milling process with a volume of 10 L increased the specific surface area and pore volume. In Fig. 12b, the balance clearly describes the performance contribution of Si and SiOx in an electric energy storage reaction. Simply put, Si contributes to specific capacitance and SiOx improves stability.213–216 Aiming at the defect of the capacity attenuation of Si-based materials during the charge and discharge processes, several methods have been adopted and these methods include the nano-crystallization of Si-based materials (as the synthesis of biomass-derived nanomaterials is economical and convenient60,201,217,218), coating with a carbon layer, compounding with other substances (e.g., metal oxides, graphene, etc.), and converting the Si-based materials into silicates. In addition, adjusting the contents of Si and SiOx can alleviate the mechanical strain because SiOx contains relatively small amounts of active Si, and oxygen-containing compounds are formed during the discharge function as the buffer volume expands; this has been verified by Cho et al.111

Fig. 12c briefly describes the relationship between the Li+ transport efficiency and the Si NP size. Si NPs with smaller sizes form a thinner Si wall, which shortens the path travelled by Li+ during the process of insertion and de-insertion, thus improving the ion transport efficiency. For instance, Xing et al.123 obtained SiO2 NPs with uniform sizes of ∼25 nm via HCl leaching, while the size of the SiO2 NPs obtained via water rinsing was ten times larger.

As shown in Fig. 12d, porous morphology appears in the activated samples. ZnCl2 is often used as an activator for raw biomass materials for the following reasons: (1) ZnCl2 can swell and dissolve cellulose in the biomass materials, thus penetrating into the biomass and forming the pores.142 (2) At high temperatures, ZnCl2 promotes material dehydration without affecting the carbon in the material, thereby increasing the production of activated carbon.143,219 (3) Even at high temperatures, ZnCl2 does not react with SiO2. Additionally, the formation of voids is due to the removal of the activator after activation, which occupies space during the previous activation.220 It is also indicated that the formation of abundant pores via activation can effectively alleviate the volume expansion of Si. In addition, RH-P–C/SiO2 obtained after activation shows rougher and wrinkled surfaces.142

Generally speaking, four factors, namely, morphology, performance contribution of Si and SiOx in the electrode materials, particle size and transmission distance, and activation process have been proposed, which are also the key factors to improve the performance of Si-based materials in the field of electrochemical energy storage.

5. Conclusion and prospects

To summarize, this review is devoted to studying the process of extracting Si from biomass and remedying the defects in the electrochemical properties of biomass-derived Si-based materials by means of morphology control, doping and compounding with other materials. It is clear that biomass-derived Si-based materials have aroused wide interest in research, showing their irreplaceable status and great potential in the field of electrochemical energy storage.

Biomass materials as Si sources have the following advantages: (1) Biomass wastes such as RHs and bamboo leaves are transformed into electrical energy storage materials, which are economically green. While alleviating the pollution by agricultural wastes, the pressure of energy crisis is effectively reduced. (2) The natural and precise 3D hierarchical structure of biomass can be accurately reproduced by nature and partially retained during heat treatment. This unique structure not only adapts to the volume change of Si-based materials during charging and discharging, but also maintains the stability of the structure. These are conducive to form a stable SEI film, provide rich and coherent natural ion transport channels, and provide substrates for metal oxides, etc. to improve the capacity performance.44,221,222 (3) Biomass materials are usually rich in oxygen-containing groups such as OH− and O2−, thus providing abundant active sites. Biomass materials can also be used as carbon sources to synthesize Si–C composites in one step. Besides, nitrogen and sulphur are ubiquitous in biomass materials; thus, heteroatom-doped Si-based materials can be prepared in a straightforward manner without additional doping, which can improve the conductivity of the materials.223–227 (4) Compared with the traditional preparation technology of nanomaterials, the synthesis of biomass-derived nanomaterials is more attractive due to its simplicity, economy, and environmental protection.130,131

Nonetheless, there are still some problems that urgently need to be solved for the synthesis and application of biomass-derived materials. For example, the method of introducing heteroatoms in one step is simple and economical, but the content of heteroatoms is low. To increase the content of the target heteroatoms, the use of toxic chemical reagents is inevitable in processes such as amination and vulcanization. Second, the integrity of the three-dimensional fine structure of biomass is damaged in the calcination process. Moreover, known research is limited in practical applications. In this regard, the following improvement methods and related prospects should be considered: (1) Developing innovative biomass culture technology and purposefully introducing heteroatoms at the biomass growth stage without affecting the normal survival of the biomass to achieve a low content of heteroatoms as well as to use reagents that are less toxic than those used in the traditional methods. (2) To buffer the volume change of Si-based materials and the maintenance of structural integrity of the biomass materials, the reinforcement technology should be used reasonably at various stages (even at the biomass growth stage) to improve the overall structure of the biomass and to enhance its flexibility and stability. (3) Si present in biomass should be used either to extract SiO2 for further treatment or to functionalize it on the basis of retaining the biomass structure and other existing elements. Targeted and directional extraction technologies should be developed to improve the efficiency of materials. For example, in view of the growth of silicates on SiO2 substrates, theoretical models should be established to find the most suitable biomass materials for substrates and to improve and reinforce their structures. (4) Aimed at the limitation of practical applications, the development of an economical and green method should be carried out to put the extraction and energy storage of biomass Si-based materials in practical production and realize the industrialization and commercialization for these materials.

As renewable resources, biomass-derived Si-based materials are a major breakthrough to improve energy waste and shortages. Although the commercialization of biomass Si-based materials is facing many challenges, the current achievements are encouraging. Therefore, it is an irresistible trend to extract Si from biomass and use it for electrochemical energy storage. In addition, the use of biomass-derived materials should not be limited to electrical energy storage but can also be extended to electrocatalysis, biofuels, etc. The comprehensive utilization of biomass materials is more conducive for exploring the use value of renewable energy. In the process of synthesis, the advantages of biomass materials should be fully taken into account so that their utility is more valuable.
