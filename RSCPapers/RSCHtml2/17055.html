Abstract

The introduction of organic functionalities into porous inorganic materials not only makes the resulting hybrid porous framework to be more flexible and hydrophobic, but also provides additional scope for anchoring metal binding sites, which is beneficial for different frontline applications. Furthermore, the nanoscale porosity and high surface area of these organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials offer a better dispersion of active sites, which greatly enhances their application potential in adsorption, sensing, drug-delivery, energy storage, optoelectronics, light harvesting and catalysis. Easy post-synthetic functionalization of these hybrid materials has widened their application potential. Herein, we highlight several important synthetic strategies to design a wide range of organicâ€“inorganic hybrid porous materials starting from the respective molecular precursors and their task-specific applications in energy and environmental research. We also outline the recent developments in their respective application areas together with various challenges that need to be overcome.

1. Introduction

Over the past few decades, the fabrication of new ordered porous materials has significantly increased in both the scientific and technological domains. The ability of porous materials to interact with other ions, atoms and molecules not only through their surfaces, but also throughout their bulk, makes them stand out compared to other materials. Traditionally, this class of materials has been employed in selective adsorption,1 catalysis2 and ion exchange.3 However, the application of this fast-growing research field has been considerably extended in areas ranging from medical diagnosis to energy storage.4,5 Based on the dimensions of their pores, porous materials are classified by IUPAC as: microporous: pore size below 2.0 nm, mesoporous: between 2.0 to 50 nm and macroporous: pore dimensions above 50 nm. The performance of a porous material towards a particular application depends directly on its shape, size and void space. Over the years, the demand for monodispersity of the pore size and shape and high void volume has enabled researchers to enhance the application potential of these materials significantly.

Several emerging porous hybrid organicâ€“inorganic materials, such as organo-silicates,6 periodic mesoporous organosilicas (PMOs),7 metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs),8 metal-organophosphonate frameworks and metalâ€“organic polymer composites9,10 allow precise control of their functional moieties through detailed design of their structures at the molecular level. The hallmark of these materials is the presence of a â€œmolecular scaffoldâ€ possessing both inorganic and organic moieties, which is far more extensive than that in other porous materials.11,12

The introduction of organic functionalities in polyoxometalates (POMs),13 which consist of early transition metal polyhedra of different heteroatoms (V, Nb, Ta, Mo, W, etc.) results in a rich family of organicâ€“inorganic hybrids. These hybrid POMs have found utility in various applications such as acid/oxidation catalysis, photocatalysis, and adsorptive removal of pollutants from water. The removal of the highly toxic hexavalent chromium from aqueous contaminants using suitable porous adsorbents is very challenging from an environmental perspective. Zr-MOF functionalized with alkyl thiol groups was successfully utilized for the reductive removal of toxic Cr(VI)14 by virtue of the formation of a Cr(VI)â€“thiolate complex on the surface of the hybrid framework. Generally, hybrid nanostructures of POM-based sulfonated ionic liquids with a carboxylate MOF exhibited excellent catalytic activity for the synthesis of biofuels via esterification/transesterification reactions.15 By virtue of the fluorescence property associated with organic ligands, MOFs facilitate the energy transfer process, thereby acting as efficient photocatalysts for several organic transformations (oxidation, reduction and Câ€“C coupling reactions).16 Ti-MOF when coupled with an inorganic nanomaterial to form the NH2-MIL-125(Ti)/BiOCl composite, it showed high photocatalytic activity for the decomposition of organic dyes.17

Another fascinating area where these hybrid frameworks have found huge potential is asymmetric catalysis. Through surface functionalization, chiral metal complexes can be anchored over organically functionalized mesoporous silica and the resulting material can be successfully utilized in the enantioselective Friedelâ€“Crafts hydroxyalkylation reaction.18 One of the unique classes of hybrid porous nanomaterials is chiral PMOs (ChiMO),19 where one can achieve high enantioselectivity in catalytic transformations. Here, the bridging organosilane precursor bearing a chiral metal salen complex can be prepared via the Schiff base condensation of aromatic aldehydes with chiral amines followed by complexation with a metal salt, which acts as the chiral centre for carrying out the asymmetric catalysis under liquid-phase reaction conditions.

Chen et al. developed another organicâ€“inorganic hybrid system, where cobalt phosphide nanoparticles were uniformly distributed in N-doped carbon, and the resulting CoP/NMC20 material showed high efficiency in a dye sensitized solar cell. Here, melamine and resorcinol condensed with formaldehyde in the presence of triblock copolymer F127 under hydrothermal conditions together with cobalt phosphate, resulting in the formation of the CoP/NMC nanocomposite under carbothermal reduction. The carbonization of mesoporous nanocomposites is considered one of the versatile strategies for the synthesis of highly reactive nanoparticles stabilized within high surface area carbon-based materials and these heterojunction nanostructures have been intensively studied in several energy conversation processes.21â€“23 The immobilization of VPO4 nanoparticles over hierarchically porous carbon via the hydrogel-mediated synthesis route resulted in high storage capacity for Li+ and Na+.24 Among the hybrid systems, Zhang et al. threaded polyethylene glycol along the nanochannels of crystalline chalcogenide [Sn3Se7]n2nâˆ’ layers, where this type of hybrid nanocomposite is in high demand in photoelectrochemical applications.25 Alternatively, Cu(II) can be immobilized at the donor sites of the imine and Î²-ketoenamine moieties of 2D hexagonal COFs, efficiently catalysing the [2+3] dipolar cycloaddition of different alkynes and azides for the synthesis of value-added 1,2,3-triazoles.26 Organically pillared layered Ti(IV)/Zr(IV) phosphonates27 are another class of organicâ€“inorganic hybrids, which have found several useful applications in adsorption and catalysis. Zirconium phosphonate synthesized with the naturally occurring phosphonate precursor phytic acid, bearing multiple phosphates has been successfully employed as a heterogeneous catalyst for the Meerweinâ€“Ponndorfâ€“Verley reduction of biomass-derived levulinic acid or its ester into Î³-valerolactone biofuel.28 Pillaring of the layered metal phosphates using different spacer organic moieties resulted in not only efficient cross-linking, leading to framework stability, but also induced nanoscale porosity, hydrophobicity and chemical functionalization at the pore surface.29 Pillaring of the rigid azo dye 4-((5-chloro-4-methyl-2-sulfophenyl)azo)-3-hydroxy-2-naphthalene-carboxylic acid could enhance the interlayer spacing to as high as 0.86 to 1.72 nm in the case of an Mgâ€“Al-layered double hydroxide (LDH).30 Thus, the choice of organics in the resulting organicâ€“inorganic hybrid material is crucial in directing its chemical and physical properties. In the following sections, we provide a brief account of the synthesis of some of the major classes of organicâ€“inorganic hybrid porous nanomaterials and their frontline applications in the energy, environment and catalysis fields.

2. Synthesis

2.1. Synthesis of organically functionalized mesoporous silicas

Organic functionalization over porous inorganic silica-based materials endows organicâ€“inorganic hybrid molecular sieves with enhanced porosity and multifaceted properties. This class of advanced materials has received a significant amount of attention over the past decade owing to their high specific surface area, narrow pore size distribution, large pore volume and high structural stability together with long-range ordering. The typical synthesis of mesoporous silica materials necessitates â€œsoft-templatingâ€ with structure-directing agents (SDAs) such as cationic/anionic surfactants or amphipathic polymers. A silica precursor such as tetramethyl orthosilicate (TMOS), tetraethyl orthosilicate (TEOS) or sodium silicate is stirred with the SDAs at the desired pH and temperature. The self-assembly of these SDAs results in the formation of surfactant micelles. The silica precursors hydrolyse and condense around the ordered assembly of surfactant micelle templates, resulting in the formation of well-ordered mesostructures. Upon the removal of the templates by either calcination or solvent extraction, a mesoporous silica material with well-organized uniform pore channels are obtained. This pore structure and pore size can be controlled by altering the nature of the SDAs, by varying the nature and amount of silica precursors, pH of the medium, synthesis time and temperature. Organic functionalization of these porous silica frameworks not only endows them with surface polarity/hydrophobicity but also incorporates various functionalities such as acidic, basic, chiral, redox, biomolecules, and drugs, either inside the pore channels or within the pore walls, giving rise to advanced properties in the resulting porous materials. From the perspective of materials scientists, the immobilization of organic moieties on silica surfaces can be performed via two different ways including (i) post-synthesis or grafting (ex situ functionalization) and (ii) co-condensation or direct incorporation (in situ functionalization), as illustrated in Scheme 1.

2.1.1. Post-synthesis or grafting (ex situ functionalization). The post-synthesis functionalization process is a two-step process, which requires the availability of free and geminal silanol groups to react with organosilanes containing different functional groups. This method is relatively straightforward, and it enhances the possibility of selectively grafting organic groups on the external surface, pore surface or at the pore entrance. Over the years, post-modification with various organic groups including amino, thiol, alkyl, and aryl moieties has been extensively studied. The grafting technique is beneficial to enhance the quality of post-synthetic modification. Immobilization of organic moieties in the mesoporous silica framework can be accomplished with or without solvents. Bourkaib et al. functionalized a silica support (SBA-15 and MMS) with (3-aminopropyl)triethoxysilane (APTES) by suspending the silica support in dry toluene, followed by the addition of the requisite amount of APTES under reflux conditions.31 In a water-free environment, APTES reacts only with the surface SiOH groups, forming covalent Siâ€“Oâ€“Si bonds, without undergoing self-polymerization. This technique is known as the â€œsolvent approachâ€. Alternatively, a â€œsolvent-freeâ€ approach was introduced by Jaroniec et al., where â€œas-synthesizedâ€ mesostructured silica containing SDA undergoes template displacement by organosilanes (TDS process) through electrostatic interaction between an anionic organosilane and cationic surfactant, resulting in the formation of various organosilane-functionalised mesoporous materials via a one-step procedure.32 In 2009, Gartmann and BrÃ¼hwiler not only demonstrated that the type of organosilane employed for functionalization strongly determines the degree of pore and/or external surface grafting but also visualized the role of the solvent on the uniform distribution of functional groups.33 The superiority of 3-aminopropyltris(methoxyethoxyethoxy)silane (APTMEES) over the frequently used APTES for the external surface functionalization of mesoporous silicas without blocking the mesopores was attributed to the lower mobility and ability to penetrate the SDA-filled channels of the as-synthesized materials. In 2000, RÃ¶ser and co-workers extensively studied the surface morphology of MCM-41 with varied pore sizes (2.8 and 3.8 nm), grafted with disilazane reagents of type HN(SiR1R22)2, constituting organic functionalities R1,2 = H, Me, Ph, n-Bu, n-Oct, and vinyl of different longitudinal and lateral extensions.34 They showed that the surface hydroxyl consumption and silylation efficiency depend on the steric bulk/shape of the R group, given that tetramethyldisilazane showed the highest surface coverage and was less sterically hindered compared to the generally used standard hexamethyldisilazane. The selective adsorption of low-concentrated nonylphenol, an endocrine disrupter, on hexagonal mesoporous silica (MPS) grafted with octylsilane (C8-MPS) and dodecylsilane (C12-MPS) was investigated by Inumaru et al.35 The introduction of hydrophobic moieties upon grafting in the inorganic nanospaces proved to be an effective blueprint towards designing molecular selective adsorbents.

Recapitulating the grafting methodology, its superiority lies in producing hybrid frameworks with more protruding functional groups and well-defined pore sizes. However, the inability to produce a homogeneous distribution of functional moieties throughout the pore channels calls for other suitable approaches (such as co-condensation and direct synthesis). The poor distribution of organic functionalities in the porous matrix can be attributed to the calcination of the as-synthesized material, resulting in the lower availability of silanol groups on the surface, and thus decreased ability to undergo condensation with organosilanes. The possibility of pore blockage after functionalization is also another limitation. Hence, extensive research has been carried out to obtain better textural parameters for porous organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials than that synthesized by post-synthesis.

2.1.2. Co-condensation (in situ functionalization). The co-condensation or one-pot synthetic approach involves the functionalization of silica materials by the addition of organosilanes together with silica precursors, resulting in the co-assembly of the organic and inorganic precursors together with SDAs in one pot. This method introduces organic units homogeneously into the porous nanostructures compared to the ex situ technique. This in situ approach of synthesising hybrid porous nanostructures results in the insertion (fossilization) of organic moieties on the internal surface of the mesoporous silica framework without any possibility of pore blockage. However, with an increase in the amount of organosilanes in the initial gel, the crystallinity and the textural parameters of the product decreases. The extent of self-condensation of the functional organosilanes over cross-linking via co-condensation with the silica precursors results in a poor concentration of surface-exposed functionalities compared to the organic functionalities initially used in the synthetic gel composition. In 2003, Lin et al. developed a synthetic strategy to control the multifunctionalization and morphology of hybrid MCM-41-like materials by varying the molar ratio of the organoalkoxysilane precursors.44 Monofunctionalization of mesoporous silicas with 3-[2-(aminoethylamino)ethylamino]propyl-trimethoxysilane (AEPTMS) or 3-cyanopropyl-triethoxysilane (CPTES) was carried out via the base-catalyzed co-condensation route. The authors also synthesised multifunctional mesoporous silicas by co-condensing tetraethoxysilane (TEOS) with AEPTMS and CPTES in the same reaction mixture. It was observed that there was a change in the morphology of the functionalized mesoporous silicas upon changing the molar ratio of the two organosilanes. A change in the shape of the functionalized mesoporous silicas from rod- to sphere-shaped was observed as the relative amount of AEPTMS over CPTES increased. Moreover, the hydrophilic nature of AEPTMS possibly elevated the pore size of the mesoporous materials upon increasing its composition in the reaction mixture. Based on the heat of transfer of different salts from water to 0.1 M cationic cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) solution, an anionic lyotropic series (citrate < CO32âˆ’ < SO42âˆ’ < CH3CO2âˆ’ < Fâˆ’ < OHâˆ’ < HCO2âˆ’ < Clâˆ’ < NO3âˆ’ < Brâˆ’ < CH3C6H4SO3âˆ’) was reported by Larsen and Magid,45 which inspired this group to investigate the various electrostatic matching effects of different anionic organoalkoxysilanes with that of CTAB, which govern the extent of organic functionalization on the pore surface of MCM-41-type mesoporous silica nanosphere (MSN) materials via the interfacially devised co-condensation technique.46

Again, in 2003, they investigated the role played by various organoalkoxysilane precursors (containing primary amine, secondary amine, urea, isocyanate, vinyl and nitrile groups) in the base-catalysed co-condensation technique for the synthesis of hybrid mesoporous silicas.47 They revealed that the extent of functionalization and morphology of the particles were determined by the size, concentration and hydrophilicity/hydrophobicity of the organoalkoxysilane precursors. The hydrophobic (e.g., isocyanate, nitrile or vinyl groups) organoalkoxysilane was effectively condensed in the siliceous framework to produce highly dense functionalization compared to the hydrophilic (e.g., primary amines) organoalkoxysilane, giving rise to small rodlike nanoparticles. In addition, the organoalkoxysilanes with longer organic moieties, irrespective of being hydrophilic or hydrophobic, produced hybrid mesoporous silicas with a larger mesopore diameter. Compared to the standard MCM-41, the strong interaction between the hydrophobic moieties of the functional groups and nonpolar tails of the CTAB molecule at the micelle/water interface resulted in a reduction in the curvature of the self-assembled micelles, producing a smaller particle size. Over the years, in catalytic research field, ferrocene has become a potent candidate, while azobenzene, owing to its excellent photochemical properties due to the reversible isomerization between the trans- and cis-forms of the azo bond, has attracted much attention in the scientific world. In 2018, Abboud et al. synthesised a 2D hexagonal ferrocenylazobenzene-functionalized mesoporous MCM-41 material (Fc-Az-M14) by co-condensing 4-triethoxysilyl-3â€²-ferrocenylazobenzene (TEFA) and TEOS in CTAB and basic medium, which exhibited the properties of both ferrocene and azobenzene.48 A thorough investigation on the physicochemical properties and catalytic performance of a hydrophobically modified SBA-15, which was synthesised by co-condensing TEOS with propyl-triethoxysilane (PTES), was reported by Zong and co-workers in 2019.49 The change in the pre-hydrolysis time of TEOS resulted in a variation in the distribution of functional moieties in the hybrid SBA-15 material, which was reflected by the change in the pore size in the materials. A combination of high hydrophobicity with largest pore size proved beneficial towards the hydrogenation of AQ (2-ethyl-anthraquinone). Recently, in 2021, Jun et al. employed the co-condensation of undecyl-bipyridyl-linked methallylsilane with TEOS to produce bipyridine-functionalized SBA-15, Methallyl-bpy@Si.50 This material was further loaded with Pd (Pd-bpy@Si) and Rh (Rh-bpy@Si), producing a hybrid catalyst for Câ€“C bond coupling and selective hydrogenation reactions, respectively.

Generally, in contrast to the post-grafting methodology, co-condensation presents a faster and more convenient way to functionalize mesoporous silicas preferably with a homogeneous distribution and higher loading of organic functional moieties. However, with an increase in the concentration of functional groups in the silica network, there is a considerable decrease in the degree of mesoscopic order compared to its pure siliceous counterparts. Also, the co-condensation technique restricts the high-temperature calcination process to preserve the organic functionalities, resulting in a decrease in the hydrothermal stability of the hybrid material.

In recent years, chemists and materials scientists around the world focused on designing advanced functionalized materials comprised of multiple functionalities. Soltani et al., for the first time, reported the synthesis of nano-silica KCC-1 possessing multiple functionalities such as amine (â€“NH2), thiol (â€“SH) and tetrasulfide (â€“Sâ€“Sâ€“Sâ€“Sâ€“) groups, for the removal of acid fuchsine (AF) and acid orange II (AO) from water, reflecting a significant advancement in environmental research.51 This group further modified this work by substituting the thiol group with cationic 1-methylimidazolium group, enhancing the anionic AO and AF dyes uptake via effective electrostatic attraction.52 Mesoporous silica aerogels, owing to their ultra-low density and large specific surface area (âˆ¼800 m2 gâˆ’1), have attracted significant attention worldwide. Surface functionalization of these silica aerogels via various functional moieties and biopolymers assimilates interesting properties, which prove beneficial for numerous applications such as CO2 adsorption, gas purification, and chromatographic applications. Although the concept of molecular imprinting has been known for some time, Saad and co-workers recently for the first time implemented it over porous silica aerogels, which were further phenyl-functionalized to improve their adsorption capacity towards methylxanthines and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs).53 The authors highlighted the indisputable influence of molecular imprinting towards the selective adsorption of target molecules, thus suggesting a new potential strategy to fabricate porous siliceous materials with wider application.

2.2. Synthesis of periodic mesoporous organosilicas (PMOs) employing bridging organosilanes

A distinction exists in the scientific community between PMO materials and organically functionalised mesoporous silicas, which were mentioned previously. PMOs are a different class of hybrid periodic mesoporous silicas, which are synthesised through a solâ€“gel process involving bridging organoalkoxysilanes having the general formula (RO)3Siâ€“Râ€²â€“Si(OR)3 (where R = alkyl group and Râ€²= alkyl/alkenyl group), in the presence of some structure-directing agent (SDA) (Fig. 1). Upon aging the reaction mixture for hours or days, it is then filtered, and the solvent is extracted to remove the surfactant templates, producing a highly ordered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid PMO material. It is worth mentioning that these organic functional groups (Râ€²) become a part of the framework via covalently bonded non-hydrolyzable Siâ€“C bonds (Fig. 2). In contrast to either co-condensed or post-grafted organo-functionalized mesoporous silicas, where a reduction in surface area occurs with an increase in the extent of functionalization, PMO materials offer higher organo-functionalization without compromising their surface area.

In 2003, Bhaumik et al. synthesised titanium-incorporated ethane-bridged hybrid mesoporous silsesquioxane by mixing 1,2-bis-(trimethoxysilyl)ethane (BTME) and tetraethylorthosilicate (TEOS) in a solution containing octadecyltrimethyl ammonium chloride (C18TMACl) as the surfactant.57 Tetrabutylorthotitanate (TBOT) was used as the titanium source. Recently, in 2020, a novel nitrogen and sulphur-rich PMO material, DMTZ-PMO, bearing thiadiazole and thiol moieties grafted in its pore walls, with a high specific surface area of 971 m2 gâˆ’1 was synthesized by Das et al.61 The thiadiazole and thiol-bridged organosilane precursor was synthesized via substitution reaction between 1,3,4-thiadiazole-2,5-dithiol and 3-choloropropyltriethoxysilane, which was further condensed with TEOS in a CTAB-assisted basic medium. Cellulose nanocrystals (CNCs) in water form chiral nematic liquid crystals, which were utilized as a template in the condensation of (RO)3SiCH2CH2Si(OR)3 or (RO)3SiC6H4Si(OR)3 to synthesize chiral nematic mesoporous organosilicas (CNMOs).62,63 However, the real challenge is to incorporate long alkylene spacers without destroying the chiral nematic structure. Recently, a chiral bis-organosilane precursor 1,1â€²-((1R,2R)-1,2-diphenylethane-1,2-diyl)bis(3-(3-(triethoxysilyl)propyl)urea) was condensed in an EtOH/water mixture in the presence of CTAB as an SDA to produce chiral PMO nanoparticles by Reziq and Omar in 2020.65 Furthermore, these chiral PMO were modified with magnetite nanoparticles (NPs) through the oil in water (o/w) emulsion technique to obtain magnetic chiral PMO NPs.

Recently, considering biomedical applications especially drug delivery, significant research has been carried out to design PMO containing degradable organo-bridged alkoxysilanes. These hybrid mesoporous organosilica nanoparticles can undergo degradation on-demand under specific pathological conditions at the cellular level. To preserve the degradable nature of these organosilica nanomaterials, it is crucial to have suitable organic cleavable bridges such as disulphide or oxamide/ester-based alkoxysilanes in the material. Biocompatibility with target delivery enhances the potential of these PMOs as a promising drug delivery nanocarriers.66 In the spectrum of catalysis, obtaining equivalent efficiency in heterogeneous catalysts to that of their homogeneous congeners (transition metal complexes) has been a challenge for material chemists around the globe. The incorporation of metal complexes into functionalized pockets of PMOs via post-synthetic treatment not only maximizes the catalytic performance but also imparts the ease of separation. Recently, Kataoka and Inagaki employed the evaporation-induced self-assembly (EISA) approach to fabricate a 2,2â€²-bipyridine-PMO thin film, which later served as framework to affix MoO2Cl2 complexes.67 This new approach to obtain metal complex-grafted PMO thin films can serve as a microreactor for the synthesis of fine chemicals in continuous flow reactors. The versatility in chemical composition offers heterogeneity and idiosyncrasy in the application of PMOs, which is not surprising, given that they were first developed by materials chemists. However, to conduct PMO research for particular applications, synergy between academic and industrial research is crucial.

2.3. Synthesis of metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs)

The term metalâ€“organic framework (MOF) was first coined by Yaghi et al. in 1995,68 where they reported a solvothermal synthetic route for generating a zeolite-like, crystalline, extended channel system of Cu(4,4â€²-bpy)1.5Â·NO3(H2O)1.25. Since then, MOFs have gained immense attention in the field of hybrid porous materials. The robustness of these organicâ€“inorganic hybrid open frameworks, their facile synthetic strategy and flexible network topology with the ability to support permanent porosity make them superior to other hybrid porous systems. Metal organic frameworks (MOFs) are a particular class of crystalline hybrid porous materials containing a regular array of metal ions or clusters, forming nodes, and coordinated with organic â€œlinkerâ€ molecules, generally ditopic or polytopic organic carboxylates (or other molecule with similar negative charge), together forming a cage-like structure. With the discovery of MOF-5 in 1999 by Yaghi and coworkers, these crystalline microporous polymeric materials have emerged as an interface between molecular coordination chemistry and materials science.69 MOFs epitomize the sophistication of isoreticular chemistry, leading to a versatile framework with varying dimensionalities and topologies. The high surface area, tunable porosity, high pore volume, magnified surface-to-volume ratio, ultra-low densities, and microreactor environment with high guest loading capacities of these hybrid materials make them highly applicable in catalysis,70 CO2 adsorption,71 energy storage,72 sensors,73 drug delivery,74 biomedical sensing,75 and others (Fig. 3).

Traditionally, the hydrothermal or solvothermal technique is employed firstly to overcome the solubility problem of heavier organic ligands, and secondly to initiate a rapid nucleation process to obtain MOF crystals. Banerjee and co-workers synthesized two distinct 2D fluorinated metal organic frameworks (F-MOFs) by solvothermally reacting copper(II) nitrate with 4,4â€²-(hexafluoroisopropylidene)bis(benzoic acid) and 3-methyl pyridine in N,N-dimethyl formamide (DMF) and N,N-diethyl formamide (DEF) solvents.76 With a change in the solvents, the extent of deprotonation of H2hfbba under suitable conditions resulted in divergent changes in the resultant porous hybrid frameworks. In pursuit of more stable and chemically-inert frameworks, FÃ©rey et al. designed a chromium-terephthalate porous architecture, MIL-101 (MIL stands for Material of Institute Lavoisier).77 MIL-101 is a robust MOF with a surface area greater than 4000 m2 gâˆ’1, which is typically synthesised hydrothermally at 220 Â°C in an autoclave under autogenous pressure for 8 h by reacting a chromium salt with H2BDC assisted by small amount of HF. A 9 Ã… by 9 Ã… large square-shaped 3D porous open-framework, HKUST-1, was first reported in 1999 by Williams et al., which was solvothermally synthesized using cupric nitrate and trimesic acid in H2Oâ€“EtOH solvent at 180 Â°C under autogenous pressure for 12 h.78 The weakly coordinated water or solvent molecules to the Cu2+ sites, are removed upon mild activation under vacuum, leaving behind a porous hybrid architecture with open metal sites. The high affinity of zirconium towards oxygen donor ligands is reflected by the unprecedented thermal and chemical stability of the Zr(IV)-based MOF, UiO-66 (UiO stands for University of Oslo), which was first discovered in 2008 by Cavka et al.79 In a typical solvothermal synthesis, ZrCl4 and H2BDC were dissolved in DMF and crystallized under static conditions in a pre-heated oven at 120 Â°C for 1 day. In 2002, You et al. came up with a novel 3D MOF having a zeolite-like topology, where imidazole was used as the linker.80 The two coordinating N atoms of the imidazole moiety were oriented at an angle of 144Â°, which replicates the Siâ€“Oâ€“Si angle of 145Â° in zeolites, resulting a tetrahedral building block. Later, these hybrid porous frameworks became known as zeolitic imidazolate frameworks (ZIFs), and in 2008, Banerjee et al. solvothermally synthesized twenty-five different ZIF crystals using either Zn(II)/Co(II) nitrate and imidazolate/imidazolate-type linkers in DMF or DEF solvent at a temperature in the range of 65 Â°C to 150 Â°C for 48 to 100 h.81 In contrast to zeolites, these hybrid ZIFs have much lower densities, as measured by the number of tetrahedral vertices per unit volume (T/V), indicating their more open framework, and hence easier functionalization of their pores. Our group also explored this field considerably, where in 2017 Das et al. solvothermally synthesized a new microporous Ce(III)-based MOF containing multicarboxylate linkers (1,4-naphthalenedicarboxylic acid) in the presence of 4-amino triazole or 5-amino tetrazole.82 The presence of an unpaired electron of Ce(III) resulted in the paramagnetic nature of this MOF. Recently, Chakraborty et al. hydrothermally synthesized a novel tetradentate phosphonate ligand-based MOF, H8Lâ€“Co-crystals, by reacting (ethene-1,1,2,2-tetrayltetrakis(benzene-4,1-diyl))tetraphosphonic acid and cobalt nitrate hexahydrate in a Vensil high pressure tube at 353 K for 30 min.83 This Co-based MOF showed very good electrocatalytic activity towards the HER with a low overpotential of 243 mV and low Tafel slope of 102 mV decâˆ’1 in acidic medium.

A faster route for the large-scale production of MOFs is still a challenging task for materials scientists. Microwave dielectric heating-assisted hydrothermal synthesis allows rapid crystallization with a narrow particle size distribution. Molecules such as water and DMSO possess dielectric moments. Heat is generated due to the molecular rotation and collision to align themselves with the alternating electric field of the microwave. In a typical synthesis process, the substrates are dissolved in a suitable solvent, transferred to a quartz or Teflon-lined vessel, sealed and heated in a microwave for the appropriate time. Chang et al., for the first time in 2005 reported Cr-MIL-100, which synthesized by microwave irradiation at 220 Â°C in aqueous media.84 In comparison to conventional hydrothermal synthesis for 4 days, the authors reported 44% yield of Cr-MIL-100 in just 4 h using the microwave-assisted technique. In the following year, Masel and Ni reported microcrystals of three known MOFs comprising Zn4O clusters and BDC-NH2 (IRMOF-1, IRMOF-2, IRMOF-3) with a uniform size and cubical morphology, synthesized through microwave treatment at 150 W for a few seconds.85 Since the nucleation of all crystals takes place at once, microwave-assisted synthesis results in the formation of particles with a narrow size distribution. Recently, SolÃ­s et al. synthesised NH2-MIL-125(Ti) MOFs solvothermally under microwave irradiation at a temperature of 200 Â°C for a reaction time duration of 15 min.86 Briefly, 2-aminoterephthalic acid (ATA) was dissolved in DMF, followed by the addition of titanium(IV) isopropoxide and methanol. The resulting mixture was transferred to a Teflon-lined vessel for microwave heating. They reported that at a temperature below 180 Â°C, an amorphous-type material was formed, while high exposure to microwave irradiation (i.e., 4 h) resulted in collapse of the MOF framework.

Another methodology for faster and homogeneous nucleation with rapid crystallization of MOFs is the sonochemical method. In 2008, Son and co-workers for the first time utilized the sonochemical method to synthesise high-quality MOF-5 crystals with a size ranging between 5â€“25 Î¼m within 30 min, by dissolving zinc nitrate hexahydrate and terephthalic acid in 1-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) under an N2 atmosphere.87 The resultant mixture was then transferred to a transparent horn-type tube reactor for ultrasonic treatment, where a temperature of 164 Â°C was obtained upon increasing the power to 50% of the maximum power, resulting in faster crystallization. Generally, organic solvents with a high vapour pressure reduce the cavitational collapse intensity, which consequently reduces the temperature and pressure.88 However, NMP has a high boiling point (202 Â°C) and a low vapour pressure, making it suitable for sonochemical synthesis. The conventional solvothermal synthesis of porphyrin-containing MOFs requires a prolonged reaction time with controlled reaction conditions. Recently, Yu and co-workers sonochemically synthesised Zr-based porphyrinic MOF-525 and MOF-545 with high purity and uniform distribution of size, in just 2.5 and 0.5 h respectively.89 Zirconyl chloride octahydrate and tetrakis(4-carboxyphenyl) porphyrin (TCPP) were dissolved in DMF and charged in a glass vial for sonication. Benzoic acid and trifluoroacetic acid were used as a modulator for MOF-525 and MOF-545, respectively. The rapid nucleation and crystal growth under a relatively shorter reaction time compared to conventional synthesis resulted in an enhancement in defect sites for the sonochemical method, as estimated by UV-vis spectroscopy.

The researchers at BASF pioneered the electrochemical (EC) synthesis of MOFs in 2005, where anodic dissolution continuously supplies metal ions to the synthesis solution containing organic linkers and a conducting salt.90 [Cu3(BTC)2] MOF crystals were synthesised electrochemically by Ameloot et al., where they applied an anodic voltage to the copper electrode, resulting in the dissolution of Cu(II) ions in the synthesis solution containing 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid (BTC) and methyltributylammonium methyl sulfate (MTBS) as the ligand and conducting salt, respectively.91 They reported that upon increasing the voltage, smaller crystals were obtained due to the higher concentration of metal ions near the surface, which favoured nucleation, whereas dilution with water resulted in the formation of less crystals with a larger size. The EC method serves as an eco-friendly route for the synthesis of MOFs. In 2021, Liu et al. electrochemically synthesised a Cu3(HHTP)2 (HHTP: 2,3,6,7,10,11-hexahydroxytriphenylene) 2D-MOF film over a single-crystal Cu(100) anode.92 Cu foil served as the anode and cathode, which were submerged in deionised water containing HHTP ligand and ammonia. The applied potential across the electrodes remained proportional to the dissolution of Cu2+ ions into the synthesis solution. Migration of the organic HHTP anions towards the anode took place due to the induced electric field, where the coupling of Cu-HHTP took place via electrostatic forces.

The synergistic effects obtained in the presence of mixed-metals in MOFs have become a hot topic recently in materials science. Various synthetic strategies have been proposed to fabricate mixed-metal MOFs (MM-MOFs). The one-pot synthesis approach involves the suitable selection of metal precursors based on similar Coulombic charge, ionic radius and chemical nature for the facile homogeneous co-incorporation of metal ions into the framework.93 However, this strategy lacks control of the final metal distribution in the framework. Another approach is based on â€œcoordination chemistryâ€, where metalloligands are used as linkers capable of binding a second metal ion in the satellite positions, apart from that in the lattice nodes. Conventionally, porphyrin-based linkers are widely studied to fabricate these multivariate MOFs. The different arrangements of SBUs in the framework dictate the physical and chemical properties of MM-MOFs.94 In view of the wide applicability of MOFs, the introduction of additional metal ions in the framework will be a growing field of research in the coming years to enhance their performance beyond that of homometallic systems.

2.4. Synthesis of porous metal organophosphonates

Organophosphonate ligands are superior to the traditionally used phosphate tetrahedra due to the presence of an additional functional group. Organophosphonates are prototypical hybrid systems, comprising inorganic building units of phosphite ions together with a flexible organic moiety. The tendency of these organophosphonates to bridge a wide range of metal centers in 2D open frameworks has attracted attention from materials scientists all over the world. The use of surfactants or amines to induce porosity in materials is a common strategy used worldwide. A mesoporous aluminum organophosphonate using methylene diphosphonic acid (HO)2OPCH2PO(OH)2 as the organophosphonate precursor and octadecyltrimethylammonium (C18TMA) as the surfactant was synthesised by Kimura in 2003, which was the first example of a templated non-silica based organicâ€“inorganic hybrid mesoporous framework.95 In a typical synthetic process, the surfactant, tetramethylammonium hydroxide and methylene diphosphonic acid were mixed until a clear solution was formed, which was followed by the addition of aluminium isopropoxide to obtain a white solid product. Calcination of the as-synthesized product for the removal of the template (C18TMA molecules) without decomposing the methylene groups was carried out under N2 flow at 400 Â°C to obtain an ordered hexagonal matrix. In 2006, Bellezza et al. synthesised an anionic surfactant sodium bis(2-ethylhexyl) sulfosuccinate (AOT)-mediated porous zirconium benzenephosphonate (P-ZrBP), where AOT was first dissolved in deionized water, followed by the addition of zirconyl chloride octahydrate and benzenephosphonic acid.96 The resultant mixture was stirred for 16 h, and then centrifuged and washed with water and EtOH to obtain a white surfactant-free mesostructured hybrid product. They reported that the zirconium metal ions interact with the negatively charged head groups of the spherical micelles, and upon the addition of benzenephosphonic acid, zirconium benzenephosphonate (P-ZrBP) precipitates into a lamellar framework surrounding the supramolecular assembly of self-assembled AOT surfactant. Kimura employed methylene diphosphonic acid as an organophosphorous precursor for the synthesis of a mesoporous aluminum organophosphonate using octadecyltrimethylammonium chloride as the template under hydrothermal conditions.98 However, this hybrid mesoporous material lost its periodicity considerably upon removal of the template molecules during the low-temperature calcination process. The antagonistic relationship between porosity and crystallinity for metal organophosphonate hybrid materials was discussed by Shimizu and co-workers, where they designed a tetrahedral organophosphonate ligand, 1,3,5,7-tetrakis(4-phosphonatophenyl)adamantane (H8L), containing a Cu-organophosphonate having a diamondoid net topology.97 The unprecedented â€œanti-SBUâ€ approach governs the formation of organophosphonate-based MOFs given that no true SBUs exists, highlighting the structure-directing role of these ligands not only on the overall topology, but also on the geometry of the resultant metal cluster. Recently, in 2021, Bhanja et al. hydrothermally synthesized a template-free microporous hybrid nickel phosphonate (NiPPA).98 In a typical synthesis, a nickel nitrate solution was poured into diethylene-triaminepentakis(methylphosphonic acid) solution, maintaining the pH to 6 using ammonia solution, followed by thermal treatment at 150 Â°C for 48 h. A green-coloured product was obtained by centrifugation, which was further washed with water and absolute ethanol to remove the unreacted precursors. Microporosity was achieved in the framework given that each phosphonate group coordinating with Ni(II) ions is separated from each other by a short distance. In the same year, a tetraphenylethylene tetraphosphonic acid (H8L)-based Ni-MOF (Fig. 4) was synthesised at 45 Â°C by reacting the tetradentate phosphonate ligand (H8L) with nickel nitrate in a mixture of solvents containing water, dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) and dimethylformamide (DMF) in a 2:1:1 molar ratio for 2 days.99 Blue needle-shaped single crystals of Ni-MOF were obtained in the Vensil high pressure tube. 3. Applications

3.1. Catalysis

Over time, the evolution of catalysts occurred through a trial-and-error process based entirely on accumulated experiences and chemical knowledge. Today, advanced synthetic strategies with modernized surface-characterization techniques and molecular modelling have enabled the design of tailor-made optimal catalysts, with precision at the molecular level. This is more pronounced in the case of heterogeneous catalysts, given that they allow alteration of single or multiple catalytic sites. Heterogeneous catalysts can be defined as solid materials consisting of a high surface area, where the catalytic sites are embedded, enabling chemical reactions to occur at or near the interface between two phases. The ease of separation of heterogeneous catalysts from the reaction mixture gives them the upper hand over their homogeneous counterparts. Based on porous materials, significant advancements have been made towards the ab initio design of catalysts in both the academic and industrial spheres. Currently, heterogeneous catalysis is considered the mainstay of various chemical and energy industries. However, in contrast to homogeneous catalysts, the lack of in situ information restricts their mechanistic understanding. The synergistic study between theoretical research and experiment may open up a new dimension on the critical impression of heterogeneous catalysts in the near future. In the following sections, we summarize the different catalytic processes over hybrid porous materials.

3.2. Adsorptive removal of pollutants from water

Unpolluted water is a basic necessity for all living organisms and is considered central to the evolution of human civilization. However, water pollution is a major global problem that not only affects the growth of aquatic organisms but also poses a threat to human health. Rapid industrialization, mining and environmental catastrophes have led to an alarming increase in the presence of toxins in natural water resources worldwide, resulting in global water scarcity. These pollutants are mainly comprised of heavy metals and organic dyes, which are non-biodegradable and chemically stable. The introduction of heavy metals into our environment by various human activities has led to serious health problems upon long-term exposure. Adsorption is basically the accumulation of any compound at the interface between two phases such as liquidâ€“solid and gas-solid. 3.3. Photocatalysis

Utilizing solar energy for visible-light driven catalysis is a propitious strategy for environmental remediation. Generally, the photocatalytic property of a material strongly depends on its distinctive electronic structures, filled valence band (VB) and empty conduction band (CB). Other thermodynamic factors such as better matching of the VB/CB levels with the redox potential and appropriate wavelength of incident light (with energy equal or greater than the band gap of the material, Eg), governs the efficiency of a particular heterogeneous photocatalyst. Typically, heterogeneous photocatalysis involves three main processes, as follows: (1) absorption of incident light and charge excitation, (2) charge separation and transfer and (3) surface charge reactions (oxidation/reduction). A good photocatalyst should have a strong absorption of visible light, excited states with longer lifetime and high charge mobility. The two most notable applications of photocatalysis are water splitting and air/water purification. Porous hybrid materials are potent candidates for photocatalyst design, given that they have tunable band gaps, bringing together the merits of both organic and inorganic chemistry. Moreover, the porous nature of these materials enable better interaction with the substrates throughout their bulk compared to other state-of-art photocatalysts.

3.4. Biomass conversion

The devastating impacts imparted by the over consumption of fossil fuels have made biomass the focus as a clean and sustainable source for the production of platform chemicals, fuels and energy.171 Owing to the high carbon content of biomass, it can be easily converted to contemporary energy systems, guided by economic incentives. Biomass can be generally classified as lignocellulosic and non-lignocellulosic in nature. Lignocellulosic biomass involves vegetal origin, and hence its conversion into valuable chemicals, bio-fuels and bio-gases (e.g., H2, CH4, CO) is cost effective and environmentally sound. Currently, biomass conversion processes are largely dominated by hybrid catalysts. Hybrid porous materials play the main role in heterogeneous catalysis owing to their high surface area, tunable pore size, fascinating control over morphology and thermal stability.172

It was observed that the conversion of glycerol to dioxane increased with an increase in the loading amount of TSA, highlighting the enhancement in the BrÃ¸nsted acidity of the catalyst.

In the last few years, a large spectrum of state-of-the-art porous hybrid materials has been developed for the conversion of biomass, introducing new properties in materials. Microporous zeolites and functionalized silica materials were modified with heteroatoms. However, despite the impressive advancements in the field of biomass conversion using heterocatalysts, many challenges still need to be solved, especially the leaching of inorganic metals from the porous frameworks, which reduces the efficiency of heterocatalysts. Nowadays, more focus is devoted to the physicochemical properties of hybrid heterogeneous catalysts. In recent years, for the biomass conversion, lignocellulosic has been used extensively given that it has a great potential for producing high value chemicals, which can replace fossil resources. For future perspective, the use of solar energy to cleave the Câ€“O and Câ€“C bonds from lignocellulosic materials will draw ample attention as a result of their many attractive reactive species.184 Hence, the rational design of hybrid porous heterocatalysts containing suitable photosensitizing scaffolds needs to be explored for continuing transformative innovations.

3.5. Selective gas adsorption

Gas adsorption properties are based on molecular sieving, which is very demanding from environmental perspectives. Selectivity originates when there is a difference in the interaction of the adsorbates with the surface of the adsorbent under a particular condition. Pore size and shape are the two major factors that allow the selective adsorption of guest molecules or gases. In addition, the interaction between the guest molecule and the surface of the host plays a crucial role in selective adsorption. In general, a good adsorbent should possess the following salient features: (i) good adsorption capacity, (ii) selectivity, (iii) regenerability and (iv) favourable adsorption kinetics. In the case of hybrid porous materials as adsorbents, preferential adsorption based on polarity, H-bonding, quadrupole moment and surface property of the pores play a vital role in selective adsorption. Moreover, hybrid porous materials allow surface modification and pore size alteration, overcoming the limitations of each material.

In 2003, Kaneko and his group developed a porous lanthanide framework, [Er2(PDA)3], which showed selective adsorption towards CO2 over Ar and N2, indicating a relationship between the pore size of the adsorbent and kinetic diameters of the adsorbates.185 The space-filling model of [Er2(PDA)3] suggested that it possesses a pore window of ca. 3.4 Ã…, which matched well with the kinetic diameter of CO2 (CO2 = 3.3 Ã…, Ar = 3.4 Ã… and N2 = 3.64 Ã…). Even though the kinetic diameter of CO2 and Ar differed by only 0.1 Ã…, CO2 was selectively adsorbed over Ar. This unprecedented selectivity was attributed to the dipole-induced-dipole interaction between the electric field of the framework and CO2, together with the interaction between the quadrupole moment of CO2 and electric field gradient of the framework. The authors also mentioned the additional donorâ€“acceptor affinity between the Lewis acidic Er(III) sites and CO2 molecule. The size-selective adsorption of H2 or O2 over CO or N2 was observed using the Mg3(NDC)3 microporous MOF.186 The pore openings for Mg3(NDC)3 varied from 3.46 to 3.64 Ã… in comparison to the kinetic diameter of 3.46 Ã… for O2, 3.64 Ã… for N2 and 3.76 Ã… for CO. The authors suggested its potential application in the separation of O2 from N2 in air and H2 from CO in fuel cell applications. The substitution of transition metal ions with lighter main group elements results in an enhancement in hydrogen storage capacity. A comparative study was reported by Yang et al. in 2011 between ethanol-extracted and high temperature-calcined SBA-15 to graft amine for selective CO2/N2 adsorption.187 The authors suggested that the surface silanol group density increased from 3.4 OH per nm2 for SBA-15-cal to 8.5 OH per nm2 for SBA-15-ext, showing a steady increase in amine grafting from 2.2 to 3.2 mmol gâˆ’1. The enhancement in the surface basicity of the extracted amine-SBA-15 was reflected in its higher CO2 adsorption isotherm. Interestingly, at 298 K, the N2 adsorption for the amine-SBA-15-cal and amine-SBA-15-ext was 0.028 and 0.015 mmol gâˆ’1, respectively. Hence, considering the condition pertinent to flue gas, amine-SBA-15-ext showed 2.8-times CO2/N2 selectivity over its calcined counterpart. CO2/CH4 separation has been considered a cutting-edge field of research, with the purpose to make biogas economically viable. The naturally associated high surface area and tunable pore properties of PMO make them one of the prime candidate for selective CO2 adsorption. Ferreira et al. designed a phenylene-PMO, followed by surface modification with Al2O3 using atomic layer deposition (ALD), with the goal to fabricate a novel CO2 adsorbent.188 The surface acidity of this PMO/Al2O3 composite material was elevated by the tetrahedral and bipyramidal Al-sites, which increased the interaction between the CO2 quadrupole and composite, promoting the selective separation of CO2/CH4. Phenylene-PMO also showed very high selectivity for the removal of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) such as hexanal and butyric acid vapors from air at a low sorbent dose.189

Hydrogen storage for energy carrier application in vehicles is a cutting-edge field of research. Fuel cell applications involve two major schemes, i.e., dissociative adsorption (DA), where the H atoms get dissolved by the metal alloys by dissociating the Hâ€“H bonds, and associated adsorption (AA) of hydrogen, where H2 gets bound as a molecule through strong H-bonding.190 The former process has the disadvantage of overcoming a high energy barrier to cleave the Hâ€“H bond. The existing metal oxide clusters and organic aromatic linkers in MOFs show significant affinity for H2, illustrating the recent advancement in the AA process. Using density functional theory (DFT) and grand canonical Monte Carlo (GCMC) simulations, Froudakis and co-workers revealed that by doping Li atoms into MOFs, their hydrogen storage capacity could be exceptionally enhanced.191 The observed increase in the interaction energy between the Li atom-affixed MOF and hydrogen molecule induced a strong dipole, resulting in strong binding. The authors explained that 3 H2 molecules strongly interact with 1 Li atom. In 2007, Han and Goddard reported the synthesis of an Li-doped MOF, namely Li-MOF-C-30, which exhibited 5.99 wt% gravimetric H2 uptake at 100 bar and âˆ’30 Â°C, fulfilling the Department of Energy (DOE) 2010 target.190 They also reported that at 300 K and 20 bar pressure, the H2 uptake capacity was 3.89 wt%, which increased to 4.56 wt% at 50 bar pressure. This work suggested that the incorporation of electropositive metal ions is a promising strategy to amplify the H2 uptake capacity.

Modification of MOF surfaces can be used as a better tool to achieve the separation of important and challenging gas mixtures. In the case of almost similar molecules such as propylene and propane, where the difference is only two hydrogens, with almost the same boiling points, separation becomes difficult. Accordingly, MOFs are potential participants among the porous materials that can separate olefins and paraffins based on their thermodynamic mechanism.192 Lately, hierarchical porous hybrid materials are drawing increasing attention because of their long-range pore size distribution. However, gas separation using porous hybrid materials is still in its infancy. Exfoliating 2D porous hybrid materials to obtain monolayer membranes enhances the maximum flux, thus reducing the cost of separation. However, the high price of 2D materials limits their use in large-scale industrial applications. Hence, it still remains an ongoing challenge for material chemists to fabricate cost-effective 2D porous hybrid membranes from a market perspective.

3.6. Fuel cell

The demand for energy in society is ever-increasing, and thus difficult to satisfy. Over the years, material scientists around the world have devoted considerable efforts to designing materials that can convert chemical energy directly into electrical energy through a clean carbon-free system. A fuel cell is comprised of three active components, i.e., an anode or fuel electrode, a cathode or oxidant electrode and an electrolyte. Following the waterâ€“hydrogen cycle, H2 and O2 from water splitting can be recombined into water with the generation of energy. However, the energy generated upon the recombination of H2 and O2 is less than that required to split water. Hence, the suitable design of catalysts is required to overcome this hurdle. Desirable features in fuel cell materials/membranes include: (i) high conductivity of protons, (ii) impermeable to water, and (iii) high mechanical and thermal stability. Recently, porous hybrid materials, owing to their strong porous framework, high specific surface areas, tunable pore structures, wide functionalities and excellent mechanical and thermal stability, have gained tremendous attention as potent catalysts in fuel cell applications.

The structural features of MOFs such as open metal sites, ordered pore apertures and presence of organic linker together with desirable functional moieties, have prompted their extensive use in various fuel cell applications. A composite material composed of a Zn-MOF and polyaniline was reported by Guo et al. in 2016 via the in situ oxidative polymerization of aniline monomer bearing a carbonized Zn-MOF suspension.193 This composite material showed a high specific capacitance of 477 F gâˆ’1 at a current density of 1 A gâˆ’1, accounting for the synergistic effect between the carbonized MOF and PANI. The Zn-MOF exhibited good electrical conductivity, while the sandwich-like structure of carbonized Zn-MOF/PANI decreased the electron transport distance and electrolyte diffusion resistance. The authors discussed that the carbonized Zn-MOF did not alter the structure of PANI, during the overall chargeâ€“discharge process. In the same year, Wen et al. synthesized another composite material graphene oxide/MOF-5 via a solvothermal process, which was further annealed at 1000 Â°C to obtain a reduced graphene oxide/carbonized MOF-5 (rGO/CMOF-5) hybrid material.194 The rGO layers were embedded inside the hybrid structure, which acted as an efficient mini-current collector, reducing the electron transportation lengths to increase the conductivity. The high electrical conductivity and excellent electron transport properties of this material facilitated its use as a supercapacitor electrode material. It was observed that the incorporation of rGO increased the electrical performance of the hybrid material, showing a high specific capacitance of 312 F gâˆ’1 at a current density of 0.5 A gâˆ’1 with a retention of 89% after 5000 cycles, which was higher than that of pure rGO (105 F gâˆ’1) and CMOF-5 (206 F gâˆ’1). To explore this hybrid material for practical application, a symmetrical supercapacitor was successfully constructed using the rGO/CMOF-5 hybrid (positive and negative electrodes), which was separated by a cellulose paper separator in 6 M KOH electrolyte, giving an energy density of 17.2 W h kgâˆ’1 at a power density of 250 W kgâˆ’1 with a cycling stability of 81% capacitance retention after 5000 cycles at a current density of 2 A gâˆ’1. The demand for active and stable electrocatalysts is growing constantly for commercial fuel cell applications. Recently, Sun and group designed a PtNP-encapsulated ultrathin 2D Co-MOF-derived carbon layer.195 The carbon layer gave extra stability to the NPs, mitigating the leaching problem, which enhanced the stability by nine times that of Pt/C catalysts. Moreover, this novel Pt encapsulated catalyst also showed excellent catalytic activity against the ORR, which is 5.9 and 12 times higher than that of Pt/C and Pt/rGO, respectively. In addition, it also exhibited good catalytic activity toward the HER, with a low overpotential (23.1 mV in acidic media, while 92.1 mV in alkaline media at a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’1) compared to Pt/C catalysts (23.1 mV in acidic media and 92.1 mV in alkaline media).

Duan et al. reported the synthesis of a copper and nitrogen-based carbon hybrid material, a pyrolyzing porphyrin-based MOF in the presence and absence of air (Cuâ€“NC-air and Cuâ€“NC, respectively).196 The authors performed a study on ORR catalytic activity for nitrogen-dopped carbon, revealing an enhancement in its performance upon addition of copper as a co-dopant (Fig. 11). In addition, it was also found that the air-impurities during pyrolysis resulted in a high surface area and higher electron transfer number, owing to the fractional etching of carbon, which generated additional porosity and increased extra sites for ORR activity. The electron transfer number of Cuâ€“NC-air was 3.9, while that for the Cuâ€“NC sample was 3.0, showing good stability up to 5000 cycles for ORR catalysis. In 2020, Feng et al. synthesized a nanocomposite phase change material (PCM) comprised of polyethylene glycol (PEG) as a core low-temperature energy storage material (phase change temperature range of 46â€“65 Â°C) and MCM-41 as a solid matrix for advanced thermal energy storage.197 The PCM was synthesized via the solution impregnation method, and â€“NH2 groups were grafted by replacing the hydroxyl group of MCM-41. No phase transition was observed in differential scanning colorimetry when hydroxyl groups were present in MCM-41 even up to 70% loading of PEG. Interestingly, the phase change was observed after the grafting of amine on MCM-41, and 30% loading of PEG resulted in an increment in the phase change enthalpy from 0 J gâˆ’1 to 58.76 J gâˆ’1. The authors explained that the conformational transition was the key to the phase transition of the polymer chains, and hence the excessive surface absorption of PEG on MCM-41-OH in contrast to MCM-41-NH2 restricted the conformational transition.

To generate electrochemical devices, materials should have high ionic and electrical conductivities, which can be achieved by employing microporous hybrid systems possessing organic linkers with more than one functionality. Together with the common coordination sites, additional functional groups in organic linkers can act as guest interaction sites. Not only the organic linkers, the electrochemical performance of any porous material depends on its morphology, size and surface area, promoting faster electron transport. The use of multi-metallic systems can also enhance the electronic conductivity of the material. Moreover, further investigations on microporous hybrid materials showing excellent heat transfer ability can be useful in designing various advanced thermal materials. Hydrogen is likely to play an important role in decarbonizing the global energy systems. Recently, various classes of crystalline hybrid porous materials with well-defined pore volumes, high surface area and tunable pore sizes have been modified to form proton exchange membranes for hydrogen fuel cell applications. Lately, two main strategies have been developed to design proton conductive porous membranes for hydrogen fuel cells. Firstly, the incorporation of proton donors (such as sulfonate, imidazole and phenolic hydroxyl) groups in the hybrid porous skeletons, and secondly, post-synthetic treatment of proton carriers such as H3PO4 in the hybrid porous solid matrixes can result in the formation of better proton conducting membranes. Relevant research on the stability of proton conducting membranes can assist in overcoming unfavourable redox reactions and high humid conditions, as witnessed recently. Various post-synthetic strategies can amplify the processability of these materials, leading to the commercialization of these porous hybrid platforms.

3.7. Bio-sensing/biomarkers

Generally, biosensors are specific absorbents that can selectively immobilize analyte-targeted probes, such as aptamers, DNA, and antibodies from a complex environment containing coexisting biomolecules. Recently, hybrid porous nanomaterials have attracted significant attention as sensitizers due to their unique features such as tunable pore size, extended conjugation, high bioaffinity towards a wide number of probes, good fluorescence properties and additional linker effects. Compared to other non-hybrid systems (i.e., single organic/inorganic), the presence of both an extended Ï€-electron system and open metal sites facilitates the detection of various bacteria and biomolecules. The high porosity of these materials allows a high loading of bioactive molecules, while their tunable surface functionalities allow better conjugation of bioactive molecules, both by in situ or post-synthesis, enabling strong binding sites for the targeted analyte.

MOFs are potent candidates as natural enzyme mimetics given that they consist of uniform cavities together with good catalytic activity. Recently, Zheng et al. reported an MOF, [Zr6O4(Î¼3-OH)4(OH)6(H2O)6(BTC)2]Â·nH2O, also known as MOF-808, with peroxidase mimetic catalytic activity toward the oxidation of 3,3â€²,5,5â€²-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) in the presence of H2O2 as an oxidant.198 The conversion was indicated by the formation of a green colour in the pH range of 3â€“10. The ability to function at both acidic and neutral pH makes this hybrid material suitable for use in biological systems (Scheme 13). The important biological molecule ascorbic acid (AA) has immense applications in the chemical, biological and nutritional domains. The deficiency of AA causes cardiovascular diseases and cancer, while the extra accumulation of AA causes urinary stones, stomach cramps, etc. In 2017, Xian and Chen et al. reported the synthesis of a cerium-based MOF ZJU-136-Ce, which was highly sensitive and showed selective â€œturn-onâ€ fluorescence activity toward AA.199 It was revealed that in ZJU-136-Ce, the mixed valence state of Ce existed as Ce3+/Ce4+ (Fig. 12). A controlled study was performed to understand the mechanistic pathway by addition of different concentrations of KMnO4 (0â€“50 Î¼M) to ZJU-136-Ce. Interestingly, the fluorescence intensity of ZJU-136-Ce decreased gradually with an emission spectrum at 917.55 eV, indicating the presence of Ce4+. When 100 Î¼M AA was added to the ZJU-136-Ce MOF, the peak at 917.55 eV totally disappeared, which was attributed to the specific oxidation reaction between Ce4+ and AA.

To measure dysfunction in biological systems or to detect the presence of toxic agents or drugs in living systems, biomarkers are quite handy. The detection of the biological phospholipid lysophosphatidic acid (LPA) is an important tool in the pathophysiology of ovarian cancer. For the first time, Zaworotko et al. reported the synthesis of Tb-ZMOF, Eu-ZMOF and mixed MZMOF-3, with a zeolite-like topology for the selective detection of LPA in the presence of a mixture of L-proline, urea, NaCl, and glucose.200 To understand the activity of the MOFs toward the detection of LPA, the fluorescence emission spectra were measured in MeOH and it was found that the mixed MOF MZMOF-3 showed the highest sensitivity, which is attributed to the energy transfer mechanism from Tb3+ to Eu3+. In the case of liver cancer, hepatocellular carcinoma can be detected by checking the level of a special type of diagnostic biomarker, cholyglycine (CG) (a combination of glycine and cholic acid) in the serum. Zhao and Yan et al. reported the synthesis of a novel composite material via the in situ growth of polyoxometalate (POM) Eu4W8 in a cationic COF, EB-TFP (formation of imine linkage between 1,3,5-triformylphloroglucinol and ethidium bromide), which was named Eu4W8@EB-TFP.201 This material exhibited a turn-off signal for the detection of CG. CG quenched the characteristic emission of Eu3+ observed at 615 nm in the time-dependent luminescence spectra, which was absent for various other serum components, indicating the weakening of the luminescence of Eu4W8@EB-TFP upon the addition of CG.

Recent studies showed the potential opportunity of porous hybrid materials as electrochemical (EC) aptasensors for biosensing applications.202 The rational fabrication of various conductive hybrid porous materials with mixed metal nodes and electroactive organic moieties can serve as a platform for EC biosensors. The effective interaction between the aptamers and porous framework is important in the design of EC biosensors. For example, the specific binding of Zr(IV) with the phosphate groups of aptamers gives Zr-based materials (UiO-66 and UiO-66-NH2) an upper hand over others.203 With respect to dimensionality, 2D materials have an edge over their 3D counterparts given that they possess high permeability and more exposed active sites. Achieving a uniform size distribution with structural consistency of the hybrid porous materials and controllable doping of aptamers in the porous matrix are few of the major challenges in this domain. Hence, astute, contemporary synthetic strategies such as the use of microwaves and microemulsions in the design of new hybrid porous materials, in collaboration with nanotechnology and crystal engineering, can pave a path to overcome these difficulties.

4. Future perspectives

Here, we demonstrated various important strategies reported till date for the synthesis of organicâ€“inorganic hybrid porous materials, which will largely help in designing new functional frameworks using novel building blocks. Among the frontline applications, these hybrid porous materials offer ample scope that is yet to be fully explored, especially in the adsorptive removal of water pollutants and purification of industrial gases. The ease of post-synthesis surface functionalization of organic moieties has sparked huge opportunity in designing organicâ€“inorganic hybrid porous materials for adsorption, sensing, optical, biomedical and catalytic applications.

The union of both inorganic and organic materials in these porous hybrid systems will open numerous opportunities in cross-disciplinary applications. However, efforts should pursue achieving specific preferred morphologies, such as coreâ€“shell, heterostructure hybrids, 2D nanostructures, oriented self-assemblies and composite materials. New protocols have been developed for the synthesis of organometallic precursors, which will promote the synthesis of novel organicâ€“inorganic hybrid porous frameworks in future. Organocatalysis is an emerging area of research today,204,205 where hybrid PMO-based frameworks can be employed as a catalytic platform. Especially, the synthesis of chiral bridging organosilanes will help in designing suitable PMOs, which can contribute significantly in asymmetric organocatalysis. The successful use of fluorophoric building moieties in the hybrid frameworks of PMOs/MOFs and immobilization of transition metals on the surfaces of COFs/POPs can open new opportunities in photocatalysis and related optoelectronic applications. Hybrid porous materials by virtue of their large pore volume and surface functional groups can host photosensitizer molecules in their void spaces, and the resulting composites have huge scope in developing efficient solar cells.

Alternatively, the chemistry of nanoporous metal phosphonates is growing very rapidly and one can expect new applications of these hybrid materials in catalysis and biomedical applications in the future. There is a huge possibility of designing low-cost metal phosphonates using tailor-made bridging phosphonates, which have good potential to be explored as electrocatalysts for the hydrogen evolution and oxygen evolution reactions. The pyrolysis of composites of organic and inorganic nanostructured materials under controlled conditions is another challenging area of research in designing highly reactive hybrid porous nanocatalysts for photoelectrochemical water splitting and CO2 reduction reactions. Given that the demand for renewable energy and mitigation of greenhouse gases is continuously increasing, it is expected that these heterojunction nanocomposite materials will be explored intensively in the near future.
