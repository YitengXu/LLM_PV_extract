Toward sustainable and systematic recycling of spent rechargeable batteries

Abstract

Ever-growing global energy needs and environmental damage have motivated the pursuit of sustainable energy sources and storage technologies. As attractive energy storage technologies to integrate renewable resources and electric transportation, rechargeable batteries, including leadacid, nickelmetal hydride, nickelcadmium, and lithium-ion batteries, are undergoing unprecedented rapid development. However, the intrinsic toxicity of rechargeable batteries arising from their use of toxic materials is potentially environmentally hazardous. Additionally, the massive production of batteries consumes numerous resources, some of which are scarce. It is therefore essential to consider battery recycling when developing battery systems. Here, we provide a systematic overview of rechargeable battery recycling from a sustainable perspective. We present state-of-the-art fundamental research and industrial technologies related to battery recycling, with a special focus on lithium-ion battery recycling. We introduce the concept of sustainability through a discussion of the life-cycle assessment of battery recycling. Considering the forecasted trend of a massive number of retired power batteries from the forecasted surge in electric vehicles, their repurposing and reuse are considered from economic, technical, environmental, and market perspectives. New opportunities, challenges, and future prospects for battery recycling are then summarized. A reinterpreted 3R strategy entailing redesign, reuse, and recycling is recommended for the future development of battery recycling.

1. Introduction

Modern society faces both energy and environmental issues that can strongly affect human life and social development.1,2 The world energy consumption in 2017 was 586 quadrillion British thermal units (Btu, 1 quadrillion Btu = 293 TW h), of which renewable energy accounted for 13% (Fig. 1a). It is projected that the total world energy consumption will increase to 663 quadrillion Btu by 2030 and then to 736 quadrillion Btu by 2040.3 Although fossil fuels dominate the world's energy consumption now and will continue to do so in the near future, renewable energy is the fastest growing form of energy converted into electricity for use, and is predicted to rise from 78 to almost 130 quadrillion Btu over the period from 2017 to 2040.3 Among nonhydroelectric renewable energy sources, wind and solar are forecast to grow the most, reaching 2.5 and 1.4 trillion kW h in 2040, respectively (Fig. 1b and c).3 However, the use and development of renewable energy has two challenges from the perspectives of time and space. Namely, the generation of renewable energy is inherently intermittent, and has strong regional characteristics; that is, such power plants are typically built in remote areas. Therefore, advanced energy storage technologies are desperately needed to maximize the use of renewable energy.

In the last few decades, the greenhouse effect has become the largest environmental issue facing the international community.1,2 According to the data collected by the National Oceanic & Atmospheric Administration, the atmospheric abundance of carbon dioxide (CO2) has increased by 1.81 ppm per year on average over the period from 1979 to 2017, and this increase has been accelerating in recent years (Fig. 1d).4 The recent global CO2 abundance in May 2018 was 411.25 ppm, which was an increase by 1.6 ppm compared with the value measured in May 2017 (409.65 ppm).5 The Annual Greenhouse Gas Index (AGGI) is defined as the ratio of the total direct radiative forcing due to long-lived greenhouse gases (GHGs) for any year to that which was present in 1990, such that the AGGI of 1.41 in 2017 indicated an increase in total direct radiative forcing of 41% since 1990. Among the CO2 emission sectors, transportation has been the leading source of CO2 emission since 2000. It has been proved that electrified transportation using electricity generated from renewable energy has a great potential to lower GHG emissions.6 Thus, much effort has been expended to develop electric vehicles (EVs) and plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (PHEVs).

Because of their desirable features of flexible energy and power density, high energy efficiency, simple operation, and environmental friendliness, rechargeable batteries [e.g., lithium-ion batteries (LIBs), and lead (Pb)acid, nickelmetal hydride (NiMH), and nickelcadmium (NiCd) batteries] are important for applications in electrified transportation and large-scale electricity grid energy storage.710 The total global battery market size is forecast to reach over $100 billion in 2025 with a compound annual growth rate of 10% between 2010 and 2025.11 Among the four types of rechargeable batteries currently available, Pbacid batteries have the largest market share.12 The low cost and reliable performance of Pbacid batteries are anticipated to drive their further growth in the future.

As the most popular technology for energy storage in applications ranging from consumer electronics to EV and grids, it is expected that the LIB global market will be worth over $32 billion by 2020, with it enjoying the highest growth rate of all rechargeable battery types (>10%).13 Energy storage systems (ESSs) and EVs are predicted to constitute more than 80% of the LIB market. In particular, according to the statistics, the global electric car (including EVs and PHEVs) stock surpassed two million in 2016 and three million in 2017.14,15 This high annual growth rate is influenced by the booming Chinese electric car market, with the USA following it (Fig. 2a and b).15 This number is projected to range between nine million and 20 million by 2020 and between 40 million and 70 million by 2025.14 Such huge demand for LIBs will bring about a corresponding large number of spent LIBs.16 Driven by the booming EV industry, the quantities of retired LIB packs will exponentially increase by 2050 (Fig. 2c). Optimistic, middle, and pessimistic results have been estimated from different data sources.17 In 2035, the forecast number of available end-of-life (EOL) vehicle batteries ranges from a pessimistic value of 1.38 million to an optimistic value of 6.76 million.

Spent rechargeable batteries contain heavy metal elements, including nickel (Ni), cobalt (Co), and Pb, which are hazardous to human health and the environment if disposed of inappropriately.1820 It is important to note that Ni, Co, and Pb are all classified as carcinogenic and mutagenic materials.18 In addition to heavy metals, the organic and strong acid/alkaline electrolytes of rechargeable batteries are also polluting. Co is considered strategically important because it is widely used in industry and by the military.18 Thus, it is the most valuable element among the main heavy metals used in rechargeable batteries and its price has increased markedly since 2016, with an average price of $55496 ton1 in 2017.21,22 The latest average price of Co in May 2018 was $89940 ton1, the maximum value by far. As an essential raw material for the synthesis of cathode materials of LIBs, the price of battery-grade lithium carbonate (Li2CO3) has nearly tripled over the last decade, reaching as high as $25100 ton1 in China.23 The high content of Li in spent LIBs (27 wt%) can be considered as a Li resource. As calculated by Tarascon et al.,2 one ton of Li can be extracted from 28 tons of spent LIBs, which compares favorably with the consumption of conventional Li resources, like 250 tons of mineral or 750 tons of brine per ton of Li. Therefore, the recycling of spent rechargeable batteries is highly desirable and beneficial from both economic and environmental perspectives. Great effort has been made over the past decades to investigate the recycling of spent batteries using processes with high recovery efficiency, simple operation, low cost, and pollution-free features.2433 However, with the continuous development of battery materials and technologies, as well as changes in the battery application market, the recycling of spent batteries is also facing new challenges.3437

In this Review, we first briefly introduce the structure and composition of the four most common types of rechargeable batteries. Guided by the conventional recycling processes of the most widely used LIBs, the representative and latest technologies developed in the last two decades to recycle different components are discussed in detail. Having identified the similarities and differences between the recycling of Pbacid batteries, Ni-based batteries, and LIBs, the latest research progress in the recycling of Pbacid and Ni-based batteries is also discussed. The economic assessment of typical technologies runs through the discussion of the recycling processes. A summary of the existing battery recycling enterprises is presented, and the features of recycling technologies are highlighted for each enterprise. We then discuss the urgent need to assess the overall sustainability of certain battery recycling technology with the help of life-cycle assessment (LCA). As a future development trend, the concept of the second use of retired EV batteries is introduced, and its feasibility is analyzed from economic, technical, environmental, and market perspectives. The laws and regulations regarding battery recycling in the EU, USA, and China are briefly summarized. Finally, the future challenges and opportunities for battery recycling are considered.

2. Structure and composition of rechargeable batteries

Regardless of their chemistry, the four main types of rechargeable batteries share a similar structure and energy storage mechanism involving charge transfer reactions within the electrode materials, as illustrated in Fig. 3.38 The only difference between the batteries lies in their material composition and thus their electrochemical parameters.8 An LIB is a type of rechargeable battery in which lithium ions (Li+) move from the cathode to the anode during charging, and vice versa when discharging. Intercalated compounds are used as electrode materials in LIBs; usually lithium metal oxides, including LiCoO2 (LCO), LiNixCoyMnzO2 (NCM, x + y + z = 1), LiNixCoyAlzO2 (NCA, x + y + z = 1), LiMn2O4 (LMO), and lithium iron phosphate LiFePO4 (LFP) are as cathodes and graphite carbon materials as anodes.3941 The electrolyte, which allows Li+ to move through the separator, is composed of soluble lithium salt and non-aqueous organic solvents.42,43 It should be noted that the choice and weight percent of electrode materials in LIBs vary considerably because of the different compositions needed to meet the demands of high energy density or high power density.8

Pbacid batteries have been used as energy storage devices for more than a century since they were first invented in 1859 by Plant.39 Pbacid batteries still remain the main choice for automotive starting, lighting, and ignition applications because of their low cost and robust performance.13 Pbacid batteries are composed of a lead dioxide (PbO2) cathode, sponge metallic Pb anode, fiberglass mat separator, and sulfuric acid (H2SO4) solution electrolyte. All of these components are contained in a case that is typically made of polypropylene. During discharge, the PbO2 cathode is reduced to lead sulfate (PbSO4), while the Pb anode is oxidized to PbSO4. During charging, the two reactions take place in the opposite direction. The specific energy of a Pbacid battery is about 30 W h kg1 with a nominal cell voltage of 2 V.10 PbSO4 is the main component in a spent Pbacid battery paste, accounting for nearly 50% by weight. Lead oxides, including PbO and PbO2, together make up the remaining 50% of the paste.33

NiCd batteries with an alkaline potassium hydroxide (KOH) electrolyte possess an energy density about double that of Pbacid batteries. In NiCd batteries, nickel hydroxyl oxide (NiOOH) is used as the cathode and Cd as the anode. During discharge, NiOOH is reduced to nickel hydroxide [Ni(OH)2] at the cathode, and Cd is oxidized to cadmium hydroxide [Cd(OH)2] at the anode.44 The nominal cell voltage of a NiCd battery is 1.2 V.13 The advantages of NiCd batteries include their fast discharging capability and tolerance to overcharging and discharging, but they suffer from the major drawback of a memory effect. NiMH batteries are related to NiCd batteries but have a different anode material; instead of Cd, rare-earth metal hydrides are used as the anode, including a variety of valuable rare-earth elements, like lanthanum (La), niobium (Nb), and cerium (Ce).10,13 The electrolyte composition and cell voltage are the same as those of NiCd batteries, but their energy density is about 40% higher than that of NiCd batteries. During discharge, the NiOOH at the cathode is reduced to Ni(OH)2, which is the same reaction as that in NiCd batteries; the rare-earth metal hydride at the anode is oxidized. When charged, the reactions are reversed.44 The two most valuable components in Ni-based batteries are Ni and rare-earth elements, with contents of 30% and 810%, respectively.

3. Recycling strategies of different battery types and components

The goal of recycling processes is to separate the components of spent batteries into different fractions that can be reintroduced into the production of useful materials. In addition, the scrap volume is decreased and the hazardous emissions are properly controlled.45 In the case of LIBs, because Co, Li, and Ni are precious metals that are important in many industrial applications, the recovery of these metals is the primary objective in the recycling of spent LIBs. Current recycling technologies can be classified into two main categories: pyrometallurgy based on high-temperature pyrolysis and hydrometallurgy based on low-temperature solution chemical reaction.2,25,31,32 Some representative recycling processes and technologies are illustrated in Fig. 4.

3.1 Pretreatment

The LIB structure is complex with numerous components, so pretreatment processes are generally required to remove the case and to concentrate the valuable fraction, paving the way for subsequent pyrometallurgical or hydrometallurgical recycling.4648 Because of the differences in treatment scale and research focus, the pretreatment processes used for laboratory and industrial applications are slightly different, as shown in Fig. 5. It is worth noting that safety concerns should be emphasized during pretreatment processes, especially in industrial applications.47,49,50 As Diekmann47 summarized, there are three main kinds of potential hazards in spent LIBs disposal, namely electrical, fire and explosion, and chemical hazards. These hazards cumulate greatly with a larger energy density and processing capacity. Therefore, a safe and effective separation of different components is the main goal of pretreatment.

3.1.1 Manual pretreatment. In laboratory-scale studies, because considerable effort has been devoted to the subsequent pyrometallurgical or hydrometallurgical recycling process, pretreatment is usually conducted by a simple routine practice. That is, the individual spent cells are dismantled manually with knives and saws, in a process conducted using relatively simple protective measures to address safety concerns, including wearing safety glasses, masks, and gloves.51,52 The main challenge lies in the separation of current collectors and active materials, because these are made with the requirement of long-lasting adhesion. Three kinds of separation methods are usually used depending on the properties of the current collectors and binders.

The first method is mechanical separation, which can roughly separate current collectors and active materials through crushing and screening.50,5355 This method is often used in industry and will be discussed in detail in Section 3.1.2. The second method is to remove the adhesive binder by use of a solvent or calcination. Based on the similarityintermiscibility theory, the organic polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) binder can easily dissolve in certain organic solvents, among which N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) is the most effective one for PVDF.5658 However, the toxicity and high cost of NMP limit its use on a large scale. Greener solvents have been explored as an alternative to NMP. In particular, ionic liquids (ILs) are considered to be a promising candidate because of their numerous favorable features, including low vapor pressure, high thermal stability, and satisfactory solvating properties. ILs are generally composed of organic cations and inorganic or organic anions. It has been confirmed that ILs can aid waste recycling, such as in the dismantling of waste printed circuit boards.5962 Based on previous explorations, Zeng and Li63 developed a new method using a heated IL to separate cathodes and aluminum (Al) current collectors. 1-Butyl-3-methylimidazolium tetrafluoroborate ([BMIm][BF4]) was chosen to verify the feasibility of their method because of its suitable viscosity and excellent hydrophilicity. The results showed that a peel-off rate of 99% could be obtained when the experiment was conducted at 180 C for 25 min at 300 rpm. PVDF begins to decompose at 350 C and this is completed at 600 C.64,65 Therefore, calcination at a relatively low temperature range of 400600 C is preferred to burn off the PVDF binder. Almost no impurities are introduced into spent active materials after removing the PVDF binder by solvent dissolution. In contrast, a certain amount of F [in the form of lithium fluoride (LiF)] was found in the active materials after PVDF calcination, which was attributed to the reaction of released hydrofluoric acid (HF) with active materials.6567 The third method to separate active materials involves the dissolution of current collectors. For example, Al foil can dissolve in alkaline solution, while the cathode active materials and PVDF binder remain unchanged.68 However, Al foil cannot be recycled directly in its metallic form using this method.65,69

The purity of active materials obtained by manual pretreatment is relatively high because other components can be removed as a whole. The pretreatment method can minimize the effects of other factors, such as impurities, on the fundamental research of novel recycling processes. However, manual pretreatment is not suitable for industrial applications because of the low processing yield.

3.1.2 Mechanical pretreatment.
3.1.2.1 Dismantling and classification. For industrial applications where the amount of processing is huge and large volumes of battery packs are involved, mechanical treatments other than manual dismantling are feasible. In particular, when dealing with large LIB packs from EVs, preliminary dismantling to smaller modules or cells is recommended to avoid potential hazards. At present, because the architecture of packs from different manufacturers are quite different and a high voltage remains, most battery packs are dismantled manually by specialized technicians; however, this will be an inadequate approach to cope with the enormous quantity of spent battery packs anticipated in coming years70,71 and instead high-level equipment automation is urgently required to improve pretreatment efficiency in the future. Usually, recycling enterprises receive a mixture of spent batteries with different chemistries that require disposal. Some recycling processes are sensitive to the battery composition, which will have a negative effect on the recycling efficiency, so preliminary classification is recommended.50
3.1.2.2 Crushing. To decrease the volume of spent batteries and to concentrate the valuable components, a large number of small cells or modules obtained after sorting and dismantling are subjected to a crushing process, for which the potential hazards mentioned above can easily occur. During crushing, all the components are exposed, and the contact between the cathode and anode fragments can cause micro-short-circuits.47 Meanwhile, because of the violent friction and high-speed impact of crushing, this process can increase the temperature to as high as 300 C.72 As a result, the electrolyte can easily decompose and volatilize to generate toxic gas, even HF if humidity is present. Diekmann et al.47 investigated the gas components released during crushing and showed that the electrolyte components, including solvents and salts, as well as the state of health (SOH) of spent LIBs greatly influenced the final released gas, as shown in Fig. 6a and b. Dimethyl carbonate (DMC), ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC), and CO2 were the main gas components released during crushing. The overall amount of these components released at a SOH of 80% was lower than the gas release of uncycled cells. This is because DMC and EMC are reduced to form a solid electrolyte interface (SEI) during cycling and lithium alkyl carbonates (ROCO2Li, where R is an organic group) are reduced with traces of water and CO2 to form Li2CO3. Li et al.49 found that DMC and tert-amylbenzene were the two dominant organic vapor compounds detected during dismantling, with emission quantities of 4.298 and 0.749 mg h1, respectively, from one dismantled 18650 LIB cell. Usually, the gas emissions are collected and purified by an air filter (such as an alkaline solution or activated carbon) to avoid secondary pollution.

To minimize these hazards, deactivation or precautionary steps are carried out before or during crushing. Discharging in salt solution before crushing is used to remove the remaining energy and thus decrease the electrical hazard.49,64,69,7375 The influence of the sodium chloride (NaCl) concentration and discharging time on the discharge efficiency of LIBs were investigated by Li and colleagues.49 They found that 10 wt% NaCl solution achieved a high discharge efficiency of 72% in a reasonable time of 358 min. Moreover, relatively high levels of Na, Al, and Fe were detected in the concentrated NaCl solution following discharge. In addition to discharging in advance, crushing can also be conducted in salt solution, namely via wet crushing, to lower the reactivity and toxic emission of spent cells.76 Compared with dry crushing, wet crushing has a higher safety performance but more impurities are present in the fine fractions because of the scouring action of the water flow. The other two alternatives to deactivate spent batteries are crushing under an inert atmosphere [nitrogen (N2) or CO2] or at low temperatures (liquid N2) to prevent the release and explosion of flammable gas components during the crushing of battery cells or modules.47,50,51

Crushing changes not only the particle size and distribution, but also the properties of electrode components.45,55,72 As revealed by Zhang et al.55 in their detailed surface analysis of Co-enriched crushed products of spent LIBs by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), the fine fraction of LCO and graphite was coated by an outer layer of organic compounds that originated from the decomposition of the electrolyte during crushing. Moreover, the similar organic coating on LCO and graphite influenced the subsequent separation process.72


3.1.2.3 Sieving. Sieving or screening is usually used as a preliminary process to separate and concentrate the metallic fraction of spent batteries. Multiple sorting sieves are available depending on the properties of components, roughly including the fine fraction (<1 mm) and coarse fraction (>1 mm).50,54,55,76,77 Sieving provides a basic overview of the valuable metal distribution among the various particle sizes in a crushed sample. Typically, the coarse fraction mainly contains plastic, separator, Al foil, and Cu foil, whereas the cathode and anode active materials dominate the fine fraction. Wang and Gaustad50 used more elaborate sorting sieves to separate the crushed components of four types of cathode batteries into five size fractions: ultrafine (<0.5 mm), fine (0.51 mm), mid (12.5 mm), coarse (2.56 mm), and the largest fraction (>6 mm) (Fig. 6cf). They yielded a Co content of 82% in the ultrafine fraction, 68% in the fine fraction, and almost zero in the largest fraction in the case of batteries with an LCO cathode (Fig. 6c). They also calculated the economic value of each fraction in the four types of batteries. Although Co is the most valuable metal in terms of unit price of all the metals in LIBs, the potential recoverable value of Ni exceeds that of Co in mixed cathodes because of the higher content of Ni. In the case of LFP and LMO cathodes, Cu is the dominant valuable component in all fractions. The same researchers also found that the size fractions for different types of batteries showed a considerable variability in material concentration. Thus, sorting or classification by a cathode before pretreatment was suggested to decrease the uncertainty of the input materials and to improve the purity of the output products.
3.1.2.4 Separation. After crushing and sieving, the different components of spent LIBs have been separated and concentrated preliminarily. Then, based on their different physical characteristics, including thermal properties, density, magnetic characteristics, wettability, and electromagnetic behavior, a separation process is performed to further remove impurities.78

Thermal treatment, as discussed above in Section 3.1.1 on manual pretreatment, aims to remove the organic impurities in a fraction, including the binder, conductive carbon, and electrolytes. It should be noted that the calcination temperature and atmosphere strongly affect the pretreatment process and therefore must be controlled carefully. Calcination at too low a temperature (<600 C) is insufficient to decompose organic impurities, while too high a temperature will oxidize the Al current collector to form aluminum oxide (Al2O3) on the surface of active materials, which is detrimental in the subsequent leaching process.65,79,80 Calcination in air has little effect on the structure of lithium transition metal oxides, while the oxidation of Fe2+ to Fe3+ occurs in spent LFP materials.8183 By contrast, a vacuum or reducing thermal treatment atmosphere can lower the valence state of the transition metal ions in the cathode materials, which is beneficial to the subsequent leaching process.79,80 Sun and Qiu80 performed a detailed study of vacuum pyrolysis as a pretreatment for the recycling of spent LIBs. Their results showed that the cathode powder peeled completely from the Al foil under the optimal experimental conditions of 600 C, a vacuum evaporation time of 30 min, and residual gas pressure of 1.0 kPa. The main components of the separated cathode powder were LCO and cobalt oxide (CoO). Instead of the cobalt(II,III) oxide (Co3O4) phase generally detected in spent cathodes, CoO was speculated to originate from the decomposition of LCO during the vacuum pyrolysis process. CoO dissolves more readily in acid solution than Co3O4. Reducing thermal treatment in a N2 atmosphere also lowers the valence state of metal elements, thus decreasing the amount of reducing agents needed during the subsequent leaching process.79 Thermal treatment has the advantage of being a simple operation, but air pollution and energy consumption should be taken into consideration in practice.

Mixtures with different sizes and densities will form different states of motion in certain separation media, which is the underlying principle of the gravity separation method.46,47,53,84 For different components with the same particle size after sieving, a sufficient density difference is crucial to achieve effective gravity separation. Among the components in spent LIBs, the low-density parts mainly consist of the separator, plastics, and Al foil. Air velocity is another major factor that affects the separation efficiency. For example, in the spouted bed elutriation process investigated by Bertuol et al.,84 the crushed fractions were separated at three different air velocities of 10.210.5 m s1 for the first light fraction of polymers, Cu, and Al with smaller diameters, 10.613.0 m s1 for the second fraction of Cu and Al with larger diameters, and 13.020.7 m s1 for the third fraction of Al external casing. The overall loss in relation to the initial mass was only 3.3 wt%.

Based on the wettability difference between the cathode and anodeLCO is hydrophilic and graphite is hydrophobicthe separation of a cathode from an anode can be achieved through the flotation process.55,72,85 The only separation factor here is the different surface properties of the materials. Both LCO and graphite particles after crushing and sieving are coated by a layer of organic compounds (Fig. 7a), which comes from the decomposition of electrolytes during crushing, as discussed above.72 XPS analysis indicated that the surface layer mainly consisted of 75% organic compounds, 6% metal fluorides, 5% metal oxides, and 2% phosphates (Fig. 7bc).72 The organic layer needs to be removed in advance to guarantee effective flotation separation.

It was found that the separation of LCO and graphite by the flotation method was greatly enhanced in the presence of a Fenton reagent, which returned the original wettability of LCO and graphite (Fig. 7d).86 As shown in Fig. 7e, the C 1s spectrum of the sample after the use of Fenton reagent did not contain a peak from carbon black at 284.3 eV, and the intensity of the peaks originating from high oxidation states of carbon increased, indicating that the outer organic layer was oxidized and decomposed through reaction with the Fenton reagent. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of particles before and after modification (Fig. 7f and g) also confirmed the positive effect of the Fenton reagent. However, the separation efficiency of the Fenton-modified sample in flotation was not satisfactory. Further surface analysis by field-emission electron probe microanalysis showed that although the organic layer was decomposed, a new Fe-containing inorganic layer was formed after Fenton modification (Fig. 7h and i), which led to a similar hydrophobicity of all the particles and thus poor separation by flotation.87 Therefore, hydrochloric acid (HCl) was used to remove the iron hydroxide [Fe(OH)3] layer, and this improved Fenton-modified flotation approach resulted in a concentrate grade of 75% (Fig. 7j and k).

A simple grinding flotation method was developed by the same group.88 XPS analysis of the samples before and after grinding (Fig. 7l and m) showed that the content of graphite carbon on the surface increased substantially, while that of fluorinated functional groups decreased, indicating that graphite partly adhered to the LCO particles during the grinding process. A dry modification mechanism was proposed, in which LCO and graphite received different mechanical forces from the grinding media (Fig. 7n). The horizontal shear force and vertical rolling pressure broke the internal structure of graphite and exposed many new hydrophobic surfaces, whereas only part of the original surfaces of LCO was exposed. The enhanced wettability difference between LCO and graphite following grinding improved the concentrate grade of LCO and graphite to as high as 97.13% and 73.56%, respectively (Fig. 7o).88

Other separation methods using magnetic or electrostatic separation have been reported.46,73,77,78,89,90 In magnetic separation, magnetically susceptible materials, such as Fe and Co, can be separated through magnetic force. This method is usually used to remove nonmagnetic impurities, like plastics and separators.


3.1.2.5 Mechanochemical treatment. By definition, a mechanochemical (MC) reaction is a chemical reaction that is induced by mechanical energy. This coupling reaction includes mechanical breakage and the chemical behavior of mechanically stressed solids.91,92 High-energy milling, whether in a dry or wet medium, is the most widely used method to provide the mechanical force to induce chemical reaction. Various mill types are used, including ball, planetary, vibration, pin, and rolling mills. Planetary ball mills are particularly suited for MC reactions because of their high energy density, simple set-up and handling, and cleanability.91 MC reactions have been widely applied in mineral engineering, extractive metallurgy, chemical engineering, materials engineering, and waste management.9395 The main mechanisms of MC reactions are a particle size decrease, specific surface area increase, and structure breakage.91,92 Specifically, in the recycling of spent batteries, MC reactions are usually used as a pretreatment to improve the recycling yield in two ways:9698 the first is to break the crystal structure of cathode materials, which facilitates the subsequent leaching process so that it can occur even at room temperature (RT); the second is to react with other materials to form soluble compounds.

Zhang and co-workers verified the positive effect of the MC reaction in both cases. First, they investigated the influence of MC treatment on the room-temperature acid leaching of valuable metals from spent LiCo0.2Ni0.8O2.97 The MC process was conducted in a planetary mill with Al2O3 powder. The results showed that the spent crystalline LiCo0.2Ni0.8O2 was pulverized and became amorphous after 60 and 240 min, respectively. Accordingly, in the subsequent leaching process with nitric acid (HNO3), more than 90% of the Li, Ni, and Co were extracted at RT. In a later study, they used polyvinyl chloride (PVC) as a grinding aid with LCO in a planetary ball mill for 30 min, which gave amorphous lithium chloride (LiCl) and crystalline cobalt chloride (CoCl2) as the products of the MC reaction (Fig. 8a and b).96 Finally, the LiCl and CoCl2 were extracted in water at RT; the extraction yields reached over 90% for Co and nearly 100% for Li. PVC is an excellent chlorine atom donor with a high chlorine content. Meanwhile, the carbon derived from the PVC was found to reduce Co3+ in LCO into water-soluble Co2+ in CoCl2 during the MC reaction.

According to previous studies, grinding aids play an important role in the MC process. The multiple elements in the electrode materials of LIBs provide more possibilities for the investigation of grinding aids. Guan et al.99 explored the feasibility of using metallic Fe powder as a grinding aid to improve the acid leaching of metal ions after the MC reaction (Fig. 8ce). The influences of parameters like the Fe to LCO mass ratio, rotation speed, and MC reaction time on the leaching of Li and Co with HNO3 were investigated in detail, and an MC mechanism was proposed based on the results of X-ray diffraction (XRD), SEM, and XPS analyses. The results revealed that the crystal structure of LCO was converted to an amorphous state after the MC process, which enhanced the subsequent leaching of Li and Co. The greatly improved leaching of Co was mainly attributed to the MC reaction-induced reduction of its oxidation state from 3+ to 2+ by Fe. Grinding aids with a chelation capability were also investigated to convert lithium oxides to more soluble compounds. Wang et al.100 used ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) as a grinding aid that could chelate with Li and Co from LCO (Fig. 8fh). Then, a simple water leaching process was used to leach Li and Co, which displayed leaching efficiencies of 99% and 98%, respectively. The reaction mechanism of EDTA and LCO during grinding was explained by the enhancement of the reactant activity, which facilitated the formation of stable and soluble LiEDTA and CoEDTA complexes. However, the high cost of EDTA hinders its large-scale use as a grinding agent. Thus, more economical grinding aids composed of PVC and Fe powder were explored by the same group to separate Li and Co from spent LCO cathodes (Fig. 8ik).101 During grinding with PVC and Fe powder, Li reacted with PVC to form soluble LiCl, while Co remained as Co2O3 in the water leaching residue. Finally, the residue was calcined at 800 C for 2 h in air to yield magnetic CoFe4O6. The combined grinding aids not only improved the water leaching process, but also selectively separated Co and Li simultaneously.

For the most part, the use of MC reactions is limited to the pretreatment before the leaching and disposal of spent lithium oxide cathodes. Actually, it is expected that MC reactions could play a more important role in dealing with spent LFP cathodes, which are difficult to dissolve in the traditional acid leaching process. Yang et al.102 recently developed an MC reaction as a pretreatment to selectively recycle Fe and Li from spent LFP batteries (Fig. 9). EDTA2Na was used as a chelating grinding aid. The effects of various parameters during the MC pretreatment and acid leaching processes were systematically investigated, including the activation time, mass ratio of cathode powder to additive, acid concentration, solid-to-liquid (S/L) ratio, and leaching time. Analysis of the MC reaction mechanism using XRD and Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) analyses indicated that the PO/PO4 bond/tetrahedral structures and the (311) plane of the LFP cathode were probably destroyed during MC activation, as shown in Fig. 9a and b. The degraded structure facilitated the subsequent acid leaching process, which was conducted at RT. Following MC reaction pretreatment, the leaching efficiencies of Fe and Li improved markedly to 97.67% and 94.29%, respectively, compared with 40% for Fe and 60% for Li in the case without the MC reaction (Fig. 9c). Finally, the Fe and Li species after leaching were recovered by selective precipitation as FePO42H2O and Li3PO4, respectively (Fig. 9d). After the whole process, 93.05% of the Fe and 82.55% of the Li were recovered from the spent LFP batteries. The above examples reveal that the MC reaction can lower the acid and energy consumption of leaching processes, and can improve the recovery selectivity of Li in LFP battery recycling. This research provides a closed-loop recycling process for spent LFP batteries that has great potential for industrial application.

Because of its simplicity and economical use of solvents, mechanochemistry is considered practical and environmentally friendly for the recycling of spent batteries. It is worth noting that the high energy consumption of ball milling needs to be balanced with its productivity in the future development and application of this approach.

Most of the above-mentioned pretreatment methods are derived from the mining industry, for which the research methodology and equipment are mature and can be generalized for the recycling of spent batteries.78 Generally, because of the complexity of the battery composition and the limitation of each single method, a series of methods is used in combination to obtain optimal separation results.25

3.2 Pyrometallurgical processes

The pyrometallurgical process here refers to a single pyrolysis treatment of spent battery components. The products after pyrolysis fall into two main categories: electrode materials and alloys. The former involves the regeneration of spent cathodes after supplementation with a Li source, and will be discussed in Section 3.3.4. To form alloys, high temperatures of about 1000 C are required. Co-, Fe-, and Ni-based alloys are the most common alloy products.103,104 Because of their high energy consumption and emission of hazardous gases, pyrometallurgical processes are not recommended for use in laboratory-scale research. In contrast, many recycling enterprises use the pyrometallurgical process because of its simplicity and high productivity.

In situ reduction pyrolysis is becoming a research hotspot in pyrometallurgical recycling.90,105107 For instance, Xu et al.90 developed a novel and environmentally friendly roasting process to recycle Co and Li2CO3in situ. A mixture of LCO and graphite powder was directly calcined at 1000 C under a N2 atmosphere for 30 min. Products including Co, Li2CO3, and graphite were formed. The feasibility of reaction between LCO and graphite was confirmed by thermodynamic analysis (Fig. 10a). The product mixture was separated by a wet magnetic separation process. In subsequent research, the feasibility of the in situ process for recycling spent LIBs with an LMO cathode was confirmed.106,107 The mixed electrode materials of LMO and graphite after mechanical separation were converted into MnO and Li2CO3in situ at 800 C for 45 min under oxygen-free conditions. Subsequent water leaching of the residue recovered 91.3% of the Li in the battery as Li2CO3. Finally, the filter residue was burned in air to remove graphite, and Mn3O4 with a purity of 95.11% was obtained. The conversion mechanism of mixed LMO and graphite powder under enclosed vacuum conditions was proposed to entail a three-step collapse process of the cubic spinel LMO during which Li and O were released, and then Li2CO3 along with MnO were formed as products (Fig. 10b).106 The economic profitability of this process was assessed in a complex way, which included an analysis of the depreciation cost of equipment, electric power consumption, cost of equipment maintenance, water consumption, and labor costs. It required proper assumptions and efficient relevant data information. Assuming ten tons of spent 18650 LMO batteries are recycled per day, and the cost of factory construction and the revenue of the recovered Al, Cu, and Fe are excluded, the calculated collection and transportation cost and recycling cost were $4110 and $2368.65, respectively. Along with revenues of $8587, the profit was calculated to be $2108.35. The concept of in situ recycling developed in the above example was proved to be universal for other mixed cathodes of LCO and NCM (Fig. 10c). A deep understanding of the structure of electrode materials underlies this novel recycling process. Combined with the simplicity of calcination, this process shows great potential for industrial-scale application in the future.

3.3 Hydrometallurgical technologies

Compared with pyrometallurgical technologies, hydrometallurgical ones are considered to be more sustainable because of their features of limited waste emission and high metal selectivity, recycling efficiency, and content of value-added products.2,18,25 Although some challenges still exist, such as addressing the complex operation steps and waste water emission, it is agreed that hydrometallurgical technologies have a greater potential to realize sustainability than pyrometallurgical ones and will ultimately prevail in the recycling of spent batteries. The hydrometallurgical recycling process of spent LIBs usually entails low-temperature leaching, purification, and separation to obtain the final products.108
3.3.1 Leaching. As a standard and indispensable procedure in the hydrometallurgical recycling process, leaching is generally used to dissolve metals in the spent materials from the solid state into solution for further processing. Leaching can be considered as a pretreatment step before the subsequent purification and separation process. Therefore, the leaching efficiency strongly influences the overall recycling of metals.

There are two types of leaching processes, alkali leaching and acid leaching; the latter attracts more attention than the former because of its higher efficiency. Alkali leaching with an ammonia-based system [ammonia (NH3), ammonium carbonate ((NH4)2CO3), ammonium sulfate ((NH4)2SO4), ammonium chloride (NH4Cl), or other alkaline reagents] is relatively selective for specific elements, such as Ni, Co, and Li, because of the formation of stable metal ammonia complexes.109111 Ku et al.110 used an alkali leaching system with NH3 and (NH4)2CO3 as the leaching reagents and ammonium sulfite [(NH4)2SO3] as the reducing agent to investigate the leaching behavior of Ni, Co, Mn, Al, and Cu. They found that Co and Cu were leached out completely as they can form stable Co(NH3)62+ and Cu(NH3)42+ complex ions. Ni leached out moderately, while Mn and Al hardly leached out at all. The unstable nature of Mn and Al in ammonia-based solution resulted in the formation of the corresponding Al2O3, MnCO3, and Mn oxides, as revealed in the XRD pattern of the leaching residue. Zheng and colleagues109 developed a different ammonia-based leaching system that used NH3 and (NH4)2SO4 act as the leaching solution and sodium sulfite (Na2SO3) as the reducing agent. Similarly, Co, Ni, and Li exhibited high leaching efficiencies of over 98.6%, whereas that of Mn was only 1.36%. (NH4)2Mn(SO3)2H2O was found in the residue, which was explained by the reduction of Mn4+ into Mn2+ and subsequent precipitation. Study of the leaching kinetics indicated that the leaching process was controlled by a chemical reaction.

Unlike alkali leaching, acid leaching can dissolve almost all transition metal oxides into solution.112 The development of acids and reducing agents used in the acid leaching process is depicted in Fig. 11. In its early stage of research, acid leaching generally involved the use of strong inorganic acids, like HCl,113116 H2SO4,45,69,117121 and HNO3.122125 Generally, excess acid is used in the leaching process to guarantee high efficiency, which thus results in a large amount of waste acid. Recently, Li et al.126 developed a selective leaching process using stoichiometric H2SO4 and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) as an oxidant to leach Li from spent LFP; Fe and P remained in the residue as FePO4. The leaching efficiencies of Li, Fe, and P in this process were 96.85%, 0.027%, and 1.95%, respectively. Then, 95.66% of the Li in the leaching solution was precipitated as Li3PO4 through reaction with Na3PO4. This novel method markedly lowered the amount of waste acid and provided a new approach to recycle LFP.

Given that leaching with strong inorganic acids could cause considerable secondary pollution, such as toxic gas emission (Cl2, SOx, and NOx) and waste acid solution, Li and co-workers52,127132 proposed an environmentally friendly recycling process using organic acids, including citric acid, malic acid, and succinic acid, to replace the typically used acids without sacrificing the leaching efficiency. Organic acids have the advantage of biodegradability, making them easy to dispose of.133137 Additionally, they usually possess chelating or complexing properties, which offers possibilities for the subsequent recycling process. For example, citric acid has a relatively strong acidity and chelating property and was the first organic acid applied in the acid leaching of spent LCO cathodes.128 High leaching efficiencies of more than 90% for Co and nearly 100% for Li were achieved under mild leaching conditions.

Based on the necessity of the redox reaction of metals, ascorbic acid, which can act as both a leaching and reducing agent, was investigated to simplify the leaching process.52 The optimum leaching conditions were determined to be an ascorbic acid concentration of 1.25 M, leaching temperature of 70 C, leaching time of 20 min, and S/L ratio of 25 g L1. Under these experimental conditions, as much as 94.8% of the Co and 98.5% of the Li were recovered at a relatively low temperature in a short time. Another attractive organic acid is oxalic acid (H2C2O4), which has a relatively strong acidity and reducibility. Oxalic acid can dissolve Li into solution and precipitate Co as oxalates because of their different solubilities, thus separating Li and Co directly through leaching.138140

Apart from the acids used directly, bioleaching is another favorable method to produce acids (both inorganic and organic acids) for the leaching process.141147 The principle of bioleaching involves microorganisms producing acids through metabolism to dissolve metals from spent materials.148152 Certain kinds of bacteria and fungi possess the capability for use in bioleaching. For example, the chemolithotrophic and acidophilic Acidithiobacillus ferrooxidans bacteria can use elemental sulfur and Fe2+ to produce H2SO4 and Fe3+ in the leaching medium.141,145 The fungus Aspergillus niger exhibited good potential for the production of a high concentration of organic acids and has been proven useful for the bioleaching of spent LIBs.143,144 At present, the bioleaching process is limited by its vulnerability to pollution and long period needed for microorganism culture. However, it might prove the most environmentally friendly method for the recycling of spent LIBs in the future.

There are also some other potential sources of acids. Recently, Liu and Zhang153 used PVC as a source of HCl through the dechlorination of PVC during subcritical water oxidation. The produced HCl then facilitated metal leaching at the same time. This co-treatment process of both spent LIBs and PVC achieved high leaching efficiencies of 98% for Li and 95% for Co. The concept of waste utilization to produce valuable materials has been applied in the regeneration of electrode materials as well.154

In addition to acids, reducing agents also play an important role in improving leaching efficiencies. This is because metals in lower valence states dissolve more readily. For instance, Co2+ is much more water soluble than Co3+. The types of reducing agents used include inorganic, organic, and metallic materials.117,155168 H2O2 is the most commonly used reducing agent in the leaching process because of its low cost and high efficiency.117,155,156 However, H2O2 is unstable and easily decomposes during exposure to light and/or heat. Later, glucose was explored as an alternative green reducing agent to H2O2.54,157,158,163,169 Meng et al.169 speculated that glucose was oxidized to form monocarboxylic acids, including gluconic, tartaric, oxalic, and formic acids, which promoted the leaching of Co from LCO. Pagnanelli et al.158 found that postponing glucose addition during leaching with H2SO4 using glucose as a reducing agent improved the leaching efficiencies of Li and Co to 92% and 88%, respectively, compared with that obtained when glucose was added initially (60% for both metal ions). Analysis of the concentration changes of the glucose intermediates indicated that this was caused by the shift of the oxidative pathways at different leaching times. When glucose was added at the beginning of leaching, the intermediate arabic acid was formed and accumulated, making the oxidation a rate-limiting process. In contrast, if glucose was added after leaching for 2 h, the reductive intermediate glyoxylic acid was formed, which increased the reducibility of glucose. Jouli and co-workers162 recently examined the feasibility of Cu and Al current collectors as reducing agents in the leaching of NCM. Cu and Al were chosen because they are frequently found in spent cathode materials. The results showed that high leaching efficiencies of >90% were obtained with 1 M H2SO4 at 30 C in the presence of Cu with an NCM/Cu ratio of 1:1.2 (w/w) or in the presence of Al with an NCM/Al ratio of 1:0.7 (w/w). These novel reducing agents showed environmentally friendly features, including a low energy demand and toxic gas emissions.

Now that the optimal leaching conditions have been well studied, researchers are shifting their focus to investigation of the leaching mechanisms, including the thermodynamics and kinetics aspects (Fig. 13). Thermodynamics research ranges from theoretical analysis to experimental testing.118,139,160,161,170172 Specifically, the theoretical calculation of certain reactions can be used to explore reaction trends and possible leaching products. For instance, the Pourbaix diagrams, also known as EhpH diagrams (Eh: the voltage potential with respect to the standard hydrogen electrode), of MH2O (M = Li, Ni, Co, and Mn) systems are useful to understand the stable regions of different phases in aqueous solutions.118 The EhpH diagram of the CoH2O system showed that the Co3+ phase cannot dissolve in strong acid solutions until the redox potential reaches +1.84 V, indicating a reducing agent is needed to facilitate the leaching process. Similarly, spinel structures containing Mn(III) and Mn(IV) require a strong reducing condition and strong acid solution to form a soluble Mn(II) phase.118 On the other hand, the high bonding energy of FePO in the olivine-structured LFP material makes it hard to dissolve in strong acid solution even with a reducing agent.208 Theoretical calculation of the Gibbs free energy change (rG) can predict the possible trends and products of different leaching reactions.139,171 The leaching mechanism can also be analyzed from a mechanistic viewpoint, which is based on an understanding of the crystal structure of electrode materials.173 Various characterization methods, like XRD, SEM, energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy, FTIR spectroscopy, Raman spectroscopy, and ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy have been used to confirm leaching reactions.160,161,170,172,174

3.3.2 Solvent extraction. Solvent extraction is the process of using a two-phase system, normally organic and aqueous phases, to separate different metal ions by their uneven distribution in the two phases. The effectiveness of solvent extraction can be determined by the extraction yield and phase separation performance, and several factors are used to characterize these two performance indicators (Fig. 14). First is the distribution ratio (D), which is calculated as the ratio of the concentration of certain metal ions in the organic phase to that in the aqueous phase at equilibrium. A larger D value indicates a higher extraction yield of metal ions.189191 The pH1/2 value and separation factor (SF) are used to determine the separation performance of different metal ions. The pH1/2 value is the equilibrium pH value when 50% of the metal ions are extracted, while the SF value between A and B is calculated according to the equation SF = DA/DB. Normally, the larger the pH1/2 value (pH1/2(A)pH1/2(B)), the better the separation performance between A and B metal ions.192 Regarding SF, generally, SF > 10 means a possible separation, SF > 100 indicates good separation, and SF > 1000 suggests excellent separation.189
Fig. 14 Illustration of the solvent extraction process, mechanism analysis, and factors related to extraction, scrubbing, and stripping.

In the recycling of spent batteries, solvent extraction is usually performed after leaching to separate certain metal ions or to remove impurities from the aqueous leaching solution.192,193 The most commonly used solvent extractants include bis-(2,4,4-trimethylpentyl)phosphinic acid (Cyanex 272),51,184,194200 di-(2-ethylhexyl)phosphoric acid (D2EHPA),135,190,194,201 and 2-ethylhexyl phosphoric acid mono-2-ethylhexyl ester (PC-88A),189,202 among which Cyanex 272 attracts the most attention because of its high selectivity for the separation of Co.183,185 PC-88A is used to extract Co and D2EHPA to extract Mn, whereas Li is difficult to extract from such mixed-metal leaching solution. During the extraction process, several parameters, including the extraction system, equilibrium pH, extractant concentration, organic/aqueous (O/A) ratio, saponification, temperature, and time, can affect the extraction performance (Fig. 14).

The experimental design needs to be optimized to find the best extraction conditions, as summarized in Table 2. Some basic conclusions can be drawn from these results. The extraction process is usually performed at room temperature and can be finished in a short time (within 30 min), which are both advantages of the solvent extraction method. Meanwhile, the pH of all the extraction experiments indicate that an acid solution is found at the equilibrium state. In a conventional extraction process, the extraction of a single metal is preferable to obtain a high-purity product. However, this usually requires multiple extraction stages because of the similar chemical properties of transition metal ions.196,202,203 Recently, Yang et al.204 developed a co-extraction and co-precipitation process to avoid the separation of metal ions. Almost 100% of the Mn, 99% of the Co, and 85% of the Ni were co-extracted and separated from Li using D2EHPA in kerosene. The optimal extraction conditions were investigated and are shown in Fig. 15ad. After stripping and adjusting the element ratio, Ni1/3Co1/3Mn1/3(OH)2 precursors and the corresponding NCM cathode materials were obtained by co-precipitation and calcination. The remaining Li in the aqueous solution was precipitated as Li2CO3. This approach avoided the complicated separation of Ni, Co, and Mn, and provided a new idea for the extraction of multiple elements.

For a complex solution with multiple metal ions, mixtures of extractants are usually used instead of a single extractant. This is because metal extraction with higher selectivity can be achieved from the synergistic effects of multiple extractants. The synergistic enhancement coefficient R was calculated as R = Dmixture/(Da + Db).192,205,206 A value of R larger than 1 means the mixed system shows synergistic enhancement. Cheng et al.192 reported that Cu extraction was enhanced after the addition of Acorga M5640 (5-nonylsalicylaldoxime) to Ionquest 801 (2-ethyl-hexyl phosphoric acid mono-2-ethylhexyl ester), as shown in Fig. 15e and f. These organic solutions play different roles in this mixed system, with Ionquest 801 acting as an extractant and Acorga M5640 as a synergist.

Interfacial chemical reactions are considered to be the main extraction mechanism, and involve the release of protons (H+) during the dissociation of acidic extractants at the O/A interface. Slope analysis, usually for the relationship of logD vs. pHe or logD vs. log[H2A2](org) (H2R2 is the dimeric form of the extractant), is commonly used to determine the number of reacted extractant molecules in the extraction process.189,195,197,198 For instance, during the extraction of Co with Cyanex 272, the slope of a plot of logD vs. pHe plot was 1.93, which indicated that two H+ were released with the extraction of each Co2+ into the organic phase (Fig. 15g). Based on the reaction equation, a plot of logD vs. log[H2A2](org) with a slope of 3 revealed that three molecules of dimeric Cyanex 272 solvated one Co2+ to form CoA2(HA)4 in the organic phase (Fig. 15h).195

To determine the required number of extraction or stripping stages, an O/A ratio isotherm, namely a McCabeThiele diagram, can be constructed.190,195,201 As shown in Fig. 15i and j, it can be predicted that three stages will be required for Mn extraction at an O/A ratio of 1, and two stages will be needed for Mn stripping from a loaded organic solution at an O/A ratio of 2.190 Following the extraction, there are two more necessary steps under normal conditions. One is the scrubbing step to remove the co-extracted impurities and the other is stripping to transfer the metal ions from the organic phase to aqueous solution for final treatment. H2SO4 is the most commonly used stripping agent.

Solvent extraction is widely used in industrial applications and can provide products with high purity, but it has the disadvantages of complex operation and high solvent cost. Although there is still much research to be done, current knowledge of the extraction of metals in different media is helpful for researchers investigating the extraction of transition metals within the acidic environment (mainly sulfate-based media) of spent LIB recycling. Future research should focus on the use of cheap solvents and the closed-loop utilization of solvents.

3.3.3 Chemical precipitation. Like solvent extraction, chemical precipitation is used to separate metal ions or remove impurities (Fig. 16).113,136 The separation mechanism of chemical precipitation relies on the different solubilities of metal compounds at certain pH. Generally, transition metal hydroxides and oxalates have much lower solubility compared with that of the corresponding lithium compounds. Meanwhile, impurity metal ions, like Fe3+, Al3+, and Cu2+, usually precipitate at a relatively lower pH (Fig. 16a).186,208,209 Thus, it is reasonable to remove impurities first to avoid co-precipitation contamination in the subsequent separation process. The transition metal ions are then precipitated, leaving Li+ in the solution to be recycled. Based on the different solubilities of compounds, the most commonly used precipitants include sodium hydroxide (NaOH), H2C2O4, ammonium oxalate [(NH4)2C2O4], sodium carbonate (Na2CO3), and sodium phosphate (Na3PO4), which can react with transition metal ions and Li+ to form insoluble precipitates of transition metal hydroxides, carbonates, or oxalates, and lithium carbonate, phosphate, or fluoride (Fig. 16bd and Table 3).136,157,159,160,188,210212 As well as the pH-related parameters discussed above, other parameters, such as the concentration of metal ions and the temperature, also affect the chemical precipitation process. For example, to maximize Li recovery and purity, the raffinate is concentrated to increase its Li+ concentration above 20 g L1.187 Meanwhile, the precipitation of Li+ with saturated Na2CO3 solution is conducted at 90 C, because the solubility of Li2CO3 in aqueous solution is inversely proportional to temperature.137,157,181,210 Some metal ions can selectively form particular precipitates. The operating conditions used for chemical precipitation are generally determined by trial and error. However, the precipitation tendency of each metal ion can be obtained through theoretical simulation. Ma et al.215 simulated the precipitation properties of Ni, Co, and Mn in an OHNH3CO32 system by calculating the rG of all the precipitation reactions. The effects of pH, the initial metal concentration, carbonate concentration, and NH3 concentration on the precipitation process were investigated. For example, when the initial metal concentration was 0.19 M, the carbonate concentration was 0.19 M, and the NH3 concentration was 1.25 M, and the precipitation order of metal ions was Mn > Co > Ni, with the carbonate precipitation tendency being stronger than that of hydroxide precipitation (Fig. 16fh). The same group also investigated the effect of the leaching solution system on chemical precipitation.214 When the leaching system contained a chelating agent like citric acid, the complexing effect of citric acid changed the precipitation properties of the metal ions, making them more difficult to separate by precipitation in a citric acid system than in an H2SO4 system (Fig. 16ik).136,214 Another theoretical prediction method for chemical precipitation is the solidliquid equilibrium (SLE) phase diagram. Cai and colleagues216 used an SLE phase diagram to optimize chemical precipitation in the recycling of LFP and LiCoxMn1xO2. Specifically, the optimal amount of NaOH was determined from the SLE phase diagram to obtain both a high purity and high yield of precipitated FePO4 instead of the Fe(OH)3 impurity. The selective precipitation of cobalt sulfide (CoS) and manganese sulfide (MnS) can be realized by controlling the amount of sodium sulfide (Na2S). Hence, theoretical investigations can help to optimize precipitation reactions in a rational way instead of by trial and errors as used previously.

In its early stage of research, chemical precipitation only involved the separation of Co2+ and Li+, which are the main metal ions found in cathode materials. With the increasing application and market share of NCM and NCA cathodes, the leaching solution has now become more complicated, often containing multiple metal ions. Moreover, Ni, Co, and Mn possess similar chemical properties, so they often co-precipitate and are thus difficult to separate by ordinary chemical precipitation. The co-precipitation of Ni, Co, and Mn was thus developed to directly synthesize carbonate or hydroxide precursors as raw materials for the production of electrode materials.73,74,217 This approach can reintroduce all the transition metal ions in a leaching solution into the LIB production chain without the need for separation to produce electrode materials with high added value. The details of co-precipitation will be discussed later in Section 3.3.4. Chemical precipitation is widely used because of its simple operation and low cost. However, the operating conditions need to be precisely controlled to obtain the target products with high purity in the case of co-precipitation with other metal ions.

3.3.4 Other synthetic methods. In addition to the main chemical processes discussed above, other methods, such as electrodeposition, adsorption, and crystallization, have also been investigated for battery recycling.218220 Electrodeposition uses two electrodes to supply energy to the leaching solution to induce redox reactions of the ions in the solution. Metallic films, alloys, and multilayers with specific structures and morphologies can be electrodeposited by controlling the pH, current density, and voltage.122,123,221225 Electrodeposition has the advantage of achieving a high product purity because it does not introduce other impurities, but it has a high energy consumption because of the use of electricity.

Selective adsorption can also achieve the separation and recovery of metal ions. Most research focuses on the adsorption of Li+, because many metal oxides have high selectivity and adsorption capacity for Li+, and are referred to as lithium-ion sieves.210,226228 The most promising lithium-ion sieve is manganese oxide derived from spinel lithium manganese oxide; its selective adsorption feature originates from the memory effect of the sites formed after Li extraction. Modification, including acid treatment and surface coating, can improve the adsorption capacity of the sieves.229

The solutions left after H2SO4 leaching or stripping can be crystallized to obtain crystals of sulfates.68,185 It was found that the purity grade of CoSO4H2O crystals obtained by crystallization relied on the water evaporation rate; whereby higher evaporation rates of water led to contamination of the final solid with Al and Li. The optimal water evaporation rate determined after weighing the crystallization rate and purity of the product was 85%.68

3.3.5 Resynthesis of cathode materials and other high-value-added products. To satisfy the growing demand for high-energy and low-cost LIBs, a wide variety of cathode materials, in terms of the types and elements, are being developed and commercialized. As a result, the separation of multiple metal elements in the recycling process using an extraction or precipitation method is becoming much more complex.73,208 Great effort is being made to resynthesize new cathode materials to avoid the need for separation process and yo maximize the use of metals in spent materials. More importantly, from the viewpoints of economy and energy consumption, this kind of closed-loop recycling can lower the cost by producing high-value-added products and can also decrease the total cradle-to-gate energy consumption of LIBs according to LCA analysis.230,231 Heat treatment, co-precipitation, solgel, and relithiation are the main resynthesis methods, and the resynthesized cathode materials include LCO, LFP, and NCM. Based on the properties of different cathode materials, every resynthesis method has its own applications (Fig. 17).

The heat-treatment method involves the direct solid-state calcination of spent cathode materials like LCO and LFP with a simple elemental composition (Fig. 18).64,75,232237 Song et al.64 showed that the resynthesized cathodes exhibited satisfactory electrochemical performance compared with that of commercial ones (Fig. 18ac). The improved electrochemical properties are associated with the repair of the layered structure during the high-temperature calcination process. Zhang and co-workers234 continued to investigate the regeneration of LFP through the heat-treatment method. FePO4, Li3PO4, Fe2O3, and P2O5 existed as impurities in the sample before heat treatment. After pretreatment, the spent LFP cathode powder was directly heat treated at 150800 C for 1 h under Ar/H2. Because impurities easily form during inhomogeneous element diffusion, the resynthesis of NCM and NCA materials with multiple metal elements cannot be achieved by this calcination method. Multiple metal ions can be mixed homogeneously at the molecular level using these chemical solution methods. Generally, co-precipitation is carried out in a hydroxide or carbonate system at the optimal pH of 11 or 8, respectively (Fig. 20).74,213,217,238241 Wang et al.217 successfully resynthesized a high-performance Ni1/3Mn1/3Co1/3(OH)2 precursor from leaching solution by carefully controlling the co-precipitation reaction parameters. When the pH reached 11, almost no metal ions were left in the solution, indicating the complete hydroxide co-precipitation of Ni, Co, and Mn.208 The final LiNi1/3Mn1/3Co1/3O2 cathode material had a uniform size distribution and spherical shape (Fig. 20a and b). Compared with the conventional co-precipitation method used to synthesize new cathode materials, the use of waste leachate for resynthesis requires more consideration. For instance, waste leachate normally contains Fe, Al, Cu, and Mg impurities, which need to be removed to a tolerance threshold level.213,238,242 It is believed that impurity concentrations in LIB cathode materials should be less than 100 ppm.73 However, trace amounts of Al, Cu, and Mg can improve the electrochemical performance. As revealed by Sa et al.,238 a small amount of Cu (2.5%) had a positive effect on the capacity retention of NCM materials (Fig. 21ad). The mechanism underlying this improvement was speculated to originate from the occupation of Mn sites by Cu, which slightly changed the lattice parameters during Li insertion/extraction. Similarly, the threshold concentration of Mg impurity in leachate was explored by Weng and colleagues.213 They found that Mg was doped in Co sites and that Mg2+ in the resynthesized cathode material Li[(Ni1/3Co1/3Mn1/3)1xMgx]O2 was beneficial to cycling performance when x 0.01 (C 360 mg L1), as illustrated in Fig. 21eh. In addition to the impurity issue, the concentration of transition metal ions in leachate (<1 M) is usually much lower than that used in the traditional co-precipitation synthesis of precursors (2 M), and thus needs to be concentrated by evaporation.217 Moreover, although Li in the leachate is theoretically not supposed to precipitate, its effect on the final products should be verified quantitatively. Given the comprehensive research discussed above, co-precipitation recycling technology exhibits great potential for industrial applications in terms of both performance and economy.

The solgel method is another universal approach to synthesize cathode materials. Because a chelation agent is essential in the solgel method, organic acid leaching solutions are feasible for this method, with the organic acids being directly used as the chelation agents.155,243246 Different from co-precipitation, Li+ in the solution can all be reused as a Li source without separation, and the particle size of the resynthesized materials is usually smaller (in the range of 100300 nm) than that obtained from co-precipitation. Citric acid is a commonly used chelating agent in conventional solgel synthesis, so it is natural to use citric acid as a leaching agent and then as a chelation agent in the following solgel resynthesis process. For example, Yao et al.244 used citric acid as both a leaching and solgel chelation agent in the recycling of spent NCM cathodes (Fig. 22ac). Compared with those of freshly synthesized NCM, the resynthesized NCM cathodes showed a similar morphology and exhibited satisfactory performance, with an initial discharge capacity of 147.2 mA h g1. Similar to the effects of Cu and Mg impurities discussed above, the positive effect of Al doping on the electrochemical performance of NCM was confirmed by Li and co-workers.245 Mixed spent cathode materials (LCO, NCM, and LMO) were leached with citric acid and H2O2. The solgel method was then used to resynthesize new NCM cathodes. A trace amount of Al was found in the resynthesized cathode, and was responsible for its better cycling performance compared with that of cathodes synthesized directly from chemical reagents. The mechanism underpinning this effect lies in the strong AlO binding energy, which maintains the layered structure during the insertion/extraction of Li. The characteristics of the solgel method make it suitable for the fundamental research and modification of electrode materials, but not for large-scale material synthesis in industry.

The two general resynthesis methods discussed above are based on pure leaching solution; another type of method involves the relithiation of spent cathode powder without a leaching process. Relithiation can be achieved by electrochemical, chemical, and hydrothermal methods.247250 Gaustad et al.247 demonstrated the refunctionalization of an EOL LFP cathode through chemical and electrochemical methods. The relithiated LFP recovered its original capacity of 150155 mA h g1 (Fig. 22dg). The capacity fading mechanism of active Li+ loss underpins the relithiation method, making it feasible to resynthesize spent cathodes after regular service life.

Relithiation can also be achieved by the hydrothermal method, as investigated by Zhang and He.249 Spent LCO powder was dispersed in a concentrated LiOH solution and reacted hydrothermally at 80 C under ultrasonic radiation for 6 h. The Li+ in the LiOH solution and the ultrasonic effect both facilitated the rearrangement of atoms and thus the recovery of the layered structure. The regenerated LCO showed improved electrochemical performance, with an initial discharge capacity of 133.5 mA h g1 and a high capacity retention of 99.5% after 40 cycles. The relithiation method provides a possibility to regenerate electrode materials without breaking the whole structure. However, for spent cathodes with a destroyed structure, relithiation alone cannot completely restore the original structure, and thus a short annealing may be applied to improve the performance.250

In addition to spent cathodes, the large amount of cathode scraps generated during the battery production process cannot be neglected. Unlike spent cathode materials, electrode scraps have not been assembled into cells and have not been subjected to charge/discharge cycles, indicating that the scrap materials still possess a complete structure. Therefore, the emphasis of regenerating electrode scraps is on the separation of active materials from the current collectors followed by a subsequent regeneration treatment.58,6567,251 Zhang and co-workers65 conducted a comprehensive investigation of NCM scrap regeneration with different separation methods (direct calcination, solvent dissolution, and basic solution dissolution) and calcination temperature (200900 C). The results showed that the scraps regenerated with solvent dissolution and heat treatment at 800 C delivered the highest initial discharge capacity of 157.8 mA h g1 at 0.2C. The variation of the electrochemical performance was attributed to the content of LiF originating from PVDF decomposition and the morphology change of particles during the separation and calcination processes. In another study, Song et al.66 regenerated LCO cathode scraps using the solvent method and heat treatment. Dimethylformamide (DMF) and ethanol were used to separate the active materials from the current collectors, and then low-temperature treatment at 250 C was verified to be the optimal condition to improve the crystallinity of LCO. The regenerated LCO cathode exhibited an initial discharge capacity of 154.6 mA h g1 and a capacity retention of 92.0% after 360 cycles.

The emerging resynthesis method has attracted much attention because of its various advantages, as discussed above. The main concern in resynthesis lies in whether or not the resynthesized cathodes are comparable with commercial materials in terms of cost and performance. With the wide application of transition metals in many industries, other high-value-added products have also been explored.252261 For example, Perez et al.257,258 proposed a novel approach to resynthesize metalorganic frameworks (MOFs) from simulated LIB waste solution. MOFs are compounds consisting of metal ions or clusters coordinated to organic ligands to form one-, two-, or three-dimensional structures. MOFs show potential in many applications, such as catalysis, separation, gas storage, sensing, and purification.262264 The transition metals Cu, Zn, and Co are the most commonly used metal components in MOFs. In this preliminary study, two organic ligands [1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid (BTC)] were tested to verify the feasibility of their approach. A simulated waste solution consisting of NiSO46H2O, MnSO46H2O, and CoSO46H2O in DMF was mixed with the ligand and solvothermally reacted at 120 C for 2 days. A crystalline material with a regular shape was formed using BTC. In a further study, 1,4-benzenediphosphonic acid and 1,3,5-benzenetriphosphonic acid (BTP) were used to form phosphonate-derivative MOFs. It was found that 80% of the Mn was selectively precipitated after hydrothermal reaction at 150 C with BTP. This novel concept provides a new direction for the design of recovered materials from spent LIBs.

Because of the multiple valence states of transition metals, they are often used as catalysts in many applications. Freitas et al.260 converted spent LCO cathodes into a mixture of Co3O4 and LCO, which exhibited a 90% photocatalytic discoloration efficiency of methylene blue dye after 10 h. The Co3O4 and LCO mixture could also be used as an anode for water electrolysis, as investigated by Garcia and colleagues.259 After calcination at 400 C for 24 h, the regenerated mixture increased the current density of oxygen evolution to 1010 mA cm2 with a concentration of Co3O4 and LCO of 10% w/w. These catalytic properties were attributed to the presence of Co3+ in both LCO and Co3O4. In a pioneering work by Hu et al.,265 Li0.50MnO2 was recovered in situ from a recycled LiMnO2 battery, and then acted as an efficient catalyst for a LiO2 battery. Tuning the electrochemical performance of transition metals to obtain catalysts is an appealing approach to maximize the value of products.

3.4 Recycling of anodes and electrolytes

It is generally accepted that the remaining components of batteries after removal of the cathode materials possess relatively lower values. Thus, less attention has been paid to the recycling of anode and electrolyte materials. However, the toxicity of electrolytes, limited Li element reserves, and the wide application of carbon materials mean that proactive thinking about the proper recycling of anodes and electrolytes is necessary before they enter the waste stream in large volumes.
3.4.1 Anode recycling. In a traditional battery recycling process, the anode is either destroyed by burning or remains as an after-filtration residue.266 The main valuable components of spent anodes are the graphite carbon material and Li residue. The Li residue in an anode mostly remains in the graphite lattice voids and SEI component in the form of Li2O, LiF, Li2CO3, ROCO2Li, and CH3OLi. Among these materials, Li2O, ROCO2Li, and CH3OLi are water soluble, whereas the others are almost insoluble in water.266269 Based on this behavior, He et al.268 developed a leaching process with HCl to recycle Li in spent anode materials. They found that a high leaching efficiency of 84% could be achieved in deionized water, and the highest leaching efficiency of 99.4 wt% was obtained using 3 M HCl at 80 C for 90 min. Moreover, the crystal structure of spent graphite was improved after acid leaching.

As a carbon material, spent anodes can also be modified for other purposes as a carbon source (Fig. 23ag). Most studies have focused on the synthesis of graphene.270273 For example, Chen and co-workers273 found that spent anode graphite can be used as a cheap candidate for the high-yield production of high-quality graphene. With the assistance of ultrasound, the exfoliation efficiency of the spent graphite was 3 to 11 times that of natural graphite, which was attributed to the decreased interlayer force after cycling. The conductivity of exfoliated graphene was as high as 9100 S m1. This is a representative example showing a process of increasing the value of recycling by synthesizing high-value-added products.

In a similar vein, Yao et al.274277 conducted a series of studies investigating the use of spent anode carbon materials as an adsorbent for waste water treatment (Fig. 23eg). They modified the surface of spent graphitized carbon with nanostructured Mg(OH)2 for use as an efficient adsorbent to remove phosphates from water. The resulting material showed the highest phosphate adsorption capacity achieved to date (588.4 mg g1).277 After kinetic analysis with multiple models, a heterogeneous multilayer adsorption mechanism was proposed. Then, the same group prepared MnO2-modified graphite sorbents from spent LIBs for the treatment of waste water contaminated by the heavy metals Pb, Cd, and Ag.275 The MnO2 particles served as active sorption sites. Their results showed that the graphite adsorbent coated with ultrafine MnO2 particles exhibited high removal rates of 99.9%, 79.7%, and 99.8% for Pb2+, Cd2+, and Ag+, respectively.

Because of the damaged layered structure of graphite and the SEI component remaining after cycling, the regeneration of spent graphite as a new anode material for LIBs is generally not feasible, as the purity requirement for battery-grade graphite is higher than 99.9%.266,278 However, after proper modification, satisfactory electrochemical performance can be achieved.279,280 For instance, Nowak et al.281 recently revealed that the performance of spent graphite can be restored after the careful treatment with electrolytes (Fig. 23h and i). The effects of three different electrolyte extraction methods, namely thermal evaporation of the electrolyte, electrolyte extraction with subcritical liquid CO2 and acetonitrile, and electrolyte extraction with supercritical CO2 on anode recycling, were investigated. The results showed that the best electrochemical performance of the recycled graphite anode was obtained with electrolytes extracted with subcritical CO2 and acetonitrile. In contrast, supercritical CO2 at high pressure resulted in graphite with a lower crystallinity, which thus had an adverse effect on its performance as an anode.

3.4.2 Electrolyte recycling. The electrolyte in spent LIBs is another important source of Li. However, the volatile organic compounds and toxic lithium salts in electrolytes are severe threats to the environment and human health. Therefore, electrolytes should be recycled or disposed of carefully. However, as for anode materials, research on the recycling of LIB electrolytes is rare, for several reasons: (1) the majority of the electrolyte is adsorbed on the separator and active materials, which makes it difficult to extract and collect; (2) the electrolyte components are volatile, inflammable, and toxic, indicating that their recycling requires cautious operations that need to be conducted in a confined environment; (3) the electrolyte components are complex, including lithium salts, diverse organic solvents, and additives, making their separation and purification difficult. Despite these obstacles, steady progress on LIB electrolyte recycling has been made in recent years. The first method that is expected to be effective is solvent extraction, which was used to extract spent electrolytes in the pretreatment process in early research.282 However, the extraction yield was usually low and more impurities were introduced.

Consequently, researchers instead turned their focus to supercritical CO2 extraction, which possesses the following desirable advantages: (1) CO2 has a supercritical phase that is relatively easily available at a moderate critical pressure of 73.8 bar (7.38 MPa) and temperature of 31.1 C; (2) supercritical CO2 has a large dissolving capacity for organic substances, especially non-polar ones, and electrolyte components are relatively tolerant to this operation; (3) CO2 can be easily separated from the extract and reused; (4) no further impurities are introduced and hazardous emission is avoided as well; (5) the extraction technology is mature.283 Grtzke et al.284 pioneered work on the supercritical CO2 extraction of LIB electrolytes, which was conducted in a static autoclave with CO2 at the supercritical head pressure of helium. Electrolytes in commercial 18650 cells were extracted and analyzed by gas and ion chromatography. It was found that solvents, including EC, DMC, and EMC, could be extracted, whereas LiPF6 was barely recovered. Moreover, four aging products of diethyl carbonate (DEC), dimethyl-2,5-dioxahexane dicarboxylate, ethylmethyl-2,5-dioxahexane dicarboxylate, and diethyl-2,5-dioxahexane dicarboxylate were identified in the aged battery electrolytes, and the concentrations of these aging products were related to the aging temperature and SOH of the batteries. To improve the overall recovery rate and extraction of the conducting salts, Nowak and co-workers285 developed a flow-through method to extract LIB electrolytes with supercritical and liquid CO2 with the addition of different solvents. Their results showed that the linear carbonates (DMC and EMC) were extracted more effectively with liquid CO2, while a larger amount of cyclic carbonate (EC) was extracted with supercritical CO2 (Fig. 24a and b). The extraction of lithium conducting salts with pure supercritical or liquid CO2 was poor. These results could be attributed to the non-polar nature of the CO2 extraction medium. However, the addition of solvents to CO2 improved the extraction of all the components, especially LiPF6. The highest recovery rate of 89.1 3.4 wt% was achieved using liquid CO2, additional solvents of acetonitrile and propylene carbonate (3:1), and an additional 20 minutes with liquid CO2 only (Fig. 24c and d).

3.5 Pbacid battery recycling

Similar to the recycling of spent LIBs, the recycling technologies of spent Pbacid batteries can be mainly divided into two categories: pyrometallurgy and hydrometallurgy, in which pyrometallurgy includes direct and indirect smelting.32,33,289 Prior to the pyro- or hydrometallurgical process, pretreatment involving physical separation is usually performed. In this process, the spent Pbacid batteries are dismantled into Pb waste and grids, H2SO4 waste, and plastic materials. The H2SO4 waste is sent to the acid plant for further purification. The remaining Pb waste is recycled as new Pb compounds for reuse in Pbacid batteries.290 Most of these processes are similar to those used in spent LIB recycling. The main difference is that desulfurization and final refining processes are needed when dealing with spent Pbacid batteries.291,292
3.5.1 Pyrometallurgical technologies. Direct smelting refers to the high-temperature treatment (>1000 C) of spent Pbacid batteries in a blast, electric, reverberatory, or rotary furnace without prior desulfurization. The Pb bullion obtained after smelting is usually subjected to a refining process to improve its purity. The decomposition of PbSO4 occurs during high-temperature smelting, which results in the emission of a large amount of SO2 but has a high energy consumption. Moreover, harmful Pb dust, ash, and other pollutants are also produced. As a result, the direct smelting method has gradually been replaced by the improved indirect smelting method.

Indirect smelting involves a desulfurization process before smelting to lower the SO2 emission.292,293 The desulfurization process is generally carried out at lower temperatures than that of smelting or in aqueous solutions by adding a desulfurization reagent. Qiu et al.292 developed a vacuum smelting process to recycle all the valuable materials, including grid, paste, and plastics, from spent Pbacid batteries. The vacuum smelting of grids at 626.85 C for 30 min yielded a Pb recovery ratio of 96.29% with a purity of 99.81%. Before the vacuum reduction of Pb pastes, the S content was decreased from 7.87 to 0.26 wt% through desulfurization with Na2CO3 solution. Vacuum reduction was then conducted at 726.85 C for 90 min, giving a Pb bullion with a purity of 99.91%. Finally, the vacuum pyrolysis of plastics yielded more than 93 wt% liquid oils. This vacuum smelting process was highly efficient and released less pollutants than from direct smelting.

In addition, to obtain metallic Pb or Pb oxides for reuse in Pbacid batteries, other high-value-added products have been considered as well. For example, ultrafine lead sulfide (PbS) powder was prepared through a combination of sulfurization and inert gas condensation.294 Initially, the optimal conditions to prepare the PbS powder were investigated using pure metallic Pb particles and S powder. This novel process was then successfully used to prepare PbS powder from spent Pbacid batteries. A schematic of this preparation process is shown in Fig. 26a. The theoretical feasibilities of all the possible reactions were verified by calculating their standard rG (Fig. 26b). The XRD and SEM results presented in Fig. 26c and d indicate uniformly dispersed PbS particles with a high purity were obtained. This work provides a new perspective to deal with the S in spent Pbacid batteries.

3.5.2 Hydrometallurgical technologies. Potential environmental pollutants, including SO2 and Pb-containing slag, and a high energy consumption hinder the future use of pyrometallurgical technologies. Hydrometallurgical technologies were then proposed to minimize Pb pollution and to obtain high-purity Pb (99.9%) without the need for smelting at high temperature.

Conventional hydrometallurgical processes start with desulfurization, which is identical to pyrometallurgical processes. In the desulfurization process, sulfur in the paste is converted to soluble sulfates by reacting with Na2CO3, NaOH, (NH4)2CO3, or NaC2O4 solutions.295299 Then, sodium sulfate (Na2SO4) is obtained after crystallization. The insoluble product PbCO3, Pb(OH)2, or PbC2O4 is collected and converted to a Pb-containing solution through leaching. Pb and PbO in the paste readily dissolve in acid solution, while PbO2 needs to be reduced with a reducing agent.300,301 The leached Pb-rich solution is then subjected to cementation or electrolysis treatment to obtain Pb or Pb compounds. Finally, a refining process at low temperature is necessary to obtain battery-grade Pb products. Almost no Pb-containing gas or dust is emitted to the environment in this process. However, other problems appear, such as severe pollution from the toxic and corrosive hexafluorosilicic acid used to dissolve the Pb compounds and the high electricity consumption of the electrolysis process. As a result, hydrometallurgical processes are still in the research stage and are not yet commercially available. Sustainable recovery processes with lower cost and lower pollution urgently need to be developed, with some pioneering work showing promise.302305

Recently, Yang and Kumar et al.300,301,306315 developed a novel hydrometallurgical process that was systematically explored to extend its application prospects. This process entailed three essential steps: leaching, crystallization, and calcination (Fig. 27a). Trisodium citrate hydrate (C6H5Na3O72H2O) was used for desulfurization to form lead citrate hydrate [Pb(C6H6O7)H2O] and Na2SO4.301,307309,311 A non-toxic organic acid [citric acid monohydrate] (C6H8O7H2O) or acetic acid (CH3COOH) and C6H5Na3O72H2O were explored as leaching and crystallization agents, respectively, with H2O2 used as a reducing agent when dealing with PbO2.301,308,309,311 Unlike conventional hydrometallurgical processes, the Pb was not dissolved in the leaching solution for the following electrowinning or cementation, but crystallized to form a Pb organic precursor. Then, the precursor was calcined between 300 C and 450 C to remove the organics, which themselves served as fuel. 3.6 Ni-Based battery recycling

The recycling technologies of spent NiMH and NiCd batteries are almost identical to those of spent LIBs, including the two categories of pyrometallurgical and hydrometallurgical processes.31,318 The main difference is that the composition of NiMH batteries is more complex than that of LIBs because they contain rare-earth elements, such as La, Ce, neodymium (Nd), and samarium (Sm), which are in increasing demand.319322 Traditional pyrometallurgical technology is focused on the recovery of high-value ferronickel, which plays an important role in stainless-steel production. After crushing and separation, the separator and binder can be removed.323 The remaining electrode materials are used to produce an alloy by reduction smelting. Depending on the target product, different alloys (ferronickel or NiCo alloys) can be obtained.324,325 The reduction smelting process is carried out in an electric arc furnace with melting aids. However, the pyrometallurgical process leads to the loss of rare-earth elements, which are transferred to the slag for use in roadbed construction. Hydrometallurgical processes are used to recover the individual rare-earth elements.326,327 Leaching is also the first step to dissolve the materials in solution, followed by precipitation, solvent extraction, electrochemistry, adsorption, or crystallization to recover the target elements.

The leaching process mainly uses inorganic acids, such as H2SO4, HNO3, and HCl.207,328330 The most commonly used reagents to precipitate rare-earth elements include phosphoric acid, Na2SO4, and NaOH. After the rare-earth elements are recovered, other metals (such as Ni, Mn, Zn, and Co) can also be further precipitated using NaOH, NH4OH, or NH3 solution. D2EHPA, Cyanex 272, and TBP (tributyl phosphate) used in the recycling of spent LIBs are universal to the extraction of metal ions from Ni-based batteries.331333 In particular, ILs have been explored to extract the rare-earth elements.334,335 For example, an extractionscrubbingstripping process using the low-cost IL trihexyl(tetradecyl)phosphonium nitrate was developed to extract rare-earth elements from Ni or Co.335 The extraction yield of the rare-earth elements reached more than 99% with a purity of 99.9% in the loaded IL phase after scrubbing. Study of the extraction mechanism revealed that La3+ and Sm3+ were extracted as the anionic complexes [La(NO3)6]3 and [Sm(NO3)5]2, respectively. Compared with conventional solvent extraction, ILs are non-volatile, less flammable, and have higher thermal stability, making them promising candidates for environmentally friendly recycling processes in the future.

4. Practical applications in industry

Many industrial recycling processes have been developed over the last few decades to deal with the toxic and valuable components in spent batteries. The main recycling technologies include pyro- and hydrometallurgical methods, which can be used separately or combined together.24,341345 The whole recycling process involves a combination of different steps, including deactivation, mechanical pretreatment, and pyro- or hydrometallurgical treatment. Generally, the different compositions and structures of spent batteries mean that they require individual processing techniques to avoid cross-contamination. For example, Pbacid batteries are usually treated separately because of their mature recycling process and simple structure. LIBs and Ni-based batteries can be treated together because of their similar composition and structure. Initially, pyrometallurgical technology was used by most recycling companies because of its easy operation. During calcination, the burning of electrolytes and plastics can also provide energy. With the increasing demand for valuable metals, like Co, Li, and rare-earth elements, many enterprises are now investing in the installation of hydrometallurgy treatment units to optimize the recycling performance. Retriev Technologies (formerly Toxco Inc.), established in May 1984, is one of the leading and most diverse battery recycling enterprises in the world.346 Retriev Technologies uses advanced processing technology and equipment certified by the US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) to recycle multiple types and sizes of batteries, including alkaline, Li-based, Ni-based, and Pbacid batteries. Depending on the structure and components of the different battery types, there is a tailored process for each battery type to ensure that all of the valuable and toxic components are recovered and treated properly.

In the recycling of Li-based batteries, hydrometallurgical technologies are used to effectively recover valuable metals (Fig. 32a). First, large power battery packs from EVs or stationary applications go through a manual disassembly process, where the packs are carefully dismantled into separated cells. A proprietary cryogenic process is used to pretreat highly reactive Li-based batteries before further physical separation. Spent batteries are chilled to 200 C in liquid N2 to ensure that Li maintains a relatively inert state. The separated cells are then fed to an automated crusher, which operates in a liquid solution to lower the reactivity of the batteries and prevent gas emission. Hazardous electrolytes are neutralized and three types of materials are produced: plastics, metal solids, and metal-enriched liquid. The plastic materials floating at the top can be recovered directly for disposal or recycling. The metal solids contain various amounts of Cu, Al, Ni, and Co depending on the chemistry of the spent LIBs and are used as raw materials to produce LCO for new battery electrodes. The metal-enriched solution is solidified for further metal purification. In particular, Li is precipitated using Na2CO3 to obtain Li2CO3.

There are two processing lines used to recycle spent Pbacid batteries: an automated line and a manual line. The automated process is carried out in a high-impact enclosed crusher, which can effectively separate all the battery components. The manual disassembly line can provide complete Pb plates, which are preferred by some consumers. In addition to Pb products, the battery casing (plastic or steel) and H2SO4 are the other two components. Only H2SO4 is treated on-site by Retriev Technologies. The Pb products and battery casing are shipped to other secondary smelters to produce pure Pb for reuse in new Pbacid batteries.

The recycling of NiMH and NiCd batteries involves a pyrometallurgical process, in which the batteries are smelted to remove the plastic and separator materials. Cd ingots and ferronickel materials are obtained, and can be reused as raw materials in many applications, such as in construction and stainless-steel production. When dealing with large NiMH batteries from EVs, a manual disassembly process is necessary to ensure safe smelting.

Umicore provides recycling services for all sizes and types of spent LIB and NiMH batteries through its unique ultrahigh temperature (UHT) process combined with a state-of-the-art hydrometallurgical process (Fig. 32a).347 Compared with other pyrometallurgical recycling technologies, the UHT process possesses the following advantages: (1) it can safely treat different types of batteries with a large processing volume of 7000 metric tons per year; (2) the spent batteries are treated directly without any potentially hazardous pretreatment; (3) the environmental effect is controlled at a minimum level by using a gas cleaning system so there is almost zero waste emission. Meanwhile, the energy consumption is lowered by combustion of the battery components (electrolyte and plastics).

Two types of products are generated by the UHT process, namely an alloy containing the valuable metals Co, Ni, and Cu, and high-purity inert slags, which can be used as additives in the construction industry. In the subsequent hydrometallurgical process involving H2SO4 leaching, solvent extraction, and precipitation, the alloy is further purified and converted into new active cathode materials for rechargeable batteries.

Accurec provides recycling processes for nearly all commercially available battery types except Pb- and Hg-containing batteries. The core technology developed by Accurec is the vacuum thermal recycling (VTR) process, which separates different metals by distillation (Fig. 32b).348 Specifically, non-precious metals in the batteries generally have lower melting points and higher vapor pressures than those of precious metals, making them easy to extract by distillation. After sorting and classification, the batteries are fed into an impact mill. The electronics and plastic materials are removed by magnetic separation. The electrolytes are removed by pyrolysis. The remaining electrode materials are then heat treated under vacuum and condensed to obtain the target products. When dealing with spent NiCd and NiMH batteries, the VTR process is used to extract the Cd, hydrocarbons, and electrolytes. The remaining metals are further treated to produce NiFe or NiCo alloys.

Batrec Industrie AG mainly uses a closed two-stage thermal pyrometallurgical process to recycle spent Ni-based batteries. For the recycling of LIBs, the company developed a special mechanical process that guarantees safe disposal and complete separation of the components contained in spent batteries.349 After sorting, the spent batteries are sent to a crusher, in which the batteries are crushed in a CO2 gas atmosphere and the released Li is neutralized. With the completion of the crushing and neutralization step, the protective atmosphere is collected and treated in a gas scrubber to minimize atmospheric pollution. The individual components of the batteries, such as the fine electrode materials, metals, and plastic, are separated in a subsequent mechanical separation process and then shipped to other recycling companies for further processing.

Campine developed a closed-loop pyrometallurgical process to convert spent Pbacid batteries and other Pb-containing wastes into raw Pb materials, including a full range of Pb alloys and soft Pb (Fig. 32c).350,351 The process has three main steps: separation, reductive smelting, and refining. The batteries are first drained and the acid solution is neutralized. The batteries are then crushed and separated in a tank based on density differences. The Pb mixture, which mainly includes metallic Pb, Pb oxides, and Pb sulfate, is then fed into a furnace for smelting. Antimonial Pb or hard Pb is produced in this step. To obtain high-purity Pb, a refining process is necessary to remove impurities, like Cu, Sn, and As. During the whole recycling process, effective pollution control measures, including the treatment of gases and liquid emissions, are implemented to protect human health and prevent environmental pollution.

5. Life-cycle assessment of battery recycling

5.1 Definition and phases

The increased awareness of the importance of environmental protection associated with a certain product or service has inspired the development of methods to better understand and address this issue. One of the methods is LCA, which is also known as life-cycle analysis and cradle-to-grave analysis and which aims to assess the potential environmental effects associated with all the stages of a product's or service's life, from raw material acquisition through materials production, manufacture, use, repair and maintenance, recycling, to final disposal.352 LCA can assist in identifying opportunities to improve the environmental performance of products and services at various stages in their life cycle, and can help governments, industries, and non-governmental organizations (NGOs) make more informed decisions. For practitioners of LCA, the ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 standards detail the requirements for conducting an LCA.

According to the above two standards, an LCA is carried out in four distinct phases, comprising goal and scope definition, inventory analysis, impact assessment, and interpretation. An LCA starts with a specific definition of the goal and scope of the study, which determine the depth and breadth of the LCA. The technical details of this step mainly include the functional unit, system boundaries, and any assumptions and limitations. The definition of the functional unit depends on the goal of the study and use stage. The results of different studies can be compared only if the functional units are consistent. In the specific analysis of a battery or recycling process, the commonly used functional units are per kilogram battery mass, per watt-hour capacity, and per kilometer vehicle driving range.353,354

The inventory analysis phase is an inventory of the input/output data with regard to the system being studied. This phase involves data collection and creating an inventory of flows from and to nature for a product or service system. Inventory flows include the inputs of energy and raw materials, and the outputs of the products, by-products, and wastes released to the environment.355 In practice, inventory analysis is conducted based on the separate unit processes that make up all of the life-cycle stages, and the final results are the sum of the unit processes.353,356,357

After the inventory analysis is the impact assessment, which aims to evaluate the potential environmental effects based on the inventory analysis results. This analysis consists of several aspects, like selection of the impact categories, indicators, and characterization models; the classification used to sort the inventory into specific impact categories; and the impact measurement. Many indicators are considered for the environmental impact assessment, such as energy consumption; GHG emissions; equivalent CO2 emissions, which is the amount of CO2 and other GHGs produced; acidification; gas emissions of species like NOx and SO2; eutrophication; toxicity; and carcinogens. Among these indicators, GHG emissions and energy consumption are the most important and commonly used for a battery LCA.6,358

Interpretation is the final phase of the LCA, in which the results of the life-cycle inventory and the life-cycle impact assessment are summarized. The conclusions of the interpretation can be used as guidelines for recommendations and decision making in conformity with the goal and scope of the study.

As a service, it is subjectively believed that recycling is beneficial to the environment. However, an LCA analysis is required to provide quantitative conclusions.359 Some LCA software tools and data sources have been commercialized and are available. For example, the GREET (Greenhouse gases, Regulated Emissions, and Energy use in Transportation) software developed by ANL can be used to study energy consumption and pollutant emissions in the production, use, and disposal of vehicle and power source technologies, including different types of rechargeable batteries.360 The generalized boundary (cradle-to-grave, cradle-to-gate, or grave-to-cradle), process, and material/energy flow analysis for an LCA of a battery are shown in Fig. 33. However, environmental impacts can be decreased substantially if a proper recycling process is used. Liu et al.368 conducted an LCA of Pbacid batteries used in electric bicycles in China. The EPA and the Department of Energy carried out an LCA of LIBs in EV and PHEV applications to identify the process (including materials extraction and processing, manufacturing, use, recycling, and disposal) that had the largest public health and environmental impacts.372 For the recycling stage impact assessment, three recycling technologies for LIBs (hydrometallurgical, pyrometallurgical, and direct recovery processes) were assessed based on a best-case scenario, with life-cycle inventory data primarily obtained from the relevant battery recyclers. The final data were the average of the three results; the individual impact of each process was not presented. Although the impacts varied markedly across the lifetime stages for all three battery chemistries, the materials extraction and use stages dominated in most of the impact categories. Recycling lowered the overall life-cycle impacts, especially in the categories of the ozone depletion potential, occupational cancer, and non-cancer hazards. This offset impact came from the use of recycled materials instead of virgin materials in the extraction and processing stage. The report also pointed out several opportunities to decrease the environmental impacts of LIBs in EV applications, among which recycling was identified as beneficial to lowering the environmental impact when battery manufacturers maximize the use of recycled materials in the production of new battery materials, thus forming a closed-loop materials flow.

Hao et al.373 conducted a grave-to-cradle LCA of recycling on the energy consumption and GHG emissions of EV production based on the predictions for China in 2025. The whole process was composed of three phases: (1) the recycling process, including vehicle dismantling, vehicle recycling, battery recycling, and tire recycling; (2) material recovery; and (3) vehicle production. A hydrometallurgical process was used to recycle the NCM battery. The calculated results showed that the GHG emissions from EV production could be lowered by 34% through recycling. Among all the recycling materials and processes, steel recovery in vehicle recycling and NCM cathode recovery in battery recycling were the two main contributors, providing 61% and 20% of the total decrease, respectively. Because the battery is an essential factor in the development of future EVs, it was suggested that there was huge growth potential for battery recycling.

When assessing a recycling process, one issue that remains is how to allocate the credit for the recycling; that is, whether it should be given to the lifetime of the battery or to the material itself.352 For Pbacid batteries, it is reasonable to allocate the credit to the battery product, because almost 90% of the recycled materials are reused in new Pbacid batteries. For LIBs, the situation is more complex because not all the components are recycled and only some of the recycled materials are reused as raw materials to produce new LIBs. It is the closed-loop process that indicates the value of recycling, which aims to minimize the energy consumption and toxic emissions associated with the primary production of raw materials.

To quantify this benefit of recycling to the production of batteries, Dewulf and co-workers230 conducted a detailed LCA comparison of a recycling scenario involving Co and Ni recycling and reuse in battery production and a production scenario with virgin materials. The recycling scenario included a smelting process developed by Umicore. Fig. 34b shows the requested inputs for the production chain to obtain 1 kg of LiMeO2 (Me = Ni, Mn, Co) for an LIB cathode. The two scenarios exhibited similar energy requirements for MnSO4 production as well as cathode material production. Conversely, considerable differences existed in the production of CoSO4 and NiSO4, with 96.9 MJ for the recycling scenario and 236.1 MJ for the production scenario, indicating that the recycling scenario saved 51% of the natural resource compared with that needed for the production scenario. Further research on the kind of natural resources that could be saved by recycling was conducted. It was found that fossil resources and nuclear energy consumption decreased by 45.3% and 57.2%, respectively, by recycling. This research proved the positive effects of recycling on the production of battery materials. Note that as the authors stated, the virgin mining process could not be totally replaced by recycling.

For an investigation of the comparison of different recycling processes, Tian et al.374 compared the environmental impacts of five recycling processes of Pbacid batteries in China, namely the direct reverberatory furnace melting of the battery, the rotary furnace melting of the desulfurized lead paste, the QSL furnace, the citrate leaching process, and the alkaline leaching process, and evaluated them in seven categories. Their results indicated that the greenness of innovative processes, such as citric acid leaching and alkaline leaching, may not be greater than that of traditional processes, especially in terms of the indirect environmental impacts, which depended on the off-site emissions from the chemicals and energy consumption.

Hendrickson et al.231 used LCA and geographic information systems to assess the impacts of the EOL LIB recycling network in California. As part of this network, battery recycling processes (pyrometallurgy and hydrometallurgy) were analyzed in terms of resource use and environmental emissions (Fig. 34c) based on the material inputs, recycling processes, slag outputs for cement use, and materials recovered. The results showed that recycling could offset the impacts associated with LIB production. The pyrometallurgical recycling process could lower the primary energy demand and GHG emissions by 656% and 23%, respectively. When comparing the two main types of recycling technologies, hydrometallurgical recycling had advantages in terms of decreased electricity consumption and air pollutant formation, whereas pyrometallurgical recycling used less water than hydrometallurgy.

Dunn et al.358 examined the effects of three recycling processes on the cradle-to-gate energy consumption and GHG emissions of LMO batteries. As shown in Fig. 34d, all the recycling processes induced large decreases in energy consumption compared with that of virgin materials production, with the largest potential decrease of 48% determined for the direct recycling process. In their latest LCA research on recycling, different cathodes of LIBs and recycling technologies (pyrometallurgical, indirect physical, and direct physical processes) were considered, and more focus was paid to the environmental impacts assessment (GHG and SOx emissions).6 As illustrated in Fig. 34e, the direct recycling process had the greatest potential to lower both GHG and SOx emissions by 8198% and 72100%, respectively, when compared with those of virgin materials production. Pyrometallurgical recycling was only used to recover LCO cathodes, and the SOx decrease was particularly large (almost 100%). This was because of the huge amount of SOx emitted during the production of virgin Co materials. Moreover, the relative environmental benefits of EVs over internal combustion engine vehicles (ICEVs) was also addressed. Except for the fact that the batteries were charged by coal-derived electricity, EVs had lower petroleum consumption and GHG emissions compared with those of ICEVs. This series of studies confirmed the positive influence of recycling, which can mitigate LIB life-cycle impacts in terms of energy consumption and toxic gas emissions.

Assessment of a specific recycling technology depends on the cumulative effects of all the stages involved. Because of the limited data available for battery recycling (especially LIBs), only a few LCA studies to date have involved a detailed assessment of a specific recycling process.6,231,358,374377 Dunn and colleagues377 conducted the first systematic analysis of the energy and materials flows of four LIB recycling technologies, namely hydrometallurgical, pyrometallurgical, intermediate physical, and direct physical processes. The energy consumption and CO2 emissions of every step in each recycling process were calculated and then the main contributing process was identified. For example, in the LCA of the hydrometallurgical process based on citric acid leaching displayed in Fig. 34f and g, high-temperature calcination was calculated to be the most energy-intensive step, and was also the main contributor to CO2 emissions because of the combustion of the graphite carbon and PVDF binder.

The above studies confirmed the environmental benefit of closed-loop recycling, even though the specific results varied between groups. This data discrepancy mainly originated from the different battery chemistries, data sources, assessment scopes and boundaries, and assumptions used.378 For now, energy consumption and GHG emissions are the main assessment indicators for battery recycling. However, other indicators cannot be neglected and need to be assessed selectively according to the goal of the study. More reliable results require sufficient precise data, which urgently need to be updated for LIB production and recycling.359

6. Second use of retired electric vehicle batteries

6.1 Motivation and definition

Electric transportation systems with EVs in full or hybrid forms are considered an important strategy to lower both our dependence on fossil energy and emissions of GHGs. The market share of EVs using LIBs as a power source has increased rapidly in the last decade. However, the prohibitively high cost of power batteries, currently making up approximately 50% of the vehicle cost, impedes further market penetration of EVs.379,380 Much effort, including developing low-cost materials for batteries and improving production efficiency and throughput, is therefore being expended in attempts to lower battery costs. With the increasing production and promotion of EVs as a green transportation choice, the quantities of retired LIB packs will exponentially increase by 2050, as discussed in the introduction section.17 In most cases, automotive manufacturers recommend battery retirement when the energy or power density drops to 7080% of the original value, which is inappropriate for continued use in a vehicle.381,382 There are three options to deal with the retired batteries: remanufacturing, repurposing, and recycling (Fig. 35).383 The final decision depends on many factors, such as the performance and damage state of the batteries and the cost and benefit of the different choices. Because retired EV batteries still contain substantial electric energy after automotive use, a common belief is that additional value could be extracted by reusing them in other energy storage applications. Such reuse would mean that the high cost of the batteries could be shared by the secondary users and lowered through the extension of the total battery service lifetime. This post-application of retired EV batteries has been referred to as battery second use (B2U) or reuse.70,380

B2U is an operational process that consists of acquiring a retired battery system from a vehicle, repurposing it, integrating it into a new battery system, and then deploying it into another application. To verify the feasibility of B2U, systematic studies have been conducted that mainly consider the aspects of economic,70,71,381,384387 technical,70,71,385,388392 environmental,393398 and market feasibility.70,388,391,399403

6.2 Second use of retired nickelmetal hydride batteries

The first research on B2U was conducted by ANL in the late 1990s to examine the B2U feasibility of retired NiMH batteries, which were the most promising EV batteries at the time.389 The performance test results were compared with those for Pbacid batteries to assess competitiveness. Specifically, NiMH battery modules were first cycled to simulate EV usage. Their remaining capacity and power capability were then determined and adjusted to 30 or 45 W h kg1, representing the standard and advanced performance of Pbacid batteries, respectively.70 The life performances of these NiMH battery modules were tested in four simulated stationary storage applications: (1) utility load following; (2) utility frequency regulation and spinning reserve; (3) uninterruptible power sources (UPSs) for stand-by power and telecommunications applications; and (4) accelerated life testing. The final results showed that the performances of the simulated retired NiMH battery modules were at least as good as those of new Pbacid batteries, demonstrating the technical feasibility of B2U in terms of performance.404

The second comprehensive study, conducted at Sandia National Laboratory in 2003, aimed to evaluate the economic and technical feasibility of B2U of NiMH batteries in stationary applications.70 A total of eight different stationary applications (transmission support, area regulation and spinning reserve, load leveling/energy arbitrage/transmission deferral, renewables firming, power reliability and peak shaving, light commercial load following, distributed node telecom backup power, and residential load following) for B2U were considered through a technical and economic feasibility analysis. Overall, the technical barriers were not the main concern and they could be overcome before practical implementation. Of the eight applications, four (transmission support, light commercial load following, distributed node telecommunications backup power, and residential load following) were identified as possible candidates for economically viable B2U of EV batteries.

Although the above two studies focused on NiMH batteries, many of the process-oriented results are considered broadly applicable to other battery chemistries, like the LIBs widely used in current EVs. This early research laid foundations for future B2U evaluation, especially in terms of technical and economic feasibility. With the booming development of EVs in the 2010s, it is forecasted that a substantial number of retired LIBs will become available by around 2030. Therefore, it is predicted that the B2U of LIBs will emerge as a research hotspot.

6.3 Economic feasibility analysis

Economic feasibility analysis involves a costbenefit analysis. The costs associated with repurposing EV batteries into second stationary applications can be divided into two categories (Fig. 36a).70,381,387 The first is the repurposing cost, which refers to the cost of components unique to the reconfiguration of retired EV batteries and mainly consists of the cost of retired EV batteries, collecting, disassembling, sorting, testing, and reassembling the retired EV batteries. The second category refers to the cost components common to all energy storage applications of a given type, including the cost of the repurposed battery from the first category, operation and maintenance (O&M), and the balance of system (BOS). O&M mainly includes normal operation, routine and unscheduled maintenance, monitoring, and repair or replacement of damaged battery modules or packs. The BOS cost typically refers to the cost of all the non-battery equipment of an ESS, mainly including installation and startup, interface equipment, power conditioning systems and controls, accessories, facilities, and transportation. For second stationary applications with repurposed batteries to be economically viable, the cost of the repurposed batteries should be lower than the cost of a new battery, which decreases over time, as shown in Fig. 36b.385,400,405410 Based on the conservative scenario assumption (an annual decrease in the cost of 6% after 2020) recently published by the Boston Consulting Group (BCG), the cost of a battery pack will decrease from $300 kW h1 in 2015 to around $135 kW h1 in 2030.407 Most of the predictions reach a consensus that $200 kW h1 can be achieved in 2020.406410 Therefore, a maximum allowable value for the repurposed battery selling price should be estimated and set. Furthermore, by subtracting the cost of repurposing from the selling price, the maximum achievable buying price for a retired battery can be obtained, which is also the value to the automotive battery owner.385

In an earlier economic analysis of a plug-in EV B2U study by Neubauer et al.385 in 2012, the repurposed battery selling price was estimated to range from $38 to $132 kW h1 based on a predicted new battery cost of $125440 kW h1. The repurposing cost was strongly affected by the module size and cell fault rate. It was found that the optimal module size decreased as the cell fault rate increased. Moreover, the replacement of faulty cells within modules was not recommended from an economics viewpoint, and a smaller module size (of the order of several kilowatt hours) was more economically favorable than a larger one. With a high repurposed battery selling price of $132 kW h1 and low cell fault rate of 0.01%, the calculated repurposing cost was approximately $32 kW h1. The potential benefits could be realized when using the repurposed batteries in a UPS ESS, which had an estimated payback of seven years. In a subsequent study in 2015, the calculated lowest and highest repurposed battery selling prices were $44 and $180 kW h1, respectively, based on an updated new battery cost prediction of $150250 kW h1.71 It can be seen that the repurposed battery cost dominated the total system cost, followed by the labor cost (Fig. 37b).

Narula et al.381 explored the cost competitiveness of retired EV or hybrid EV LIBs for B2U in power grid applications. First, a selling price for the repurposed batteries was proposed based on the projected cost of new batteries in 2020. Then, when analyzing the potential benefits of energy storage applications, a second lifetime of five or ten years was assumed based on different application types. Individual applications as well as multiple applications were explored. According to the estimated new battery cost in 2020 of $360440 kW h1, a competitive price for a retired battery was assumed to be between $75 and $220 kW h1 as the low and high cost boundary values, respectively. The unitary system costs breakdown in terms of USD per kilowatt hour and USD per kilowatt for ESSs with repurposed batteries were summarized. Then, the system costs for a given energy storage application with different power and energy capabilities were calculated. Fig. 37c presents the high and low estimated values of system cost for an ESS with a power capability of 500 kW and storage capacity of 1 MW h. From the analysis results of the five categories of applications, it was found that the cost distribution depended on the performance requirements of the different applications. Specifically, the cost of the retired batteries and transportation were the major components for applications requiring high energy performance. Meanwhile, the BOS and O&M costs dominated the system cost for applications requiring high power performance. The benefit analysis of individual applications showed that area regulation, transmission and distribution upgrade deferral, and electric service power quality were the most likely profitable applications of B2U.

Williams387 conducted an economic analysis on the basis of three actual vehicles: the Toyota Prius, Chevy Volt, and Nissan Leaf. The energy storage cost components of these three cases are summarized in Fig. 37d. The BOS cost was the major component in all cases, while repurposed batteries only accounted for 612% of the total cost. With the addition of value from B2U in stationary grid applications, the decrease in battery lease payments was calculated and a decrease of 22% was obtained as the baseline for the Chevy Volt.

Foster et al.17 explored the cost benefit of three potential applications after vehicle life: remanufacturing, repurposing, and recycling. Different from the above conclusion, remanufacturing, that is, the replacement of failed cells within the battery, was calculated to be economically feasible, decreasing the cost of a new battery by approximately 40%. Repurposing and recycling may be profitable as well provided that certain conditions are met. Research and development costs for new applications and the price of Li salts were crucial factors determining the economic feasibility of repurposing and recycling, respectively. It should be noted that many of the above conclusions were drawn on the basis of specific assumptions, which may change over time and space. Therefore, uncertainty exists and should be considered when dealing with certain types of retired batteries and application scenarios.411

6.4 Technical feasibility analysis

The whole second use process faces technical challenges, including assessment of a battery's performance in its first vehicle-based life, testing and verification of the battery SOH after vehicle life, prediction of its second use performance, safe disassembly of battery packs, testing of failed cells within the pack, verification of the battery size for repurposing, design of new battery management systems to interface with application scenarios, reconfiguring used batteries for second applications, and BOSs in the second use application.388,390,392,412416 Predicting the performance of batteries in their second life is most challenging because it is strongly related to the current SOH and operational history during their life in the vehicle.71,393,417,418

Before disassembly, technicians can presort damaged packs through visual examination of any physical damage and/or leakage. Meanwhile, the battery chemistry and calendar life of vehicle use can be identified from the label information.70 Because of the high voltage and liquid coolant, the disassembly of battery packs is hazardous and requires skilled technicians, as well as specialized equipment. As for the determination of optimal battery size for repurposing, most studies tend to use the pack or module level.70,71,386,393,412 Because the cells within a battery pack are generally welded together, dismantling to the cell level is neither technically nor economically viable. Then, voltage and resistance tests are conducted to identify the damaged modules. The current SOH of retired batteries in terms of capacity and power capability can be determined through specific testing procedures, such as the Technical specifications of performance test for smart grid energy storage batteries (DB31/T817-2014, China) and USABC Reference Performance Test.70,415

Prediction of the remaining performance requires an understanding of the battery degradation. Neubauer et al.71,382 developed a general methodology and tools to predict battery degradation during the first automotive and second application lifetimes. Based on the Battery Lifetime Analysis and Simulation Tool for Vehicles (BLAST-V) model studies used to predict battery degradation in automobile life, it was found that driver patterns and climate have the greatest effects on battery degradation. Then, two different EVs, a midsize sedan battery EV with a 75-mile electric range (BEV75) and a midsize sedan PHEV with a 20-mile electric range (PHEV20), and three climate patterns (cold, moderate, and hot) were chosen and simulated. The prediction results from BLAST-V and the present value of throughput method showed that, compared with resistance growth, the capacity loss during vehicle life had a larger effect on the second use value. Meanwhile, it was found that the most important parameter to identify the remaining battery performance was the capacity loss related to the calendar effect (Q1), as shown in Fig. 38. Thus, the authors recommended a high-confidence identification strategy for Q1, which combined an in situ measurement of the remaining battery capacity while recording the battery temperature.

6.5 Environmental feasibility analysis

Continued research has indicated that the value of B2U is not only in the reuse of battery cells to lower the initial price of the vehicle, but also that B2U has the potential to maximize an EV battery system over its lifetime, which can provide additional environmental benefits to the whole B2U system. It is rational that B2U can postpone the recycling process, which can lower the burden of final recycling and disposal.401 Compared with technical and economic analyses of B2U, relatively fewer environmental assessment studies have been conducted. Earlier research was mainly a rough calculation of the carbon reduction credits from energy storage. For example, Williams and Lipman386 estimated a modest carbon reduction value of $12 year1 in California if otherwise wasted wind energy was stored by the battery devices. A similar calculation was also conducted by Narula381 to verify the positive environmental effect of energy storage. Incidental benefits, including reduced fossil fuel use and air emissions (CO2 and NOx), can be obtained through energy storage. Energy use and GHG emissions are considered as the main indicators of the environmental assessment of B2U. Usually three scenarios are analyzed to address the environmental considerations corresponding to both first EV and second-life battery applications. The first scenario is the LCA of the reuse phase in the whole life cycle of an EV battery. The second one is the LCA comparison of EVs with and without a second-life application. The last one is to compare the LCA of B2U with that using other energy storage technologies (like a Pbacid battery or natural gas) for the second-life application.

In recent research by Ahmadi and colleagues, a complete life cycle of EV batteries (cascaded scenario), including production, EV use, repurposing, second use in a stationary application, and recycling, was investigated.398 As many as six indicators were calculated in the LCA: global warming potential (GWP), particulate matter formation potential, freshwater eutrophication potential, photochemical oxidant formation potential, metal depletion potential, and fossil depletion potential. Within the phases of the cascaded scenario, it was concluded that the energy consumption of the use and reuse phases was much higher than that of the other phases (Fig. 39a). For the environmental impact analysis, the battery pack manufacturing phase generated the most GHG emissions (40%). The use and reuse phases were also the major contributors to the GHG emissions (Fig. 39b). Furthermore, the results were compared with those of a conventional scenario; that is, an ICEV for mobility use and a natural gas peaking power plant for a stationary application. The calculated results showed that the cascaded scenario had less environmental impact than the conventional scenario, except for the metal depletion potential indicator.

Richa et al. investigated the impact of the addition of reuse on the net environmental impacts. It was found that the reuse of a retired EV LIB in the stationary application could reduce the net cumulative energy demand (CED) and GWP by 15% compared with that without stationary use (Fig. 39c and d).396 Cicconi et al.395 used LCA to estimate the environmental impact of retired PHEV batteries in second smart-grid applications. Global warming, acidification, and eutrophication were chosen as the indicators. Compared with the original life cycle of an EV without B2U, an environmental gain of 25% was obtained after B2U. Another study conducted by Faria et al.420 also verified the environmental benefits of B2U in household energy storage for peak shaving and load shifting. Retired LIBs from Nissan Leaf EVs were used as the object. Four categories, including abiotic depletion, acidification, eutrophication, and global warming, were assessed. The results showed that the positive environmental impact was strongly related to the electricity generation mix. Sathre and co-workers394 analyzed the impact of the second-life use of retired plug-in EVs on the future energy system of California by 2050. The results suggested that the contribution of B2U to diurnal energy shifting was modest.

Ahmadi and colleagues393,397,398 systematically investigated the environmental feasibility of B2U through the LCA method. In a previous study, they compared the CO2 emission of three paths: path 1 was an ICEV for mobility with natural gas for peaking power, path 2 was a PHEV for mobility with natural gas for peaking power, and path 3 was a PHEV for mobility with repurposed batteries for peaking power (Fig. 39e).397 The recycling process was not considered in this study. A considerable decrease of CO2 emissions (56%) was achieved with B2U in path 3 compared with that of the other paths. Moreover, a new concept of charge efficiency fade (or energy efficiency fade) was introduced, which affects the reuse process much more than the vehicle use process. Taking charge efficiency fade into consideration, CO2 emissions will increase over time. Later, the authors improved on their previous study by using energy and GHG emissions as indicators for an environmental feasibility analysis.393 Similar positive effects of B2U in terms of energy and GHG emission decreases were confirmed. This series of studies indicated that B2U can be beneficial from the perspective of minimizing environmental effects.

In addition to the comparison of B2U and natural gas in stationary energy storage, other common technologies have also been considered. Richa and colleagues396 investigated the environmental feasibility of B2U compared with that of a new Pbacid battery for stationary energy storage (Fig. 39f). It was estimated that a repurposed LIB outperformed a new Pbacid battery, with a net decrease of 13% to 46% in CED and 12% to 46% in GWP. Because the above conclusions were circumstance-specific, they could not be compared between different cases. However, a common belief was confirmed that B2U can be beneficial to the environment if it is performed under certain conditions.

6.6 Market feasibility analysis

Market analysis of B2U mainly involves identification of the potential application markets. A proper business model also needs to be considered in practice after an application is determined. Application selection is closely related to the performance and cost of the repurposed battery, the technical requirements of the potential application, and the market size and competition (supply of repurposed batteries and demand for the relative application). A consensus has been reached that a substantial number of retired batteries from EVs will become available by 2030.

First though, repurposed batteries must find their right place in the energy storage market. Given their relatively poor performance in terms of energy and power density, as well as the large size of the battery pack and module, some advanced battery markets, such as consumer electronics and EVs, are not suitable for repurposed batteries. For applications dominated by Pbacid batteries with relatively low performance requirements, the competition strongly depends on the price/performance ratio of repurposed batteries.71 It is widely accepted that stationary energy storage is the most promising application for repurposed batteries in the future.381,385,401,418,421426 Eyer and Corey419 grouped the main 19 grid energy storage application scenarios into five categories: electric supply, ancillary services, grid system, end user/utility customer, and renewables integration. Preliminary costbenefit calculations indicated that area regulation application, an ancillary service, had the largest potential benefit.386 However, based on the allocation analysis by Neubauer,71 high-value applications, such as area regulation and transportable transmission upgrade deferral, all have small market potential and fierce competition. A more plausible strategy would be to shift focus to the application scenario with the highest demand; that is, peak-shaving services. Neubauer71 suggested two cases for investigation: user-side behind-the-meter storage and peaker plant replacement, of which the latter was estimated to be the most promising scenario.

Based on the size of packs, stationary applications can be classified into large-scale applications (MW h, MW), such as load leveling for renewable energies, and small-scale applications (kW h, kW), such as residential/light commercial load leveling.393,399,418 Considering that the energy/power density of repurposed batteries is of the order of kW h kW1, it seems more appropriate to use them in small-scale applications. Moreover, the construction of small applications is simpler and less risky than that of large ones, which could facilitate the initial implementation of B2U.384 Kootstra et al.416 investigated the operation feasibility of a photovoltaic grid stabilization system armed with a repurposed LIB pack. The calculation indicated that the peak demand of grid electricity could be lowered by over 70% with the battery pack, and the sudden fluctuations in photovoltaic output could also be stabilized. Similarly, Knowles and co-workers424 simulated the application of repurposed batteries as buffer packs for the home storage of solar power. It was verified that the use of repurposed batteries could increase the utilization of renewable energies. Aziz et al.422 developed a storage system consisting of five EVs, five used EV batteries (after one year of use in an EV), and a solar panel to support a building energy management system. Their test results indicated that this utilization strategy was viable by controlling the electrochemical performances of the EVs and used batteries and setting a peak cut threshold.

When implementing the second use of vehicle batteries in practice, the optimal business model needs to be identified.400,402,403 Klr et al.403 argued that there were three market forms that may be considered in B2U, including a closed market, an intermediary-based market, and an open market, as shown in Fig. 40. The closed market is led by the automotive or battery manufacturers, who are fully responsible for the repurposing of vehicle batteries. In the intermediary-based market, an intermediary collects retired batteries from car owners and repurposes the batteries for second use in other applications. The open market is a marketplace where buyers and sellers can negotiate directly over the repurposing and application of retired batteries. Based on the conceptual transaction cost analysis and empirical inquiries, the intermediary-based market led by automotive manufacturers was the most likely option for B2U. Another business model of battery lease was also preliminarily investigated. Lih et al.388 estimated a profit rate of 35% could be achieved when a 10 kW h LIB pack had a total service life of 15 years (three years of first use and 12 years of second use) under the battery lease model. Regardless of the business model, the B2U service should be customer-focused, including the car owners and buyers of the second application.400 The providers of B2U, whether intermediaries or automotive manufacturers, should make efforts to improve the costperformance ratio of repurposed batteries and customers product experience.

7. Laws and regulations

Mandatory laws and regulations are the first incentive for battery recycling. Developed countries, including the USA, EU members, and Japan, have already started to compile legislation on the recycling of used batteries. Different laws, regulations, and directives related to battery recycling have been enacted, revised, and updated to cope with battery development, as shown in Fig. 41. Generally, the laws and regulations are enacted from the perspective of the life-cycle management of batteries. Systematic legislations with clear responsibilities of relevant stakeholders are important for battery recycling.
Fig. 41 Timeline and development of laws and regulations on battery recycling in the EU, USA, and China.

The US legal system has contributions mainly from the federal, state, and local levels. The Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA) of 1976 is the principal federal law that governs the disposal of solid and hazardous waste, and has the main goals of protecting human health and the natural environment from the potential hazards of waste disposal and decreasing the amount of waste through source reduction and recycling. EPA regulates the treatment of solid and hazardous wastes under the RCRA. For example, the Universal Waste Rule set standards for the identification of universal waste, which mainly includes hazardous waste batteries, certain hazardous waste pesticides, and hazardous waste thermostats. Another federal law, The Mercury-Containing and Rechargeable Battery Management Act of 1996 (known as The Battery Act), also makes important advances in battery recycling, because it phases out the use of Hg in batteries and requires labeling to facilitate the collection, recycling, and disposal of spent small sealed Pbacid batteries, NiCd batteries, and certain other batteries. Most states have adopted the battery recycling regulations published by EPA and also enacted their own regulations that are at least as stringent as the federal regulations.437 For example, the California Rechargeable Battery Recycling Act of 2006 bans all household batteries from solid waste landfill disposal and requires retailers to take back rechargeable batteries for recycling at no cost to consumers. The New York State Rechargeable Battery Law of 2010 aims to maximize the removal of used rechargeable batteries from the solid waste stream by requiring battery manufacturers to take back and recycle used rechargeable batteries that are sold or disposed of in the state of New York.438 This law requires the shared responsibility for the collection and disposal of used rechargeable batteries from many stakeholders, including battery manufacturers, local retailers, and consumers. Retailers are required to accept used batteries from customers, and to conspicuously maintain collection boxes or other suitable receptacles that are convenient for consumers to safely deposit used rechargeable batteries. Battery manufacturers are responsible for arranging and financing the return and recycling of all used rechargeable batteries collected by retailers.

The first version of the European Council Directive on batteries and accumulators was 91/157/EEC, which required the disposal of many battery types, including alkaline, Pbacid, NiCd, NiMH, Li-based, and Hg-containing batteries, and LIBs.439 The Directive 2006/66/EC on waste batteries and accumulators (The Battery Directive) regulates the manufacture, collection, recycling, and disposal of used batteries and accumulators.440 The aim of this directive is to decrease the amount of hazardous substances used and to prohibit the marketing of batteries with more than 0.0005% Hg and 0.002% Cd by weight. Similar European directives parallel to the Battery Directive are the Restriction of Hazardous Substances Directive (RoHS; Directive 2002/95/EC) and the Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment Directive (WEEE; Directive 2012/19/EC).441,442 RoHS requires the substitution of various heavy metals with other substances in new electrical and electronic equipment entering the market. WEEE aims to provide incentives to improve the design of electrical and electronic equipment to facilitate recycling. It initiates the reuse and recycling of used electrical and electronic equipment to decrease the amount of waste generated. Producers are responsible for recycling used electrical and electronic equipment free of charge.

China is one of the major countries producing batteries and EVs, and the Chinese government has enacted a complete legal system on batteries recycling, which is mainly divided into three levels: law; regulation; and specific technical policies, specifications, and guidelines.443 The Prevention of Environmental Pollution from Solid Waste Law supports the comprehensive utilization of resources, the full recovery and rational utilization of solid waste, and the adoption of economic and technological policies conducive to the comprehensive utilization of solid waste. The Technical Policy of Waste Battery Pollution Prevention is the first policy specifically related to the treatment of batteries. This policy is applicable to technical selection to prevent and control pollution in the whole recycling process, including in the classification, collection, transportation, comprehensive utilization, storage, and treatment of waste batteries, including Hg-containing, NiCd, and Pbacid batteries. The goal is to gradually decrease and eventually eliminate the use of Hg in primary battery production, and to collect and dispose of waste batteries in a safe, efficient, and low-cost manner. In response to the upcoming issue of the treatment of retired power batteries, the Technical Policy of Recovery and Utilization of Power Batteries for Electric Vehicles was developed. The purpose of this policy is to instruct relevant enterprises to rationally conduct the production and recovery of EV batteries, to clarify the main body responsible for the recovery of used batteries, and to implement an extended producer responsibility system.444

8. Opportunities, challenges, and future prospects

The recycling of spent rechargeable batteries has been extensively studied around the world, and great progress has been made in the last few decades. However, the growing energy demand for various applications forces researchers to continually develop new battery materials and systems. At the same time, with the development of society and the improvement of living standards, people's awareness of the importance of environmental protection has increased markedly. Therefore, continuous research is still needed to develop the most appropriate recycling technologies in response to the changing demand for recycling. Based on the existing experience and predicted future development of this field, a reinterpretation of the reduce, reuse, recycle (3R) strategy as redesign, reuse, recycle for battery recycling is proposed, along with a more comprehensive 3E assessment standard of efficiency, economy, environment, as shown in Fig. 42. Redesign and recycling represent the source and end treatment of batteries, respectively, and reuse refers to the second use of retired power batteries. This strategy faces the following challenges and opportunities:
Fig. 42 Proposed 3R and 3E strategy for battery recycling in the future.

(1) Technical aspect: the pretreatment of large volumes of power batteries has great potential dangers, like short circuit and an explosion risk. In practice, there are almost no well-established processing lines for automatic disassembly, which are needed urgently for efficient and safe recycling in the future. Because of the complex composition of batteries, especially LIBs and NiMH batteries, multiple steps are necessary to obtain high-purity products; nevertheless, complicated operations have an associated large uncertainty. Considering most processes are designed for the separation of different metal elements, a simple recycling process with high recycling and separation rates is desirable. Meanwhile, the impurity level needs to be carefully controlled under a threshold value, which is particularly important for the regenerated materials used in new batteries.

(2) Economic aspect: economic benefit is the direct incentive for manufacturers to recycle spent batteries. The economic analysis is based on costbenefit calculations. Clearly, decreasing the costs and increasing profits are the two ways to maximize economic benefits. The cost of a recycling process can be divided into fixed and variable costs, which mainly correspond to the used recycling technology and materials, respectively. Therefore, simple operational processes and low-cost materials are desirable. Meanwhile, the benefit of recycling can be increased by obtaining high yields of high-purity products or high-value-added products. In addition to the benefits from products, the positive environmental and social effects of recycling should also be quantitatively incorporated into the economic analysis.

(3) Environmental aspect: the environmental impact of a recycling process can be considered from two perspectives; namely, source control and end-treatment control. At present, recycling enterprises decrease environmental pollution by end-treatment processes, which require the installation of waste water and gas-treatment systems. However, based on the concept of sustainable development, it is strongly recommended that the pollution control strategy should be transferred from end treatment to source prevention. Specifically, for the materials used in the batteries, the use of non-toxic elements to replace toxic materials without sacrificing performance is desired.2,445 As for the recycling process, non-toxic reagents and recycling technologies with low emissions and energy consumption are preferred. As a last resort, end treatment should be used to minimize any pollution emission. At present, most recycling processes focus on the recovery of high-value electrode materials, even though the toxic electrolyte is the main source of environmental pollution. More attention should be paid to electrolyte treatment in the future. Evaluation of a certain recycling process depends on a reliable LCA. Greenness and economy need to be quantitatively calculated and balanced to determine an optimal recycling strategy.

(4) Design aspect: in the process of battery recycling, the mixing of different batteries can result in cross-contamination, which makes the subsequent separation and purification process extremely difficult. Therefore, a pre-screening process is recommended. A user-friendly design of batteries from the inside out would be conducive to their efficient pretreatment. For example, a uniform size and shape of a specific battery type are desirable for automatic dismantling. Clear and user-friendly labels of the battery type and composition should be presented in a prominent position on the batteries.290 The design of large battery packs should consider that a simple and easily disassembled structure can aid recycling.

(5) Market aspect: a complete and clear battery recycling industry chain needs to be established to define the responsibilities of each participant. In addition to mandatory measures, such as laws and regulations, economic incentives implemented by governments and enterprises can assist people to voluntarily recycle spent batteries. Recycling systems need to be established predominantly by governments, battery manufacturers, or automotive enterprises to cover the whole recycling process involving collection, transportation, recycling, and disposal. Well-functioning recycling systems, such as Bebat in Belgium and Call2Recycle in the USA, provide valuable references for the development of such systems in other countries.446,447

(6) Popularization aspect: a complete recycling process begins with collection, which cannot be achieved efficiently by recycling enterprises alone. Universal education on the benefits of recycling and the dangers of waste batteries could facilitate their recycling. Governments, enterprises, educational institutions, and NGOs should all contribute to raising people's awareness of recycling.

(7) Networking aspect: a massive data volume is essential to develop a proper recycling strategy. The more information that recycling enterprises have about waste batteries, the better they can optimize their parameters to achieve a higher recycling efficiency. With the development of artificial intelligence and the Internet of Things, it is conceivable that in the future, all batteries that are produced may be connected to the internet.448 Thus, a massive data volume on battery production and use stages could be stored, collected, and shared on certain platforms for use by recycling enterprises and other stakeholders. Such data would aid the identification and sorting stages of recycling. More importantly, when dealing with large power batteries for second use in stationary applications, data of the vehicle life of retired batteries are the basis of evaluation for repurposing. It is expected that no insurmountable technical barriers exist to accumulating such data; the biggest obstacle is the reluctance of manufacturers to share data.

9. Conclusions

Energy and environmental issues are inevitable challenges for human development. As important energy storage devices, rechargeable batteries play a vital role in helping to solve these two issues. Consequently, the production of rechargeable batteries is increasing at a high rate every year, and thus the fate of corresponding spent batteries needs to be settled urgently. The four types of rechargeable batteries are similar in structure but quite different in their materials chemistry, leading to different recycling situations. As the first commercial rechargeable battery, the recycling technologies of spent Pbacid batteries are relatively simple and mature. The most noteworthy feature of Pbacid battery recycling is that the Pb pollution must be controlled carefully and minimized to a safe threshold. Similar to Pbacid batteries, the main material composition of Ni-based batteries is also simple, with Ni as the recycling focus. In addition, the recycling of rare-earth elements from Ni-based batteries is attracting more attention because of the increasing demand for these materials. The multiple types and sizes and complex chemical composition of LIBs complicate their recycling. No consensus has been reached on the optimal recycling process for LIBs because there is still much room for improvement of both pyro- and hydrometallurgical technologies. In addition to the most valuable cathode materials, more attention is now being paid to other components of batteries, including electrolytes and anodes.

When systematically evaluating a particular recycling technology with LCA, the factors of efficiency, economy, and environment should all be considered and balanced. With regard to the upcoming substantial number of retired power batteries from EVs, although great uncertainty still exists about the economics of B2U, most researchers are cautiously optimistic about the future development of this strategy. To achieve highly sustainable recycling, a shift to material and structure design from the source is desirable. The proposed 3R and 3E strategy provides a novel perspective on battery recycling and guidance for the future direction of this field.
