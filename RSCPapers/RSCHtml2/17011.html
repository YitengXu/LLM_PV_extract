Abstract

Aryloxybenzothiadiazole (ArOBtz) derivatives show aggregation-induced enhanced emission (AIEE) in the solid-state and are promising candidates for optoelectronic applications. However, understanding the AIEE is a challenging task and is necessary for the rational molecular design of emitters. Therefore, in the present study, electron acceptors (F, CN, NO2, and COOH) on the benzothiadiazole ring have been screened for emission in solution and aggregated phases. Herein, we report QM (DFT/TDDFT) and ONIOM (QM/MM) studies on the four ArOBtz derivatives in comparison with the parent molecule with typical characteristics of AIEE, optoelectronic and non-linear optical properties. Starting from the optimized crystal structure of the parent compound, the structures of the designed clusters have been pre-optimized with MM and then with QM/MM to explore their absorption and emission in the solid phase. The results indicate that in the aggregated phase, the surrounding environment reduces intra-molecular rotations and molecular motion that lead to enhanced emission. Natural bond orbital (NBO) analyses reveal that the ground state structure is stabilized from electron delocalization and operative pushpull effects. Interestingly, nitro-benzothiadiazole exhibits prominent AIEE phenomena, with an emission wavelength beyond 700 nm in solution and in the cluster, reinforced by the magnification of its oscillatory strength by 100 times when aggregated. This dinitro-aryloxy-benzothiadiazole derivative is proposed as a near-infrared emitter for dye-sensitized solar cell, optoelectronic, and non-linear optical applications.

1. Introduction

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) have emerged as promising alternatives to silicon-based conventional photovoltaic solar cells, primarily due to their non-toxicity, abundance, less-complex fabrication methods, and low cost, whilst possessing decent power conversion efficiency, as reported by ORegan and Grtzel.13 A plethora of iridium and ruthenium complexes have been employed as sensitizers in DSSCs.46 However, the limited availability of these metals coupled with their sophisticated synthesis has prompted the need for metal-free organic dyes for DSSC applications.7 In this context, the design of organic fluorophores for optoelectronic applications has seen tremendous growth in recent years.811 Organic solar cells derived from fullerenes have been the focal point in organic semiconductors for almost twenty years.12,13 In the last decade, non-fullerene-based acceptors have become important in organic semiconductor devices due to their tuneable molecular architecture, and more importantly, the discovery of non-fullerene acceptors.14,15 These molecules find a wide range of applications in the fields of organic light-emitting diodes, solar cells, transistors, and molecular imaging.1620 The amendable nature of their optical and electronic properties has led to the synthesis of a myriad of fluorescent organic compounds.8,21,22

However, in the solid phase, organic molecules suffer from a reduction in their emission. This arises from intermolecular stacking interactions and molecular motion resulting in non-radioactive decays, a phenomenon also known as aggregation caused quenching (ACQ). To overcome this, research has been directed towards the synthesis of emitters with twisted conformations to restrict molecular rotations and/or vibrations in the aggregated form.23 As a result, the intensity with which these molecules emit light is more pronounced. This effect was first described by Tang's group,24 and is known as aggregation-induced enhanced emission (AIEE). There have been substantial reports of molecules that exhibit AIEE including, tetraphenylene-bonded benzothiadiazole,25 alkynyl-bonded benzothiadiazole,26 and aryloxy-benzothiadiazole27 moieties.

Recently, Pazini et al. developed aryloxy benzothiadiazole (ArOBtz) based aggregation-induced enhanced emitters which have attracted much attention.28 Designing efficient molecules for better optoelectronic properties requires a comprehensive understanding of the structure at the molecular level. Even though there have been extensive studies on the AIEE behaviour of organic molecules,2933 tuning the same using appropriate substitutions are limited. In addition to these, the computational modelling of AIEE derivatives requires a better understanding of electronic properties in the solution and solid phases. This triggers our curiosity to envisage the AIEE properties of these ArOBtz molecules and to gain insights into the structureproperty relationship for regulation of their optoelectronic properties. Therefore, in this work, ArOBtz derivatives are examined as candidates with better emissive characteristics using density functional theory and its time-dependent formulation (DFT/TDDFT) calculations in solution and aggregated phases. The objective of the present work is to (i) computationally design and model aggregated systems to understand their AIEE and (ii) systematically investigate and understand the impacts of electron-withdrawing groups (EWGs) namely, fluoro, cyano, nitro, and carboxylic acid groups as auxiliary acceptors, on the AIEE property of aryloxy-benzothiadiazole derivatives. Fig. 1 depicts the molecular structures of the compounds. DFT and combined quantum mechanics and molecular mechanics (QM/MM) simulations have been performed to illustrate the role of aggregation on emission. The role of the EWGs on the electronic and optical properties has been investigated using natural bond orbital analysis (NBO) and partial density of state analysis (PDOS). The non-linear optical and optoelectronic properties have been calculated to evaluate their suitability for application in dye-sensitized solar cells.

2. Methodology

Theoretical calculations based on DFT have been performed to divulge the optoelectronic properties of the designed candidates. We have studied the structureproperty relationship of the designed candidates in solution and cluster using QM and two-step MMQM/MM models respectively. The molecules considered in the present study are optimized to understand the electronic structure of the compounds at the singlet ground and excited states at the B3LYP/6-31+g(d,p)3436 level. The optimized geometries are verified for their lowest minimum structure on the potential energy surface from harmonic frequency analyses. PDOS has been computed to investigate the contribution of atoms to the stabilization of frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs). The NBO method3739 has been used to identify the ground state stabilizing interactions and electronic delocalization.40 The non-linear optical property has been evaluated from the first-order hyperpolarizabilities () of the molecules. The optimized geometries are subjected to TDDFT41 calculations to simulate the vertical excitations from the S0 S1 state in gas and solvent phases at the CAM-B3LYP/6-31g(d)42,43 level. The S1 geometry was taken to compute the fluorescence of the molecule. Solvation has been accomplished with the conductor-like polarizable continuum model (CPCM)44,45 wherein the absorption has been recorded in cyclohexane, toluene, dichloromethane, and isopropanol solvents, while the emission was calculated in cyclohexane solvent as reported in the literature.28

To gain insights into the nature of the absorption and emission spectra at the aggregation-induced enhanced emission state, a two-layer ONIOM approach has been adopted.46 This approach requires a cluster of the molecules and an optimum number of molecules for the cluster has to be chosen to model AIEE. Therefore, we have computed the absorption and emission spectra of the parent molecule (HB) on clusters of different numbers of 8, 14, 24, 40, and 78 molecules (ESI, Fig. S1). The results are compared with experimentally reported values (absorption and emission) and it is found that the 8 molecule cluster yields values closer to the experimental ones. Therefore, the aggregation effect is modeled using the ONIOM46 approach with QM/MM calculations using the cluster of 8 molecules. One molecule in the center is treated at the QM level while the MM level is applied to the remaining seven molecules. Theoretical calculations of the QM region have been done at the DFT/6-31G(d) level whereas the universal force field (UFF) was adopted for the MM region with charge equilibration. In the ONIOM calculations, an electronic embedding scheme was employed; this was adopted to incorporate the electrostatic interaction from the MM system in the QM Hamiltonian which allows the wave function to be polarized.46 Here, the optimization for the ground state geometry is modelled using DFT/B3LYP, whereas absorption (S0 S1) and emission (S1 S0) have been calculated using CAM-B3LYP within the TDDFT formalism.

To study AIEE in the aggregated phase for the designed molecules, starting with the optimized cluster of HB, the hydrogens are replaced with the EWGs. However, upon substitution, the packing in the cluster is found to be affected. The substituents are larger than the hydrogens and hence the intermolecular distances in the cluster are greatly reduced, which is to be expected. Therefore, molecular mechanics optimization has been performed with all atoms being treated as flexible in the cluster. This allowed the molecules to adopt equilibrium positions. The MM-optimized structure was then used as an input following the same ONIOM model as adopted for the parent molecule to obtain structural and spectral properties. All structures are confirmed to be the lowest energy structures on the potential energy surface with real frequencies from harmonic frequency analysis. All the calculations have been carried out with the Gaussian 09 program package.47 Gaussview and Chemcraft48 were used to visualize the results.

3. Results and discussion

In the present work, 4-(4-methoxyphenoxy)-7-(4-methoxy-3,5-dimethylphenyl) benzo[c][1.2.5]thiadiazole (HB) (Fig. 1) is chosen as the reference compound from the reported aryloxy-benzothiadiazole derivatives with ArOBtzAr architecture.28 To investigate the role of electron-withdrawing groups in tuning the AIEE character of HB, four new derivatives are designed by substituting the two hydrogens at C13 and C10 on the benzothiadiazole acceptor units with fluorine (F), cyanide (CN), nitro (NO2) and carboxylic acid (COOH) groups and are named FB, CB, NB, and CAB respectively. Herein, we present a comprehensive study on the structureproperty relationship of the four ArOBtz derivatives in solution and aggregated phase using QM and ONIOM models respectively, in comparison with HB as the reference for their electronic and optical properties. The individual molecule is referred to as a monomer throughout the study to compare with the cluster.

C. FMO analysis

The reactivity, stability, electronic and optical properties of a molecule can be understood from the analysis of their frontier molecular orbitals.52 The highest occupied molecular orbitals (HOMO & HOMO1) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbitals (LUMO & LUMO+1) are portrayed in Fig. 6. In the parent molecule, HB, the HOMO is delocalized between the Btz and Ar groups, whereas the ArO group makes up HOMO1. The LUMO is principally positioned over the Btz core. However, the LUMO+1 is spread throughout the molecule. In these molecules, the LUMO is primarily delocalized over the Btz core, however, the LUMO+1 is spread throughout the molecule. This indicates that * transitions (HOMO LUMO) and weak charge transfer transitions (HOMO1 LUMO and HOMO LUMO+1) are possible. However, in all of the designed molecules, upon introducing EWGs, the ArO group also contributes to the HOMO, which previously had only minor contributions from the oxygen (O2) of the aryloxy group. Upon introducing fluorine for hydrogen, the HOMO1 and HOMO are spread throughout the molecule, whereas the LUMO is principally found on the Btz core. This gives rise to * transitions. On the other hand, replacing the hydrogens with CN, NO2, or COOH results in the ArO fragment being chiefly responsible for the stabilization of HOMO in addition to it being spread over the molecule. On the other hand, the HOMO1 is spread throughout the system. The LUMO and LUMO+1 are largely due to the Btz core and the oxygen atom attached to it. As a result, the * transitions are more predominant than charge transfer transitions.

To quantify the contributions of the fragments to the HOMO and LUMO, PDOS analysis has been carried out using QMForge software.5355 The computed PDOS is represented in Fig. 7, wherein the lower portion is for the HOMO, while the upper portion is for the LUMO. The corresponding values are given in SIT 5 (ESI). It can be observed that with an increase in the size of the substituents, the Ar group contributes to the lower amounts in the HOMO. This is due to the increasing dihedral angle (2: C7C9C15C16) between the Ar and Btz groups. Interestingly, fluorine has the highest contribution (34%) to LUMO compared to that of all EWGs. Among the different substituents, cyano contributes to 25% of the stabilization of the HOMO in CB which is from the unsaturated carbonnitrogen triple bond. On the other hand, the cyano, nitro and carboxylic acid groups induce higher contributions from the ArO group for the stabilization of both the HOMO and LUMO. The same observation can be understood from the NBO analysis of the pushpull effect operating in CB, NB, and CAB in Section 3.2. The observations in the FMO diagrams are further substantiated by the PDOS analysis. These observations reveal that the prosthetic nature of the ArO group in HB in contributing towards the HOMO and LUMO has been minimized by allowing it to participate in electronic delocalization.

The electronic levels of HOMO1, HOMO, LUMO, and LUMO+1 are represented in Fig. 8. From the figure, it can be observed that the HOMOLUMO gap decreases upon the replacement of hydrogen with EWGs. Of all derivatives, the nitro substituent has the lowest HOMOLUMO gap with 2.75 eV. The HOMOLUMO gap in FB and CAB are closer to each other as for CB and NB. The LUMO of these compounds is stabilized as NB > CB > CAB > FB > HB, while the HOMO is stabilized as CAB > NB > CB > FB > HB. It is well known that for DSSC applications, the dye molecules are expected to have a HOMO closer to the redox potential of the I/I3 electrolyte to favor electron recovery. Alternatively, a LUMO level closer to the conduction band of TiO2 will facilitate easier electron injection to the conduction band.56 For all the designed candidates, the HOMO levels (6.41 to 5.72 eV) are lower than that for the I/I3 redox couple (4.80 eV).57 Therefore, the regeneration of the oxidized dye is thermodynamically favorable.58 On the other hand, the LUMO levels of these systems (3.26 to 0.83 eV) are higher than the conduction band of TiO2 (4.0 eV).59 Hence, the electron that is excited from the HOMO to the LUMO level can be transferred with much ease to the conduction band of TiO2, more easily in CB and NB. The inferred stability of the molecules is further substantiated by the stabilizing interactions observed in NBO studies [Section III.B. & SIT 4, ESI]. The EWGs result in increased delocalization by involving the almost non-stabilizing ArO group. As a result, they are responsible for the increased stability of the candidates.

Upon aggregation, the HOMOLUMO separation in FB becomes greater than HB but reduces for the remaining compounds [SIT 6, ESI]. For instance, in the cluster, HB has a HOMOLUMO gap of 3.24 eV, and the same increases to 3.32 eV for FB. However, CB and NB are the most stable structures in the clusters, with a HOMOLUMO separation of 2.86 eV and 2.76 eV respectively, as predicted for the single entity. Again, the LUMO and HOMO levels are stabilized to a greater extent in the designed molecules, indicating an efficient electron transfer to the conduction band of TiO2 and a more favorable regeneration of the dye in the clusters. However, in aggregated form, FB is destabilized from the increased HOMOLUMO gap. Therefore, the designed candidates (except for FB) in the cluster can be promising materials for DSSC applications.

D. TDDFT studies

To model the excited state properties of absorption and emission, a suitable functional must be selected that will yield results in accordance with the experimental max values from the TDDFT calculations.60 Therefore, a set of hybrid, dispersion-corrected, PBE-hybrid, meta-hybrid, -dependent gradient-corrected and range separated hybrid XC functionals (B3LYP,34,36 B97XD,61 PBE0,62 M06-2X,63 BMK,64 and CAM-B3LYP42) are employed to compute the absorption maxima of HB in conjugation with the 6-31g(d) basis set [SIT 7, ESI]. The results reveal that M06-2X, CAM-B3LYP, and BMK XC functionals predict values with reduced deviations from the experimentally reported HB. The geometry of the S1 state is then optimized at the M06-2X, CAM-B3LYP, and BMK functionals to compute the S1 S0 emission. The fluorescence values (max) obtained using the CAM-B3LYP XC functional with the 6-31g(d) basis set is closer to the experimental values and is employed for all TD-FT calculations. The recently reported QM/MM calculations employed the 6-31g(d) basis set and hence the same is adopted here for comparing the TDDFT results between the solvent and cluster models.65,66
D.1. Absorption spectra. The UV-vis absorption spectra of the derivatives were recorded in the gas phase and in four different solvents: cyclohexane, toluene, dichloromethane, and 2-propanol, as reported earlier. The TDDFT results of the absorption spectra are given in Table 2 and SIT 8 (ESI). In solution, the max values increase in the following order: CAB < FB < HB < CB < NB. The unmodified parent compound (HB) absorbs at 374 nm in cyclohexane. All transitions are between the singlet ground state (S0) and singlet excited state (S1) and these transitions are allowed, as indicated by the non-zero oscillator strengths. Moreover, the solvatochromism is only 2 nm. Therefore, charge transfer transitions are not observed.

To understand the AIEE nature of the designed compounds, an understanding of the mechanism and the extent to which the intramolecular vibrations and rotations occur in the molecule during the fluorescence from S1 to S0 is necessary.68,69 Reports on the reduction of intramolecular motion and restriction of intramolecular motion being responsible for AIEE can be found in the literature.70,71 This has been accomplished by calculating the root mean squared deviation between the S0 and S1 geometries (Fig. 9). Comparing the ground state and excited state structures, the root mean squared deviation (RMSD) is greater in the case of monomers than the molecule in the QM region of the cluster. It is important to note that the extent to which the RMSD has decreased [RMSD = RMSD (monomer) RMSD (cluster)] upon aggregation is maximum in NB. This reveals that the molecular motions have been greatly restricted in NB. CB stands in second place for this difference. SIT 9 (ESI) lists the key bond parameters in the excited state, from which it is evident that the dihedral angle (2) between the Btz and Ar groups has decreased for CB, NB, and CAB only upon aggregation but is still far from planarity. Hence a twisted architecture is still maintained. Moreover, the dihedral angles 1 and 3 that represent the free rotation of the aryloxy group with respect to the Btz ring are found not to deviate to a greater extent as occurred in solution for the monomer. Hence, upon aggregation, the free rotation of the aryloxy group is also restricted in the excited state. Therefore, the surrounding environmental molecules account for the reduced RMSD of the QM region upon aggregation.72 As a result, there will be a greater possibility for the energy to be emitted radiatively and not utilized for molecular motions (rotations and vibrations), ultimately assisting in enhanced luminescence in the aggregation phase.73,74 Comparing the molecular structures and their fluorescence results from the TDDFT calculations, it is clear that the introduction of nitro groups induces the AIEE effect upon aggregation. The decreased molecular motions when fluorescing from S1 to S0 (from RMSD values for the QM part for the cluster) and reduced RMSD values are responsible for the magnification of the oscillator strength for NB. However, a decrease in the emission wavelength for CB upon aggregation relative to the solution can be observed and the widening of the HOMOLUMO gap could be one of the possible reasons for this. However, CB also exhibits similar RMSD values between the ground and first excited states and NB showed the second-best RMSD. Therefore, its oscillator strength increases by ten times upon aggregation, and hence, displays an aggregation induced blue shift in emission. Interestingly, NB fluoresces in the near infrared region (>700 nm). On the other hand, it is important to note that FB has comparable RMSD in solution and cluster, and hence neither the fluorine substituent nor its surrounding environment is assistive in promoting AIEE.

In summary, we have attempted to design better emitters with AIEE phenomena by replacing hydrogens with electron-withdrawing groups and their electronic structure has been characterized. This has been studied as an isolated molecule in solution and clusters with the help of QM/MM calculations. To model clusters of the designed candidates, we have performed MM optimization to equilibrate the clusters to reduce unfavorable interactions in the cluster and studied it subsequently with QM/MM calculations. The molecular design should maintain the twisted molecular architecture to avoid stacking but improve upon the existing electronic features. By introducing electron-withdrawing groups as auxiliary acceptors to the benzothiadiazole ring, the acceptor unit is made more electron-deficient, but the twisted architecture is maintained. The nitro group turns out to be the most impactful in enhancing the AIEE phenomenon. The design of AIEE-active compounds has been evaluated by analyzing their electronic structure and how it is affected in the presence of different electron-withdrawing groups. The AIEE property of the designed compounds has been validated with TDDFT calculations in solvent as the monomer and in clusters as aggregates. The emission spectra reveal the changes in the electronic structure of the molecules between the ground and excited-state responsible for the AIEE activity. These are the RMSD, RMSD, and oscillator strengths. Finally, the suitability of the designed compounds for optoelectronic applications has been studied. LHE and radiative lifetime can be employed as parameters to understand their optoelectronic applications. With this approach, we have modelled AIEE for nitro benzothiadiazole and it is found to emit in the near-infrared region.

4. Conclusions

Combining QM and ONIOM (QM/MM) calculations in the ground and excited states, the AIEE of aryloxy-benzothiadiazole derivatives (F, CN, NO2, and COOH as substituents) has been studied for optoelectronic applications in solution and aggregated phases respectively. Our results show that the derivatives exhibit twisted structures with an electronic pushpull effect which is established by NBO analysis, necessary for the AIEE property and optoelectronic applications. The electron-withdrawing substituents are found to reduce the HOMOLUMO separation favouring their application as emitters in solar cells. FMO contribution analyses reveal that the aryloxy group stabilizes the NB, CB, and CAB while it is not involved in the stabilization of HB and FB molecules, enhancing its participation in electronic delocalization of the molecule. The designed candidates demonstrate better NLO properties than the parent compound. In solution, the absorption and emission spectra of cyano and nitro derivatives are red-shifted compared to other derivatives. However, upon aggregation, nitro and carboxylic units made the benzothiadiazole a more electron-deficient unit, while the cyano group shifts the emission to lower wavelengths than the parent compound but with a more emissive nature. QM/MM calculations reveal that an increase in the radiative lifetime and light-harvesting efficiency upon aggregation indicates that both cyano and nitro result in better AIEEgenic molecules. Interestingly, when the nitro group is introduced, a near-infrared emission is achieved both in solution and aggregated phase with a nearly non-emissive nitro-Btz displaying strong emission characteristics upon aggregation. The molecular motion in NB has been restricted upon aggregation and hence it emits radiatively displaying greater oscillator strength in the aggregated form. This study reveals that all the designed candidates are qualified to be organic emitters for DSSC applications. In summary, the approach to model AIEE for derivatives without a crystal structure with MM optimization followed by a QM/MM study will enable in designing better candidates for optoelectronic applications. Moreover, RMSD, RMSD, and oscillator strength are useful parameters to explain the phenomenon of AIEE in the designed candidates. A more accurate computational exploration of AIEE could be accomplished by employing molecular dynamics simulations instead of MM optimization and this is in the pipeline.
