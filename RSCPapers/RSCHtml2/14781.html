Abstract

As a green and earth abundant material, graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4) with aromatic tri-s-triazine units has become a new research hotspot and drawn broad interdisciplinary attention in area of energy storage and conversion and in recent years has shown great potential for application in supercapacitors, which makes it a promising candidate to complement graphene in material chemistry. It exhibits a wealth of attractive properties, including chemical and thermal stability, a nitrogen rich framework, environmentally friendly characteristics and mild synthesis conditions. However, g-C3N4 is mainly limited in electrochemical-related applications because of its inherent low electronic conductivity and low surface area. This review summarizes recent advances in the usage of g-C3N4-based systems as electrode materials for supercapacitors and difficulties of carbon nitride usage as a supercapacitor (low conductivity and low surface area) and methods of overcoming these problems. We present the recent advances in the field of composite materials that include at least one carbon nitride-based component for supercapacitor electrodes. We focus on cases in which a single material by itself suffers from a drawback that can be overcome by combining it with other components, enabling the fabrication of a composite material with enhanced performance. Finally, the future opportunities of carbon nitride are addressed for use in supercapacitor devices.

1. Introduction

Electrochemical energy storage has become an important necessity in today's world. Much attention has been paid to finding an optimal way of approaching this venture from both theoretical and applied aspects. Electrochemical energy is plentiful, clean, and renewable. To utilize it efficiently, though, an easy-to-maintain, durable storage device must be found that can be manufactured at a reasonable price. An electromechanical capacitor, also known as a supercapacitor, is one such device which operates at a much higher power capacity compared to conventional batteries, while the energy density it holds is significantly lower. Consequently, it has been commonly used in portable electronics, power back-up devices, and also, in hybrid electric vehicles (HEVs) where it delivers cranking power for the system.13 Electrode materials have been attracting the focus of many research studies in recent years with the aim of providing better means of utilizing them.47 Different types of materials, such as carbon, conducting polymer, metal (oxide, sulfide and phosphide) organic framework, and MXene, are used as electrodes in supercapacitors.824 Among these, carbon supercapacitors, due to their singular properties (e.g. low weight, natural abundance, facile availability, large surface area, high electrical conductivity, and excellent chemical stability, rapid charge/discharge kinetics along with bipolar operational flexibility) stand out.2536 Heteroatom doping of carbon materials (e.g. O, N, B, S and P) also plays a vital role in their volumetric performances. Heteroatoms have also been confirmed to show great potential in improving capacitance in carbon materials by providing additional pseudo-capacitance through surface faradaic reactions. By furnishing a pair of electrons, they can change the carbon materials' donoracceptor characteristics, which in turn leads to an increase in pseudo-capacitive reactions.3741 Nitrogen has been one of the most widely probed heteroatoms when it comes to its function in supercapacitors. Its functional groups have been recently proved to increase the wettability of electrodes, and consequently, improve their electrical conductivity and capacitive performance. N-doping satisfies the conditions necessary to improve electron-donor properties, decreases the work-function, provides high surface energy and surface reactivity, and also, supplies high pseudo-capacitance through additional faradaic reactions.1,37 Introduction of nitrogen into carbonaceous materials is achieved through two dominant routes, post-treatment and in situ.4245 When using post-treatment, carbon materials are treated with N-rich reagents in plasma or CVD, incorporating nitrogen-containing functionalities. Here, the nitrogen doping procedure is quite complicated, can be toxic, and results in low concentrations of nitrogen. Also, since nitrogen-containing functional groups can only be incorporated on the surface of carbon materials, they are usually chemically unstable. Furthermore, special instruments are required to conduct the procedure, and the necessary conditions are hard to provide. A more accessible way to produce nitrogen-containing carbon materials for supercapacitors as electrodes is therefore required. In the in situ method, a variety of N-enriched carbon precursors are used to produce nitrogen-containing carbon materials. This is an easy-to-handle operation making the in situ approach a favorable method to synthesize nitrogen-doped carbon materials.4648

Graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4) is a two-dimensional (2D) graphite-like structure which has emerged as a desirable material due to its high nitrogen content, exceptional chemical and thermal stability, unique optical features, attractive electronic structure, environmentally friendly properties, and simple synthesis conditions, which make it applicable in many fields, including catalysis, hydrogen storage, capture and storage of carbon dioxide, water decontamination, solar energy conversion, and even detection of toxic molecules.4956 However, due to its innate low electronic conductivity and small surface area, g-C3N4 is not suitable for electrochemical applications for the most part. As such, it has been the subject of many studies, which have led to acquisition of in-depth knowledge in subjects such as history of CNs, their synthesis, structure, and multipurpose utilizations.5760 In comparison to these intensively investigated areas, the prospect of using g-C3N4 and its hybrids as electrode materials for supercapacitors is in the primarily stages. Although most of the carbon nitride applications have been extensively reviewed in numerous articles,5765 their usage as a supercapacitor has almost been overlooked or just a short discussion is provided in these review articles. Therefore, a comprehensive review of the application of carbon nitride in supercapacitors can open a new way in this field. The two-dimensional (2D) graphite-like carbon nitride (g-C3N4) features desirable physicochemical characteristics in addition to a nitrogen-rich framework, and also supplies more active reaction sites for electron donor/acceptors streamlining additional pseudo-capacitance contribution. Furthermore, it is easy to prepare. On the other hand, there are major obstacles (e.g. low conductivity and low surface area) to overcome to make it applicable. Over the past three years, numerous articles have been published outlining these obstacles and ways to get around them. Our aim in this study is to provide a review of the recent advances in the usage of g-C3N4-based systems as electrode materials for supercapacitors. Also, the recent advances made in the field of hybrid materials that include at least one carbon nitride-based component for supercapacitor electrodes will be covered. In many instances, a material's shortcomings can be overcome by making appropriate modifications or by its combination with other materials (e.g. combining g-C3N4 with other nanomaterials). Significant enhancements in photochemical and electrochemical properties can be achieved in some cases. All of this will be the focus of this study.

In conclusion, a summary of the new directions the field is taking and interesting new prospects at the very cutting edge of the field will be provided. g-C3N4-based supercapacitors, due to their unique characteristics, have the potential to provide us with the means to achieve a sustainable future without harming our environment.

2. Synthesis, structure and properties of g-C3N4

There are generally seven phases of C3N4, including -C3N4, -C3N4, cubic C3N4, pseudocubic C3N4, g-h-triazine, g-o-triazine, and g-h-heptazine with band gaps of about 5.49, 4.85, 4.30, 4.13, 2.97, 0.93, and 2.88 eV, respectively. It has been demonstrated that the fundamental tectonic units establishing g-C3N4 allotropes are triazine (C3N3) and tri-s-triazine/heptazine (C6N7) rings (Fig. 1).57 Due to the size of the nitride pores and the different electronic environments of the N atom, energy stability is achieved. Tri-s-triazine based g-C3N4 has provided the most favorable energy readings. Furthermore, it is the most stable C3N4 phase under ambient conditions.66 Polymeric g-C3N4 consists of easily accessible carbon and nitrogen elements, and as such, is suitable for providing the required reactions to alter its surface activity without changing its structure and composition significantly. The polymeric feature of g-C3N4 allows for easy tuning of its surface chemistry at the molecular level. Also, it has the lowest band gap among the seven phases of C3N4, due to the presence of sp2-hybridized carbon and nitrogen, establishing its -conjugated electronic structures.67 According to thermogravimetric analyses, g-C3N4 is thermally stable at temperatures as high as 600 C in air, which makes it the most stable allotrope among all C3N4 structures. This stability can be attributed to the presence of CN heterocycles in its structure.68 In addition to that g-C3N4 is also chemically stable and not dissolved under harsh chemical conditions such as acids, alkalis, or organic solvents, making it as a robust material under ambient conditions.69 Having all these desirable properties, g-C3N4 is believed to be a promising metal-free semiconductor photocatalyst, and can be expected to bring about many changes in the field of photocatalytic applications. On the other hand, bare g-C3N4 has a high recombination rate of charge carriers and also lacks a desirable electrical conductivity.57,70 Due to its high condensation degree and the presence of the heptazine ring structure, g-C3N4 demonstrates several advantages including high physicochemical stability and a unique electronic band structure. Layered g-C3N4 exhibits a C/N ratio of almost 0.75, which can be further tuned by choosing suitable nitrogen-rich precursors and condensation approaches. Precursor type and reaction conditions have been recognized as pivotal factors influencing physiochemical properties such as the C/N ratio, specific surface area, porosity, absorption edge, and nanostructure shapes and morphologies in g-C3N4.57

As a result of structural imperfections and deficient condensation, the amine functional groups demonstrated about 2% hydrogen content when the molar ratio of C/N was lower than 0.75 (the ideal number for g-C3N4).71 Achieving a lower hydrogen amount through an efficient approach is a challenging endeavor, and consequently, producing an ideal, fully condensed g-C3N4 with a stoichiometric C/N ratio of 0.75 is not simple. It has been observed that the presence of a trace amount of amine groups can result in improvements in g-C3N4's surface activity which in turn results in better interactions with reactant molecules.72

Many methods such as solgel, CVD, shock wave compression, solvothermal procedures, polymer precursor condensation, and pyrolysis have been used to produce CNs. Generally speaking, carbon nitride materials produced by direct condensation of nitrogen containing organic sacrificial precursors (for example, urea, thiourea, melamine, dicyandiamide and cyanamide) are bulk materials with small surface areas, in most cases below 10 m2 g1. The effects of precursors and synthesis parameters on g-C3N4 structures with various properties have been extensively compared and discussed by Ong et al.57,73,74

3. The role of nitrogen

Nitrogen atom doping enhances the physical properties (such as conductivity and wettability) in carbon materials.1 Substitutional introduction of the more electron-rich N into the C network brings more electrons to the delocalized -system of carbon materials which results in improved electrical conductivity. Furthermore, in comparison with non-polar carbons, N-containing carbons are fused with more heterogeneous species, and as such, demonstrate better wettability. The polar functional groups in the surface exhibit high affinity to solvents with the same polarity, especially in the pervasively used aqueous systems. This hydrophilic class favors the wetting of electrodes first and ion transportation next; therefore, the capacitive performance in the material is improved. In addition, the injection of extra electrons results in an increase in the density of states at the Fermi level, and thus, improves the quantum capacitance of graphene near the Fermi level in comparison with the pristine undoped material. Moreover, the nitrogen inclusion enriches pristine carbon materials with more redox reactions for pseudo-capacitors, whose specific capacitances are several orders of magnitude greater than those of traditional EDLCs without losing the fast charge/discharge kinetics.48

As shown in Fig. 2, N-rich functional groups include pyrrolic N (N-5), pyridinic/pyridone N (N-6), oxidized pyridine-N (N-X) and quaternary N (N-Q), in general. Pyrrolic N atoms and pyridinic N incorporated into five and six membered heterocyclic rings are bonded to two carbon atoms and consequently, result in electron donor properties. Pyridone nitrogen atoms donate electrons as well, when they are adjacent to a ring carbonhydroxyl group. The pyridinic and pyrrolic N are always placed at the edge of the graphitic carbon plane. Quaternary N atoms, are put inside the graphitic carbon plane and are bonded with three carbon atoms. They can be positioned both at the edge and within the graphene layers. N-Q can significantly enhance electron transfer and significantly elevate the electrical conductivity of carbon materials.

Pyrrolic N tends to be gradually transformed to pyridinic and quaternary N at temperatures above 600 C, while pyridinic N converts into quaternary N at temperatures above 800 C by thermal treatment, owing to the much higher thermal stability of quaternary N. However, these alterations are only possible at high temperatures and are unlikely under mild electrochemical conditions. On the other hand, the conversion between pyridone nitrogen and N-6, or N-X and N-6 are reasonably within reach under common electrochemical conditions.

4. Carbon nitride based supercapacitors

As noticed, carbon nitrides as defective graphene-like structures are a kind of very important functional material, which have become good catalysts due to their rich non-graphitic nitrogen except when used as electrode materials due to their poor electrical conductivity.

Note that layered g-C3N4 with ultrahigh nitrogen content is fabricated by directly heating cyanamide or melamine at 550 C. Although a large amount of nitrogen atoms in the structure are redox-active non-graphitic, the wide bandgap (2.7 eV) hinders it from being used in energy storage applications due to the low conductivity and low surface area. Hence, it is essential to develop a physical or chemical procedure to modify CNs' electronic and textural properties. Different approaches have been accepted to resolve these concerns such as morphological control of the CN nanostructure, synthesis of conductive carbon nitride, compositing with other materials and combining some of these methods. Also, g-C3N4 could be used as both the sacrificial template and N-doping source. In the following sections, we review the recent advances on the metal-free g-C3N4, the g-C3N4/transition metal oxide, metal hydroxide, metal sulfide, and the g-C3N4/conductive polymer for improved supercapacitor applications.

Nanostructures (tubes, wires, rods, sheets, spheres, etc.) are found have more efficiency than their bulk counterparts. For supercapacitors, their performance was found to be higher than that of their bulk materials owing to their higher specific surface area. The large specific surface area prepares more active sites for reaction, shortens ion diffusion paths and increases the redox ability of charge carriers. In this case, Tahir et al. have synthesized tubular g-C3N4 through treating melamine and ethylene glycol solution with HNO3 before annealing at 450 C to develop high performance supercapacitors.76 Tubular-like structures have a dense and uniform morphology with an average diameter of about 0.8 m (Fig. 3A), tubular g-C3N4 has significant advantages over its bulk component because of its distinctive morphology and high surface area (182.61 m2 g). The prepared tubular g-C3N4 exhibited a good specific capacitance of 233 F g1 at a current density of 0.2 A g1 in 6 M KOH electrolyte. Furthermore, the tubular g-C3N4 retained 90% of its initial capacitance after 1000 cycles. Also, this research group has synthesized g-C3N4 nanofibers (GCNNFs) with a 1D structure without using any hard or soft template by a green and facile route.77 The surface area of GCNNF is found to be much higher (165 m2 g1) in comparison to the GCN sample (4 m2 g1). The high surface area of GCNNF provides a large electrodeelectrolyte interface contact area and thus improvement in mass transfer characteristics, as well as increased redox capability. GCNNF grew in fibrous morphology having a very dense and uniform structure with an average diameter of 100 nm and length of about 20 m as shown in Fig. 3B. The GCNNF supercapacitor electrode shows a high specific capacitance of 263.75 F g1 and excellent cycling stability in 0.1 M Na2SO4 aqueous electrolyte with a capacitance retention of 93.6% after 2000 successive cycles at a current density of 1 A g1. GCNNFs exhibit a high capacitance of 208 F g1 even at 10 A g1, with an appreciable capacitance retention of 89.5%, which indicated its good rate capability.

This seems to be conflicting with the fact that rich non-graphitic nitrogen and high electrical conductivity coexist in a hypothetical material of conductive carbon nitride (CCN) or the as-called few layer carbon simultaneously has both high nitrogen doping and electrical conductivity. Although the reported graphene materials as promising electrode materials possess high conductivity, they still suffer from lack of efficient nitrogen doping as redox active centers during energy storage applications. Xu et al. reported a CCN with a unique tubular structure synthesized by the combination of nitrogen rich precursor and atomic NiN coordination.78 Ni(HNCN)2, acting as the carbon, nitrogen, and Ni catalyst source simultaneously, is the perfect raw material for non-graphitic nitrogen-rich few layer carbon synthesis. Optimization between heteroatom doping and conductivity is achieved by tuning the temperature of the space-confined reaction. Remarkably, an extraordinarily high N doping level (15 at%), especially the ultrahigh contents of redox active pyridinic N (9 at%) and pyrrolic N (5 at%), and a high conductivity of 2.3 S cm1 are obtained for CCN at a temperature of 800 C (CCN-800). The leading method that has been used for synthesis of carbon nitride by condensation of cyanamide or melamine is too resistive (Fig. 4a). They suggest a possible analogous CVD procedure to obtain CCN as shown in Fig. 4b: starting from Ni (HNCN)2 which provides self-grown fragments of NCN or NCCN and Ni metal catalysts for graphene growth in the space-confined reaction, CCN is achieved by the combination of a nitrogen rich precursor and atomic NiN coordination. The crystal structure of Ni(HNCN)2 showed that each Ni2+ ion is coordinated by six nitrogen atoms, indicating a large amount of covalent NiN bonding. Upon heating, the in situ produced metallic Ni from Ni(HNCN)2 would catalyze amorphous carbon into sp2 carbon and atomic NiN bonding could keep nitrogen active sites stable. After acid etching for Ni removal, the final product with a unique tubular structure is about 70 nm in diameter with a hollow interior structure and is well imaged by transmission electron microscopy as shown in Fig. 4c. The HRTEM image (Fig. 4d) shows that the shells consist of few interconnected curved multilayers (69), providing an electron transport highway with excellent electrical conductivity. The elemental mappings of EELS (Fig. 4e and f) show the distribution of carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen elements in the CCN, in which nitrogen and oxygen dopants exhibit brighter contrast in curved regions than the relatively flat area. The specific capacitances of 372, 273 and 230 F g1 are obtained at 2, 50,100 mV s1 with two additional faradaic peaks in acidic media, demonstrating its good capacitive performance.

4.1. Carbon nitride composite with carbon nanomaterials

Taking into account the high conductivity and stability of carbon materials, some researchers attempted to synthesize composites of g-C3N4 and other carbon materials including carbon spheres, carbon nanofibers, graphene derivatives, and carbon nanodots to improve the conductivity and performance of g-C3N4 for application in supercapacitors. Graphene sheets are advanced 2D carbon nanomaterials composed of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal network. Graphene has many advantages, such as large specific surface area and outstanding electrical, mechanical and thermal properties. Since both g-C3N4 and graphene possess similar graphitic microstructures and g-C3N4 contains a high-loading of nitrogen functional groups, modifying graphene with g-C3N4 is considered to be an effective strategy to increase the performance of the composite material in comparison to a single analogous component.

Chen et al. have prepared graphene/g-C3N4 composites (g-C3N4@G) via a hydrothermal reaction and lyophilization.81 The process for the synthesis of 3D g-C3N4@G is illustrated in Fig. 5A-a. The hydrothermal procedure permits the formation of g-C3N4 grafted graphene basal planes with a loosely packed 3D structure with a density of 13.2 mg cm3 as shown in Fig. 5A-b and c similar to that of the common 3D graphene. The conductivity of g-C3N4@G (0.38 S m1) is quite comparable to that of the pristine graphene sample (0.35 S m1). It was observed that the composite shows the highest specific capacitance of 264 F g1 at a current density of 0.4 A g1 from galvanic charge and discharge tests in LiClO4 as the electrolyte medium. This capacitance value showed more than 75% improvement over that of the supercapacitor made from a pure 3D graphene electrode (152 F g1). The results revealed that the reported 3D composite material is much desirable as an electrode material in supercapacitors. Furthermore, they found that g-C3N4 was advantageous in such a way that it could decrease the self-aggregation of graphene nanosheets, leading to an improvement in capacity and also cycle performance with no obvious degradation that could be observed during the cycling experiment over 10000 chargedischarge cycles, demonstrating that the prepared g-C3N4@G supercapacitor has good long-term electrochemical stability.

Wen et al. used hydrothermal reaction to prepare graphene/g-C3N4 composites with various ratios of g-C3N4 and these electrodes were used as negative electrodes in asymmetric supercapacitors with Ni-MOF/CNT as the positive electrode in 6 M KOH.82 RGO/C3N4-X (X = 2, 3, 4, 5) was adopted to name the composite with different contents of g-C3N4 nanosheets. For comparison, pure rGO was also synthesized through a similar procedure without the presence of g-C3N4 nanosheets. rGO/C3N4-3 exhibits a higher current density response compared to the bare rGO at the same scan rate, demonstrating the higher specific capacitance of rGO/C3N4-3. This observation lies in the fact that the heteroatoms (nitrogen in the C3N4 and/or oxygen on the rGO), which induce electron donor characteristics in carbon, can not only improve the electron transfer and promote the wettability of the interface between the electrolyte and the electrode, but also generate reversible pseudocapacitance, being in good agreement with the CV shape (Fig. 5B-a). By contrast, the smaller current density response than that of the bare rGO is observed by increasing the doping content to 4 mg and 5 mg, which is attributed to a smaller electrical double-layer contribution of g-C3N4 at higher values of g-C3N4. The galvanostatic chargedischarge curves of all electrodes at a current density of 1 A g1 are displayed in Fig. 5B-b. Noticeably, all curves have a nearly linear and symmetrical shape with a slight curvature, representing good capacitive properties and electrochemical reversibility. The specific capacitance of all electrodes at different chargedischarge current densities is summarized in Fig. 5B-c. It can be seen that the specific capacitance of rGO/C3N4-3 decreases from 288 F g1 to 212 F g1 with the increment of current density from 0.5 A g1 to 10 A g1, retaining 74% of its initial specific capacitance.

A porous nitrogen-rich carbon material from carbon self-repairing g-C3N4 (CC3N4) assembled with graphene was primarily reported by Ding et al.83 They designed a facile strategy for the construction of a monolith of porous nitrogen-rich carbon via solution self-assembly of a carbon-repairing g-C3N4 and GO colloidal suspension. The carbon source from ethanol was in situ introduced into g-C3N4 during the polymerization of melamine (MA) in the solvothermal process by hydrogen bonding. Then, GO sheets were introduced into CC3N4 to form porous frameworks through cross-linking interactions, such as and hydrogen-bonding interactions. During the hydrothermal process, GO was transformed into reduced graphene oxide (rGO) to obtain the CC3N4@rGO hybrid. A bulk C3N4 powder and GO colloidal suspension was also prepared by the same procedure (labelled as C3N4@rGO). The synthesis method is schematically illustrated in Fig. 5C. The material exhibits a specific capacitance as high as 379.7 F g1 and energy density of 52.7 W h kg1 at a current density of 0.25 A g1 from galvanic charge and discharge tests and 85% capacitance retention after 10000 galvanic chargedischarge cycles at a high constant current density of 10 A g1. To highlight the critical role of CC3N4 in the CC3N4@rGO hybrid, the pristine rGO and C3N4@rGO hybrid samples were investigated to estimate the electrochemical performance. The specific capacitance of CC3N4@rGO was 379.7 F g1, much higher than the 211.4 F g1 for C3N4@rGO and 195.1 F g1 for bare rGO at a current density of 0.25 A g1. The improved capacitance can be ascribed to the better electrical conductivity, which arises from: (1) extending delocalized p-electrons by activation of inert g-C3N4 through the formation of substitutional or interstitial C configuration; (2) the intimate contact between rGO and CC3N4, caused by the extended p-conjugated planar layers of CC3N4, and the larger specific surface area of hybrid material. It is worth noting that the CC3N4@rGO still retained a large specific capacitance of 252 F g1 at a high current density of 10 A g1.

A large molecular structure limits the number of nitrogen atoms at the edge of the structure, which are the major contributors for pseudo-capacitance. Hence, Lin et al. developed a facile route to both increase the edge nitrogen content of g-C3N4 and the functional groups for use as strong hinges when combined with graphene materials by pre-oxidizing the g-C3N4 in concentrated nitric acid (Fig. 6a).84 The treatment of g-C3N4 with oxidizing agents such as concentrated nitric acid successfully cuts the large layered g-C3N4 molecules to smaller segments. As a result, the proportion of edge nitrogen atoms to all the nitrogen content in g-C3N4 is increased theoretically, as illustrated by Fig. 6b. The composite displayed high specific capacitance values of 265.6 F g1 and 243.8 F g1 in a three-electrode configuration in acid and alkaline electrolytes, respectively. These capacitance values are nearly 60% and 45% higher than the corresponding specific capacitance of the composite made using the g-C3N4 and graphene without peroxidation of g-C3N4. The material possesses an excellent cycling stability with over 94% capacitance retention after 5000 CV cycles in both alkaline and acid electrolytes. As shown in Fig. 6c, the contributions of surface capacitive effects and diffusion controlled processes are 86.6% and 13.4%, respectively. Therefore, the surface capacitive effects are believed to dominate the capacitance of the composite sample. It is believed that the oxidation of g-C3N4 by strong oxidizing agents leads to higher electron conductivity of the composite material by breaking the polymeric structures and inducing higher levels of edge N and O doping of the composites. In addition, it is recognizable that more homogeneous dispersion of oxidized C3N4 on graphene oxide layers results in better ion diffusion ability of the composite sample. The higher solubility in water of oxidized C3N4 avoids the aggregation of the polymeric material on the graphene oxide layers as more channels are available for ion transportation within the composite sample. As a result, ions can move more freely without being hindered by large polymeric chunks.

In another research, g-C3N4/mesoporous carbon sphere composites were successfully synthesized using a simple, three-step treatment, and the effects of the mesoporous carbon sphere (MPCS) content on the electrochemical properties of the composites were also studied.85 The MPCSs were homogeneously captured and dispersed on the prepared graphitic carbon nitride (GCN), forming a GCN/MPCS nanocomposite structure. This study showed that there is an optimum amount of MPCSs that maximizes the capacitance. In addition, the number of MPCS particles is not proportional to the electrochemical capacitance. MPCSs increase the capacitance by increasing the specific surface area but also prevent the electrolyte from accessing the GCN. Thus, the sample containing moderate amounts of MPCSs, GCN/MPCS (2) typically 1:2 by weight (GCN/MPCS), showed excellent supercapacitive performance with a specific capacitance as high as 352.44 F g1 at a scan rate of 5 mV s1.

Broccoli-like porous carbon nitride is synthesized by simple one-step carbonization of a composite comprising a Zn-based metal organic framework (ZIF-8) and melamine.86 The introduction of melamine into the ZIF-8 framework not only increases the N content of the composite and the surface area of the carbonization product, but also induces the formation of a flower-like structure. The carbon obtained from the ZIF-8/melamine composite by the proposed carbonization process is found to have a unique three-dimensional broccoli-like shape, a nanoscale size, and an extremely high doping N content (28.3 at%). These properties substantially improve the electrochemical performance of the composite, as represented by a high specific capacitance of 359.1 F g1 at a current density of 1 A g1, much higher than that of ZIF-8.

A facile two-step method is used to synthesize graphitic carbon nitride/nitrogen doped carbon sphere (g-CN/NCS) composites by the co-calcination of glucose, urea and carbon nanospheres (Fig. 7).87 The carbon spheres could work as spacers to separate the ultrathin g-CN sheets, leading to an expanded interlayer distance and more exposed sites with double-bonded nitrogen. In turn, g-CN sheets work as conductive networks among carbon spheres to improve the conductivity of the whole composite electrode. As a result, the as-prepared g-CN/NCS material exhibits a significantly enhanced electrochemical capacitance, a great rate capability and high durability in 1 M H2SO4 solution due to the synergistic effects. The synthesized g-CN/NCS shows excellent electrochemical energy storage performance as an electrode material for supercapacitors in terms of good specific capacitances (403.6 F g1 at 0.1 A g1 and 220 F g1 at 10 A g1) and long-term cycle stability without any capacitance decay after 5000 GCD cycles at 20 A g1. The enhanced performances of g-CN/NCS can be ascribed to its stable 3D porous architecture, high nitrogen doping level, and enhanced conductivity owing to the introduction of NCS, which provides efficient pathways for electron and ion transportation and abundant electroactive sites for charge storage.

Transition metal sulfides (TMSs) have received growing interest as superior electrode materials for energy storage devices, such as lithium-ion batteries and supercapacitors. Compared to other nitrogen-containing carbon micro/nanomaterials, g-C3N4 can offer more active sites due to its high nitrogen content, improving the electron donor properties, and the wettability of electroactive materials, resulting in efficient charge transfer. 4.6. g-C3N4 as a sacrificial templates and N-doping sources

Regardless considerable efforts have been allocated to the simple synthesis of g-C3N4 nanosheets to improve their optical and electrical properties,133136 little attention has been paid to using CNs as sacrificial templates and N-doping sources. Interestingly, since C3N4 is regarded as a promising N source to produce nitrogen-doped carbon materials because of its easy accessibility and fairly high N content which undergoes complete thermolysis at 750 C,137 layered g-C3N4 can be exploited as a self-sacrificial template, thereby making additional purification and isolation steps necessary. Motivated by this perspective, Li et al. pioneered a two-step heating of a mixture of dicyandiamide (for the in situ formation of g-C3N4) and glucose for acquisition of directly synthesized graphene-like flakes.138 Fischer and co-workers synthesized ternary metal nitride nanoparticles using g-C3N4 as both the reactant reagent and template,139 Wen et al. synthesized highly crumpled N-doped graphene nanosheets (C-NGNSs) using bulk g-C3N4 as the N source.140Fig. 18 schematically describes the entire procedure for preparing C-NGNSs, which starts from graphene oxide (GO) and cyanamide (NH2CN). The oxygen-containing groups in GO react with amine groups from NH2CN to form either amides or alkyl ammonium ions via ring-opening amination of epoxides. Cyanamide was deposited on the GO surface due to the hydrogen bonding and electrostatic attractive forces between negatively charged GO and positively charged cyanamide during drying. The obtained GO-NH2CN product was then transferred to a furnace and heated at 400 C to induce the polymerization of cyanamide and thus form a thin C3N4 polymer layer on the surface of the GO (GO@p-C3N4). The calcination temperature was tuned between 750 and 900 C for the production of C-NGNS samples, so that GO could be effectively reduced to graphene and the C3N4 polymer on graphene could completely decompose into nitrogen-containing species (e.g., C2N2+, C3N2+, and C3N3+)141 that provide nitrogen sources for finally evolving into C-NGNSs. On the other hand, the formation of the C3N4 polymer will alleviate the stacking of graphene during the annealing treatment. The C-NGNSs-900 possesses a specific capacitance approaching 248.4 F g1 at 5 mV s1 and good cycle stability.

This obstacle was addressed by Li et al. by virtue of plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition (PECVD), which can dissociate the feedstock gases at lower temperatures.145 They evaluated a protocol for g-C3N4 template-directed in situ synthesis of nitrogen-rich porous graphene-like carbon sheets (NPGCs) by using PECVD followed by a high-temperature pyrolysis process, which totally avoids the post-synthetic template removal. As supercapacitor electrodes, the NPGCs show a quite encouraging specific capacitance of 261 F g1 at 1 A g1, excellent rate capability (189 F g1 even at 100 A g1), and outstanding cycle performance (97% capacitance retention at 10 A g1 after 20000 cycles).

The p-C3N4 and Ti2CTx complexes are then heat-treated to obtain nitrogen-doped Ti2CTx nanosheets. The triazine-based p-C3N4 decomposes above 700 C; thus, the nitrogen species can be surely doped into the internal carbon layer and/or defect sites of Ti2CTx nanosheets at 900 C. The extended interlayer distance and c-lattice parameters (c-LPs of 28.66 ) of Ti2CTx confirm that the p-C3N4 grown between layers delaminate the nanosheets of Ti2CTx during the doping process. CV curves for pristine Ti2CTx, 500N-Ti2CTx, 700N-Ti2CTx, 900N-Ti2CTx, and 900-Ti2CTx (without NH2CN) in 6 M KOH electrolyte at a scan rate of 100 mV s1 are shown in Fig. 19b. In contrast, the CV curves of the 500N-Ti2CTx and 700N-Ti2CTx show a lower integrated area than that of the pristine Ti2CTx electrode. Interestingly, the CV curve of 900N-Ti2CTx is the most rectangular, and its integrated area is the largest in comparison with those of other electrodes. Electrodes prepared under controlled conditions display smaller integrated areas in CV curves than that of 900N-Ti2CTx. These results demonstrated that p-C3N4 induced nitrogen doping in Ti2CTx which is effective in increasing the electrochemical performance. The relationship between current (extracted from the current density of the electrode at 0.35 V for the CV profile of the discharge curve) and the applied sweep rate is shown in Fig. 19b. The evolved discharge currents of the 900N-Ti2CTx electrode have an almost linear relationship with scan rates ranging from 10 to 100 mV s1. The value of R2 = 0.9999 (the pristine-Ti2CTx electrode shows the value of R2 = 0.9995) implies that the electrochemical reaction was noticeably rapid enough and occurred over the entire electrode surface, even at a high scan rate, while 500N-Ti2CTx and 700N-Ti2CTx electrodes did not show a linear dependence (R2 = 0.5990 and 0.9484, respectively). Based on the results from Fig. 19a and b, although pristine-Ti2CTx as an electrode still showed high performance, it can be concluded that polymerization for growth of p-C3N4 on the surface of Ti2CTx has a negative effect on Ti2CTx due to nonconductive and electrochemical inertness of p-C3N4 covering the reactive surface of Ti2CTx. However, after the decomposition in the nitrogen doping process at a high temperature of 900 C (900N-Ti2CTx), the electrochemical performance is significantly enhanced even in comparison with Ti2CTx. Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of all samples in 6 M KOH electrolyte are observed at a current density of 1 A g1 (Fig. 19d). As reported, pristine-Ti2CTx resulted in a capacitor electrode with symmetric charge/discharge characteristics, but the charge/discharge curves of 500N-Ti2CTx and 700N-Ti2CTx electrodes show asymmetric curves as p-C3N4 formed on the surface. However, as expected in CV curves, the charge curves of the 900N-Ti2CTx are almost symmetric with respect to the discharge curves within the voltage range of 0.00.75 V without any significant IR drop, indicating that the capacitive reversibility of the 900N-Ti2CTx electrode is quite high, which is consistent with its electrochemical performances. High nitrogen doping (15.48%) in Ti2CTx improves the electrochemical performance and energy storage ability. Due to the synergetic effect of delaminated structures and heteroatom compositions, N-doped Ti2CTx shows excellent characteristics as an electrochemical capacitor electrode, including rectangular cyclic voltammetry results, high capacitance (327 F g1 at 1 A g1, increased by 140% over pristine-Ti2CTx), and long-term stability performance (96.2% capacitance retention after 5000 cycles) at high current density (5 A g1).

5. Conclusion and outlook

The field of supercapacitors has gained much attention as the focus of intensive, multidisciplinary studies by materials, surface and electrochemical science groups throughout the world. The importance of supercapacitors for energy storage has pronouncedly increased as well. The performance of a supercapacitor relies on factors such as electrochemical properties of electrode materials used, electrolyte and voltage range. However, most research studies are focused on the development of new electrode materials that will yield better performances. Among the challenges faced in this century is unquestionably energy storage. Therefore, it is important that new, environmentally friendly and low-cost energy storage systems be found, in response to the needs of emerging ecological concerns and modern society. In recent years, graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4) has begun to attract attention in the field of supercapacitor electrode materials because of its unique electronic feature, 2D structure and facile preparation process.61 Furthermore, it was reported that high nitrogen content in g-C3N4 can provide active sites for faradaic reactions, which could increase the surface polarity, improve the wettability of the electrode and consequently, enhance the mass transfer efficiency.43,4648 Furthermore, like other two-dimensional materials such as graphene, g-C3N4 also possesses excellent mechanical strength and flexibility as the covalently bonded 2D nanomaterials can bear certain elastic deformation without breaking.102 However, some properties of pristine g-C3N4, such as low surface area and poor conductivity, restrict its applications in many fields.57,84 Therefore, most research studies have focused on the modification of g-C3N4. The g-C3N4, coupling with other nanomaterials to form hybrid materials, exhibits great improvement in its photochemical and electrochemical properties. Different strategies including constructing hybrid materials with high electrical conductivity and downsizing those to the nanoscale have been proved to be effective in enhancing the electrochemical performance.76,83,84 The single-layer g-C3N4 easily restacks because of the van der Waals forces, resulting in low specific power and energy density which limits their applications in high power density supercapacitors. Hence, the combination of g-C3N4 with pseudocapacitive materials has become more and more important. The reasonable and reliable combination of the two components would produce new electrode materials with enhancement in individual material's performance. Also, g-C3N4 could be used as a sacrificial template and N-doping source. Since g-C3N4 is regarded as a promising N source to produce nitrogen-doped carbon materials because of its easy accessibility and fairly high N content and undergoes complete thermolysis at 750 C, layered g-C3N4 can be exploited as a self-sacrificial template, thereby making additional purification and isolation steps obsolete. Table 1 provides a short summary of carbon nitride based materials discussed herein. In summary, carbon nitride materials and their composites with diverse configurations show great potential as SC electrodes for high-performance energy storage. Owing to their intriguing features, including high nitrogen content, hydrophilicity and stability, carbon nitride materials or their combinations with other materials can exhibit superior electrochemical performance in SCs. Although many significant results have been achieved, the carbon nitride based materials remain broad space for further exploration for SCs. Additionally, carbon nitride and hybrid SCs based on carbon nitride materials hold great potential in practical applications. Application of carbon nitride in supercapacitors is in the beginning stage, which needs more efforts in the future, for example, there are no reports of carbon nitride and metal phosphide composites. It is also expected that carbon nitride after proper functionalization with metals (oxide, sulfide, and phosphide) or other materials, and nano-architected or different functional groups on the surface will further expand their application possibilities in supercapacitors. Although the aforementioned shortcomings can be alleviated by several means. To name a few, these include selection of appropriate g-C3N4 precursors, optimized reaction temperatures and durations for the condensation process, an exfoliation assisted strategy, hard and soft template approaches, a supramolecular preorganization route, and template-free methods to account for highly porous g-C3N4 nanostructures with controllable morphologies.57,147150 Understanding the mechanisms of energy storage in carbon nitride and their relationship with active-sites and the composition needs more comprehensive theoretical and experimental investigations which could affect future research directions. Also, well-developed nanostructures with high surface area could undoubtedly favor achieving more abundant active sites and better mass transfer over bulk g-C3N4. The future perspective in this field could be synthesis and development of carbon nitrides with different C/N stoichiometric ratios such as C3N5, C3N6, C3N7 and C3N8 and different dopants.147,151 Looking at the future, there is a limitless scope of opportunities and challenges present for this booming research hotspot. We hope that this review article will be a good guiding star for the next decade of research in the field of g-C3N4-based nanomaterials and application in supercapacitors.
