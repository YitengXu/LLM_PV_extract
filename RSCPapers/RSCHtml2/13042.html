Dye-sensitized solar cells based on an electrospun polymer nanocomposite membrane as electrolyte

Mini Thomas and Sheeja Rajiv *
Department of Chemistry, Anna University, Chennai-600025, Tamil Nadu, India. E-mail: sheeja@annauniv.edu

Received 30th October 2018 , Accepted 12th February 2019

First published on 13th February 2019

Abstract

In this work an innovative quasi-solid state (QSS) polymer electrolyte containing rare earth oxide nanoparticles and an ionic liquid was developed to enhance the efficiency and stability of dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs). For the fabrication of DSSCs, 3-decyl-1,2-dimethylimidazolium tetrafluoroborate ([dmim]BF4) ionic liquid (IL) and dysprosium oxide nanoparticles (Dy NPs) were synthesized, characterized and introduced into electrospun polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) nanofibers. These nanofibers were studied by X-ray diffraction (XRD), Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR), diffuse reflectance spectroscopy (DRS-UV), differential scanning calorimetry (DSC), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) techniques and examined for their electron uptake, porosity and ionic conductivity. The photovoltaic and electrochemical effects of this electrolyte in DSSCs were investigated. From the results, it was found that DSSCs assembled with IL and Dy NPs incorporated PVDF nanofibers had a higher conversion efficiency of 6.4% and they had better stability than liquid electrolyte. These polymer electrolyte based DSSCs were found to be stable and efficient and such electrolytes show promising potential for use in solar cells.

1. Introduction

The conversion of sunlight to electricity using dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) has become a pertinent research area after its inception in 1991.1,2 In the cell, the electrolyte has a key role in charge transportation between the electrodes and the regeneration of dye during DSSC operation.3 Despite the excellent performance of liquid electrolyte containing the triiodide/iodide redox couple, DSSCs suffer drawbacks such as leakage, evaporation, contamination of the solvents, and electrode corrosion, which hinder the outdoor applications of DSSCs.4,5 To surmount these problems, several attempts have been made to replace liquid electrolyte with polymer and solid state electrolytes.6,7 These electrolytes also have the disadvantages of low short circuit current density (Jsc) and cell performance. QSS electrolytes in the form of electrospun polymer nanofibrous membranes have attracted significant attention through the development of one dimensional fiber morphologies with unusually high porosity. Their interconnected architecture has the capacity to entrap a large amount of liquid electrolyte. Their extremely high specific surface area, flexibility in surface functionalities and superior mechanical performance further aid their implementation as electrolytes.3,6 Electrospun PVDF membrane based electrolytes are endowed with these properties and the presence of electron withdrawing fluorine atoms improves the ionic transport and reduces the recombination rate at semiconductor/polymer electrolyte interfaces.7,8 However, compared to a cell with liquid electrolyte, a PVDF membrane shows low efficiency, maybe due to its crystalline nature and inadequate ionic conductivity.9 Some attempts have been reported to enhance the ionic conductivity and reduce the crystallinity of such membranes by the addition of nanoparticles4,11 (e.g., CoS, ZnO). Further, the dispersion of nanoparticles creates continuous ion transport pathways inside the polymer matrix, into which the redox pair (iodide/tri-iodide) can easily migrate. This improves the ionic mobility and promotes the ionic conductivity, which is aided by the porosity of the membrane.13 Besides, NPs containing polymer electrolyte showed high penetration through a porous TiO2 film surface. The polymer crystallinity is reduced by such addition of NPs due to their interaction (Lewis-acid base type) with the polar group of the polymer, which in turn improves the electrochemical and photovoltaic properties of the polymer membrane.14 Utilization of rare earth oxide nanoparticles in polymer electrolyte remains unexplored. Among them, Dy NPs are thermally and photochemically stable and their semiconductive nature helps in fast diffusion of charge carriers.15,16 Imidazolium based room temperature ionic liquids (RT-ILs) possess lone pairs of electrons on the nitrogen atom, which can easily form charge transfer complexes with iodine, thereby preventing iodine sublimation.10,11 The ILs also increase the ionic conductivity of the polymer matrix through stacking of imidazole segments.12 The cation moiety such as the imidazole group on the uncovered TiO2 surface hinders the unfavourable recombination reactions and enables DSSCs to exhibit superior performance. In the present work, an IL and Dy NPs were synthesized and incorporated into PVDF by electrospinning and their photovoltaic and electrochemical properties and stability were studied.

2. Experimental

2.1 Materials

Poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF) (average Mw = 275000 g mol1), dysprosium hexanitrate, iodine (I2), lithium iodide (LiI), 4-tert-butylpyridine (TBP), 1-propyl-3-methylimidazolium iodide (PMII), acetonitrile (AN), N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF), NaBH4, 1,2-dimethylimidazole, acetone and absolute ethanol were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich Co., Ltd. Bromodecane was purchased from SRL Chemicals Pvt. Ltd, India. Nanocrystalline TiO2 (nc-TiO2) paste coated on FTO conducting glass (15 sq1), cis-diisothiocyanato-bis(2,20-bypyridyl-4,40-dicarboxylato)ruthenium(II)bis(tetrabutylammonium) (N719 dye) and a Pt counter electrode were purchased from GreatCell Solar's DSL, Australia.

2.2 Instrumentation

The morphologies and sizes of the Dy NPs and polymer fibers were examined with high resolution scanning electron microscopy (SEM; FEI Quanta FEG 200). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis was conducted to investigate the presence of dysprosium oxide nanoparticles using a JEOL instrument model JEM 2100 with a voltage of 200 kV and the filament used was LaB6. The functional groups were identified by Fourier transform infrared studies using a BRUKER Alpha FT-IR spectrometer in the range of 4000400 cm1. The ionic liquid ([dmim]BF4) was characterized by 1H and 13C NMR studies (Bruker, CDCl3, 400 MHz) using TMS as an internal standard. To observe the thermal behaviour of the prepared samples, Differential Scanning Calorimetry (NETZSCH DSC-204 Differential Scanning Calorimeter) was performed in the temperature range of 25 to 300 C. The crystallinities of the polymer nanofibers and nanoparticles were examined using an X-ray diffractometer (D8 ADVANCE, Bruker). The optical band gaps of the polymer fibers and material were calculated by using a JASCO V-750 DRS-UV/VIS spectrophotometer. The thicknesses of the electrospun nanofibers were measured by using a digimatic micrometer. The photovoltaic performance (JV) was measured under 1 sun using a LOT Technologies solar simulator equipped with a 150 W xenon lamp and an air mass (AM) 1.5 global filter, which provided a light power of 100 mW cm2. Cyclic voltammetric measurements were performed using a CH Instruments electrochemical workstation. A three electrode system was used, where a glassy carbon (GC) electrode with a diameter of 3 mm served as a working electrode (WE) and a platinum wire as a counter electrode (CE). Ag/AgCl used as a reference electrode (RE) dipped in 0.1 M phosphate buffer at pH 7. The surface of the WE was polished with alumina powder before use. The samples were dissolved in dimethylformamide (DMF) so as to get a concentration of 3 mM. All the cyclic voltammograms (CVs) were recorded at 25 C with a scan rate of 50 to 500 mV s1. Linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) measurements were performed using a cell consisting of membrane based electrolyte sandwiched between two fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) platinised glass plates. The limiting current was obtained from the LSV curves using a Metrohm Autolab 302 N potentiostat/galvanostat. The measurements were performed in the 0.8 to 1.5 voltage range, with a scan rate of 50 mV s1.

2.3 Preparation of 3-decyl-1,2-dimethyl-1H-imidazole-3-ium tetrafluoroborate

1,2-Dimethylimidazole (C5H8N2) (0.2 mol) and bromodecane (C10H21Br) (0.1 mol) were taken in a round bottom flask immersed in an oil bath and heated to 140 C. After 2 h, a slightly viscous liquid was formed. At this point, the solution was allowed to cool for 10 minutes, and again heated in the oil bath to about 140 C for 1015 minutes.17 The oil was dried under vacuum. An aqueous solution of NH4BF4 (1.43 g, 0.013 mol) was then added, resulting in the product (3 g, 0.013 mol). The solution was stirred continuously until phase separation occurred. The compound obtained was extracted with dichloromethane and evaporated in a vacuum and the solvent was removed under reduced pressure to give C15H29BF4N2.

Yield = 96% (3.31 g); pale yellow solid; 1H NMR (500 MHz, CDCl3): 0.7149 (t, 3H), 1.531.788 (m, 2H), 3.8928 (s, 3H), 3.84 (s, 3H), 4.044.06 (t, 2H), 7.2830 (d, 1H), 7.389 (d, 1H). 13C NMR (CDCl3, 100 MHz) (ppm): 2632 (CH2), 14 (CH3), 122123 (CH2CH2), 144 (NCN), 36 (NCH3).

2.4 Synthesis of dysprosium oxide nanoparticles

Dy NPs were synthesized using a previously reported combustion method16 by the reaction of dysprosium nitrate (Dy(NO3)36H2O) with urea at 650 C. The combustion method was chosen because of the good yield and simplicity of the procedure.

2.5 Preparation of polymer solution for electrospinning

A PVDF solution (A) with a concentration of 20% (w/v) was prepared by dissolving the appropriate amount of PVDF into a DMF/acetone (4:1, v/v) mixed solution. The Dy NPsPVDF solution (B) was made by dissolving 0.3 g of Dy NPs in solution A. An IL/PVDF solution (C) was prepared by adding 0.3 g of IL in DMF to solution A. An IL/PVDF/Dy NPs solution (D) was prepared by adding 0.3 g of Dy NPs to solution C. All the prepared solutions were stirred continuously for 24 h at room temperature to obtain a homogeneous solution.

2.6 Electrospinning process

The prepared polymer solutions were loaded into a 5 mL syringe attached to a stainless steel needle with a diameter of 0.3 mm. The flow rate of the solution was adjusted to 0.5 mL h1 with the help of a syringe pump. An aluminium foil was placed as a collector at a distance of 15 cm from the needle and a power supply of 26 kV was used. The electrospinning parameters were set after optimization. The electrospun fibers were labelled A, B, C, and D accordingly based on their solutions.

2.9 Fabrication of DSSC devices

The screen-printed annealed nc-TiO2 pasted electrodes were immersed in absolute ethanol containing 0.5 mM of N719 dye for 24 h at ambient temperature (30 C) in the dark for the dye adsorption process. Electrospun nanofibrous mats were cut into 0.7 cm 0.7 cm square pieces after drying and sandwiched between a dye adsorbed TiO2 photoanode and a Pt counter electrode as per earlier reports.20,21 The dye-adsorbed nc-TiO2 electrode and Pt counter electrode were assembled using a 60 m thick hot melt gasket frame (MSOO4610-10) between the electrodes. The gasket frame with an optimal thickness of 60 m was used as a spacer in order to allow for addition of a sufficient amount of electrolyte, which in effect improves the efficiency of the DSSC.6 For a spacer thickness of less than 60 m, the required quantity of electrolyte for production of free electrons could not be added. A spacer thickness of greater than 60 m leads to a higher electrolyte quantity, which would lead to the recombination of the free electrons due to the increase in their path length through the electrolyte. The cell was pressed together with two clips. A drop of the prepared electrolyte solution containing 0.05 M iodine, 0.1 M lithium iodide, 0.6 M of PMII, and 0.5 M of TBP in 1 mL of a mixture of valeronitrile (15%) and acetonitrile (85%) solvents was introduced into clamped electrodes. The active area of the DSSC was 0.49 cm2. For comparative studies, another DSSC was fabricated using liquid electrolyte without electrospun nanofibers.

3.2 Study of the morphology

Fig. 2(A1) shows smooth, beadless electrospun PVDF nanofibers with an average diameter of 265 nm. Fig. 2(B1) exhibits the formation of synthesized Dy NPs, which were found to be irregularly shaped NPs with an average diameter of 93 nm. Fig. 2(C1) shows that the average diameter of the Dy NPs incorporated PVDF nanofibers decreases to 118 nm. This is because addition of Dy NPs into the polymer solution can lead to an increase in the conductivity of the solution.22,23 Addition of Dy NPs into the PVDF solution resulted in a higher charge density on the surface of the ejected jet due to the presence of more charge carriers, which imparts greater force of elongation. This favours the formation of smooth nanofibers of substantially smaller diameter. This effect is pronounced due to the high charge to size ratio of the Dy NPs. The addition of 0.3 g Dy NPs was determined by optimisation. On lowering the weight percentage, a mixture of beads and non-continuous fibers was generated, whereas an increased weight percentage hindered the fiber formation. Incorporation of the IL and Dy NPs into PVDF nanofibers (Fig. 2(D1)) leads to an increase in fiber diameter and rougher fiber surfaces compared to pristine PVDF.24 Rough surfaces of the fibers were observed after the addition of IL, which may be due to the change in the surface tension of the precursor solution, which gives the nanofiber a backward pulse on being stretched by electrical force. The rough surface increases the surface area, which can take up a large amount of electrolyte. Even though a gradual increase in conductivity is seen on adding the IL, other solution parameters like viscosity and surface tension show an increase as a consequence of the larger decyl group in the IL. It has been shown that higher viscosity results in higher chain entanglements, which cause delayed flow of the solution jet during its travel to the collector at fixed process parameters, resulting in an increased diameter.25 All the prepared nanofibers showed high porosity, which helped in the uptake of a large amount of liquid electrolyte, thereby facilitating faster ionic mobility and high conductivity.18

3.4 XRD analysis

An X-ray diffractogram of Dy NPs shows a crystalline morphology with peaks at 2 of 28.3, 34.5, 39.2, 47.7 and 58.6, respectively, in accordance with the literature.15,16 In the XRD characterization of PVDF, the polymer shows an amorphous nature and peaks appeared at 14.14, 30.07, and 40.81, indicating the phase of PVDF in accordance with the literature.10 The Dy NPs incorporated PVDF polymer did not exhibit the characteristic peaks of Dy NPs and appeared as amorphous. This demonstrates that the dispersion of Dy NPs in the PVDF matrix changed PVDF to an amorphous form and the amorphous nature increased with increasing dispersion.23 When the IL was incorporated into the polymer solution, there was a decrease in the intensity due to the disorder in the polymer chains created by the IL that enhanced the amorphous phase of the system.26 IL and Dy NPs incorporated PVDF fibers also showed a decrease in the intensity of the peak. The percentage crystallinity is calculated by separating the intensities due to the amorphous and crystalline phases of the diffracted graphs. The percentage crystallinity (Xc%) is estimated as the ratio of the crystalline area (Ac) to the total area of the amorphous (Aa) and crystalline phases.27
Xc% = [Ac/(Ac + Aa)] 100(5)
The percentage crystallinity values for Dy NPs, PVDF, PVDF/IL, PVDF/Dy NPs, and PVDF/IL/Dy NPs were 64.5%, 42.6%, 39%, 31%, and 11.7% respectively. In this observation, the percentage of crystallinity decreased upon addition of the IL and Dy NPs (Fig. 5).

3.7 Differential scanning calorimetry studies

Fig. 6 displays DSC thermograms of a pristine electrospun PVDF membrane along with IL, Dy NPs, and IL/Dy NPs incorporated electrospun PVDF membranes. It was observed that each sample exhibits an endothermic peak, indicating its respective melting temperature. The observed shift in Tm may be due to the change in the orientation of polymer chains upon addition of the IL and Dy NPs. It was also observed that the melting enthalpy (Hm) decreases on addition of fillers. These observations signify a decrease in crystallinity and therefore an increase in ionic conductivity on incorporation of the IL and Dy NPs into PVDF. A comparison of the DSCs of the pristine PVDF and IL/Dy NPs incorporated PVDF shows an absence of the sharp melting peak on incorporation. This further shows a change from crystalline to amorphous nature in the polymer on incorporation of IL/Dy NPs.

3.8 Electrochemical characteristics of the membrane electrolytes

3.8.1 Ionic conductivity. The ionic conductivity of electrospun PVDF was found to be 2.1 103 S cm1. In addition, the incorporation of Dy NPs and the IL into the nanofibrous membrane improved the ionic conductivity to 3.2 103 S cm1. The enhancement of the ionic conductivity in composite polymer electrolytes was attributed mainly to the formation of efficient ion transport channels.13
3.8.2 Ionic conductivity and temperature dependence study. The Arrhenius equation was used to describe the conductivitytemperature behaviour of membrane electrolytes.30 Arrhenius plots of ionic conductivity vs. temperature (temperature range 25 C to 70 C) of the different prepared membranes (Fig. 10) show that the ionic conductivities of the membrane electrolytes obey the Arrhenius equation. It was also observed that the IL and Dy NPs incorporated PVDF membrane based electrolyte shows higher conductivity than the others. The increment in ionic conductivity may be due to the polymer flexibility. From the DSC studies (Fig. 6), it was clear that the polymer loses its crystalline nature on the incorporation of the IL and Dy NPs. Such an attainment of amorphous nature leads to greater ionic conductivity. The increase in temperature increases the ionic mobility, which leads to an increased ionic conductivity, as can be seen in Fig. 7.

The PVDF/Dy NPs/IL membrane and PVDF/Dy NPs show cathodic and anodic peaks, indicating oxidation and reduction reactions respectively. Fig. 8C shows the cyclic voltammograms of PVDF/Dy NPs/IL at different scan rates, which proves its electrochemical stability also.31 Furthermore, in the scanned electrochemical window, PVDF was found to be electrochemically inactive and PVDF/IL had a reduction reaction peak only.

Due to the large excess of iodide ions in the electrolyte, the current is limited by the diffusion of tri-iodide ions only. The efficiency of PVDF/IL/Dy NPs was comparable with that of the liquid electrolyte but with the advantage of stability as shown in Fig. 12. It was also noted that there was an increase in the Voc and a decrease in the Jsc value of the PVDF/IL/Dy NPs when compared to the liquid electrolyte. The liquid electrolyte exhibits high ionic conductivity and good interfacial contact properties, which are essential for high Jsc and efficiency. The larger value of Voc of the membrane is related to the reduction of charge recombination on the TiO2 and electrolyte interface. Also the presence of IL containing imidazolium cation increases ionic conductivity, by forming a Helmholtz layer on the surface of TiO2 present on the working electrode. This hampers the direct contact of triiodide ions and the surface of TiO2, which efficiently suppresses the recombination reaction and enhances Voc of DSSC. The lower Jsc values of the membranes compared to the liquid electrolyte can be attributed to the limitation of ionic conductivity.32 However, other samples (i), (ii), and (iii) showed lower values for all the photo-voltaic parameters compared to (iv). This may be due to the fibre matrix of sample (iv), which provides a high diffusion coefficient, ionic conductivity, large porosity and low Rs, R1CT, and R2CT values.

3.10 Stability measurements

Stability studies of the prepared nanofibrous membrane electrolytes and liquid electrolyte with respect to time are presented in Fig. 12. Although the photovoltaic performance of the QSS-electrospun membrane based electrolyte was slightly lower than the corresponding liquid electrolyte based DSSC, the former revealed excellent long-term stability. Fig. 12A shows that the decay was much slower for the cell having the electrospun polymer nanofibrous membrane compared to that of the liquid electrolyte. Even though the power conversion efficiency of the liquid electrolyte was 6.9%, it decreased gradually over 10 days to 2.3%. On the other hand, even though the power conversion efficiency of the PVDF/IL/Dy NPs nanofibrous membrane was initially 6.4%, it could be maintained up to 6.1% over the same period of study. This low stability of the liquid electrolyte was due to the evaporation and leakage of the volatile liquid solvent.30 Such a setback was not seen in the nanofibrous membrane as the porous nanofibers in the cells hold the electrolyte solution without evaporation. From the normalised conversion efficiency graph, it is clear that the efficiency of the liquid electrolyte decreased drastically to 34%, whereas that of the PVDF/IL/Dy NPs nanofibrous membrane could be maintained at 93% over a period of 10 days.

4. Conclusion

Due to their high porosities and large surface area to volume ratios, electrospun nanofibers used as electrolytes in solar cells have shown great promise. The room temperature ionic liquid 3-decyl-1,2-dimethylimidazolium tetrafluoroborate and dysprosium oxide nanoparticles were synthesized, characterized and incorporated into an electrospun PVDF nanofibrous membrane to form an electrolyte for DSSCs. The electrolyte's photovoltaic effects and electrochemical behaviour were investigated. The Voc, Jsc, FF and values of a DSSC device using such an electrospun PVDF/IL/Dy NPs nanofibrous membrane electrolyte were found to be 0.81 V, 13.93 mA cm2, 56% and 6.4% under AM1.5, respectively, compared to pristine PVDF nanofibers and nanofibers incorporated with either Dy NPs or the IL. This may be due to its high ionic conductivity, diffusion coefficient, porosity and electron uptake in the uniform electrospun interwoven structure. It showed lower R1CT, R2CT and Rs compared with the DSSCs fabricated with the other samples. This electrospun PVDF/Dy NPs/IL membrane electrolyte showed better stability without any leakage of the electrolyte and has potential for long term use.
