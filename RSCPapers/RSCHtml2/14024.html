Perovskite solar cells from the viewpoint of innovation and sustainability

Abstract

Innovation is essential around the themes of climate change and sustainability. Commercial photovoltaics (PV) have noticeably contributed to getting to 22.1% share of the gross final energy consumption in Europe from renewable sources in 2020 but a steep further increase is urgent in the near future. Over the last few years, great success has been achieved by perovskites applied to PV, with mixed anions and cations in shared lattices that reached record efficiency values close to those of Si in laboratory-scale solar cells (26%). Their use has recently shed light on a medium/long-term compositional instability that arises from the partial miscibility of the species with similar role in the atomic lattice. The chemical route to prepare the materials for Perovskite Solar Cells (PSCs) also needs to be critically reviewed. Material waste and reuse are other concerns to be faced. This perspective paper indeed tackles some aspects for innovation and sustainability on the PSC field for production purposes. Some hints for technologically affordable processes based on in-vacuum deposition of Perovskites are provided in light of their sustainability and for the need to reduce production/maintenance costs. It is also discussed how to make in-vacuum production further competitive by boosting the material quality. Innovation is also projected into the theme of making sustainable choices for device architectures and materials. Carbon-based PSCs are highly focused since they allow avoiding the use of complex, unstable and costly HTLs. From the material side, pros and cons of using fully inorganic CsPbI3 are commented, framed by the current revival of single-cation perovskites. CsPbI3, in particolar, enables recycling and reuse initiatives thanks to the overall mass preservation under degradation. Some closing remarks are provided on the safe use of Pb as its effective sequestration before release from the PSC into the environment is properly engineered. We lastly trust that initiatives bringing together academic and industrial know-how in complementary fields able to take up responsible innovation will contribute to accelerating the ecological transition and will enable the societal transformation to fulfil the 2050 EU agenda for a sustainable future.

1. Introduction

Innovation is a dynamic concept. For many decades a relevant innovation booster was the miniaturization of microelectronic devices to provide a progressively increasing number of functionalities in the same space. Miniaturization is also associated with the industrial throughput since the higher the number of devices patterned on a wafer the higher the production output per unit area and time.

Innovation has been more recently centred around the themes of climate change and sustainability. Following or, in some cases, even preceding the perception of the population, the European Council with its new investment program decided to pilot the transition towards climate neutrality by 2050 giving energy a central role, as energy production is today responsible for more than 75% of the EU's greenhouse gas emissions. For a large success of the initiatives, citizens and science must be mutually interconnected with the good practice allied to sustainable innovation, hereinafter.

According to the Eurostat data, the EU reached a 22% share of its gross final energy consumption from renewable sources in 2020.1 It has been reported that With more than half of energy from renewable sources in its gross final consumption of energy, Sweden (60%) had by far the highest share among the EU Member States in 2020, ahead of Finland (44%) and Latvia (42%). At the opposite end of the scale, the lowest proportions of renewables were registered in Malta (11%), followed by Luxembourg (12%) and Belgium (13%).2 In this scenario, Italy stays at 20%. As a further representative parameter, Eurostat calculated that in 2020, renewable energy sources made up 38% of gross electricity consumption in the EU: wind, hydro and solar powers remained at 36%, 33%, and 14%, respectively. Having reached this level, the generated electricity from renewable sources in 2020 has surpassed that of fossil fuels at 37%,3 a target that has not been reached worldwide so far.

To further increase the slope of changes toward the decarbonized ERA projected for 2050 in the EU, new technologies should simultaneously act on different sides and should be likewise sustainable.

Boosting photovoltaics (PV) is aligned with preserving ecosystems, living in greener cities, and ensuring soil health and food.4 A valuable PV contribution to decarbonization is expected; moreover, new generation PV could egregiously satisfy new design of cities with Building Integrated and Applied PV (BIPV and BAPV) and with indoor installations; lastly, vertical and in-house farming, as well as agrivoltaics for proximity food deployment, can benefit from new concepts in PV.

Therefore, where do the challenges lie?

PV technologies with high production costs or complex production flow charts are not suitable to convey large product dissemination. The use of chemicals also needs to be critically reviewed. Material waste and reuse are other concerns to be faced.

Cost is not the only bottleneck; the rigid, opaque, and heaviness character of the technologies currently in the market limit the field of application.

To capture more photons, to be converted into current to power utilities, two pathways can be traced: (1) boosting the efficiency for solar energy conversion; (2) covering more surfaces with PV devices. The first target is limited by the thermodynamic threshold for photon-to-current conversion that is close to 30% of the incident solar power, the well-known ShockleyQueisser limit5 that mostly arises from energy dissipation inside a device. The second pathway is lined up with the development of smart cities and spaces wherein extended architectonic elements can be made energetically active.

An affordable solution exists to satisfy both requests and relies on the incoming market uptake of perovskite solar cell (PSC) technologies. Progress on PSC performances has been steadily advancing since 2009 through materials and device engineering. Different device architectures, various kinds of transporting layers, and the versatile perovskite (PSK) compositions have constantly boosted the photoconversion efficiency6,7 to the current 25.7% (NREL certified).8 The certified record values for single crystals and HIT solar cells are currently 26.1% and 26.7%, respectively.8 Easy compositional modifications of the PSK lattice accomplished by simple solution-based deposition processes represent a huge strength to satisfy specific demands.6,9 More recently, all-inorganic perovskite solar cells have made progress in terms of efficiency reaching a value around 19%.10,11 Together with the outdoor PV, dedicated hybrid solar cells for indoor powering12 and aerospace13 are likewise under the spotlight.

The hallmarks introduced by the perovskite materials can be summarised as follows: free excitons at room temperature; low trap density and defect tolerance; direct and tuneable bandgap; wide absorption coefficient in the visible range; diffusion length of several tens of microns; a record Voc/Eg value of 0.75. The high absorption capability is one main strength since it enables the use of thin films of the order of tens of nanometers. Band tuning is a special power in terms of adaptability to different applications including tandem solar devices. Tandem Si/PSK solar cells have indeed emerged as an incoming business based on the complementary light absorption of the single components to cover a portion of the solar spectrum as large as possible. It has been demonstrated that tandem solar cells14 are versatile over different technological schemes (2-, 3-, 4-terminal) with projected efficiency of up to 45%.

PSCs, with respect to Si-based solar cells, exploit a light-absorbing material whose atoms are not rigidly covalently linked (Fig. 1). Inside the perovskite, instead, the cations have a configurational arrangement that locally changes in the picosecond timescale as shown in Fig. 2. This impacts the mutual position of the ionically bonded Pb and I species. The consequent dynamic nature of the whole structure settles the special quasi-liquid nature of the lattice and the dynamic character of the defects with the consequent possibility of self-healing.15 One of the practical consequences is the recovery of the material (and device) performances.16,17

Within this unifying picture, the commercialization of PSC-based products is currently limited by issues in up-scalability and durability. Are those two issues also in relationship with innovation and sustainability?

The motivation for this perspective paper is to offer a viewpoint on innovation based on the sustainability of the production processes for PSCs. We start discussing the up-scalability for the production of the active layer, for which sustainability forces as progressively as rapidly to move from solution to in-vacuum processes. The related technological issues are discussed in sections 2 and 3. Special focus is spent towards the perovskite material for a convenient trade-off between quality and thickness. Section 4 is on carbon-based architectures that are of huge interest to produce devices without HTL. Section 5 is dedicated to fully inorganic perovskites that enable recycle and reuse due to the mass preservation under degradation. Section 6 is a discussion on the use of lead.

2. Sustainable up-scalability for large-scale perovskite production

The scale-up of a device does not simply consist of applying a multiplication factor to its small scale. This technological action rather forces to reconsider methodologies and the entire layout of the device. Firstly, uniformity and reproducibility of the materials deposited on large areas must be guaranteed to reduce failures in the production output.

The first penalty paid by the scale-up of PV devices is on the photo-to-current conversion efficiency.19 The loss of performance could even reach 3040% of what is achieved in laboratory-scale devices. The main bottlenecks are thickness, compactness, and stoichiometry uniformities. Deposition methods by solution processing are well known to suffer from all these issues. Moreover, to allow complying with sustainability criteria, the use of solvents, contributing to marine eutrophication well above the use of Pb,20 should be limited as much as possible. Although it is clear20 that PSK fabrication is more environment friendly than Si-cell fabrication, viable paths to further reduce the risks have been undertaken. These comprise the implementation of vacuum deposition methods and the engineering of HTL-free PSCs.

The theme of economically sustainable growth of perovskite manufacturing for photovoltaics was tackled in a recent paper.21 From that paper, it comes out that a manufacturing plant producing 1 MW per year necessarily addresses a niche market that accepts a selling price above 1$ per W, whilst a Giga-factory producing 1000 MW per year sells products at less than 1$ per W. To fulfill this last condition, the throughput of production is the pivotal parameter. Under this scenario and following the Allied Market Research report,22 vapor-deposition methods for flexible PSCs are projected as the most lucrative segments by 2030, with the expectation to grow at the highest Compounded Average Growth Rate (CAGR) of 32.9% compared to pure solution and vapour-assisted solution methods. We believe this is a reliable economical and sustainable evaluation of the incoming scenario considering the easy applicability of the physical deposition methods in the production chain of semiconductor industries. It is a fact that evaporation is largely used for silicon solar cell panels and for any other technological product. A current proof on the viability of physical methods for PSK deposition comes from Enel Green Power investing on PSK/Si tandem solar cells in a Giga-Factory that has been recently co-funded by the European community (commented at the end of the paper). It is easily drawn from those case-study that semiconductor industries are well willing to accept vacuum deposition equipment in their equipment portfolio for massive production of PSCs at high throughput.

Vapor-deposition, also called evaporation or sublimation, is a process under vacuum conditions that has been especially taking hold in the last decade2325 since its pioneering publication from the University of Oxford.26 The response of the research community in reproducing the method was not so immediate since evaporation lacks versatility with respect to the more consolidated chemical counterparts: changing recipes, procedures, and mixing materials is easier by chemistry. The evaporation method is, on the other hand, solvent-free, reproducible, up-scalable, and indeed intrinsically suitable for high production throughput. The paramount value added is its compatibility with industrial flow charts, which sets the stage for partial or total reconfiguration of pristine sites of production from standard semiconductor devices to PSC fabrication.

A recent increase of interest in evaporating perovskites under vacuum conditions is testified by the deployment of dedicated equipment worldwide and by the expression of interest of leading semiconductor industries to integrate PSCs with technologies under production. One example, as already introduced, is represented by Enel Green Power in Italy.27 Oxford PV in the UK is currently highly committed to PSC/Si tandem business, whilst other companies in Europe are more focused on PSC products for selling (e.g., Saule technologies in Poland) through a B2C scheme. In the same pathway, on-topic research programs under Horizon Europe are boosting the lab-to-fab transition, with similar counterparts in USA and Japan, for the scope of accelerating the market uptake of PSCs.

Conventional vacuum chambers dedicated to PSC deposition work under high vacuum conditions (HV-PVD) in a ballistic regime that rules the gas transport from the source to the substrate. The sources are effusion cells placed a long way from the substrate (tens of centimeters). After effusion from the source, the species, having gained kinetic energy that is proportional to the sublimation temperature, travel and spread inside the vacuum chamber. Some of them reach the substrate and deposit on it with a yield that depends on the sticking coefficient.28 Typical working pressure is 105107 mbar. A record of 20.8% efficiency was achieved under 1 106 mbar with 590 nm-thick layers of MAPbI3.29 A close value was achieved by co-evaporated MAPbI3 with graded Fermi levels.30 Deposited at 4.4 105 mbar, a 350 nm-thick MAPbI3 provided 15.7% cell efficiency.31 Increasing the pressure in conventional evaporation chambers would drastically reduce the deposition rate at the substrate side due to the augmented collisions during traveling. It indeed proves that the vapor pressure of the species impinging on the substrate is crucial.

On the opposite side, Close Space Sublimation (CSS) has emerged as an easy and complementary method to infiltrate organic cations into a pristine lead-halide layer. In CSS, the substrate and source are a few millimeters apart so that atmospheric pressure is used. Spin-coated PbI2 seed layers, placed at an 1 mm distance from the source,32 were converted with CSS into MAPbI3 layers enabling PSCs with 16.2% efficiency (4 mm2 active area). With 820 nm-thick films,33 a high efficiency of 20.4% was achieved under illuminated aeras of 0.07 cm2.

Drawbacks of CSS are the lack of control and reproducibility moving from laboratory-scale to industrial-scale devices, and the need to prepare a lead-halide layer seed with other methods. This mixed preparation method is indeed not as environmentally friendly as pure HV-PVD.

Up-scalability with HV-PVD, once conjugated with semitransparency and stability, would fulfill the industrial demands in many application fields: from BIPV to roofing to tandem;34,35 from wearable to flexible devices; and from aerospace to agrivoltaics installations. 3. Sustainable up-scalable deposition method for perovskite defect mitigation

To increase PSK stability and transparency, lattice imperfections must be reduced during growth. Ionic migration44 and surface defect aggregation45 on the one hand generate defect states that can trap photo-generated carriers or reduce their mobility; on the other hand, they produce local changes of morphology and composition that progressively drives the transition of the PSK material towards inactive byproducts. In Fig. 5 we present degradation phenomena that occur under different working conditions. They are either thermally activated or catalyzed by external agents (e.g. water molecules). Pb aggregates are formed under conditions that promote the release of iodine species as soon as neighborhood isolated Pb atoms are left to bundle. PbI2 formation is a well-known phenomenon driven by water molecules under moisture conditions; it is also observed under nitrogen and temperature above RT. The presence of intrinsic defects in the as-prepared lattice accelerates both kinds of degradation processes.
Fig. 5 (a) Representation of defect evolution in a perovskite lattice with volatile species release and consequent local lattice rearrangement. (b) STEM image of Pb aggregation (c) compositional change from MAPbI3 to PbI2 after heating.

Deposition of PSK by evaporation is reported to have good chances to succeed in defect mitigation and controlling the PSK quality. One of the seminal papers on evaporated semitransparent PSK46 by co-deposition of PbCl2 and MAI at 2 103 mbar reports a good crystal quality of the layer and an efficiency of 9.9% on a 5 5 cm illuminated area. Efficiency and semitransparency generally have opposite trends and thus a compromise is needed. To boost the efficiency of semitransparent PSK layers, the role of the MAI vapor pressure was highlighted28 as pivotal in compensating for low stitching coefficients due to re-desorption of the evaporated species. This finding addresses alternative engineering of the PVD chambers.

In HV-PVD, in fact, the attaching probability of the species on a substrate is piloted by the gas and substrate temperatures. To add further variability for the fine-tuning of the material quality, we recently proposed47 a method called LV-PSE (Low-Vacuum Proximity Space Effusion) to sequentially deposit both the lead-halide and the organic moieties in the same chamber at the pressure of a rotative pump. The mainstay of the method resides in the use of a relatively high working pressure 102 mbar, coupled with a substratesource coaxial geometry at a distance of 13 cm. This combination enables the building blocks of the templating halide to adjust with high order and subsequently allows the molecules of the organic halide to impinge on the template layer with enough energy for the benefit of the final PSK quality.

The compactness of the equipment and the use of low vacuum conditions are huge advantages for the initial investment and maintenance costs (more than halved with respect to HV-PVD). As a further value, waste of materials at the chamber wall with LV-PSE is negligible. Finally, the method of deposition at short distances with all the aforementioned advantages can be exploited from laboratory to industrial scale-up for large area and high throughput production.

A comparative description of LV-PSE vs. standard HV-PVD is shown in Fig. 6a. The picture describes the impact of scaling the sourcesubstrate distance together with reducing the vacuum conditions (LV) on the kinetics of the adatoms: the vapor pressure close to the sample surface (P) and the temperature (T) are empowered for the benefit of the quality of the deposited layers. The same pressure conditions applied to a chamber with standard geometry (larger sourcesubstrate distance of tens of cm) would produce cold adatoms at very low vapor pressure on the far substrate (extremely low deposition time and poorly deposited materials).

4. Sustainability by carbon-based architectures

The use of solvents and sophisticated materials in the flow chart for PSC production raises concerns about sustainability for environmental preservation and product costs. Residual solvents are also killer against the near and medium-term durability of the PSK layer and its interfaces.

Besides the implementation of vacuum deposition methods, a solventless production chain or the use of eco-friendly/abundant materials would add value to sustainability. A contribution in this direction is currently given by HTL-free architectures wherein the classical and costly Spiro-OMeTAD is avoided. It is operationally unstable and inclined to mediate the migration of halide ions and metal ions such as Au, Ag, and Al.4850 Carbon-based PSCs (HTL-free C-PSCs), instead, offer superior long-term stability5154 that compensates for its reduced selectivity to hole extraction. A mix of graphite flakes, carbon black, curing resin, and solvent is used and deposited directly on the perovskite layer by different methods such as doctor-blading, ink-jet printing, and hot press transfer.55 In addition, the structural stability and the hydrophobic character of carbon contacts play an important role in protecting the perovskite layer from environmental humidity. P-type doping can be done in C-electrodes to modulate the Fermi-level for improved selectivity.52 Barichello et al.56 used a fully printable HTL-free C-PSC achieving 10.66% efficiency with a water treatment of substrate before perovskite deposition.

Although doctor blading methods are widely applied in PSC fabrication, a drawback often encountered is the long-term durability of the interface with perovskite primarily due to a solvent-mediated interaction of the C-electrode with the perovskite surface.6,16,45,5759

We recently experienced17 that the preservation of this interface can be achieved with a solvent-free deposition method based on hot-pressing a free-standing dried CAl foil directly on the perovskite layer (HP-PSC). In the hot-press transfer method, the glass substrate was coated with the commercial carbon paste by doctor blading, forming a wet carbon film. Then it was soaked in ethanol for solvent exchange. After this, the carbon film was peeled off from the glass substrate and it was dried at RT on an Al foil. As a result, a (C + Al) free-standing electrode (A = 0.25 cm2) was formed and directly hot-pressed onto the perovskite layer under a pressure of 0.1 MPa at 100 C for 40 minutes. The fabrication process is shown in Fig. 8. The method guarantees longer stability of device performances17 but, at the same time, it needs control of the interface adhesion to avoid local failure, especially in large-area devices. Full recovery of device performances was demonstrated over 90 days of operation, with electrical analyses done under air conditions that highlight the beneficial role of the carbon electrode against humidity.

Another approach to prevent solvent-mediated interfacial degradation consists of applying a vacuum-curing after DB (DBv-PSCs).60 In the treatment, a pressure of 102 mbar for 24 h on the devices is applied. DBv-PSCs, compared to the hot-press transfer method, provide a better guarantee of the carbon electrode adhesion to the underlying PSK through the whole contact area and indeed they are technologically more reliable for performance recovery after use.

Lastly, mesoporous architectures based on porous materials offered an easy integration by PSK impregnation as the last step of production. They are carbon-based HTL-free PSC structures (mC-PSCs)6163 consisting of three mesoporous layers including an electron transport layer (ETL, usually TiO2), an insulating layer (usually ZrO2), and a porous conductive carbon top electrode. The whole structure has multiple advantages: high-temperature resistance (400500 C); fully printable;64,65 less costly being without spiro-OMeTAD;66 and highly stable against moisture.67,68 Further discussion can be found at the end of next section.

5. Sustainability by fully inorganic perovskites

CsPbI3 is the photoactive perovskite with the simplest stoichiometry and high photovoltaic yield69,70 with a view to capturing the solar spectrum in tandem with Si solar cells.71,72 Tandem solar cell technologies are nowadays attracting interest for public/private investment worldwide and are expected to contribute to the ecological transition pathway while boosting the photo-conversion efficiency above the thermodynamic limit of each isolated component. The first mainstay of CsPbI3 to be exploited is a bandgap of 1.731.78 eV, well complementing the absorption range of the Si bottom cells in tandem cooperation.

Since mixed-cation mixed-anion perovskites have similar values of the bandgap amplitude, is there any other reason why CsPbI3 is more sustainable than other perovskites?

CsPbI3 has superior endurance against the formation of volatile species. All perovskites containing organic cations, including mixed-cation compounds, suffer from a process called proton exchange, strongly catalysed by water molecules and consisting of a proton moving from MA+ and/or FA+ towards the iodide moieties with the consequent local lattice destabilization and formation of volatile species. Mass loss is an irreversible process that makes reuse and recycling unfeasible unless methods to capture volatile species or reintroduce them are engineered. Otherwise, the perovskite layer progressively transforms into its inconvenient PbI2 by-product.

The choice of Cs poses a guarantee for mass conservation. Photoactive CsPbI3 phases (hereinafter called black) can settle to orthorhombic (), tetragonal () or cubic () with increasing temperature. The last phase is formed by annealing at 350 C. Ageing of photoactive CsPbI3 occurs and consists of a polymorphic transition towards an orthorhombic photo-inactive -phase (called yellow phase) without mass loss. Fortunately, this transformation is fully reversible by applying a temperature.

A stable photoactive CsPbI3 black phase against its yellow counterpart can be achieved through different temperature routes: (1) at low temperature (LT, 80100 C) by using specific additives like hydroiodic acid (HI), organic ligands, or elemental doping like europium (Eu) and (2) at high temperature (HT) by annealing the film at 320350 C and rapidly cooling it at RT.73 Preserving a robust photoactive CsPbI3 phase is demonstrated by pressure-directed octahedral tilting that counteracts yellow phase formation.74 Stabilization is indeed a main issue that is under the spotlight to definitely enable the use of single-cation perovskites.75 This special focus to CsPbI3 is wholly justified by the achievement of record efficiency values in the last few years that are competing with the rest of the records (comparison shown in Fig. 9a).76 Solar cell efficiency with CsPbI3 has reached a threshold of 20% in 2021 with the use of a new urea-ammonium thiocyanate (UAT) molten salt as an additive in CsPbI3 precursor solution;77 or even it has achieved the record of 21% in 2022 by the addition of PTAI-based LD perovskites at grain boundaries to enhance the phase stability of CsPbI3 and to suppress non-radiative recombination.78 The use of additives has been reviewed in ref. 79 wherein it is, among the others, commented how partially substituting lead with smaller B-site cations can stabilize CsPbI3 perovskites by reducing the extent of octahedral rotation or tilt. This kind of partial substitution can change the bandgap value associated with the material and indeed it must be rationalised depending on the applicative fallout.

Under the attempt to preserve the optical response of CsPbI3 and primarily to avoid bandgap value upsetting, it has been recently demonstrated that using europium as an additive enables the formation temperature of the -CsPbI3 phase at 80 C and, at the same time, extends the stability window before the transition to the -CsPbI3 phase. Eu can be integrated from either EuI2 or EuCl3 precursors.80 It plays many roles: (1) promoting the formation of the -phase through its ionic coordination with Ivia octahedral bonding in synergy with Pb; this increases the nucleation rate of the -phase; (2) locally compensating charge deficiencies that likely arise from the different kinetics of atoms during reactions; iodine-coordinated Eu atoms can indeed occupy interstitial positions to balance the lack of Cs atoms in the neighbourhood; and (3) promoting the accumulation of exceeding and/or slower atoms in a compliant amorphous self-material enveloping the -phase into small-size grains (snowplow effect). Experiments and theoretical models were combined to provide a comprehensive descriptive framework on the durability of -CsPbI3 with Eu addition, that is, in the end, extended hugely above what occurs without Eu, i.e. 250 days vs. a few hours.80

Eu incorporation is a key matter. It is a related fingerprint that Eu addition changes the unit cell volume of the perovskite (Rietveld refinement procedures from XRD patterns).80 The phenomenon is gathered by the rightward shift of the (004) peak of the -CsPbI3 phase (Fig. 9b) that features a shrinkage of the c-axis (the longest axis in the unit cell) with respect to what is measured in pure CsPbI3. The effect was observed regardless of the Eu-precursor used as shown in Fig. 10a.80

The volume shrinkage of the unit cell is a feature that testifies the active role of Europium first in compensating local Cs deficiency (Eu has an ionic radius close to that of Pb, and both are smaller than that of Cs) and, indirectly, in the nano-structuring of the CsPbI3 grains. Eu incorporation and its beneficial roles are mostly preserved if a second annealing is applied after the one at a low temperature (80 C), with the unit cell volume kept under 944 3 (measured at room temperature after fast quenching). The addition of a high temperature step of annealing has instead a certain impact on the crystallite size, with average values (diameter) slightly increased e.g. after 350 C and fast quenching to RT: 20 nm after 80 C vs. 5060 nm after 80 C + 350 C. Despite this increase of the crystalline size (it is the maximum average size inside a morphological grain wherein the lattice is crystallographically free of defects as probed by an X-ray beam through the full width at half maximum of the XRD peaks), the stability of the -CsPbI3 phase formed after quenching is still high. It is even higher than in the sample after the first step annealing at 80 C, with a jump of temperature for endurance from 80 C to 100 C in stress tests.81 For some applications, e.g. in monolithic HTL-free architectures of solar cells, the use of this double annealing can be conveniently applied.

Lattice defects, besides acting as recombination centers for photogenerated carriers, represent weakness points for the long-term endurance of the lattice architecture. The perovskite phase change, as destabilized by intrinsic defects, can be further accelerated by the presence of water in the environment. This action likely increases the defect level and upsets the perovskite lattice order towards more stable atomic configurations. This theme is shown in Fig. 12 wherein the adsorption energy of typical species in the environment (N2, O2, H2O) on the CsI-terminated CsPbI3(001) surfaces is considered (Fig. 12a). Calculations are based on density functional theory using the PerdewBurkeErnzerhof (PBE) implementation83 of the generalized gradient approximation for the description of the exchangecorrelation functional along with semiempirical vdW corrections based on the D3 scheme.84 Results show differences in the absorption of the various molecules, with a gradual increase from N2 to O2 and H2O (Fig. 12b). It is important to note that H2O interacts with both A-site cations and halogen anions of the CsPbI3(001) surface, destabilizing the chemical equilibrium of the surface. Such a process strongly accelerates the transformation of the perovskite lattice into the inactive yellow orthorhombic -phase of CsPbI3 (see inset of Fig. 12b).

A black-phase of europium-enriched CsPbI3 was integrated into monolithic mesoporous (mp) architectures made of mp-TiO2/mp-ZrO2/mp-carbon by double-step annealing (80 C + 350 C) and fast quenching. The applicability of europium and other rare elements for industrial purposes can be debated; nonetheless important and consolidated fingerprints can be found in the pathway of microelectronics and optoelectronics. From the use of this single species and from its similarity to Pb in terms of ionic radius and affinity to iodine in forming iodides we can learn a simple method for enabling and favoring black phases of CsPbI3 against its yellow phase. This was made possible by a synergetic action on the nucleation rate and on the mass transport. Further improvements and/or alternative strategies can indeed take inspiration from this study.

An important update has been just published in Science87 on the long-term durability of CsPbI3-based devices. The new frontier is using a 2D layer of close stoichiometry on top of the 3D structure of the perovskite. It has been shown87 that devices with this 2D capping layer did not degrade at 35 C and required >2100 hours at 110 C under constant illumination for a 20% degradation of their initial efficiency. 6. Sustainability compatible with Pb use

Before concluding, we would like to devote a few considerations to the use of lead. Many papers have been published on this subject, mostly to propose a reasonable choice among lead-free perovskites (e.g., Ag2BiI5,88 ASnX389). Although these classes of perovskites currently are not performing equally to their Pb-based counterparts, they certainly deserve special attention.90 Different from that, recent papers aimed at a closer analysis of the costbenefit trade-off in dismissing Pb for perovskites,91 an approach that we particularly like. This point-of-view is strengthened by the evidence that photoconversion efficiency values able to compete with the ones in the market within a short lapse of time are solely currently achievable by using lead.

Another main argument to consider in the overall balance is how much Pb is included in perovskite layers inside a solar cell, module, or panel. The answer is that, in all these cases, the Pb content is very little basically for two cross-correlated reasons: (1) in any perovskite lattice the iodide cage around a Pb atom is pretty large and two Pb atoms are 0.63 nm apart (SiSi distance in bulk Si is 0.23 nm); and (2) thin perovskite layers thus contain a small integrated amount of Pb, with 4 Pb atoms inside 1 nm3 of perovskite (e.g. MAPbI3).

Concrete action to further reduce the Pb content inside a solar cell consists of scaling down the thickness of the perovskite layer. Thus, the advent of new techniques for layer deposition that allows the thickness down-scaling and concurrently preserves the quality of the material, such as the LV-PSE method, becomes pivotal for efficient, low cost, and environmentally friendly device production.

As an additional argument in favour of Pb, its use for PSC manufacturing remain enormously lower than for battery production per year.93

More recently, debate has started on possible Pb-leakage during catastrophic events or along the path of technology disposals. According to this, an interesting experiment was set up in ref. 92 that simulates Pb release and its dissolution from real devices in rainfall water. In a 200 mm 200 mm PSC minimodule containing a 300 nm-thick perovskite film, we estimate an integral amount of Pb of 4 1019 atoms. In 1 m2-large panels, this amount increases to 9 1020atoms, about three orders of magnitude less than one mole of lead. Assuming 5 mm rainfall per day for 1 week fully degrading the perovskite layer inside one panel (1 m2), we calculate 10 ppm of Pb finally diluted in water. This number stays well below the safety level as reported in ref. 93 for soils (400 ppm) and health (300 ppm) risks. Nonetheless, the implementation of special tapes, as reported in ref. 92, that capture most (over 99.9%) of the leakage without compromising the performance and operation of the PSCs is already feasible.

Effective encapsulating remedies and capturing Pb atoms before their release in the environment are, in our opinion, fundamental challenges that, once solved, would enable the use of Pb against any risk. At the same time, methods to reuse captured Pb in tape or coverings will further support a new sustainable era of Pb-containing PSC devices.

7. Remarks and conclusions

We register worldwide an urgent need to speed up the ecological transition to take care of our environment. Lately, we are also experiencing how the upheaval of the geopolitical equilibrium kick-starts the clean energy share against the use of fossil fuels, especially in Europe. The International Energy Agency (IEA) has recently (03/03/2022) published a document in which A 10-point plan to reduce the European Union's Reliance on Russian Natural gas is discussed94 to be implemented within a year to restrict Russian natural gas imports while maintaining consistency with the European Green Deal and ensuring energy security and affordability. Among the actions discussed by the IEA, we notice tracing a fast track on renewable energy projects in Europe by a simplification of administrative barriers and procedures to tackle a reduction by 6 billion cubic meters of imported gas within a year: a new challenge that enormously shrinks the timescale along a procedural pathway already signed by the declared EU objectives. Wasting energy is likewise faced by encouraging a thermostat reduction of 1 C by consumers with an impact in reducing gas use by some 10 billion cubic meters within a year.94 Climate neutrality would benefit from the new choices and could be even closer than 2050. In January 2021, CNN has claimed: History made: Renewable energy surpassed fossil fuels for European electricity in 2020, with renewables delivering 38% of electricity last year, compared to 37% delivered by fossil fuels. The 2020 data show an increase in electricity generation to 1052582 GW h, which was 29994 GW h more than the generation from fossil fuels.95 But today it comes out that this scenario is not sufficient for Europe for energy independence.

Perovskites come to the aid of existing technologies for renewable energy production due to their versatility, spreadability, and, to a certain extent, sustainability, provided the development of solvent-free or solvent-poor up-scalable processes are adequately boosted with funding dedicated to lab-to-fab projects. Research must nowadays quickly focus on innovation. Consistently, the development of pilot lines is advisable in collaboration with leading companies in the field of energy production or by private capitals that want to invest in a brighter future, even complementary drawing from focused actions within the European project plan for funding. Synergy must be particularly pragmatic to be effective in a few years.

In the European landscape, Enel Green Power recently announced the new project TANGO (iTaliAN pv Giga factOry) to expand the production capacity of its 3SUN factory in Catania, Sicily (Italy), up to 3 GW per year from the current 200 MW/y through an investment of around 600 million euros, of which nearly 118 million was contributed by the EU's Innovation Fund.

The 3 GW production facility is expected to be fully commissioned by July 2024, after starting with the first 400 MW in September 2023, making 3Sun Europe's largest gigawatt-scale factory producing high-performance bifacial PV modules. The project will foster next-generation high-efficiency solar technology in Europe, and it will contribute toward the achievement of the EU's increased ambitions of 40% energy consumption from renewable sources by 2030 helping to reduce the continent's energy dependency.

The technological roadmap of the factory aims to push current Si Heterojunction technology (SHJ) close to Si cell theoretical efficiency limits given by Shockley-Queisser theory and then to switch to tandem Perovskite/SHJ technology to overcome 30% cell conversion efficiency. From an industrial point of view, SHJ is strategic due to the use of innovative technological processes; it is characterized by a simplified process flow with a small number of steps, significantly lower than those of conventional photovoltaic technologies. These characteristics allow for a very high level of manufacturing automation and the utilization of advanced artificial intelligence algorithms for improved quality process control. Moreover, all the process steps, both for cells and module fabrication, are carried out at temperatures below 200 C, thus preserving all the high mechanical and electronic properties of Si wafers allowing for high performance and reliability of solar cells.

The industrialization of monolithic tandem perovskite/SHJ will require a technological effort to reach the high performance level that is now at the laboratory scale, but it will have a minor impact on the industrial process and factory operations. Indeed, the process flow will include a few additional process steps for top cell deposition with minor changes on the process steps for SHJ bottom cell deposition and on the assembly process (interview to Marina Foti, EGP).

We trust that initiatives bringing together academic and industrial expertise in complementary fields able to uptake innovation, coupled with the uniqueness of perovskite solar cells, will accelerate the ecological transition and enable the societal transformation to fulfil the 2050 EU agenda for a sustainable future.
