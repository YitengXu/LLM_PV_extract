Abstract

Electrochemical power tools are regarded as essential keys in a world that is becoming increasingly reliant on fossil fuels in order to meet the challenges of rapidly depleting fossil fuel supplies. Additionally, due to the industrialization of societies and the growth of diseases, the need for sensitive, reliable, inexpensive, and portable sensors and biosensors for noninvasive monitoring of human health and environmental pollution is felt more than ever before. In recent decades, electrospun fibers have emerged as promising candidates for the fabrication of highly efficient electrochemical devices, such as actuators, batteries, fuel cells, supercapacitors, and biosensors. Meanwhile, the use of synthetic polymers in the fabrication of versatile electrochemical devices has raised environmental concerns, leading to an increase in the quest for natural polymers. Natural polymers are primarily derived from microorganisms and plants. Despite the challenges of processing bio-based electrospun fibers, employing natural nanofibers in the fabrication of electrochemical devices has garnered tremendous attention in recent years. Here, various natural polymers and the strategies employed to fabricate various electrospun biopolymers are briefly covered. The recent advances and research strategies used to apply the bio-based electrospun membranes in different electrochemical devices are carefully summarized, along with the scopes in various advanced technologies. A comprehensive and critical discussion about the use of biopolymer-based electrospun fibers as the potential alternative to non-renewable ones in future technologies is briefly highlighted. This review will serve as a field opening platform for using different biopolymer-based electrospun fibers to advance the electrochemical device-based renewable and sustainable technologies, which will be of high interest to a large community. Accordingly, future studies should focus on feasible and cost-effective extraction of biopolymers from natural resources as well as fabrication of high-performance nanofibrous biopolymer-based components applicable in various electrochemical devices.

1 Introduction

Electrochemical devices are tools that can generate electricity through chemical reactions or that use electrical energy to activate chemical reactions. These power tools are key building blocks in a world that is becoming increasingly reliant on fossil fuels to meet the challenges associated with rapidly depleting fossil fuel supplies.1,2 Actuators, batteries, fuel cells, sensors, and supercapacitors are known as growing electrochemical devices that work by conversion of chemical reactions into electrical energy.3,4 Electrochemical actuators have the potential to assist disabled people via providing efficient artificial muscles that mimic the function of natural skeletal muscles.5,6 Living without portable power tools, such as cellphones, laptops, and so on, is meaningless in today's world. Batteries are an essential component of the vast majority of the devices we use on a daily basis.7,8 Clean energy with no carbon dioxide emissions could be generated using fuel cells. These devices are capable of providing the electrical energy required in inaccessible areas such as buses, boats, spacecraft, and so on.9,10 According to the available literature, supercapacitors could also be beneficial power tools through providing many rapid charge/discharge cycles for elevators, buses, automobiles, etc.11,12 Moreover, electrochemical sensors can be employed in a wide range of applications, including biological or chemical warfare, food industries, medicine, and home monitoring.13,14 Polymeric components are assumed to be an integral element in fabrication of the above-mentioned electrochemical devices. Besides polymeric membranes, nanofibers fabricated through electrospinning set-ups have also paved the way for the development of higher performance electrochemical devices.4,15–17 Although one goal of using electrochemical tools is to apply fewer fossil fuels and reduce pollution, both are increased by producing synthetic polymers.

In recent decades, pollution from petrochemical products as well as fossil fuels has received wide attention due to its numerous adverse effects on human health and well-being.18,19 Alternative sustainable, environmentally friendly, renewable, and biodegradable substances that are less harmful to the environment have been the subject of numerous research efforts over the years.18,19 Based upon the findings, the use of biomaterials is becoming more popular to reduce the consumption of nonrenewable resources.20,21 Biopolymers are commonly referred to as biomolecules made up of multiple monomeric units covalently linked together. The term “bio” denotes that the materials used are derived from plants or living organisms and are biodegradable.22–24 In contrast to the simple and random structure of synthetic polymers, biopolymers possess a complex 3D network. Besides biocompatibility and non-toxicity, sustainability is the main feature that distinguishes biopolymers from synthetic ones. After the usage, these polymers are reabsorbed in the environment via natural processes. Biological degradation of these polymers occurs easily as a result of their exposure to bacteria in compost, soil, and other environments which efficiently reduces emission of CO2, pollution, and global warming. Moreover, the rising cost of petroleum fuel sources, as well as a growing interest in the use of renewable and environmentally friendly resources, has led to the development of natural biopolymers in a variety of fields.25–27

Biopolymers are classified in different ways such as based on their rate of degradability, type of polymer backbone, structural repeating unit, resource, and application. In terms of repeating units, they could be categorized into polynucleotides, polypeptides, and polysaccharides. In addition, they are generally grouped into natural and semi-synthetic categories based on the source of biopolymers.25–27Fig. 1 depicts natural biopolymers frequently applied in various electrochemical devices. As a result of biopolymer's beneficial characteristics, natural polymer-based components have been extensively utilized in various electrochemical devices in different forms of meso/microporous polymer films, micro/nanofibrous membranes, etc. Among them, the use of biopolymer-based nanofibers has led to the fabrication of high-performance and versatile electrochemical devices. This can be attributed to the high surface-to-volume ratio of the nanofibers, the presence of tiny pores between the fibers, and the porous structure of the nanofibrous membranes.

The electrospinning method is the main production technique for biopolymer-based nanofibrous components applicable in the various electrochemical devices mentioned above. Apart from the electrospinning technique, several methods have been developed to produce nanofibers, such as self-assembly, phase separation, drawing, and template synthesis. However, none of them have the advantages and versatility of the electrospinning method. The main advantages of this technique compared to other methods are simplicity, low cost, ability to produce continuous nanofibers with a diameter of several tens of nanometers to several tens of micrometers, and fabrication of highly interconnected porous structures with a very high specific area. These interpenetrating network structures have excellent potential for exposure to different electrolytes to transport ions in electrochemical devices. Also, structures with completely different morphologies and properties can be easily achieved by controlling the electrospinning process conditions: for example, nanofibers with a porous surface, bi-component or multi-component fibers, hollow fibers, and core–sheath fibers. The capability of spinning a wide range of synthetic and natural polymers, ceramics, and combinations, and also the facility of surface functionalization to impart desired properties are other advantages of this technique. Moreover, today, industrial electrospinning machines have been developed, which have opened up the possibility of mass and fast production of these fibers. Due to these advantages, nowadays electrospun nanofibers have gained much attention in a wide range of applications, such as sensors, actuators, supercapacitors, fuel cells, solar cells, batteries, tissue engineering, drug delivery, wound dressing, and filtration. Despite the advantages, this method also faces some challenges that limit the use of this method for some natural and synthetic polymers, such as the need for precise control of electrospinning parameters and environmental conditions (i.e., polymer concentration, voltage, spinning distance, flow rate, temperature, humidity, etc.), insolubility of some polymers, toxicity of chemical solvents, and lack of complete evaporation of solvent from produced nanofibers. The following section provides approaches to the fabrication of homogeneous electrospun fibers from biopolymer resources and their current challenges.

2 Biopolymer-based electrospun fibers

Polysaccharides are the main class of natural biopolymers which could be subdivided into natural, anionic, and cationic groups. These biopolymers consist of long monosaccharide chains bonded through glycosidic linkages. Cellulose, chitosan, and alginate have been widely employed in numerous electrochemical tools as cost-effective, abundant, and renewable polysaccharides.28–30

Cellulose, the agriculture waste biomass, is formed via the linking of linear chains of D-glucose. It is the most frequent and affordable natural biopolymer. This biopolymer has appropriate thermal and mechanical characteristics. Meanwhile, the fabrication of electrospun cellulose is challenging due to its high crystalline structure. Dissolution of cellulose requires expensive solvents and sometimes applying a high temperature. Co-Solvent systems and microwave irradiation are other methods applied for dissolving cellulose. The mentioned challenges have resulted in employing cellulose derivatives such as cellulose acetate (CA). This biopolymer can be easily dissolved in common solvents (e.g., acetic acid, acetone, etc.) and then electrospun. Afterward, the fabricated CA fibers can be re-converted to cellulose electrospun fibers via deacetylation. Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) is commonly utilized to obtain cellulose nanofibers from an electrospun CA mat. Complete regeneration could be achieved through using alcoholic NaOH, while an aqueous one results in the regeneration of cellulose only on the surface of the electrospun fibers. It is worth noting that many studies apply electrospun cellulose mats to produce carbon nanofibers (CNFs).31–33

Chitosan could be obtained from chitin which is a frequent biopolymer on the earth. Crab shells and fungi cell walls are the primary chitin resource that are disposed of in seafood industries daily. Fluoroacetic acid, trifluoroacetic acid, and dichloromethane are the solvents used for preparing an electrospinning solution containing the chitosan biopolymer. Meanwhile, the low viscosity of the solution is a challenging factor toward the fabrication of chitosan electrospun fibers. Electrospinning of chitosan with the association of a synthetic polymer and deacetylation of chitin nanofibers are the most applied methods to get defect free chitosan nanofibers.34–36

Alginate, algin, or alginic acid is a linear copolymer that could be found in brown algae (seaweed). Source, maturity, season, and other factors affect the repeating alginate unit. The worm-like structure of the alginic acid residues has restricted the electrospinnability of this polymer. In other words, low viscosity and lack of entanglement between the polymer chains are the main challenges toward the fabrication of alginate electrospun fibers. In addition, the cationic structure of alginate results in enhancement of the electrical conductivity in the polymer solution, leading to difficulties during the electrospinning process. Based on the findings, an increase of the polymer concentration from 1 to 6 wt% only results in the formation of droplets. The use of co-solvents (such as glycerol and water) and hydrosoluble polymers (e.g., PVA, PEO, etc.) and modification of the structure (sulfation, esterification, etc.) have been widely reported as efficient strategies for the formation of bead-free alginate electrospun fibers.37,38

Gelatin is another well-known natural polymer obtained from the hydrolysis of collagen extracted from the skin and bones of animals. It can be easily dissolved in hot water (above 40 °C) and form electrospun fibers. Kotatha et al.39 maintained the syringe temperature at 45 to 50 °C to fabricate gelatin nanofibers from the gelatin/water electrospinning solution. Naghibzadeh et al.40 also dissolved gelatin in glacial acetic acid solvent and produced electrospun nanofibers by maintaining the syringe at a constant temperature of 35 °C.

Lignocellulosic plants are the main resources of the natural lignin polymer. Lignin is available as a waste product from the paper and pulp industry and is also available commercially in a purified form from most renowned chemical industries. Two different methods, Kraft and Organosolv, are commonly used to produce lignin. In the first procedure, sodium hydroxide and sodium sulfide assist in extracting the polymer, while organic solvents are employed in the second process. In 2007, electrospun lignin fibers were synthesized via dissolving lignin in ethanol. In addition, curly lignin fibers were fabricated by García-Mateos et al. by applying H3PO4/ethanol as solvent. Since the extracted lignin has a low molecular weight, its electrospinning is difficult. Therefore, a small amount of an electrospinnable polymer with high molecular weight such as PAN, PVA, PVP, and PEO is usually used in its electrospinning process to improve its electrospinning ability and thus produce desirable nanofibers. But anyway, the renewable lignin biopolymer is used as the main component of nanofibers in these studies. Notably, lignin is a cost-effective precursor for the preparation of carbon nanofibers (CNFs).41–44 Although the formation of natural nanofibers is challenging, environmental concerns have led to increasing evaluation and examination of these membranes applicable in various electrochemical devices as illustrated in Fig. 2.

3 Electrochemomechanical actuators

Artificial muscles have been developed to mimic the function of natural skeletal muscles by utilizing various structures and mechanisms. In natural muscles, when an electrical signal is sent from the brain through the nerve cells, calcium ions are released in the muscle cell membrane. This phenomenon causes intramuscular interactions and changes in the length of actin and myosin myofibrils, resulting in changes in muscle volume.

Polymeric actuators are intelligent materials that can respond mechanically to external stimuli from various sources such as electrical signals,49,50 pH,51 pressure,52 light,53,54 magnetic fields,55 and temperature changes.56 While polymeric actuators are commonly referred to as artificial muscles in the literature, electroactive actuators are more functionally similar to natural muscles (Fig. 3A). Electromechanical and electrochemomechanical muscles have been developed using electronic and ionic electroactive polymers, respectively. While electrostatic forces and electric dipole rearrangements in the electronic group cause dimensional changes, the ion exchange mechanism is responsible for volume changes in ionic electroactive polymers.57 The required voltages for actuation of the electromechanical artificial muscles are too high, limiting their use in many practical applications, particularly in medical fields. In contrast, the electrochemomechanical artificial muscles can be actuated by very low voltages, on the order of 1 V, compared to more than 1 kV in electromechanical artificial muscles.58 Also, dimensional changes remain stable in the electrochemomechanical muscles even after cutting off the electrical potential, and the reverse potential is required to return to the initial situation. Meanwhile, in electromechanical muscles, dimensional changes return immediately after cutting off the potential, so the electrical signal is always essential to maintain their position.59 In addition, the wireless electrochemical actuation of electrochemomechanical artificial muscles has recently been investigated, which could open up new horizons in this field by eliminating the need for a direct physical connection to an electric power supply.60–62

The use of biopolymers such as chitosan, silk, cellulose, cellulose acetate, gelatin, and others in the design of actuators has always been considered, particularly in biological and medical applications where the non-toxicity, biocompatibility, and biodegradability of components are critical. On the other hand, due to the biocompatibility and doping ability with biomolecules, conducting polymers could be used as biomaterials in conjunction with other biopolymers to fabricate fibrous bioactuators for in vitro or in vivo applications.63,64 Moreover, bioactuators should be able to operate at neutral pH and in biological fluids of the body such as blood, urine, sweat, and so on for practical in vivo applications.65 However, the actuation performance of electrochemomechanical bioactuators in biological fluids may be inferior to that of organic electrolytes. Ismail et al.66 investigated the performance of a chitosan/polyaniline fibrous linear actuator. This actuator demonstrated an actuation strain of 0.4% in a potential cycle between −0.2 and +0.8 V. A chitosan/polypyrrole (PPy) fibrous actuator with an actuation strain of 0.54% has also been produced in another effort.67

In recent years, various nanomaterials such as nanoparticles, nanotubes, and nanofibers have been used to synthesize artificial muscles and improve their mechanical, electrical, and electrochemical properties.68–74 The diffusion of ions is expected to be facilitated as a determining factor of the actuation stress and strain as well as the response time of the electrochemomechanical artificial muscles by using highly porous nanofibrous structures. It has been shown, for example, that the response time of fibrous electroactive actuators made from polymeric gels decreases with decreasing fiber diameter.75 Asai et al. developed a novel nanofibrous actuator and investigated the effect of electrospinning solution concentration on nanofiber diameter. The results showed that using a lower solution concentration results in the formation of nanofibers with a smaller diameter, which increases the specific surface area and actuation strain of the produced actuator.76,77 As a result, much attention has been paid to the use of nanofibrous structures in the fabrication of conducting polymer-based actuators. Despite the efforts made, direct fabrication of conducting polymer nanofibers using the electrospinning process is challenging due to their low molecular weight, inherent brittleness and poor solubility in common solvents.78 It should be noted that the formation of fibers from a polymer using the electrospinning technique needs minimum requirements that are influenced by various parameters. One of the most important requirements is dissolving the polymer in a suitable solvent and obtaining a desirable polymer solution in terms of molecular weight, concentration, viscosity, electrical conductivity, and surface tension. For instance, if the viscosity of the polymer solution is too low, the entanglement of molecular chains will be insufficient to stabilize the balance of surface tension and electric forces of the solution, and thus a stable and uniform jet is not formed. The low inherent solubility of most conductive polymers in common solvents is caused by their strong inter- and intramolecular interactions. Also, if the solution of these polymers is prepared, they have insufficient viscosity for the electrospinning process, which is caused by their low molecular weight.79 Blending conducting polymers with electrospinnable ones,80,81 using the co-axial electrospinning method to produce core–shell nanofibers (the core is the conducting polymer),82 and in situ polymerization of conducting polymers on the surface of the substrate nanofibers83–85 are the most common alternative methods. However, blending the conducting polymers with a non-conducting component or using them in the core portion results in a nanofibrous layer with low electrical conductivity and poor electrochemomechanical properties for use as an actuator. Electrospinning followed by in situ chemical or electrochemical polymerization, on the other hand, is a simple and versatile technique for producing conducting polymer nanofibers. In fact, a core–shell structure is also created using this method, but the conducting polymer is on the surface of the fibers, which lays the groundwork for producing an effective nanofibrous conducting polymer-based actuator.

As a result, in recent years, researchers have used the method mentioned above to fabricate nanofibrous electrochemomechanical actuators based on conducting polymers (Fig. 3B).46,86–89 Based on the results, the actuation mechanism of conducting polymer nanofibers could be anionic or cationic exchange, or a combination of both, depending on the type and size of cations and anions in the electrolyte solution. In a potential cycle between −0.8 and +0.8 V, the prepared nanofibrous actuators could have an angular displacement of more than 720°. Moreover, the produced nanofibrous actuators exhibit faradaic behavior, so their angular speed and bending angle are under the control of applied current and consumed charge, respectively. These actuators can also act as a sensor to detect working conditions. Furthermore, the geometry effect of the nanofibrous layer on the actuator's final responses was investigated. The results indicated that a multilayered cylindrical structure of the nanofibrous layer has superior actuation performance than a single layer because it increases the active surface area of the actuator and thus facilitates the diffusion of the ions.90

The CA biopolymer has been used to make high-performance nanofibrous bioactuators. The electric dipole arrangement of CA is responsible for the actuator's actuation when an electric current is applied.91 The use of conducting polymers, such as polyaniline nanoparticles, can improve the actuation performance of CA nanofibrous actuators even up to four times.92 In this case, the ion exchange mechanism of conducting polymers, in addition to the piezoelectric actuation of cellulose acetate, plays the main role in the final performance of these actuators. The bacterial cellulose biopolymer has also gained interest in biomedical applications due to its excellent biocompatibility, non-toxicity, hydrophilicity, porosity, and mechanical properties. The nanofibrous bacterial cellulose/conducting polymer actuators have good actuation performance in terms of bending mechanical movement, response time, and stability.93

Silk fibroin is another biopolymer that has been used in nanofibrous bioactuators. This natural biopolymer is an ideal candidate for various in vivo and in vitro biomedical applications due to its unique properties such as non-toxicity, biocompatibility, biodegradability, mechanical properties, and excellent processability.94–98 By coating the silk fibers with conducting polymers, an electrochemomechanical bioactuator that mimics natural muscles can be fabricated. Severt et al.99 synthesized aligned silk/PPy nanofibrous bundles using electrospinning and sequential chemical and electrochemical polymerization processes (Fig. 4A). In the electrochemical polymerization process, they used two different dopants, namely para-toluene sulfonic acid and sodium dodecylbenzenesulfonate. Moreover, a biologically compatible electrolyte, phosphate-buffered saline solution, was used to assess the stress and strain generated during the electrochemical actuation of these linear bioactuators. While both anionic and cationic ion-exchange behavior was observed in silk/PPy nanofibrous bioactuators doped with para-toluene sulfonic acid, only the cationic exchange mechanism was observed in the phosphate-buffered saline electrolyte in the sample doped with sodium dodecylbenzenesulfonate. Poly(hydroxymethyl-3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) (PEDOT–OH) was also electrochemically polymerized on the surface of silk nanofibers in this study. The results showed that the actuation stress and response time of the silk/PEDOT–OH nanofibrous actuator are more appropriate than those of the silk/PPy sample (Fig. 4(Ae–h)). The strain generated by both of these nanofibrous actuators is several orders of magnitude higher than the actuation strain reported in silk/PPy film-based actuators.100

Researchers are also interested in electrospun gelatin nanofibers coated with conducting polymers as biocompatible electrodes in electrochemical applications. Harjo et al.102 recently used the chemical polymerization process to coat PPy on the surface of gelatin–glucose nanofibers to prepare a linear bioactuator. The results of electrochemomechanical responses showed that the obtained bioactuators have good actuation strain and stress with high stability. These actuators have been shown to perform better in an aqueous solution than in a propylene carbonate solution. Also, the generated gelatin–glucose/PPy nanofibrous actuators have simultaneous sensing capability for monitoring the surrounding conditions such as the applied current density and mechanical load and can be used as dual function actuator-sensor devices. Furthermore, the electrochemical actuation properties of gelatin/glucose coated with PPy were examined in a cell culture medium as the electrolyte solution. The actuation in this electrolyte was based on the cationic (sodium and partly calcium ions) ion-exchange mechanism.103

It should be noted that conducting polymers synthesized using electrochemical polymerization are likely to have more desirable electrical conductivity and electroactivity characteristics than those formed via the chemical method due to their more molecular arrangement and fewer structural defects.104,105 Therefore, researchers have been interested in coating the surface of electrospun nanofibers by electrochemical polymerization to create high-performance nanofibrous actuators. That is why the highest reported actuation strain among the nanofibrous electrochemomechanical actuators, i.e., 18%, has been achieved by a gelatin–glucose/PPy bioactuator produced using the electrochemical polymerization process and dodecylbenzenesulfonate as a dopant.102 This significant performance has also been related to the properties of the gelatin–glucose nanofibrous substrate. The generated stress of this actuator was 0.14 MPa, which is within the range of natural skeletal muscles. Using tetrabutylammonium triflouromethanesulfonate as a dopant in the polymerization process instead of dodecylbenzenesulfonate led to lower actuation stress and strain (Fig. 4B). Furthermore, these nanofibrous actuators exhibited remarkable actuation stability even up to 1000 cycles, making them promising bioactuators for use in practical applications.

The eggshell membrane with a 3D fibrous and porous biopolymeric structure similar to electrospun membranes has recently piqued the interest of researchers, especially for biomedical applications owing to its high specific surface area, high porosity, flexibility, biocompatibility, non-toxicity, low cost, and easy availability.106–108 Coating the eggshell membrane surface with conducting polymers could make it potential to be used in electrochemical biodevices. Beregoi et al.109 evaluated the actuation performance of the PPy coated ESM actuators. Electrochemical actuation of these bioactuators in a potential cycle between −0.6 and +0.6 V revealed that PPy oxidation and reduction result in the insertion and expulsion of electrolyte ions and solvent molecules into and out of the structure and mechanical movements.

Developing dry bioactuators that can operate in the air without being immersed in an electrolyte solution, which enables more practical applications, has always been of interest to researchers. A dry actuator is typically constructed from two electroactive electrodes and a polymeric electrolyte layer. When one electrode is oxidized, the other electrode is electrochemically reduced, and electrolyte ions are transferred from one electrode to the other. As a result, this actuator experiences a bending movement. However, dry linear actuators can also be fabricated if the actuation of two conducting polymer electrodes is based on two different ion-exchange mechanisms.65,110 Using ionic liquids in polymeric electrolytes has recently attracted the attention of many researchers to improve the actuation performance of dry nanofibrous actuators.77,111–113

Finally, the results show that in recent years, much progress has been made in designing and understanding the actuation mechanisms of nanofibrous actuators. Despite the advantages of nanofibrous actuators based on biopolymers, not much research has been reported on these devices. Therefore, investigation of the performance of nanofibrous actuators based on biopolymers such as lignin, gelatin, and alginate can be a topic of future studies in this regard. In most cases, the biopolymeric nanofibrous substrate plays a passive role, which provides biocompatibility to the actuator. However, some biopolymers, such as cellulose acetate, have intrinsically electrical actuation ability, which enhances the final actuation performance of the bioactuators due to the synergistic effect with the active component. It can be stated that the performance of nanofibrous bioactuators has reached close to that of natural skeletal muscles in terms of generated actuation strain and stress. However, the main challenges for the commercialization of these actuators are related to lifetime, durability, and, more importantly, their response time. One of the problems faced by biopolymer actuators based on conducting polymers is the potential drop along the actuator, which causes all parts of the device not to participate in electrochemical reactions. As a solution, metallization of the surface of electrospun biopolymer nanofibers and then coating with conducting polymers is suggested for further research. Developments in nanofibrous bioactuators as electrochemomechanical artificial muscles are promising for future advanced applications such as smart wearable devices, bio-robotics, computer-integrated surgery, drug delivery, e-skin, and smart textiles.

4 Application of nanofibrous biopolymers in rechargeable batteries focusing on anode materials and separators

The tremendous need to tackle fossil-fuel-based energy storage problems requires preparing, engineering, and formulating green materials for use in sustainable electrochemical energy storage technology (SEEST).114–116 Rechargeable batteries are regarded as an indispensable component of sustainable technology for stationary and mobile energy storage in industrial and domestic settings.117–120 Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs), as the most reliable energy storage devices, are considered as key tools in both developed and developing countries.121,122 LIBs are the dominant energy storage devices in today's electric vehicle markets, while lithium–sulfur technology has revealed an extremely high gravimetric energy density of 2600 W h kg−1 as the next generation of LIBs.123,124 However, the growing success and safe operation of rechargeable batteries in SEEST will necessitate the development of effective methods and the application of sustainable, cost-effective materials that are abundant around us.125,126 Combining sustainable bio-battery materials with nanotechnology and an electrospinning strategy can help us to get closer to designing an advanced, safe SEEST.

Biologically inspired synthetic methods of polymers have attracted widespread attention in SEEST.125 Carbonization of biopolymers at high temperature results in unique properties such as a 3D connected architecture and high surface area, making them ideal structural templates for engineering a wide range of nanoscale bio-based materials.127 Additionally, the inherent N,O-doped feature of carbon derived from biopolymers leads to interlayer space expansion of the graphitic carbon domains, providing active sites for charge storage.128,129 Moreover, as a low-cost, versatile, and straightforward setup, electrospinning can synthesize ultrathin nanofibers with exceptional performance.130,131 The study of nanofibrous biopolymers as rechargeable battery electrodes and separators is seldom reported and overviewed, but it could be very interesting for future battery technology development. This section focuses on biopolymers that have been fabricated using the electrospinning technique for lithium/sodium-ion (Li/Na-ion) battery components, particularly anode electrodes and separators.

4.1 Enhancement of lithium/sodium storage device electrodes

Li/Na-ion batteries include Li/Na intercalation compounds as the cathode and anode materials. The working principle of rechargeable batteries is based on the reversible redox reaction between these materials.132 The anode and cathode are physically separated by a porous membrane and ionically are connected through a Li/Na-based electrolyte.4,133 Typical cathode materials in Li/Na-ion batteries are transition metal oxides that receive Li/Na ions and electrons during discharge.134 Graphite and hard carbon are also the most commonly used anode electrodes.135–137 When the battery is charging, these negative electrodes store the Li/Na ions, which are then released with the flow of electrons to the cathode side during the discharge process.132Fig. 5A(a) shows a schematic illustration of the charge–discharge process in Li/Na-ion batteries.

The electrodes' characteristics determine the performance of the battery.140 The structural stability, porous architecture, and flexibility of the electrodes should be considered the main parameters to be applied for improving portable SEEST.3 Shifting from powder-form to fiber-based materials can help overcome the potential bottlenecks of battery systems such as electrode structural changes, low electronic conductivity, dendrite growth, short life, and low energy density.141,142 In addition to electrochemical performance, in line with the philosophy of a reduced environmental impact, choosing renewable resources and viable and sustainable low-cost precursors could potentially mitigate the negative effects of CO2 emissions and develop a competitive battery system.143,144 The recent advances in engineering bio-based materials as nanofibrous electrodes with the electrospinning technique for rechargeable batteries are presented in the following.

Graphite and other carbonaceous materials are the most commonly utilized anode electrodes in Li/Na-ion batteries owing to their high availability, processability, and suitable structural stability.145 Meanwhile, many researchers are looking for new materials to replace graphite due to its low theoretical capacity (372 mA h g−1vs. Li+/Li), which is considered the primary issue with conventional batteries.146 Unlike carbon nanostructures based on graphene, carbon nanotubes, and mesoporous carbon, these new materials are expected to be low in cost while having a large production potential.137,147–149 CNFs produced by electrospinning are promising candidate electrodes for low-cost and large-scale commercial production of rechargeable batteries.150,151 The most common precursor material used to synthesize CNFs is petroleum-based polyacrylonitrile (PAN).152,153 However, due to the high preparation cost and the environmental issues associated with greenhouse gas emissions, this precursor is not recommended for battery application.154 Apart from PAN, cellulose and lignin are viable and sustainable precursors for the production of CNFs. Cellulose and lignin biopolymers have recently been used to fabricate electrodes, mostly for application in flexible rechargeable batteries, owing to their unique structure, surface chemistry, CO2 neutrality, biocompatibility, and most importantly, their abundance and low cost.155–157 Jin et al.143 investigated a sustainable and freestanding electrode based on biomass waste lignin to design Na-ion batteries. Due to the robust morphology of bio-based carbon fibers, the mechanically stable and 3D interconnected electrode exhibited a high specific capacity of 292.6 mA h g−1 at 0.02 A g−1 and excellent cycling performance in this study. Fig. 5A(b) confirms the structural integrity of the electrodes after cycling. Aside from the Na-ion battery, bio-derived CNFs from lignin were also applied in LIBs. In this work, lignin was blended with two other bio-based polymers, namely thermoplastic elastomer polyurethane (TPU) and polylactic acid (PLA), to compare the electrochemical and physical properties of carbonized lignin/TPU and lignin/PLA electrodes.121 This research showed that the compatibility of lignin with different polymers is critical in the ultimate carbonized CNF morphology. In contrast to the homogeneous structures of lignin/TPU, lignin/PLA demonstrated well-defined porosity, leading to additional sites for Li-ion insertion (Fig. 5B(c)).

In comparison to graphite, which is the most common anode electrode in rechargeable batteries, transition metal oxides (TMOs) such as cobalt, iron, nickel, and titanium oxides have attracted great attention to maximize the utilization of Li/Na-ion batteries through a novel conversion mechanism.158,159 However, TMOs have significant volume changes during the insertion and de-insertion of Li/Na ions, as well as surface instability and low electronic conductivity, which hinder direct use of these materials as the electrode in batteries.152,160,161 To address the above-mentioned issues, Xiaojing et al.126 proposed an electrospinning technique to design a flexible Fe2O3 nanoparticle electrode based on lignin. Lignin-derived electrospun CNFs with an average fiber diameter of ∼200 nm were used as a substrate to functionalize Fe2O3 nanoparticles in order to accelerate the reversible conversion reaction of the metal oxide with lithium and improve the electrochemical stability of a half-cell battery. The engineered sample showed a high specific discharge capacity of 715 mA h g−1 after 80 cycles at 50 mA g−1 current density. Fig. 5B(d) demonstrates the charge/discharge profiles of the half-cell including the prepared flexible electrode in the first, second, and third cycles. Other researchers also have confirmed the mentioned findings. For example, to promote the electrochemical performance of the Fe3O4 phase as the anode material in LIBs, lyotropic CA as the CNF phase was introduced to fabricate the Fe3O4@CNF electrode via electrospinning.162 The synergetic effect of Fe3O4 and CNFs was observed to form disordered structures with a large surface area, which increases the porosity, electrolyte penetration, and Li+ ion transferability in the designed electrode. In terms of capacity and electrochemical stability, Fe3O4@CNFs performed better than FexOyNFs as the blank electrode (Fig. 5B(e)).

4.2 Electrospun biopolymer-based separators

As mentioned earlier, lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) are composed of an anode, a cathode, a liquid electrolyte, and a separator. The separator is placed between the cathode and the anode in the presence of a liquid electrolyte which prevents physical contact of the electrodes and allows transportation of ions between electrodes. An ideal separator must be highly porous, have excellent wettability toward liquid electrolytes, and be thermally stable and mechanically strong enough to withstand the winding operation during the assembly of LIBs.163–165 In the past decade, cellulose-based biopolymer separators have been successfully explored for application in LIBs owing to their good wettability.166 Various electrodes such as LiMn2O4, LiCoO2, and LiFePO4 cathodes with graphite anodes have been used to examine the compatibility of cellulose-based separators and compare their performance with commercially available polypropylene (PP), polyethylene (PE), and PP/PE/PP separators. Since the micro-scale diameter of biopolymers brings some limitations in the porous structure and physical properties, these polymers are currently applied as a separator for the LIBs. On the other hand, nanofiber biopolymers possess a well-controlled and uniform porous structure. These structures have lengths and diameters of up to several microns and offer the best physical properties.166,167 Sheng et al.168 summarized major types of cellulose such as cellulose, nanocellulose, and cellulose derivatives, as well as various methods for the preparation of cellulose-based LIB separators, including electrospinning, force spinning, paper making, solution casting, conventional coating, and phase inversion method, and concluded that the papermaking process could be a good choice for low-cost bulk production. Chen et al.169 presented a detailed study on the advancements in the structural and physical characteristics of nanocellulose in energy storage applications such as Li/Na ion batteries, lithium–sulfur batteries, and supercapacitors. They also presented the various methods of nanocellulose fabrication and their impacts on the structure and surface chemistry of nanocellulose. In the literature, a lot of cellulose derivatives or composite cellulose separators are also studied. Sun et al.170 introduced the zeolitic imidazolate framework (ZIF) in the cellulose nanofibers to make an eco-friendly composite separator membrane using the vacuum filtration method and examined the increment of the porosity from 42 to 55%. Liu et al.171 also introduced an environmentally friendly composite separator (PVA/cellulose nanofiber-Li+) prepared by a non-solvent induced phase separation method and claimed that the designed composite separator showed a porosity of over 60% and an ionic conductivity of 1.1 mS cm−1. In addition, the engineered separator exhibited a reversible capacity retention of 93% compared to the commercial PP separator which showed only 80% capacity retention after 50 cycles. Asghar et al.172 prepared a cellulose/polyvinylidene fluoride–hexafluoropropylene blend separator using the phase inversion method. They declared improvement of the porosity from 40 to 85%, enhancement of electrolyte uptake from 170 to 310%, and increment of ionic conductivity from 0.89 to 1.89 mS cm−1 compared to the pure polyvinylidene fluoride–hexafluoropropylene membrane. Liu173 focused on the safety and better performance of the LIBs and prepared a polyformaldehyde/cellulose nanofibrous separator by a thermally induced phase separation method. They obtained a tensile strength of 116 MPa and a puncture strength of 9.4 MPa for the prepared separator, which were significantly higher than those of the commercial PE separator (85 MPa and 5.1 MPa). Moreover, they declared an ionic conductivity of 1.39 mS cm−1 and 0.76 mS cm−1 for the formaldehyde/cellulose blend separator and commercial PE separator, respectively. Zhang et al.174 fabricated a thermally stable northern bleached softwood craft/polysulfonamide/nanofibrillated cellulose composite separator by using a papermaking procedure. The thermal shrinkage of the prepared composite membrane was 0%, while the commercial Celgard separator showed 55 and 66% thermal shrinkage, respectively, in vertical and horizontal directions after exposing to 180 °C for 1 h. Furthermore, the composite and the Celgard separator membranes exhibited a capacity retention of 78 and 71% in the application of LIBs.

Song et al.175 explored a composite separator with the polymer substrate of sodium alginate and attapulgite as inorganic additives with the help of a phase inversion method and achieved thermal stability with almost no thermal shrinkage at 250 °C for 2 hours. Also, the composite separator retained a capacity of 82% after 700 cycles at 5C. Uddin et al.176 prepared a non-woven PVA/lignin membrane using electrospinning. The separator was thermally stable and lost only 1% weight at 248 °C. In addition, a weight loss of about 40% was displayed after 400 °C, whereas the Celgard separator lost 99% of its weight at 260 °C. Liu et al.177 established an environmentally friendly, low-cost, biodegradable composite separator (CNF-LS). The composite membrane showed thermal stability at 270 °C and electrochemical stability in the voltage range of 3 to 4.8 V exhibiting a capacity of 188 mA h g−1 at 0.2C.

Despite the above-mentioned advancements in biopolymer-based CNFs via the electrospinning technique for fabrication of environmentally friendly electrodes and separators with fine diameter, excellent mechanical and physical properties, high electrical conductivity, and outstanding architecture, much work remains to be done to achieve batteries suitable for practical applications. For this goal, we propose an investigation of cellulose-based LiFePO4 as an alternative to nickel or cobalt-based cathode materials to combine with the CNF anode in a full-cell LIB configuration. This strategy introduces a practical green battery with respect to both anode and cathode. In addition to conventional LIBs, electrospun CNF biopolymers with their hierarchical porous structure could be composited with the sulfur cathode in lithium–sulfur batteries to suppress the shuttle effect, the most common challenge for this kind of battery.178 Although there are countless reports of engineering the cathode side of lithium–sulfur batteries, finding a suitable substituent for lithium metal has received less attention.179 Researchers working on sulfur-based batteries should consider the potential of CNF biopolymers as anodes in the fabrication of lithium–metal-free sulfur batteries for practical applications. Additionally, a lot of efforts have been put on a variety of biopolymer-based separators with various structures and components by using different fabricating strategies. Although biopolymer separators have been successfully developed for high performance LIBs, there are still some issues that need to be resolved in the future, such as flammable property, hygroscopic nature, and thermal shutdown. To overcome these issues, the chemical modification method can be employed to graft the polymer on the surface of biopolymer separators at the molecular level to get the low moisture absorption, best flame retardancy, and shutdown enabled biopolymer separator. It is also noteworthy that, to the best of our knowledge, there is no report regarding the application of both biopolymer-based electrodes and separators in rechargeable batteries. Therefore, it is of great value to design and characterize bio-polymer based electrospun electrodes and separators in a battery structure.

5 Electrospun biopolymers applicable in the synthesis of fuel cells

This section representatively describes the various applications of natural polymer nanofibers in fuel cell (FC) energy conversion devices. In recent decades, many researchers have dedicated their attempts to develop fuel cells as an alternative renewable, environmentally friendly energy source (with an electrical energy conversion efficiency of more than 60% with lower emission than conventional combustion engines).180–187 By considering some of their notable advantages, including high energy density, cycle stability, desirable thermal and mechanical stability, and high power density, which outperform conventional combustion-based technologies, FCs can act as a bridge between the present and forthcoming energy demand in a sustainable way.188–192 Owing to higher energy conversion efficiency, FCs can pave the way to find more applications in housing power, electronics, power plants, passenger vehicles, and military applications (see Fig. 6).187,193–195 The rationale behind FCs is the redox reaction at the anode and cathode electrodes.196,197 At the anode side, hydrogen molecules undergo an oxidation reaction (H2 → 2H+ + 2e−), where H+ ions migrate to the cathode side from the electrolyte, and electrons flow towards the cathode side through the external circuit. At the counterpart electrode, the reduction reaction occurs at the cathode side, where oxygen is reduced to water (O2 + 4H+ + 4e− → 2H2O).196,198,199 Based on this concept, FCs are composed of anode, cathode, and electrolyte; also, water is the main by-product. According to Table 1, FCs can be categorized according to their electrolyte into proton exchange membrane FCs (PEMFCs), alkaline FCs (AFCs), solid oxide FCs (SOFCs), phosphoric acid FCs (PAFCs), molten and carbonate FCs, (MCFCs) and microbial FCs (MFCs). By considering the importance of FCs as a green energy source, lots of materials have been investigated to improve the performance of their components; among them, natural polymer nanofibers due to their high surface area and eco-friendly nature have received researchers’ attention.192,200,201 As public awareness about natural resources and the environment has grown, renewable and environmentally friendly materials have received much attention.202 In this regard, natural polymer nanofibers are of great interest as a choice for several industrial applications.203 They are eco-friendly, abundant in nature, cheap, non-hazardous, recyclable, and relatively inexpensive than synthetic reinforcements such as glass/carbon fibers; consequently, they can provide several environmental and economic benefits.192,200,201 Notably, the appropriate usage of natural polymer nanofibers can reduce environmental pollution and resolve waste disposal complications.204,205 Incorporating natural polymer nanofibers in different components of FCs can improve the performance and provide appropriate properties, especially in PEMFCs, AFCs, and MFCs.204,206 It is worth mentioning that natural polymer nanofibers can reinforce proton conduction because they can establish order and so enhance the fuel cell's performance.206,207 In addition, the incorporation of natural polymer nanofibers can have a profound effect on ionic conductivity (due to the presence of functional groups) and control the swelling properties in ion-exchange membranes.208 Furthermore, they can greatly increase the mechanical properties of prepared membranes due to their high tensile strength.202,209 Also, natural polymeric nanofibrous substrates are promising candidates for GDL in FC applications due to their outstanding mechanical properties, tunable surface chemistry, anisotropic character, renewable nature as well as biocompatibility.208

As previously stated, electrospun biopolymers can have a positive role in facilitating proton conductivity. Xu et al.216 prepared cellulose nanofibers via the solution blowing method and embedded these natural polymer nanofibers in a sulfonated poly(ether sulfone) membrane as a proton-conducting membrane in DMFCs. The incorporation of nanofibers improved the mechanical, thermal, and morphological properties of the prepared membranes. Embedding solution-blown nanofibers enhanced the proton conductivity, methanol permeability as well as dimensional stability of the provided membranes.209 According to the proton conductivity measurement at different temperatures, the composite membrane with 5 wt% nanofibers at 80 °C exhibited a maximum value of 0.13 S cm−1. The result showed that incorporating electrospun biopolymers improved the proton conductivity by around 1.6 times compared to the pristine membrane. Cellulose can create effective interactions with sulfonic acid groups in sulfonated poly(ether sulfone) and construct some proton-conducting channels by possessing a large number of hydroxyl groups.217,218 Although these membranes were not applied in an FC setup, methanol permeability and selectivity values (4.45 × 10−7 cm2 S−1, and 8.9 × 104 S s cm−3, respectively) were quite promising.

By considering the reinforcing properties of electrospun biopolymers in PEMs, Cai et al.207 treated nano cellulosic fibers with different concentrations (0.1, 0.15, 0.2, and 0.25 mol L−1) of phosphoric acid solutions and then embedded them in sulfonated poly(ether sulfone) for use as a PEM. As shown in Fig. 7B, SEM images and size distributions of phosphoric acid treated nano cellulosic fibers as well as their composite membrane with sulfonated poly(ether sulfone) were investigated. Furthermore, the effect of different phosphoric acid concentrations on the physicochemical characteristics of prepared membranes was studied. The composite membrane doped with 0.25 mol L−1 phosphoric acid exhibited a maximum proton conductivity of 0.154 S cm−1 at 80 °C and 100% RH, a minimum methanol permeability of 4.41 × 10−7, and a maximum selectivity of 17.6 × 104 S s cm−3. It is worth noting that hydroxyl groups of cellulose nanofibers and phosphate groups of phosphoric acid can create some hydrogen bonding interactions. Moreover, as depicted in SEM images (Fig. 7B), this can change the morphology of the nanofibers. According to the results, phosphoric acid-treated nanofibers can potentially be applied in FCs.

Embedding imidazole groups in cellulose nanofibers is another strategy to improve their compatibility and the proton-conducting ability for PEMFC application. In one approach,217 imidazole groups were incorporated in solution-blown cellulose nanofibers in the presence of an acid–base pair. The acid–base pair can facilitate protonation/deprotonation and consequently improve the proton transfer ability of the final membrane.220 The imidazole-treated nanofibers could effectively reduce fuel crossover through the membrane. The interaction between nitrogen groups in imidazole and functional groups in the membrane could form a cross-linked structure and narrow transportation channels, preventing fuel permeation (SEM images depicted in Fig. 8A(a–d)). The proton conductivity of the composite membrane reached up to 0.132 S cm−1 by introducing imidazole-treated nanofibers into the sulfonated poly(ether sulfone) polymeric matrix at 80 °C and 100% RH.

In DMFCs, the contribution of the PEM is more critical than in PEMFCs, because the membrane in DMFCs not only acts as a proton conductive medium but it also has an inevitable role in establishing a direct contact between oxidant and methanol.181,189,221 Notably, methanol crossover from the anode to the cathode side can have some poisonous effect on the cathode's electrocatalyst.206,222 In another attempt,220 different amino acids were immobilized onto cellulose nanofibers to shape clusters as proton transfer pathways. It is worth mentioning that acid–base pairs can facilitate proton transfer by the Grotthuss mechanism.212 In this regard, acid groups in the polymeric backbone (–SO3H) and base groups in amino acids (–NH2) can assemble an acid–base pair and facilitate proton conduction. In addition, the 3D network of functionalized cellulose nanofibers can be helpful in creating proton pathways through the membrane. Also, the suitability of amino acid-incorporated cellulose nanofibers was confirmed by FC performance analysis. According to Fig. 8B and SEM images, all immobilized amino acid cellulose nanofibers maintained three-dimensional networks. Also, the modification with amino acids was confirmed by TEM images as well as EDS mapping. Sulfonated polysulfone was used to fill the inter-fiber voids of the amino acid modified cellulose nanofibers. Moreover, physicochemical investigation was conducted on prepared composite membranes formed by sulfonated polysulfone and amino acid modified cellulose nanofibers. The hybrid membrane with 20% Cell-Ser cluster exhibited a maximum proton conductivity of 0.264 S cm−1 at 80 °C. Furthermore, the fuel cell performance of all amino acid-functionalized cellulose nanofiber incorporated SPSF composite membranes tested at 60 °C and 100% RH and the sulfonated polysulfone-Ser PEM reached 87.22 mW cm−2. Based on the evaluations, the Cell-AAs embedded sulfonated polysulfone membrane can be a promising candidate for DMFC application.

AFCs are another well-known class of FCs in which natural polymer nanofibers are utilized. This kind of FC has demonstrated significant potential as a source of energy due to its considerable properties, such as faster reaction kinetics, inexpensive materials for its electrocatalyst part, and simple operating system in comparison to PEMFCs. Although AFCs are a promising approach in FCs, there are still some challenges toward finding an appropriate anion exchange membrane.

Recently, some researchers have investigated the incorporation of natural-polymer based nanofibers in the anion exchange membrane. It has been shown that cellulose can tolerate an alkaline medium. Das et al.223 have employed the cellulose nanofiber isolated from Citrus tangerine for application in AFCs. The cellulose extraction steps from the orange peel are shown in Fig. 9A(a). First, they quaternized cellulose nanofibers with 1,4-diazabicyclo-[2.2.2]-octane, and then they incorporated them in a quaternized polysulfone backbone to achieve an AEM. The cross-linked structure of quaternized polysulfone and quaternized cellulose nanofibers could efficiently prevent the membrane from excessive swelling and improve the mechanical and chemical stability in an alkaline medium. Also, AFM images of polysulfone crosslinked with dibromobutane and quaternized polysulfone with 5 wt% quaternized cellulose are depicted in Fig. 9A(b–d). Furthermore, a maximum ionic conductivity of 74.23 mS cm−1 was achieved for a membrane consisting of 15 wt% quaternized cellulose nanofibers in quaternized polysulfone, which was 2.7 fold higher than that of the membrane without quaternized cellulose nanofibers. As shown in Fig. 9A(e and f), the cross-linked structure along with quaternized cellulose nanofibers gave the membrane desirable durability in KOH solution after five days, making it a promising candidate for AFCs for long-term application.

In a case study, according to Fig. 9B(a), sulfonated nano bamboo fibers were introduced into the quaternary ammonia poly(ether ether ketone) matrix to maintain its mechanical properties as well as prevent it from excessive swelling. Upon reinforcing with sulfonated nano bamboo fibers, the swelling ratio diminished significantly from 27.5 to 7.5% at 80 °C, whereas IEC was as high as 1.76 mmol g−1. By taking into account the noticeable properties provided by sulfonated nano bamboo fibers, the prepared membrane not only exhibited desirable mechanical stability, but also showed a proton conductivity of up to 0.069 Ω cm−2 at 30 °C and 0.0124 Ω cm−2 at 80 °C, which is suitable for AFC applications.224 Also, the single cell performance of the sulfonated nano bamboo fibers/quaternary ammonia poly(ether ether ketone) membrane was measured at 60 °C without and with different backpressures with a peak power density of 930 mW cm−2 (see Fig. 9B(b and c)).

As another approach, a freestanding and mechanically flexible mat was synthesized from lignin-derived cellulose nanofibers with supercritical CO2, followed by thermal treatment at 180 °C. Next, an electrocatalyst was produced by deposition of Ag nanoparticles on the surface of the prepared mat to evaluate its application for oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) in a 0.1 M KOH aqueous solution. Lignin-derived cellulose nanofibers exhibited high specific surface area (∼583 m2 g−1), a desired mat structure, excellent electrical conductivity, as well as higher resistance against corrosive electrolyte in the fuel cell environment. Also, by considering that lignin is a by-product of cellulose, it is abundant, and consequently, the utilization of lignin is environmentally friendly and cost-effective. The prepared electrocatalyst was tested for ORR in an alkaline medium (0.1 M KOH), and its performance in terms of silver loading, limiting current density, and mass activity was also evaluated. The electrocatalytic activity of lignin-based nanofibers was compared to that of commercial HiSPEC 4100 TM Pt/C, and the mass activity of the prepared electrocatalyst (15 wt% Ag/lignin-derived cellulose nanofibers) was 119 mA mg−1, which was greater than that of the commercial one (98 mA mg−1).225

Also, natural polymer nanofibers have found application in microbial FCs (the schematic working principle of a MFC is depicted in Fig. 9B(d)). An esterified cellulose nanofiber (E-CNF) from corncob was mechanochemically treated to produce GDL material with application in MFCs. As shown in Fig. 9B(e and f), carbon cloth as a GDL was coated with esterified cellulose nanofibers, and the electrochemical performance of MFCs containing a uniformly esterified cellulose nanofiber-based GDL was evaluated. According to Fig. 9B(g), the power density for the esterified cellulose nanofiber-based air cathode (ACE-CNF) reached 1518.3 ± 39 mW m−2, 30.1% higher than that of the reference sample (1166.7 ± 29 mW m−2, for PTFE based air cathode (ACPTFE) containing MFCs prepared by a traditional brushing method). Although the reported results were promising about the application of natural polymer nanofibers in MFCs, there is a need for further enhancement that can be achieved by optimizing the structure and thickness of the esterified cellulose nanofibrous layer.226

In conclusion, FCs are one of those renewable energy systems that have been drawn the interest of many researchers as an alternative green energy resource and can play an essential role in the transition from fossil fuel to more green sources, as endorsed by the Paris climate change agreement.227,228 In this regard, some efforts have been focused on decreasing the cost of final FCs, including improving the performance of individual cells, minimizing the usage of expensive electrocatalysts and electrolytes, and finding cost-effective alternative materials. As an environmentally friendly and cost-effective material, natural polymer based nanofibers can provide us desirable properties, such as high surface area and fully interconnected pores, and this review sheds light on some practical applications of natural polymer nanofibers in different components of FCs. The electrospun biopolymers have been used as the membrane and GDL in PEMFCs. Also, they have found some applications in GDL and membrane preparation of AFCs. Furthermore, these structures have been applied as a GDL as a component of MFC devices. By considering these aspects, incorporation of natural polymer based nanofibers in different components of the fuel cell can impart desired functions including water retention, enhancement of mechanical and thermal stability, and construction of proton transfer channels, in addition to playing an effective role in fuel barrier properties of fuel cell's proton exchange membrane.

The future offers high hopes for natural polymer nanofibers as a promising bio-based component for FCs since these materials are appropriate materials with application in ion exchange membranes and electrocatalyst layers.

6 Electrochemical sensors using bio-polymeric nanofibers

Sensors are integral to developing next-generation electronics for numerous applications, including environmental and healthcare monitoring.229 The electrochemical (EC) module of sensors over other strategies (optical, calorimetric, chemiresistive, and hybrid modes) enables the detection of low trace levels of harmful and infectious pathogens, biomarkers, and gaseous and water pollutants.230–235 The state-of-the-art EC sensors are related to developing novel nanomaterials and rapid data processing techniques to attain high-performances. Polymer-based functional nanomaterials have been extensively explored to replace currently available inorganic material-based commercial sensors due to their energy efficiency, cost-effectiveness, mechanical and physicochemical flexibility and scalable fabrication.236,237 However, the massive utilization of sensors to design smart connected societies has raised a probable threat of solid-waste management and ecosystem contamination 2. To attain sustainability in EC sensors, biopolymers have emerged as promising eco-friendly, economical and energy-efficient materials for environmental and healthcare monitoring.238,239 The biocompatible attribute and high aspect ratio of biopolymers with nanofibrous morphology facilitate long-term in vitro and in vivo stability in EC sensors. Moreover, due to the ease of fabrication, high surface-to-volume ratio, robust charge transport, controllable physicochemical attributes and processing flexibility, electrospun biopolymer nanofibres are compatible with devising miniature and sensing devices. However, although there is much literature dedicated to electrospun biopolymers, reports on their utilization to architect EC environmental sensors and biosensors are still in their infancy.

Majorly, biome-derived polysaccharide, polypeptide and polyphenol nanofibres fabricated by electrospinning have been employed to design modern-age EC sensors.239 However, they have been utilized in nanocomposites, additives, substrates and functional materials rather than in pristine form to architect EC sensors. Their role in architecting EC sensors employed for healthcare and environment monitoring is critical, where biocompatibility, optimization and non-toxicity are prerequisites. They are used for multipurpose in architecting EC biosensors, including immobilization, fabricating substrates and electrodes and surface functionalization.

In healthcare sectors, electrospun nanofibre-based EC sensors are employed for early, real-time and point-of-care monitoring of various biomolecules, biomarkers and pathogens. For instance, Ahmadi et al.240 developed an EC paper-based analytical device utilizing an alkaline regenerated electrospun nanocellulose-paper substrate for blood glucose monitoring. Further, the EC biosensor was architected with three screen-printed electrodes, with a working electrode fabricated from reduced graphene oxide (rGO) modified Au deposited over a cellulose nanofibre substrate. The biosensor array demonstrated excellent selectivity, reproducibility, stability and sensitivity (around 9.9 × 10−4 kΩ−1 mM−1) towards 3.3–27.7 mM glucose with the lowest detection limit (LDL) of 0.1 mM. The fabricated biosensor demonstrated the prospects for flexible, portable and degradable devices addressing biocompatibility and solid-waste management issues.

On the other hand, Kim et al.241 developed a wearable and non-invasive epidermal glucose EC biosensor based on cellulose/β-cyclodextrin electrospun over a reverse iontophoresis electrode. An excellent in vitro interstitial fluid glucose (linear range sensitivity: 0–1 mM) monitoring was observed with a significant diffusion coefficient (9.0 × 10−5 cm2 s−1) and low LDL (9.35 × 10−5 M). These outcomes reveal the potential of electrospun biopolymer nanofibre-based EC sensors to be employed for personalized diabetes monitoring and diagnosis, one of the most considerable inventions required in the field of non-invasive blood glucose monitoring at a global scale. Moreover, the use of two biopolymer blended electrospun nanofibres to architect EC sensors has also been reported to include the merits of both the precursors due to probable synergistic effects. Following this, Yezer et al.242 demonstrated the excellent glucose monitoring efficacy of a cellulose acetate–chitosan blended electrospun nanofibre based EC biosensor. The improved glucose monitoring behaviour of the fabricated EC sensor was attributed to both the biopolymers' merits and synergistic effects. However, the energy required to operate EC biosensors is another bottleneck hindering their commercial and wearable prospects. In this context, Lv et al.243 reported a self-powered and highly flexible biosensor based on a Au-modified bacterial cellulose-based substrate for ultralow facial glucose monitoring with a broad linear detection range (0–50 mM).

Moreover, there are reports on the utilization of biopolymers to fabricate nanogenerators based on the principle of harvesting triboelectricity.244,245 For instance, Zhang et al.244 demonstrated the use of a bio-derived gelatin-based triboelectric nanogenerator integrated with a polyaniline hybrid sensor for ammonia detection. It proposes that the combination of two different biopolymers can be utilized for devising biocompatible self-operational EC sensors by following the same technique. Thus, these modern-age energy-efficient glucose biosensors possess latent capacity to replace the current invasive diabetes detecting strategies, solving a major global issue of premature and diabetic mortalities, regular harsh invasive screening, and associated issues of kidney failure, blindness, heart strokes and lower limb amputation.

The utilization of electrospun biopolymer nanofibre-based biosensors is extended to the detection of micronutrients. For instance, Chauhan et al.246 demonstrated enhanced detection of 25-hydroxy vitamin-D3 with a sensitivity of 0.16 μA ng−1 mL cm−2 and LDL of 10.0 ng mL−1 utilizing an electrospun cellulose acetate fiber based EC biosensor (Fig. 10A and B). The obtained outcomes were according to ELISA measurements, and hydrophilic conducting paper substrate utilization turned it into an ecofriendly, cost-effective, disposable and portable module. EC biosensors can also be utilized for understanding and investigating cellular functioning and genetic molecules. For example, Civan et al.247 reported an electrospun cellulose monoacetate/tetraethyl orthosilicate nanofibre based EC biosensor to investigate guanine oxidation in single strand deoxyribonucleic acid molecules. On the other hand, Pavinatto et al.248 detected 17α-ethinylestradiol (EE2), which is a female synthetic hormone possessing high estrogenic action and latent potential to disrupt the endocrine system, through an electrospun PVP/chitosan/rGO nanofibre based EC biosensor.

Additionally, these sensors have been employed to detect and monitor life-threatening diseases and toxic chemicals. Vasudevan et al.250 reported a cellulose acetate–MoS2 hybrid-based EC sensor for early-stage diagnosis of severe cardiac disease and acute myocardial infarction, and for detecting the troponin I level. The fabricated EC biosensor was also evaluated to detect other biomolecules in human serum, illustrating the latent ability to transform healthcare diagnosis by detecting multiple biomolecules through a single EC sensor. Further, ochratoxin A is an abundant mycotoxin teratogenic, nephrotoxic, and neurotoxic to humans and animals. Al-Dhabi et al.251 and El-Moghazy et al.252 reported innovative ultrasensitive strategies for its selective and low trace detection employing hybrids of electrospun biopolymer based EC sensors.

Moreover, Liberato et al.253 designed a label-free EC biosensor based on electrospun polyamide-6 (PA6)/chitosan nanofibres to detect Leishmania braziliensis in human serum. The addition of chitosan into the hybrid system surged the charge carrier transport, which effectively enhanced the detection efficacy of the fabricated biosensor. The outcomes revealed the detection range to be 2.5 to 10 pg mL−1 with LDL of 0.2 pg mL−1, which was significantly lower than that of the enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay. Furthermore, the biosensor could detect two nonspecific antibodies (T. cruzi and β-actin) with the integration of principal component analysis strategies. Thus, EC biosensors based on electrospun biopolymer nanofibres cover a wide range of biomolecules and biomarkers, possessing huge potential in designing personalized healthcare sensors.

Electrospun biopolymer-based EC sensors have also been reported to monitor several environmental conditions and contaminants. For instance, Nath et al.254 demonstrated EC detection of pH using a membrane made of red cabbage extracted anthocyanin hybridized with gelatin nanoparticles and captured inside electrospun ethyl cellulose nanofibres which can also be utilized for monitoring milk spoilage. On the other hand, Teepoo et al.249 demonstrated the utilization of chitosan–gelatin hybrid electrospun nanofibres in architecting EC sensors to detect hydrogen peroxide, which is a critical molecule in numerous environmental, industrial and physiological processes (Fig. 10C). They immobilized horseradish peroxidase through composite biopolymer nanofibres and observed excellent sensitivity (44 μA mM−1 cm−2), optimum selectivity and low LDL (0.05 mM). The demonstrated outcomes revealed the potential prospect of a designed EC biosensor for detecting hydrogen peroxide for healthcare and environmental monitoring. The reports on monitoring water contamination by detection and adsorption of numerous heavy metals and radionuclides are significant in number and have already been discussed in the previous section. However, the potential of electrospun biopolymer nanofibre-based EC sensors is yet to be evaluated for gas/vapour monitoring and requires dedicated research and development.

Hence, the electrospun biopolymer nanofibre-based EC sensors are promising for healthcare and environmental monitoring. However, their detection and monitoring efficacies can be improved using various strategies, including incorporating advanced nanomaterials such as metal carbides and nitrides (MXenes),233,234 borophene,229,255 and others,239 integrating rapid-data processing techniques and improving device architecture. Moreover, the integration of modern-age technologies such as artificial intelligence, cloud computing, 5G generation and internet-of-things with EC sensors has the potential to develop future-generation, sustainable, personalized and remotely accessible monitoring and sensing strategies.229 Considering these features, biopolymer-based EC biosensors have the potential to transform the personalized healthcare sector due to their biocompatible nature. Thus, the smart, miniature and self-powered biosensors can be utilized in skin-embedded modules by incorporation of triboelectric fundamentals and are the near future of personalized healthcare.

In addition, the sensors are a crucial vector for developing intelligent and connected societies, which also creates a threat of massive solid-waste generation. Owing to their biocompatible and eco-friendly nature, biopolymer-based EC sensors exhibit considerable potential to address this challenge and develop sustainable sensing strategies. They can be easily reused, recycled, degraded or repurposed compared to conventional inorganic-material-based sensors, which is required for ecosystem preservation and attaining a circular economy.

7 Biopolymer-based electrospun supercapacitors

Supercapacitors are devices with the ability to store electrochemical energy with a fast delivery rate.256 These electrochemical tools are classified into two main categories, including electric double-layer capacitors (EDLCs) and pseudocapacitors.257 A supercapacitor device comprises electrodes, a separator, and an electrolyte as the main components. Although each part is important, the performance of the supercapacitor majorly depends on electrode materials. The electrodes of EDLCs are generally made up of carbonaceous materials. In addition, they are based on the energy storage principle arising from the electrostatic charge separation on the interface of porous conducting electrodes and absorbed electrolytes. To approach a highly efficient EDLC behavior, the electrodes should possess a high surface area and suitable porosity (containing sufficient meso- and micropores) as well as appropriate mechanical strength or flexibility, conductivity, and chemical stability.258 Electrospun carbon nanofibers are being explored to meet these specific requirements. Meanwhile, pseudocapacitance has been observed in the case of most metal oxides, polymers specifically conductive polymers, metal complexes (nitrides, sulfides, and hydroxides), etc. These materials display faradaic behavior involving redox reactions at the surface or interface, similar to the battery materials. Compared to EDLC materials, pseudocapacitive materials suffer from poor conductivity along with a low accessible surface area to the electrolyte component.259 In addition, despite higher power density than that of the battery structures, lower energy density has restricted their use in energy storage devices.

Freestanding and flexible supercapacitors are becoming popular among worldwide researchers due to their application in wearable electronics. Electrospun CNFs are emerging as electrode materials for flexible supercapacitor applications. In most attempts, PAN and pitch-based CNFs have been utilized for the synthesis of versatile electrodes due to their high carbon content and ideal thermal stability. However, these materials are petroleum-based and non-renewable resulting in the release of toxic and harmful gases during processing. Therefore, natural polymers have been recently considered as the only hope for the fabrication of electrospun carbon nanofibers from nontoxic and highly abundant resources. As a result, biopolymers have emerged as new eco-friendly raw materials to prepare the supercapacitor electrodes. Further advantages of biopolymers over the PAN polymer are requirement of shorter or no stabilization process due to their pre-aromatic structure, higher stability, easy extraction process and availability which make them a better substitute for carbon nanofiber formation. Also, biopolymers on conversion into carbon nanofibers provide a more porous structure and hence good surface area, which is required for EDLC type supercapacitor applications. In 2021, Du et al.260 extracted lignin from three different plant species, including poplar, pine, and corn stalk. In this method, lignin was first extracted and separated by using an ethanol–water mixture, followed by processing and purifying lignin through an Organosolv treatment method. As mentioned earlier, the short length of the lignin polymer chains and their highly complex aromatic structure have made it impossible to fabricate pure electrospun lignin.261 So, the PAN polymer was used as a support polymer, with a weight ratio of 30 to 50% to the lignin weight to obtain homogeneous and bead-free electrospun fibers. Then, a smooth carbon nanofibrous network was gained after a heat treatment procedure at 950 °C. From this attempt, the surface area of carbon fibers decreased in all three cases with an increase in lignin content. The carbon nanofibers obtained from poplar tree-derived lignin possess the highest surface area owing to their better morphology than CNFs prepared from the other cases, i.e., pine- and corn-based lignin. In addition, the surface area for a low lignin content (50%) extracted from poplar, pine, and corn stalk was estimated as 1063, 986, and 930 m2 g−1, respectively. Moreover, the final electrochemical performance was broadly influenced by the type of lignin structure. The poplar lignin-based CNFs (50% PAN) showed the highest capacitance value of 339.2 F g−1 as compared to pine-sourced (302.5 F g−1) and corn stalk-based (280.7 F g−1) lignin nanofibers in the voltage window of 1 V. The reason can be assigned to the effect of morphology on the enhancement of ion diffusion through the electrolyte and therefore improvement of ion exchange between the electrodes. A higher surface area (2048 m2 g−1), as well as a superior electrochemical capacitance of 428 F g−1 at 1 A g−1, was achieved by applying heat treatment at a temperature of 1000 °C and using KOH for activation of corn stalk-based lignin.262 As compared to an acid or base electrolyte, a neutral electrolyte offers stability at big voltage windows resulting in higher capacitance. Also, it prevents the formation of O2 and H2 species during charging–discharging. Similarly, Wu et al.263 utilized lignin/PAN-based carbon nanofibers (1:1) with a total polymer concentration of 28 wt% to obtain a capacitance value of 207 F g−1 at 0.5 F g−1 current density. Due to the fabulous characteristics of the lignin/PAN-based electrospun electrodes and a high demand for supercapacitors with superior electrochemical performance, recent researchers have tried to combine the obtained CNFs with the oxide materials. Such electrospun composites can exhibit both EDLC and pseudocapacitive behaviors due to the existence of carbonaceous and metal oxide structures, respectively. Therefore, the carbon-based materials were incorporated with several oxide materials such as SnO2 and NiCo2O4 resulting in enhanced morphology along with high electrochemical performance features. As an example, Cao et al.264 prepared highly porous core–shell CNFs from lignin–PAN material as the core and tin chloride as the shell. After the electrospinning process, the composite nanofibers were heat-treated at 200, 300, and 400 °C for 2 h under room conditions to complete the tin oxide (SnOx) formation as the shell component and stabilization of the lignin/PAN structure. Then, further heat treatment was carried out in the N2 atmosphere to carbonize the lignin/PAN core section. Meanwhile, a temperature of 400 °C caused the covering of micropores in the CNF core component through the formation of SnOx material and so reduction of surface area (29 m2 g−1) compared to the lower temperature heat treatment (554 m2 g−1 at 300 °C). Finally, the prepared nanofibers at 300 °C were crushed and mixed with conductive carbon black and PTFE binder to form a slurry and examined in the presence of an alkaline electrolyte (6 M KOH). The obtained specimen presented a capacitance retention of 229 F g−1 at 0.2 A g−1 current density showcasing a high energy density of 7.2 W h kg−1 and an appropriate power density of 3.6 kW kg−1. Lei et al.265 fabricated PAN/lignin-based CNFs doped with Ni and Co salts. The proposed method incorporated Ni and Co salts through the immersion method after the electrospinning procedure. Based on the declared results, the fabricated smooth electrospun fibers were changed into a peculiar hierarchical morphology by applying the heat treatment optimized stabilization and carbonization conditions. In addition, a high capacitance value of 1757 F g−1 was obtained at a current density of 2 mA cm−2. Also, 138% capacity retention was gained after 5000 charge/discharge cycles. Moreover, in the PVA–KOH gel electrolyte, hierarchical nanofibers demonstrated a good capacitance value of 134.3 F g−1 even at a high current density of 1 A g−1 in an asymmetrical supercapacitor device with N doped rGO as the negative electrode. Besides the PAN support polymer, several polymers with more affordable costs such as PEO, PVP, and PVA have also been applied to fabricate electrospun lignin-based CNFs.257,266–268 These polymers are also able to provide higher concentration of lignin for the electrospinning procedure compared with the PAN polymer. However, it seems that using the PAN polymer with good thermal stability as a common precursor for the production of carbon materials is considered an ideal option for fabrication of lignin-based nanofibrous supercapacitors.269,270 In a case study, Hérou et al.270 fabricated electrospun fibers from a lignin–PEO (9:1) alkaline solution followed by a pressing process. The pressed fibers were then thermally treated for stabilization (200 °C) and carbonization (800 °C) to develop CNFs with a higher surface area of 716 m2 g−1. The pressing mechanism led to the formation of a more dense and connected electrospun network without any side effect on the micropore content. Consequently, they could achieve a volumetric capacitance value of 30 F cm−3 over a 0.1 A g−1 current density with an energy density of 6 W h L−1. Butnoi et al.269 applied iron nitrate material as an additive with lignin/PEO-based fibers to get a versatile composite containing CNFs and Fe3O4 or Fe2O3 by varying the iron nitrate content from 10 to 20%, respectively. The provided pure CNFs revealed a low specific capacitance value of 102 F g−1 at 0.1 A g−1. On the other hand, the addition of Fe3O4 in the composite structure led to an increase in the specific capacitance up to 216 F g−1 and a high energy density of 43 W h kg−1 at the same current density, which could be linked with the double pseudo capacitance effect in the presence of Fe2+ and Fe3+. In another study, Singh et al.257 demonstrated flexible and free-standing Kraft lignin/PVA based CNFs as an efficient electrode for supercapacitor devices. Despite having a low surface area, they obtained a high energy density (62.6 W h kg−1) and an appropriate power density (1.2 kW kg−1) which displayed the possibility of designing high-performance materials without having the pseudocapacitive effect. The obtained high capacity was attributed to the morphology and stability of the CNF structure that withstands the high working voltage up to 2 V (Fig. 11A). In addition, the reported method involved a higher lignin content compared with that in other attempts made for fabrication of the lignin/PVA based CNFs by Lai et al.225 and Ago et al.266 Ma et al.271 utilized the lignin/PVP resource with the zinc nitrate hexahydrate additive to synthesize hierarchically porous CNFs. Pre-oxidation and carbonization conditions caused the migration of the Zn species toward the surface of CNFs as well as increment in the fiber shrinkage to a larger extent (∼83 nm). The use of lignin also contributed to higher shrinkage (in comparison to PAN or phenolic resin polymers) in the nanofiber diameters during the heat treatment procedure, which is beneficial for increasing the surface area.126,272,273 Notably, as a special treatment, composite nanofibers have also been subjected to acid treatment (2 M HCl) to etch ZnO, known as ‘pickling’.274 This treatment resulted in the formation of a highly mesoporous surface on the CNF structure, which helps to store charges via reversible ion adsorption at the electrode/electrolyte interface. For example, Ma et al.126 figured out 289 F g−1 capacitance for the electrospun lignin fibers embedded with zinc nitrate hexahydrate. The symmetric device fabricated using the optimized fibers showed a capacitance of 217 F g−1 at 0.1 A g−1 with proper flexibility as well.

Despite challenges during the electrospinning of cellulose, it has been extensively considered a powerful resource for the fabrication of supercapacitors. So, most researchers have synthesized electrospun cellulose through the treatment of CA electrospun fibers in an alkaline aqueous solution (e.g., NaOH).197,263,275–277 Nevertheless, pure CA-based CNFs have shown low specific capacitance and poor stability.278 Hence, several attempts have been made to enhance their capacitance via activation, doping heteroatoms, and the addition of high capacitance additives. Ramadoss et al.279 fabricated inter-bonded CNFs by electrospinning the CA polymer. The nanofibers were then activated in a CO2 atmosphere to achieve a capacitance of 241.4 F g−1 and a power density of 84.1 kW h kg−1 at 1 A g−1. The resulting capacitance value was much higher than the one obtained by Fan et al. (202 F g−1). In another work, Kuzmenko et al.275 prepared CNFs from CA/reduced graphene oxide (CA-rGO) through direct carbonization at 800 °C after fabrication of CA-GO nanofibres through electrospinning and immersion methods (Fig. 11B). The prepared electrode demonstrated a volumetric capacitance of 46 F cm−3 (voltage 0–1 V). In addition, the packing density of the obtained material was highest among other reported cases280 and thus suitable for high volumetric demand supercapacitors. Doping with heteroatoms, especially nitrogen, is also an important factor in enhancing the capacitance value. Despite synthetic sources, N-doping could be carried out using natural products. Recently, Wang et al.197 fabricated N-doped CNFs from CA along with soy protein, a natural dietary protein obtained from soybeans. The electrospun nanofibers revealed a capacitance of 219 F g−1 at 0.2 mA g−1, a low resistance, and an excellent EDLC behavior. Also, in a two-electrode system, the capacitance value reached 85 F g−1 at 0.2 mA g−1 current density. In addition, a capacitance retention of 94.1% was reported after 50000 cycles. In another study, the combination of lignin and cellulose (1:1) was applied to enhance the performance of the produced CNFs. The fibers were treated with different concentrations of phosphoric acid. Based on the results, the treated nanofibers with 40% of H3PO4 (PL-5) showed the highest surface area of 837 m2 g−1. Moreover, the applied solvent eliminated the chance of polymer phase separation during electrospinning and so improved the fiber spinnability. They also achieved a capacitance of 346.6 F g−1 at 0.1 A g−1 by using the KOH (6 M) electrolyte. Finally, the combined biopolymer-based nanofibers revealed a high energy density of 24.3 W kg−1 at a high power density of 4000 W kg−1.281

Besides lignin and cellulose, chitosan and biomasses have also been explored in the field of supercapacitors. Chen et al.276 used acetylated sugarcane bagasse along with the PAN polymer (5:5) to yield defective porous CNFs, which led to the formation of a highly flexible nanofiber sheet. The synthesized CNFs showed a proper capacitance value of 289.5 F g−1 at 0.5 A g−1. Solid-state symmetric devices were also fabricated using a PVA/LiCl gel electrolyte which resulted in providing a high energy density of 56 W h kg−1 and a high power density of 1.26 kW kg−1. The obtained electrochemical performance was attributed to plenty of pores in the electrospun structure, narrow distribution of the pores, and high membrane flexibility. As discussed earlier, heteroatom doping in CNFs causes an increment of surface area, enhancing adsorption of the electrolyte ions, and improvement of the electrochemical performance. Chitosan possesses self-doped nitrogen groups in the structure, while its spinnability is limited due to the formation of a highly viscous solution. Taking advantage of chitosan N-doping, Chen et al.282 electrospun a chitosan/PVP polymer to obtain smooth and homogeneous nanofibers. The prepared nanofibers were further doped with P-atom via immersion of the blend nanofibers in a KNO3/NH4H2PO4 solution. Wettability and electrolyte contact features were improved due to N-doping, while P-doping helped to achieve a high electron capacity delivery. The fabricated CNFs exhibited a high surface area of 1529.4 m2 g−1 and a high capacitance of 358.7 F g−1 at 1 A g−1. The reported high performance was linked with the co-doping of N and P atoms causing increment of the carbon interlayer spacing and easier diffusion. Furthermore, conductivity was enhanced due to alternating fiber connections as well as the formation of a network-like structure which caused enhancement of the Faraday redox reaction and so the improvement of the electrochemical performance.

The above discussion shows that lignin and cellulose are the most popular biopolymers among all other biopolymers in the fabrication of carbon nanofibers for supercapacitor applications. The use of biopolymers as a carbon nanofiber precursor offers an alternative to the high cost conventional petroleum based precursors. These nanofibers have shown tremendous performance in terms of specific capacitance, energy density, power density and flexibility. These carbon nanofibers can be further treated to increase the active surface area without sacrificing their flexibility property for wearable electronics applications.285 However, the work on biopolymers except lignin and cellulose is still limited and exploration of other biopolymers is still needed. This review also illuminates that the main reason for non-exploration of other biopolymers for carbon nanofibers is the challenges in the direct spinnability of biopolymers via electrospinning. So, there is a strong need to modify the structure of existing biopolymers to make them spinnable and heat stable so that a good conductive carbon nanofiber network can be devised for supercapacitor applications.

8. Conclusion and future perspective

In the current energy-dependent world, the development of electrochemical power tools for converting and storing energy plays a crucial role. Academic research on fabricating versatile electrochemical devices is developing at an incredibly fast pace. Actuators, batteries, fuel cells, sensors, and supercapacitors are the main electrochemical devices that have attracted the interest of numerous researchers as well as various industries.

Polymeric structures serve as the foundation for all electrochemical power tools. Various forms of these structures, such as films, fibers, particles, etc., are used to synthesize different components of the devices mentioned above. In recent decades, the findings have confirmed fabulous and superior advantages of electrospun polymers compared with those of the other polymeric configurations. This could be linked with the unique characteristics of the nanofibrous networks such as interconnected fine fibers, tiny pores, high surface to volume ratio, and porous network. Meanwhile, there is a growing awareness regarding the danger of the petroleum-derived material applications and their negative impacts on the environment. Based on the historical data, the amount of polymer waste will rise to around 12500 million tons by 2030, while each 100 million ton can damage the ecosystem amounting to US $ 13 billion a year. This growing issue has rendered many researchers to explore novel materials that can be used in a variety of devices while reducing the usage of fossil fuels and their derivatives.

Owing to the rising sustainability and environmental concerns, the development of novel biocompatible and eco-friendly resources is in high demand. In this area, biopolymers have been considered as safe materials for a wide range of applications. In addition, there is increasing motivation to use natural resources such as microorganisms and plants to fabricate nanofibrous functional materials applicable in electrochemical tools. These renewable and biodegradable polymers are stiff, non-corrosive, lightweight, and cost-effective. They assist in preserving petroleum-based raw materials and decreasing the CO2 volume released into the atmosphere. Electrospun biopolymers are regarded as a promising choice for providing electrochemical actuators by facilitating ion diffusion in a non-toxic, biocompatible, and biodegradable fibrous matrix. In addition, electrospun bio-derived CNFs from cellulose and lignin resources have represented well-defined porous structures as anode materials, leading to the formation of more sites for Li/Na insertion in lithium and sodium-ion batteries. Also, cellulose-based nanofibers have resulted in the preparation of battery separators with high strength and proper electrolyte uptake. Moreover, high surface-to-volume ratio, cost-effectiveness, and interconnected pores of natural-polymer based electrospun fibers have enabled their practical usage as a gas diffusion layer and membrane in PEMFCs and AFCs. Furthermore, electrospun membranes derived from biopolymer and biomass sources could be beneficial in the creation of efficient, freestanding, and flexible supercapacitors with appropriate capacitance retention and energy density by using an eco-friendly route.

It is worth noting that several factors have limited the practical usage of electrospun biopolymers. As a result, renewable polymers account for beyond 5% of the commercial market. The current review paper may encourage and motivate scientists and manufacturers to overcome eco-centric trepidation by investing in the development and growth of novel bio-based fibrous materials while taking into account the following recommendations and crucial aspects. First, the limited production rate of biopolymers has remained a significant challenge, particularly from an economic point of view. New procedures and methodologies for the feasible and affordable fabrication of bio-based materials are valuable to be designed and engineered. Novel natural resources should be explored and characterized in order to broaden the application of biopolymers and substitute hazardous synthetic polymers. Moreover, standard processes for polymer extraction and electrospun fiber preparation should be defined and evaluated to save cost, energy, and time of exploring raw materials. For instance, water as the solvent in the electrospinning process is not only a safe and low-cost alternative but also improves the nanofibers’ stability by increasing crosslinking. Second, the inherent characteristics of biopolymers have restricted their electrospinnability. As a result, various strategies such as adding a synthetic polymer, structural modification, employing co-solvents, specific electrospinning conditions, and even more should be employed to ease the fabrication procedure, as several cases have been stated in this paper. As an example, designing hybrid or composite biopolymer-based materials can optimize the performance of the fibrous structures in terms of tensile strength, flexural strength, and Young's modulus. Third, most efforts have declared the durability of bio-based nanofibrous membranes in lab-scale experimentation, while to our knowledge, no research has been conducted so far to arrive at a durable final commercial product that can meet electrochemical application requirements. Therefore, in situ evaluations are extremely valuable to be monitored in future works along with developing new experimental methods. Fourth, less attention has been devoted to end-of-life treatment and reuse of the biopolymer. A detailed study on the recycling and degradation behavior of these materials will be very vital for a circular bioeconomy. Fifth, the procedure of biopolymer carbonization should be optimized to gain major biopolymer conversion and minimize the unwanted side-products through modifying the reaction time, temperature, and other impactful parameters. Last, application of synthetic polymers as a support material for electrospinning of the biopolymers is inevitable, which should be reduced by employing modified electrospinning devices or novel synthesizing methods.
