Abstract

This review article discusses the current designs and synthetic procedures for organic small molecules as hole-transporting materials (HTMs) with a focus on their structure–property correlation, conductivity, and photovoltaic performance, as well as their high hole mobility and stability. In comparison to non-planar spiro-like compounds, various π-conjugated aromatic and planar molecules have been studied as being important for the generation of new HTMs. Since heteroatoms, such as oxygen, sulfur, nitrogen and silica, have been shown to have an impact on the search for more stable and cost-effective HTMs and perovskite solar cells (PSCs), developing a new molecular architecture with efficient π–π stacking to increase charge mobility or integrating dopant molecular structure into HTM would be a viable approach for generating dopant-free HTMs. A deeper understanding of perovskite/HTM can also provide insight into the design of novel molecular architectures capable of achieving effective and stable systems.

1. Introduction

Solar cells seem to be the most powerful platform, owing to the fact that they use free radiation and are the most self-sustaining of all renewable energy technologies. Since the introduction of perovskites in 2009, all from basic expertise and structural elements to computer architecture and stability, they have advanced significantly. Since they can be synthesized from inexpensive solvent-processing materials and exhibit unit quality comparable to that of commercially available technologies, perovskites are particularly well suited for photovoltaic applications. Numerous techniques have been developed to enhance the PSC power conversion efficiency (PCE). The advantage of PSCs is their ease of operation in n–i–p or p–i–n configurations using solution processors (Fig. 1). By reviewing structural alterations and analyzing their influence on solar efficiency, the goal is to develop universal methodologies for producing low-cost and effective HTMs, opening the way for future scale deployment of PSCs. Up-to-date insights on recently-discovered organic molecular HTMs, both doped and doping-free, are detailed in this article. These methods are essential to enabling potential scalable applications of PSCs. Molecular weight, simple purification, and reproducibility are possible advantages of small organic molecules. Although organic molecules offer many benefits, such as clear molecular weight, simple purification, and strong reproducibility, they also have some disadvantages.

This article provides a recent advance in design, synthesis, and characterization of new small molecules as HTMs, their effect on the photovoltaic efficiency, and the underlying chemistry including doped and dopant-free compounds employed in PSCs and then outlines the requirements needed for commercial viability. This study covers the highest performing organic small molecule HTMs, both doped and dopant-free, as determined by the HTM database, which now comprises over 400 publications on organic molecular hole transporting materials. A comprehensive representation of molecular design and organic synthesis of novel small molecule HTMs, as well as their influence on photovoltaic performance, has been provided in this study, with particular emphasis on the chemistry involved. When looking at the structure of novel HTMs, the most fundamental designs make use of various central scaffolds, which are mostly adorned with arylamine derivatives, to achieve their structural stability. Further advancements in system stability by the use of novel materials are on the horizon, as research moves closer to satisfying industry requirements. The authors note that the large number of HTMs prepared stems from the design and engineering of a diverse array of new and chemically transformed existing molecules, with organic synthesis playing a critical role. All organic HTMs in this study are categorised into four primary categories based on their functional groups and donor–acceptor moites, such as spiro-OMeTAD, spiro core derivatives, D–A–D based molecules, and dopant free HTMs. As a result, in this review, various types of small organic HTMs, have been highlighted in order to summarise and conclude the relationship between the molecular structure and device performance with the goal of providing some guidance for the further development of PSCs. To progress toward dopant-free HTMs, modelling molecular architecture with improved π–π stacking to increase charge mobility or incorporating dopant chemical structures into HTM would be a viable route. Future progress in the marketing and environmental protection of sustainable green energy options has been enhanced in order to address this challenge.

1.1 Perovskite

Since 2009, researchers have focused their attention on organic–inorganic metal halide PSCs due to their minimal production cost and high PCE.1–7 The Korea Research Institute of Chemical Technology, had recorded a recent PCE of 25% and this closes the PCE gap between PSC and market-dominant crystalline silicon-based photovoltaic technology.8 PSCs have received great user interest for their increased PCE owing to their high photophysical properties. The ideal perovskite crystal structure is ABX3, in which A represents a monovalent organic cation (such as CH3NH3+ (MA+) and HC(NH2)2+ (FA+)), B means a divalent inorganic cation (usually Pb2+ or Sn2+), and X is a monovalent halogen anion (Cl−, Br−, or I−) (Fig. 2A and B).4,5,9–12 One of the most promising choices in the next decade for accommodating increasing energy needs will be to transform solar power into electricity, without affecting the climate change scenario. (1) A thin film and organic hybrid architecture have reduced energy-producing problems such as high energy costs and high energy costs. (2) The second step involves the cost incurred for photovoltaic solar energy elements like solar cells and modules, inverters, frames, land and installation. This process becomes effective and efficient if it is highly stable and has good efficiency. Device performance is increased with the experiments implementing the semiconductor organic–inorganic perovskite absorbers.

Perovskite has proved to be the best photovoltaic candidate due to its (1) easy large-scale manufacturing using a simple spin-coating or spray printing process. (2) Perovskite's energy levels and energy gap are easily controlled by minor modifications in the components of the perovskite materials. (3) The perovskite absorption region covers the maximum range of visible wavelengths, which matches the solar spectrum and high molar extinction coefficient. (4) Perovskite's binding energy is weak and it benefits the exciton dissociation at room temperature without external electric field assistance. (5) Charge separation is achieved due to the perovskite carrier's long diffusion length. (6) In perovskite, both excited electrons and holes transfer spontaneously. Direct band-gap, high absorption coefficient and increased charge carrier mobility, large PCE, the low-cost precursor for the photoactive layer, and cheap fabrication process and minimal loss during charge recombination are the factors that lead to the increased performance.13–15

1.2 Typical PSCs structure

Normally, PSC has a perovskite photoactive layer (PAL), an electron transporting layer (ETL), hole transporting layer (HTL), and a counter electrode. In general, perovskite devices can be classified according to their configuration into the conventional/regular (n–i–p) structure and inverted planar structure (p–i–n). In the conventional structure, the first is the deposition of compact or mesoporous metal oxide (ETL, n-type) and upon that an active perovskite layer exists and the third layer is a p-type HTL layer (p-type). In the case of the planar inverted (p–i–n) structure, there is a HTL, (p-type) deposited and next is the perovskite active layer followed by the ETL, (n-type).16–19 In the case of planar PSC, PAL is sandwiched between ETL and HTL, where HTL performs the role of extraction and transportation of holes from photoactive layers to conductive electrodes. The two types of solar cells are meso-structured and planar hetero-junction solar cells depending on the architecture of the system. Those two are again subdivided into two types depending upon the position of p-type and n-type selective contact materials. The fabrication of planar hetero-junction PSCs is easier and can be used in both conventional (n–i–p) and inverted (p–i–n) structures.20,21

1.3 PSCs working principle

The practical relevance of the solar cell is validated using high efficiency, which is one of the central criteria. The prime effort of PSCs research is to fabricate highly efficient devices. Short-circuit current density (Jsc), open-circuit voltage (Voc) and fill factor (FF) define the solar cell PCE and these parameters are determined by the perovskite film quality, perovskite composition,22 interfacial contact,23 and the architecture of the device.24 As seen in Fig. 3A–C, there are three most common device architectures of PSCs mentioned in the literature. The most important and active layer is the perovskite layer and it is sandwiched between electron and hole transporting layers. PSCs have been closely examined in the last few years and the operating principles of PSCs have been extensively investigated, and they are very similar across diverse architectures. On the irradiation of the perovskite layer using light, excitons (hole–electron pairs) are generated.

Fig. 3D shows the simplified operating mechanism and key charge transfer processes of a typical PSC. The light illuminated from the transparent conductive substrate is absorbed by the perovskite. Electrons and holes are generated and transported by the ETL and HTL respectively. Generated photogeneration of electron–hole pairs has resulted as the perovskite light-harvesting layer is excited by the incident photons from the transparent electrode. Before the migration to the anode, electrons are separated (1) from the holes and injected (2) into the conduction band (CB) of the electron transporting material. Whereas, the holes generated in the perovskite are transferred (3) to the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) level of the HTM, before transporting the hole into the cathode and this generation is effective with the high diffusion lengths of charge carriers. Compared to the perovskite layer, the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the ETL should be lower, and the HOMO of the HTL should be higher, since the reverse values affect the performance by hindering electron/hole extraction. 2. Hole transporting materials

HTL studies mainly focus on the improvement of either stability or efficiency or both. Efficiency is externally associated with the hole mobility of the HTM and with the physical link between the HTL and the perovskite layer. The efficiency and stability of the PSCs mainly depend on HTM. The HTM should have good solubility, high polarity and protic solvents that might dissolve the perovskite layer. HTMs prefer to have high thermal, photochemical stability and hydrophobicity to avoid degradation and increase the long-term stability of perovskites. HTM should be cost-effective with simple synthetic schemes for large-scale fabrication. Various researchers were carried out on HTMs to understand the relativity of HTM and PSC performance. Based on the organic25–29 and inorganic30–32 nature,33,34 HTM is categorized into three types.35 Normally, organic HTM used in PSCs is categorized as: polydispersed polymers and monodispersed small molecules.20,36–40

Despite the inorganic HTMs viz., CuI, CuSCN, and NiO, having good properties such as high hole mobility and lower cost, they also have the limitation of the partial dissolving ability of the perovskite in the solvent used for deposition, which reduces the stability of the device.41 Most of the inorganic HTMs have high internal hole mobility and hence no dopant is needed to increase the stability of the device, but in terms of efficiency, it is proven that organic HTMs are better compared to inorganic HTMs.42,43 The limitations of the polymeric material used in HTM are complexity in purification, minimized solubility, tricky characterization with an undefined molecular weight, and a minimum infiltration into the nanostructured material and these constraints have led the researchers to fabricate the small molecular HTMs. HTM made of organic small molecules has the merit of being built with a flexible structure, a proper chemical structure with an exact molecular weight (MW), and batch-to-batch reproducing ability. As this review focuses on the application of organic small molecules HTM in PSCs, this review is different from the previous reviews.25,35,44

2.1 Desirable characteristics of HTM for PSCs applications

Optimization of the HTM facilitates the hole extraction and its transportation from the perovskite to the electrode. Simple techniques and optimal transferring of the charge from the photons released cancel the perovskite light-harvesting layer by the transparent electrodes and it leads to the photo-generation of electron–hole pairs. The need to develop a low-cost, efficient, and stable HTMs remains a challenge to be addressed. HTM is claimed to be efficient because it aligns the energy of its HOMO with the valence band (VB) of the perovskite photodetector. The HTMs also have high solubility and excellent properties in film formation. Tasks of the HTM include: (i) good extraction of photo-generated holes that improves the transportation to the counter electrode and acts as a powerful barrier for blocking the electron transfer to the anode; (ii) prevention of direct contact of the perovskite and the metal electrode to improve the device stability (typically Au, Ag and Al) and protecting from the diffusion of the electrode into the perovskite layer; (iii) suppression of the charge recombination loss at the interfaces between the perovskite and HTM layer through complete uniform perovskite layer coverage and (iv) impacting the Voc with the dopants in the HTM and minimized energy disorder of the HTM layer (related to the chemical structure of the HTM), and with distinct HTM values for mobility/conductivity.25,27

2.2 The recent advancement of organic small molecule HTMs

In recent years, a wide range of different molecular structures, from simple organic molecules to more complex structures, have been described in the literature. A key component in PSCs, HTMs ensure efficient hole extraction at the perovskite/HTM interface as well as the avoidance of undesired charge recombination processes, resulting in enhanced device performance. Organic semiconducting materials offer several benefits, such as versatility of synthesis, which provides great scope for tuning optoelectronic properties, lowering the production cost, compatibility with several substrates (transparent glass, flexible plastic, paper and clothes), light weight and low-temperature fabrication and large scale processing. Generally, a successful HTM applied to PSCs should exhibit some crucial properties such as (i) being simple to synthesize and purify (i.e., easy to mass-produce), (ii) displaying high hydrophobicity and stability, (iii) possessing suitable energy levels for the LUMO and HOMO (to improve the Voc and PCE), and (iv) providing good conductivity and hole-transfer rates (to improve the FF).25,45–47 The most important role of an effective HTM is to facilitate the hole transfer process from the perovskite layer, by presenting suitable energy levels aligned with the valence band energy edge of the perovskite, and then transport the positive charges to be collected at the anode.37,48–51 Specifically, small molecules exhibit various advantages like easy synthesis and purification processes compared to polymer materials, but this replacement of spiro-OMeTAD with these small molecule HTMs is very rare.

2.3 The current state of representative dopants and dopping mechanisms for organic small molecule HTMs

Researchers looking to develop new HTM candidates are keenly interested in better understanding the relationship between HTM and PSC performance. Recent studies have shown that doping HTLs with beneficial materials improves device stability and passivates trap state. High hole conductivity should be one of the new HTMs' design parameters. Spiro-OMeTAD is currently the most widely used HTM in PSCs, but its high cost and low hole conductivity make large-scale production impractical. The conductivity of spiro-OMeTAD has been improved by a variety of methods, including doping. Undoped spiro-OMeTAD has a hole conductivity of 105 S cm−1.

HTL research focuses on improving either efficiency or stability, or both, as the primary goal. There is a direct correlation between the HTL's hole mobility and the physical contact with the perovskite layer, which determines efficiency. Due to the intrinsically low hole mobility of organic HTMs, various conjugated moieties and structures have been used to overcome this limitation. When it comes to organic charge transport, chemical oxidation is the most common method of doping (specifically for p-type doping).52–54 Chemical dopants are the most frequently employed dopants for solution-based PSC fabrication, as seen in Fig. 4.25,27,55–60 (1) Generate highly stable radical cations quantitatively and reproducibly at a reasonable rate in order to improve charge-transport efficiency (2) give off desirable properties, such as inertness, in the event that byproducts are inevitable (i.e., increased hydrophobicity or thermal stability, reduced phase segregation). (3) The PSC (i.e., adequate energetic alignment of HTL with PAL for hole extraction) is highly efficient and stable, (4) but sustaining minimal prices (i.e. not constrained to inert conditions, a small number of syntheses, and the ability to control the redox properties of the HTL) in order to encourage widespread accessibility to researchers.

To enhance the electrical properties of organic small molecules HTMs (spiro-MeOTAD and other organic HTMs), chemical additives such as 2,3,5,6-tetrafluoro-7,7,8,8-tetracyanoquinodimethane (F4TCNQ), bis(trifluoromethane)sulfonamide lithium salt (LiTFSI), benzoyl peroxide, 4-tert-butylpyridine (TBP), and tris(2-(1H-pyrazol-1-yl)-4-tert-butylpyridine)cobalt(III) bis(trifluoromethylsulphonyl)imide (FK209) are frequently used (Fig. 4).61–77 To name a few of the advantages that doping appears to have, charge transport, recombination suppression, and trap formation inhibition are all possible outcomes. Doping, on the other hand, has gained widespread acceptance as a method for improving the optoelectronic properties of PSCs.74,78

The number of charge carriers in the organic semiconductor can be increased to improve conductivity. In order for a doping process to work, a dopant's electron affinity must be compatible with the energy levels of an organic semiconductor. A growing number of doping materials and techniques have been developed to date in order to modify energy levels, increase conductivity, reduce traps, and improve the film morphology of doped layers. Doped HTLs have made significant progress in recent years in adjusting energy levels, improving charge transport, reducing trap states, and modulating film morphology in planar PSCs. Various dopants, including polymers, fullerene, metal oxides, organic halide salts, inorganic acids, solvents, and nanoparticles, have been reported for use in the engineering of HTL and ETL in p–i–n or n–i–p PSCs thus far.79

It is possible to modify the properties of a host material by using dopants, which are substances that can be injected into the host material. Increased conductivity, altered alignment of energy levels, and improved morphology of thin-film insulators are thought to be accomplished most effectively through the use of dopants. Extrinsic dopants have been studied in this context, and researchers have attempted to limit the flaws and device instability caused by these extrinsic dopants, while still attaining potential performance increases in spiro-OMeTAD-based PSCs. The investigation of novel doping substances is still in its early stages. More in-depth discussion is provided on doping mechanisms and their relevance to PSC performance and stability. 2.4 A recent scientific discovery and advancement of dopped organic small molecule HTMs

Flexible molecular structure and efficient photo-electrical properties make the organic small molecular HTMs as a best suited candidate in a PSCs with high performance and spiro-OMeTAD are one such organic small molecular HTM.81,82 At the same time, the disadvantages are high cost, complicated multistep synthesis, expensive precursors, tedious purification process and poor hole mobility and these are solved with various other HTMs similar to spiro-OMeTAD. Also, the HOMO energy level of spiro-OMeTAD (−5.13 eV) does not fit with the valence band (VB) of perovskite (−5.43 eV) because of its huge energy offset.56 Selection of alternative conjugated skeletons promotes strong intermolecular π–π stacking and it acts the best alternate to spiro-OMeTAD. HTM with no spiro-linkage are meant to be the best alternate to spirobifluorene design and such HTMs are used in PSCs like thiophene derivatives, 3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene pyrene, acene,83,84 benzodithiophene,85 carbazole,86,87 dithienosilole,88–92 indacenodithienothiophene, phenoxazine,93 spirofluorene,94,95 truxene,96–98 triphenylamine,99 and phthalocyanine.100–103 Cost of HTM can be decreased by simplifying the spiro-OMeTAD's molecular structure.48,104 Variation in electron-withdrawing cores or end capping electron donating groups can tune the optical and electronic properties of small-molecule HTMs with D–A–D structure.105 HTM that includes phenoxazine,106 and S,N-heteropentacene107 as cores with absorption in the visible and near infrared region has shown more than 15% of PCE. An electron-withdrawing group might tend to minimize HOMO energy level for achieving a higher Voc in a PSCs. Very few studies found on the usage of D–A–D type HTM in PSC devices,24,50,108–110 leads to the decision to explore the hypothesis through synthesis and evaluation of two novel HTMs to develop efficient and stable PSCs. With only few reports on D–A–D type HTMs, only benzothiadiazole (BTD) unit was applied highly as an electron deficient core.111,112 Similarly, it shows facile synthesis procedure, cheap productions, and flexible processing. In solid thin films tough intermolecular interactions produce high crystallization and this gives high charge carrier mobility and hence D–A conjugated small molecules are best suited for HTLs (Fig. 5).38,88,113–116

Recent studies have shown that the most effective PEA2PBI4 perovskite passivation additive in a three dimensional (3D) perovskite is the bio halide salt phenethylammonium iodide (PEAI).2,117–119 To fabricate excellent PSCs, Cui and his team designed and proved the reduction in carrier recombination loss promoted by the built-in electric field in p-type perovskite/n-type perovskite homo-junctions.120 An outstanding PCE of 16.1% was achieved through a PSCs that uses the PC-casted additive-free spiro-OMeTAD layer that was exposed to simulated AM 1.5 G sunlight with a 1.00 cm2 opening. 2.4.1 The spiro-OMeTAD molecule: background and synthesis. Spiro-OMeTAD is the prime and extensively used HTM in the PSCs area. In the original form, spiro-OMeTAD has low hole mobility, and it is due to the presence of its propeller-like structure from the triphenylamine (TPA) moiety and attributed to the sp3-hybridization atom linkage.123 High intermolecular distance is achieved through these structural properties and the resultant dopants are indispensable. “Spiro concept” (Fig. 6A) includes two extended p-systems that are linked to a tetrahedral sp3-hybridized atom, which results in an orthogonal configuration between the aromatic rings. Morphological stability is enhanced with this spiro linkage, but the electronic properties of the electroactive moieties remain the same. The excellent properties of 9,9′-spirobifluorene make it extensively used in photovoltaic applications (Fig. 6B).25,56,124,125 The advantages of this kind of molecular structure are: (i) a highly sterically needed structure that stimulates molecule rigidity, (ii) high glass transition temperature (Tg), (iii) crystallization is effectively minimized below the glass transition temperature (Tg) and amorphous state stability is improved, and (iv) weak intermolecular π–π interactions that increase the solubility. Spiro-OMeTAD was the first spiro-based HTM reported for PSCs and it was developed with a spiro-bifluorene core. The efficiency of this HTM inspired the researchers to develop various such HTMs with spiro architecture. The spiro strategy is successful for designing efficient HTMs in the solar cell because of its symmetric globular structure, high molecular weight, and minimum cohesion between the molecules. These features result in increased stability in the amorphous state.126

Chemical doping refers to one of the significant techniques used in organic electronics to enhance the device efficiency through energy level controls, increased conductivity, reduced ohmic loss in the transport layer and injection barrier observed at the electrode interface.127,128 Fundamental concepts of p-doping in organic semiconductors is the generation of additional mobile charge carriers by electron acceptors which removes the electrons from the HOMO to generate holes in the intrinsic HTM.129 Additional charge carriers lead to the improvement of charge carrier density in the organic semiconductor and result in increased conductivity. An efficient doping process needs the best suited electron affinity of the dopant based on the energy levels of an organic semiconductor. The typical HTL of PSCs is highly doped with p-type dopant to provide the required electrical conductivity for the state-of-the-art spiro-OMeTAD and other wide bandgap HTMs. Various p-type dopants were fabricated and realized including 2,3,5,6-tetrafluoro-7,7,8,8-tetracyanoquinodimethane (F4TCNQ),130–132 copper(II)66 and cobalt(III) complexes.133,134 At present, the combination of second-generation cobalt complex-based FK20980 and Li-TFSI and an additive of 4-tert-butylpyridine (tBP) as the morphology controller is often used to improve the HTM conductivity. Currently, including ZnTFSI2 as a dopant exhibits improved stability and hole mobility.56

In spite of the advantages, using dopants is critical since it causes various issues with stability because of its complex oxidation process and poor ion migration. HTL becomes highly hydrophilic with Li-TFSI, due to its hygroscopic nature.135–141 It tends to cause chemical degradation and has an adverse effect on the device stability. Researchers had shown the reaction of tBP with perovskite and dissolve it. This induces corrosion and might stimulate a chemical reaction with HTM.142–145 High research interest was focused to identifying the best HTM that could be used in scalable manufacturing with less expense and also to give a complete knowledge of the link between HTM structure and performance of PSCs. This success has grabbed the attention of various researchers who have focused on spiro-type compounds and enhancing the PCE with minor modifications to the structure.56 Nevertheless, the hygroscopic Li salt and the corrosive pyridine derivative may cause the corrosion of the perovskite layer, resulting in a negative effect on the device stability. Therefore, it is very important to explore additional doping materials to improve the devices performance.

2.4.2 Spiro core-HTM derivatives. Spiro-OMeTAD is the commercially available HTM. Its development is complex, purification is expensive and hole conductivity is very low and these factors have a high impact on cheap and large scale photovoltaic applications.146–148 Various researchers were proposed as an alternative to spiro-OMeTAD, but it has not been satisfactory. More understanding is needed about the efficiency of spiro-OMeTAD as an HTM. Large-scale production of traditional spiro-OMeTAD is limited due to its tough purification process and low yield synthesis. The hygroscopic nature of spiro-OMeTAD attracts moisture and it affects the long-term stability of the device. It is observed that additional dopant or additive improves the electrical properties of the low charge carrier mobility (∼10−5 cm2 V−1 s−1 in pristine film) of spiro-OMeTAD.146,149 HTM could be oxidized by Li-TFSI under light or heat excitations, but the process of oxidation in the open air affects the device reproducibility as the oxidation degree is uncontrolled and it deteriorates the device stability.52,150–152 Device performance and stability are degraded by the pinhole channels created by the migration of Li-TFSI.81 Inclusion of dopants and oxidation increases the complexity of processing and also the cost.55,153 For increasing the PSCs various thermally stable new small molecule dopant-free HTMs were seen.91,122,154,155

Efficient HTM is achieved through the improvement of the design diversity of spiro-fused rings. 3. Summary and outlook

In the last decade, organic–inorganic halide PSCs has been significant interest as a possible solar clean energy source with a PCE exceeding 25%, which is significantly higher than existing technologies. This study focuses on the major groups of small organic molecules based on published papers by scientists and academics on the synthesis and development of new types of HTMs based on small organic compounds in PSCs. In summary, organic small-molecule HTMs have been the most frequently created category in PSCs due to their synthetic variety, adjustable characteristics, high purity, and ease of solution processing.

According to the discussion above, we categorized and carefully evaluated organic HTMs according to their functional groups, architectures, dopant composition, and component categories in this review paper. Consequently, triphenylamines were used to create the majority of the novel HTMs. For a perovskite/HTM heterojunction, it's essential to choose the correct HTM first. Furthermore, chemists have developed several modified wide ranges of synthetic techniques were used, with a particular focus on the chemical structure–property relationship; stability and system efficiency were also discussed. As a result, extending the conjugation system, which relies on donor–acceptor moiety, was used to develop the bulk of new HTMs. Chemical doping is an important way to control the energy levels of optoelectronic materials, and tune the optical properties of HTMs. Recently, it has been revealed that spiro-linked organic small molecules can be used to improve charge carrier mobility and device performance by overcoming the spiro-OMeTADs such as a complicated synthesis process, high cost, and the requirement for hygroscopic dopants. However, for most of the dopants, only a few current and well-studied materials are permitted. There is considerable enhancement in the overall power output and reliability of PSCs, but fast deterioration and a very short operating lifetime are commonly mentioned problems for high-efficiency doped HTMs. The next step for researchers should be to investigate novel materials as dopants. We believe that doping techniques are critical for further improving the system efficiency of PSCs compared to industry, given their progress in other photovoltaic technologies.

A novel fused planar core structure and extended π-conjugated HTMs exhibit equal or better performance and enhance the HTMs to have comparably high conductivity and hole mobility without external dopants. A wide range of chemical structures with excellent mobility, conductivity, and thermal stability may be produced in the context of DF-OHTMs. The DF-OHTMs have made a considerable contribution to device stability, but they've also become popular because of improvements in perovskite device power conversion efficiency that have come about as a result of using these materials. Based on the analysis above, the use of dopant-free and strongly hydrophobic hole transporting materials as a protective layer may be one successful technique for strengthening PSCs. Tiny variations in the chemical structure, such as similar atoms and electronic properties, as well as changes in modification functions or the length of the p-conjugated systems, will result in major differences in the behaviours of hole transporting efficiencies. While this approach offers an enhanced durability, it drastically affects the reliability, pinholes in the film morphology, humidity, and thermally induced volatility, as well as the perovskite–HTM interface and hazardous chemical interactions. Apart from their significant importance to system reliability, DF-OHTMs have proven extremely desirable due to their increase in the power conversion efficiency of perovskite systems. The performance of the unit using a DF-OHTM, on the other hand, still seems to be poor, but that can be compared with the spiro-OMeTAD device.

Despite the fact that many articles on HTMs have been released, there is still a need to evaluate and assess improvement in organic HTMs. Molecular architecture and synthesis are aided by the association found between chemical structures and their physicochemical properties. Another approach is to look at a wide range of chemical structures as p-type compounds, which can lead to new paths to improving the PCEs. The impact of HTM's optical bandgap (Eg) is constantly a subject of discussion. Our analysis demonstrates that a critical field of review is the sustained growth and production of low-cost, reliable HTMs as a replacement for commonly publicized established traditional materials such as doped spiro-OMeTAD and PTAA, which are both expensive and unstable. It is highly challenging to create innovative HTMs that contain all desirable characteristics, such as (i) low cost and ease of processing, (ii) suitable HOMO level alignment with the perovskite's valence band, (iii) glass transition temperature greater than 100 °C, (iv) outstanding thermal and photochemical stability, (v) dopant-free, (vi) perovskite surface trap passivation, and (vii) do not include ink. Another important problem in moving this technology towards commercialization is the toxicity of lead and the solvents used to dissolve the perovskite precursors and HTM materials during the manufacture of PSCs. When wide-area manufacturing procedures are taken into consideration, the situation becomes even more severe due to the high quantities of solutions and solvent vapours. A great deal of effort is being put into finding ways to make perovskites that are free of lead. New HTMs have a promising future in light of current molecular engineering trends and advancements, as well. It is envisaged that new functionalized materials would increase the performance and stability of PSCs by simultaneously encapsulating (for humidity and UV radiation) and trapping passivation.

Given the strength of synthetic organic chemistry, there is no doubt that the development of novel and more efficient molecules will be limited only by the scientists' creativity. And we hope that this review will serve as a catalyst for the establishment of such partnerships and the continued growth of high-performance PSCs. PSCs are expected to continue to improve as the design and synthesis of HTMs for them improves. Nonetheless, there is no question that organic chemicals will continue to play a significant role in the PSC architecture. We hope that this summary of recent HTM advances arranged by the chemical family will act as a catalyst for the chemists working on this exciting field of organic molecules for the next generation.
