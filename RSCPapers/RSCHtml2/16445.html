Tin oxide based nanostructured materials: synthesis and potential applications

Abstract

In view of their inimitable characteristics and properties, SnO2 nanomaterials and nanocomposites have been used not only in the field of diverse advanced catalytic technologies and sensors but also in the field of energy storage such as lithium-ion batteries and supercapacitors, and in the field of energy production such as solar cells and water splitting. This review discusses the various synthesis techniques such as traditional methods, including processes like thermal decomposition, chemical vapor deposition, electrospinning, sol–gel, hydrothermal, solvothermal, and template-mediated methods and green methods, which include synthesis through plant-mediated, microbe-mediated, and biomolecule-mediated processes. Moreover, the advantages and limitations of these synthesis procedures and how to overcome them that would lead to future research are also discussed. This literature also focuses on various applications such as environmental remediation, energy production, energy storage, and removal of biological contaminants. Therefore, the rise and journey of SnO2-based nanocomposites will motivate the modern generation of chemists to modify and design robust nanoparticles and nanocomposites that can effectively tackle significant environmental challenges. This overview concludes by providing future perspectives on research into tin oxide in synthesis and its various applications.

1. Introduction

Toxic contaminants such as organic pollutants, including dyes, pharmaceutical compounds, organic compounds, pesticides, and inorganic pollutants including heavy metals and salt ions, are released into water bodies by various industries, agricultural sectors, and even households.1–5 These pollutants are carcinogenic and neurogenic and thus profoundly impact the lives of living organisms and threaten our environment. Also, these contaminants accumulate in aquatic organisms and reach the human body via the food chain.6–9 Over the past two decades, many methods have been used, such as coagulation, membrane processing, adsorption, ion exchange, and photocatalysis to remove these toxic contaminants.10–12 Photocatalysis using nanocatalysts is the most promising method to decrease the pollutants in the environment. Researchers have achieved great heights in this field but are yet to overcome the obstacle of large-scale applications.13

A significant amount of work is underway to synthesize, characterize, and modify nanocatalysts, as they have shown applications in pharmaceuticals, textiles, cosmetics, and even energy science and engineering.14–17 Nanoscience deals with nanomaterials having a size range of about 1–100 nanometers. Nanomaterials show improved electrical, optical, magnetic, and catalytic properties compared with their bulk material due to their high surface area to volume ratio and significantly smaller size.18–20 Moreover, nanoparticles are more reactive as the surface contains 90% of the atoms, whereas in the case of bulk particles, the atom concentration on the surface decreases to 1%. Due to these exceptional characteristics, nanoparticles are profoundly used in critical sectors of modern science and engineering, such as in industry, biosensors, nano-medicines and pharmaceuticals, photocatalysis, and cleaning agents.21 Among the nanoparticles, SnO2 is relatively popular among researchers because of its wide variety of applications, as shown in Fig. 1. SnO2, commonly called tin oxide, forms a strong interaction between the adsorbed contaminant chemically and physically, thus increasing its stability. As the SnO2 nanoparticles have a high bandgap (∼3.7 eV) and thermal stability (∼873 K), they are primarily used in glass coatings, catalysis, sensors, cognitive remediation, and lithium-ion batteries.22–25 SnO2 is also ideal for optoelectronic devices,26 field emission displays,27 light-emitting diodes,28 dye-based solar cells29 and anti-static coatings.30 As a transition metal nanoparticle, SnO2 is biocompatible, less toxic, and eco-friendly in terms of synthesis and usage.31

The synthesis of nanoparticles by traditional methods is conducted with various precautions due to the involvement of toxic reagents. These traditional methods involve synthesizing nanoparticles, especially SnO2, by various physical and chemical methods such as hydrothermal, electrochemical, electrospinning, microwave irradiation, photochemical, and solid vapor deposition methods.32–36 As these methods were not environment-friendly and cost-effective, researchers switched to green synthesis.37 The green synthesis methods were pure and non-toxic and were eco-friendly and cost-effective.38,39 This piece of literature focuses on the structure, properties, and synthesis of SnO2 nanoparticles and nanocomposites. Also, it discusses various applications, such as environmental remediation by photocatalytic activity and sensors, energy production by solar cells and water splitting, energy storage by lithium-ion batteries and supercapacitors, and biological contaminant removal because of the antimicrobial activity of SnO2 nanoparticles and SnO2-based nanocomposites.

1.1. Scope of the review

Many articles that specifically deal with the green method of synthesizing tin oxide nanoparticles and their applications were recently published. To date, and to the best of our knowledge, no comprehensive review has focused on the various synthesis procedures of SnO2 nanoparticles and their potential applications in the field of environment and energy. This review provides a broad overview of the advantages and limitations of traditional and green synthesis methods for SnO2 nanoparticles and their nanocomposites. Previously, few pieces of literature have been published that have showcased some specific applications of SnO2 nanoparticles like photocatalytic degradation, sensors, or lithium-ion batteries. However, a single review article is yet to be published covering nearly all the applications of tin oxide nanoparticles and their nanocomposites, including photocatalytic activity, solar cells, fuel cells, supercapacitors, water-splitting, and antimicrobial activity. In addition, we also discuss the role and applications of tin oxide nanocomposites. Finally, we offer a new perspective on designing and synthesizing nano tin-oxide-based structures in diverse applications. We anticipate that this review will inspire a broad community of scientists involved in the design and development of SnO2-based nanoparticles and their nanocomposites.

Since the discovery of SnO2 nanoparticles, incredible research progress has been achieved not only in the field of green synthesis methods, where they act as antimicrobial agents or show cytotoxic activity, but also in various applications, including environmental remediation energy production, and storage. Over the last decade, there has been a significant increase in the number of publications in the field of energy and the environment reporting the application of SnO2-based nanoparticles and nanocomposites as critical materials. 2. Synthesis of tin oxide and tin-oxide-based nanocomposites

2.1. Traditional methods

2.1.1. Thermal deposition. This synthesis method is typically performed in a tube furnace, where the source materials, usually used in condensed and powdered form, are vaporized at a very high temperature. Then this vaporized material is condensed to form the desired nanostructure by controlling conditions like substrate amount, temperature, and pressure.40–44 Researchers are working on various synthesis routes to prepare nanomaterials and nanocomposites for specific applications. For nanocomposite synthesis, factors such as the kinetic energy of the added dopant atom, source to substrate ratio, and metal coating thickness come into play.45,46Fig. 347 shows the horizontal furnace along with its various heating zones. Thermal deposition, when paired with evaporation–condensation, vapor–liquid–solid, and vapor–solid processes, produces exciting results. Luo et al.48 synthesized tin oxide nanowires and nanobelts by thermal oxidation aided by the vapor–liquid–solid process to study photoluminescence. The prepared nanowires were obtained very precisely due to the fine-tuning of the temperature in the tube furnace. To achieve the desired morphology, Zhang et al.49 used the thermal deposition method to synthesize graphite-based TiO2/SnO2. Here graphite was used along with the source material, and TiO2 was added as the substrate to obtain the desired ultrawide nanowires and nanobelts. 2.1.2. Chemical vapor deposition (CVD). In this CVD method, the substrate interacts with one or more precursors. With the help of carrier gases, the vaporized precursors are introduced to the substrate. The desired nanocomposite and nanostructures are obtained when the atoms released from the precursor bind powerfully to the substrates, leading to nucleation and growth of the product.50,51 As the vaporized precursors achieve the high temperature in the furnace, supersaturation is reached and they precipitate on the surface when cooled, resulting in nucleation and growth of the nanostructure.52,53 Wang et al.54 used the thermal chemical vapor deposition technique to synthesize plate- and chrysanthemum-shaped SnO2 on single silicon substrates. In this process, a carbothermal evaporation of tin oxide and activated carbon took place with the help of a Au–Ag alloy. Cube-like SnO2 was also synthesized using the chemical vapor deposition technique without the aid of any catalyst.55 Ma et al.56 produced molybdenum-doped SnO2 to test its conductivity using chemical vapor deposition. The synthesized tin oxide nanoparticles were tetragonal in structure, and showed lower resistance and higher transmittance. The SEM images of SnO2 and Mo-doped SnO2 are shown in Fig. 5.56 The researchers found that the SnO2 nanostructures were spherical and agglomerated to form homogeneous sizes before doping. The CVD method has been proved to be very important in nanotechnology as it provides a dense coating and porous surface, varying the morphology.57

2.1.3. Electrospinning. Ultrathin nanofibers are synthesized by electrospinning through an electrically charged jet of a polymer solution. The electrospinning setup consists of a syringe holding the polymer solution, two electrodes, and a DC voltage supply in the kilovolt range, as shown in Fig. 6.58 Due to the high voltage, the dripping polymer from the syringe is added to the fiber. The polymer becomes looped by the fiber due to the bending of the fiber by the charge, and the diameter decreases. This process goes on until ultrathin nanofibers are obtained and collected.59
Fig. 6 Schematic diagram of the electrospinning setup. Reproduced from ref. 58 with permission of Elsevier, copyright 2010.

Apart from nanofibers, the electrospinning method results in the formation of nanotubes and nanoflowers like 1D SnO2 structures.60,61 As the nanostructures produced by this method are highly porous, they have potential applications in photocatalysis, lithium-ion batteries, and sensors. Jiang et al.62 synthesized Eu2O3/Au/SnO2 nanofibers via the electrospinning method to detect the presence of acetone in an aqueous solution. Due to the synergistic effect of the ternary nanofibers, the nanocomposite showed a very high efficiency in sensing acetone. Researchers also synthesized porous rutile SnO2 nanotubes by using a precursor solution of poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO), chloroform (CHCl3), and dimethyldineodecanoate Sn (C22H44O4Sn).63 Wang et al.64 developed SnO2 hollow microtubes for efficient photocatalytic activity, which showed speedy degradation, high stability, and reusability. Fig. 764 shows the XRD spectra of SnO2 microtubes before and after photocatalytic activity. It was found that both the intensity and position of the peaks were similar and were not affected by the photocatalytic activity, concluding that the synthesized microtube was stable in UV light irradiation.

2.1.4. Spray pyrolysis. In spray pyrolysis, the characteristics of significant nanoparticles, such as the properties, thickness, and surface morphology, are often controlled by the deposition temperature and the preliminary droplet size.65 This technique is used mainly due to its low operational cost, extensive surface area coverage, and ease of mass production.66–68 Spray pyrolysis is mainly used to add a metal oxide layer, including SnO2, on various nanoparticles and nanocomposites.69 Itoh et al.70 synthesized an In/SnO2 nanocomposite by adding SnO2 onto indium nanoparticles by salt-assisted spray pyrolysis (SASP) and low-pressure spray pyrolysis (LPSP). The prepared nanocomposites were crystalline and dense, and required minimal heating time. SnO2 heterostructures were synthesized using SnCl4·5H2O as a precursor, with the help of chemical spray pyrolysis.71 A polycrystalline sample was obtained with a bandgap of 3.54 eV and was used for optical applications.

Furthermore, Paloly and Bushiri72 synthesized nanostructured SnO2 thin films using the chemical spray pyrolysis technique. The researcher duo studied the changes in the properties of the produced heterostructures by using six solvents. Similarly, another group of researchers synthesized SnSe–SnO2 nanocomposites by adding a thin layer of SnO2 on SnSe nanospheres by spray pyrolysis.73 Kumar et al.74 synthesized Nd/SnO2 nanofilms using the spray pyrolysis technique to study the optical properties and optoelectronic applications. The produced nanocomposite showed high crystallinity and a good surface morphology. Liu et al.75 synthesized an Sb/SnO2 nanocomposite using the spray pyrolysis technique and investigated the photovoltaic activity. This research group used the nanocomposite for organic solar cells and concluded that this synthesis technique solved the existing interfacial problems.

2.1.5. Vapor–liquid–solid growth. In 1964, Wagner and Ellis76 first introduced the metal catalyst-supported vapor–liquid–solid technique for the growth of silicon whiskers. The metal catalyst is transformed into liquid alloy droplets at high temperatures and absorbs the vapor component. Then this liquid alloy droplet helps precipitate the vapor components on the liquid–solid interface. Thus, the research duo concluded that a vapor-phase precursor is needed to obtain a solid 1D nanomaterial, where the vapor phase supersaturates a liquid catalyst. This technique is widely used, highly efficient, and accepted for producing 1D nanomaterials, where the produced nanowires are grown on the metal-coated surface.49,77–79 Budak et al.80 mentioned that one-dimensional crystalline SnO2 nanowires had been produced by the carbothermal reduction of tin oxide nanopowder followed by thermal evaporation of the reduced vapor precursor and growth via the vapor–liquid–solid (VLS) growth mechanism. Similarly, Choi81 synthesized metal oxide nanowires for optoelectronics using a vapor–liquid–solid (VLS) growth mechanism. In addition, Johari et al.42 also showed the synthesis of SnO2 nanowires via a vapor–liquid–solid (VLS) growth mechanism where the metal oxide is grown on Au/Si nanospheres to form a SnO2/Au/Si nanocomposite.
2.1.6. Sol–gel processing. This method is one of the prevalent traditional methods among researchers to synthesize nanoparticles and nanocomposites. Sol–gel processing also helps in varying the morphology of binary and ternary nanocomposites.82 The method is preferred for producing oxide materials and is easy to use as it works even at room temperature. The nanostructured materials are converted first to a colloidal suspension called a sol and then to an irreversible gel by the hydrolysis and polymerization of compatible precursors. The reagents are added to the solvents rapidly to achieve discrete nucleation and proper growth.83 Periathai et al.84 synthesized nanosized rutile tetragonal SnO2 by sol–gel processing, maintaining a constant pH of 8, and following a soft chemical synthesis route.85 SnO2/SiO2 was synthesized by Liu et al.86 to oxidize the sulfur-containing contaminants from water and wastewater. The authors followed a sol–gel methodology using SnCl2-based DESs as the loading component.

Similarly, using the sol–gel spin coating technique, Sivakumar et al.87 synthesized Ti-doped SnO2 to investigate its electrical and optical properties. The AFM micrographs obtained were used to study the surface morphology, shown in Fig. 8.87 The study revealed that the microstructures had grown homogeneously, and the doped Ti atoms were attached to the surface of the SnO2 without diffusing into it. The doping % of Ti gradually increased, leading to a decrease in the size of the desired Ti-doped-SnO2 nanostructures.

2.1.7. Hydrothermal and solvothermal process. This conventional methodology is primarily carried out in a sealed vessel, where the solvents are used at temperatures higher than the boiling point. In this process, a Teflon-lined steel chamber is used to control the temperature but not the pressure. So, the pressure developed elevates the boiling point, and the lining withstands a temperature of 623 K. Due to these characteristics, the hydrothermal and solvothermal method is widely accepted among researchers to grow nanomaterials.88,89 Hydrothermal terminology is adopted when water is used as the solvent for the reaction, while solvothermal terminology is applied when any solvent, including organic solvents, is used. Single crystalline microcubes of SnO2 were synthesized by Lupan et al.90 by the hydrothermal method. Fig. 990 shows that the TEM images confirmed that the SnO2 cube had a height and width of 0.33 nm. These data matched accurately with the distance between the 001 and 110 planes of rutile tin oxide. Bai et al.91 synthesized a p-Sb2O3/n-SnO2 nanocomposite by the solvothermal method to sense hydrogen gas. Also, sensors such as a SnO/SnO2 nanocomposite were also synthesized by the solvothermal method to sense organic compounds like triethylamine.92

2.1.8. Template-based synthesis. This methodology is used to synthesize metal oxide nanorods and nanotubes.93 The template acts as a base to grow the desired nanomaterials, and after the product is achieved, the template is washed away by thermal decomposition or by chemical dissolution. Water-soluble templates are usually washed with distilled water, whereas other templates are washed with concentrated basic solution, yielding 1D nanomaterials.94 With the help of dimethylsulfoxide micelles as a template, Wang et al.95 synthesized SnO2 nanotubes. To further study the quality of the crystal and vibration properties, the researchers conducted Raman scattering, as shown in Fig. 10.95 Apart from DMS, some popular templates used by researchers are anodic alumina oxide,96,97 manganese dioxide,98 zinc oxide,99 carbon nanotubes,100 natural cellulose fibers,101 and polycarbonate membrane.102 Dai et al.103 synthesized carbon-coated SnO2 by using a self-sacrificing template for a lithium-ion battery anode. The tin oxide produced was hollow; hence it was very efficient for the lithium-ion anode.

2.1.9. Electrodeposition. This technique is mainly used to synthesize 1-D nanorods and nanotubes in a very cost-effective manner.104 The mechanism is based upon the movement of ions due to the applied electric voltage. First, the ions are transported and discharged near the electrode region; this is followed by the disintegration of the ion–ligand bond. After the disintegration, the atoms are incorporated onto the substrate resulting in nucleation and growth of the desired nanostructure. In addition, the growth of the nanostructure on the substrate electrode depends on several factors, such as lattice orientation and adhesion energy.105–107 Kim et al.108 synthesized a Cu/SnO2 nanocomposite for enhanced charge capacity. The researchers concluded that the charge capacity increased to 729 mA h g−1 at a 0.2C rate due to the addition of tin oxide. Another group of researchers also doped tin oxide produced from the electrodeposition method with carbon nanofibers to form a SnO2/CNF nanocomposite by the chemical vapor deposition technique.109 This nanocomposite is used as an anode in lithium-ion batteries with a high-capacity retention of 78% and a sizeable coulombic efficiency of 99.8% at the 100th cycle. 2.2.2. Microbe-mediated synthesis. Most microbes are non-toxic and can be used in the physiochemical synthesis of SnO2 nanoparticles as they are eco-friendly and cost-effective. The microbes include various bacteria, fungi, and yeasts used to produce tin oxide nanoparticles and nanocomposites by intracellular and extracellular mechanisms. Furthermore, this method does not require any toxic chemicals or high energy. The intracellular mechanism involves the uptake of metal ions in the microbial cell, followed by the formation of nanoparticles in the presence of enzymes, coenzymes, and other biomolecules inside the cell. However, the extracellular mechanism is slightly different as the metal ions stick to the microbe's surface. Then, the enzymes and proteins on the surface reduce the metal ions, resulting in stabilized nanoparticles.143 The study concluded that the extracellular synthesis mechanism is very efficient as it produces nanoparticles in bulk and skips some steps to recover these nanoparticles.144 Bacteria like Lactobacillus sporogenes,145,146Stenotrophomonas maltophilia,147Magnetospirillum gryphiswaldense,148Pseudomonas aeruginosa,149Bacillus licheniformis,150 and Salmonella typhimurium151 are used by researchers for the extracellular bacteria-mediated synthesis of nanoparticles. 3. Assessment of various synthesis methods for SnO2 and SnO2-based nanocomposites

Among the traditional methods, it was found that the sol–gel method has been a prevalent and efficient method for the synthesis of nanoparticles.156 However, the use of toxic chemicals and a high energy need lead to a search for alternate synthesis methods.157 Synthesis procedures like thermal decomposition and electrospinning require a high temperature and a lot of energy, which can prove lethal and relatively expensive in the long run. Furthermore, the sol–gel, hydrothermal and solvothermal processes involve chemicals that are harmful to the environment. Also, the high cost of raw materials, volume shrinkage, and cracking of the prepared nanomaterial during drying make it less cost-effective.

It has been concluded from the above discussion that the green synthesis methods have been relatively popular among scientists as they are non-toxic, chemical additive-free, cost-efficient, and easy to follow. The tin oxide nanoparticles synthesized from the green processes were pure, and the processes were easy and eco-friendly, which encouraged researchers to follow the processes. Among the different green synthesis methods, plant-mediated synthesis has been the most followed process, as secondary metabolites such as phenols, flavonoids, tannins, terpenoids, and carbohydrates present in the plant help reduce and stabilize the metal and metal oxide nanoparticles.158 Also, plant-mediated synthesis is cost-effective, easy to conduct, and less hazardous than other green methods. Microbe-mediated synthesis is also an eco-friendly process; however, more research is required in that field to synthesize SnO2 nanoparticles.143 Furthermore, in the case of biomolecule-mediated synthesis, an amino acid acts as the capping agent, thus reducing the use of toxic chemicals. So, from the study, it was concluded that plant-mediated green synthesis is the best for producing SnO2 nanoparticles among all the traditional and other green synthesis methods.

4. Structure and properties of tin oxide nanoparticles

Tin(IV) oxide, also called stannic oxide (SnO2), is an amphoteric colorless solid.159 SnO2 has a molecular mass of 150.69 g mol−1, a 6.90 g mol−1 density, a melting point around 1773 K, and a boiling point around 2073 K. The tin in stannic oxide, also known as cassiterite, has an oxidation state of +4, is chemically and thermally stable, and shows high optical transparency and reflectivity in the IR region.160–162 The properties mentioned above, combined with the oxidizing ability of tin oxide, make the nanoparticles a perfect weapon to remove toxic contaminants from water and wastewater.163

Stannic oxide possesses a rutile structure, having unit cell dimensions of a = b = 0.4737 nm and c = 0.3186 nm, an octahedral crystal, and a space group no. 136 P42mnm.164,165 As shown in Fig. 14,166 one tin ion is surrounded by six oxygen ions while one oxygen ion is surrounded by three tin ions, leading to a 6:3 coordination structure.167 Also, tin oxide has several polymorphs (Fig. 15168), such as the rutile type (P42/mnm), CaCl2 type (Pnnm), α-PbO2 type (Pbcn), pyrite type (Pa3), ZrO2 type orthorhombic phase I (Pbca), cotunnite type orthorhombic phase II (Pnam), and fluorite type (Fm3m).168–172 These polymorphs are attained when the stable pristine rutile structure is subjected to a very high mechanical pressure.

Researchers found that SnO2 showed photocatalytic activities and it was utilized for photocatalytic applications. In addition, tin oxide was cost-effective, thermostable, non-toxic, and highly photosensitive.173–175 The high photocatalytic activity was due to the octahedral structure, which increased the recombination time of the electrons and holes.176,177

In their studies, Zhang et al.178 revealed that tin oxide showed excellent electrical conductivity due to defects in the crystal lattice of the metal oxide. Due to these defects, the bandgap decreases significantly, making SnO2 a perfect material to construct semiconductors. The 3.6 eV bandgap corresponds to the photoactivation in the UV range of the electromagnetic spectrum, which helps in the photodegradation of contaminants, especially organic wastes.179 In order to maintain electrical neutrality, the extra oxygen anions initiate the oxidation of Sn2+ to Sn4+.180,181 Due to constructive properties like low electrical resistance, high conductivity, and high optical transparency,182 SnO2 showed many practical applications, discussed further in this review literature.

Furthermore, to increase efficiency, doping of nanomaterials is conducted. As the introduced dopant alters the electronic arrangement of the nanomaterial, it improves the optical and structural properties. So, in this piece of literature, applications of SnO2 and SnO2-based nanocomposites will be discussed. Also, due to its affinity towards organic toxins and gases like carbon monoxide and hydrogen, the metal oxide can be used as a sensing material.183 In this review, the role of SnO2 as a semiconductor and its photocatalytic applications will also be discussed.

The nanocomposites, produced by electrochemical anodization, were hydrophobic and became superhydrophilic when irradiated with UV light. Similarly, researchers also modified TiO2/SnO2 to inverse opal (IO) films and produced a nanocomposite with a polystyrene support.264 To reduce the high 3.8 eV band-gap of TiO2, researchers added SnO2–IO films, thus leading to enhanced photoelectrochemical behavior. The photocurrent density (Jsc) was found to be 4.67 mA cm−2 at 0.5 V (vs. NHE), and this improved performance was due to the meager charge-transfer resistance (Rct) in the photoelectrode and the semiconductor junction. Advanced studies revealed that the performance of the nanocomposite improved by 20% when it was doped with 10% fluorine atoms, forming a F–TiO2/SnO2 semiconductor.

Chen et al.265 synthesized a 3D and 1D nanodendrite array of BiVO4/SnO2 for photoelectrochemical water splitting. The presence of tin oxide led to enhanced charge separation efficiency and photocurrent density. The 3D array showed a charge separation efficiency of 88%, whereas the 1D array showed a charge separation efficiency of 55% when the PEC took place under visible light irradiation. In addition, researchers doped the nanocomposite with gold metal, leading to high light absorption and decreased recombination under visible light irradiation.266 The synthesized Au–BiVO4/SnO2 IO heterostructure showed a photocurrent density of 3.83 mA cm−2 and a charge separation efficiency of 70.8% at 1.23 V vs. RHE. Furthermore, researchers further studied the BiVO4/SnO2-based nanocomposite to make the photoanode commercially available for generating clean and efficient energy.267 Under irradiation with LED light, the semiconductor showed a photocurrent density of 1.1 mA cm−2 in 0.1 M NaOH (sodium hydroxide) electrolyte at 1.23 V vs. RHE (reversible hydrogen electrode).

Fuel cells are static energy conversion equipment that converts the chemical reaction of fuels directly into electrical energy and produces water as its byproduct.268,269 In comparison with fuel cells, conventional heat engines show less efficiency and even add to pollution. Fuel cells work as batteries and engines by sharing similar characteristics to batteries in load conditions and operating like engines.270 The schematic illustration of a fuel cell is shown in Fig. 27.271 The major drawback of fuel cells is the installation cost, and various researchers are working on developing these power conversion devices to reduce the cost.

Dou et al.272 synthesized SnO2 nanorods by the hard template method and doped them with platinum to form a Pt/SnO2 nanocomposite. Furthermore, the researchers replaced the Pt/C anode with the prepared nanocomposite and found that the electrochemical stability improved significantly. The Pt/SnO2 nanocomposite maintained most of its electrochemically active surface area under ten hours of potential held at 1.6 V, while its electrochemically active degradation rate was one order of magnitude lower than that of Pt/C under potential cycling between 0.6 and 1.2 V. 5.3. Energy storage

5.3.1. Lithium-ion batteries. Lithium-ion batteries are known for their high capacity, cyclability, and flexible designs and are considered a more eminent power source for portable electronic devices. Graphite anodes were usually used in lithium-ion batteries, but they showed a pretty low storage capacity of 372 mA h g−1. Thus, to enhance the storage capacity, researchers replaced the graphite anodes with SnO2 nanoparticles.274 However, SnO2-based anodes had certain drawbacks, such as showing poor retention capacity during high cyclability, which was due to significant volume changes and agglomeration of Sn nanoparticles during the charge–discharge process.275 Firstly, various structures such as nanowires, nanosheets, hollow spheres, nanoflowers, etc., of SnO2 were used to overcome the tin accumulation.276,277 Secondly, researchers hybridized SnO2 with carbon-based materials to improve the retention during cycling as they support the mechanical strain produced during volume change, thus minimizing the effect.278,279 Thus, nanocomposites like SnO2/CNT, SnO2/graphene, and SnO2/carbon composites showed higher storage capacity along with higher retention during cycling.280

To improve the reusability and enhance the electrochemical performance of lithium-ion batteries, researchers introduced carbon nanotubes (CNT) as they have very high electron conductivity.281 So, the synthesized SnO2/CNT nanocomposite contains Sn–C bonds, which propel the electron transfer. This property leads to an increase in the reversibility as the Sn reacts with Li2O and forms SnO2. So, the studies concluded that SnO2/CNT nanocomposite reversibility is directly proportional to the number of Sn–C bonds. Researchers also synthesized a SnO2/CNT nanocomposite to increase the capacity of lithium-ion batteries.282 With the help of polyvinylpyrrolidone, the SnO2 nanoparticles were stacked on the CNT sheets uniformly. When used as anodes, the nanocomposites showed an enhanced reversible specific charge capacity of 850 mA h g−1 and improved the recyclability performance. In addition, Sun et al.283 synthesized a Sn–SnO2@CNT nanocomposite for enhanced performance of lithium-ion batteries. Sn–SnO2 resulted in a decrease of volume and surface morphology, so the researchers embedded it onto the surface of carbon nanotubes (CNT). When acting as an anode, the nanocomposite showed a specific charge capacity of 1059 mA h g−1 and a phenomenal cycling ability, having 1000 cycles with 86% retention ability.

Researchers produced a C–SnO2/CNT nanocomposite as an anode in lithium-ion batteries using the hydrothermal process and carbonization.284 It showed an excellent specific capacity of 1572 mA h g−1 and improved cycling ability, having 100 reversible cycles and still maintaining the reversible specific capacity at 1000 mA h g−1 (63.61% retention ability). Such a remarkable electrochemical performance was attributed to the hierarchical sandwiched structure and substantial synergistic effects of the ultrafine tin oxide nanoparticles and the carbon coating. Furthermore, Liu et al.285 synthesized an ultrafine 3D SnO2/CNT nanocomposite by the solvothermal approach for improving the retention ability. The SnO2/CNT nanocomposite anode improved the retention ability of the lithium-ion battery to 76% at 100 reuseable cycles with a maximum specific capacity of 1109 mA h g−1. The specific capacity vs. cycles plot is shown in Fig. 28.285 It also improves the rate performance and stability along with solving the significant volume change issue during recyclability.

Using a redox reaction, Wang et al.286 synthesized a SnO2/graphene composite to improve the lithium-ion battery's rate performance and cyclic stability. When acting as an anode, the nanocomposite showed a reversible charge specific capacity of 840 mA h g−1 at a current density of 67 mA g−1 with a retention ability of up to 86%, over 30 reusable cycles. Researchers further worked on the nanocomposite to improve its capacity.287 The SnO2/graphene nanocomposite synthesized by the gas–liquid interface reaction exhibited a high reversible specific capacity of 1304 mA h g−1 at a current density of 100 mA g−1 and exceptional rate capability; even at a high current density of 1000 mA g−1, the reversible capacity was still as high as 748 mA h g−1. Yao et al.288 synthesized a SnO2/graphene nanocomposite by embedding tin oxide nanoparticles homogeneously on a graphene matrix. When used as an anode, the nanocomposite showed an enhanced specific storage capacity of 765 mA h g−1, credited to the 3D structure. The hydrothermal-process-synthesized F–SnO2@RGO nanocomposite is used as an anode to enhance the storage and stability of lithium-ion batteries.289 The fluorine atom improved the electron transportation and the lithium-ion diffusion in the nanocomposite. The nanocomposite showed a significantly high specific capacity of 1277 mA h g−1 and high retention ability even after 100 cycles. Thus, the uniform spread of fluorine-doped tin oxide particles over reduced graphene oxide sheets enhanced the electrochemical performance. Tang et al.290 prepared a SnO2/graphene nanocomposite by a hydrothermal and calcination process to enhance the electrochemical performance of lithium-ion batteries. The researchers found that the synthesized composite showed a reversible capacity of 675.8 mA h g−1 after 100 cycles at a current density of 500 mA g−1. It even showed a retention ability of 96.37% after 100 cycles. In addition to that, ultrathin SnO2/graphene was used as an anode to improve the capacity of lithium-ion batteries.291 The nanocomposite showed a maximum capacity of 940 mA h g−1 and high stability even after long-term use. When used as an anode, this nanocomposite is ultrastable as it is used in flexible lithium-ion batteries for portable and wearable electronic devices.

Besides CNT and graphene, many carbon-based composites, nanomaterials, and nanocomposites are also used as the anode in lithium-ion batteries. Researchers synthesized a hollow nanostructured SnO2/C composite to enhance the electrochemical performance of tin oxide-based anodes in lithium-ion batteries.292 The synthesized nanocomposite showed a high specific capacity of 788 mA h g−1 and was recycled up to 900 times with a retention ability of 69.8%. The nanocomposite showed superior performance compared with pure tin oxide as it avoided the agglomeration of SnO2 in the voids. Similarly, to avoid this situation, Versaci et al.293 synthesized SnO2 nanoparticles with a C45 support. The SnO2/C45 nanocomposite, when used as an anode, showed excellent cycling performance with a 100% retention ability after 600 cycles. It showed a maximum specific capacity of 1400 mA h g−1 and high electrode stability, thus improving the electrochemical performance.

Furthermore, researchers also synthesized a Sn/SnO2@NC nanocomposite by the hydrothermal method to use it as an anode to improve the lithium-ion battery's cycling and rate performance.294 The heightened electrochemical performance can be somewhat credited to the heterostructured microcube that can accelerate the transfer rate of lithium ions by decreasing the transmission paths and this is partly credited to the nitrogen-doped carbon coating that can overcome the volume effect and contribute to fractional lithium storage capacity. It showed a specific capacity of 1058 mA h g−1 and was recycled up to 100 times with a retention ability of 46%. Using the co-precipitation method and CaSn(OH)6 nanocubes as a sacrificial template, researchers synthesized hollow SnO2@C nanoboxes to be used as an anode in lithium-ion batteries.103 The synthesized nanocomposite showed a specific capacity of 806.5 mA h g−1 and was recyclable up to 100 times with a retention ability of 97.5%. This improved performance and cycling stability were due to the nanocomposite structure, which provided a buffer space for volume expansion, a large electrolyte contact area, and a short ion transfer path.

5.3.2. Supercapacitors. Supercapacitors, also called ultracapacitors, are electrochemical energy storage devices that combine the high energy storage capability of conventional batteries with the high power delivery capability of conventional capacitors.295–297 These are mainly used in all industries ranging from hybrid electric vehicles, such as Tesla, Ather, Ford, etc., to small pieces of equipment due to their higher capacity and long recyclability.298 The supercapacitors changed the dynamic of the automobile industry as they reduced the harmful emissions and the dependency on non-renewable sources such as natural gas, petrol, and diesel.299 Metal oxide such as SnO2-based supercapacitors showed very high capacity and stability. The SnO2-based nanocomposite was compatible, eco-friendly, and cost-effective when used as a supercapacitor.300,301

Rani et al.302 used the impregnation method to synthesize SnO2@C composite electrodes for supercapacitors. The synthesized nanocomposite showed a specific capacitance of 432 F g−1 at 1 A g−1 current density in 1 M potassium hydroxide electrolyte with a capacitance retention of 95.5% after 2000 cycles. The electrodes also led to an energy density of 29.4 W h kg−1 and power density of 418 W kg−1 when a 1 A g−1 current density was applied. The presence of bimodal pores facilitates the continuous flow of electrons, and the optimized design stabilizes the cycles. Furthermore, researchers have also worked on a ZnO/SnO2 composite, which would work as the electrode for supercapacitors to enhance their energy capacitance.303 The studies showed that the nanocomposite has a specific capacitance of 2307 F g−1 at a 20 mV s−1 scan rate in 3 M potassium hydroxide electrolyte with a capacitance retention of 79% after 7000 cycles. When the optimum current was applied, the hybrid supercapacitor showed an energy density of 47.28 W h kg−1 and a power density of 10400 W kg−1. To improve the electrochemical performance of supercapacitors, researchers combined mesoporous carbon hollow spheres with hollow double-shelled tin oxide to form an electrode with a good electrical conductivity and a controllled volume expansion.304 The synthesized SnO2/MesCHS nanocomposite showed an enhanced electrochemical performance, having a specific capacitance of 470 F g−1 at a 20 A g−1 current density with a capacitance retention of 88.4% after 4000 cycles along with a coloumbic efficiency of 86.5%.

In addition to that, researchers, with the help of the co-precipitation method, synthesized ternary Fe–SnO2@CeO2 electrodes for applications in supercapacitors.305 The synthesized nanocomposite showed a specific capacitance of 348 F g−1 at a 1 A g−1 current density with a capacitance retention of 85% after 5000 cycles. At the same current density, the three-electrode system-based supercapacitor showed an energy density of 32.2 W h kg−1 and a power density of 747 W kg−1. Similarly, a ternary rGO–SnO2–polyaniline nanocomposite was used as an electrode to enhance the performance of the supercapacitor.306 The electrodes were assembled in 1 M sulphuric acid and showed a specific capacitance of 524.2 F g−1 at a scan rate of 5 mV s−1. Wang et al.,307 using the hydrothermal method, synthesized a Co3O4–SnO@SnO2 nanocomposite to enhance the supercapacitors’ electrochemical performance, as shown in Fig. 30.307 The produced nanocomposite showed a specific capacitance of 1.056 F cm−2 at 1 mA cm−2 current density with a capacitance retention of 91.5% after 2000 cycles. The improved performance was credited to the high electrical conductivity and low internal resistance.

5.4. Removal of biological contaminants

Researchers utilized tin oxide nanoparticles and their nanocomposites to remove microorganisms, such as bacteria, viruses, and fungi. The tin oxide synthesized for the mentioned cause was produced by the green synthesis method or traditional synthesis method. However, modern researchers prefer the green method as it is eco-friendly and less toxic.308,309

As reported, using Daphne mucronata (D. mucronata) leaf extract as a capping agent, researchers synthesized SnO2 nanoparticles to study the antimicrobial effect.310 The synthesized nanomaterial had a very high surface area of 147 m2 g−1, and it showed antimicrobial activity for both bacteria and fungi along with the photodegradation of rhodamine 6G dye. The order of activity was Gram-negative bacteria (GNB) > Gram-positive bacteria (GPB) > fungi. Khan et al.311 synthesized a SnO2 nanoparticle aqueous extract of Clerodendrum inerme to study the antimicrobial activity. The studies concluded that the nanoparticles showed both antibacterial and antifungal activity resulting in removal of microorganisms such as Escherichia coli, Bacillus subtilis, Aspergillus flavus, and Candida albicans. The nanoparticles were also doped with copper, and showed hemolysis activity and acted as an antitumor and anticancer agent, as shown in Fig. 31.311 Researchers also developed an eco-friendly, stable, and cost-efficient Ag–SnO2 nanocomposite that showed antimicrobial activity.136 The nanoparticles were produced from extracts of Saccharum officinarum and showed antimicrobial action against Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Escherichia coli, and Bacillus subtilis. Researchers studied the Gram-positive bacterium Staphylococcus aureus and the Gram-negative bacterium Klebsiella pneumoniae using SnO2 nanoparticles synthesized from Stevia rebaudiana leaf extract.312 The study revealed that the nanoparticles showed a greater inhibition effect on Gram-positive bacteria than Gram-negative bacteria. However, it was found that the antimicrobial activity was directly proportional to the concentration of the nanoparticles, i.e., a decrease in the number of nanoparticles decreases the inhibition effect and vice versa.


Fig. 31 Schematic illustration of the synthesis and application of tin oxide nanoparticles made from extracts of Clerodendrum inerme. Reproduced from ref. 311 with permission of Elsevier, copyright 2018.

Using the biogenic wet synthesis method, Kumari et al.313 synthesized quantum-confined SnO2 nanoparticles to study the antimicrobial activity. When added to ethylene glycol, these resulted in an improvement of thermal conductivity and antibacterial activity. Similarly, using the same synthesis procedure, researchers also developed SnO2 nanoparticles that showed an inhibitory effect on the Gram-negative bacterium Escherichia coli.314 The antibacterial mechanism is shown in Fig. 32.314 It was confirmed that when the bacterial body comes in contact with the tin oxide nanoparticles, it undergoes dehydrogenation, which results in cell death with the release of some amount of hydrogen peroxide. Along with the antimicrobial activity, the nanoparticles also acted as antioxidants and degraded pharmaceutical compounds.


Fig. 32 Antibacterial activity by SnO2 nanoparticles. Reproduced from ref. 314 with permission of Elsevier, copyright 2015.

Further studies solidified the theory that the antimicrobial activity was directly proportional to the concentration of nanoparticles.315 Researchers also developed SnO2 nanoparticles using the biogenic wet synthesis method with peptides as the precursor to study the antibacterial activity.316 The presence of peptides ensures biocompatibility, and the nanoparticles showed very high stability and effectiveness during the antimicrobial activity. In another study, Nathan et al.317 synthesized SnO2 nanoparticles from Stevia rebaudiana plant leaf extracts in a cost-effective and environment-friendly process. As per the author's opinion, the antibacterial effect was seen to be maximum on the Gram-positive bacterium Staphylococcus aureus compared with the Gram-negative bacterium Klebsiella pneumoniae. The above result was confirmed by Gowri et al.,318 although they prepared the tin oxide nanoparticles using aloe vera gel extracts and experimented on different Gram-negative bacteria. To test the antibacterial ability, the researchers applied SnO2 nanoparticles to a cotton textile using the pad dry cure method. The studies concluded that the nanoparticles showed a more inhibitory effect on the Gram-positive bacterium Staphylococcus aureus compared with the Gram-negative bacterium Escherichia coli.

Erkan et al.319 synthesized a Pd/TiO2/SnO2 nanocomposite to inhibit bacteria and viruses such as Escherichia coli, Staphylococcus aureus, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, and Aspergillus niger spores. The addition of palladium enhanced the antimicrobial activity by increasing the activity of the semiconductor oxides. The antimicrobial efficiency for inhibiting bacteria and fungi was reported as Escherichia coli > Staphylococcus aureus > Saccharomyces cerevisiae > Aspergillus niger. Furthermore, another composite Au/SnO2 was produced to check the antibacterial ability on Escherichia coli and Bacillus subtilis.320 As per the author, the synthesized nanocomposite showed very high efficiency in inhibiting Escherichia coli compared with Bacillus subtilis. A group of researchers prepared Co/SnO2 nanocomposites to investigate their antimicrobial properties.321 The synthesized nanocomposite was tested against Escherichia coli and Bacillus subtilis using the diffusion method. The study concluded that the nanocomposite showed an equal inhibitory effect on Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria.

6. Future perspectives

The ease of synthesis, high stability, compatibility, and adaptive properties of tin oxide make it an exciting nanomaterial for various applications in environmental remediation and energy production and storage. Generally, traditional and green synthesis methods are used for the production of tin oxide nanomaterials and nanocomposites. However, after extensive research, it was concluded that the green methods are far superior to the traditional methods as they are non-toxic, cost-effective, free of chemical additives, and eco-friendly. The different green synthesis procedures using different plant extracts led to various shapes and sizes of tin oxide nanoparticles, showing various applications. However, some subtle improvements are needed to prosper in this field and close the research gaps.

A proper study should be conducted to find the accurate stoichiometry between the precursor and plant extracts, eventually linked with surface morphology. Also, the mechanism that the plant extracts follow for synthesizing SnO2 nanoparticles is yet to be unravelled. The role of a particular plant extract in synthesizing a particular type of tin oxide nanoparticle is not fully understood. Hence, further research should be conducted in this field. Apart from the stems, fruits, and leaves, other parts of plants such as flowers should be studied, as various colours and components may bring some changes in nanoparticles. As discussed above, researchers have not mentioned the optimum temperature and the reaction conditions for synthesizing SnO2 nanoparticles and their nanocomposites. Experimental trials should be conducted in this direction.

Furthermore, it was found that most reported SnO2 nanoparticles are spherical-shaped. However, various shapes and sizes of SnO2 nanoparticles affect the efficiency, versatility, and stability. Also, the extraction of plants using mixed solvents has not been studied yet. So, further research should also discuss the role of reaction time, temperature, pH, the solvent used, and the extract concentration. In addition, the applications of biomolecule-mediated synthesized nanoparticles are pretty limited, so further studies are required in this field.

The nanoparticles developed by green synthesis methods showed extreme stability during the photodegradation of contaminants in an aqueous solution. However, the traditionally synthesized tin oxide nanoparticles sometimes become unstable and dissolve in water, leading to secondary pollution.322 More investigations should be done to improve the stability of these nanocomposites. Also, the large-scale use of these SnO2 nanoparticles has not been studied. It is essential as the photodegradation of contaminants is needed for the industrial sector such as textile industries, pharmaceutical companies, chemical industries, and many more. Also, future researchers should be directed on the cost-effectiveness and ease of preparation of the desired photocatalysts. Sensors made up of SnO2 nanoparticles and nanocomposites were also used to detect gas and other toxic chemicals. However, these sensors lacked a statistical method to analyse the complex data. Also, the sensors are sensitive to varying temperatures and pH, so more studies should be conducted in this field, and more focus should be given to amplifying the sensitivity to the desired contaminant. In addition, sensors lose their longevity when they have more exposure to the desired gas. So, future researchers should work in this field to improve the sensitivity of tin-oxide-based nanostructured materials towards a particular contaminant/pollutant.

Energy production by solar cells and water splitting is an efficient and straightforward procedure. Nevertheless, these are not very cost-effective, and the nanocomposites are mainly synthesized by the traditional method. So, more focus should be given to the green synthesis of these nanocomposites, resulting in a more eco-friendly and chemical additive-free environment. Also, the high energy usage and installation cost of the solar cells cause significant concern. So, future research work should be focused on enhancing the efficiency of SnO2-based solar cells to overcome these drawbacks. Lithium-ion batteries and supercapacitors made up of tin oxide nanoparticles show enhanced photochemical ability. However, these energy storage devices are still unable to catch up with the needs of modern society. The tech-generation requires high storage power and a very high number of recycles, which these nanocomposites lack. Researchers should work on this in the future by making it more enhanced as well as cost-effective. Studies regarding the antimicrobial activity of SnO2 nanoparticles have also been discussed. Researchers have conducted green synthesis of SnO2-based nanostructured materials for antimicrobial activity. However, more improvement is needed to enhance their performance. Further investigation must be done for the remediation of viruses and fungi.

More research should be carried out to produce SnO2 nanoparticles and SnO2-based nanocomposites using the green synthesis method for various applications, such as solar cells, water splitting, lithium-ion batteries, and supercapacitors. As discussed above, a proper mechanism is yet to be found to synthesize tin oxide nanoparticles and their nanocomposites. Thus, it is essential to research this field to know the exact chemical reactions and mechanisms undergone during the synthesis. The actual reaction mechanism will allow monitoring and enhancing of the biosynthesis process, which is essential for the large-scale production of SnO2 nanoparticles and nanocomposites. Hence, understanding the rapidly growing synthesis method discussed herein will help facilitate future research progress on SnO2 nanoparticles and nanocomposites and their enormous potential for industrial or large-scale production in the near future.

7. Conclusion

Traditional and green methods are usually used to prepare tin oxide nanoparticles and nanocomposites. However, due to the drawbacks of the traditional methods, researchers switched to green methods. The green synthesis methods were given prime focus as they are chemical additive-free, non-toxic, eco-friendly, and cost-effective. Plant extracts, microbes, and biomolecules were used as capping and reducing agents for the efficient synthesis of tin oxide nanoparticles. However, as discussed in this piece of literature, plant-mediated synthesis is the best synthesis technique. This review also focussed on the structure and characterization of tin oxide nanoparticles and composites. The tin oxide nanoparticles showed varieties of applications due to the use of different precursors in their synthesis. In this review, we discussed the various applications such as: (i) environmental remediation, which included SnO2 nanoparticles and nanocomposites working as catalysts for photocatalytic degradation, and acting as sensors; (ii) energy production, which included SnO2 nanocomposites working as a photoanode for solar cells and water splitting; (III) energy storage, which included a SnO2 nanocomposite electrode for lithium-ion batteries and supercapacitors; and (iv) biological remediation where the SnO2 nanoparticles and nanocomposites showed antimicrobial ability. It was concluded that tin oxide nanoparticles and their nanocomposites might have excellent potential for environmental remediation, energy production, and storage. This review will help understand the synthesis mechanisms and their drawbacks and lead a path to future research, eventually leading to large-scale production and application.
