Abstract

Scientific research is involved in the inception of flexible energy devices, and ferroelectric (FE) materials are making their mark on the process. In particular, devices based on inorganic FE (IFE) materials are finding a remarkable role in flexible energy devices, such as energy harvesters, infrared (IR) sensors, and energy storage capacitors because the latest technologies are particularly concentrating on self-powered flexible sensors and energy harvesters for a sustainable world. Moreover, the combination of IFEs with organic/inorganic polymers in flexible devices seems to satisfy the requirements of high mechanical strength, piezoelectric coefficient, pyroelectric coefficient, and energy storage properties essential for harvesting, sensing, and storage applications, respectively. In this review article, a systematic analysis of flexible IFE-based energy materials is presented. Starting with a brief introduction about energy generation in dielectric materials, the importance of IFEs, and the current state-of-the-art of IFE-based flexible materials in harvesting energy, IR sensing, and storing energy capacity are discussed. Further, the existing challenges and future perspectives in facilitating high-quality flexible IFE devices for real-world applications are envisaged.

1. Introduction

Humans have always been intimidated by darkness, and electrical energy is the prime source that saves the world from darkness. Nowadays, the depletion of non-renewable energy sources and expansion of the global population have caused a surge in hunger for sustainable energy.1,2 Therefore, the research community is looking at all possible ways of providing electrical energy to satisfy the human world, from consumer electronics to ultramodern space technology, and this is rooted in the development of energy devices. Introducing flexibility to these devices can dramatically enhance their applications for the emerging Internet of Things (IoT). IoT, which is considered one of the most important advances in technology, uses batteries as a key source of energy. However, batteries require a huge amount of money for maintenance in terms of replacement and power construction.2 Additionally, the current IoT uses sensors that are directly connected to power sources. Thus, failure or periodic replacement of the power source interrupts the smooth operation. An amicable solution to this problem is the development of long-term energy storage devices and self-powered energy harvesters.2

In fact, recently, dielectric materials have been receiving a great deal of attention in the areas of energy harvesting, sensing, and energy storage applications.313Fig. 1 shows the domains where dielectric materials make their mark. But there are several intrinsic parameters that determine the performance of a dielectric material as an energy harvester, an infra-red (IR) sensor, or an energy storage capacitor.313 For example, energy harvesting applications require dielectric materials that can convert energy from mechanical vibrations and heat fluctuations into electrical energy.14 On the other hand, IR sensing applications demand dielectric materials with a large spontaneous polarization change w.r.t. thermal deformation.14 While energy storage demands dielectric materials that can store electrical energy in terms of electrostatic displacement.1113 Therefore, a dielectric material that can perform the functions of energy harvester, infrared sensing, and energy storage will be a very good option for future energy technology.

2.2. Dielectric materials and energy harvesting

Energy harvesters involve the conversion of wasted energy in the form of light, heat, mechanical energy, etc. into electrical energy.1 Therefore, a material with the best energy scavenging skill to capture wasted energy and transform it into useful electrical energy can be explored for energy harvesting applications. Generally, a dielectric material can generate electrical energy using the squandered energy.22 A more detailed explanation on energy conversion by a dielectric material is portrayed in this section.
2.2.1. Piezoelectric current generation. A great deal of energy is lost as mechanical and thermal energy from different sources, such as sun, human motion, and wind.1 Consider the possibility of converting this lost energy into meaningful electricity. This would be revolutionary and provide a new path to self-sustaining technology. Nanogenerators (NGs) are energy-conversion devices that transform unused energy into usable electric power. Piezoelectric nanogenerators (PENGs) are NGs that use piezoelectric materials to transform mechanical energy into electrical energy.1 A piezoelectric material is a non-centrosymmetric material that uses mechanical energy to generate electrical energy. 2.3. Dielectric materials and infrared (IR) sensing

Sensors are crucial in futuristic military applications, robotics, e-skin, etc. High-end applications, such as military uses and robotics, demand sensing capabilities with high resolution, sensitivity, and a quick response time.34,35 For instance, threat identification in defense or military applications entails sensors for applications such as tracking, night vision, guidance systems, and rifle sights.34 This can be accomplished with the help of a sensor that can detect IR radiation. Generally, IR sensors are categorized into two types, namely photon-based and thermal-based, depending on the operating mechanism. In photon detectors, the incoming photon is absorbed by a semiconducting material that develops a photocurrent at the pn junction, thereby generating an electrical signal to operate the detector. Usually, group IIIV and IIVI semiconductor materials, such as gallium arsenide and mercury cadmium telluride, are preferred for the fabrication of photon detectors.35 However, the processing of these materials is challenging due to their low growth rate and the necessity for cooling (usually to 77 K) to reduce thermal noise for smooth functioning in the IR spectrum at longer wavelength. As a consequence, device fabrication becomes bulkier and more expensive. In contrast, thermal detectors work on the principle of absorbing IR radiation to create thermal fluctuations, thereby generating the required electrical energy for the smooth operation of the detector. Besides, these thermal-based IR detectors no longer require separate cooling and consume little power for the functioning of the detector. This makes them ideal candidates for self-powered, low-cost, compact, and lightweight applications from basic consumer electronics to ultramodern defense technology.35,36

The performance of a thermal-based IR detector depends on the ability of a material to induce a temperature change under the influence of incident IR radiation.14 For example, every heat-emitting body is a source of IR radiation and a material that can generate thermal change as a function of incident IR radiation will be a suitable option for the fabrication of an IR detector. However, a pyroelectric material is capable of generating a current response to thermal changes (as previously mentioned in Section 2.2.2) and can be used as a sensing element for IR detectors. A pyroelectric IR (PIR) detector has advantages such as sensitivity over the entire electromagnetic spectrum (depending on the materials), low operating power, fast response (picosecond range), and cost effectiveness. Moreover, the pyroelectric materials used in PIR respond to thermal changes at ambient temperatures and in the frequency range 2060 Hz.14 Such an ability of a PIR is explored in applications, such as image detection, burglar alarms, fire detection, and pollution monitoring.14,35 For instance, a schematic representation of a pyroelectric material as a sensing element for detecting images is shown in Fig. 7. A pyroelectric IR (PIR) imaging detector consists of a pyroelectric material, a multiplexer chip, a Fresnel lens usually made of germanium, and an infrared source.14 The germanium lens concentrates the IR radiation towards the pyroelectric material. The IR causes a temperature change in the pyroelectric material and generates an electrical signal in the material. This electrical signal is transferred to the multiplexer, which is soldered to the pyroelectric material. Finally, a video synthesizer creates a pattern or image of the source of IR radiation. Here, a germanium lens is used because it is transparent to IR radiation and opaque to visible light. Furthermore, a stationary object produces constant IR radiation, which will not lead to a temperature change. Therefore, a light chopper is introduced between the germanium lens and the pyroelectric material to induce a thermal change to detect a stationary object.14

2.4. Dielectric materials and energy storage

Energy storage devices have become an unavoidable factor in modern technology to reduce global pollution and fossil fuel depletion. This has paved the way for the introduction of different energy storage technologies like batteries, supercapacitors, and dielectric capacitors.1113 However, high-power applications, such as oil drilling and aircraft engines, require high energy density, superior power density, and excellent thermal stability. Energy storage devices like batteries and supercapacitors are unable to fulfil the criteria for high-power applications owing to their slow charge/discharge rate, low power density, low operating voltages, and low temperature stability.11 Batteries and supercapacitors require a chemical reaction to convert chemical energy into electrical energy. This reaction causes a delay in the movement of charges and, therefore, low power density. Additionally, batteries and supercapacitors both work on the principle of electrochemical reaction. Therefore, the smooth operation of these devices necessitates low operating voltages, as high voltages electrochemically break down the electrolyte. In contrast, a dielectric capacitor is characterized by ultrafast charge/discharge speed, high power density, excellent thermal stability, and, ultimately, very high operating voltages.11 This is due to the fact that dielectric capacitors store energy directly on the capacitor plates. Thus, the discharge speed is directly related to the conduction capability of the capacitor plates. Consequently, there is a faster charge/discharge rate in dielectric capacitors. This in turn generates a huge power density in dielectric capacitors. As a result, dielectric capacitors can play a significant role in advanced applications, as shown in Fig. 9.11,12

Generally, dielectric materials are classified into linear dielectric (LD), paraelectric (FE), ferroelectric (FE), and anti-ferroelectric (AFE). Commonly used dielectric materials for ESD studies are LDs, AFEs, and FEs. Despite their reduced energy loss, LDs are unsuitable for energy storage capacitors due to their low polarization value. 3. Flexible ferroelectric materials and recent developments

Ferroelectric materials could emerge as potential candidates for energy harvesting, IR sensing, and energy storage applications. From Sections 2.12.3, it is clear that a material with a very high degree of spontaneous polarization could contribute exceptional piezoelectric and pyroelectric currents. For instance, IFEs show a very large spontaneous polarization change near the phase transition temperature, leading to a large pyroelectric current.1 Near the phase transition temperature, dipoles lose their orientation ability with a tremendous decrease in the spontaneous polarization level. Consequently, there is a decrease in the number of free charges bound to the material surface. In order to compensate for the charge equilibrium, a redistribution of free charges takes place and, therefore, a larger current flow takes place in IFEs than in other dielectric materials.1 For example, an inorganic PMNPT possesses a very high piezoelectric coefficient and pyroelectric coefficient (Fig. 6), which can be utilized for the fabrication of piezoelectric/pyroelectric energy harvesters. This elucidates the statement that IFEs can be optimal options for energy harvesting and sensing applications. However, their brittle nature limits their use in flexible device applications and is the case for all IFEs. Strategies should be implemented for the synthesis, design, and fabrication of flexible IFEs for energy applications. Furthermore, investigations on Pb- and Pb-free flexible FEs can provide insight into different applications, such as self-powered devices, wearable electronics, robotics, e-skin, and power circuits for hybrid electric vehicles.111 In this section, we will discuss different flexible IFEs and their importance in the fabrication of flexible devices. The section deals particularly with advances in IFEs for flexible energy harvesting, IR sensing, and energy storage domains.

3.1. IFEs and flexible energy harvesters

The conversion of mechanical vibrations by a material into useful electricity relies heavily on the piezoelectric property. However, most investigations on PENGs have concentrated on zinc oxide (ZnO) nanowires (NWs).4145 However, piezoelectric ZnO NWs suffer with drawbacks such as low piezoelectric coefficient, low output voltage, and low power generation. Furthermore, single-crystal ZnO NWs with diameters less than 100 nm and lengths greater than 50 m are difficult to fabricate. However, the key problem with most PENGs is electrostatic noise or the triboelectric effect related to contact between the measuring equipment and the NG due to compressive forces. Recently, Lee et al. fabricated an electrostatic noise-free PZT nanofibre-based PENG.51 The flexible PENG was fabricated by combining an electrospun PZT nanofibre textile and a PDMS matrix. Structural analysis of the PZT nanowire confirmed a well-crystallized highly textured (101)-oriented polycrystalline perovskite phase of PZT (Fig. 14(a)). In this work, a PZT nanofibre/PDMS composite layer of equal thickness was formed by spreading PDMS on the PZT textile. The PENG formed in this way eliminates the electrostatic noise associated with the triboelectric effect due to mechanical friction. To prove output generation is purely piezoelectric dependent, Lee et al. fabricated a PDMS sample alone and tested it under bending motion. But the electric impulse in the PDMS sample was inconsequential (Fig. 14(b) and (c)). This implies that the voltage output of the PDMS/PZT composite nanogenerator was solely derived from the electrons caused by piezoelectric potential under mechanical deformation. The statement was again substantiated by measuring the output voltage and current of the PDMS/PZT composite PENG as a function of PZT film thickness (Fig. 14(d)). The PENG was exposed to periodic bending and unbending for different thicknesses. The results revealed a linear dependency of the generated output current on the film thickness, originating directly from the piezoelectric effect. During bending, the mechanical force transfers a strain to the PENG through the PDMS matrix and thus accumulates the generated charges induced by the piezoelectric potential of the PZT at the electrode ends. During unbending, these accumulated charge carriers move in the opposite direction. In effect, the net piezoelectric potential vanishes and there is no triboelectric effect. Thus, all the output parameters generated are based purely on the piezoelectric effect, not the triboelectric effect. A flexible device with a PZT thickness of 0.80 m and area of 0.8 cm2 generated a Vo equal to 1.1 V and Io equal to 1.4 A.

Even though Pb-based systems are highly recommended for flexible energy harvesting applications, the presence of highly toxic Pb is a serious concern when it comes to safeguarding human beings as well as the environment.39 The research community is searching for alternatives to Pb-based systems, so that this problem can be eradicated. This led to the introduction of different Pb-free perovskite IFEs into flexible energy harvesting technology. In 2010, Park et al. introduced a BTO thin film-based PENG on a plastic substrate. Initially, the BTO was radio frequency (rf) sputtered onto a Pt/Ti/SiO2/Si substrate and subsequently annealed at 700 C to form a BTO crystalline phase from the amorphous film.52 Secondly, a metalinsulatormetal (MIM) structure with a gold (Au) top electrode and Pt as the bottom electrode was etched using an inductively coupled plasma-reactive ion etcher (ICP-RIE). The ICP-RIE etching was carried out using aluminium (Al) and SiO2 masks. Then, the MIM structure ribbon was separated by removing the Si layer using tetramethylammonium hydroxide via anisotropic wet etching. Afterwards, the freestanding MIM structure was transferred onto the PDMS. The MIM-inked PDMS elastomer slab was placed on a polyurethane (PU)-coated Kapton film (plastic film). Once the MIM structure was fully settled on the plastic substrate, the PDMS was removed. The top of the BTO/PU/plastic substrate with the metal electrodes was spin-coated with a diluted UV-sensitive epoxy. Finally, the metal contact area was photolithographically opened to form a flexible PENG. The entire fabrication is schematically illustrated in Fig. 15(a). BTO film showed a tetragonal phase with excellent crystallinity and was confirmed using XRD and Raman spectroscopy. Furthermore, the tetragonal phase suggested a ferroelectric BTO. Before measuring the energy harvesting properties, the PENG was poled at 200 kV cm1 for 15 h at a temperature of 150 C. As illustrated in Fig. 15(b) and (c), the output performance of the PENG was investigated under periodic bending and unbending modes. The repeated bending/unbending motions using a finger induced piezoelectricity and generated a maximum Vo of 1.0 V and Io of 26 nA. In addition, the PENG produced a current density of 0.19 A cm2 and a power density of 7 mW cm3. This showed the possibilities of BTO-based perovskite oxide films for the fabrication of a flexible energy harvesting device. However, the high piezoelectric coefficient in BTO is attributed to the relatively smaller size and large charge of Ti4+.53 This constitutes a higher displacement of Ti atoms along the field direction during poling and therefore superior piezoelectricity. Different structures of BTO, such as microplatelets, nanofibres, nanotubes, and nanoparticles, have been studied for energy harvesting, and nanofibres caught researchers attention owing to their high mechanical strength and high-power generation even with small mechanical changes.53 Yan et al. demonstrated that a BTO nanofibre/PDMS composite has very good energy harvesting potential.53 In this work, the energy harvesting performance of the BTO nanofibre-based PENG was studied by aligning BTO nanofibres in different orientations, such as vertically, horizontally, and randomly on the PDMS matrix: namely BTNF-V, BTNF-H, and BTNF-R, respectively. The PDMS was mixed with aligned electrospun BTO nanofibres, as shown in Fig. 15(d). A schematic representation of the three different alignments for testing piezoelectricity is shown in Fig. 15(e). The remarkable energy harvesting performance in BTNF-V arises from the high orientation of domains in the field direction during the poling process. Furthermore, the BTO density in the applied field direction is greater in BTNF-V than in the other two structures.

As a result, a strain-induced piezoelectric effect as well as a temperature-induced pyroelectric effect emerged with temperature variation, indicating a simultaneous effect or hybrid effect. The hybrid PN achieved a peak output voltage of 0.34 V and a current of 0.34 A cm2 in this manner. In addition, the inorganic materials are usually tougher than biological materials and the flexible PZT-based PN are excellent energy harvesters for a harsh environment such as strong base conditions or humidity. Zhang et al. in 2016 developed an adaptable PZT-based PN working on pyroelectric, piezoelectric, and triboelectric properties.71 The device comprised a PZT thin film as the pyroelectric and piezoelectric active material, a flexible polyamide film with fluorinated ethylene propylene (FEP) as the triboelectric active material, thin ITO film mixed with Ag NWs/PDMS film as top electrode, and Ag as the bottom electrode under the PZT. Initially, the electric dipoles are in equilibrium state at room temperature due to zero spontaneous polarisation change. As the temperature is varied (heating or cooling), a spontaneous polarisation develops, and there is electron flow through the external circuit. Consequently, a pyroelectric potential is produced across the electrodes. Simultaneously, the ferroelectric PZT PyNG exhibits a significant response to visible light indicating the photoelectric effect, which might be used for solar cell applications. Furthermore, there is a compressive force on the FEP-initiated triboelectric action in the polyamide film. However, the electrostatic equilibrium prevents the electron flow whenever the FEP comes into contact with the polyamide layer. Furthermore, the compressive strain creates a positive piezoelectric potential between the top and bottom electrodes. This in turn creates an electron flow from Ag to the ITO. As the polyamide layer separates or moves away from the FEP, triboelectrification begins. This charges the FEP charge and thereby induces charges on the ITO. Thus, the electrons are propelled by this electric potential from the ITO to Ag. As a result, the gadget is capable of scavenging various energy sources, such as thermal, solar, and mechanical energy. This single hybrid NG generated an Io of 5 A and Vo of 80 V. Furthermore, the ferroelectric PZT energy harvester charged a 10 F capacitor to 5.1 V within a short timespan (90 s).

Despite the fact that PZTs are excellent materials for energy harvesters, the presence of toxic lead as well as its high cost impede their use to a certain limit at the present time. Alternative low-cost eco-friendly ferroelectric materials with comparable properties to PZT need to be investigated to solve the problem. Yang et al. fabricated a Pb-free KNbO3-based flexible PyNG in 2012.72 A composite film comprising KNbO3 NWs with a single crystalline structure and PDMS was tested for energy harvesting studies. The top and bottom electrodes were made of Ag and ITO films, respectively. The dipoles in the KNbO3 NWs rotate when the device is exposed to a heat change because KNbO3 is a multidomain material. The dipoles experience higher lattice thermal vibration with increased amplitude as the temperature rises. This causes a net change in Ps by strengthening the internal electric dipole interactions. Subsequently, a charge flow took place, resulting in a peak voltage of 10 mV and a peak current of 120 pA. Recently, Zhao et al. fabricated a flexible BTO-based hybrid nanogenerator capable of harvesting mechanical, thermal, and light energy.73 A BTO ceramic disc with Ag top and bottom electrodes was installed on a PDMS layer, as shown in Fig. 21(a). The outputs generated by the device from different sources, such as light energy change via light illumination, mechanical motion via pressure, and thermal change via different temperatures, are shown in Fig. 21(b). Furthermore, Pb-free BFO thin films have proved to be an excellent candidate for photodetector (PD) applications owing to their exceptional residual polarization (100 C cm2) and a narrow optical bandgap, which play a crucial role for efficient photovoltaic performance.74,75 More importantly, a self-polarized polycrystalline BFO film with an LNO buffer layer exhibited a stable photovoltaic effect under a zero-bias voltage. Very recently, in 2022, Li et al. fabricated a flexible self-powered UV-visible PD using BFO/XTiO3 (X = Sr, Zn, Pb) multilayer films.75 The BFO/XTiO3 (X = Sr, Zn, Pb) multilayer films were deposited on Ni foil using the solgel technique. The multilayer-based BFO flexible PD showed fast response speed and high sensitivity. The BFO/ZnTiO3 reported a very good current responsivity of 7.4 mA W1 at zero bias under 365 nm illumination. This shows a vision of Pb-free materials in multiple-energy harvesting by scavenging different forms of wasted energies. 3.2. IFEs and flexible pyroelectric infrared sensor

Flexible inorganic pyroelectric infrared detectors have advantages over traditional pyroelectric infrared detectors due to their high flexibility, robustness, mechanical strength, etc.7681 Evidently, as discussed in the previous section, the pyroelectric coefficients of the IFEs are very much higher compared to polymer materials and, thus, the combination of inorganic-polymer materials augments the pyroelectric coefficient.77,78 The best way to enhance the pyroelectric coefficient of flexible inorganic-polymer detectors is to form a 03 type composite. A 03 type composite is a composite structure formed with ceramic material (especially nanoparticles) also termed nanofillers embedded in the polymer matrix.80 In such a system, it is possible to tailor the volume fraction in order to control the pyroelectric properties. In fact, suitable poling and composition can significantly reduce the dielectric properties and thereby improve the FOM of the detector.80 However, there are only a limited number of investigations on flexible pyroelectric sensors as it is an emerging field. Yang et al. prepared a 03 type composite flexible film comprising PbTiO3 (PT) and P(VDF-TrFE) via a solution casting technique.80 In this method, the composite film was formed by the dispersion of PT NW in the P(VDF-TrFE) matrix followed by a stirring procedure for almost 3 hours at 70 C. Afterwards, the residue was drop casted onto a quartz substrate for densification to form the 03 type PT/P(VDF-TrFE) composite film. The 50 wt% PT/P(VDF-TrFE) reported a very good pyroelectric coefficient (72.78 C m2 K1) compared to the P(VDF-TrFE) polymer (52.74 C m2 K1). Furthermore, the work reported a very good FOM with FD > 18 106 Pa1/2 and FV > 0.26 m2 C1. Jayalakshmy et al. reported a flexible Pb-free pyroelectric LiNbO3/PVDF (LN/PVDF) 03 type composite structure synthesised via a solvent-cast technique by varying the LN nanofillers in the PVDF matrix.77 The capability of the LN/PVDF composite film as a sensing element was determined in terms of its pyroelectric coefficient and FOM. The composite film exhibited a very good pyroelectric coefficient (127 C m2 K1) and FOM (FD = 143.27 103 C m J1), suggesting that they are very good for sensor applications. Luo et al. reported another Pb-free 03 type composite film composed of K0.5Na0.5NbO3 (KNN) nanofillers and P(VDF-TrFE).81 The work reported a pyroelectric coefficient of 63 C m2 K1 and an FD of 4.94 106 Pa1/2. The enhanced FOM in KNN/P(VDF-TrFE) composite film is attributed to the decreased dielectric permittivity and dielectric loss, which in turn enhances the sensitivity. Li et al. prepared BTO-based 03 type composite film and studied the FOM for sensing applications. Instead of BTO nanoparticles as nanofillers, the group used dopamine (DM)-modified BTO nanoparticles (DM-BTO) in the PVDF matrix for the formation of the 03 type composite film. The incorporation of DM improves the compatibility between BTO and the polymer matrix. Furthermore, the DM favours the transformation of the PVDF -phase into the -phase, beneficial for the suppression of dielectric permittivity as well as dielectric loss and thereby promoting the FOM of the film. More importantly, the DM-BTO/PVDF augments the breakdown strength of the composite film, which is attributed to the reduced negative dielectric effect (NDE) caused by voids, deficiencies, gaps, boundaries, etc. However, high dielectric loss followed by breakdown of the film are possible in the case of BTO nanoparticles alone. This will increase the probability of breakdown of the composite film in the case of BTO-nanoparticle-embedded PVDF.

DM modification allows enhancement of the junction between BTO and PVDF, subsequently reducing the deficiency and thus augmenting the breakdown of the film by depleting the NDE. As shown in Fig. 22(a) and (b), it is evident that the pyroelectric performance of the composite film increased with the dispersion of DM-BTO. The composite film revealed a pyroelectric coefficient of 42 C m2 K1, which is almost 75% higher than that of the PVDF polymer (Fig. 22(a)). Consequently, a very good detection capability (FD = 96.6 pC m J1) is evidenced in Fig. 22(b). However, a real-time investigation of the sensing ability of a flexible pyroelectric sensor is essential. Wu et al. fabricated an infrared device using Pb(Zr0.52Ti0.48)O3 (PZT) and P(VDF-TrFE) composite material.82 In this study, a dual capacitor structure (DCS) was formed, as shown in Fig. 22(c), in order to reduce the noise in the signal arising from the surrounding temperature changes. The composite film was synthesised using the solution cast technique. First, a suspension containing PZT and P(VDF-TrFE) was cast onto a glass plate to remove the organic solvents. Then, the mixture on a glass plate was annealed for almost 4 hours at 120 C to form a flexible composite film. The top and bottom electrodes were Fe/Ni electrodes. After that, the composite film was polarized and then the opposite polarities were serially connected to form the DCS sensor. The DCS was connected to a pre-amplifier and finally the packaging process released the device. The entire processing of the IR detector is shown in Fig. 22(d). 3.3. IFEs and flexible energy storage

Even though IFE ceramic systems are receiving significant attention, the low dielectric breakdown strength (less than MV) limits their application in high-power applications, such as hybrid electric vehicles, military technology, space technology, and pulsed power technologies.11 Moreover, the possibilities of miniaturizing the electronic devices suppresses the demand for bulk ceramics. Thin-film-based IFEs, on the other hand, are an excellent choice for the fabrication of energy storage capacitors due to their outstanding dielectric breakdown strength (in the MV range) and miniaturised size, which can be explored for real-time applications. The high dielectric breakdown strength enhances the ESD, as the energy storage density is controlled by the thickness to volume ratio. At lower thickness, the dielectric breakdown strength become an intrinsic property of the material (below a critical value) and therefore turns out to be independent of dimension. This in turn results in a very high energy storage density. In addition, the large dielectric breakdown strength of the thin-film capacitor provides safety over a wide range of voltages and reduces the possibility of irreversible failure.12

Recently, IFE-based thin films have found a path in flexible energy storage capacitor fabrication owing to the brittle nature of substrates such as Pt-coated Si, strontium titanate, and lanthanum aluminate. Moreover, the difficultly in integrating these rigid substrate-based materials onto flexible substrates is a challenge. Furthermore, IFE-based thin films exhibit excellent dielectric constants as well as superior thermal stability compared to biaxially oriented polypropylene (BOPP) and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF), which are considered pivotal factors determining the performance of a dielectric energy storage capacitor.1113 Therefore, the fabrication of flexible IFE-based thin films will be a suitable option to yield high energy storage properties.

Recent investigation showed that relaxor FEs (RFEs) have excellent energy storage properties compared to FEs, owing to their slim PE loop and excellent dielectric permittivity.83 Shen et al. in 2019 fabricated a flexible Pb-based IFE ceramic film energy storage dielectric capacitor. The group fabricated RFE Pb0.91La0.09(Zr0.65Ti0.35)0.9775O3 (PLZT 9/65/35) thick film on a flexible LNO/fluorophlogopite (F)-mica substrate via an rf magnetron sputtering technique.84 F-mica has advantages, such as high mechanical flexibility, good thermal stability, a smooth surface, and excellent chemical stability, which make it a suitable flexible substrate for the fabrication of IFE film capacitors. In this work, LNO is the bottom electrode and Au is the top electrode. Here, LNO enhances the epitaxial growth of PLZT 9/65/35 thick films due to the fact that both PLZT and LNO possess a perovskite structure. The relaxor behaviour in PLZT 9/65/35 thick film was evaluated using the modified CurieWiess law and the measured value of the degree of diffuseness () was 1.99. According to the modified CurieWiess law, a typical RFE is expected to have a value equal to 2. Hence, the PLZT 9/65/35 thick film exhibits a high degree of diffuseness. The PLZT/F-mica RFE capacitor displayed a very good breakdown strength of 1998 kV cm1, which is ascribed to the dense microstructure and low leakage current (Fig. 23(a)). At 1998 kV cm1, the thick film capacitor showed an ESD of 40.2 J cm3 and an of 62% (Fig. 23(b)). The energy storage performance as a function of bending radius and bending cycle is illustrated in Fig. 23(c) and (d). The flexible thick film capacitor exhibited a negligible variation in the energy storage performance w.r.t. bending radius and bending cycle. In fact, the bending cycle test performed at 4.5 mm bending radius demonstrated a stable energy storage performance up to 2000 cycles, indicating the application of PLZT 9/65/35 thick film in flexible electronics as it is the normal bending radius of wearable devices. In the same year, Nguyen et al. reported high energy storage performance in Pb0.9La0.1Zr0.52Ti0.48O3 (PLZT) RFE thin films grown on flexible Ti foil using pulsed laser deposition (PLD).85 The relaxor behavior in PLZT flexible thin film was confirmed using the broad dielectric maximum, which is a characteristic of RFEs. PLZT flexible thin film showed a slim PE loop with an ESD of 40.9 J cm3 and an of 80.2% at 3000 kV cm1. Moreover, the PLZT flexible thin film exhibited fatigue-free behavior (up to 1010 cycles) and superior thermal stability (room temperature to 200 C) required for pulsed power applications.

4. Perspectives and concluding remarks

Flexible energy devices can revolutionize the integration and miniaturization of the electronic industry. This review article is a vision of recent advances of flexible IFEs in the area of energy harvesting, infrared sensing, and energy storage devices. There are reasonable grounds for focusing on these areas in particular. For instance, a lot of energy is wasted in the form of mechanical and heat energy. Energy harvesters perform the function of converting this wasted energy into electrical energy and can play a crucial role in electrical energy generation. Another enormous source of heat energy is IR radiation. This is not only light: every living body emits IR radiation and it is possible to convert IR power into electrical energy for sensing applications. IR sensors are important in applications such as thermal imaging and gas detection. Also, electrical energy storage is an inevitable part of human life, from basic consumer electronics to ultramodern pulsed power technology. Therefore, a material that can fulfil all these functions will be the best alternative and IFEs could be good contenders. Large piezoelectric and pyroelectric coefficient of IFEs can be explored in energy harvesting and IR sensing applications. On the other hand, the large spontaneous polarization, excellent breakdown strength, and fast chargedischarge speed of IFEs can be utilized for energy storage dielectric capacitor applications. For example, batteries alone cannot meet the requirements of high power and high temperature applications, such as HEVs and high-temperature oil well drilling due to the failure of the power system. An IFE can withstand high fields, high temperatures, and can discharge enormous power for the smooth operation of a power system.97 In this way, IFEs could be a powerful material for the future energy world.

Compared to polymers, IFEs exhibit exceptional piezoelectric, pyroelectric, and energy storage properties. Though IFEs show exceptional electrical properties, their brittle nature inhibits their application in flexible devices. Furthermore, IFEs show disadvantages such as low breakdown strength due to their large hysteresis loss and large dielectric loss compared to polymers. These drawbacks prevent flexible electronic applications of IFEs, emphasising the relevance of IFEpolymer combinations, IFEflexible substrate combinations, etc. Therefore, it is necessary to make a detailed investigation of the performance of flexible IFE materials as an emerging domain of science. In this review article, various IFE materials and their recent developments in flexible energy harvesting, IR sensing, and energy storage were briefly revised. Different approaches, such as IFE thin film fabrications on a polymer substrate, 03 type IFEpolymer composite films, and IFE on flexible substrate using various preparation techniques were introduced to improve the piezoelectric, pyroelectric, mechanical strength, and energy storage performance of flexible devices. For instance, there are a lot of investigations of flexible NGs formed from IFE filmpolymer substrates in the area of energy harvesting and sensing applications, while 03 type flexible IFE-composite films are investigated for IR sensing applications and IFEs on flexible substrates (F-mica, Ni foil, etc.) for energy storage capacitor applications. From Tables 1 and 2, it is clear that IFEs plays a significant role in the fabrication of flexible energy harvesting and energy storage devices.

Even though IFEs possess very good piezoelectric, pyroelectric, and energy storage properties compared to polymer FEs, there are limitations that prevent these materials achieving exceptional performance. For instance, the high crystallization temperature and mechanically brittle nature of these IFE oxides are key problems that obstruct the formation of flexible energy harvesters. Usually, the crystallization temperature of an IFE oxide is much greater than the degradation temperature of most polymer substrate.98,99 One possible way to overcome this issue is by transferring the IFE oxide from a rigid substrate using a lift-off process with water-soluble Sr3Al2O6 as a sacrificial layer, which is compatible with epitaxial functional IFE oxide.98,99 Furthermore, low-temperature chemical solution deposition (CSD) techniques, such as spin coating and spray pyrolysis, must be explored effectively to develop high-quality IFE oxide thin films compatible with plastic as well as organic polymer substrates.100103 In most cases, the CSD involves organic solvents with carbon residues, whose decomposition temperature (>500 C) is greater than the degradation temperatures of plastic and polymer substrates (<350 C).101,102 Instead, the strategy of using aqueous solvents with covalent bonds in CSD resolves this problem due to covalent bond breakage at low temperatures. This causes enhanced nucleation and crystallization of the functional IFE film even at low temperatures. This in turns reduces energy consumption during the fabrication process and allows direct integration with flexible plastic/polymer substrates.102,103 Secondly, it is necessary to maintain the mechanical stability between the IFE and the polymer matrix of the IFEpolymer nanocomposite.104 The higher thickness of the IFE films (greater than 100 nm) causes mechanical instability between the IFE film and the polymer matrix, which creates delamination or a crack in the films.105 This can be improved only by playing with the interfacial energy between the IFE and the polymer.105 One possible method has been to functionalize the IFE surface with a low interfacial energy polymer matrix.104,106,107 This in turn eliminates the defects associated with agglomeration and elevates the dispersibility.104 Thus, the formation of nanoceramicpolymer composites has become a popular method for transforming an IFE bulk dielectric ceramic into the printable inks required for high productivity and industrial applications.107 To create epoxy-based inks for screen printing nanocomposite dielectric films, functionalized IFE nanoparticles and the polymer are combined. By using a conductive elastomer and an insulating elastomeric spring element (as a substitute for a liquid metal electrode), these IFEpolymer composite films are then combined into reliable mechanical energy harvesting devices to enable maximum electrostatic transduction.107 Moreover, nanoscale ferroelectric HfO2, HfxZr1xO2 (HZO) (with x ranging from 0 to 1), and ZrO2 are suitable candidates for large-area, cost-effective energy storage.108114 Given the possibility of achieving robust ferroelectric polarization in sub-10 nm thick HZO films, flexible HZO thin films deposited on PET substrates did not show obvious deterioration in ferroelectric properties during spalling and transfer.115 However, flexible HZO films have not being investigated for energy harvesting, while FE HfO2 thin films deposited on an Si substrate demonstrated excellent current responsivity with an ultrafast response time (in the s range) utilizing the ferro-pyro-phototronic effect will be useful for applications such as security alert systems, memory storage, and vision sensors.115 Therefore, we anticipate that IFE-based polymer composites and flexible HZO thin films will play a crucial role in energy harvesting.

Most investigations on the energy harvesting performance of these flexible NGs concentrated on low-frequency (60 Hz) microscale applications, such powering devices like LEDs and watches, and charging capacitors.116,117 The necessity of scaling up these devices to macroscale power at high frequency could bring up self-sustained energy areas such as structural health monitoring (SHM) and self-sustaining clean room research facilities.116 For instance, energy harvesters could play a crucial role in the aviation industry to detect damage in flight, and a schematic illustration is shown in Fig. 28. Furthermore, clean rooms in research institutes operate almost 24 hours.117 There will be 24 7 operating instruments, such as roughing pumps and electrodynamic shakers, whose frequency of operation is greater than 60 Hz.115 Therefore, an investigation into the energy harvesting performance of these flexible IFEs at high frequency could pave the way for a self-sustaining energy based clean room in the future. Additionally, self-powered PD applications necessitate materials that can harvest light energy in the UV-visible region. Some recent investigations have revealed the possible use of IFE-based ceramics and films, such as BFO, BTO, BCZT, and BNT, as PDs due to the ferro-pyro-phototronic effect.
