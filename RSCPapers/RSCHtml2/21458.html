Abstract

Two-dimensional (2D) materials have been the focus of materials research for many years due to their unique fascinating properties and large specific surface area (SSA). They are very sensitive to the analytes (ions, glucose, DNA, protein, etc.), resulting in their wide-spread development in the field of sensing. New 2D materials, as the basis of applications, are constantly being fabricated and comprehensively studied. In a variety of sensing applications, the solution-gated transistor (SGT) is a promising biochemical sensing platform because it can work at low voltage in different electrolytes, which is ideal for monitoring body fluids in wearable electronics, e-skin, or implantable devices. However, there are still some key challenges, such as device stability and reproducibility, that must be faced in order to pave the way for the development of cost-effective, flexible, and transparent SGTs with 2D materials. In this review, the device preparation, device physics, and the latest application prospects of 2D materials-based SGTs are systematically presented. Besides, a bold perspective is also provided for the future development of these devices.

1. Introduction

Highly sensitive biochemical sensors are exceedingly desirable in various fields, including healthcare products, medical analysis, environmental monitoring, food safety tests, etc.1 A variety of detection methods, including optical, magnetic, and plasmonic analysis techniques, have been developed for the rapid and accurate identification of low-concentration target molecules. Although these findings exhibit attractive sensing mechanisms, they also have some limitations that should not be ignored, such as time-consuming operation, high cost, low resolution, and noisy background.2 The monitoring of changes in electrical signals can offer more accurate and specifically precise information about target molecules, and electrical sensing devices also support label-free and real-time measurements.3 The materials used in these devices profoundly influence the features of each sensing strategy. Consequently, the exploration of advanced materials for sensors can provide more opportunities to boost the advancement of this emerging area.4

Low-dimensional materials, in particular, two-dimensional (2D) materials, are more attractive, not only due to their novel physical phenomena that occur when charge and heat transport are confined to a plane,5 but also because of their unique electrical, optical, mechanical, and chemical properties.6 A 2D material refers to a type of material that is separated by van der Waals forces.7 Atoms form atomic layers in a 2D direction (in-plane) by tightly bound covalent bonds or ionic bonds, which are connected in the vertical direction (out-of-plane) by the weak van der Waals forces.8 The interactions of such weak van der Waals forces between adjacent layers allow the layered material to be separated by mechanical9,10 or liquid phase exfoliation11,12 techniques to form independent large-area atomic layers or even monoatomic layer.13

The wave of research into 2D nano-materials began in 2004,14–17 when the demonstration of how thin layers of carbon atoms can be separated from a large amount of graphite revolutionized the study of 2D materials.18,19 Therefore, graphene has been characterized with excellent chemical, mechanical, and electrical properties,20,21 which has aroused significant research interest in the fields of science and engineering.22,23 The unique 2D structure of graphene has led to a number of remarkable physical properties, such as massless carriers (electrons and holes), high transparency monolayer (97.7%), high electrical conductivity, and in particular, high carrier mobility of 105 cm2 V−1 s−1, which is approximately 2–3 orders of magnitude better than many representative semiconductors such as silicon. Many “low-hanging graphene fruits” have already been successfully harvested; therefore, researchers have now turned their attention to other 2D atomic crystals,24 ranging from transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs, namely, MoS2, MoSe2, WS2, WSe2),25–27 hexagonal boron nitride (h-BN),28 to black phosphorus (BP)29 and other 2D-Xenes materials (Si, Ge, Sn, B) as well.30–32 Similar to graphene, these 2D materials have widely unique properties, such as electrical, thermal, optical, and mechanical properties, which are not very common in materials with 3D structures. Each 2D material has its specific advantages, so it is possible to fabricate a variety of sensors based on these advantages. For example, the conductivity and carrier mobility of graphene are significantly higher than those of many other semiconductors used in transistor-based sensors, including organic semiconductors,33–35 silicon nanowires,36–38 and carbon nanotubes (CNTs).39–41 TMDs have multiple synthesis methods, low cytotoxicity, and adjustable bandgap. Therefore, they are widely applied in implantable biosensors and wearable electric devices. BP has a direct bandgap, small work function, strong absorbability, and remarkable carrier mobility as well. Thus, it has been a popular channel material in transistor-based chemical sensors. This comes down to their dimensionally constrained effects and atomically thin band structures modulation.42,43 Owing to their unique properties and large SSA, these types of 2D materials are of great significance in a wide variety of applications, including optoelectronics, catalysts, biochemical sensors, supercapacitors, solar cells, and lithium-ion batteries.44–46 Moreover, 2D materials with complementary properties and fascinating functions enable rapid advances in science and engineering and accelerate the development of global nanotechnology applications to meet social challenges in the fields of energy, electronics, sensors, and health.47–49 2D materials also have tremendous appeal in a new generation of nano-electronic devices, because it is relatively easy to make complex structures using 2D materials compared to 1D materials.50

2. Solution-gated transistors of two-dimensional materials

2.1. Synthesis methods for two-dimensional materials

With the advancement in research and more systematic explorations, a growing number of 2D nanomaterials has been discovered.64–66 Moreover, the physical, chemical and photoelectrical properties of these materials have been theoretically explored.67,68 The excellent features and broad application prospects of these materials have prompted researchers to search for more perfect methods for the synthesis of 2D nanomaterials. Hence, the research on the preparation of 2D materials in recent years has entered a rapid development stage.69,70 The conventional preparation methods can be divided into the following two categories: one is the top-down methods, which start from the macroscopic block materials and utilize the layered structure with little damage to thin or separate the bulk material into fewer layers or even single layers.71 The commonly used top-down methods include mechanical exfoliation,72 liquid-phase exfoliation,73 ion intercalation, and electrochemical exfoliation. The other is the bottom-up method. The characteristics of this method include the realization of a range of well-defined nanostructured materials via association of starting elements, for synthesizing the required 2D materials by physical or chemical reaction.74,75 These methods include chemical vapor deposition (CVD), molecular beam epitaxy (MBE), physical vapor deposition (PVD), and thermal solution synthesis.
2.1.1. Mechanical exfoliation. The mechanical exfoliation method is a conventional method for realizing the preparation of 2D nanomaterials, which uses transparent tape to peel and separate layered crystals to obtain 2D materials.76 In fact, the principle of mechanical exfoliation technology involves the application of external force to weaken the van der Waals interaction between the crystal layers without breaking covalent bonds in each layer to separate 2D nanomaterials into fewer layers by mechanical force.77 For example, a block of bulk graphite material is first placed on the adhesive surface of the tape. Then the surface is peeled off and separated into thin sheets using the adhesive surface of another tape. This process can be repeated several times to obtain the desired number of appropriate layers of 2D materials.78,79 Then, the sheet material of the transparent tape is glued and pressed on a clean and flat substrate such as SiO2/Si. Finally, the single or multi-layer 2D nanosheets on the substrate can be obtained by stripping the transparent tape. When a suitable substrate is used, ultra-thin 2D crystals are obtained, which can be identified under optical microscope. Owing to layered structure characteristics of transition metal compounds, mechanical exfoliation method can also be far and wide used in the preparation of 2D transition metal compounds, such as MoS2 nanosheet materials with various thicknesses.80,81 Mechanical exfoliation method is simple and direct and thus advantageous to obtain high-quality single- or few-layer nanocrystals from their native multi-layer structures.82–84 Moreover, this method does not involve chemical reaction; therefore, the surface chemical activity of the obtained 2D samples is relatively low. Thus the 2D crystals obtained by this method can still maintain good stability even after being exposed to the air for several months.85 However, the disadvantages of this method are also obvious. It is challenging to obtain monolayer samples when there is little difference between non-layered or interlayer forces and in-plane forces. Moreover, the size of crystal materials fabricated by this method is usually less than 20 μm, and various layers of samples exist at the same time, which is not conducive to large-scale industrial production and only suitable for laboratory research.
2.1.2. Liquid phase exfoliation. Inspired by the mechanical exfoliation method, the researchers found that layered crystals dispersed in a liquid medium could also be peeled off into ultra-thin low-dimensional nanosheets if appropriate mechanical forces are applied to them.86 Compared to the low yield obtained via mechanical exfoliation, liquid-phase exfoliation makes it possible to prepare 2D nanocrystalline materials in large quantities.87 In this process, first, the layers of bulk materials are dispersed under ultrasonication in a specific solvent, with adjusted ultrasonic intensity and time. Then, under the action of ultrasonic vibration, the sample is exposed to a shear force, which leads to the breaking of the interlayer van der Waals bonds. However, at the same time, the stability of covalent bonds in the plane does not get damaged by ultrasonication, so that the suspension solution with a single layer or fewer layers of low-dimensional nanosheet can be obtained.88 After being centrifuged, the layered suspension is extracted layer by layer, dried or filtered out of the solvent, and the remaining part is low-dimensional material samples with various layer thicknesses. Compared to the mechanical exfoliation method, this method is more appropriate for preparing large-scale 2D nanofilm;89 however, the SSA of layered materials increases sharply after the achievement of 2D layered structure.90 Therefore, improved solvent or improved method is always the focus of liquid phase exfoliation method.91,92
2.1.3. Hydrothermal synthesis. Hydrothermal synthesis is a bottom-up preparation technique and is also the traditional preparation method commonly used to synthesize low-layer 2D nanomaterials.93,94 Hydrothermal synthesis refers to the preparation process in which the material reacts with the precursor in solution and crystallizes the substance from the organic or aqueous solution under higher vapor pressure and temperature. This method can be used to prepare a variety of 2D transition metal layered structural materials, including MoS2 and WS2.95,96 Hydrothermal synthesis can also be used for the preparation of 2D nanocrystals and other materials to form hybrid structures.97 Owing to its strong controllability, hydrothermal synthesis is considered as a convenient and repeatable method to prepare ultra-thin 2D nanomaterials with controllable size and thickness, thus it has potential industrial application value.98 In particular, this method is widely used because it can aid in preparing various types of 2D ultra-thin nanomaterials with non-layer structure without expensive top-down method. Wet-chemical synthesis can also be used to synthesize ultra-thin 2D nanomaterials with layered structure characteristics.99,100 Transition metal sulfide materials can be prepared in large quantities via this method. Therefore, this method has a good advantage in solution thermal synthesis of advanced functional materials such as energy catalysis. Synthesized 2D nanomaterials are easy to disperse in both organic and aqueous media. Thus they offer a board range of applications.
2.1.4. Chemical vapor deposition. The 2D material prepared by CVD has the advantages of high purity, good quality, and large area.101 It is a representative bottom-up method and a common III–V nanomaterials development technology. In the past decade, CVD method, as an effective and reliable method to prepare 2D ultra-thin nanomaterials with large area, has been continuously developed and recognized, which has significantly promoted the research on the application of 2D materials in the field of nano-electronic including photoelectric devices.44,102 In the cavity, precursors undergo high-temperature reactions or decomposition on the substrate surface, including oxidation reaction, reduction reaction, and redox reaction.103 Under appropriate experimental conditions, the required 2D nanomaterials can be obtained on the substrate. Compared to the low yield and productivity in micromechanical separation technologies, CVD can lead to industrial-scale production of materials, such as polysilicon.104 CVD technology is considered as a potential method to prepare ultra-thin 2D nanomaterials,105 which have excellent application prospects in the fields of electronics and optoelectronics.106,107 However, at present, CVD method still encounters some shortcomings, such as temperature, pressure, gas concentration, gas atmosphere, and other instability poles,108 which interfere with the experimental reaction process and uncontrollably affect the crystallinity and density of samples.109

2.2. Sensing mechanisms

The 2D material-based SGTs consist of three electrodes (source, drain, and gate), and a 2D-material channel, both of which are in direct contact with the electrolyte solution. Moreover, the source electrode is connected to the ground; and the drain electrode and the gate electrode are applied with different voltages. The electrolyte solution is conductive, thus the gate voltage corresponds to the voltage applied across the gate electrode/electrolyte interface and the electrolyte/channel interface. Both interfaces have an electronic double layer equivalent to a capacitor. In the case of other field-effect transistors (FETs), the channel current can be regulated by gate electrode field effect.

According to the above-mentioned formula, four parameters (μ, Ci, VG, and VT) can be changed to adjust the current of the device. The analyte may vary one or more of these parameters, and the possible sensing mechanisms are summarized as follows:

(1) Changes of the carrier mobility in 2D materials. The interaction between analytes and 2D material channels of the device may significantly change the properties of the 2D materials, in particular, carrier mobility and Fermi levels, which can change the performance of the device and be used as a basis for detection. In particular, the surface of some 2D materials is rich in oxygen-containing functional groups with strong chemical activity, which can be easily detected by using this principle. Many biosensors, such as DNA,112 cells,113 and bacterial sensors,114 are based on this mechanism. However, an obvious problem (poor selectivity) is encountered in this principle, which needs to be improved by surface modification technique.

(2) Changes of the gate capacitance. SGTs can be regarded as two electronic double layers (EDLs) capacitors, and the change of these EDL capacitors directly affects the performance of the device. The EDL capacitance is related to the ion concentration and charge distribution near the channel or gate electrode.

(3) Changes in the interfacial potential. Owing to the transistor's intrinsic amplification, the Dirac point of its transfer characteristic curve gets shifted due to the slight changes of channel or gate interface upon chemical or biochemical absorption of the analyte. Current research focuses on potential changes at channel interfaces, and almost all pH and ion sensors are based on this mechanism. Many biosensors, such as bacterial115 and DNA116 sensors, work in similar manner. However, the modification of channel can affect the performance and stability of the active layer, thus some studies use the change of grid interface potential for detection. Although little research has been done in this area, many other types of chemical and biosensors with functional gates will be implemented in the future.

(4) Changes in the electrolyte potential. Electrolytes are electrically conductive and therefore equipotential in most cases. However, in some special cases, potential drop exists in the electrolyte between gate and channel, which also affects the performance of devices.

2.3. Advantages

FETs with 2D materials as channel materials are expected to be widely applied in food safety, environmental monitoring, biological disease diagnosis, and other biosensor fields. Compared to other detection technologies, 2D FETs have advantages, including high sensitivity, high selectivity, no labeling, simple operating process, less reagent consumption, and rapid analysis, etc. It is extremely suitable for the detection of biological molecules, and it can be predicted that in the future it will play an increasingly important role for specific detection applications in the medical field. Compared to the 1D silicon nanomaterials such as nanowires and CNTs,117,118 2D materials are becoming increasingly important because of their unique properties such as high electron mobility, bipolar field effect, large SSA, high transconductance, and easy functional characteristics. These characteristics are more suitable for solution-gated FET biosensors. The SGTs can operate in electrolyte solutions, directly in contact with the target substance, which indicates that they are good at detecting biologically relevant molecules and reactions in the solution system.

Noteworthy, sensitivity is an essential parameter of sensors. In analytical chemistry, the detection limit is not only the minimum concentration or amount of the analyte but also an important parameter to describe the sensitivity of a biochemical sensor. The detection limit is usually defined as the concentration of analyte that can produce an output signal three times the standard deviation of the noise level, and the noise profoundly impacts sensitivity. In a typical graphene-effect transistor, the noise in the low-frequency region is inversely proportional to the frequency.20 The changes of the environment around the transistors do not significantly affect the signal-to-noise (S/N) ratio, thus the main factor affecting the S/N ratio is the internal quality of the devices.119 The SGTs have excellent sensitivity as a biosensor for the following reasons:

(1) Inherent signal sensitization of transistors. Transistor sensors act as both sensors and amplifiers. Even small changes in the surrounding environment can bring about significant changes in gate voltage and channel current. They are very sensitive to the small changes at the gate electrode/electrolyte interface and electrolyte/channel interface.

(2) Unique properties of 2D materials. 2D materials have higher carrier mobility than other semiconductors, such as organic semiconductors, silicon nanowires, and CNTs. Therefore, comparatively, 2D transistors have larger transconductance, which exhibits the ability of gate voltage to effectively regulate channel current. And it is also the critical reason for sensitization of transistor signal.

(3) Low working voltage of the transistor. The operating voltage of the transistors is usually below 1 V, so the sensors based on SGTs are easy to integrate and suitable for wearable applications.

(4) Transistor miniaturization. SGTs can be perfectly miniaturized without compromising the performance of the sensor and without losing S/N ratio. The channel current is merely related to the ratio of width to length of the channel and has nothing to do with the actual size of the channel.

3. Solution-gated transistors of two-dimensional materials for chemical and biological sensors

3.1. Graphene

Philip Wallace120 explored the unusual electronic structure of graphene in 1947. However, influenced by the prediction that 2D crystal materials could not exist because of thermodynamic instability at room temperature, graphene was only used as a 2D theoretical model and did not receive extensive attention. In 2004, Novoselov and Geim in the UK first obtained graphene and studied its properties for the first time.14 Graphene is a monolayer structure formed by sp2 hybridized carbon atoms covalently bonded in a honeycomb lattice. Moreover, the distance between two adjacent carbon atoms in a single sheet is approximately 1.42 Å. The rapid adoption of graphene as a material of interest is attributed to its unique thermal, optical, electronic, mechanical, physical, and chemical characteristics as follows:

(1) Large SSA: According to the theoretical calculation, the SSA of graphene is 2630 m2 g−1. Owing to the large SSA, graphene may be used in chemical catalytic processes as a catalyst or its nano-carrier.

(2) High electronic mobility: Graphene has a stable lattice structure, thus it has higher conductivity and less resistance among the existing 2D materials. At room temperature, the electrical conductivity of graphene is twice as high as that of indium antimonide, the semiconductor material currently used. Graphene has significant potential in the field of electronics and is likely to replace silicon in the future to produce electronic semiconductor devices with better performance and lower loss.

(3) The mechanical properties: Graphene has both good strength and toughness. Unlike its electrical properties, its mechanical properties are affected by temperature changes. Moreover, fascinating features of graphene such as tensile limit strain and young's modulus vary depending on temperature.

(4) The thermal properties: Graphene has a thermal conductivity of up to 5300 W m−1 K−1, more than twice that of conventional diamond materials (2200 W m−1 K−1). However, many difficulties are encountered in making graphene without defects. Through the study of single-atom defects, it was found that the graphite containing defects was significantly affected by the defect density. The thermal conductivity decreased sharply with the increase of defects.

(5) The optical properties: The transmittance of graphene monolayer over a broad range of wavelengths is 97.7%, and it does not vary with the wavelength of light. Theoretical and experimental results reveal that the large area of graphene film endows such excellent optical properties. The excellent light transmittance combined with ultra-high conductivity and elasticity makes graphene have a good application prospect in electronic devices.121–124

The significant advantages of highly conductive graphene include large SSA, high mechanical strength, and good production potential, which provide an excellent platform for the chemical sensing application. These unique electronic characteristics make it possible to produce high-performance conductive semiconductor devices using graphene as the channel material. The graphene surfaces are combined with a variety of analytes through van der Waals forces, electron transfer, or covalent bonds. Therefore, graphene has several uses as an electrocatalyst for electrical sensing applications. The high surface-volume ratio of graphene is attributed to the fact that each carbon atom in graphene can be regarded as a surface atom, which provides the largest superficial area per unit volume. Thus, the chemical environment has a significant influence on charge transfer by graphene. Graphene is also characterized with excellent flexibility and mechanical strength, making it compatible with flexible, elastic, and even conformal-performance electronic devices, which make it a promising candidate for a wearable electronic material.

Graphene's surface is essential for intermolecular interactions; therefore, the most important and direct way to regulate the graphene sensing performance is to control and manipulate its surface chemistry. In order to enhance the specific action and strength, various covalent and non-covalent methods have been used to modify its surface. The molecular level adjustment of the graphene surface can be carried out by doping or adding functional groups after synthesis and by high levels of control to reduce defects in structure and composition, which can improve its sensing performance for functional device manufacturing. The sensitivity and selectivity of graphene materials can be further improved by integrating graphene materials with other functional nanostructures, including metal and metal oxide nanoparticles, organic acceptors, conductive polymer, protein, or nucleic acid. Graphene should be considered in the application of biochemical sensors, surface chemistry and species, lattice defect and modification of electronic property, as well as in customization of each property.

3.1.1.1. pH sensors. pH is an essential parameter in many biochemical reactions. Bergveld's ion-sensitive FET made on silicon in 1970 was the first transistor-based pH sensor. Since 2008, SGGTs have been researched and used in pH sensors.125

Wang et al.130 deposited HfO2 film (20 nm) and SiO2 film (25 nm), as a passivation layer, respectively, on the graphene film (Fig. 1a and b). The top layer of SiO2 film was used as a sensing layer, while the bottom layer of high-quality HfO2 film protected the channel from the influence of electrolyte. The devices maintained high carrier mobility (5000 cm2 V−1 s−1) and improved the stability and reproducibility of the graphene sensor. They also used 3-aminopropyltrimethoxysilane (APTMS) to functionalize the top layer of SiO2 film to allow immobilization of proteins and DNA. To discuss the principle of the device, they demonstrated a bare device and APTMS-functionalized device for pH sensing. Both devices were found to be sensitive to the pH of phosphate buffer saline (PBS) solutions in the range of 4–9 and the sensitivity of 46 and 34 mV per pH was acquired, respectively. They further demonstrated that the application of solution gate voltage to a pulse resulted in the elimination of hysteresis phenomenon in the graphene transducer transfer curve, leading to a significant increase in ion potential resolution of sensors. Their research opened the door to the selective detection of biomolecules using graphene-based devices.

Lee et al.132 found that the influence of unknown ions in the detection solution environment must be considered in order to comprehensively understand the graphene sensor. They prepared several groups of pH buffer solutions (pH = 6–8), which provided various ions within a specific concentration range, and studied the electrical response of these SGGTs. In contrast to previous results, the Dirac point voltage (VDirac) moved horizontally to the negative voltage with the increase in pH in the selected series of buffers. Moreover, significantly different VDirac values were observed in buffer solutions with the same pH values, but different ion composition and concentration. These results indicated that the sensitivity of the device to different pH values was not simply caused by changes in the concentration of hydroxyl or hydrogen ions. Instead, it must be considered that the buffer layer screens for the ability to charge impurities in graphene. Fig. 1c shows that an abnormal negative VDirac shift was found in high concentration buffer, while only a typical positive VDirac shift was observed in low concentration buffer. The results reveal that graphene transistors cannot be successfully applied for pH detection if the solution composition is not calibrated. This report offered valuable insight into the work of graphene transistors and provided appropriate guidance for the practical application of chemical or biological graphene sensors.

Kwon et al.134 fabricated a graphene-based electrolyte-gated pH sensor and introduced defects into channels to investigate the effect of engineering edge defects in the graphene mesh FETs. Furthermore, in order to reduce the side effects associated with graphene patterns, they directly synthesized the network-structured graphene using silica as a growth mask. At the copper–silica interface, due to the dissociation of carbon atoms, a hexagonal tightly wrapped single layer of silicon spheres overlying the copper foil and the graphene grid was formed. Compared to FETs, single-layer graphene is grown on copper by CVD. Fig. 1d shows that the resulting mesh is a set of hexagonal arrays with circular holes with an average diameter of 300 nm and a spacing of 600 nm. A cross-sectional view of the electrolyte-gated graphene-based FET sensor is shown in Fig. 1e. Compared to graphene FETs, graphene mesh FETs were characterized by a significant increase in Dirac point displacement (30–100 mV per pH) due to electrostatic gating of hydrogen ions. More importantly, when the defect sites are completely inactivated, the Dirac point displacements of the two sensors driven only by the graphene surface are similar, both in the range of 6–8 mV per pH. This result explained the significant differences in pH response reported for graphene FETs and the need to control graphene defects for credible multi-periodic sensor operations.

These studies clearly show that the pH sensors can be realized using SGGTs and the relationship between the pH value of the sensor and the gate voltage shifts basically conforms to the Nernst equation.155 Although these devices show a very large response, and their selectivity still requires improvement due to the limitations of the functionalization of graphene surfaces. For example, the interference of some ions, in particular, the pH detection in the case of complex ionic components in the solution, has not been well studied.


3.1.1.2. Ion sensors. Ion sensors have essential applications in environmental monitoring, food safety, and human health. Thus, investigation of the interaction between ions and graphene is of particular importance, which could be used as a basis for biosensors because these devices work in aqueous solutions. In recent years, except for pH sensors, various ion sensors fabricated based on graphene transistors have attracted extensive research attention.

An et al.150 reported a graphene-based flexible adapter sensor that could be used to detect Hg2+ ions with high selectivity and sensitivity (Fig. 2a). Graphene monolayer produced by the CVD method was effectively transferred onto a flexibly bendable substrate, and a specific adaptor for binding mercury ions was modified on the graphene surface. Furthermore, the sensor exhibited good performances in the detection of the actual sample (Fig. 2c). This study provides an advanced method for mercury analysis equipment, which was expected to be applied to analyze real samples.

Graphene-based materials have many application prospects in ion electrochemical sensing. However, the structure and defects of graphene and the effects of chemical functionalization on the electrical sensing properties of prepared analytical devices undeniably need further study. Therefore, new chemical strategies are required to adapt the physical and chemical properties of graphene to the desired sensing properties for target applications.

3.1.2.1. Glucose sensors. Glucose is an irreplaceable part of metabolic activity. The blood glucose test has great significance to the human body, in particular, for diagnosing diabetes.189 Nowadays, glucose biosensors dominate most of the biosensors market. The research on electrochemical glucose sensors has been carried out for more than 50 years.190

Park et al.158 demonstrated a fast-response and high-sensitivity FET sensor that utilized reduced graphene oxide (rGO)–carboxylatedpolypyrrole (C-PPy) nanotube (NT) hybrids as conductive channels, which responded rapidly to the detection of glucose with high sensitivity and specificity. Comparative analysis of the characteristics of rGO, C-PPy NTs, and rGO/C-PPy NT hybrids indicated the successful preparation of the expected structure based on the specific interaction between graphene sheets and C-PPy NTs. The rGO/C-PPy NTs formed a reliable electrical contact that was established between the patterned-microelectrodes and remained stable even when exposed to liquid electrolyte. Owing to the bridge between graphene layers, which was formed by the insertion of the C-PPy NTs leading to effective electron transport, these rGO/C-PPy NT hybrids showed higher hole transportation ability than C-PPy NTs and graphene sheets. Glucose oxidase (GOx) was used as a capture probe to bind tightly to C-PPy NTs by chemical coupling reaction, and rGO/C-PPy NTs hybridization was used to catalyze glucose oxidation of GOx in the FET biosensor to detect glucose. The synergistic effects between graphene and C-PPy NTs, including enhanced conductivity, p-type semiconductor performance, and surface area, resulted in good sensitivity (LOD = 1 nM, S/R = 3.22) and fast real-time response (<1 s) for glucose sensing. Compared to the non-hybrid C-PPy NTs, the sensing performance of rGO/C-PPy NT hybrid was increased by 5 orders of magnitude, and the detection limit was 2–3 orders of magnitude higher than the sensitivity of glucose sensor reported in previous studies. Besides, FET biosensors show both good reproducibility and air stability. The specificity of biosensors to glucose was also demonstrated by the characterization of their reactions in biological fluids involving uric acid (UA) and ascorbic acid (AA). The FET biosensor based on rGO/C-PPy NTs shows a broad range of application prospects in many fields.

Zhang et al.159 presented a novel solution-gated graphene transistors (SGGTs)-based glucose sensor that used CVD-grown graphene to form its channels and grids (Fig. 3a and b). Graphene, a semi-metallic material, was used not only as a semiconductor channel but also as a gate electrode for SGGTs. Therefore, the sensor can be considered as a full graphene device. The graphene gate electrodes were modified using the enzyme GOx and two biocompatible polymers, namely, chitosan (CHIT) and Nafion. The graphene gate was changed with Pt nanoparticles (PtNPs), and the electrocatalytic activity of the electrode and the sensitivity were remarkably improved. Full graphene SGGTs with PtNPs-functionalized graphene gate electrodes were used to detect H2O2 with the detection limit down to 30 nm. Further, the optimized detection limit of glucose sensor was 0.5 μM with great selectivity, so as to achieve sufficiently sensitive detection of glucose in body fluids such as saliva. Fig. 3c illustrates that the sensing mechanism was the result of GOx catalyzing the oxidation of glucose. This is ascribed to the fact that GOx catalyzes the oxidation of H2O2 and H2O2 produced by glucose oxidation near the gate electrode, thereby adjusting the effective gate voltage applied to the transmission resistance. As a potential sensor, the sensitivity of SGGT sensor depends on its transconductance, and SGGT sensor has significantly higher transconductance than that of traditional transistors. Moreover, the device can be easily manufactured at low cost. The device has a very simple structure and could be cut with a layer of graphene to fabricate transistor arrays quickly.

3.1.2.2. DNA sensors. DNA is a primary genetic material of most organisms, the center of life chemistry, and the key to the study of life phenomena. The research on DNA sensor is to meet the needs of rapid, convenient, highly sensitive, and highly selective detection of specific DNA sequences. Electrochemical DNA sensors have become a very active research field due to their sensitivity, capacity, low cost, and low energy consumption. Detection of nucleic acid molecules, in particular, short DNA strands is challenging, thus many studies have focused on the use of SGGTs in unlabeled DNA sensors.

Fu et al.167 fabricated a frequency-doubling biochemical sensor device based on bipolar characteristics of SGGT (Fig. 4a), which provided a new tenability and flexibility for designing graphene-based biochemical sensors. By applying a bias voltage to the ambipolar devices in the standard source structure, the sinusoidal voltage applied at a specific frequency on the electrolyte gate can be rectified into the sinusoidal wave of double frequency on the drain. Strong electrolyte gate coupling and ultra-high carrier mobility of graphene provided unprecedented gain (unit gain) and purity (over 95% of the total output energy) for graphene bipolar multipliers. Unoptimized devices showed an impressive 11-mer single-strand probe DNA (ssDNA) molecules detection limit of 4 pM in 1 mM PBS buffer solution with an ionic strength of above 20 mM. The principle was that the graphene frequency multiplier could detect single-stranded DNA molecules with a negatively charged short chain attached to the surface of graphene. The sensitivity of the devices was excellent due to the enhanced drift characteristics of the graphene bipolar frequency multiplier and improved low-frequency 1/f noise performance.

Li et al.192 exploited graphene electrochemical transistors with a functionalized gate to achieve sensitive label-free detection of DNA. The study provided a low-cost and simple method for highly sensitive detection of DNA. However, the graphene transfer process may result in fluctuations in SGGT performance.

Currently, DNA can be detected by molecular modification on graphene in the channel. However, graphene modification encounters certain limitations. In contrast, the gate of SGGT is generally Au metal, and it is relatively easy to assemble DNA probes on Au. Therefore, the method of gate-modified DNA probes may be a convenient and efficient method for detecting DNA in the future.

3.2. Transition metal dichalcogenides

2D TMDs, due to their atomic thickness, adjustable band gap width,199 excellent mechanical properties,200–202 and good biological safety, have broad application prospects in the fields of transient electronics,203 bioresorbable electronics, and clinical medicine.204,205 MX2 is the acknowledged chemical formula of TMDs, where M denotes transition metal element (such as Ti, Hf of group IVB; Ta and Nb of group VB; and Mo and W of group VIB, etc.), and X represents chalcogen (S, Se, and Te, etc.). As a new type of inorganic non-metallic materials, synthesis methods of TMDs mainly include mechanical exfoliation, liquid-phase exfoliation, molecular beam epitaxy (MBE), atomic layer deposition (ALD), CVD, and so on. Samples with different qualities are widely applied in electronic devices, sensors with high sensitivity, drug delivery, tumor therapy, biological imaging, etc. The absence of a bandgap in graphene restricts the sensitivity of SGGTs; however, solution-gated TMDs transistors, represented by MoS2, are prominent in the field of chemical and biological sensing because of their high sensitivity, easy patterning ability, and simple device fabrication.
3.2.1. Chemical sensors. A chemical sensor is a sensing platform that converts a sample component or other chemical information into electrical signals. In most TMD-based SGTs chemical sensors, the sensing mechanism involves the mutual effects between TMDs materials and analytes, which cause changes in electric potential. Then changes in channel currents are converted immediately. At present, pH sensor and ion sensor are the two main trends of TMD-based SGT chemical sensors.

The pH sensing is based on the process of protonation (engendering a positive charge) and deprotonation (generating a negative charge) of hydroxyl group in the solution. Both processes can change the surface potential of the gate dielectric. In a solution with a lower pH, the hydroxyls are more likely to undergo protonation. The higher the pH value, inversely, the easier the deprotonation of surface hydroxyls. Under a specifically applied bias with varying pH, the MoS2 pH sensors were successfully illustrated via a significant current change. Sarkar et al.206 developed a solution-gated MoS2 (2 nm) FET as a pH sensor. Sensing sensitivity of pH was usually inferred from the curves in subthreshold, saturation, and linear regions. Among them, the subthreshold region exhibited much higher sensitivity than other regions, which indicates that the subthreshold swing (SS, SS = dVGS/dlog10(ID)) is a critical parameter for pH sensors. The excellent SS (78 mV per decade) and high sensitivity are the results of the combination of the ultrathin property of MoS2, solution-gated structure, and original interface. When pH ranged from 3 to 9, the sensitivity ceiling achieved 713. Compared to SGGTs as pH sensors, TMD-based SGTs exhibit better performance in sensitivity because of the adjustable bandgap, which compensates for the shortcomings of TMDs such as low conductivity and electrostatics susceptivity.

For ion sensors, compared to the reported SGGTs used to detect a variety of ions such as Na+, K+, Mg2+, and Pb2+; TMD-based SGT chemical sensors expand the range of ions, such as As3+, Hg2+. The detection sensitivity, response time, ion selectivity, and other properties of TMD-based SGT are improved compared to those of previously reported sensors based on traditional electrochemical methods. An and Jang207 described the fabrication of SGT Apt-sensors for As3+ detection, based on CPPy-coated flower-like MoS2 nanosphere (CFMNS) (Fig. 7a). They utilized vapor deposition to form CFMNS and conjugated Ars-3 to it (Apt-CFMNS). Then Apt-CFMNS was conformed into a solution-gated system with a liquid electrolyte composed of 10 mM PBS solution. Noteworthy, the solution-gated system can effectively work under relatively low gate voltages, which can availably reduce the harm to sensor structure, provide low-power dissipation, and prevent redox reactions in water, ionic liquid, or other solutions. The measurement shows that CFMNSs SGT sensors have an extraordinary performance of response: less than 1 s (Fig. 7b). At the same time, because of the solution-gated structure, the target ionic solution directly contacts the channel material without a dielectric. Therefore, the direct reciprocity between aptamers and As3+ led to the variation of field-induced current, and the sensor exhibited an improved detection limit (1 pM) and a low S/N ratio of 4.57. Compared to that of conventional sensors, sensitivity of TMDs-based SGT sensors is 2–4 orders of magnitude higher (Fig. 7c).

The conductance of MoS2 changed regularly in Hg2+ solutions with different concentrations. It has been demonstrated that solution-gated MoS2 FETs can work as ultra-sensitive Hg2+ sensors with the remarkable detection limit of 30 pM (Fig. 7f) and an on–off ratio exceeding 106. In 2010, Zhang et al.209 presented SGGTs as Pb2+ ion sensors employing AuNPs to modify the surface of graphene. In contrast, MoS2 SGT does not need further modification of MoS2, which makes the manufacturing costs and difficulties associated with MoS2-based Pb2+ ion sensors much lower than those of graphene. Many studies have shown that the TMDs-based SGT has extraordinary potential in highly sensitive and point-of-care testing (POCT) of ions in solution environments.

The enormous possibility of TMDs in chemical sensing is partly attributed to the unique chemical and physical capabilities of TMDs. Moreover, the design of the solution-gated structure also has a meaningful impact. Ultra-thin 2D layered semiconductive materials such as graphene and TMDs produce new electrical properties of carriers because of the quantum confinement effect and quantum tunneling effect. Thus, nowadays, most transistors are fabricated with ultra-thin channel materials. In other words, it reflects the inadequacy of traditional gate-control strategy in controlling channel conductance of thicker materials. For example, the back-gate via thick dielectrics and channels provides relatively weak gate coupling and field penetration. Therefore, thick channels cannot achieve a high switching current ratio in generic devices.211 SGTs may provide potential strategies to overcome these challenges. Huang et al.210 put forward that deionized (DI) water could act as a solution top gate for layered materials field-effect switching, including SnS2, BP, and MoS2, for the detection of glucose. Fig. 7 g exhibits the devices built by them, which consist of layered SnS2, which was exfoliated from bulk to a thickness of 80–100 layers (50–60 nm). Fig. 7i demonstrates that the electric field applied through SiO2 back gate could not effectively turn off the SnS2 channel of this thickness even with a large voltage. However, the channel was effectively closed under the solution top-gate structure, with on–off ratios of up to 105 (Fig. 7h). The DI water gate or other solution gates are facilely fabricated and can withstand rapid bias changes because the efficient coupling between electric field and layered materials provides near-ideal SS, high on–off current ratios, and enhanced short channel behavior even for FETs with bulk-like active materials. Nonetheless, such gate coupling and control is an elusive dream with a conventional back-gating structure.

For ultra-thin TMDs FET, the solution-gated structure can further improve the carrier mobility of materials. Perera et al.212 reported the manufacture of ionic solution-gated MoS2 (3 nm thick) FET with ambipolar behavior. With the decrease in temperature to 77 K, the mobility was more than 220 cm2 V−1 s−1, and on–off ratios reached 107 and 104 for electrons and holes, respectively. The enhancements of mobility and a near-optimal SS of 50 mV per decade are attributed to the solution gate. Solution gates play roles as ultra-thin dielectrics which effectively reduce the Schottky barrier of MoS2/metal contacts. It can be said that the solution-gated structure provides another way to construct FETs with good performance: through a vast electric field on the channel surface via a solution gate, a valid on–off switch for the thicker channel is feasible. It is not necessary to reduce the channel thickness to the ultimate 2D utmost for many materials.

Compared to ultra-thin single-layer or few-layer devices, SGTs with thicker channels213,214 are relatively insensitive to the thickness fluctuation of the active layer.215,216 Whether it is a few-layer or a multi-layer device, the solution-gated structure can availably improve the carrier mobility of channel materials, afford near-ideal SS, weaken the Schottky barrier, and thus the resistance of metal/semiconductor junctions.217,218 To a certain extent, SGTs overcome the limitation that TMDs transistors must use high-quality, ultra-thin channel materials, which significantly reduces the manufacturing cost. We believe that it will facilitate the manufacture of high-yielding chemical sensors shortly.

3.2.2. Biological sensors. Represented by MoS2, WS2 and WSe2, TMDs have become quintessential channels for biological sensors from the perspective of both security and device function. Owing to the excellent biological safety, the research on 2D TMDs is flourishing in flexible electronics, biomedicine, implantable biosensors, etc. Hurt et al.219 tested the cytotoxicity of exfoliated MoS2 nanoflakes and molybdate (MoS2 degradation product) on mouse macrophages and human lung epithelial cells. They measured the critical values of the non-toxic dose of MoS2 and molybdate, which were far lower than MoS2 content in one transistor. Chae et al.220 evaluated the cytotoxicity and genetic mutagenesis of MoS2 and WS2, and demonstrated that mechanical stripping and CVD-grown TMDs with low dose did not induce harmful effects on cell viability and genetic defects in a short time. Moreover, Chen et al.221 conducted a subcutaneous injection of MoS2 to test the tissue immune response and long-term biocompatibility of TMDs. No change was observed in CD4+T, B, and other lymphocytes that might trigger the immune response in the implanted area, which proved that transient MoS2 was non-toxic and compatible with bioelectronics. Many studies have demonstrated that TMDs have excellent biological compatibility222 and relatively low cytotoxicity, which guarantees the low risks and fewer side effects of TMDs-based SGTs as biological sensors.

Low concentration detection (LCD) of biomolecules such as polypeptide and protein, has emerged as a hot research topic in biosensing. Graphene gas sensors can detect a single target molecule by directly absorbing it, showing excellent sensitivity. However, the mechanism may also result in non-specificity.223 In contrast, good selectivity and interference immunity make the TMD-based SGT progressively more attractive in biosensing. In the case of CNTs, it is complex and complicates to create a bandgap, which leads to low yield.224 However, TMDs such as MoS2, even with 5 nm channel length, can maintain brilliant gate coupling over the channel to maintain a near-ideal SS. Thus, TMDs are highly conducive for the low-cost manufacture and scale-down of biosensors and can facilitate the fabrication of low-power and high-density biological sensors.225 In general, TMD-based SGTs not only retain the LCD capability but also show good interference immunity and high integration, which makes TMD-based SGTs become an ideal platform for biosensing.

Sarkar et al.206,226,227 employed four-layer MoS2 (2.7 nm thick) to fabricate SGT biosensor for the detection of biomolecules, as shown in Fig. 8a and b. Once the streptavidin PBS solution was added and target molecules were captured, a notable decrease in current occurred. In a specific detection experiment, on the addition of the nonspecific biotin (IgG) at pH 4.75, the current change of the biotin-functionalized devices did not unfold visually. To obtain improved SS and, thus, sensitivity, ultra-thin (2.7 nm) MoS2 was explored. Even under a thick dielectric (35 nm), an excellent SS of 90 mV per decade was obtained, and sensitivity (ΔI/I0) of 196 was achieved for 100 fM streptavidin solution (Fig. 8c). Yang and coworkers228 also used multi-layer MoS2 to fabricate an SGT biosensor for detecting prostate-specific antigens (PSA, a cancer marker protein). The specific binding events between PSA and receptor antibodies prompted the obvious change of drain current and improved the sensitivity and response rate. In another work, Lee et al.229 simplified the fabrication of MoS2 (n-type, 20–80 nm) FET biosensors, making direct use of the highly hydrophobic MoS2 surface for PSA detection (Fig. 8d). In their study, the hydrophobic MoS2 surface (the contact angle 75.77°) allowed for the adsorption of physical molecules on the sensor surface. The fluctuation of the off-current for anti-PSA modified MoS2 transistors decreased visibly, and a good minimum concentration (1 pg mL−1) was obtained (Fig. 8e and f).

TMDs have been the popular materials for chemical and biological sensing because the TMD-based SGTs have great possibilities in sensitivity and selection specificity to some biological molecules; however, the types of molecules to detect have yet to be explored. Besides, more attention should be paid to work stability, reliability, service life, and other issues related to TMDs SGT. These sensors have potential applications such as flexible wearable devices, body surface sensors, and implantable sensors. As label-free, ultra-sensitive chemical and biological sensors with low-cost manufacturing and low-energy modes, TMD-based SGTs can provide great assistance in medical, biosecurity, legal medicine and guarantee the security of environment, and water supplies.

3.3. Few-layer black phosphorus

As a representative layered material, BP has attracted extensive attention in the field of batteries,230,231 photodetectors,232 fiber lasers,233–241 biomedicine,242–244 and basic and applied research.245,246 Among various FET channel materials, few-layer BP is also considered as promising candidate material because of its remarkable electrical properties. BP-based transistors can exhibit a larger current on–off ratio (103 to 105)247,248 than graphene transistors, and demonstrate higher carrier mobility (∼1000 cm2 V−1 s−1)249 than that of TMDs FETs. Moreover, considerable molecule adsorption energies, direct bandgap, large surface-to-volume ratios, and facile fabrication processes make BP have broad development prospects for chemical and biological sensing.
3.3.1. Chemical sensors. As chemical sensors, through the surveillance for the resistance change prompted by the adsorption of ions or molecules,250 BP FETs exhibit high sensitivity and accuracy for the label-free detection of target analytes in the circumstances with ultra-low concentration.

BP chemical sensors have good selectivity and sensitivity for many inimical ions in water, such as As3+, Ag+, and Hg2+. Zhou et al.251 developed a BP-based electronic sensor for As3+ real-time (less than 2 s) detection (Fig. 9a). Under the interference of other ions such as Ca2+, Cd2+, Cu2+, and Mg2+, the BP sensor showed excellent selectivity for the detection of water samples (Fig. 9b). The detection limit of 1 nM was achieved, which fully meets the testing biosecurity requirements of water samples. Wang et al.252 fabricated high-performance BP transistors for the detection of Ag+. They demonstrated that Ag+ could be absorbed on the BP surface and be detected rapidly (60 s) because of the conjugated π bond (Fig. 9c), and a low detection limit (10−10 mol L−1) was obtained.

However, BP sensors reported so far have been mainly fabricated or transferred on substrates, where the dangling bonds pervade. Moreover, the scattering caused by defects on the substrate surface reduces the carrier mobility of few-layer BP and drastically exasperates its electrical properties and chemical stability.253 Li et al.254 first developed BP FETs with a solution-gated free-standing structure to overcome the disadvantages of the traditional BP-based transistors mentioned above. The transistors exhibited significant enhancements for the detection of Hg2+ with extremely low concentration in the absence of substrate. In this study, mechanically exfoliated few-layer BP flakes were transferred onto Si substrate (300 nm SiO2) (Fig. 9d). After the electrode deposition was completed, they applied 1:6 BOE to etch SiO2 layer beneath BP and created the suspended structure. The BP SGTs sensors could sense Hg2+ down to 0.01 ppb (far lower than the tolerance level recommended by WHO) with a response rate of 3 s (Fig. 9e and f). Comparative experiments proved that the detection results of Hg2+ would not be disturbed due to the interfering ions, including Na+, K+, and Ag+. A stability test was carried out in a nitrogen environment (1% relative humidity) as well. As a result, the BP transistors were still in good order 3 days later, with IDS variation of less than 5%.

Li et al.53 reported an air-stable BP chemical sensor for ion detection in 2015. In that device, BP was encapsulated with ionophore coating, and over the next 30 days, no significant degradation occurred. Although the air-stability of BP could be improved, it was far from enough compared to other chemical sensors,255 in particular, when BP sensors worked in humid environments or solution. This drawback significantly limits the practical application of BP-based SGTs in the field of chemical sensors. Thus, how to enhance the stability of BP without compromising its performance and capacity is still an enormous challenge.

3.3.2. Biological sensors. Zhang et al.256 reported that the puckered surface morphology between proteins and BP film produced unique interaction mechanisms. The interaction plays an important role in the generation of ultra-sensitivity toward biomolecules and indicates that BP possesses potential applications in biosensors.

Chen et al.257 used the mechanical exfoliation method to fabricate BP nanosheets (30–40 nm) for human IgG detection. The typical mechanically exfoliated magnitude of BP nanosheet was in the range from several hundred nanometers to several microns. Further, the antibody probe was conjugated with AuNP (5 nm) and then was sputtered on the BP. Under a constant bias (VDS = 0.01 V) across the electrodes, the response curves (IDS) of BP-based biosensors were recorded. The sensitivity of the BP device increased from 5 to 10% when the concentration of IgG increased from 10 to 500 ng mL−1 (Fig. 9g and h). This study demonstrated both a low detection limit (10 ng mL−1) and selective specificity to IgG (Fig. 9i). Wood et al.258 found that the atomic layer deposition of AlOx was an effective method to passivate the surface of BP nanosheet. The BP device covered by the Al2O3 passivation layer on the surface could maintain the desired on–off current ratio and mobility over 14 days under standard conditions.

At present, the fabrication of passivation layers is a temporary strategy to improve the stability of BP-based biosensors. However, the existence of passivation layers can reduce the sensitivity and extend the response time, thus it is an obstacle for the realization of high-performance biosensors. Moreover, the cytotoxicity and biocompatibility of BP are still debatable, which fundamentally restricts the development of BP-based SGTs as biological sensors.

3.4. Other two-dimensional layered materials

3.4.1. Chemical and biological sensors. Except 2D layered materials mentioned above (graphene, TMDs, and BP), other 2D materials can be used to construct SGTs as chemical or biological sensors, which have been demonstrated in fiber lasers,260–276 biomedicine,277 and cancer therapy.278

Yoshizawa et al.279 demonstrated the use of InP-based SGTs in acid electrolytes. The carrier concentration of epitaxial InP was 8 × 1016 cm−3, and Hall mobility was as high as 2700 cm2 V−1 s−1. Before it was utilized as a pH sensor, it was indispensable to evaluate and calibrate the linear region transfer characteristics (VG = 0 V, VD = 0.5 V). In the case of using H3PO4 electrolytes, the higher sensitivity (57 mV per pH) was acquired, which approached the theoretical value. However, when the H3PO4 electrolyte was replaced with HCl, the lower sensitivity (48 mV per pH) was obtained. In this research, SiN films (H+ selective membrane) of different thickness varying from 2 to 7 nm were employed, and it was found that the charged interface states acted as an essential factor affecting potential controllability of SGT and enhancing the sensitivity of chemical or biological sensors.

Majd et al.280 developed a new solution-gated FET biosensor (Fig. 10a) for label-free gene detection of HIV-1 using RF magnetron sputtering p-type NiO thin film (∼5–40 nm, band gaps: ∼3.0–3.58 eV, respectively). On the surface of NiO, HIV-1, 5′-amino-modified probe ssDNA was immobilized by a covalent bond. Electrical measurements showed that NiO SGTs provided high carrier mobility (>8.0 × 10−2 cm2 V−1 s−1 at VG = −0.1 V and VDS = −1.0 V), superior SS (312.67 mV per decade), and ideal on–off ratio (1.09 × 106) certainly. To summarize the advantages of the NiO and SGT, efficient HIV DNA sensors accomplished the detection of target HIV-1 DNA at the linear region from 1 aM to 10 nM with an LCD of 0.3 aM (Fig. 10b). The proposed biosensor exhibited a high percentage recovery (103.5–108.2%) in real blood samples. Thus it offers a reliable approach for quantitative and preventative assessment of HIV-1 target. These outcomes demonstrate that NiO SGT can provide new opportunities and openings in advanced medical diagnostics.

MXene, as a new member of the 2D material family, has a hydrophilic surface and good conductivity as high as that of metals. Its good electrical, mechanical, and chemical properties and cost-effective machinability make it a promising material in the fields of energy storage and electrochemical sensing, and it also has great potential for biosensing. Xu et al.282 reported an MXene-micropattern-based FET biosensor for monitoring neural activity (Fig. 10e and f). Neuronal growth was achieved on MXene, which demonstrated the biocompatibility of MXene. Compared to the calcium-sensitive dye (Fluo-4) for the detection of neural activity, a better temporal resolution was exhibited on the MXene SGT system.

In a word, the importance of solution-gated structure to SGT sensors is demonstrated herein, not simply relying on the superior channel materials. The solution-gate also provides a reliable guarantee and guidance for other 2D materials in the field of chemical and biological sensing.

3.4.2. Other applications. The solution gate has been pervasively taken to induce many intriguing physical properties by arising the carrier density through vast electric fields on materials.283,284 For one, superconductivity of large varieties of materials, including KTaO3, ZrNCl, and La2−xSrxCuO4, etc. has been implemented using the solution gate strategy.285–290 Furthermore, the magnetic phase transition has also been achieved via the solution gate.291,292 Lately, Chen et al.281 conducted solution gating experiments on ferromagnetic 2D Cr2Ge2Te6 (CGT), and explored the character of oxygen in the solution gating effect (Fig. 10c). The results proved that oxygen did not affect the solution gate on the CGT SGT but only affected leakage current of the solution gate. It provides a potential possibility for SGT to be used for the detection of O2 and other gases (Fig. 10d). Choi et al.293 explored that the superconducting critical temperature (Tc) could be modulated in the Nb film-based SGT. Tc could be degraded or enhanced depending on positive or negative VG applied. Furthermore, Maruyama et al.294 reported in situ modulations of the Tc of LiTi2O4 (LTO) thin films by solution gate. LTO thin films (Tc of 11.5 K) were synthesized on MgAl2O4 substrates. Double-layer LTO was integrated into a transistor system utilizing a solution with or without Li+. The present observation indicates the feasibility of modulations of superconductive properties by reversible lithiation and delithiation.

To sum up, by a solution gating configuration, the present studies imply the possibility of reversible modulation of superconductivity, magnetic properties, and many other alluring physical and chemical properties. It makes SGT a promising and practical high-efficiency device in many fields besides chemical biosensors.

3.5. Summary

Different 2D materials show different characteristics, and sensor performance is the extension of material properties. Thus, it is necessary to compare and summarize the advantages and disadvantages of 2D materials and SGT sensors (Scheme 2). As one of the earliest 2D materials, graphene exhibits ultra-high carrier mobility, remarkable chemical stability, and excellent biocompatibility. These advantages endow SGGTs with high sensitivity and make SGGTs suitable for the extremely low concentration detection. However, the zero bandgap of graphene indicates that SGGTs cannot implement the switch, and thus show the lack of selectivity. TMDs constitute a new class of 2D materials with low cytotoxicity and excellent mechanical properties. These two factors make TMDs have a broad range of potential applications in flexible electronic devices and implantable sensors. Moreover, the adjustable bandgap plays a significant role in the wonderful selective specificity of TMD-based SGTs. However, the low carrier mobility and biodegradability restrict the response rate and service life of TMDs-based sensors, respectively. BP has more distinct strengths and weaknesses than graphene and TMDs. First, BP possesses higher carrier mobility than graphene and maintains a direct bandgap, which makes BP become the first choice for electrochemical sensors. Nonetheless, the poor chemical stability and uncertain cytotoxicity limit the performance of BP-based SGTs as biological sensors. The main development strategies of sensors are to further expand the advantages of sensors or make up the obvious disadvantages.

4. Conclusions and outlooks

Graphene, TMDs, BP, and other 2D materials certainly have disadvantages whether they act as materials or sensors. Nonetheless, these materials still have good application prospects in the manufacturing of chemical and biological sensors, in particular, for constructing SGT sensors. Recently, with the rapid development of high quality and low-cost feasible methods for synthesis of 2D materials, large-scale manufacture of SGT sensors capable of complex and diversified functions has become a reality. SGGTs are hypersensitive to electriferous analytes or electric fields due to the electronic properties of graphene, which endow SGGTs with high sensitivity and ability of miniaturization. Compared to SGGTs and BP-based SGTs, TMD-based SGTs do not possess high mobility, but lower cytotoxicity and better biodegradability make such devices present more significant potential in the field of biosensors. The carrier mobility of BP-based SGT is as good as that of graphene-based SGT. Once the problem of low chemical stability is overcome, such a device operates as a critical role in the field of sensors. SGTs have been widely applied in various types of chemical or biological sensors, including pH, ion, DNA, and protein sensors. They are also extensively used to study the physical properties of various channel materials under field effects. However, because of the difficulty of fabricating equipment with ideal density and uniformity, these sensing technologies are still far from practical applications. Furthermore, the complex interaction between 2D channel materials and other organic or inorganic materials is still uncertain, and a large number of experiments are still required to verify the effectiveness and feasibility of SGTs application.

In in vitro, SGTs can be placed in an oral cavity, nasal cavity, eyes, body surface, and blood. In the oral cavity, saliva provides a liquid environment for the sensor, while the sensor specifically combines with biomarkers in saliva to produce an electrical signal. Therefore, non-invasive diagnosis of some diseases can be successfully achieved by monitoring electrical signals. In 2012, researchers developed a type of SGGT as the ultra-sensitive bioelectronic nose (b-nose) capable of coupling with olfactory receptors 2AG1 that can selectively combine with the specific odorant AB. Based on this type of research, it is completely possible to realize the combination of SGT nasal gas sensors and flexible circuits to help human sensitively distinguish whether there are specific gas molecules in the environment. It can also fulfill the application of forewarning and tracking by improving the sense of smell.295 Studies have shown that micro-current can stimulate Muller cells to up-regulate the transcription of BDNF, which can effectively prolong the survival time of photoreceptor cells and delay the loss of retinal function. Therefore, it is feasible to use the SGTs micro-current system in moist eye environment for ophthalmology treatment, and it may provide a protective effect on the entire retina, in contrast, drug injection only works locally. Moreover, SGTs can also be used on the body surface because sweat can provide a liquid service environment for the sensor. Besides, sweat contains ions, amino acids, hormones, proteins, polypeptides, and other secretions. SGT sensor can non-invasively monitor human body electrolyte imbalance, lactate index, sweat glucose level, and dehydration status, which will be a very efficient and low-cost diagnostic mode in the future.

In in vivo, the applications of SGTs include modulation of conduction of nerve signals on synapse, detection of disease markers in the blood, measurement of blood viscosity, and monitoring of cell transmembrane potential (TMP).

SGT is the platform of “electric–chemical–electric” signal transmission, therefore can work in the neuromuscular junction (a contact point between the ends of a motor neurons axon on a skeletal muscle fiber). The nerve endings in the action potential cause the release of acetylcholine (Ach) and trigger action potentials of skeletal muscle. Ach ligand-modified SGT can be settled in the neuromuscular joint to facilitate the modulation and transmission of nerve signals. In blood, the principle for the identification and detection of disease markers is similar to that in the oral cavity assay mentioned above; nonetheless, the feasibility remains to be explored due to the more complex interaction between SGT and the internal environment. Recently, many reports about microfluidic solution delivery systems have been presented to detect ion concentration and fluid flow velocity. Based on these observations, we boldly envision a future where the SGT could be integrated into needles and even implanted directly into blood vessels for rapid detection of blood flow velocity (viscosity). With the continuous development of micro-machining technology, it is feasible for the low-cost mass production of high-performance SGTs in line with human cell size (average 10–20 μm), which paves the way for SGTs to detect the single-cell TMP in the humoral environment. With the continuous progress of micro-machining technology, it is probable to produce the low-cost and high-performance SGTs matching human cell size, which makes it possible for SGTs to detect single-cell TMP in the humoral environment. The normal TMP, an indicator of a cell's ability to make adenosine triphosphate, falls between 70 and 90 mV. However, age, disease, environmental chemicals, and even stress can change the TMP. When the TMP drops to 15–30 mV, cells are more likely to become cancerous. Therefore, the detection of cell TMP may become an important approach to monitoring human health in near future.
