Abstract

Hole-transporting materials (HTMs) have a vital influence on the improvement of performance for perovskite solar cells (PSCs). Starting from arylamine-based HTM (e.g., Spiro-OMeTAD), we design three molecules (CQ4â€“CQ6) with different conjugated Ï€-bridge cores with a fused aromatic ring instead of the single carbon atom. To achieve a goal that provides an effective strategy for HTMs design, the frontier molecular orbitals, optical absorptions, and hole transport for these HTMs in this work were investigated using DFT and TD-DFT combined with Marcus theory. The simulated results indicated that CQ4 with better performance, such as good charge separation, smaller recombination energy, and the highest hole mobility, may be an alternative HTM for PSC applications. Therefore, the promising HTM of CQ4 was synthesized and further applied to PSC devices. The results show that the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of the Spiro-OMeTAD-based PSC device is 17.12%, while the CQ4-based PSC shows a better PCE of 17.48%, due to its high hole mobility and uniform smooth film morphology, which ultimately promoted a higher fill factor. In addition, the experimental data can reproduce the theoretical results very well, indicating that the provided model of molecular design is a feasible way to obtain the alternative HTMs.

1. Introduction

Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have attracted wide attention due to their low cost, ease of manufacture, and especially their excellent power conversion efficiency (PCE), which has been increased from 3.8%1 to 25.5%.2 In general, the structures of PSC devices consist of FTO/ITO conductive glass, electron transport layer, perovskites, hole-transporting layer, and silver electrode (Ag).3 Hereinto, hole-transport materials (HTMs) as hole-transporting layers play a significant role to extract holes from perovskite to metal electrodes (e.g., Ag) in order to reduce non-radiative recombination at the interface, thus achieving a high efficiency of devices.4,5 So far, HTMs are mainly divided into inorganic materials (such as CuSCN and CuI)6,7 and organic materials (including organic small molecules and polymers).8â€“10 Organic small molecules have the advantages of accurate molecular weight, high purity, easy adjustment of molecular structure, and controllable energy level, which are considered ideal HTMs for PSC applications.11â€“13 In addition to PSC applications, organic Ï€ conjugated molecules have become potential materials for many applications due to their unique optical and electronic properties, such as organic light-emitting diodes, and organic solid state lasers.14â€“16

2,2â€²,7,7â€²-Tetrakis (N,N-di-p-methoxyphenylamine)-9,9â€²-spirobifluorene (Spiro-OMeTAD) as the most typical HTM is applied in PSC devices for achieving high PCE.17,18 The core configuration of spiro in Spiro-OMeTAD can improve the quality of film-forming by reducing the aggregation behavior of molecules and has high thermal stability.19â€“21 In recent years, a great deal of research indicated that the spiro core is also one of the ideal units for the construction of new HTMs.22â€“24 For instance, Xu et al.25 designed a spiro-based HTM (X60), which was applied to the device with a compact structure of TiO2/nc-TiO2/perovskite/HTM(X60)/Au for finally achieving a PCE of 19.84%. Gao and co-workers26 reported two novel di-spiro ring HTMs (G1 and G2), and the results showed that di-spiro HTMs with a large Ï€-conjugated system can promote rigidity and crystallinity, which is good for improving the hole-transporting ability. The PCE of the G2-based device reaches 20.2%. In addition, large Ï€-conjugate cores in arylamine-based HTMs can also promote Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking, thus improving hole mobility26,27 Deng et al.28 reported two spiro-based HTMs (SC and ST), of which SC has extended Ï€ conjugation, high solubility, and low crystallinity, and PCE is up to 21.76%, which is attributed to its smooth and pinhole-free film morphology.

Although the spiro configuration in arylamine-based HTMs can yield a highly rigid structure through a high degree of distortion, its highly twisted structure will also weaken the interaction between molecules, which is not conducive to charge transfer to a certain extent.29,30 Moreover, the conditions for the synthesis of spiro cores are harsh. For example, the synthesis of Spiro-OMeTAD needs to be carried out at a low temperature (âˆ’78 Â°C), which limits its large-scale commercial development and application.31 Therefore, many efforts are remaining to be done to develop novel small-molecule HTMs as alternatives to Spiro-OMeTAD.32,33

In fact, a great deal of research has proved that it is a feasible strategy to simulate the geometric structure, optical absorption, and charge mobility of organic materials by combining Density Functional Theory (DFT) and time-dependent Density Functional Theory (TD-DFT) with Marcus theory.34â€“37 The providing theoretical models can screen the promising HTMs for further using these HTMs into PSC device applications. The strategy not only can provide an insight into the influence of molecular structure on material properties theoretically, but also can shorten the development cycle and reduce the development cost for promising HTMs. For example, in our previous work,38 we designed three HTMs (CQ1â€“CQ3) with different cores (dibenzofuran, dibenzothiophene, and dibenzoselenophene, respectively). According to theoretical calculations, CQ2 has better charge separation ability and higher hole mobility compared to CQ1 and CQ3. A potential HTM of CQ2 was synthesized and further applied in PSC devices to explore its device performance.

In this work, we attempted to construct twisted molecular structures by replacing single carbon atoms with fused aromatic rings. Fused aromatic rings extend the conjugation of the molecule compared to a single carbon atom. We designed three molecules (CQ4â€“CQ6), and the molecular structures are shown in Fig. 1. Compared with Spiro-OMeTAD, the designed CQ4â€“CQ6 is a twisted structure with extended Ï€ conjugate cores and four triphenylamine derivatives as terminal groups. Calculations using DFT, TD-DFT, and Marcus theory were used to study their frontier molecular orbitals, optical absorptions, and hole transport. Compared with Spiro-OMeTAD and other designed molecules of CQ5 and CQ6, the theoretical results show that the designed CQ4 has the highest hole mobility, smaller recombination energy, and the best charge separation. To further investigate the influence of molecular structure on the device performance and verify the reliability of the calculated model in this work, the alternative HTM of CQ4 was synthesized and further applied to PSC devices. In fact, the synthetic routes of CQ4 are simpler than that of Spiro-OMeTAD, using the simple Suzuki coupling reaction with a yield of up to 88%. Due to the extended core of CQ4, it can be effectively stacked through intermolecular Ï€â€“Ï€ interactions, leading to a better hole transport ability and intermolecular charge transfer (ICT). Moreover, CQ4 as HTM in PSC applications exhibits a matching energy level with that of perovskites and a better uniform film morphology on perovskite materials. The results showed that the PCE of the CQ4-based PSC device reached 17.48%, which is higher than the Spiro-OMeTAD-based device (17.12%) under the same conditions. Our work proves that the strategy of replacing the C atom of the spiro core with a fused aromatic ring is feasible. Moreover, the experimental data can reproduce the theoretical results very well, indicating that the provided model of molecular design is an effective approach to achieve the goal of molecular design for potential HTMs.

Then, we further analyzed the specific reasons for the hole mobility of these molecules. Usually, the face-to-face stacking in the molecular stacking model is in favor of orbital overlap, so there is considerable interaction in these pathways of the molecules, and the face to face stacking model of molecules gives a good v.53 In general, the v between adjacent molecules decreases exponentially with the increase in ri. The face-to-face stacking paths of both Spiro-OMeTAD and CQ4â€“CQ6 are T2, with ri values of 14.642, 11.672, 17.315, and 9.665 Ã…, respectively. Both the T2 pathways of Spiro-OMeTAD and CQ4â€“CQ6 are the shortest paths of hole transfer, so there are considerable interactions in these pathways of each molecule. Due to the large transmission distance between the dimers of CQ5, the molecular interactions in those paths are small, which ultimately provides a smaller v value. This prediction can be verified according to the v values in Table 3. The results show that the distance between the dimer and the stacking mode in the molecules has a significant effect on v, and the electron coupling plays an important role in determining the hole mobility. The Î» of CQ4 is not the lowest, but its molecular stacking is more compact and provides a large v. Notably, although CQ6 has the smallest hole recombination energy, it shows lower mobility among all molecules. In other words, the stacking of molecules plays a central role in charge transfer mechanisms. In general, enhanced Ï€â€“Ï€ conjugation of cores in HTMs can reduce the recombination energy, which is conducive to the improvement of hole mobility to further enhance the PCE of the PSC device to a certain extent.54,55 But a stronger core does not necessarily improve hole mobility and the device performance. For example, Nazeeruddin et al.56 reported two HTMs (TTPAâ€“DBQT and TTPAâ€“DBST), TTPAâ€“DBST with stronger Ï€â€“Ï€ conjugation than that of TTPAâ€“DBQT. However, the conductivity and PCE value of TTPAâ€“DBQT is higher than that of TTPAâ€“DBST.

Based on the above theoretical results, it is found that CQ4â€“CQ6 have appropriate energy levels and good optical absorption, as well as good stability, which are crucial for potential HTMs for application in PSC devices. It is noteworthy that CQ4 not only has lower Î» and larger v, but also has better charge separation ability compared with CQ5 and CQ6, which can effectively avoid adverse charge recombination effect and theoretically have the best hole transport ability (3.87 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1). In addition, the synthesis cost of CQ4 is lower than that of CQ5 and CQ6. To achieve a goal that verifies the computational method for the material design of alternative HTMs, the potential CQ4 as the target was further synthesized and used as an HTM to explore its performance in PSC devices.

2.4. Hole extraction and charge recombination at interfaces

The film morphologies of the HTMs with dopants were investigated by atomic force microscopy (AFM), and the topographic AFM images of Spiro-OMeTAD and CQ4 capping on the perovskite layers are shown in Fig. 6(aâ€“c). The root mean square (RMS) roughness of the perovskite layer is 20.5 nm, and the RMS value is significantly reduced after spin-coating Spiro-OMeTAD or CQ4. The RMS roughness of the spin-coating Spiro-OMeTAD and CQ4 is 7.79 nm and 7.08 nm, respectively. As illustrated in Fig. 6(aâ€“c), the surface of the Spiro-OMeTAD film has obvious pinholes, and the surface of the CQ4 film is complete and smooth, indicating that the contact between CQ4 and perovskite interface is more effective. Therefore, the CQ4 film in the PSC device can provide good charge transfer from the perovskite to HTM and reduce the recombination loss, which leads to an ultimately increased FF.
Fig. 6 (aâ€“c) AFM image of perovskite film without or with capping different HTMs of Spiro-OMeTAD and CQ4. (d) Steady-state PL spectra of the perovskite films with and without HTMs. (e) Time-resolved PL decay of pristine perovskite film and perovskite/HTM films.

Steady-state photoluminescence (PL) spectroscopy is an efficient way to investigate the hole extraction on the perovskite/HTMs interface. As shown in Fig. 6(d), the perovskite film has a strong PL peak between 700 nm and 825 nm. When Spiro-OMeTAD and CQ4 are coated on the perovskite film, PL intensity is quenched. The results show that the holes at the interface between perovskite and HTMs have been extracted effectively. Especially, CQ4 yielded a slightly higher PL quenching efficiency compared to Spiro-OMeTAD, which confirms that CQ4 has a strong hole-extraction capability in PSCs and can transfer holes from perovskite to CQ4 more effectively. The hole extraction efficiencies at perovskite/HTMs interfaces have been explored by time-resolved photoluminescence (TRPL) measurement. As presented in Fig. 6(e), the original perovskite film exhibits a long PL decay, which is strongly inhibited when the perovskite layer is in contact with HTM. The PL decay lifetimes for the Spiro-OMeTAD and CQ4 films on the perovskite film are significantly shorter than the perovskite film. The results illustrate that CQ4 exhibits a comparable hole-extracting ability to Spiro-OMeTAD.

Compared to Spiro-OMeTAD, the CQ4-based devices have a similar twisted structure, but their photovoltaic efficiency is improved. The increase of PCE of CQ4 devices is mainly due to the increase of FF. In terms of molecular structure, the extended Ï€-conjugation results in the different electronic properties of Spiro-OMeTAD and CQ4. The lower recombination energy of CQ4 results in the higher hole mobility of its film. In addition, CQ4 film has a more uniform surface morphology and slightly higher hole extraction ability than Spiro-OMeTAD film. Together, these factors indicated that the hole extraction and hole transport in the CQ4 film are promoted, which leads to increased interaction between the perovskite and HTMs, thereby reducing charge recombination at the perovskite/HTMs interface.65 Therefore, CQ4-based devices have stronger photovoltaic performance than Spiro-OMeTAD-based devices.

3. Conclusion

In this work, starting from the arylamine-based HTM (e.g., Spiro-OMeTAD), we design three molecules (CQ4â€“CQ6) with different conjugated Ï€-bridge cores with a fused aromatic ring instead of the single carbon atom. To achieve a goal that provides an effective strategy for HTMs design, the frontier molecular orbital energy levels, optical properties, and hole mobility for these HTMs in this work were investigated using DFT and TD-DFT combined with Marcus theory. The simulated results indicated that CQ4 with better performance, such as good charge separation, smaller recombination energy, and the highest hole mobility, may be an alternative HTM for PSC applications. Therefore, CQ4 as a promising HTM was synthesized and applied in PSC devices. The results show that CQ4 is easy to synthesize and has a high yield (88%). The PCE of the PSC based on CQ4 is up to 17.48%, which is higher than that of the Spiro-OMeTAD (17.12%) under the same conditions. The better PSC performance of CQ4 than that of Spiro-OMeTAD is attributed to the improved hole mobility; uniform and smooth film morphology, which ultimately promoted a higher FF. This work proves that the strategy of replacing the C atom of the spiro core with a fused aromatic ring is feasible. Moreover, the experimental data can reproduce the theoretical results very well, indicating that the provided model of molecular design is a feasible way to obtain alternative HTMs.
