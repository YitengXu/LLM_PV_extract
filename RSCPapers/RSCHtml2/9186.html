Functionalized 1,3,5-triazine derivatives as components for photo- and electroluminescent materials

Abstract

Extensive research on the synthesis and application of triazine derivatives for electroluminescent devices, phosphorescent emitters, hole transport materials for perovskite solar cells, and TADF materials has been published recently. This review covers article data on the modern trends in design of functionalized triazines obtained within the period 2010–2021. Star-shaped aryl(hetaryl) and arylvinyl derivatives of 1,3,5-triazine, their photoluminescence and application for fluorescent sensors to nitro-containing explosives or proton-controlled optical sensors are discussed. Examples of 1,3,5-triazine derivatives exhibiting two-photon absorption, two-photon excited fluorescence and nonlinear optical properties are reviewed. Hyper-branched and linear polymers for multilayer data storage application are reported. The future development of 1,3,5-triazine-based functional materials is undoubtedly related to the creation of more advanced molecules for PhOLEDs, highly effective organic chromophores with thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF), and nonlinear optical (NLO) materials as well as ones for energy storage.

1. Introduction

π-Conjugated compounds based on the 1,3,5-triazine core have been studied as attractive molecules for functional photoelectric materials due to the intriguing structural and electronic properties of the s-triazine moiety.1

1,3,5-Triazines may be considered as triaza-analogs of benzenes with three alternate carbon atoms replaced by nitrogen atoms. s-Triazines possess high affinity for electrons and good thermal stability, so they can serve as effective electron acceptors in the design of donor–acceptor systems.2,3 In addition, the structural symmetry and the spatial coplanarity make s-triazine attractive for functionalization with a variety of structural (electron-donating) units. Moreover, s-triazine in many cases would improve the electron-injection and electron-transportation abilities of its conjugated derivatives.4,5

Within the whole catalogue of organic (semiconductive) materials, 1,3,5-triazine derivatives have become a focus due to their exceptional properties derived from their unique structure combining strong electron-accepting ability and high symmetry. Consequently, introducing a 1,3,5-triazine unit into the backbone of conjugated molecules would influence the electron-injection and electron-transportation abilities of such molecules as well as increase their heat resistance. Undoubtedly, from the point of view of optoelectronic material applications, organic molecules possessing both good electron-injection and/or transportation ability along with high (thermo)stability are highly desirable. Thus, in recent years, one can observe an increasing interest in the synthesis and investigation of the chemical/photophysical/electronic properties of s-triazine-based π-conjugated small molecules and oligomers/polymers.

Two types of 1,3,5-triazines for possible application in optoelectronic devices can be outlined: s-triazines, containing donor moieties at all three positions (2, 4 and 6) of the triazine core, and 2-aryl-subsituted s-triazines, bearing one donor and one acceptor substituent at 4- and 6-positions of a conjugated system; the first type of star-shaped molecule is more widely used.

In most small molecule-based OLEDs, the emitter layer includes a phosphorescent dye that is doped into the host material to avoid concentration quenching and to optimize the charge balance. Efficient energy transfer from the host molecule to the phosphorescent dye molecule is required. To meet this requirement the energy of the triplet state of the host should be higher than that of the guest energy, and for the blue phosphorescent emitters the triplet energies exceed 2.8 eV. Organic molecules containing aromatic amine or carbazole fragments possess significantly high hole conductivity, while electron-deficient heterocycles such as s-triazines exhibit good electron conductivity. For this reason, s-triazines are used as electron transport layers in OLEDs, while the hole conductivity of the most of s-triazines is insufficient for their application as host materials. s-Triazine derivatives represent effective building blocks for thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF) molecules.6–8 Thus, various 9H-carbazol-9-yl-1,3,5-triazine derivatives have been prepared and are widely used as high triplet energy host materials for the blue TADF OLEDs.9,10

s-Triazine-based push–pull systems have found application as fluorescent sensors11 or probes12 for nitro-containing explosives. Recently, the nonlinear optical properties of 1,3,5-triazine derivatives have been systematically studied.13,14 Finally, the s-triazine unit is remarkable for other reasons, such as supramolecular interactions with applications in host–guest chemistry, anion recognition, sensors, electronics, and magnetism.15

2. Synthesis and photophysical properties of aryl- and heteroaryl-1,3,5-triazines

A common approach to symmetrically substituted, i.e., containing three identical aryl moieties, 2,4,6-triaryl-1,3,5-triazines 2 is based on the acid-catalyzed trimerization of the corresponding aromatic nitriles 1. Thus, 2,4,6-triphenyl-1,3,5-triazine 2а (TRZ) was obtained by means of cyclotrimerization of benzonitrile in the presence of combined catalyst PCl5–HCl,16 and 2,4,6-tris(4-bromophenyl)-1,3,5-triazine 2b was prepared by trimerization of 4-bromobenzonitrile in the presence of CF3SO3H (Scheme 1).17

According to CV measurements, the reduction potentials of 3a–e were from −2.09 to −2.14 V (vs. Fc/Fc+), and the electron affinity of the s-triazine core was affected slightly by the terminal groups attached to the phenylene spacer. 1-Naphthyl derivative 3c (Tg 133 °C) was proved to be the only compound in these series to exhibit glass transition. Incorporation of an electron-donating diphenylamino (DPA) residue represents a common approach for designing luminophores. Zhang et al. described the synthesis and photophysical studies of 2,4,6-triphenyl-1,3,5-triazine derivatives containing varying numbers of diphenylamino fragments.20 Thus, s-triazine 4, bearing the NPh2 residue only in one phenyl ring, was obtained via Buchwald–Hartwig amination of 2-(4-bromophenyl)-4,6-diphenyl-1,3,5-triazine with diphenylamine in 98% yield.21 Suzuki cross-coupling of 2-chloro-4,6-diphenyl-1,3,5-triazine with 4-diphenylamino-phenylboronic acid22 or its pinacol ester20 afforded 4 in 84% and 80% yields, respectively (Scheme 3). Molecules including highly π-conjugated systems and stronger electron-donating groups compared with 2,4,6-triphenyl-1,3,5-triazine (TRZ) were designed to improve the OLED device performances. Thus, 2,4,6-triaryl-1,3,5-triazine derivatives, containing an electron-donating component such as triphenylamino (TPA) residue with additional substituents, were described.25,26 Chromophores 8a–c in which the bis-tBu-TPA moiety is located at the para or meta position in one phenyl substituent of triphenyltriazine (8a, 8b) or bound with TRZ through a propylene bridge (8c) were synthesized by Suzuki cross-coupling reaction (Fig. 4).25 Compounds 8a–c exhibited triplet energy values of ET = 2.5, 2.7 and 3.0 eV, bathochromic shift of fluorescence band upon the increase in solvent polarity, as well as phosphorescence at 77 K. Wang et al. observed the formation of charge transfer complexes (CTCs);25 both intra- and intermolecular charge transfer processes were considered to explain the formation of CTCs with all three molecules. para-Substituted derivative 8a was shown to be the most susceptible to CTC formation in both solution and thin films. As a result EQE in PhOLED was reduced despite the better balance between charge flows in the emitting layer. meta-Substituted molecule 8b demonstrated the highest EQE due to the compromise between balanced charge flows and CTC formation. The smallest CTC formation in the case of compound 8c was beneficial for placing most singlets and triplets due to its relatively high ET value. In addition, this triazine provided the best morphological stability of the desired glassy emitter layer, which in general is promising for fabrication of excellent PhOLEDs.

Star-shaped compounds 9, 10 (Fig. 5) of donor–π-acceptor (D–A) type, in which 1,3,5-triazine and dimethoxytriphenylamine moieties are bound through thienylene or phenylene spacer, were synthesized as hole transport materials (HTM) for perovskite solar cells.26 These compounds were prepared in ∼50% yield by means of Stille cross-coupling reaction of cyanuric chloride with (tributylstannyl)thiophen-2-yl or (tributylstannyl)phenyl derivatives of dimethoxytriphenylamine. Both molecules exhibited fluorescence emission in chlorobenzene at λem = 502 nm (9) and 558 nm (10). The corresponding HOMO levels of −5.04 eV (9) and −5.11 eV (10) were calculated according to CV values. Devices based on compounds 9, 10 were created, their characteristics were obtained and compared with those of 2,2′,7,7′-tetrakis[N,N-di(4-methoxyphenyl)amino]-9,90-spirobifluorene (spiro-OMeTAD), representing the best HTM at that time. Although the photophysical properties of the two HTMs (9, 10) were similar, the photovoltaic performance was quite sensitive to the structural modification of the peripheral substituents. In particular, the compound 10-based device demonstrated Jsc = 19.14 mA cm−2, Voc = 0.93 V and ΦF = 0.61, affording PCE = 10.90%. Under similar conditions, the PV cells based on compound 9 and spiro-OMeTAD exhibited Jsc of 20.74 and 21.37 mA cm−2, Voc of 0.92 and 0.94 V, and ΦF of 0.66 and 0.67, as well as conversion efficiency of 12.51 and 13.45%, respectively.

The perovskite-based cell containing 9 as HTM showed a photovoltaic performance close to the spiro-OMeTAD-based cell. In addition, this cell demonstrated good stability for 250 h.

Functionalized tris(aryl)triazine 11, in which triphenylamine is attached to the triazine core through meta-phenylene spacer, was synthesized as a possible candidate possessing thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF).6 2,4,6-Tris(3-bromophenyl)-1,3,5-triazine was subjected to the Suzuki cross-coupling reaction with (4-(diphenylamino)phenyl)boronic acid; the obtained compound 11 exhibited fluorescence at λem = 458 nm and a quantum yield of 17.6% in toluene. Moreover, passing nitrogen through the solution (to suppress triplet states quenching by oxygen) led to a higher quantum yield value of 44.2%. The results show that 11 can emit more delayed fluorescence and is promising for future applications as a TADF material.6 To confirm the TADF behavior of 11, the prompt and delayed fluorescence lifetime measurements were carried out in toluene, and the lifetime values of 12.47 ns and 166 μs were determined. The time-resolved photoluminescence and phosphorescence spectra were recorded for compound 11 in solid films with 100 ms delay at 77 K, and the energy gap between the singlet and triplet states of 0.18 eV was evaluated.

Dendrimers 12, 13 were prepared via demethylation of methoxy groups in compound 10 by using BBr3 and the subsequent O-alkylation with 9-(6-bromohexyl)-9H-carbazole/tricarbazole in 65 and 55% yields, respectively. Both dendrimers were compared with the starting compound 10. 1,3,5-Triazines 10, 12 and 13 demonstrated high thermal stability with Td = 437, 388, 427 °C, respectively. All three compounds exhibited bright yellow fluorescence with λem at 590–594 nm in dichloromethane, λem 541–553 nm in films and Φf values of 44 (10), 56 (11) and 71% (12).

For all the compounds 10, 12 and 13 quantum-chemical calculations and CV studies7 were carried out, and the corresponding HOMO, LUMO, S1, T1 and ΔEST energy values were estimated (Fig. 6a). It was demonstrated that the incorporation of an extra phenyl bridge to increase the distance between donor (D) and acceptor (A) moieties in the emission core of 10 represents a promising route to achieve both the small singlet–triplet energy splitting and enhanced PL quantum yields (PLQYs) simultaneously. Analysis of experimental data for the compounds 10, 12 and 13 showed that the introduction of non-conjugated aliphatic chains in the carbazole dendrons can not only maintain the small ΔEST and the core emission, but it can also enhance the PLQYs. Thus, the peripheral dendrons can efficiently encapsulate the core 10 to decrease the triplet–triplet annihilation. To confirm the TADF nature of compounds 10, 12 and 13, decay curves of the transient photoluminescence in pure films were obtained. The results showed double exponential decay with short and long lifetimes of 16 ns and 0.4 ms for 10, and long lifetimes of 0.5 ms and 0.8 ms for 12 and 13, respectively. In addition, these molecules showed large two-photon absorption (TPA) cross-sections and strong two-photon fluorescence (λmax = 576–578 nm). For the most representative tri-branched molecule 16, TPA cross-section was as large as 1.64 × 10−20 cm4 GW−1.

Fluorene derivatives as hole-transporting units were incorporated into the 1,3,5-triazine moiety as electron-transporting unit.5,28–33 9,9-Diphenyl- and 9,9-dimethyl-containing 1,3,5-triazines 17, 18 (Fig. 8) were presented as new bipolar host materials.28 These compounds were prepared in 66% and 45% yields via Suzuki reaction starting from 4,6-diphenyl-2-chloro-1,3,5-triazine and the corresponding fluorene-boronic acid pinacol ester under microwave irradiation. Derivatives 17, 18 showed fluorescence in diluted THF at λem = 382 nm (17) and 385 nm (18) and phosphorescence in 2-MeTHF at 77 K at 491 and 527 nm (17), 487 and 524 nm (18). The triplet energy values (ET), calculated from the highest energy phosphorescence peaks, were of 2.63 and 2.64 eV, respectively.

Green phosphorescent OLED devices based on 17, 18 were fabricated, and these devices demonstrated the EQE values of 14.52 and 14.30%, respectively. Such improved performance of these devices is attributed to the balanced charge flux and exciton confinement within the emitting layers.

Zhong et al. synthesized and studied 9,9-diethylfluorene-substituted 1,3,5-triazines, in which the fluorenyl group is either directly attached to the s-triazine cycle (19), or separated from it by a phenylene bridge (20), or contains a terminal phenyl substituent (21) (Fig. 9).5,29 Compound 19 was directly prepared from cyanuric chloride and 9,9-diethyl-9H-fluoren-2-yl magnesium bromide through Ni-catalyzed cross-coupling reaction.29 Compounds 20, 21 were obtained by the cyclotrimerization reaction of 4-(9,9-diethyl-9H-fluoren-2-yl)benzonitrile or 9,9-diethyl-7-phenyl-9H-fluorene-2-carbonitrile, respectively, in the presence of trifluoromethanesulfonic acid in anhydrous CH2Cl2.5 Star-shaped molecules 19–21 exhibited an intensive blue photoluminescence, and the emission maxima were observed in solution in the following order: 19 (385 nm) < 21 (412 nm) < 20 (424 nm). In the solid state a slight red shift of the emission band was observed, and the emission maxima followed the order: 19 > 20 = 21, which indicates a partial π-stacking in all three compounds. However, the degree of π-stacking in 20 and 21 was lower than in 19.

Electroluminescent devices with the configuration ITO/NPB/Alq/1,3,5-triazine/LiF/Al were fabricated based on these 1,3,5-triazines, and the performance was studied.5 The maximum luminance was less than in the control device Alq/Alq; however, maximum current efficiencies of the devices containing 19, 20 or 21 were remarkably improved from 3.1 cd A−1 for the reference device to 4.5, 4.0, and 4.9 cd A−1, respectively. These results indicate that the application of compounds 19–21 enables the devices to improve the balance between the hole and electron transporting.

Phosphorescent light-emitting devices (PhOLEDs) with the configurations of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/MEH-PPV/hole-blocking layer/LiF/Al were fabricated. The comparative device was based on poly[2-methoxy-5-(2′-ethylhexyloxy)-1,4-phenylene vinylene] (MEH-PPV), and the maximum luminance of devices based on 19, 20 and 21 was promoted from 1358 cd m−2 to 2683, 2327, and 2871 cd m−2, respectively. Furthermore, devices 19, 20 and 21 showed maximum current efficiencies of 0.97, 0.97, and 1.37 cd A−1, respectively, which were more than four times higher than 0.25 cd A−1 of the MEH-PPV-based device. These results suggest that the abovementioned s-triazine derivatives possess excellent hole-blocking capacity.

According to the literature data, alkenyl-substituted 1,3,5-triazines are less promising candidates in terms of their application in electronic device materials compared with aryl- or hetaryl-substituted derivatives of s-triazines. However, the synthesis of such derivatives is widely presented, and some photophysical properties have been revealed. Thus, mono-, di- and tris(arylethenyl) derivatives of 1,3,5-triazine 119–121 (Fig. 44), containing electron donor substituents in the aryl fragment, were synthesized and studied.79–81 All the compounds were obtained by means of aldol condensation reaction between 2,4,6-trimethyl-1,3,5-triazine and the corresponding arylaldehyde.

The structures of compounds 119а and 120а were confirmed by X-ray data.79 Linear and non-linear optical properties of compounds 119–121а were analyzed, and all the collected photophysical data indicated that incorporation of more branched substituents into the s-triazine ring leads to significantly enhanced linear and nonlinear optical responses. The spectral behavior of 120а was closer to that of 121а, while it was different from that of 119а. The spectral positions (λmax) of the linear absorption (from 410 to 430 nm), the single-photon excited fluorescence (SPEF) (from 492 to 522 nm) and the two-photon excited fluorescence (TPEF) (from 499 to 522 nm) showed redshift of the emission when the branch number increased from 119a to 121a. The compound 119a was second harmonic generation (SHG) active in the crystalline state.

Structures of compounds 119b,c and 120c were also confirmed by X-ray data.80 In all these derivatives the styryl moiety was conjugated with the s-triazine ring to result in a planar conformation of a whole molecule. Compounds 119b,c–121b,c also exhibited NLO properties, and they demonstrated the same “structure–photophysical properties” relationships as those for compounds 119a–121a. Meier et al. described an extended series of derivatives (121b,e–g, Fig. 44).82 Alkoxy- or dimethylamino groups at the peripheral benzene rings, which acted as electron donors, and the central 1,3,5-triazine ring acting as an electron acceptor resulted in the efficient intramolecular charge transfer (ICT). The push–pull effect in these compounds became evident by the polarization of the olefin-based bridges, which was expressed by the considerable differences in the chemical shifts observed in 13C and 1H NMR spectra. Protonation of the 1,3,5-triazine core enhanced the effect, as was demonstrated by a bathochromic shift; a secondary protonation of dimethylamino groups, however, led to the hampering of ICT. Thus, upon the addition of trifluoroacetic acid, the yellow compound 121b changed its color to violet, and it then became colorless.

Push–pull systems of the series 119d–121d, like in previous series, exhibited linear and NLO properties. The maximum of SPEF band demonstrated red shift from 490 nm (119d) to 510 nm (121d) and the quantum yield increased from 5 to 20%, respectively. Derivatives 120d and 121d showed sufficiently high σ2PA values (240 and 175 GM). Systems 119–121d were studied as initiators of two-photon induced polymerization (2PP), which represents the basis of 3D printing as a powerful micro-production tool. Specialized two-photon initiators (2PIs) are essential components of applied photosensitive polymerizable compositions. All studied systems showed a remarkable increase in sensitivity, i.e. threshold values for the fabrication of ideal structures as well as thermally induced degradation of the composition were decreased. The performance of the 2PIs based on compounds 120d and 121d at 800 nm was similar to the well-established quadrupolar ketone-based 2PI reference, but derivative 121d exceeded it by a significantly lower manufacturing threshold at extremely high recording speeds of up to 200 mm s−1 and a wider gap for ideal processing parameters.

In the next work102 the same team studied the photophysical properties of polymers 170а,с and 171. It was shown that polymers exhibited both one- and two-photon excited fluorescence at 499 nm (170а), 460 nm (170с), and 474 nm (171) with quantum yields of 25, 35, and 71%, respectively. Optimized two-photon absorbers are used as nonlinear NIR-antennas, and task-specific on-demand functional materials such as sensors and biologically active units can be created by combining chromophores with active units.103 Thus, a strategy based on a successful combination of NIR-antenna and photochromic units led to multilayer data storage applications. Thus, push–pull oligomer 171 was designed as nonlinear near-infrared antenna for two-photon activated volumetric optical memories.13 This polymer has a maximum TPA cross-section of 6930 GM at 740 nm, and emission centered at 520 nm with a fluorescence quantum yield of 70%, and exhibits efficient up-conversion of the excitation energy into fluorescence emission. Due to its high two-photon absorption the polymer represents promising material for nonlinear NIR-antenna. Multilayer data storage was demonstrated by using the composite films with polymer 171 incorporated into photochromic 1,2-bis(2-methylbenzo[b]thiophen-3-yl)hexafluorocyclopentene (PC) (Fig. 67).

6. Conclusions

In this review we have demonstrated that 1,3,5-triazine-based systems display a wide set of promising chemical, supramolecular, linear and nonlinear optical, as well as redox properties, which makes these heterocycles attractive candidates for various practical applications.

The intriguing structural and electronic properties of the s-triazine moiety, its structural symmetry and the spatial coplanarity allow us to obtain linear, quadrupole and octupole push–pull systems, oligo- and polymers based on this heterocycle.

Some general important tools for tuning the photophysical properties and practical usability of s-triazine-based molecules/materials were applied: incorporation of amino-derived, π-rich heterocycles as electron donors, extension and branching of the π-system, and introduction of long alkyl chains to provide good solubility of the chromophore.20,70 The transition from mono- to bis-s-triazine derivatives was also accompanied by a significant improvement in photophysical properties.95

Star-shaped s-triazine derivatives are widely used as host materials for high-efficiency fluorescent OLEDs with EQE > 25% and a wide color gamut.20,40,47 In this regard, it should be noted that 1,3,5-triazine triphenylsilyl derivatives represent a mixture of hole-transport and electron-transport material types for application in high-performance clear blue long-life PhOLEDs.46

Much attention has been paid to the design of carbazole-substituted 1,3,5-triazine-based TADF emitters, and a new strategy based on hyperconjugation for advanced TADF materials was developed via introducing the nonconjugated aliphatic chains into carbazole dendrons. This not only can maintain the small ΔEST and the core emission, but also enhance the PLQYs because of their rigid molecular structures.22

One can see a growing number of works on the design of 1,3,5-triazine-based nonlinear optical functional materials. Thus, enhancement of the two-photon absorption cross-section reached 6930 GM by sufficient electronic coupling between the strong charge transfer acceptor–donor–acceptor quadrupolar branches through the s-triazine core,88 and due to larger emission quantum yield (71%) in the range of fluorophores with different 2,4,6-tris(thiophen-2-yl)-1,3,5-triazine/fluorene ratio.100,102 Optimized two-photon absorbers are used as nonlinear NIR-antennae and combined with active units to perform specific tasks upon demand.103 Multilayer data storage is demonstrated in composite films of photochromic compound 1,2-bis(2-methylbenzo-[b]thiophen-3-yl)hexafluorocyclopentene (PC) doped with water-soluble nanoparticles of 2,4,6-tris(thiophen-2-yl)-1,3,5-triazine/fluorene polymer.13

1,3,5-Triazine-based polymer 165 was reported as a cathode material of Li-ion battery and demonstrated the charge–discharge process characteristics of both the free radical redox of triphenylamine unit and the bipolar redox of triazine unit in the polymer, which made it present a multistage discharge voltage plateau during the charge–discharge process.24

Among 4,6-diphenyl-1,3,5-triazine molecules, a series of highly twisted derivatives with indolinospirobifluorene donor was described; for these structures simultaneous intramolecular charge transfer through a bond (TBCT) and charge transfer through a space (TSCT) in one molecule were observed.8 Novel TADF materials with tunable TBCT and TSCT states exhibited high quantum yield (up to 92%) and TADF properties.

Good candidates for electrochemiluminescence (ECL) generation were obtained by combination of an electron-deficient 1,3,5-triazine core with three fluorene arms substituted with diarylamino or carbazolyl electron donors; compounds demonstrated high PL quantum yields and exhibited stable radical ion species; light was produced during recombination between the cation radical and the anion radical.31

Based on all the above, in our opinion, the future applications of 1,3,5-triazine-based scaffolds should be focused on creation of more advanced materials for PhOLEDs, highly effective organic chromophores with thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF), and nonlinear optical (NLO) materials as well as ones for energy storage.
