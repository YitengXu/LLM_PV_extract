Abstract

The thermoelectric properties of intrinsic n-type Î²-Ga2O3 are evaluated by first-principles calculations combined with Boltzmann transport theory and relaxation time approximation. The electron mobility is predicted by considering polar optical phonon scattering in Î²-Ga2O3. A temperature power law of Tâ0.67 is obtained for the intrinsic electron mobility. Due to the ultra-wide band gap of 4.7â4.9 eV, Î²-Ga2O3 has a large Seebeck coefficient. As a result, a maximum power factor of 3.1 Ã 10â3 W mâ1 Kâ2 is obtained at 1600 K. A clear anisotropy in lattice thermal conductivity is observed, with the highest thermal conductivity of 23.1 W mâ1 Kâ1 at 300 K along the [010] direction, and a lower value of 13.2 and 12.2 W mâ1 Kâ1 along the [001] and [100] directions, respectively. A high ZT value of 1.07 at 1600 K can be obtained at the optimal carrier concentration of 2.4 Ã 1019 cmâ3, which is superior to that of most other oxides such as ZnO. In addition, the lattice thermal conductivity can be reduced by precisely adjusting the grain size, and the lattice thermal conductivity at 300 K (1600 K) can be reduced by 73% (39%) when the grain size is decreased to 10 nm. The excellent thermoelectric properties of Î²-Ga2O3 have promoted its potential application in the field of high temperature thermoelectric conversion.

1 Introduction

Thermoelectric (TE) conversion technology can realize mutual conversion between heat and electrical energy, and thus can convert large amounts of waste heat into electrical energy for reutilization.1â3 It is a green energy conversion technology, which can alleviate the current energy crisis and environmental pollution to a certain extent. The higher the ZT value, the higher the conversion efficiency. Therefore, to achieve excellent thermoelectric performance, the materials should have large S and high Ï (to obtain high PF), and simultaneously low thermal conductivity.

Up to now several high performance thermoelectric materials such as GeTe, PbTe, SnSe and Cu2Se have been reported to achieve a maximum ZT value of 2.4â2.8.1 However, most of these materials contain heavy metal elements, which are toxic and also costly. More important is that these materials will suffer from decomposition, evaporation/sublimation and oxidation when they are working at high temperatures. The oxidation of SnSe occurs quickly at low temperatures of 200 Â°C, and a thick oxide layer can be formed at 600 Â°C.4 Formation of secondary phases also occurs frequently at higher temperatures. The easy diffusion of Cu ions in Cu2Se will lead to the formation of Cu precipitation at high temperatures.5 All these processes will result in serious deterioration of the thermoelectric performance. So most of them can only work at medium temperatures below 600 Â°C. It is necessary to find high temperature TE materials, which have applications in many important circumstances, for example, the conversion of nuclear energy to electrical power for space missions.

Due to the high thermal and chemical stability, low cost and nontoxicity, oxide thermoelectric materials have attracted extensive attention from researchers in this field.6â9 Various oxides have been investigated as thermoelectric materials, such as BiCuSeO, Ca3Co4O9, ZnO, In2O3 and SnO2. Although BiCuSeO has excellent thermoelectric properties due to its low thermal conductivity, it is difficult for large-scale industrial applications since it contains a resource-poor heavy metal element. In addition, it has a narrow band gap (0.8 eV) which limits its high temperature application due to the bipolar effect. Among these oxides, ZnO, In2O3 and SnO2 are wide band gap semiconductors with band gaps of 3.4â3.6 eV. The bipolar effect at high temperatures can be avoided since the band gap is far beyond 10 kBT,10 where kB is the Boltzmann constant. Therefore the wide band gap oxides can work at very high temperatures while keeping high stability. However, these oxide-based TE materials generally possess relatively low ZT values due to the high thermal conductivity caused by the strong ionic bonding and also the light mass of the oxygen atom. ZnO is one of the best wide band gap oxide thermoelectric materials which can achieve a ZT of 0.65 at 1247 K after co-doping with Al and Ga.11 But this is still much lower than that of the high performance TE materials such as SnSe and Cu Se due to a high lattice thermal conductivity (47 W mâ1 Kâ1 at room temperature). Nevertheless, since these materials can work at very high temperatures (usually >1200 K), a high conversion efficiency can still be obtained with a moderate ZT value. For example, with an average ZT of 1.0, cold and hot side temperatures of 300 K and 1500 K, a high conversion efficiency of 20% can be obtained according to eqn (1).

As one of the oxide materials, Ga2O3 has attracted considerable interest in recent years.12â14 There are five polymorphs of Ga2O3 (Î±-, Î²-, -, Î´- and Îµ-Ga2O3). Among them, Î²-Ga2O3 is a thermodynamically and chemically stable phase, and the other four polymorphs are metastable phases, which will be transformed into Î²-Ga2O3 after high temperature treatment. As a result, more research works were concentrated on Î²-Ga2O3.15 Due to the ultra-wide band gap (4.8 eV), exceptionally high breakdown field (8 MV cmâ1), low cost and high thermal stability, Î²-Ga2O3 has great application prospect in many fields such as high-power electronic devices, transparent conducting electrodes, solar-blind ultraviolet photodetectors, solar cells, gas sensors and catalysis.12,16,17

Î²-Ga2O3 may also be a potential thermoelectric material, which is expected to exhibit better thermoelectric properties than ZnO. First of all, Î²-Ga2O3 can achieve excellent electrical conductivity after appropriate doping. According to the reported results, the carrier concentration in Si and Sn-doped Î²-Ga2O3 can reach 1â2 Ã 1020 cmâ3, and the conductivity can reach more than 7 Ã 104 S mâ1.18,19 The electron mobility can be as high as 300 cm2 Vâ1 sâ1.20 These properties can fully meet the requirements of TE materials. Secondly, a large number of studies have shown that the maximum Seebeck coefficient in semiconductor materials is proportional to its band gap Eg as Eg = 2eSmaxT,21,22 where e is the unit charge. The ultra-wide band gap of 4.8 eV for Î²-Ga2O3 suggests that its Seebeck coefficient may also be higher than that of other oxide semiconductors. A large Seebeck coefficient of â300 Â± 20 Î¼V Kâ1 at room temperature has been reported in Î²-Ga2O3 with a carrier concentration of 6 Ã 1017 cmâ3.23 First-principles calculations also predicted that the Seebeck coefficient of Î²-Ga2O3 at this carrier concentration is â341 Î¼V Kâ1, which is consistent with the experimental data. With the temperature increasing to 1000 K, the Seebeck coefficient can further increase to about â450 Î¼V Kâ1.24 A larger Seebeck coefficient will generally lead to a high power factor. Thirdly, since the Î²-Ga2O3 polymorph is a monoclinic crystal, it has relatively low symmetry, which provides a large phase space for phonon scattering, making its lattice thermal conductivity relatively low, and the lattice thermal conductivity of Î²-Ga2O3 will show a certain degree of anisotropy. First-principles calculations showed that the lattice thermal conductivities of Î²-Ga2O3 in the [100], [010] and [001] directions at room temperature are 16.06, 21.54 and 21.15 W mâ1 Kâ1, respectively,25 while the experimentally measured thermal conductivities of the Î²-Ga2O3 single crystal are 10.9 Â± 1.0, 27.0 Â± 2.0 and 14.6 Â± 1.4 W mâ1 Kâ1 along the [100], [010] and [001] directions, respectively.26 This indicates that the lattice thermal conductivity of Î²-Ga2O3 is much lower than that of ZnO (47 W mâ1 Kâ127). Based on the above three factors, Î²-Ga2O3 is expected to obtain better thermoelectric properties than ZnO.

However, up to now the systematic study of thermoelectric properties of Î²-Ga2O3 is still very scarce. In this work, the thermoelectric performance of Î²-Ga2O3 is comprehensively evaluated by the first-principles calculation combined with Boltzmann transport theory. The potential of Î²-Ga2O3 in the field of TE conversion is compared with ZnO, which is presently an excellent oxide TE material. Our results indicate that Î²-Ga2O3 has excellent thermoelectric performance, which would be a promising high temperature thermoelectric material.

The electronic band structure of Î²-Ga2O3 is displayed in Fig. 2. Due to the fact that the temperature considered in this work exceeds 1000 K, the effect of thermal expansion on the band structure should be considered. So we also calculated the band structure with the lattice parameters at 1200 K (as shown in Fig. S1, ESIâ ).41 The lattice constant increases with temperature, but the effect on the band structure is negligible at finite temperature. Since the PBE functional will underestimate the band gap of semiconductors, the energy band structure computed by applying HSE06 is also presented in Fig. 2(c). A direct band gap of 4.84 eV is obtained, which is in line with the experimental value of 4.80 eV.31 The conduction band minimum (CBM) is located at the Î point, and the valence band maximum (VBM) is between Z and I1. Since the energy difference between the VBM and valence band extremum at Î is only 0.04 eV, it is often artificially regarded as a direct band gap semiconductor.42â44 In addition, Î²-Ga2O3 is an intrinsic n-type semiconductor, and the VBM of Î²-Ga2O3 is very flat, making the effective mass very large, resulting in poor p-type thermoelectric performance. So in this work the focus is put only on the characteristics of the n-type Î²-Ga, and the electronic structure properties are essentially not affected. Although Î²-Ga has a monoclinic structure with low symmetry, the conduction bands are isotropic. This is mainly due to the fact that the conduction band is mainly contributed by Ga 4s orbitals and O 2p orbitals, and there is a strong hybridization between these two orbitals, as shown in Fig. 2(b). The band effective mass of electrons at the Î point is also calculated, and the results show fundamental isotropy. The electron effective mass using the HSE hybrid functional is 0.27 me along three different directions. This value agrees very well with previous calculation results with HSE and also the experiment value of 0.28 (0.26) me.42

To calculate the electrical conductivity Ï, it is necessary to obtain the carrier relaxation time Ï, which is related to the carrier mobility Î¼e. The carrier mobility depends on a variety of scattering mechanisms. For Î²-Ga2O3, the influence of different scattering mechanisms on carrier mobility has been discussed in previous works, such as polar optical phonon scattering, ionized impurity scattering, and nonpolar optical phonon scattering.24,33,51 Conventionally, the carrier mobility is calculated using the deformation potential theory. As POP scattering is the dominant scattering mechanism at room temperature in Î²-Ga2O3, we only considered POP scattering to obtain the carrier mobility in this work (the corresponding parameters are shown in Table S1, ESIâ ). By comparing the results of the power factor in Fig. 6 and ZT in Fig. 8, it can be found that the optimal carrier concentration for the ZT value is different from that for the power factor. It is interesting to find that the optimal carrier concentration for power factor increases with increasing temperature, while that for ZT changes little, especially at higher temperatures. This suggests that once the electrical transport of Î²-Ga2O3 is tailored under the optimal conditions, it can be applied to other higher temperatures with persistent high performance.

Fig. 9 shows the calculated optimal ZT value of Î²-Ga2O3 as a function of temperature along the [100], [010] and [001] directions using four different carrier mobilities. The optimal ZT increases with temperature, which is consistent with previous studies. It is worthy to note that the increasing rate of ZT also increases with temperature. This suggests that Î²-Ga2O3 is especially appropriate for high temperature TE materials. Since the electrical conductivity is strongly dependent on the carrier mobility, a low carrier mobility will lead to a lower ZT value, as indicated in Fig. 8 and 9. In addition, due to the anisotropy of lattice thermal conductivity, the ZT values also differ along different directions as shown in Fig. 9. In a very recent study, Ashish Kumar et al. obtained a ZT value of 0.01 at 300 K in a Sn-doped Î²-Ga2O3 with a carrier concentration of 2.15 Ã 1018 cmâ3 and mobility of 90 cm2 Vâ1 sâ1.45 By using this carrier mobility and carrier concentration, our calculation gives a ZT value of 0.007 at 300 K, which is in good agreement with the results of Kumar et al. This further confirms the reliability of our calculations, and a high carrier mobility and optimal carrier concentration are both necessary to achieve good thermoelectric performance.

Based on the above results and discussion, it can be concluded that an elaborate tailoring of the electrical transport properties of Î²-Ga2O3 to attain the optimal carrier concentration is the first and the most important step for high thermoelectric performance. High quality crystals are also necessary for high carrier mobility. Besides, the lattice thermal conductivity is another important parameter to further improve the ZT of Î²-Ga2O3. The effective way is to introduce various phonon scattering centers such as grain boundaries, point defects, nanoprecipitates and pores. If the lattice thermal conductivity at 300 K is reduced to below the amorphous limit (1.49 W mâ1 Kâ1) by introducing strong phonon scattering centers such as pore structures,66,67 the optimal ZT along the [100] direction will be higher than 0.16. Since the crystal structure of oxides still remains after introducing nanopore structures, the Umklapp phononâphonon scattering process will cause a further decrease of ÎºL with increasing temperature as opposed to the increase of ÎºLmin shown in Fig. 7(b). Therefore the ZT values at higher temperatures will also become higher, which leads to a substantial increase in the average ZT, thus a high conversion efficiency will be achieved.

In this work, we also tried the further optimization of thermoelectric performance of Î²-Ga2O3 by decreasing the crystal size to the nanometer scale for introducing grain boundaries. The dependence of the lattice thermal conductivity on the grain size after nanocrystallization was calculated. The lattice thermal conductivity of finite-size (10, 30, 50, 100, 150 and 200 nm) Î²-Ga2O3 as a function of temperature is shown in Fig. S5 (ESIâ ). The ÎºL decreases with decreasing grain size for Î²-Ga2O3 due to the enhanced boundary scattering. When the crystal size decreases to 100, 50 and 10 nm, the ÎºL in the [100] direction at 300 K is reduced by 31% (8.42 W mâ1 Kâ1), 44% (6.84 W mâ1 Kâ1) and 73% (3.27 W mâ1 Kâ1), and at 1600 K it is is reduced by 8% (1.95 W mâ1 Kâ1), 15% (1.82 W mâ1 Kâ1) and 39% (1.30 W mâ1 Kâ1), respectively. As a result, the optimal ZT values of Î²-Ga2O3 can be further improved by decreasing the grain size.

4 Conclusions

In summary, the thermoelectric properties of Î²-Ga2O3 were systematically studied using first-principles calculations and Boltzmann transport theory. The results show that a high power factor of 3.1 Ã 10â3 W mâ1 Kâ2 can be obtained at a carrier concentration of 1.35 Ã 1020 cmâ3 taking the carrier mobility to be Î¼ = 300 cm2 Vâ1 sâ1. The lattice thermal conductivity shows clear anisotropy, which is 23.1, 13.2 and 12.2 W mâ1 Kâ1 at 300 K along the [010], [001] and [100] directions, respectively. As a result, a maximum ZT value of 1.07 at 1600 K along the [100] direction can be achieved. It is obvious that the thermoelectric properties of n-type Î²-Ga2O3 are superior to ZnO, suggesting that Î²-Ga2O3 is a potential candidate for high temperature thermoelectric materials. Furthermore, if the grain size of Î²-Ga2O3 is reduced to 10 nm through nanocrystallization, the lattice thermal conductivity is reduced to 1.30 W mâ1 Kâ1 at 1600 K, and the optimal ZT may be enhanced.
