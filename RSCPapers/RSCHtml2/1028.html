Multiscale modelling of charge transport in P3HT:DIPBI bulk heterojunction organic solar cells†

Abstract

Charge transport properties of a P3HT:DIPBI bulk heterojunction solar cell are modelled by kinetic Monte Carlo simulations based on a morphology obtained from coarse-grained molecular dynamics. Different methods for calculating the hopping integrals entering the charge transfer rates are compared and calibrated for hole transport in amorphous P3HT. The influence of intermolecular and intramolecular charge transfer on the total charge carrier mobility and hence the power conversion efficiency is investigated in detail. An analysis of the most probable pathways with low resistance for hole transport is performed, establishing a connection between charge mobility and morphology.

1 Introduction

Bulk heterojunction organic solar cells (BHJ-OSC) have been developed as a light-weight and low-cost alternative to conventional photovoltaic devices.1–8 Commonly, they are composed of a donor polymer, e.g. poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT)9–19 and a fullerene-based acceptor, e.g. PCBM.20,21 However, due to the limited chemical tunability of fullerenes, a number of non-fullerene acceptors,22–25 mostly perylene bisimide (PBI) derivatives,24,26–36 have been proposed leading to rapidly increasing power conversion efficiencies (PCE) of donor–acceptor blends.3,37–50

Although a number of rational considerations enter the development of this type of solar cell, it is still hard to predict its precise morphology, which plays a crucial role in the photovoltaic performance.51–63 The morphology depends on a number of different factors such as the molecular weight of the donor and acceptor compounds,64–69 solvation effects,70,71 film processing,72,73 regioregularity,12,74 purity and mixing75,76 of donor and acceptor materials77 as well as post-treatment.78,79 Therefore, reliable theoretical predictions on the morphology of a particular blend depending on the fabrication protocol (e.g. annealing temperatures) and its influence on the device properties, such as charge mobility, would be extremely valuable. This presents a huge challenge to theoretical modelling, since it requires the combination of various methods designed for different time and length scales ranging from quantum mechanics on the molecular scale to Monte Carlo simulations on the morphological scale. Such a multiscale simulation approach for charge mobilities in photovoltaic materials has been pioneered by Andrienko, Nelson and co-workers.80–86

A similar approach86 is adopted in this study, which aims to compute the charge carrier mobility in a blend of poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) and the non-fullerene acceptor tetrachlorinated diperylene bisimide (DIPBI)38,87 (Fig. 1) via kinetic Monte Carlo simulations (kMC)88,89 using intra- and intermolecular charge transfer rates based on Marcus theory.90 The underlying morphologies were previously generated by coarse-grained (CG) molecular dynamics (MD) simulations at different annealing temperatures of 500 K, 700 K, and 900 K before cooling the system down to 300 K and backmapping the structure onto an atomistic representation.91 Subsequently, the morphology is partitioned into charge residence sites (representing individual donor or acceptor molecules or fragments thereof), and charge hopping rates are computed using Marcus theory for all pairs of neighbouring sites. The hopping rates depend sensitively on the various quantities entering the Marcus formula, i.e. the internal and outer-sphere reorganisation energies, site energy difference, charge transfer integrals, and external driving forces. A number of different methods – both semi-empirical and first principles – for calculating these quantities will be assessed with regard to their accuracy and computational speed. Finally, the computed hopping rates are employed in kMC simulations from which information on charge dynamics, including mean site occupations and charge fluxes, is extracted and correlated to the morphology.

It is generally assumed that the charge carrier mobility μ in P3HT is determined by the relative and absolute size of crystalline, semi-crystalline, and amorphous domains in the sample.92,93 In completely amorphous P3HT, the mobility has been determined to be of the order of 10−5 cm2 V−1 s−1 in field effect transistors (FET),11,94 while a range of 10−7–10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1 has been found by time-of-flight (TOF) measurements9,10 and carrier extraction by linearly increasing voltage (CELIV).95,96 The mobility's sensitivity to the magnitude of the external electric field and temperature96,97 may contribute to the large spread in mobility values; however, differences in the morphology are the most likely source.

Numerous correlations between the P3HT molecular morphology and the charge carrier mobility are discussed, such as an increasing fraction of crystalline regimes,98–100 an enhanced order in the π–π-stacking direction,65 the order of the polymer backbone,58,101–104 and the interconnectivity of crystallite regions by tie chains.17,58,105 Random orientation of crystalline domains and charge transport across domain boundaries reduce the mobility as compared to the perfect crystal.106,107 Hence, it is of major importance for efficient charge transport to bypass amorphous regimes.58,108 A desired tailoring of morphological features requires to understand and seize control of the molecular assembly processes and thus of the resulting solid-state short- and long-range order. Experimental studies report a peak in hole mobility as a function of molecular weight Mw, making it a potential tuning parameter due to the direct impact on the evoked micro-structure.68,69

Going beyond the morphology and focusing on individual donor molecules within it, the mobility has been shown to strongly depend on the length and conformation of the polymer chains.

Theoretical transport models along a single polymer strand find an increase of mobility with chain length109 and a saturation for sufficiently long chains.104 This details that charge transport is strongly determined by the polymer conformation58 and limited by chain folding.103 In general, the appearance of rigid chains with few conformational defects leads to an increase in mobility.110 The upper bound in mobility is found in pure crystalline P3HT in thin films with self-organised conjugated lamellae, which yield up to μ = 0.1 cm2 V−1 s−1 in FETs.19,111–114

Charge carrier mobilities in P3HT:PCBM blends can be of the order 10−5–10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1 depending on the quota of regioregular P3HT in the blend15,115–117 and on the annealing temperature.15,118 The mobility can drop by four orders of magnitude in P3HT:PCBM in as-cast devices with respect to pristine P3HT, but can recuperate upon annealing.15 The mobility in pristine PCBM films is higher by one order of magnitude than in the P3HT:PCBM blends.119,120 Increasing the PCBM content to 60–80 wt% increases electron mobility and accordingly improves performance for thicker devices.120

DIPBI has been proposed38,87,121 as a non-fullerene alternative to PCBM. The DIPBI molecule is made up of two linked perylene diimide (PDI) units and possesses a slightly twisted, non-planar geometry (Fig. 1 and ESI,† Fig. S26).

In pure DIPBI organic single-crystal transistors, high charge carrier mobilities in the range of μ = 1.0–4.7 cm2 V−1 s−1121 have been determined, higher than those of PCBM-based OFETs,15,119,120,122 making it a promising candidate for solar cells. Indeed, a respectable PCE of 3.62% was measured in P3HT:DIPBI OSCs.38 There are indications that a further increase in the PCE may be prevented by DIPBI's propensity to form large aggregated π-stacks.91 The present paper aims to unravel the role of DIPBI and the morphology in charge transport in P3HT:DIPBI blends. Moreover, the simulations will provide a fundamental understanding of the relation between charge transport and morphology, which may prove useful for the future development of BHJ-OSCs.

2.4.3 ZINDO-based molecular orbital overlap method. An alternative fast approach to evaluate the charge transfer integral exploits the molecular orbital overlap method based on the ZINDO Hamiltonian.138,139 For each dimer configuration, the method constructs the matrix elements of the ZINDO-based Hamiltonian from weighted orbital overlaps between molecules A and B in their dimer arrangement. The procedure requires only self-consistent calculation of the monomers A and B; their orbitals are then rotated and translated into the correct dimer geometry.

3 Computational details

3.1 Marcus rate evaluation

The wavefunction and geometry optimizations were performed with the Gaussian 09 Rev. D.01 program package.146 For the calculation of the charge transfer integrals and the internal reorganization energy λin in P3HT, the polymer chains are reduced to the polymer backbone, substituting the hexyl side chains by methyl groups, leading to poly(3-methylthiophene) (P3MT).

An in-house code (Charge Transport Package (CTP)147) was developed to control the workflow within the multiscale model. It is provided as a GitHub repository together with data to evaluate charge transfer rates. It assists in the calculation for the components of the charge transfer rates by driving conventional software packages84,146,148–151 and assembles these results for the determination of the charge transfer rates.

The internal reorganization energy λin and the internal site energy difference ΔEin are evaluated using density functional theory (DFT) with the B3LYP functional152,153 and the 6-311G** basis set154–156 for DIPBI and P3MT with different thiophene segment lengths n ∈ [1,32].147

Charge transfer integrals were calculated using the three different methods introduced in Section 2.4. All CDFT-based intermolecular and intramolecular charge transfer integrals132,134,135 were calculated with the CPMD software package149 using the PBE functional157 in conjunction with Troullier–Martins norm-conserving pseudopotentials158 and a plane wave (PW) basis set truncated at an energy cutoff of 80 Ry.

For a DIPBI dimer, an orthorhombic simulation box of size 22.0 × 21.0 × 25.0 Å3 was chosen. For homomolecular P3MT pairs, the box size was adjusted according to their conformation – from 15.0 × 13.0 × 23.0 Å3 to 15.0 × 16.0 × 23.0 Å3 for P3MT 3mers, from 15.0 × 16.0 × 23.0 Å3 to 15.0 × 16.0 × 30.0 Å3 for P3MT 5mers, and from 15.0 × 13.0 × 35.0 Å3 to 15.0 × 16.0 × 39.0 Å3 for P3MT 7mers.

Our in-house code147 was developed to perform DIPRO calculations using orbitals generated by the Gaussian 09 or DFTB+150 programs. DIPRO charge transfer integrals were calculated in combination with the semiempirical PM3,159–161 AM1,162 ZINDO,139,163 and DFTB164,165 methods, as well as with DFT. In the Gaussian 09 package, the DFTB calculations are performed with Slater–Koster files166,167 and the Mio-1-1168,169 parametrization, while the DFTB+ program package uses the self-consistent-charge density-functional tight-binding method (SCC-DFTB)170 approach with the 3OB-3-1 set of parameters for the third-order parametrization for organic and biological systems.171–173 DIPRO/DFT calculations were carried out with the exchange–correlation functionals PBE,157 PBE0,174 and B3LYP in combination with the basis sets 6-31G* or 6-311G**.

The charge transfer integrals with the ZINDO/MOO method138 were computed using the VOTCA program package.84,151

The outer-sphere reorganization energy contributions λout were evaluated taking into account all atoms inside the simulation box using the minimum image convention.84 The Pekar factor was estimated to be cp = 0.05 from the experimentally determined dielectric permittivity of P3HT at high frequencies, εopt = 3.0,175 and at low frequencies, εs = 3.5.176 In the absence of any corresponding data for DIPBI, we approximated the cp value of the blend with that of pure P3HT.

The outer-sphere contributions to the site energy difference, Eel and Epol, (see Section 2.3) were calculated in a self-consistent fashion using the Thole model127,128 with a default cutoff of about routcut = 30 Å,84 using atomic polarizabilities and partial charges. The latter were obtained with Gaussian 09, using the CHelpG method177 with DFT B3LYP/6-311G** by averaging over 10 samples of the same species extracted from the atomistic morphology for its neutral, positively or negatively charged state, respectively.

3.2 Morphology

Atomistic molecular mechanics (MM) simulations are performed using the GROMACS program package.148,178 We use an initial, amorphous P3HT morphology published elsewhere179 with 416 P3HT 32mer polymer chains. It is reheated to T = 900 K for 15 ns in a NPT run with a velocity rescale thermostat,180 a Parrinello–Rahman barostat181 with a constant pressure of p = 1 bar and a time step of Δt = 1 fs. Subsequently, the system is cooled to T = 300 K and run for 1 ns, resulting in a unit cell with an average dimension of (30.6 × 10.4 × 10.5 nm3).

The blend simulations are performed in an orthorhombic box of size 13.9 × 26.9 × 14.6 nm3, containing 416 P3HT polymers made up of 32 thiophene units, each, and 1248 DIPBI molecules, corresponding to a density of ρ = 1.245 g cm−3. We focus on three different morphologies, which were equilibrated in coarse grained molecular dynamics runs at T = 500, 700, and 900 K, followed by a backmapping to an atomistic MM representation and re-equilibration at room temperature.91

The angle density to distance plot was generated with the Molecular Plane Analyser package developed in-house182 using an intermolecular cutoff distance of 15 Å.

3.3 Charge transport simulations

The hopping rates are calculated with CTP147 for the 12 nearest neighbours of every hopping site, defined by the distance of the center of mass (COM) in the nearest image convention in the orthorhombic simulation box. This corresponds to an average distance of the twelfth neighbour of P3HTNN12 = (10.8 ± 1.6) Å for P3HT hopping sites and DIPBINN12 = (14.5 ± 1.6) Å for DIPBI, and leads to an average distance between the hopping sites of AB = (8.4 ± 3.1) Å (Table S3, ESI†). The kMC simulations are based on a set of Marcus rates precomputed with CTP147 and they are carried out with the VOTCA program package.151 As a default, we apply a moderate external field of magnitude |Fext| = 1.0 × 107 V m−111,84 in x, y, or z direction and average over Nkmc = 72 kMC simulations per field direction with Nsteps = 1.0 × 1010 steps, each, of a single charge carrier inside the simulation box to obtain the total mobility at a temperature T = 300 K.

The kMC simulations are initialized as follows. At the beginning of every simulation, a single morphology frame is extracted from the atomistic P3HT:DIPBI MM simulation and partitioned into hopping sites using CTP.147 Each DIPBI molecule is regarded as a single hopping site, due to its extended frontier π-orbitals, whereas the P3HT 32mer chains are separated into subunits according to the torsional angle between the individual thiophene rings. If the dihedral angle θSCCS between two adjacent rings meets the cutoff criterion,

 
(21)
the rings are considered to belong to different hopping sites in the polymer chain. A cutoff dihedral angle of θcutSCCS = 75° was chosen for the SCCS dihedral angle θSCCS between two adjacent thiophene rings based on the delocalisation of the frontier molecular orbitals179 (for more details, see ESI,† Section S7). In the Marcus model, the charge-localized states represent a set of diabatic basis functions.

This procedure results in hopping sites corresponding to polymer segments of different lengths n ∈ [1,32] (ESI,† Fig. S1). For the P3HT morphology, we thus arrive at 1284 hopping sites, yielding 9985 charge transfer pairs in the neighbour list, including 878 intrachain transitions.

For the P3HT:DIPBI blend morphology at 500 K, one obtains a total of 47546 hopping pairs, composed of 3496 DIPBI ↔ DIPBI, 8726 DIPBI ↔ P3HT, and 35324 P3HT ↔ P3HT hopping pairs. The latter category is subdivided into 4851 intrachain and 30473 interchain transitions between hopping pairs. A similar partitioning is obtained for the morphologies at 700 K and 900 K (Table S3, ESI†).

As the employed atomistic force field includes united atom types, the molecules need to be saturated with H atoms at the aliphatic side chains using CTP147 prior to the quantum-chemical calculations. This also applies to the boundaries between intrachain P3HT segments, where additional H atoms are inserted to saturate the segments to be able to perform DIPRO charge transfer integral calculations. These H atoms are afterwards treated as virtual H atoms in the multipole expansion schemes and do not contribute to the outer-sphere energy contributions.

All intermolecular and intramolecular charge transfer rates are pre-evaluated for all pairs in a neighbour list using CTP147 and loaded into the VOTCA program package to perform the kMC simulations.

4 Results and discussion

Before we discuss the kMC simulations, we take a closer look at the key ingredients entering the Marcus rates and compare the performance of a variety of quantum-chemical methods.

4.1 Internal reorganization energy and site energy difference

The internal reorganization energies λin and internal site energy differences ΔEin were all calculated at the DFT/B3LYP/6-311G** level based on the optimized structures of isolated molecules. These intrinsically averaged data sets are applied to all pairs, regardless of relative orientation, conformation or local environment. The results are depicted in (Fig. 2) for hole transfer between DIPBI molecules and both P3MT → DIPBI and DIPBI → P3MT transitions as a function of the thiophene segment length n. The internal reorganization energy between two DIPBI molecules is λin = 0.130 eV.

This is the expected behaviour for a blend of the electron donor (hole acceptor) P3HT and the electron acceptor (hole donor) DIPBI. For hole transfer in the opposite direction (P3MT → DIPBI), the values for ΔEin mostly possess negative sign, due to the relation ΔEinAB = −ΔEinBA (curve not shown). If A and B are the same species, ΔEin = 0 eV.

4.2.2 Intramolecular charge transport in P3HT. The intramolecular charge transfer integral |JintAB| and the vertical energy gap ΔEintAB for P3MT with n = 2 were calculated by CDFT as a function of the dihedral angle θSCCS between two thiophene units (Fig. 5). For this purpose, the dihedral angle θ(SCCS) was varied between −180° and 180° in steps of 2.5°. For each geometry, two CDFT calculations were carried out in which the excess charge is localized on the donor A and on the acceptor B segments, respectively. Subsequently, the charge transfer integral |JintAB| and the vertical energy gap ΔEintAB are evaluated using the two diabatic wavefunctions.
Fig. 5 Intramolecular charge transfer integral |JintAB| (a) and vertical energy gap ΔEintAB (b) for hole transfer in P3MT with n = 2 as a function of the dihedral angle θSCCS (inset) obtained with CDFT.

Fig. 5a shows that |JAB(θ)| has a maximum value of 2.97 eV at θ ≈ −180°, which corresponds to a planar configuration with the methyl groups on the same side of the chain. The value of |JAB(θ)| then decreases with increasing θ and reaches a minimum at −90°. It is worth noting that even at this orthogonal orientation of the two thiophene rings, the charge transfer integral does not completely vanish, but remains at |Jmin| = 0.018 eV. A further increase in θ leads to a second, slightly lower maximum of 2.88 eV at θ = 0°, in which the side methyl groups face away from each other (regioregular P3MT). Overall, the curve exhibits a mirror symmetry with respect to θ = 0°.

The minimum vertical energy gap ΔEintAB is −0.11 eV at θ ≈ −180° (Fig. 5b). The value of ΔEintAB remains practically constant up to θ ≈ −140°, then increases up to a maximum of 0.14 eV at θ ≈ 0°, switching sign at θ ≈ −85°. The slight asymmetry of the curve with respect to θ = 0° can be understood in terms of the underlying molecular asymmetry of the dithiophene.

In the kMC simulations of P3HT or P3HT:DIPBI morphologies, the above dihedral-dependent values of |JintAB| and ΔEintAB are employed as follows. If a pair of two neighbouring P3HT segments in the morphology is separated by an angle θSCCS, which satisfies the cutoff criterion (eqn (21)), then the |JintAB(θ)| and ΔEintAB(θ) values obtained from cubic spline fits to the respective data sets are used to evaluate intrachain hole transfer rates kintAB. This procedure is applied irrespective of the length of the P3HT segments. A transition to a segment on the same chain that is not a direct lateral neighbour is made possible by employing a charge transfer integral |JAB| obtained from the DIPRO method and is considered an intermolecular transfer.

The importance of variations in the torsion angle along the polymer backbone for intrachain charge transport is undoubted.183 The excess charge induces a chain planarization that goes along with a reduction of the amount of energetic disorder along the chain and in its environment.184,185 In the literature, the dihedral dependence of the intramolecular charge transfer integral has been approximated as186

4.3 kMC simulations of pure P3HT

4.3.1 Hole mobility. As a first test of our model, we simulate the hole mobility in the well-studied system of amorphous pure P3HT. Our particular focus is on how different approximations made in the evaluation of the quantities entering the Marcus charge transfer rate affect the simulated charge carrier mobility.

The order parameter S (see ESI,† Section S5 and Fig. S7) of the employed P3HT morphology obtained from molecular dynamics simulations (see Computational details) clearly indicates that the system is in an amorphous phase. This corroborates our finding from visual inspection, where we note the absence of both crystalline regimes and extended spaces of parallel aligned P3HT lamellae. As a first approach to determine the charge carrier mobility, we treat each P3HT chain as a single molecule (n = 32) and compute the site energy contributions Ei = Eel + Epol for 416 P3HT (Fig. S8, ESI†), yielding an average energy of Ēi = (−0.131 ± 0.146) eV (eqn (5)). The average outer-sphere reorganization energy distribution is out = (0.157 ± 0.014) eV (eqn (3)).

Regarding the intermolecular charge transfer integrals |JAB|, the major drawback of treating each P3HT chain as a single entity is that frontier orbitals are not necessarily localized in the region where two neighbouring molecules interact most strongly (ESI,† Section S7 and Fig. S10) This often leads to tiny wavefunction overlaps and thus small values of |JAB|, in turn yielding unphysically small charge transfer rates. As a consequence, a very low mobility of μtot = (1.73 ± 4.04) × 10−14 cm2 V−1 s−1 was obtained from the kMC simulations with default ZINDO/MOO transfer integrals.

In an improved approach, the P3HT polymer chains were partitioned into segments according to a cutoff dihedral angle of θcutSCCS = 75° (Fig. S10, ESI†). All quantities entering the Marcus rate were then calculated based on the individual segments except for the outer-sphere contributions. This opens up a large number of additional pathways leading to a dramatically higher mobility of μtot = (6.21 ± 0.66) × 10−9 cm2 V−1 s−1 (using ZINDO/MOO transfer integrals), due to the ability of the charges to access different conjugated segments along the polymer chains.

As a further improvement, also the outer-sphere contributions, i.e. λout and ΔEout, were calculated with respect to individual segments. In addition, intramolecular hole transport along different adjacent, conjugated segments in a P3HT polymer chain was now taken into account using the CDFT rates calculated in Section 4.2.2. Together with ZINDO/MOO intermolecular charge transfer integrals, this leads to another increase in the total mobility by one order of magnitude to μtot = (9.01 ± 47.0) × 10−8 cm2 V−1 s−1.

In the following, instead of the ZINDO/MOO method for intermolecular charge transfer integrals |JAB|, we use the (unscaled) DIPRO approach in combination with the different electronic structure methods ZINDO, DFTB/3OB, AM1 and PM3 in order to assess the impact on intermolecular hole transport.

The lowest charge carrier mobility of μtot = (1.34 ± 7.76) × 10−12 cm2 V−1 s−1 is found for DIPRO/ZINDO. An increase by an order of magnitude to μtot = (5.41 ± 11.9) × 10−11 cm2 V−1 s−1 is found for DFTB/3OB. The semiempirical methods AM1 and PM3 lead to very similar results of μtot = (1.84 ± 0.25) × 10−9 cm2 V−1 s−1 and μtot = (1.85 ± 0.14) × 10−9 cm2 V−1 s−1, respectively.

In a further step, we employ linearly scaled DIPRO/DFTB/3OB and DIPRO/PM3 charge transfer integrals to match our benchmark CDFT values (see Table 1). For DFTB/3OB, linear scaling leads to an increase in the total mobility by more than one order of magnitude to μtot = (1.15 ± 0.48) × 10−9 cm2 V−1 s−1. Similarly, an increased mobility of μtot = (3.81 ± 8.18) × 10−8 cm2 V−1 s−1 was obtained for PM3.

To study the effect of the outer-sphere contributions to the total mobility, we have also carried out kMC simulations in which the corresponding terms were switched off, i.e. by setting λout = 0 and ΔEout = 0. In combination with unscaled DIPRO/PM3 transfer integrals, we thus obtained a mobility of μtot = (1.87 ± 4.00) × 10−7 cm2 V−1 s−1. Comparison to the result obtained including outer-sphere contributions shows that they have a detrimental effect on hole mobility when they are calculated based on a segmented polymer chain. The mobility is, however, vastly enhanced when the outer-sphere contributions are calculated based on the assumption that the excess charge occupies the entire polymer chain, yielding a value of 1.17 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 s−1.

Experimental hole mobilities in completely amorphous, pure P3HT range from 10−7 to 10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1.9,10,15 An even higher mobility of 0.02 cm2 V−1 s−1 was measured for intrachain transport.190 Typical time of flight experiments are performed with moderate external fields of the order of Fext = 1 × 107 V m−111 to 1 × 108 V m−1.9 This range has also been used for theoretical modelling of photovoltaic materials,84 although the local field strength in the material is not strictly known due to possible screening effects,191 the emergence of space charges192,193 due to (undesired) doping194 and imbalanced charge transport.195,196 However, the charge carrier mobility is known to be extremely sensitive to the applied external field.10 This has also been found in recent theoretical studies using a lower external field of Fext = 1 × 104 V m−1,16 resulting in a charge carrier mobility of μ ≈ 10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1 for amorphous P3HT. Theoretical mobilities of the same order of magnitude have also been obtained for a vanishing external field using the ZINDO electronic structure method and a constant reorganization energy.197

To further investigate the influence of the external field strength, we have carried out kMC simulations at various values of Fext between 1 and 1010 V m−1. As one can see from Fig. S12 (ESI†), the mobility monotonously increases with decreasing field strength. For Fext = 1 × 104 V m−1, we obtain a mobility of μ ≈ 10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1, in agreement with Adamo et al.16 Clearly, it is possible to obtain any mobility value by adjusting the external field including the experimental results. Therefore precise knowledge of the local electric field present in the material would be highly desirable.

Furthermore, there are a number of other approximations made in the multiscale modelling approach applied here that can potentially impact the mobility, e.g. finite size effects97,198 or the reduction of the P3HT chain length to 32 segments, which corresponds to a molecular weight of 5.3 kDa. Experimentally, molecular weights ranging from 5 kDa to 72 kDa and beyond have been used resulting in mobility variations of up to two orders of magnitude.68,69,103,114,199 Moreover, hole mobility crucially depends on the degree of crystallinity99,100,104 and the locally ordered packing of chain segments103,200 in the device,201 which in turn depends on the length of the polymers used. Such ordered domains are completely absent in our morphology, which is consistent with the low mobilities observed in our simulations.

Another limitation of the present model is the assumption of fixed nuclei during charge transport, not allowing for any feedback of the electronic dynamics onto the nuclear subsystem. Hence polaronic effects, which have been shown to be important in other polymeric materials202–204, are not included here.

To summarize, the choice of electronic structure method for the evaluation of the charge transfer integrals and the outer-sphere contributions influences the simulated hole mobility significantly. From the comparison of our kMC results with literature values, we conclude that charge transfer rates based on Marcus theory using the scaled DIPRO/PM3 method for interchain charge transfer integrals and the CDFT-based intrachain charge transfer integrals in combination with outer-sphere reorganization energies as well as electrostatic and polarization contributions to the site-energy difference constitute a good compromise between accuracy and computational effort for the determination of the hole mobility in amorphous P3HT.

5 P3HT:DIPBI blend

5.1 Morphologies

In the following, we will focus on the characterization of the three different P3HT:DIPBI morphologies generated with annealing temperatures of 500 K, 700 K, and 900 K in coarse-grained simulations. Since some differences in the UV/vis spectra of the three systems were found in ref. 91, we wondered if charge carrier mobilities also differ. First we analyse the planarity of the P3HT chains and then describe the stacking behaviour of DIPBI in the blend.

We define a planarity descriptor P for individual P3HT 32mers (see ESI,† Section S9, eqn (2)), determine its distribution for all chains inside the morphology, and compare the average values for an entire blend morphology (Fig. S13, ESI†). The three morphologies obtained at 500 K, 700 K, and 900 K give very similar values of = 7.66 ± 0.96, 7.63 ± 0.93 and 7.75 ± 1.00, respectively. In contrast, the value for pure P3HT is much higher ( = 17.95 ± 1.07), meaning that the planarity of the P3HT chains decreases significantly in the presence of DIPBI. This is corroborated by our finding that conjugated segments are typically longer in pure P3HT (Fig. S1, ESI†).

In order to characterize the aggregation patterns of DIPBI, we calculated the intermolecular distance dAB = |dB − dA| and the angle α = arccos(nA·nB) enclosed by the two normal vectors nA and nB for every pair of DIPBI molecules A and B within a 15 Å radius (see ESI,† Section S10 for the precise definition of dAB and nA,B). The density plots shown in Fig. 9 illustrate the frequency with which distinct relative DIPBI/DIPBI arrangements occur in the simulation box as a function of dAB and α. The DIPBI molecules are seen to favour π–π-stacking with the molecular planes aligned parallel to each other. This is indicated by the bright areas around α = 0° and α = 180°. In the density plot for the 500 K morphology, a bright spot is visible at about dAB = 4.0 Å, which can be attributed to nearest neighbour stacking. The bright feature extends up to dAB = 6.0 Å, corresponding to half overlapping DIPBI molecules, which stack with only one PBI wing, each. Analogous patterns emerge with lower density at dAB = 8.0 Å and dAB = 12.0 Å, corresponding to the second-nearest and third-nearest neighbours, respectively. The obscure region in the center around α ∈ [30°,170°] and dAB < 10 Å corresponds to physically unrealizable arrangements due to the spatial extension of the unflexible DIPBI core without side chains measuring about 15 × 15 × 4 Å3. At larger distances of dAB > 10 Å, one can also find tilted pair configurations including orthogonally aligned T-stacks.


Fig. 9 Density plots of the distance dAB and the angle α between the normal vectors to the DIPBI planes for three P3HT:DIPBI morphologies at 500 K, 700 K and 900 K.

The spots corresponding to DIPBI/DIPBI stacking arrangements are more smeared in the 700 K morphology owing to lateral shift, and the hot spot at dAB = 4.0 Å is reduced in intensity. The number of pairs in the range dAB = 12.0–15 Å is increased, when compared to 500 K.

The 900 K morphology exhibits a higher tendency for aggregation from dAB = 4.0 Å up to dAB = 7.0 Å, where the hot spots are smeared out along the distance axis due to more shear movement along molecular planes at higher temperatures. A reduced number of pairs are found for T-stacks in the interval α ∈ [50°,150°] and at dAB = 10–14 Å.

All in all, the three density plots detail the presence of stacking aggregates.

The radial distribution functions g(r) of the hopping sites centres do not alter significantly for the three morphologies (see ESI,† Section S11 and Fig. S15). Also the size distributions of the occurring P3HT segments are similar (Fig. S1, ESI†).

5.2 Hole mobility

In the following, we discuss kMC results obtained with different approximations and morphologies. We start with kMC simulations performed for a single time frame from the morphology at 500 K with charge transfer integrals |JAB| calculated using the CDFT/PBE and unscaled DIPRO/PM3 approaches for intramolecular and intermolecular hops, respectively, and internal reorganization energies λin computed at the DFT/B3LYP/6-311G* level. If we set all outer-sphere contributions to zero, we obtain a rather high charge carrier mobility of μ = (6.02 ± 0.05) × 10−3 cm2 V−1 s−1. The inclusion of outer-sphere reorganization energy contributions λout for a whole P3HT 32mer results in a reduction of the charge carrier mobility by four orders of magnitude to (2.60 ± 0.06) × 10−7 cm2 V−1 s−1.

Additional incorporation of site energy differences ΔEout contributions for a whole P3HT 32mer does not alter the mobility significantly, yielding a value of (3.07 ± 0.08) × 10−7 cm2 V−1 s−1. Analogous simulations for the morphologies obtained at 700 K and 900 K give mobilities of (2.58 ± 0.14) × 10−7 cm2 V−1 s−1 and (5.27 ± 0.15) × 10−8 cm2 V−1 s−1, respectively. These findings correlate with the increasing degree of order found with decreasing annealing temperature.91

Scaling the DIPRO/PM3 intermolecular transfer integrals to the CDFT reference data dramatically raises the hole mobility to (8.41 ± 0.07) × 10−3 cm2 V−1 s−1 for the 500 K morphology - an increase by four orders of magnitude including outer-sphere contributions for entire polymer chains (see ESI,† Section S6)

The influence of the external field strength on the charge carrier mobility was investigated in the interval Fexti ∈ [1.0 × 107, 3.0 × 108] V m−1 (Fig. 10). Overall, an inverse field dependence can be seen, i.e. the mobility decreases with increasing external field by up to four orders of magnitude in this interval. It can also be seen that charge transport is practically isotropic. The curves for μx, μy, and μz appear to be particularly close together at low external fields, but this is owed to the logarithmic scale.

The coefficient β is temperature dependent and has been shown experimentally to become negative above 250 K in the case of amorphous P3HT10 in agreement with our results (see Fig. S12, ESI† for pure P3HT). 6 Conclusions

A multiscale model to investigate the interplay of donor and acceptor components in a P3HT:DIPBI donor–acceptor blend and the resulting effects on charge transport properties in an organic bulk heterojunction solar cell has been presented. It employs morphologies obtained from coarse-grained molecular dynamics simulations, back-mapped onto an atomistic representation, and charge transport simulations using the kinetic Monte Carlo method in combination with Marcus charge transfer rates, which are parametrised using first principles or semiempirical electronic structure approaches. We find that the choice of method to compute charge transfer integrals and reorganization energies can influence the resulting charge carrier mobilities immensely. Linear scaling factors have been established to enable the use of DIPRO-based charge transfer integrals from computationally efficient semiempirical methods whilst maintaining the order of magnitude of CDFT reference data. Comparison of kMC simulations with and without intramolecular charge transfer within the P3HT oligomers clearly reveal the importance of intramolecular contributions to the overall charge carrier mobility. The analysis of low resistance pathways established useful correlations between hole transport efficiency and local morphology. In particular, it revealed that both donor and acceptor species contribute to efficient hole transport. Hence, a good stacking behaviour is required not only inside the respective D and A domains, but also at the interfaces. This knowledge could be useful to guide the design of future D/A blends. A mild influence of annealing treatments on the charge carrier mobility was found.
