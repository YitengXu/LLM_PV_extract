Graphene-guided growth of rare earth-doped Bi2Mo2O9 nano self-assembly for enhanced asymmetric supercapacitor device performance

Abstract

Nanoparticle self-assembly optimizes super-capacitive behaviour with sustainable life and ascertains the most optimal active material for electrodes. A nano self-assembly consisting of rare earth (RE = Ce and Gd)-doped Bi2Mo2O9 (Bi2xRExMo2O9) and reduced graphene oxide (rGO)-modified Bi2xRExMo2O9 (GM-Bi2xRExMo2O9; RE = Ce and Gd) was synthesized using a gel combustion method. The phase formation of the as-synthesized nano self-assembly was confirmed using X-ray diffraction. Raman spectroscopy and XPS analysis confirmed the optical properties and chemical composition of the prepared materials. The morphology of the synthesized nano self-assemblies and their effect on the rare earth doping and rGO modification were explored using SEM and TEM. BrunauerEmmettTeller analysis confirmed the surface area and pore volume. Through CV, GCD, and EIS analysis, the electrochemical performance of the nano self-assembly was examined. GM-Bi2xRExMo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd) exhibited high specific capacitance in a three-electrode configuration along with feasible cyclic stability. The fabricated asymmetric supercapacitor device of GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 and GM-Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9 exhibited an extraordinary energy density of 58.96 W h kg1 and 56.67 W h kg1, respectively, with an equivalent power density of 750 W kg1. However, GM-Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9 exhibited a higher cyclic stability of 86.55%, with the fabricated GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 asymmetric device providing a superior super-capacitive performance. We improved the electrochemical performance of bismuth molybdate by rare earth doping and rGO modification, and it can potentially be a desirable electrode material.

1. Introduction

Global energy consumption is substantially increasing in response to the modern economy and the rapid increase in population. With increasing concerns regarding environmental pollution due to the inordinate use of fossil fuels, researchers are probing alternative energy storage and conversion devices.1 Consequently, a wide range of energy storage systems has been investigated, including batteries, fuel cells, and electrochemical supercapacitors. Supercapacitors with enhanced power density and capacitance, extended cycle stability, and rapid charge and discharge rates that are inexpensive as well as nontoxic are viable candidates for sustainable power storage systems.25 However, there are limits to the applications of supercapacitors due to their lower energy density when compared to batteries, which have drawbacks such as overheating problems, lower cyclic performance, and restricted power performance.6,7

There are two categories of ultracapacitors, which are commonly known as supercapacitors: (i) electric double layer (EDLC), where storing the energy occurs due to charge accumulation at the electrodes and electrolyte interface (non-faradaic process), and (ii) pseudocapacitors, which encounter reversible oxidation and reduction reactions (faradaic process).8,9 Supercapacitor commercialization will rapidly occur if an increase in their energy density can be achieved that is compatible with their sustainable life cycle. There is a demand for ideal electrode materials to overcome their limitations. Carbon,10 transition metal oxides,11 metal sulfides,12 transition metal dichalcogenides,13 and polymers (polyaniline, polypyrrole, and polythiophene)14 have all been thoroughly examined as electrode materials for energy storage. Because of their porous surface area, supercapacitors based on carbon are inexpensive, with extended lifetimes and rapid charge kinetics that enhance their electrochemical behaviour.15,16

Bismuth molybdates are auruvillis family members with the molecular formula Bi2O3. xMoO3, where x = 1, 2, 3 corresponds to -Bi2Mo3O12, -Bi2Mo2O9 and -Bi2MoO6, which are three phases differing in their crystal structure that can incorporate perovskites such as (MoO4)2 and fluorite-like (Bi2O2)2+ slabs that are alternately positioned. Because of its exceptional features such as low melting point, thermal conductivity, and expansion, there is a broad array of applications for bismuth molybdate in energy storage,17 gas sensors,18 photocatalytic performance,19 and solar cells.20 Electrode materials, i.e., the as-synthesized materials that act as a host, undergo improved surface chemical states with enhanced conductivity and more optimal oxidation and reduction processes when doped with rare earth metals. Because of the rare earth metal complexes and their high ionic radius, doping them in active material could prompt lattice distortion, leading to lower crystallites and relatively low charge transfer resistance (Rct). Diminished Rct enhances the specific capacitance and performance rate with increased electrical conductivity.2123 The gel matrix method, which is inexpensive, aids in producing high-purity material. A gel matrix achieves high uniformity at low temperature and pressure when produced using conventional synthesis techniques such as hydrothermal, wet chemical, and coprecipitation.2426

Seevakan et al. prepared Bi2Mo2O9 spherical nanoparticles using a microwave combustion method. Excellent electrochemical behaviour of the synthesized particles was observed that enhanced the supercapacitor performance due to the specific capacitance (Cs) of 197 F g1 at 2 mA cm2 with a capacitive retention of approximately 93% after 1000 cycles.27

In this work, we used a gel combustion method to synthesize rare earth-doped bismuth molybdate (Bi2xRExMo2O9) that was able to undergo reduced graphene oxide (rGO)-modified nano self-assembly (GM-Bi2xRExMo2O9; RE = Ce and Gd). The purity and the phase confirmation of the prepared samples were analyzed by X-ray diffraction (XRD). The shape and surface morphology of the GM-Bi2xRExMo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd) nano self-assembly was characterized using scanning electron microscopy (SEM). Raman and ultraviolet (UV)-visible spectroscopy were used to examine the optical behaviour of the prepared products. An extensive analysis was performed on the impact of the rare earth-doped nano self-assembly. The electrochemical properties of the prepared nano self-assembly were explored through cyclic voltammetry (CV), galvanostatic charge/discharge (GCD), and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) analysis. A well-formed electrode was used to construct an asymmetric supercapacitor, and the electrochemical performance of the device was examined. Herein, cerium and gadolinium were incorporated as dopants to the nano self-assembly, which was expected to exhibit significant electrochemical behavior with sustainable cyclic stability.

2. Experimental section

2.1 Materials

Ammonium heptamolybdate tetrahydrate ((NH4)6Mo7O244H2O), bismuth(III) nitrate pentahydrate (Bi(NO3)35H2O), and citric acid monohydrate (C6H8O7H2O) were all acquired from Merck. Cerium nitrate hexahydrate (Ce(NO3)36H2O) and ethylene glycol (C6H6O2) were procured from CDH India and Himedia, respectively. In this research, all compounds were used without further purification.

Gadolinium oxide powder (Gd2O3) purchased from Material, Technologie & Kristalle GmbH (MaTeck) was dissolved in nitric acid and gadolinium(III) nitrate hexahydrate (Gd(NO3)36H2O), and then recrystallized in deionized water for further use. Polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF), N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP), and activated carbon were used for the electrode preparation.

2.2 Synthesis of pure and rare-earth doped Bi2Mo2O9 nanoparticles

A solgel auto-combustion method was employed to produce Bi2Mo2O9 nanoparticles. The synthesis was accomplished in the following manner. The precursors used in this experiment were 1.75 mmol of bismuth(III) nitrate pentahydrate (Bi(NO3)35H2O), 0.25 mmol of ammonium heptamolybdate tetrahydrate ((NH4)6Mo7O24), and 2 mmol of citric acid monohydrate (C6H8O7H2O). One mmol of (NH4)6Mo7O24 was dissolved in 45 mL of deionized water, and 5 mL of acetic acid was used to blend the Bi(NO3)35H2O with the prior molybdate compound. The preceding solution was sustained by continuous stirring for one hour to achieve a homogenous solution. The solution was then treated with the required volume of citric acid to generate the citrate complex. In addition, a few drops of ethylene glycol were added to enable the formation of gel in the solution.28 The obtained solution was then stirred at 80 C for gel formation.

To initiate the combustion reaction, the obtained dried flakes were preheated at 300 C. After thermal treatment, the samples were extracted and annealed at 550 C to obtain a precise Bi2Mo2O9 phase.2931 Next, 10% of Ce(NO3)36H2O and Gd(NO3)36H2O precursors were added to synthesize Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 and Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9 by adopting the above procedure. The obtained rGO (30 mg) synthesized using a modified Hummers' method was incorporated in the synthesis of GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9, and GM-Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9 using a gel matrix method. The amounts of the precursors used for the synthesis of the samples appear in Table S1. The prepared product was subsequently used for all ensuing characterizations.

2.3 Preparation of electrodes

A homogenous slurry of Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 and GM-Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd) was formulated by combining the solvent NMP with the active material, PVDF, and activated carbon (85%, 5%, and 10%), respectively, for the working electrode, which was coated on Ni-foam and thermally treated for 12 h at 70 C. In addition, a separator-like filter paper was inserted between the positive (GM-Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd)) and negative (activated carbon) electrodes to allow only the conduction of ions. From the two-electrode and three-electrode configuration, the electrochemical behaviour of the synthesized nano self-assembly was studied.

2.4 Instrumentation

The purity and phase confirmation of the prepared nanoparticles were investigated by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD, Rigaku, Japan), with a diffraction angle range of 5 to 55 at a scan rate of 2 per min. The Raman spectral studies of the pristine sample were conducted using a Horiba Jobin Yvon HR 800 Raman spectrometer under the excitation of 532 nm. The surface morphology and elemental mapping of the as-synthesized samples were examined with a ZEISS EVO 18 scanning electron microscope and a JEOL JEM-2100 Plus transmission electron microscope. The surface area and the pore size of the samples were examined using a Quantachrome Nova Station surface area and pore size analyzer and the BrunauerEmmettTeller (BET) method. The CV, GCD, and EIS electrochemical characteristics of the prepared nanoparticles were examined using a BioLogic SP-150 electrochemical workstation.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Structural analysis

The purity and the phase formation of the formulated Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 and GM-Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd) nanoparticle self-assembly were examined using XRD. Fig. 1 depicts the comparative XRD pattern of pure Bi2Mo2O9, Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9, and GM-Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd). The peaks obtained for the as-synthesized Bi2Mo2O9 agree with standard data. An intense double-shouldered peak was observed at 2 = 28.11 for undoped Bi2Mo2O9 relative to the (2 3 0) plane. In addition, rare earth to the Bi2Mo2O9 double shoulder disappeared and became a single peak. Corresponding to the plane (1 3 2), Bi2Mo2O9 exhibited a peak at 2 = 29.3. There were numerous small intense peaks at 2 = 32, 33.3, and 46.84 for Bi2Mo2O9. When Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 and Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9 were modified with rGO and thermally heated, these peaks disappeared. Variation in peaks might be the effect of dopants, and no impurity peaks were observed in the rGO-modified nano self-assembly. The samples with rGO annealed at 550 C did not show any rGO peaks, indicating that rGO creates patterns in the self-assembly and evolved from the composite at high temperatures.

3.2 Raman spectral analysis

Raman spectral study aids in the analysis of structural characteristics of the material. The Raman spectra of the as-synthesized Bi2Mo2O9, Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9, and GM-Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd) nano self-assembly ranging from 50 to 1000 cm1 is shown in Fig. 2. For pure Bi2Mo2O9, Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9, and the GM-Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd) nanoparticle assembly, considerable peaks are observed within the range of 80 to 200 cm1, with varying intensities of the peaks. The highly intense characteristic peak of Bi2Mo2O9 at 889.5 cm1 became sharp upon further doping of rare earth, which was attributed to the bonds of MO5 moieties.32 The vibration of MoO stretching of orthorhombic MoO6 octahedrons produced the hump-like low-intensity peak at 779 cm1. The peak centered at 289.8 cm1 is ascribed to the bending mode of Eg vibrations. Peaks in the 180500 cm1 range are due to the MoO6 octahedral bending movement. The peaks above 600 cm1 were due to MoO stretching, and below 400 cm1, are in accordance with Mo bonding.33 There was no abrupt change in peak shift with the addition of rGO and rare earth to Bi2Mo2O9.34,35

The surface morphology of the as-synthesized pure Bi2Mo2O9, Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9, and GM-Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd) nano self-assembly was investigated using SEM. The weight percentages of the samples appear in Table S2. Pure Bi2Mo2O9 exhibited Porifera-shaped self-assembled nanoparticles with regular pores. On further addition of Ce and Gd, the shape became disturbed. The Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 enabled complete growth of the particles with some irregular hollow spheres.

3.4 XPS analysis

The elemental composition of the pristine nano self-assembly was examined using XPS. The XPS spectra of Bi 4f, Mo 3d, O 1s, C 1s, Gd 4d, and Ce 3d of Bi2Mo2O9, Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9, and GM-Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd) are depicted in Fig. 5(af). The survey spectrum of the synthesized materials is represented in Fig. S1. The spectrum confirmed that nano self-assembly successfully occurred with the elements Bi, Mo, O, C, Ce, and Gd without any impurities. The binding energies of the pure as-synthesized Bi2Mo2O9 are 165.1 and 159.8 eV, respectively, resulting from Bi 4f5/2 and Bi 4f7/2, and there was a minimal difference in shift with the incorporation of Gd. The binding energies of the characteristic peaks Mo 3d5/2 and Mo 3d3/2 of Mo6+ species at 232.74 and 235.8 eV for Bi2Mo2O9, respectively, were shifted to 233.1 and 236.4 eV with the addition of Gd.36 The Gd 4d spectrum comprises a characteristic peak observed at 160.3 eV.
Fig. 5 XPS graph of (a) Bi 4f, (b) Mo 3d, (c) O 1s, (d) Ce 3d, (e) Gd 4d, and (f) C 1s for Bi2Mo2O9, Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9, GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9, Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9, and GM-Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9.

The peaks at binding energy 903.11, 901.8, and 881.81 eV corresponded to Ce 3d5/2 and Ce 3d3/2. Minor variations in binding energies of the peak reflected the successful incorporation of Gd and Ce.37 There was a characteristic O 1s peak at 530 eV in the high-resolution spectrum of Bi2Mo2O9, and the Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9 and GM-Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9 nano self-assembly exhibited peaks at 531.3 eV. 3.5 Electrochemical analysis

Cyclic voltammetry investigations of the as-synthesized samples were performed using 3 M KOH aqueous electrolyte at 5100 mV s1 scan rates with a potential window range of 00.5 V. Fig. 6(ad) depicts the cyclic voltammograms of (a) Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9, (b) GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9, (c) Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9, and (d) GM-Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9. During this investigation, all of these samples exhibited pseudocapacitive behavior throughout different scan rates in the half-cell test. This behaviour indicates the distinctive redox reactions that occur when ionic moieties pass through the electrolyteelectrode interface. It was also discovered that the redox peaks changed in size as the scan rate was altered, providing further evidence of charge polarization. There were different redox occurrences for all of these samples, as evidenced by their pseudocapacitive behaviour during the half-cell test at low and high scan rates. While observing redox potentials at lower and higher scan rates, Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 spotted oxidation at 0.29 V, reduction at 0.21 V, and Vlower = 0.08 V at 5 mV s1. At a higher scan rate of 100 mV s1, however, the same was observed at 0.34 V and 0.19 V, with Vhigher = 0.15 V. The scan rate difference (Vdifference = 0.15 0.08 V) indicted that the process is quasi-reversible (pseudocapacitive).4042
Fig. 6 CV graph of (a) Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9, (b) GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9, (c) Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9, and (d) GM-Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9.

Because of the increased width in the diffusion layer, the current responsiveness in the CV curve decreased at lower scan rates, whereas the opposite tendency was ascertained at increased scan rates. While scanning, peaks in redox potentials showed a linear correlation between the current and scan rate due to the diffusion-controlled process. During the redox reactions, OH diffusion was distributed among the electrolyteelectrode surface.4347 An in-depth comparison of these electrodes revealed that there was an extended surface area of the GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 nano self-assembled electrode in the potential window of 00.5 V at the scan rate of 5100 mV s1. The incorporation of Ce caused this phenomenon in the nano self-assembled electrode. The smaller-sized Ce exhibited superior ionic conductivity. The addition of the GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 nano self-assembly increased the number of electrochemically active sites, specific capacitance, ionic pathway, and overall electronic transport of the electrode material.

3.6 Electrochemical performance of the fabricated device

The electrochemical behaviour of the nano self-assembly GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9//AC two-electrode assembly was investigated at scan rates from 0 to 1.5 V. This two-electrode assembly used activated carbon as the negative electrode and GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 as the positive electrode, with the corresponding potentials set at 1 to 0 V and 0 to 0.5 V. A sheet of Whatman filter paper separated the anode and cathode. This fabricated device used activated carbon as the negative electrode material due to its greater conductivity and outstanding electrochemical reversibility. The equation was used to calculate the balancing of mass between the electrodes in an asymmetric two-electrode system.63 At first, the specific capacitances of the material under investigation (GM-Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd)) and the activated carbon in the three-electrode set-up were calculated separately. The capacitance and mass values at both electrodes were compared to determine the optimal mass weighted at the cathode in the two-electrode configuration. Only 2 mg of active mass was used on the positive electrodes, and 5 mg was used on the negative electrodes coated on nickel foam to obtain an accurate result for the device's performance. After that, a few drops of KOH electrolyte were placed on both coated Ni-foam electrodes. The KOH electrolyte-drenched Whatman paper was then employed as a separator.

4. Conclusion

In summary, the solgel combustion technique was used to successfully synthesize Bi2Mo2O9, Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9, and GM-Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd). Reduced graphene oxide was incorporated into rare earth-doped bismuth molybdate to enhance and examine its supercapacitive behavior. The monoclinic structure and the phase formation of Bi2Mo2O9 without any impurities were affirmed by XRD. The optical nature of the developed nano self-assemblies was examined using Raman spectroscopy. The surface morphology and the existence of elements of prepared materials were explored using SEM and XPS analysis.

The electrochemical performance of GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 and GM-Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9 nano self-assemblies was scrutinized with CV, EIS, and GCD analysis. In a three-electrode configuration, GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 and GM-Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9 exhibited remarkable specific capacitances of 898 F g1 and 868 F g1 at 1 A g1, respectively. After 5000 cycles, GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 and GM-Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9 displayed an admirable capacitive retention rate of approximately 92.85% and 91.29% @ 5 A g1, respectively. The two-electrode fabricated asymmetric device of GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 and GM-Bi1.9Gd0.1Mo2O9 exhibited a similar power density of 750 W kg1 in accordance with an exceptional energy density of 58.96 W h kg1 and 56.67 W h kg1, respectively. GM-Bi1.9Ce0.1Mo2O9 exhibited an outstanding supercapacitive performance with a capacitive retention of 84.13% after 10000 chargedischarge cycles. The exceptional electrochemical behavior of GM-Bi1.9RE0.1Mo2O9 (RE = Ce and Gd) implies that rare earth doping and rGO modification enhanced their supercapacitive behavior as expected and suggests that they may be reliable electrode materials for energy storage systems.
