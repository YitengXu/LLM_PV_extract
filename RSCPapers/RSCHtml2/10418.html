An open thermo-electrochemical cell enabled by interfacial evaporation

Qingchen Shen , Ziyang Ning , Benwei Fu , Shuai Ma , Zhiying Wang , Lei Shu , Lifu Zhang , Xinyu Wang , Jiale Xu , Peng Tao , Chengyi Song , Jianbo Wu , Tao Deng * and Wen Shang *
State Key Laboratory of Metal Matrix Composites, School of Materials Science and Engineering, Shanghai Jiao Tong University, Shanghai 200240, P. R. China. E-mail: dengtao@sjtu.edu.cn; shangwen@sjtu.edu.cn

Received 23rd October 2018 , Accepted 16th February 2019

First published on 18th February 2019

Abstract

This work explores an alternative approach to generate electric energy from a solar-driven interfacial water evaporation system. A thermo-electrochemical redox couple was combined with an evaporation system to take advantage of the temperature difference between the airliquid interface and the bulk solution of water. An open thermo-electrochemical cell (TEC) was demonstrated to achieve the parallel processes of clean water generation and also electricity generation. Both deionized (DI) water and seawater (from the East China Sea) were used for the preparation of the electrolyte of the TEC system. Under one-sun illumination, the peak output electric power of the TEC with the seawater-based electrolyte was 0.5 mW m2. The electric output of the TEC with the solution of seawater still reached 2.3 102 mW m2 even without solar illumination due to the temperature difference generated by natural evaporation, which provides a possibility for electricity generation at night. The TECs can also be connected in series to increase the output voltage to charge capacitors or power LEDs, as demonstrated in the work.

Introduction

The energy crisis and lack of drinkable water are two critical challenges in modern times.1,2 With the exhaustion of fossil fuels in the not so distant future, the development of renewable energy technologies is urgently needed to sustain the development of society.3,4 In this work, we explored electricity generation during the solar-driven desalination process through an open thermo-electrochemical cell (TEC) enabled by solar-driven interfacial evaporation of seawater.

Interfacial evaporation is an efficient process to generate water vapor through localizing the heat at the airliquid interface,5,6 and one of the most popular applications of interfacial evaporation is the desalination of seawater.7,8 With the first demonstration of desalination using solar-driven interfacial evaporation,7 many studies have been reported in the optimization of the interfacial evaporation system to improve the evaporation efficiency, including the development of broadband and efficient solar absorbers, thermal insulation materials and designs, and liquid transportation approaches.911

With interfacial evaporation, solar energy can be converted into thermal energy and harvested efficiently. Besides converting solar energy into thermal energy stored in water vapor, there also exist other opportunities, for example, the upward flow of water induced by interfacial evaporation, and the concentration difference and the temperature difference between the interface and bulk solution, for energy conversion. These opportunities can enable the generation of electric energy together with the generation of water vapor. Hu et al. and Guo et al. demonstrated that the upward flow induced by interfacial evaporation could be used to generate electric energy based on the streaming potential when the flow went through a nanoporous anodic aluminum oxide (AAO) membrane and carbon black nanostructures, respectively.12,13 Using an ion selective membrane, Zhou et al. reported the generation of electric energy based on the concentration difference that resulted from interfacial evaporation.14 Due to the heat localization at the airliquid interface in interfacial evaporation, the temperature difference between the interface and the bulk can be quite substantial, which provides another opportunity to generate electricity while water vapor is generated.

In this work, we explored electricity generation using an open TEC to take advantage of the temperature difference generated during the interfacial evaporation process. A TEC converts thermal energy into electric energy by using temperature-dependent electrochemical redox potentials. When there is a temperature difference between the two electrodes of the TEC, the oxidation and reduction reactions happen at different electrodes, which generates a steady electric current. Compared with conventional solid thermoelectric material systems, TECs have the potential in achieving a much higher Seebeck coefficient (Se).1520 Since the demonstration of a TEC using Pt electrodes by Quickenden and Vernon,21 carbon electrodes with high surface area and low cost, including multi-walled carbon nanotube (MWCNT) buckypaper,22 single-walled CNTreduced graphene oxide (SWCNTrGO) composite films,23 and CNT aerogel sheets,24 were developed to improve the output power density and thermalelectrical conversion efficiency. For example, the use of the CNT aerogel-based electrodes generated a high output power, 6.6 W m2, for a temperature difference of 51 C, with a Carnot-relative efficiency of 3.95%.24

A proof of concept design, the open TEC, was demonstrated in this work for the parallel processes of clean water generation and electricity generation. Most TECs reported so far are closed systems to avoid the loss of liquid during the operation of TECs. In this work, the access to the open environment enables the evaporation of liquid at the airliquid interface and the generation of the temperature difference needed for the operation of the TEC, so an open design is adapted. The I3/I redox couple was integrated into the interfacial evaporation system to generate the open TEC. Graphite felt served as both the solar absorber for interfacial evaporation and the electrode for the TEC in the system. The open TEC with a seawater-based electrolyte achieved an electric power output of 0.5 mW m2 under one-sun illumination. Compared to the original seawater, the concentrations of the primary ions in condensed water were reduced substantially. In this study, the TECs were also connected in series to increase the output voltage for charging of capacitors and powering of LEDs. The findings in this work provide an alternative approach to the generation of electricity during the solar-driven interfacial evaporation process. The electric output of the TEC with the seawater-based electrolyte still reached 2.3 102 mW m2 even without solar illumination due to the temperature difference generated by natural evaporation, which provides the possibility for electricity generation at night.

Results and discussion

Fig. 1 shows the schematic of an open TEC enabled by interfacial evaporation. Two electrodes are placed at the airliquid interface and the bottom of a beaker, respectively. The surface electrode also serves as a solar absorber to drive interfacial evaporation. The ion pair of I3/I, which shows temperature-dependent electrochemical redox potentials, was used as the redox couple in the TEC. When the system is illuminated by solar light, a temperature difference is built up between the two electrodes, which induces the difference of electrochemical redox potentials of I3/I at the two electrodes to produce electricity. Adding -cyclodextrin (-CD) can potentially increase the Se.16 The -CD shows efficient hostguest inclusion for triiodide (I3) to generate the -CDI3 complex, and the inclusion process also depends on the temperature. At the low-temperature side (bottom of the beaker), the encapsulation of I3 by -CD is enhanced, which decreases the concentration of I3 and promotes the oxidation of I to I3. At the high-temperature side, the reaction is reversed. This temperature-dependent hostguest interaction generates a concentration difference of redox ions between the two electrodes to improve the performance of the TEC.16 The potassium cation from the addition of KCl into the DI water-based electrolyte and the metal cations in the original seawater can shield the electrostatic repulsion in the -CDI3 complex to generate solid complexes at the low-temperature side, which will further enhance the concentration difference of I3 between the two electrodes and improve the Se of the TEC.16,25

Fig. 2a shows the optical image of the graphite felt that serves as both the electrode for the TEC and the solar absorber for the interfacial evaporation system. The graphite felt consists of graphite fibers with a diameter of 20 m (Fig. 2b). As shown in the insets of Fig. 2a, the graphite felt is superhydrophilic with a contact angle close to 0. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was used to characterize the surface of the graphite felt. The deconvoluted C1s XPS spectrum (Fig. 2c) shows three characteristic peaks at 284.6 eV (CC), 285.4 eV (CO) and 288.0 eV (CO).26 The hydroxyl, epoxy and carboxyl functional groups on the surface of the graphite felt generate the peaks of CO and CO.11 Such hydrophilic functional groups, in combination with the microstructure of the felt, result in the superhydrophilic surface of the graphite felt. Fig. 2d shows that the optical absorption of the graphite felt is more than 90% over a broad range of wavelengths (250 nm to 2500 nm). The electrical resistivity of the graphite felt is about 1.2 cm, which was measured using the four-probe approach.

A sealed polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) foam was placed under the top electrode to reduce the downward conductive heat loss. The use of the PDMS foam is beneficial for both the improvement of the interfacial evaporation efficiency and the maintenance of the temperature difference between the two electrodes for the operation of the TEC (Fig. S1).11,27,28 As shown in Fig. 3a, the temperatures of the top and bottom electrodes of the TEC with a DI water-based electrolyte were monitored using thermocouples. The open voltage of the TEC was measured using a multi-channel data acquisition system. Before solar illumination, the temperature of the top electrode was lower than that of the bottom electrode due to the natural evaporation at the airliquid interface. A lower temperature corresponds to a lower electrochemical potential of the redox pair, which induced a negative open voltage of the TEC.16 When the TEC was illuminated by light with an energy density of 100 mW cm2 (one sun) generated using a solar simulator, the top graphite felt absorbed light and its temperature quickly increased, which resulted in an increase of the open voltage. When the temperature of the top electrode was higher than that of the bottom electrode, the open voltage changed to a positive value. When the light was turned off, the temperature of the top electrode gradually decreased due to the continuous evaporation of water at the interface, and eventually returned back to the initial state of the TEC before illumination with the temperature lower than that of the bottom electrode. The infrared (IR) images of the TEC (insets of Fig. 3a) clearly show the temperature differences between the top and bottom electrodes of the TEC with or without light illumination. Based on the open voltage and temperature difference between the top and bottom electrodes at the steady state under illumination, the Se of this TEC was calculated to be 1.4 mV K1, which is much higher than the Se of conventional solid thermoelectric alloys such as Bi2Te3.17,29

The output power of the TEC was calculated based on the voltages measured across the resistances (Fig. 3b) both with and without solar illumination. The peak output power was 1.3 mW m2 for the TEC under one-sun illumination and was 5.3 102 mW m2 for the TEC without solar illumination. It is worth noting that the performance of the TEC without solar illumination is comparable with the performance of the natural evaporation system reported by Guo et al. recently.13 The output power reached 2.1 101 mW m2 in Guo's study, but their system is based on a different mechanism for electricity generation, which involves the use of the directional migration of ions within a carbon black film. Besides the characterization of electricity generation, the evaporation performance of the TEC under one-sun illumination was also studied. As shown in Fig. 3c, the evaporation rate of the TEC is much higher than that of the electrolyte solution without the use of graphite felt. In order to demonstrate the practical applicability of the open TEC enabled by interfacial evaporation, seawater from the East China Sea was used to replace DI water to prepare the electrolyte solution containing KI, I2, and -CD. With substantial metal cations in the seawater, there is no need to add additional KCl into the solution. The electric and evaporation performance of the TEC with the seawater-based electrolyte were then studied. Fig. S5 shows the mass change of the seawater-based electrolyte with and without the TEC under one sun illumination. The evaporation rate of the TEC reached a stable value of 1.1 kg m2 h1 after 30 min illumination. Based on the above calculation, the evaporation efficiency of the TEC with the seawater-based electrolyte was 60%, which was slightly lower than the evaporation efficiency of the TEC with the DI water-based electrolyte. In order to demonstrate the stability of the electricity generation of the TEC with the seawater-based electrolyte under one-sun illumination, the open voltage was monitored for 6 hours of operation. As shown in Fig. 4a, before solar illumination, the open voltage decreased with time due to natural evaporation. Once the simulated solar light was turned on, the voltage quickly increased to about 16 mV. The voltage slowly decreased with time, which might be due to the thermal conduction from the top interfacial electrode to the bottom electrode, as shown in the IR images in Fig. 4a. When the temperature difference between the two electrodes decreased, the open voltage also decreased. After about 2 h solar illumination, the TEC reached a thermal balance and the open voltage reached a stable value (10 mV). When the solar light was turned off, the temperature of the top electrode quickly decreased due to continuous evaporation of water at the interface, which resulted in the decreasing of the open voltage. As the temperature of the top electrode was less than that of the bottom electrode, the open voltage would become negative. Fig. 4b shows the output power of the TEC with and without solar illumination. The corresponding peak output power was about 0.5 mW m2 and 2.3 102 mW m2, respectively. To evaluate the effect of desalination, the concentration of primary ions in seawater and purified water collected after condensation was measured using ion chromatography. As shown in Fig. 4c, for the purified water collected from the condensation chamber, the concentrations of all four primary cations (Na+, Mg2+, Ca2+, K+) in the original seawater were greatly reduced. The concentration of Na+ was decreased almost four orders of magnitude, and the concentration of K+ ions was reduced to the level below the detection limit of the instrument. The concentration of iodine in the condensed water was measured to be 2.7 mg L1 using a mass spectrometer, which means that only an insignificant amount of iodine escaped from the seawater-based electrolyte with an initial iodine concentration of 2854.3 mg L1. The concentration of iodine can be further decreased by adding the condensed water generated from an interfacial evaporation system without the insertion of the TEC.

For practical use, the TECs were connected in series to increase the output voltage. Fig. 4d shows the output voltage of one TEC and also eleven TECs connected in series before and during solar illumination. Compared with the output voltage of one TEC, the steady output voltage of eleven TECs connected in series increased about ten times to reach 200 mV during solar illumination. The TECs connected in series under solar illumination were used to charge a capacitor of 2200 F. The voltage of the capacitor gradually increased as recorded in Fig. 4e. Charged capacitors of 2200 F were then connected in series to further improve the output voltage to power a LED, as shown in the inset image of Fig. 4e.

Conclusions

In summary, this work demonstrated an open TEC system for the generation of electric energy during the solar-driven interfacial evaporation process. The open TEC system takes advantage of the intrinsic temperature difference generated by interfacial evaporation between the interface and bulk water. Both DI water-based electrolyte and seawater-based electrolyte were used in the TEC system. Under one-sun illumination, the evaporation rate of the TEC with the seawater-based electrolyte reached about 1.1 kg m2 h1, and the calculated evaporation efficiency of the TEC was 60%. With improved insulation designs reported recently, the evaporation efficiency could be further increased. After desalination, the primary cations (Na+, Mg2+, Ca2+, K+) of seawater were largely reduced. The peak output electric power of the TEC system with the seawater-based electrolyte reached about 0.5 mW m2. The use of other redox couples, for example, Fe2+/Fe3+ redox couple, can minimize the risks associated with the continuous sublimation of I2. TECs can also be connected in series to charge capacitors or power LEDs. The output electric power can be increased by replacing graphite felt with other electrodes with larger surface areas that can provide more reaction sites for the redox reaction than graphite felt. Increasing the migration of ions through replacing the sealed PDMS foam with some other thermal insulators that ions can pass through can also improve the output power. With the improvement of TECs, the efficiency of the heat to electricity conversion can be further increased. Compared to a capillary driven electrokinetic generator,12,13 this TEC system can operate at a large concentration range of ions, including that of seawater. Zhou et al. also reported an electricity generation system that was based on the concentration difference formed during interfacial evaporation.14 The open TEC approach reported in this work provides an alternative approach for electricity generation during interfacial evaporation, and may operate at a higher solar flux than the system based on the concentration difference. Certainly, these different approaches are complimentary to each other. Our TEC approach can also be combined with other approaches for generating electricity during interfacial evaporation, such as taking advantage of both the capillary flow of water and the concentration difference, to harvest the waste thermal energy from the interfacial evaporation system. The electric output of the TEC with solution of seawater can still reach 2.3 102 mW m2 even without solar illumination due to the temperature difference generated by natural evaporation, which provides a possibility for electricity generation at night.

Experimental

Preparation of electrolyte solution

To prepare the DI water-based electrolyte, KI (207.5 mg) and I2 (63.4 mg) were dissolved in 100 mL DI water, and then -CD (389.1 mg) and KCl (1.5 g) were added to the solution and sonicated. To prepare the seawater-based electrolyte, KI (207.5 mg) and I2 (63.4 mg) were dissolved in 100 mL seawater (from the East China Sea), and then only -CD (389.1 mg) was added to the solution. Sonication was then used to help dissolve the solutes.

Characterization and property measurement

A field-emission scanning electron microscope (SEM) (Sirion 200, FEI) was used to characterize the microstructure of the graphite felt. An X-ray photoelectron spectrometer (XPS) (AXIS UltraDLD, Kratos Analytical Ltd.) was used to study the chemical composition on the surface of the graphite felt. An ultraviolet (UV)-Visible (VIS) spectrometer (Lambda 950, PerkinElmer) was utilized to measure the absorption spectrum of the graphite felt. Thermocouples (TT-K-30-SLE, Omega) and an IR camera (FLIR T620, FLIR Systems, Inc.) were used to monitor the temperature of the TEC system. The transient plane source method (TPS-2500S, Hot Disk) was used to measure the thermal conductivity of the PDMS foam. A multi-channel data acquisition system (Agilent 34972A, Agilent Technologies Inc.) was used to measure the output voltage. Ion chromatography (ICS-5000+/900, Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used to measure the concentration of primary ions (Na+, Mg2+, Ca2+, K+) in seawater before and after desalination. An inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometer (i CAPQ, Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used to measure the content of iodine in condensed water.

Evaporation performance measurement

One-sun illumination with an energy density of 100 mW cm2 was generated using a solar simulator (94023A, Newport). An electronic analytical balance was used to monitor the mass change of the TEC during water evaporation.
