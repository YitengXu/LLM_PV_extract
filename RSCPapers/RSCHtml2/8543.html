Abstract

As electron acceptor materials, organic small molecules with an intra-molecular pushâ€“pull electronic structure have been widely used in high-efficient organic solar cells (OSCs), usually referred to as non-fullerene acceptor (NFA) molecules. Compared with fullerene acceptor molecules, an interesting discovery is that these molecules behave in both photoinduced intra- and inter-molecular charge transfer (CT) effects, thus resulting in some unique photophysical processes and phenomena during the operation of OSCs. In this study, by separately applying a photoexcitation to a single NFA molecule and different molecular aggregates, we clarify the photoinduced electron transition details (including transition mode, peak position, and probability), the resulting intra- and inter-CT dynamics, and the modulating methods (such as the intra-molecular electronic pushâ€“pull potential, the inter-molecular aggregation mode, and the photoexcitation intensity). Clarifications of these microscopic quantum processes should be helpful to further understand the photoinduced experimental observations in NFA molecules, and thus provide a clear direction for their rational design to improve the photoinduced charge yield in their aggregates.

Introduction

Benefiting from the rapid development of non-fullerene acceptor (NFA) molecules, power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) of organic solar cells (OSCs) have increased substantially up to efficiencies of 15â€“19% over the last five years.1â€“3 Compared with fullerene acceptor (FA) molecules, NFA molecules usually have an intra-molecular pushâ€“pull electronic structure, that is, an electron-push central fused ring group and two electron-pull terminal groups, sometimes connected by two Ï€-bridges between them.4â€“6 A significant advantage of combining electron-push and -pull groups is the respective tuning for the molecular HOMO (highest occupied molecular orbital) and LUMO (lowest unoccupied molecular orbital) levels, so as to realize the targeted optimization for their energy level structures.7,8 To date, different strategies have been explored to strengthen the electron-push ability of the central group, such as extending the conjugation length,9 introducing a strong electron-push group,10 and inserting a quinone resonance structure.11 To strengthen the electron-pull ability of the terminal groups, one usually introduces halogen atoms (e.g., fluorine and chlorine).12â€“14 With these optimizations, intra- and inter-molecular charge transfer (CT) effects in NFA molecules have been successively reported.15â€“21

A basic consensus is that the NFA molecular pushâ€“pull electronic structure first induces an intra-CT effect. However, in NFA molecules with only the intra-CT effect considered, their excited state binding energy is still as high as 0.2 eV.16,22 Furthermore, the inter-CT effect was predicted in NFA molecules.16 Experimentally, Wang et al. reported that charge separation in a blend of polymer donor (PM6) and NFA molecules (Y6) is mediated by Y6 excited states instead of the traditional D/A interfacial CT states.17 The excited electron and hole in a Y6 excited state were predicted to be partially separated between Y6 molecules. By utilizing the photoexcitation dependent magneto-capacitance, Zhu et al. also reported that photoexcitation in Y6 molecules results in bulk polarization (i.e., inter-CT effect), which can be improved by strengthening the photoexcitation intensity.18 Recently, Xu et al. employed transient absorption (TA) spectroscopy and observed inter-CT signals in other typical NFA molecules (ITIC, 4TIC, and 6TIC).19 In an earlier work,16 from the static energetic viewpoint, we theoretically clarified that intra- and inter-CT effects intrinsically coexist in NFA molecules, by which the excited state binding energy can be significantly reduced and close to the thermal energy at room temperature.

Despite these advances, an in-depth understanding of the photoinduced intra- and inter-CT dynamics and the relevant experimental observations in NFA molecules remain unclear. For instance, how about the time scale of the photoinduced intra- and inter-CT dynamics? Can it be compared with the time scale for the appearance of stable CT signals observed in experiments?17,20 What are the quantitative correlations of the intra- and inter-CT dynamics with the NFA molecular factors and photoexcitation conditions? In view of these issues, by employing a universal quantum model for NFA molecules with photoexcitation included, we clarify the intra- and inter-CT dynamics, including their quantitative correlations with the molecular factors (e.g., the intra-molecular electronic pushâ€“pull potential and the inter-molecular aggregation mode) and the photoexcitation conditions (e.g., the photoexcitation energy and intensity). In particular, to understand the intra- and inter-CT dynamical results, we firstly reveal the corresponding electron transition details (including transition mode, peak position, and probability) during different photoexcitation processes.

For instance, compared with pristine ITIC molecules, Zhao et al. have experimentally observed a broader absorption range and higher absorption efficiency in fluorinated ITIC molecules (ITIC-4F).13

In NFA molecular aggregates, the photoinduced electron transitions result in an inter-CT effect, as well as an intra-CT effect.31,32 Here, we also take the first peak transition as an example to clarify the photoinduced inter-CT dynamics in different NFA molecular aggregates. Fig. 4e shows the time evolutions of the inter-molecular transferred charge quantity Qintra = âˆ’Î£nqM1,n(t) from the M1 to M2 molecule in the cases of A-to-A type J-aggregation, A-to-D type J-aggregation, and D-to-D type H-aggregation. We can see that inter-CT behaviors take place right after a photoexcitation is applied in all cases. Notably, all the inter-CT dynamics experience apparent relaxation processes (200â€“300 fs), after which the values of Qinter in different molecular aggregates tend to be stable. On the one hand, the results show that the A-to-A type J-aggregation of NFA molecules is more conducive to the inter-CT effect than the other two aggregation modes. Owing to the intra- and inter-CT synergistic effect, the excited state binding energy in NFA molecules can be significantly reduced (lowered to 0.05 eV).16 Therefore, after the inter-CT dynamics tend to be stable, a new stable polaron-like inter-CT signal should be detected in NFA molecules by the TA spectroscopy,17 which is significantly different from the traditional exciton and interfacial CT signals. Recently, by separately adding 1,8-diodoctane (DIO) into ITIC, 4TIC, and 6TIC neat films, Xu et al. obtained more A-to-A type J-aggregation in these molecular films, where they reported a stronger inter-CT TA signal than their films without DIO.19 On the other hand, Fig. 4e shows that the NFA molecular aggregation mode has a weak impact on the time scale of the inter-CT relaxation process. As we know, the inter-molecular coupling can suppress the molecular vibrations and thus lower the molecular reorganization energy.33 So, D-to-D type H-aggregation of NFA molecules experiences the fastest relaxation process (âˆ¼200 fs) due to its maximum coupling area, while A-to-A type J-aggregation experiences the slowest relaxation process (âˆ¼300 fs) due to its minimal coupling area. Furthermore, we consider the effect of the NFA molecular electronic pushâ€“pull potential Î”on on the time scale of the inter-CT relaxation process in a given aggregation mode. A comparison result for A-to-A type J-aggregation of NFA molecules is presented in Fig. 4f, where we can see that the relaxation process can be accelerated by increasing the value of Î”on. For A-to-D type J-aggregation and D-to-D type H-aggregation of NFA molecules, we obtain similar rules with A-to-A type J-aggregation (see Fig. S4 of the ESIâ€  for details). Considering the reason, with increasing the NFA molecular electronic pushâ€“pull potential, the molecular reorganization energy is lowered and thus accelerates the relaxation process.34 From these calculated time scales of the inter-CT relaxation process in different molecular systems, it appears that they are basically consistent with the time scale for the appearance of stable inter-CT TA signals (âˆ¼200 fs) observed in the experiments.17,20

Conclusions

In summary, by separately applying photoexcitation to a single NFA molecule and different molecular aggregates, we investigated the photoinduced electron transition details and the resulting intra- and inter-CT dynamics. It was found that, in addition to the red-shifts of all transition peaks, the NFA molecular electronic pushâ€“pull potential promotes the higher-energy transition modes and improves the transition probabilities of all peak transitions. With the electron transitions, the intra-CT behavior takes place synchronously, and the transferred charges increase with increasing the molecular electronic pushâ€“pull potential. After incorporating the molecular aggregations, we found that the electron transition modes and peak positions change with the aggregation modes. Furthermore, the effect of the photoexcitation intensity was considered, by which we clarified that a photoexcitation in the A-to-A type J-aggregate with a stronger photoexcitation intensity will result in a more apparent inter-CT effect. These results provide a microscopic understanding of the photoinduced experimental observations in different NFA molecular systems, and thus provide a clear direction for their rational designs. For instance, by increasing the molecular electronic pushâ€“pull potential, we can both expand the light absorption and improve the intra- and inter-CT effects (including the transferred charge quantity and time scale); by optimizing the inter-molecular aggregation modes, these light absorptions and CT effects can be further modulated in actual photoelectric applications of NFA molecules.
