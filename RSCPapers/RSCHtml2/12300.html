Abstract

Two non-fullerene small molecule acceptors based on CF3-4,4-difluoro-4-bora-3a,4a-diaza-s-indacene (BODIPY) as the central acceptor core and different donor terminal triphenylamine and carbazole units denoted as CF3-BDP-TPA and CF3-BDP-Cz, respectively, were designed and synthesized, and their optical and electrochemical properties were investigated. Both CF3-BDP-TPA and CF3-BDP-Cz showed a narrow bandgap, similar to the well-known Y6 acceptor. The molecular dipole moment of CF3-BDP-Cz is 5.53 D, which is significantly larger than that of CF3-BDP-TPA (3.347 D) and leads to strengthening the intramolecular (Câ€“Hâ‹¯Ï€) interaction, thereby enhancing molecular packing. We have used conjugated polymer PBDB-T as the donor (HOMO = âˆ’5.22 eV and LUMO = âˆ’3.45 eV), having complementary absorption spectra to both CF3-BDP-TPA and CF3-BDP-Cz. The optimized polymer solar cells based on CF3-BDP-TPA and CF3-BDP-Cz attained power conversion efficiencies of 9.89% and 13.07%, respectively. The higher PCE for the CF3-BDP-Cz-based device is linked with the larger dipole moment of CF3-BDP-Cz compared to that of CF3-BDP-TPA, which leads to a lower exciton binding energy and apparently increases both short circuit current and open circuit voltage. The increased value of the fill factor for the CF3-BDP-Cz-based device compared to that of CF3-BDP-TPA counterpart is associated with the balanced charge transport and suppressed recombination processes.

1. Introduction

Faced with the increasingly severe energy crisis and environmental problems, clean and renewable sources such as solar energy and wind energy need to be developed. Solar cells are a kind of photovoltaic technology, which can directly convert solar energy into electricity. Photon energy coming from the sun can be converted into electricity using the photovoltaic principle of semiconducting materials. As an important part of the solar cell, organic solar cells (OSCs) based on a bulk heterojunction (BHJ) active layer as a promising photovoltaic technology have attracted more and more attention for their possible commercialization.1,2 Compared with silicon-based solar cells, perovskite cells, and dye-sensitized cells, organic solar cells possess many unique advantages, such as adjustable color, lightweightness, wide material sources, low manufacturing cost, and large area manufacturing on flexible substrates.3â€“8 In the early stage of development, OSC-based BHJ active layers, which consist of a blend of polymer or small molecule donors and fullerene acceptors as light-absorbing active layers, have achieved energy conversion efficiencies exceeding 10%.9â€“11 However, some disadvantages of fullerene acceptor materials limit the further improvement of organic solar energy performance, including weak light absorption and insufficient utilization in the visible region, difficult preparation and purification, high synthesis cost, and poor energy level adjustment.12â€“16 Therefore, the development of new electron acceptor materials to replace fullerene derivatives has become a new focus in the field of organic solar cell research. In this regard, non-fullerene small molecule acceptors (NFSMAs) have shown significant advantages due to their low synthesis cost, strong absorption of the solar spectrum in the visible region, higher stability, easy adjustable energy levels, and higher open circuit voltage over fullerene-based acceptor materials.17â€“27 Since Zhan et al. synthesized a non-fullerene receptor material called ITIC in 2015,28 the energy conversion efficiency of organic photovoltaic (OPV) cells based on non-fullerene acceptor materials has been continuously making breakthroughs, which further promoted the development of non-fullerene acceptors (NFAs) having an acceptorâ€“donorâ€“acceptor (Aâ€“Dâ€“A) structure29â€“32 with an optimized power conversion efficiency (PCE) in the range of 12â€“15%. In 2019, Zou et al. reported a novel narrow-band gap star acceptor molecule, Y6, and the OPV devices based on Y6 further achieved a PCE exceeding 15%.33 The single junction OSC devices based on NFSMAs with a power conversion efficiency (PCE) exceeding 18% have been acquired in recent years.34â€“38 Although non-fullerene organic solar cells show great commercial application prospects, the device efficiency achieved so far is still not enough to meet the requirements of commercial applications. Therefore, it is essential and desirable to develop new NFSMAs with easy synthesis and high photovoltaic performance.

4,4-Difluoro-4-bora-3a,4a-diaza-s-indacene (BODIPY) dyes are a class of versatile fluorophores with excellent properties, such as relatively high fluorescence quantum yield, large molar extinction coefficient, good light and thermal stability, low optical band gap, and outstanding redox activity.39â€“42 Furthermore, their photophysical and electrochemical characteristics can be easily fine-tuned via chemical modification at the periphery and at the boron atom of the BODIPY skeleton by the introduction of appropriate electron donors and electron acceptors.43â€“45 In addition, BODIPY dyes often possess deep highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energy levels, which can result in a high open circuit voltage (VOC).46 These predominant photoelectronic properties of BODIPYs make them attractive candidates for OSC applications.47 In recent years, many BODIPY derivatives were applied in OSCs as donors and exhibited PCE in the range of 1.62â€“7.47%.46,48â€“51 However, to the best of our knowledge, only two BODIPY derivatives were used as NFSMAs in OSCs, with an optimized PCE of 13.41% reported by our group.52 On the other hand, carbazole and triphenylamine (TPA) groups are excellent building blocks with strong electron donating ability, low redox potential, and high hole mobility and have been extensively applied in the OSC field.47â€“49,53

With these considerations, in this study, we have designed and synthesized two BODIPY dyes, namely CF3-BDP-TPA and CF3-BDP-Cz, respectively (Chart 1) by Knoevenagel condensation reaction. Importantly, the meso-carbon of BODIPY is the best position for charge injection, and the introduction of an electron-withdrawing group at the meso position of BODIPY can reduce the HOMOâ€“LUMO gap.54 Therefore, the introduction of a â€“CF3 group into the meso position of BODIPY can induce significant variations in its chemical and physical properties and lower the HOMOâ€“LUMO gap owing to the strong electron-withdrawing nature (electronegativity = 3.5), and specific steric repulsion of fluorine.55 The optical properties, electrochemical behavior, and photovoltaic performances of CF3-BDP-TPA and CF3-BDP-Cz were investigated, and the influence of the donor's electron donating ability and substituted positions on their electronic and photovoltaic properties was also comparatively investigated. Both CF3-BDP-TPA and CF3-BDP-Cz show a deep LUMO energy level and elevated HOMO energy level, which are beneficial to the high open circuit voltage of OSCs. We have investigated the photovoltaic performance of these two molecules as acceptors along with conjugated polymer PBDB-T as a donor (chemical structure shown in Chart 1). The OSCs based on PBDB-T:CF3-BDP-TPA and PBDB-T:CF3-BDP-Cz showed an overall PCE of about 9.89% and 13.07%. The higher value of PCE for CF3-BDP-Cz-based PSCs compared to that for the CF3-BDP-TPA counterpart is attributed to the simultaneously improved values of JSC, FF, and VOC. The dipole moment of CF3-BDP-Cz is that of higher than CF3-BDP-TPA, which decreases the exciton binding energy in the CF3-BDP-Cz-based active layer. The high value of dipole moment can lead to a simultaneous increase in JSC and VOC and reduced energy loss. In addition, it is also beneficial for increasing crystallinity and decreasing Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking distance, which leads to the suppression of charge recombination. Therefore, PSCs based on CF3-BDP-Cz have a high value of FF. Our study would be beneficial to designing novel Ï€-conjugated NFSMAs based on BODIPY derivatives and boosting the photovoltaic functioning of BHJ-OSCs using a narrow band gap NFSMA.

2.6 Frontier molecular orbital (FMO) analysis

The HOMOs and LUMOs are usually known as Frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs) in the FMO theory, and the energy change between the HOMO and LUMO energies is termed as the HOMOâ€“LUMO energy gap (Hâ€“L gap). Some frequently used interesting optical features of the particular species are described by the Hâ€“L gap value. As the amount of absorption of light can be altered with the Hâ€“L gap value of a system, the Hâ€“L gap is directly linked to the valence and conduction bands. A smaller energy (electrons are capable of moving freely and efficiently) is required when the Hâ€“L gap value tends to zero, implying easy photochemical excitation and high electrical conductivity. Moreover, the Hâ€“L gap is also diagnostic of the chemical behavior of molecular moieties; the higher Hâ€“L gap indicates lower chemical reactivity (i.e., higher stability). Here, in this report, the structural and fascinating electronic features like HOMOs, LUMOs, Hâ€“L gap, dipole moments, and MESP of both dyes have been analyzed along with visualizing the HOMOs, LUMOs, and MESP 3D-isosurfaces.

2.8 Photovoltaic performance

The Î”ELUMO(D/A for PBDB-T/CF3-BDP-TPA and PBDB-T/CF3-BDP-Cz is about 0.58 and 0.52 eV and provides sufficient driving forces for exciton dissociation and subsequent electron transfer from PBDB-T to either CF3-BDP-TPA or CF3-BDP-Cz in their respective BHJ active layers. The morphology of the active layer also plays an important role in the charge recombination processes and therefore is the deciding factor for the overall PCE of the PSCs. A proper domain size of donor and acceptor phase is needed for efficient exciton dissociation into free charge carriers and their subsequent charge transport. If the domain sizes of the donor and acceptor phases are too small, the exciton dissociation is high, and it reduces the interpenetrating pathways for charge carrier transport towards the respective electrode and increases the charge recombination. However, if the domain sizes of the donor and acceptor are too large (above exciton diffusion length âˆ¼10â€“15 nm), the exciton dissociation is hampered. Therefore, an appropriate nanoscale morphology is required for effective exciton dissociation and charge transport. 3. Conclusion

We have designed and synthesized two simple narrow bandgap NFSMAs with the same central CF3 substituted BODIPY and different terminal donors, i.e., TPA (CF3-BDP-TPA) and carbazole (CF3-BDP-Cz), and investigated their optical and electrochemical properties. Both CF3-BDP-TPA and CF3-BDP-Cz showed broad absorption profiles up to 900 nm. Theoretical DFT simulations showed that the molecular dipole moment is higher with a more planar configuration for CF3-BDP-2 than CF3-BDP-TPA, resulting in a low value of exciton binding energy and efficient charge transport in the former. Employing a wide bandgap copolymer PBDB-T as the donor, the solution processed optimized BHJ-PSCs based on CF3-BDP-TPA and CF3-BDP-2 attain the PCE of 9.89% and 13.07%, respectively. The higher value of PCE for CF3-BDP-2 may be associated with efficient exciton dissociation, low energy loss, more nanoscale morphology of the active layer, and reduced charge recombination. These investigations demonstrated that increasing the molecular dipole moment via the simple modification of donor terminal units can improve the PCE of the PSC and reduce the energy loss in the PSCs.
