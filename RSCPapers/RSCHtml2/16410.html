Abstract

As promising non-volatile memory devices, memristors have received extensive attention since they can effectively mimic synapses and enable the fabrication of neuromorphic systems. In recent years, 2D materials have been widely used in memristors because of their unique advantages, which not only improve the performance of memristors, but also promote the applicability of memristors in flexible electronics, low-power high-temperature devices, neuromorphic computing, etc. In this review, we summarize the applications of 2D materials in fabricating memristors and their physical switching mechanisms. The challenges involved are discussed and the corresponding strategies are reviewed.

I. Introduction

In the age of big data, tremendous amounts of data are produced every year. In more than a decade (from 2005 to 2020), the digital universe grew 300 times, from 130 exabytes to 40 trillion gigabytes.1 The sheer volume of data requires the next generation of non-volatile memory (NVM) technologies to possess large storage and high speed. Recently, many new types of NVM technologies (e.g., resistive random-access memory (RRAM),2–9 magnetic random-access memory (MRAM),10–13 phase-change memory (PCM),14–18etc.) have exhibited outstanding advantages, simultaneously providing permanent storage and fast processing of large volumes of data. Among them, memristors have shown the ability to store and output information by switching between different resistance states, and the scalable nanoscale dimension of memristors facilitates obtaining higher density and large-scale integration.19–23 In 1971, based on the symmetry theory, Leon Chua predicted the fourth basic passive circuit element other than resistance, capacitance, and inductance, namely, the memristor.24 A memristor connects the charge and magnetic flux, and it defines the relationship between them. From the relation dφ = Mdq, it is evident that the memristor is a function of the amount of charge. Its characteristic is that the resistance is not fixed but varies with the passing charge. When the previous passing charge does not change, the resistance of the memristor will remain unchanged. However, at that time, no one proposed a corresponding physical model or example of a memristor. By 2008, Hewlett Packard laboratories reported the first working memristive device in the journal Nature.25 Since then, memristors have entered a stage of rapid development. In 2008, Yang et al. prepared a TiO2-based memristor device,26 which exhibited fast bipolar non-volatile switching behavior and supported the generic model of resistance conversion in oxide systems through experimental evidence. Subsequently, in 2010, Lu et al. prepared a nanoscale silicon-based memristor device27 and demonstrated that a hybrid system comprising complementary metal-oxide semiconductor (CMOS) neurons and memristor synapses can simulate important synaptic functions. It is important to note that in the process of continuous development, researchers also have had to deal with certain controversies regarding the identification of genuine memristive properties,28 which has resulted in a more comprehensive study of memristors. In short, it is inappropriate to use memristors in place of all resistive memory devices.28 However, this is not the focus of this article and will not be comprehensively discussed here. In this paper, we refer to a memristor as a device that depends on changes in resistance.

Furthermore, the memristor exhibits switching speeds in the subnanosecond scale,29,30 low programming energy consumption,31 and long write/erase endurance.32 Importantly, the resistance state of the memristor depends on its past states; therefore, it can be used to mimic the synaptic connections in the (biology) brain, which has attracted increasing attention in neuromorphic computing.33,34 Further, the memristor is expected to resolve the key problem of traditional computer architecture.35 The human brain consumes much less energy (about 20 W) in many complex learning processes as compared to the most advanced computers (about 1 MW), and its processing efficiency is also better.36,37 Therefore, using hardware computing to simulate the brain and achieve brain function in the field of neural computing may have a profound influence on next-generation artificial intelligence.38–40 Until now, Yan et al. implemented a series of synaptic functions using memristors, such as long-term potentiation (LTP) and long-term depression (LTD), spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP), and paired-pulse facilitation (PPF).41–45 This has led to the further development of memristors in the field of brain neurobionics.

In terms of device structures and functional layers, memristors mostly comprise a three-layered structure comprising a top electrode (TE), bottom electrode (BE), and a functional layer. This structure is simple and compatible with today's CMOS processes.46–48 Memristors store information by changing the resistance of the functional layer when a voltage is applied.49,50 Earlier studies on the functional layer materials that could be used in memristors have mainly involved traditional oxide materials, such as TiO2,42,51 tantalum oxide,52,53 HfOx,54 WOx,55 ZHO/IGZO,43etc. Oxide-based memory resistors have many advantages, such as relatively stable performance and mature research. However, it is difficult to reduce the dimensions of memristors based on traditional three-dimensional (3D) oxides; further, with the development of newer materials, when the film is reduced from 3D to two-dimensional (2D) architectures, other interesting characteristics may become evident, which are worth studying for scaling down the size of the device. Therefore, 2D materials as the representative of newer materials are a hot research direction and might become a strong focus for future investigations.

Subsequent to the successful exfoliation of graphene, 2D materials have attracted increasing attention because of their atomic thickness56–58 that has yielded excellent electrical, optical,59–61 thermal, and mechanical properties.62–67 2D materials as functional and structural materials have been widely used in various application fields, such as transistors, batteries, photovoltaics, and water treatment.68–81 They are expected to append or even replace conventional semiconductor materials in the fabrication of nanoscale electronic devices with smaller size, lower power consumption, and higher efficiency.82–85 The studied 2D materials include a wider variety of materials from conductors and semiconductors to insulators, such as graphene,86–89 boron nitride (BN),90,91 topological insulators,92–94 transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs; e.g., MoS2 and WS2),95–97 2D perovskites,98,99 InSe,100–103 SnS,104,105 TiS2,106 black phosphorus (BP),107–117 MXenes,118–120 antimoene,121–124 bismuthene,125–129 borophene,130 tellurene,131–136 selenium nanosheets,137,138 and 2D metals.139 Importantly, numerous 2D materials have been used in memristors because of their excellent properties.86–88,90,91,95–99,107

Due to its excellent electrical conductivity, ultrahigh structural and chemical stabilities, and large carrier mobility, graphene has shown unparalleled advantages when used as a memristor functional layer.86–88,140–142 In addition, some researchers have used graphene as an electrode143,144 to prepare a memristor, which showed nonvolatile characteristics and improved device performance. Some researchers added monolayer graphene to the BE to prevent the conductive filament (CF) from penetrating into the BE, and devices based on this structure have shown satisfactory resistive switching (RS) performances, including high endurance, fast speed, and long retention.145,146 In addition to graphene, TMDs as functional layer materials of memristors have also been widely studied.41,145,147 For example, Nokia Labs Skolkovo reported that a memristor prepared using layered MoS2 exhibits low programming voltages from 0.1 to 0.2 V and adjustable resistance of 102–108 Ω.145 Subsequently, Yan et al. fabricated an artificial electronic device using 2D layered WS2 nanosheets. The device consumed low power and successfully simulated biological synaptic functions.41 In addition, for RS devices, the physical switching mechanism has always been the focus of research, and popular switching mechanisms include filaments, atomic vacancy, and trapping and detrapping of electrons. Different mechanisms can yield different device performances. II. Memristors based on 2D materials

A. Graphene and its derivatives

1. Working as a functional layer. Lateral RRAMs comprise a three-layered structure of metal–functional layer–metal with standard plane geometry, which is similar to a hybrid transistor structure.41–43 Graphene and its other derivatives have been studied as functional layer media for use in lateral RRAMs due to their low manufacturing cost, high mechanical flexibility, and high optical transparency.153,154 In 2015, S. The arrows indicate the scanning direction of the voltage. Evidently, the device exhibits stable bipolar behavior. In addition, Huang et al. also prepared Ag/GO/ITO devices by using GO,156 where the GO film was annealed at different temperatures; the performance of this device exhibited a close relationship with the annealing temperature of the GO film. It was found that at the annealing temperature of 20 °C, the device exhibited typical storage behavior and had a high ON/OFF current ratio. 2. Working as an electrode. Graphene can not only be used as an interfacial layer between the electrode and the medium but also as an electrode. A resistive memory device based on a graphene electrode providing inherent selection elements was reported by Lu et al., as shown in Fig. 3a–d.143 They used multilayer graphene (MLG) as the conducting electrodes for improving their conductance values. It was found that the deposition sequence of MLG/Ta2O5−x/TaOy/MLG and MLG/TaOy/Ta2O5−x/MLG led to two different switching characteristics of the resistive memory. Spontaneous functionalization during the device manufacturing process in graphene materials can lead to functionalized graphene electrodes with volatility threshold switching and high overall nonlinear switching features when used as resistive memory. In addition, Lu et al. found that the epoxy group was present in the functionalized graphene electrodes, which served as a deep level trap for conducting electrons, resulting in variable range hopping with lower conductance. The high-energy electrons can enter a shallower trap at a sufficiently high voltage, which yields rapid enhancements in conductivity and current, corresponding to the transformation from HRS to LRS. This formation of the switching process is due to the volatility of electron capture/decapture in the functionalized graphene BE and nonvolatility of the oxygen-vacancies-transit-induced bipolar switching in the tantalum oxide layers.

B. TMDs

TMDs, which are expressed as MX2 (M: transition metal such as Mo, W, Sn, Hf, Zr; X: chalcogen atom such as S, Se, Te), exhibit excellent performances with respect to electrical, optical, chemical, and mechanical properties.159–161Via van der Waals forces, a layer of transition metal atoms can be sandwiched between two layers of chalcogen atoms. As a semiconductor, TMDs possess advantages such as a sizable bandgap, relatively high carrier mobility, stability in air, etc.162,163
1. MoS2. MoS2—a typical TMD—has aroused considerable attention due to its significantly unique mechanical,164 electrical,165–168 and biological properties.169–172 Correspondingly, numerous applications have been demonstrated in the fields of catalysis,173,174 electronics,175–177 energy,178,179 and biomedical applications.180–183 Environmental applications of 2D MoS2 are also expected. In fact, bulk MoS2, which exists as mineral molybdenite in nature, has been applied as environmental adsorbents184 and catalysts185 in the long term. However, the environmental applications of MoS2 are limited by the synthesis of monolayer or few-layer MoS2 nanosheets, which hold unique 2D nanosized properties. Recently, the development of advanced technologies in obtaining monolayer and large-scale MoS2 nanosheets have provided renewed potential.186,187 Following the original work, the synthesis methods,188–190 physical and chemical tuning,191 and functionalization192 have been reported, and novel environmental applications based on the photothermal effect of MoS2 have been revealed.193,194

However, when compared with graphene and its derivatives/composites, raw MoS2 nanosheets show no significant RS behaviors.195 Fortunately, it can be introduced by treating a mixing solution or by chemical functionalization with other kinds of materials (e.g., dielectric polymers).196 In 2015, Hu et al. reported the perfect odd-symmetric memristor prepared by using 1T-phase MoS2 nanosheets (Fig. 4a).197 They also demonstrated that 2H-phase bulk MoS2 exhibited ohmic properties, while 1T-phase detached MoS2 showed a particular memory response, resulting in voltage-related changes in the resistance. The conductivity of the 1T-phase MoS2 was 107 times higher than that of the 2H phase. Electrical field could induce lattice distortion and enhance the delocalization of electrons in the 1T phase, thereby significantly increasing the conductivity and leading to resistance changes. Furthermore, they yield asymmetric Ag/MoS2/Ag memristive devices. A symmetric I–V loop with compression lag could be obtained after applying a bias voltage on this device from 0 mV → 1000 mV → −1000 mV → 0 mV (Fig. 4b and c). These results also verified the existence of the negative differential resistance (NDR).

Subsequently, a breakthrough experiment was reported. An innovation for fabricating memory and memory capacitor switches for use as an inexpensive printed electronic application was introduced by Bessonov et al., which could be attributed to the easy availability and low cost of MoS2-based structures.145 They fabricated this device by depositing few-layer MoS2 tablets by thermally assisted air oxidation. Solution-treated WOx/WS2 and MoOx/MoS2 heterostructures exhibited an unprecedentedly wide adjustable resistance range from 102 to 108 Ω and programming voltage of 0.1–0.2 V (Fig. 4d). Bipolar RS is controlled by an ultrathin (<3 nm) oxide layer, which also has a capacitive contribution. Because of the high nonlinearity of switching dynamics, different synaptic flexibility mechanisms can be achieved via a series of electrical impulses (Fig. 4e and f). LTP, STP, and binary switching have been proven to facilitate the adjustment of the resistance state of memristors according to pulse amplitudes, pulse numbers, and input frequencies.

Metal-oxide-based RRAMs have been widely used because of their expandability, temperature robustness, and machine-learning potential.198 However, thicker oxide layers lead to the necessity of a relatively high programming voltage, while thinner oxide layers lead to a smaller window and greater leakage current.198 Based on these principles, a new interface engineering concept was proposed via the majorization of the oxide layer itself to decrease the programming voltages and improve the ON/OFF ratio. The concept of MoS2–Pd interface engineering was proposed, and diaphanous ITO/HfO2/MoS2–Pd NPs/ITO RRAMs were prepared by Wang et al.198 The electrode/oxide interface of HfOx-based RRAMs was designed with the mixed structure of MoS2 and Pd nanoparticles. Via interface engineering, for a relatively thick HfOx layer (∼15 nm), the setting voltage could be extremely reduced (from −3.5 to −0.8 V), with incremental ON/OFF ratio and good uniformity; further, the memory window could be increased by 30 times. In addition, this RRAM could commonly work for 200 cycles of memory switching even at higher temperatures. Further, the resistances in the LRS and HRS did not significantly decrease, exhibiting good data retention performance and stability. Owing to the high transmittance of ITO and atomic thickness of MoS2 films, the arranged RRAM exhibited higher visible-light transparency, larger RS window, and low setting voltage, making it promising for applications in wearable electronic devices and transparent circuits with comparatively low power supply voltages.

Most of the research has been done on the use of a single type of 2D-material-based memristor, which has certain shortcomings, such as high-temperature instability.96,153,154 By combining different 2D materials, a heterogeneous structure can be realized to resolve these shortcomings in electronic devices, particularly for those requiring vertical multilayered structures. Wang et al., for the first time, fabricated a robust memory resistor based on a full-layer 2D heterogeneous structure (graphene/MoS2–xOx/graphene) (Fig. 4g and h), which exhibited repeatable bipolar RS (Fig. 4i), excellent switching performance, durability up to 107 times higher, and the highest operating temperature of up to 340 °C (Fig. 4j).96 They found that the atomic structure of the switch layer (MoS2–xOx), electrodes (graphene), and atom-sharpened interface play a pivotal role in ensuring device robustness and thermal stability at elevated temperatures. In situ high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) for high-temperature operation revealed that the MoS2–xOx layer exhibited high thermal stability. Further, the scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) studies revealed a switching mechanism and good limited conduction channel based on the migration of oxygen ions. In addition, the fully layered 2D material offered good mechanical flexibility for use in flexible electronic applications; from our experimental demonstration, excellent endurance of more than 1000 bending cycles could be obtained.

Memristor based on the metal–insulator–metal (MIM) structure, which uses TiO2 or other insulating oxides, is limited by the lack of external control of the switching voltages and control over filament formation.199–201 Lauhon and Hersam reported a novel memristor element based on the grain boundary (GB) in single-layer MoS2 devices, which yielded three-terminal MoS2 memristors with gate tunability.150 In particular, the GB resistance generated by the contact could be easily modulated repeatedly and the switching ratio could reach up to ∼103 (Fig. 4k). In intersecting-GB memristors, the severally addressable local gates in the MoS2 memristors could be used to continuously adjust the SET voltage (VSET) and provide a fault-tolerant structure. By changing the gate bias voltage (∼0–40 V), the VSET value could be changed from 3.5 to 8.0 V (Fig. 4m); a controllable VSET value provided additional flexibility in designing complementary and bipolar RS structures. Furthermore, bisecting-GB memristors yield bistable resistance values of more than 3 orders of magnitude at a zero bias voltage, while the shape of the positive curves and switching ratio (∼4–6) remain relatively unchanged with gate voltage (Fig. 4l and n). A series of results have confirmed the excellent properties of such GB-based memristors.

2. WS2. In order to realize low-power operation, researchers have used advanced materials to prepare memristor devices that are different from the two main mechanisms of metal-ion CFs or oxygen vacancies.153,154 WS2 is a TMD, which is different from other 2D materials such as graphene due to its tunable bandgap and unique semiconductor properties.67,202–206 Yan et al. proposed a low-power-consumption memristor device with the Pd/WS2/Pt device structure based on 2H-phase WS2. The switching time was fast at 13 ns (14 ns), program current was low (1 μA) in the ON state, and the SET (RESET) energy could reach the femtojoules levels (Fig. 5a–d).41 TEM observations revealed the presence of more tungsten and sulfur vacancies in the WS2 films than those observed in WS2 nanosheets. Therefore, the formation of tungsten and sulfur vacancies and electron hopping between them are the main reasons for the improved RS performance, which is different from traditional metal-ion CFs or oxygen vacancies. Further, the device could realize the simulation of the synaptic function of living organisms, including excitation and inhibition under negative and positive impulses stimulation training, memory, and learning for transitions from the STDP, PPF, and STP to LTP.

In addition, in 2017, Choi et al. reported a Ag/WS2/Ag device prepared by a flexible PET substrate via the full-print manufacturing technology using detached WS2 flakes as the functional layer for use in rewritable bipolar memory devices.147 They demonstrated the structural, physical, mechanical, and electrical properties of the detached WS2 flakes for the full-printing technology. Then, they used an atom-thick layer of aluminum oxide (Al2O3) to encapsulate the devices to protect them from moisture. In other words, the Ag/WS2/Ag-based memory devices could achieve low power consumption, high switching ratio (∼103), nonvolatile bipolar behavior, longer retention time (∼105 s), low operating voltage (∼2 V), high endurance (1500 voltage sweeps), mechanical robustness (1500 cycles), low current compliance (50 μA), and unique repeatability under ambient conditions, which heralded a new possibility for flexible electronic device integration.

3. MoTe2. MoTe2 has aroused considerable interest for electronic applications because of its polymorphism ability.210,211 Zhang et al. demonstrated the structural transformation from a 2H semiconductor to an orthorhombic Td conducting phase under the action of an electric field and a distorted transient structure (2Hd) in vertical RRAM devices based on 2H-phase MoTe2 and Mo1−xWxTe2 (Fig. 6a).212 They demonstrated the possibility of using electric fields to induce phase transitions. The setting voltages of the Mo1−xWxTe2 devices were monotonously dependent on the wafer thickness (Fig. 6b). By comparing the RRAM characteristics based on 2H-phase MoTe2 samples and 2H-phase Mo1−xWxTe2 alloys, they found that the 2H-phase Mo1−xWxTe2 alloys need a smaller VSET value to trigger the RRAM behavior. The energy required to convert the semiconductor state into the metal state continuously decreases in Mo1−xWxTe2 alloys as x increases (Fig. 6c). Temperature-dependent electrical measurements reveal that the 2Hd state could behave as a semiconductor metal depending on the transient state. In addition, they used an Al2O3/MoTe2 pile with an ON/OFF current ratio of 106 at less than 1 μA to implement a selector RRAM framework.

C. Other 2D materials

1. BN. BN possesses a wide bandgap (about 5.5 eV),91,213 which is an ideal material for RRAM and other logic devices.214–216 Lanza et al. studied the existence of RS in multilayer BN reactor and developed a series of worthwhile RRAM devices with multilayer hexagonal boron nitride (h-BN) to be the active RS media (Fig. 7a).91 In addition, Fig. 7b shows the VSET and VRESET values as 0.4 and −0.8 V, respectively. The device properties can be controlled by inserting interfacial graphene electrodes and adjusting the particle size and thickness of the BN pile. The device based on BN shows the coexistence of bipolar and threshold RS behaviors for the first time, because it affords the facile generation of B vacancies and penetration of metal ions. The RS phenomenon was investigated by inserting MLG between the metal electrodes and h-BN. Due to graphene's high electrical resistance outside the plane, the interfacial electrodes of graphene were shown to shorten the power dissipation of RRAM devices, protect the medium from ambient humidity, stabilize the RS response by preventing oxygen diffusion, allow mechanical bending without compromising performance, and afford excellent transparency.

In addition, Wu et al. reported the nonvolatile RS (NVRS) capabilities of single-layer BN grown by chemical vapor deposition (CVD) vertically sandwiched between metallic electrodes.90 They studied two different device structures with three metal electrodes (Ni, Au, and Ag). Shapeless switches were fabricated in both bipolar and unipolar modes with fast switching speeds (<15 ns) and high switching ratios (107) that were achieved via pulse manipulation. The fabrication of this single-atom h-BN NVRS device extended the atomic resistance member to a 2D insulator. The classic I–V characteristics of the single-layer h-BN MIM device (Fig. 7c–f) exhibited bipolar and unipolar RS behaviors for devices with Au and Ni foils, respectively. Moreover, they investigated the temperature-related electron transport properties and performed simulations using an ab initio method to reveal the conductance-bridging mechanism in the switching process, which could be achieved by the occupation of metal ions in B vacancies and the interactions between the interfacial localized defects and metal ions. These efforts could yield newer applications in printing electronics, ultrathin flexible memory, radio-frequency switches, and neuromorphic computing.

2. BP. BP is the first and also the most investigated one in the 2D group-VA family. In 2014, Li et al. were the first to fabricate BP and they used it to fabricate a BP-based field-effect transistor.217 Then, subsequent studies revealed other promising properties of BP, such as superior mechanical properties,218,219 highly anisotropic carrier transport,220–224 negative Poisson's ratios,225 strain-induced conduction bands,226 perpendicular topological characteristic, and excellent optical responses.221,227,228 Moreover, the wrinkled structure of BP enabled it to exhibit a larger area when compared with those obtainable from flat-surfaced 2D materials; this facilitated in enhancing its drug-loading capability,229,230 use as imaging agents,110,231 and application as metal ions,232 All these abovementioned characteristics of BP make it suitable for numerous applications in transistors,233–237 batteries,238–241 energy storage,242,243 photonics,221,244,245 sensors,246–249 and photothermal cancer therapy.229,250–254

The primary difficulty in handling BP is its instability, i.e., degradation under environmental conditions, which prevents the mass production of few-layer BP using the liquid-phase exfoliation (LPE) method. Hao et al. obtained BP films from exfoliated BP nanosheets with selected solvents and reported an environmentally stable nonvolatile RRAM device with the Al/BP film/ITO structure.107 Flexible BP memory devices are made from the deposited Al layer on the top of BP films, and the degradation-produced amorphous top degraded layer (TDL) can be used as the bottom of the insulation layer of the Al electrode so as to prevent further degradation of the propagation to the BP film as well as to ensure excellent memory performance even after three months of environmental exposure. In their work, the fabricated BP memory device exhibited bistable RS behaviors, high ON/OFF current ratio (as high as 3 × 105), high stability (105 s), and high ON/OFF ratio (as high as 104).

Many researchers have proposed several techniques for enhancing the stability of BP, including covalent bonding with carbon-free radicals255 and aryl diazonium,256 intercalation of alkali metal hydrides,257 doping of fluorine adatoms,258 surface coordination of the titanium sulfonate ligand,255 metal-ion modification,232 and encapsulation by h-BN,259,260 graphene shell,261 GO,113 organic monolayers,262 and AlOx.233,263 Of course, seeking other stable phosphorene-analog photothermal materials (such as tin sulfide (SnS)) is also a possible method.104,264,265 These strategies can open up new potential for the photothermal application of phosphorene and phosphorene-analog materials for use in environmental applications.

3. Perovskites. Organolead halide perovskite materials, with their simple and cheap manufacturing process, good charge mobility, and excellent light absorption coefficient, have opened a novel era for the development of highly potent low-cost solar cells.266–270 Besides solar cells, rapid developments in perovskite technology has resulted in the direct expansion of many other infusive areas, such as applications in optical and electronics devices.271–273 In 2012, Choi and Wang demonstrated a new style of dual-function RS memory device with a simple Ag and CH3NH3PbI3−xClx perovskite bilayer structure on an analog and digital RS FTO substrate.99 The RS characteristic of the device was induced by the sudden generation and fracture of the filament under the action of a comparatively high bias sweep (Fig. 8a). This bistable RS exhibited reliable performance, with a retention time of 4 × 104 s and lifetime of more than 103 times, indicating that the Ag/CH3NH3PbI3−xClx/FTO device could work as an appropriate RRAM device. It was also shown that light illumination had a certain impact on RS performance (Fig. 8b and c). This device exhibited digital as well as analog switching characteristics, which could be used in the fabrication of neural morphological calculation devices.

Subsequently, Liu et al. fabricated an organic and inorganic hybrid perovskites (OIHPs) memristor, confirming the possibility of implementing OIHPs for producing rewritable memory devices.98 Using polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) as the RS layers as well as a two-step spin-coating process, the CH3NH3PbI3 perovskite was prepared as the charge-capture layer that formed the charge trap. The conductive mechanism of ITO/PMMA/CH3NH3PbI3/PMMA/Ag device was studied by means of its I–V characteristics. This memory device could be reprogrammed with an ON/OFF ratio of up to 103. 4. GaSe. In addition to TMDs, 2D layered III–VI semiconductor materials have received wide research attention due to their attractive optical and electrical properties.274 Here, GaSe has a direct bandgap of approximately 25 meV and an indirect bandgap of approximately 2 eV.275 GaSe is anticipated to possess remarkable electrical and optical characteristics276–278 and has broad application prospects in terahertz-wave generation, nonlinear optics, and optoelectronics.279–281 Jie et al. studied three-terminal transistors in 2D layered GaSe nanosheets by combining memristor devices with field-effect transistors.152 A memory resistor with silver electrodes exhibits nonvolatile bipolar RS features. The three-terminal 2D GaSe transistor also exhibits nonvolatile bipolar RS features with the advantages of large switching ratio, worthwhile wear resistance, excellent retention performance, and low electric field threshold. The RS characteristic shows no significant changes in the VSET and VRESET values from the scan loop at the initial bias (Fig. 9b). The corresponding ultralow electric field of GaSe-based memristors may be because of the small migration energy of the inherent Ga vacancies in GaSe, which is beneficial for practical applications in low-power NVMs (Fig. 9d).

III. Switching mechanism for memristors based on 2D materials

A. Filaments

1. Metal iron filaments. Memristors have rich RS mechanisms, such as filament, vacancy, and electrons trapping. Among them, filament is a widely recognized mechanism, which is mainly divided into metal-ion filaments and vacancy filaments. The electrodes of the memristor based on metal-ion filaments are generally considered as active electrodes. The formation and breakage of the filament can be achieved by oxidation/reduction reactions. Pan et al. reported a multilayer h-BN RS with the filament conduction mechanism.91 The typical I–V curves of this device are shown in Fig. 10a, which exhibits free-forming bipolar RS. The cycle-to-cycle variability is very small with a read voltage of 0.1 V for more than 350 cycles. The electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) profiles for non-defective and GB/CF positions of a Ti/thin h-BN/Cu memristor at LRS are shown in Fig. 10b and c, which reveal that the signals propagated in the depth because of rigorous Ti and B motion at the GB/CF locations (Fig. 10c). Moreover, the migration of B toward the Ti electrode is easier due to the lower activation energy at the GB, and Ti ions move toward the cathode to form a Ti or TiN-based CF. The stable formation and breakage of the CF is the reason for the stable switching between the HRS and LRS in the device, as well as the key to the excellent device performance.

In addition, K. Qian et al. observed that the physical mechanism of a 2D h-BN memristor with a Cu foil electrode and Ag TE is via the Ag filaments.282Fig. 11 shows the TEM image of a sample cross-section. The images are taken after the SET operation, which clearly show the CFs with a typical conical shape (Fig. 11a and d). The filament connects the Cu foil (BE) and Ag (TE) to the h-BN layer, which is localized with the thinner part near the BE and wider base near the TE (Fig. 11a). In order to study the chemical constituents of the CFs, energy-dispersive X-ray spectrometry (EDS) was used under the scanning TEM (STEM) mode. Fig. 11c (circular region marked as “1” in Fig. 11b) shows that the concentration of Ag elements in the middle of the h-BN film is somewhat large. Moreover, noncomplete CF in the memory cells of the same ON state was analyzed by HRTEM (Fig. 11e, corresponding to Fig. 11d). It is evident that the lattice stripes in the filament grow along the TE, and the lattice stripes of the filament are the same as that of the Ag electrode shown in Fig. 11e. Furthermore, Fig. 11e shows the d-spacing values measured in the TE region, namely, 0.23 and 0.20 nm that correspond to the (111) and (200) planes of Ag, respectively. These results show that Ag is the main element in the CF in the Ag/h-BN/Cu foil device. The noncomplete filament (protruding in a closed area) starts to grow along the Ag TE (Fig. 11f), and the shape of the CF also contradicts the widely accepted electrochemical metallization (ECM) theory. According to this theory, the core assumption is that Mz+ would be translated into M atoms with sufficient electrons at the cathode, and the transfer layer will be reduced during the drift of electrons that cannot be captured, which is reasonable for the high diffusion coefficients and solubility of conventional solid electrolyte Mz+ materials (Ag+ or Cu2+). The ECM theory has been supported via visual evidence, for example, the Ag/H2O/Pt and Ag/SiO2/Pt structures. For switch materials with low cation migration rates, the filament grows from the anode to the cathode, such as that observed in Cu/ZrO2/Pt and Ag/amorphous Si/Pt memory devices. In addition, the h-BN membrane can provide a much lower Ag+ migration rate for Ag TE/h-BN/Cu devices. Therefore, Ag+ can snatch the injected free electrons from the BE and then be translated into Ag atoms at the h-BN membrane before reaching the cathode, as shown in Fig. 11g. As a matter of fact, even for switch materials that have high cation migration rates, the electronic current is still the primary constituent of the program current rather than the ionic current. Hence, it is possible that the cations in h-BN are reduced by sufficient electrons.

Ag-filament-based mechanisms are the most common in metal-ion CF mechanisms and have also been demonstrated in 2D graphene quantum dot devices. X. Yan et al. proposed that the physical mechanism of Ag/Zr0.5Hf0.5O2/GO quantum dots/Zr0.5Hf0.5O2/Ag (abbreviated as “AZA”) device was the Ag filaments.283 The fitting line of the AZA for the HRS device is divides into two sections. The direct-tunneling (DT) conduction and Fowler–Nordheim tunneling (FNT) models are used to describe the conduction behaviors. In the TEM analysis of the AZA, which reveals an intermediate resistance state. The Ag filament can be found growing toward the TE, but the filaments are not completely attached to the TE. The related elemental mapping image of this device shows a gap between the TE and the peak of nanoclusters. There are some Ag nanocrystallites in this gap. Hence, with an appropriate electric field, electrons can easily tunnel through this gap, which is consistent with the analyses of the FNT and DT tunneling mechanisms. Therefore, by this research, the metal-ion CF mechanism has been further developed.

2. Vacancy filaments. Vacancy defects can be widely observed in 2D materials, which facilitate the formation of vacancy filaments; this can lead to the transition of the resistance state.284,285 Y. Yang et al. reported GaSe three-terminal memtransistors with the Ag/GaSe/Ag structure.152 Ga vacancies could arrange the CFs when an external electric field is applied, inducing the Ohmic conduction reaction in the LRS. Fig. 12a shows some mobile Se ions in p-type GaSe. Se ions move to the source electrode when a negative Vds is applied. The cumulative Se ions form a cation vacancy at the interface of GaSe and Ag electrode, as shown in Fig. 12b. This Ga vacancy can be used as the p-type CF nuclei. The CFs grow under the action of an electric field until the conductive fiber is formed as the negative voltage increases. The device switches to the LRS, that is, the SET process is activated, as shown in Fig. 12c. Se ions emigrate to the drain pole when an electric field is applied under positive Vds, leading to the breakage of the Ga-vacancy filament and resulting in the OFF state of the device (Fig. 12d). Therefore, the formation and breakage of the vacancy filament allows the device to be stably switched.

B. Atomic vacancies

1. Single-type vacancies. In addition to the filament mechanism, another switching mechanism is based on the atomic vacancies. Some vacancies are inherent in 2D materials, and some are generated by external stimuli (such as electric fields). Furthermore, some materials only form a single type of vacancy, and some materials can create multiple vacancies—the key to stable switching behavior. Bessonov et al. reported a Ag/MoOx/MoS2/Ag memristive device,145 as shown in Fig. 13a and b. The authors hypothesized that defects at the edges and along the structural dislocations of the MoS2 crystal lead to active nonstoichiometric regions, promote oxidation at lower temperatures, and avoid the degradation of polymer substrates. The ideal oxidation process can be denoted as 2MoS2 + 7O2 → 2MoO3 + 4SO2. However, according to the XPS analysis, annealing at 200 °C for 3 h can yield MoOx layers (<3 nm), as shown in Fig. 13c. Due to the limitation of oxygen diffusion, MoS2 oxidation is limited to about 3 nm. It is speculated that Mo has a lower oxidation state (Mo5+) and nonstoichiometric MoOx comprises Mo6+. In addition, due to the lattice mismatch between MoO3 and MoS2, a large number of point defects and structural distortions can appear on the heterogeneous interface. The higher the oxidation temperature, the higher is the MoO3 content on the surface (Fig. 13d). This research yields excellent vacancy-induced performance.

Subsequently, Wang et al. demonstrated a switching mechanism and a well-defined conduction channel based on oxygen ion migration on the memristors device's cross-section (Fig. 13e–g).96 In the pristine state, the MoS2−xOx layer presents uniform atomic distribution, and the average atomic ratio of Mo to S and O is about 0.5 (Fig. 13e). This indicates that the sulfur vacancies in MoS2−xOx mainly comprise oxygen that is formed after thermal oxidation (Fig. 13h). After electroforming, the atomic ratio of Mo:(S + O) is reduced to 1:1.2, and the atomic percentage of S in the channel area gets obviously decreased (Fig. 13f). The loss of S atoms (formation of S vacancies) can be attributed to the thermal migration influence under Joule heating: an increase in current causes an increase in temperature, which then drives the lighter ions to leave the channel area (Fig. 13h and i). Under the action of thermal decomposition, the oxygen ions in the channel region are driven to fill the sulfur vacancies, which results in an increase in oxygen atoms in the channel region, thereby making the device switch to the OFF state (Fig. 13j). This switching mechanism mainly depends on oxygen-ion migration, and the structural changes in the channel are small, which is considered to be the main source of the observed excellent switching properties.

Metal-ion-filament- or oxygen-vacancy-based memristor devices require large working currents and cannot meet the requirements of low power consumption.286,287 Therefore, in order to fabricate a low-power-consuming device, it is necessary to fabricate memristor devices that do not depend on metal-ion filaments or oxygen vacancies. The electron hopping between the vacancies and the formation of vacancies between sulfur and tungsten were proposed as a new RS conduction mechanism by Yan et al. in 2019.41 The TEM images of the original WS2 nanosheets and films are shown in Fig. 14a and b, respectively. The TEM images of the films (Fig. 14b) reveal more defects than those in the original WS2 nanosheets (Fig. 14a). The magnified images show standard hexagonal cells and cells with W and S vacancies (inset, Fig. 14b). Fig. 14c shows the profile lines of W atoms collected from all the turquoise-colored areas shown in Fig. 14a and b, which show W-vacancy (VW) defects in the films. These results show there are two vacancies—from tungsten and sulfur—in this device, and the device can realize stable switching because of both these vacancies.

C. Trapping and detrapping of electrons

In addition to filament and vacancy mechanisms, other researchers have found that the switching mechanism of a device can be related to the trapping and detrapping of electrons on the interface. H. K. He et al. reported a memristor with the W/MoS2/p-Si structure.149 The switching reaction of the p–n junction could be ascribed to the trap position on the MoS2/p-Si interface. In this random local potential fluctuations (RLPF) technique, low-energy holes and electrons are distributed in potential wells and spatially separated from each other, resulting in higher recombination time (Fig. 16a). Since further carriers are excited and redistributed, a persistent photocurrent is more pronounced at higher photon density, which then occupy the potential wells. Earlier studies on the hysteresis of MoS2 FETs have indicated that hysteresis was primarily caused by the trapping/detrapping processes of electrons. In addition, the influence of the interface trap position on the RS is verified. The trap location on the MoS2/p-Si interface plays an important role in the memory behavior because of the existence of a native SiO2 layer in this device. When the device is positively biased, electrons are trapped in the interface trap position; further, the electrical conductivity of MoS2 decreases and the device turns to the HRS (Fig. 16b). HRS can be quickly recovered to an intermediate-resistance state (IRS) due to the spontaneous electrons trapped in the MoS2.

Notably, H. K. He et al. observed that both HRS and LRS are volatile and their currents gradually decay to the IRS due to the occurrence of spontaneous trapping/detrapping processes. Moreover, the electrons fall off and migrate to MoS2 and the device is set to the LRS when it is reversely biased. This work proposes a novel RS mechanism, which deserves more research.

IV. Existing challenges

In spite of the tremendous advantages of 2D materials in device preparation processes196 for transparent and flexible devices, there are still some key issues that need to be resolved. First, in actual industrial production, large-area controllable preparation and transfer technologies for 2D materials still present formidable challenges. This is because the available area is too small that limits its application in large areas of electronic equipment. Second, the interaction between 2D materials and substrates or other materials in the environment limits their application (interactions may alter the properties of 2D materials or produce new materials that are not conducive to research). For example, some 2D materials may be oxidized in air and change their original properties. Third, the study of the stability of 2D materials in neural computing is also a big challenge. Research on neural computing is still in its infancy, and new materials are being constantly explored. To realize the excellent computing power of the nervous system, a stable material needs to be found that can emulate the nerve, which requires 2D materials to be further enhanced with respect to stability. Furthermore, the low optical absorption or limited spectral range inherent in most 2D materials limit the capabilities of photonic and optoelectronic devices. Because the optical and electronic properties depend on the lateral dimensions of the 2D structure, lateral quantum confinement is also a problem that needs to be resolved.288–290 Finally, the doping of 2D materials is a technical problem. Because it is easy to form defects by doping 2D materials with the traditional doping method of 3D materials, many defects can be easily formed in the preparation process of 2D materials.

V. Summary and outlook

In this paper, the applications of 2D materials, such as graphene, TMDs, BN, BP, perovskites, and GaSe in the field of memristors are summarized, which emphasize the advantages of a 2D system. The structures and characteristics of different 2D material systems are analyzed. It is found that various types of 2D material systems possess rich physical switching mechanisms, namely, filament, atomic vacancy, and trapping and detrapping of electrons. In addition, studies have found that a memristor can have a significant impact on brain-like computing and artificial intelligence.

Admittedly, the advantages presented by memristors based on 2D materials ensure that they can have promising development prospects in the future. Here, we list some of the research directions that exist for the future. First, in the short term, the performance of a memristor based on 2D materials, such as the ON/OFF current ratio, cycling performance, retention time, power consumption, and switching voltage, should be further improved such that the memristor can conform to actual storage needs. In this respect, the process of preparing 2D materials is very critical. There are many preparation methods for 2D materials, which are generally categorized into top-down and bottom-up preparation methods. The top-down method mainly involves the exfoliation method, one of the most famous being micromechanical cleavage using a Scotch tape to cleave thin flakes from the bulk-layer materials.291 In addition, there is another common method called the LPE technique, namely, 2D materials are placed in various solvents for exfoliation and sonication-force-assisted exfoliation is often used in this process.292–294 The ultrasound energy induces bubbles in the solvent, and the collapse of these bubbles produces forces that exfoliates the bulk-layer materials into 2D materials. The bottom-up methods mainly include CVD, epitaxy, and chemical synthesis from small molecules,294–298 which are the key to prepare high-quality single-layer films and will be another future research direction.

Second, the switching mechanism of a memristor based on 2D materials is an important research direction. This can involve the observation and identification of conductive channels in the memristor device with atomic resolution under different switching conditions using an in situ probing technique in conjunction with the high spatial resolution of EELS. Ideally, the formation and annihilation of conducting channels can be observed at the atomic scale. In addition, the study of defects is very important to understand the basic characteristics of the materials. There are many ways to study defects, for example, GBs in materials and GB migration can be studied via atomic force microscopy (AFM), which can directly affect the optical and electronic properties of materials. In addition, the GB structure formed in the material due to the preparation process, stress, and voltage can also be observed by aberration-corrected high-resolution TEM (AC-HRTEM) and aberration-corrected scanning TEM (AC-STEM) at the atomic scale. For atomic-vacancy defects, it is necessary to use precise instruments to characterize the atomic structure; TEM is a worthwhile technique to achieve this requirement. For atomic-vacancy defects, it is necessary to use precise instruments to characterize the atomic structure. AC-HRTEM and AC-STEM are worthwhile strategies to observe the absence of atoms and the formation of vacancies. In addition to these characterization techniques, energy for defect formation can also be determined via first-principles calculations to investigate the difficulty of defect formation. These methods can shed some light on the research direction with respect to material defects.

Third, a memristor device based on 2D materials has the ability of persistent storage and release of information via optical and electrical modulations, which is a cutting-edge research direction. Designing various hybrid systems can facilitate an increase in the number of storage levels and improve the storage performance. In this case, UV-vis light can be added to control the device to explore the effects of light (under different wavelengths and different powers) on device performance. In addition, exploring the influence of light should not be limited to the visible region; it is essential to develop optical memory devices operating in the infrared bands. Another research direction is the application of memristors based on 2D materials in flexible electronics. Electron devices for flexible wearable and future biomedical applications (such as implanted treatments and human–machine interfaces) must be flexible with good mechanical properties, and 2D materials with unique advantages have promising potential for applications in this respect; this can be expected to improve people's living standards. In addition, memristors based on 2D materials have made great progress in the applications of synaptic simulation and neuromorphic systems in the recent years, but there is still a long way to go to implement brain-like computing. Further, current neuromorphic systems based on 2D materials contain only one or several synapses or neurons, and system integration is still at a relatively low level; there is still a lot of room for development in the future. Finally, at the atomically thin limit, some excellent properties of materials are revealed, and the material characteristics and functionalities are dominated by surface chemistry and interface coupling. In order to realize the full technological potential of 2D materials and design new material systems with atomic accuracy, 2D heterogeneous systems will be an important research direction in the future. By combining the valuable properties of different 2D materials, a heterogeneous system can be used to resolve the problems that cannot be resolved by a single 2D material, particularly those that require vertical multilayered structures. 2D layered materials are connected by van der Waals force without direct chemical bonds. Therefore, different 2D materials are not restricted by lattice matching when combining, and it is easy to form heterojunctions, which has the potential for development in the future. In this respect, the preparation of large-area heterojunctions is a potential development direction. In actual production, materials are needed to meet the needs of large-area applications. Now, preparation methods can be used to fabricate single-layer 2D materials, but the preparation of continuous multilayer heterojunctions is difficult, because the growth of a continuous multilayer often destroys the quality and properties of the underlying materials. Therefore, the continuous and controllable preparation of multilayer and large-area heterogeneous structures is a potential area for future development. In other words, research on 2D-materials-based memristors is just the beginning; in order to realize these prospects, continuous innovative research is imperative.
