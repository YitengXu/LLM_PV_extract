Abstract

Cu2+xCd1âˆ’xSnS4 thin films were prepared with excess Cu concentrations (x = 0, 0.02, 0.04, 0.06, 0.08 and 0.1) by a successive ionic layer adsorption and reaction (SILAR) method. The deposited films were characterized by X-ray diffraction (XRD), field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM), transmission electron microscopy (TEM), UV-visible spectroscopy and Iâ€“V analysis. XRD analysis confirms the formation of tetragonal structured Cu2CdSnS4 thin films for all the excess Cu concentrations, while the corresponding FESEM images showed microstructural change. It may be attributed to the injection of excess Cu atoms into the Cd sites of the Cu2CdSnS4. The absorption coefficient is found to be greater than 104 cmâˆ’1 in the visible region and the bandgap energy is slightly tuned from 1.68 eV to 1.58 eV when compared to Cu2CdSnS4. The Cu2.1Cd0.9SnS4 film showed the highest photoresponse.

1. Introduction

Solar energy is the renewable energy source that is most abundant, clean, and readily available throughout the year. Photovoltaic devices/solar cells are one of the most effective means of harvesting solar energy and converting it into electricity for useful applications. The pâ€“n junction Si photovoltaic (PV) device was first reported by Chapin et al. in 1954.1 Since then, the science and technology of PV devices and systems have undergone revolutionary developments. The present bottleneck or hurdle to the use of solar cells is their production cost, because the current PV industry mainly depends on Si wafer technologies.2 The fabrication process of this Si based PV technology has a high cost.

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) are one of the most promising photovoltaic devices as they have low cost and light-to-energy conversion with relatively high efficiency.3 DSSC was reported by Regan and Gratzel (1991).4 A typical DSSC consists of three key components: a dye-sensitized semiconductor photoanode, an electrolyte with a redox couple, and a counter electrode (CE). Platinum is most commonly used as the CE, but it has a high cost. Several attempts have been made to replace Pt with a low cost, stable material without affecting cell performance.5â€“7

The CZTS compound is a p-type semiconductor that belongs to the Cu2-IIâ€“IVâ€“VI4 family. It can be used as a potential absorber layer in thin film solar cells to replace expensive solar absorbers (CIS, CISe, CIGS and CIGSe) and CE for DSSCs owing to its high optical absorption coefficient (>104 cmâˆ’1) and ideal direct band gap (1.4â€“1.5 eV). The constituents of CZTS are abundantly available in the earth's crust, are notably non-toxic and can be produced at low cost. CZTS exhibits a stannite type structure.8 A recent study of CZTS showed that it has a high power conversion efficiency of about 12%.9 The study also showed that CZTS can be an alternatative to the high-cost Pt electrode; this could considerably reduce the cost of solar cells. Sawanta S. Mali et al. (2014) and Shanlong Chen et al. (2015) reported higher efficiency of DSSC with FTO/CZTS as CE than the DSSC with Pt as CE.10,11

Cu2CdSnS4 (CCTS) is a member of the Cu2-IIâ€“IVâ€“VI4 family that exhibits a high absorption coefficient (>104 cmâˆ’1) analogous to that of CZTS.12 Thus, it is used as an absorbing material in solar cells.13 Mokurala et al. (2014) reported lower efficiency using DSSC with FTO/CCTS as CE than the DSSC with Pt as CE.14 Therefore, the present investigation was motivated to prepare the CCTS compound and to study its opto-structural, morphological and photoconversion properties.

CCTS thin films have been prepared by chemical bath deposition,15 spin coating,16,17 atom beam sputtering18 and spray pyrolysis.12 Herein, for the first time, the quaternary CCTS thin films were deposited by a simple low cost SILAR method at room temperature and their photocurrent generations were studied. Compared to other methods, the successive ionic layer adsorption and reaction (SILAR) method is a simple, less expensive, and less time-consuming method for the deposition of binary semiconducting thin films. It is also applicable in the deposition of large-area thin films. The SILAR method was first reported by Ristov et al. (1985).19 The name SILAR was ascribed by Nicolau in the mid-1980s.20

SILAR has a number of advantages: (i) it offers an extremely easy way to dope films, (ii) it does not require high quality target and vacuum at any stage, (iii) the deposition rate and the thickness of the film can be easily controlled, (iv) operating at room temperature can produce films on less robust materials, (v) it does not cause local over heating, which can be detrimental for materials to be deposited and (vi) there are virtually no restrictions on the substrate material, dimensions or its surface profile. The detailed explanation of the SILAR method is given by Pathan and Lokhande (2004).21

2. Experimental section

In this study, A.R. grade copper chloride (CuCl2), cadmium chloride (CdCl2), tin chloride (SnCl2) and sodium sulfide (Na2S) and double distilled water were used. Thin films of the Cu2+xCd1âˆ’xSnS4 system were deposited on microscopic glass slides by the SILAR method at room temperature. The quality and quantity of thin film mainly depends on how well the substrate was cleaned. Thus, the glass substrates were cleaned with chromic acid and acetone. The SILAR method involves parameters of precursor concentration, dipping time and number of cycles. In this study, we have enriched the Cu ion concentration while reducing the Cd ions into the Cu2CdSnS4 system.

For the synthesis of Cu2CdSnS4 film, cationic precursors, namely, 0.1 M CuCl2, 0.05 M CdCl2 and 0.05 M SnCl2, were dissolved in distilled water. The anionic precursor solution was prepared by dissolving Na2S in distilled water to make a 0.2 M Na2S solution. First, the precleaned substrates were immersed in the composite cationic solution for 30 s to adsorb Cu2+, Cd2+ and Sn2+ ions. Then, the substrate was rinsed in distilled water for 10 s to remove the loosely adsorbed ions if any. Subsequently, the substrate was immersed in the anion solution (S2âˆ’) for 30 s, where the chemical reaction occurred between anions and cations to form CCTS. The substrate was again rinsed in distilled water for 10 s to remove the powdery precipitate if any. The abovementioned four steps are one SILAR cycle, and it was optimized. Similarly, 70 cycles were employed to obtain homogeneous CCTS film. The same procedure was adopted to enrich Cu concentrations (2.02, 2.04, 2.06, 2.08 and 2.10) into the CCTS system. The deposited films were annealed at 250 Â°C for 2 h in an air atmosphere. Photoconductivity is a prerequisite for a material to be suitable for solar cell applications. The photoconductivity of a semiconductor involves two processes: generation and transport. The absorption of a photon by a photoconductor could result in the generation of free electrons excited from the valence band to the conduction band. Concurrently, a hole is generated in the valence band. The application of an electric field through the material results in the transportation of both electrons and holes through the material and consequent production of an electric current in the circuit. This process increases the conductivity of the semiconductor. The photosensitive responses of Cu2+xCd1âˆ’xSnS4 thin films were recorded using a visible light source in air at room temperature using a CHI604E electrochemical workstation. Fig. 16 shows the Iâ€“V characteristics of CCTS-4 (Fig. 16a) and CCTS-5 (Fig. 16b) film, which exhibit ohmic behavior for both dark and illuminated (visible light) conditions. However, the latter showed a higher photosensitivity, attributed to the visible light photons that excite the electrons in the valence band to the conduction band, and then increase in the holes in the presence of light enhances the film conductivity of the CCTS. A similar result was reported for the CZTS thin films.53 The Iâ€“V measurements show no rectification behaviour, which indicates the absence of a Schottky barrier at film contacts.

By comparing our results (Table 3) with earlier reports, it can be inferred that the present study shows high values for photocurrent.23 This may be attributed to the bigger sized particles (Fig. 3â€“6) present in the system that increase the diffusion length of the carriers. It is well known that bigger particle size is beneficial to the collection of charge and for decreasing the recombination at the grain boundaries of the final devices.14 However, the present result is relatively lower than that of CZTS and Cu2CoSnS4 thin films56,60 because the systems have low Eg values of 1.5 eV and 1.58 eV, respectively. Generally, a device with higher band gap gives lower efficiency, because it absorbs fewer photons, which in turn reduces the photosensitivity.61 Thus, further investigation are carried out to increase the photocurrent of the CCTS films from that of Cu2ZnSnS4 and Cu2CoSnS4 films by varying its deposition time, film thickness, and annealing temperature and to fabricate CCTS based solar cells.

4. Conclusions

Cu2âˆ’xCd1âˆ’xSnS4 thin films were prepared for various Cu concentrations (x = 0, 0.02, 0.04, 0.06, 0.08 and 0.1) by a SILAR method. The deposited CCTS system shows a polycrystalline nature within the tetragonal system, and increase in excess Cu concentration gives the same crystal structure. The plane (220) shows shift towards higher angles with excess Cu concentration. FESEM image shows spherical, flake, square, rhombohedral and hexagonal morphologies for increase in excess Cu concentration. The transformation of morphology may be due to the injection of excess Cu atoms into Cd sites in the CCTS system. The TEM image also confirms the polygonal, sphere and flake structures, which support the morphological changes observed in the FESEM analysis. SAED pattern confirms the polycrystalline nature of CCTS-2 films. The absorption coefficient is found to be greater than 104 cmâˆ’1 for all the CCTS films. The bandgap energy is slightly altered from 1.68 eV to 1.58 eV. The positive and low values of extinction coefficient indicate the strong absorbing nature and smoothness of the deposited films. The increase in refractive index confirms the strong absorbing nature of the films. The percentage of photosensitivity is higher for Cu2.1Cd0.9SnS4 thin films, attributed to the increase in electronâ€“hole pairs. It is concluded from the opto-structural and morphological studies that the Cu-rich films show better optical properties, which are suitable for photovoltaic applications.
