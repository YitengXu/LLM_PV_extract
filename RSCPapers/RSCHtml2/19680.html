A comparative theoretical study of exciton-dissociation and charge-recombination processes in oligothiophene/fullerene and oligothiophene/perylenediimide complexes for organic solar cells†‡

Yuanping Yi , Veaceslav Coropceanu and Jean-Luc Brédas *
School of Chemistry and Biochemistry and Center for Organic Photonics and Electronics, Georgia Institute of Technology, Atlanta, Georgia, USA 30332-0400. E-mail: jean-luc.bredas@chemistry.gatech.edu

Received 29th July 2010 , Accepted 27th September 2010

First published on 6th January 2011

Abstract

The exciton-dissociation and charge-recombination processes in donor–acceptor complexes found in α-sexithienyl/C60 and α-sexithienyl/perylenetetracarboxydiimide (PDI) solar cells are investigated by means of quantum-chemical methods. The electronic couplings and exciton-dissociation and charge-recombination rates have been evaluated for various configurations of the complexes. The results suggest that the decay of the lowest charge-transfer state to the ground state in the PDI-based devices: (i) is faster than that in the fullerene-based devices and (ii) in most cases, can compete with the dissociation of the charge-transfer state into mobile charge carriers. This faster charge-recombination process is consistent with the lower performance observed experimentally for the devices using PDI derivatives as the acceptor.

1. Introduction

Organic solar cells are currently attracting intense interest due to a number of potential advantages, such as low cost, flexibility, large-area capability, and ease of processing.1–10 The architecture of an organic solar cell typically consists of an organic bilayer or of a blend (bulk heterojunction), composed of an electron donor (D) and an electron acceptor (A) material, sandwiched between two electrodes of different nature.11,12 The mechanism of light-to-electricity conversion includes five main steps: (i) light absorption: the donor and/or acceptor materials absorb sunlight to generate excitons (electrically neutral, bound electron-hole pairs); the strength and width of the absorption spectrum need to be maximized for efficiently harvesting the incident solar radiation; (ii) exciton diffusion: excitons have to migrate to the D/A interface; a large exciton diffusion length is desirable to ensure that the exciton can reach the interface before it decays; (iii) exciton dissociation: excitons dissociate at the interface with electrons going into the acceptor and holes into the donor; the charge-transfer rate needs to be maximized while charge recombination must be suppressed; (iv) charge transport: electrons [holes] migrate within the acceptor [donor] material towards their respective electrodes; high and balanced charge-carrier mobilities are required; and (v) charge collection: good contacts as well as favorable energy level alignments at the organic/electrode interfaces should be achieved. Increasing the power conversion efficiency (PCE) can be realized through improvement in molecular design and optimization of device structure.

Recent experiments have demonstrated relatively high PCE's in polythiophene/fullerene and oligothiophene/fullerene solar cells. For instance, organic photovoltaic (OPV) cells based on a bulk heterojunction (BHJ) made of regioregular poly(3-alkylthiophenes) (P3ATs) and the fullerene derivative [6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PC61BM) exhibit PCE values as high as 3–5%;13–17 even higher values are reported when using [6,6]-phenyl-C71-butyric acid methyl ester (PC71BM) as acceptor, due to its stronger absorption across the visible region with respect to PC61BM.17 Small-molecule BHJ OPV cells consisting of α-sexithienyl (6T) and fullerene (C60 and C70) have also been fabricated and display reasonably high efficiencies (about 2%).18,19 Similar PCE's were measured in photovoltaic devices using an oligothiophene–fullerene dyad mixed with either an oligothiophene or PCB61M.20

Compared to fullerene derivatives, perylenetetracarboxydiimide (PDI) derivatives not only display good electron accepting properties,21 large exciton diffusion lengths,22 and high electron mobilities,23–26 but also exhibit stronger and broader light absorption.23,27 Therefore, PDI derivatives can be expected to be a good alternative to fullerenes as acceptor materials for OPV cells. However, a very low PCE (around 0.4%) was observed by Friend et al. for OPV cells based on poly(3-hexylthiophene):N,N′-bis(1-ethylpropyl)-3,4:9,10-perylene bis(tetracarboxydiimide) (P3HT:EPPTC) blends under illumination of 0.16 mW cm−2 monochromatic light at 540 nm;28 under 100 mW cm−2 white-light illumination, the PCE for a morphology-optimized P3HT:EPPTC blend was found to be 0.25%.29 Shin et al. have also reported that OPV devices based on blends of P3HT and PDIs with a variety of functional groups present a highest PCE of only 0.18% under 1 sun AM1.5G illumination (100 mW cm−2).30Oligothiophene/PDI-based OPV devices also demonstrate low performance. For instance, a conversion efficiency of only 0.59% was reported for bilayer heterojunction OPV cells using α-octathienyl and 3,4,9,10-perylene tetracarboxylic bisbenzimidazole under white-light illumination at 105 mW cm−2;31 an even lower value (0.02%) was obtained for those using α-quinquethienyl and N,N′-bis(3,5-dimethylphenyl)-3,4:9,10-perylenebis(dicarboximide).32 In addition, the efficiency for devices based on dendritic oligothiophene bearing PDI units was estimated to be less than 0.40% under 10 µW cm−2 illumination at 410 nm.33,34 Although the light intensity varies from experiment to experiment and influences the current density–voltage (J–V) characteristics of the OPV devices, the overall power conversion efficiency is only weakly dependent on light intensity.35 Thus, it appears reasonable to conclude that the performance of oligo- or poly-thiophene/PDI solar cells is significantly lower (by nearly one order of magnitude) with respect to devices based on oligo- or poly-thiophene and fullerene.

Here, in order to provide some understanding of the reasons for this difference, we have carried out a comparative study of the D/A complexes present in oligothiophene–PDI or oligothiophene–fullerene OPV devices. We have investigated the charge-transfer (CT) and charge-recombination (CR) processes at the D/A interface by means of quantum-chemical methods. We chose α-sexithienyl (6T) as the representative for oligo- and poly-thiophene materials and C60 and N,N′-dimethyl-PDI (Me-PDI) to represent the fullerene and PDI derivatives, respectively. The electronic couplings and electron-transfer rates were evaluated for the model intermolecular configurations illustrated in Fig. 1.

2. Methodology

The 6T, C60, and Me-PDI molecular geometries for the ground and lowest triplet states, and the charged (ionic) states were optimized with density functional theory (DFT). The geometries for the lowest singlet state were optimized by time-dependent DFT (TDDFT). Based on each of the optimized geometries, the total energies for the ground, excited, and charged states were evaluated by the same DFT or TDDFT method that was applied in the geometry optimization. All the DFT and TDDFT calculations were performed at the B3LYP/6-31G** level as implemented in the Gaussian-09 program.36 The ground-state geometry for 6T was kept coplanar since oligo- and poly-thiophenes tend to crystallize in the solid state with the thiophene units coplanar.37,38

3. Results and discussion

Our goal here is to analyze the rates of exciton dissociation and charge recombination for intermolecular configurations that are meant to model the interfacial geometries of the complexes in 6T/PDI and 6T/C60 solar cells, and to estimate how these rates might influence device performance. According to eqn (1), there are three key parameters governing the electron-transfer processes: the Gibbs free energy ΔG0, the reorganization energy λ, and the electronic coupling VIF. We first discuss these parameters before turning to a description of the rates of exciton dissociation and charge recombination.

3.1. Gibbs free energy

If we ignore the entropy contribution, ΔG0 can be estimated as the energy difference between the diabatic initial and final states. The energy of the diabatic states is composed of the total energies of the isolated states of the donor and acceptor and the Coulomb energy between the donor and acceptor. Finally, we point out that the difference between the calculated and experimental EA values for PDI is on the order of the polarization energy estimated by Gerischer and Willig (∼1.3 eV).62

The Coulomb energies of the CT states are shown in Fig. 2. The electrostatic interactions lead to a significant stabilization of these states, by up to 0.5 eV and 0.4 eV for the 6T/PDI and 6T/C60 complexes, respectively. For all the complexes, as expected, the Coulomb interactions are continuously reduced with increasing intermolecular displacements. Displacements along the long axis of 6T from 0 to 16 Å and along the short axis from 0 to 6 Å (the upper limit taken for these displacements is where the electronic couplings (nearly) vanish, see Fig. 3) result in a decrease of ca. 0.25 eV for complex 6T/PDI and 0.2 eV for complex 6T/C60 (in absolute values of the Coulomb energy). When considering the same displacements along the long and short axes, the Coulomb attraction in the 6T/PDI complex is about 0.05 eV stronger than that in the 6T/C60 complex, due to the fact that a larger number of atoms in PDI are close to the 6T molecule.

Since organic solar cells are fabricated in the form of thin films, we have made use of the experimental solid-state values for the excitation and ionization energies in the calculations of the exciton-dissociation and charge-recombination rates; in the case of EA values, due to large uncertainty in experimental solid-state data, we considered the same EA value of −3.8 eV for both acceptors. According to eqn (6), the CT energies in the solid state (which are the opposite of ΔG0 for the CR processes) are estimated to vary from 1.0 to 1.25 eV with increasing displacements for complex 6T/PDI. The corresponding energies are slightly higher for complex 6T/C60 (1.1 to 1.3 eV) due to the weaker Coulomb attraction. Our estimates of the CT energies in complex 6T/C60 are similar to the values (ca. 1.0 eV) for the CT states in P3HT/PCBM blends derived from electroluminescence measurements.67

Interestingly, the CT states lie around or even below the T1 states of the individual molecules, which indicates that triplet excitons could also dissociate to create charge carriers. This might be relevant especially for the 6T/C60 solar cells since C60 presents a high efficiency for intersystem crossing.68 Here, we have not considered the contribution from triplet excitons to the CT and CR processes.

For complex 6T/PDI, when increasing the displacements, ΔG0 for the CT process from the S1 state varies from −1.38 to −1.13 eV for 6T and is 0.2 eV lower in absolute value for PDI due to the lower S1 energy. For complex 6T/C60, the corresponding values for ΔG0 are −1.28 to −1.08 eV and −0.6 to −0.4 eV for 6T and C60, respectively.

3.2. Reorganization energy

The reorganization energy (λ) is normally decomposed into internal (intramolecular) and external (intermolecular) contributions. The former arises from the changes in the geometry of the donor and acceptor moieties and the latter in the electronic and nuclear polarization of the surrounding medium upon electron transfer. The intramolecular reorganization energy, λi, can be easily calculated from the adiabatic potential energy surfaces of the molecular states involved in the considered electron-transfer process. For instance, λi for charge recombination of the lowest CT state to the ground state is estimated as the sum of the relaxation energies in the ground state when the donor and acceptor relax from their ionic geometries to their neutral geometries. Similarly, λi for exciton dissociation can be estimated via the potential energy surfaces of the charged states.

The calculated molecular relaxation energies involved in the CT and CR processes are collected in Table 2. The values of λi for the CR process (sum of the changes in the ground-state energy from the cationic to the ground-state geometry for the donor and from the anionic to the ground-state geometry for the acceptor) are 0.20 and 0.26 eV for complexes 6T/C60 and 6T/PDI, respectively. The latter value represents the largest value of λi among all the CT and CR processes in both 6T/C60 and 6T/PDI complexes. For all molecules, the relaxation energies related to the ground state upon charging or removing the charge are found to be very similar. The λi values for dissociation of the S1 and T1 states of 6T and PDI are smaller (≤0.1 eV) than those for the CR process, while the reverse is true in the case of C60. In any event, the variation among the relaxation energies of PDI and C60 is very small, in the range of 0.01 to 0.07 eV.

While a few methodologies based on quantum/classical QM/MM approaches69 or a polarizable force field70 have been recently developed to compute the external reorganization energy (λe), these approaches remain rather complex and, to date, limited to applications to single organic semiconductor crystals. Our systems correspond to blends of two types of molecules, which further complicate an accurate evaluation of λe. The values of λe in a nonpolar solvent (toluene) and polar solvents (THF and PhCN) for the CT and CR processes in both fullerene–oligothiophene–fullerene triads and oligothiophene–perylenediimide dyads have been estimated to be 0.05 and 0.7 eV, respectively.71,72 As a result, here, we have assumed an averaged value of 0.5 eV for the overall reorganization energy, given the very similar values of λi and λe in the oligothiophene/fullerene and oligothiophene/perylenediimide systems as well as the polarizabilities of the donor–acceptor systems in nonpolar and polar solvents.

3.3. Electronic coupling

The calculated electronic couplings involving the lowest CT state for the 6T/PDI (configuration a) and 6T/C60 complexes (configurations b and c) are depicted in Fig. 3. The results confirm that the electronic couplings strongly depend on both relative molecular orientations and intermolecular distances.42 As the acceptor (PDI and C60) slides on top of the molecular plane of 6T, the electronic coupling shows an oscillatory dependence on the displacements along the long and short axes of 6T, due to the alternation in the bonding/antibonding pattern of the involved molecular orbitals.73 A larger extent of bonding/antibonding alternations for long-axis displacements with respect to short-axis displacements leads to a larger number of peaks and valleys in the electronic couplings. In most instances, the electronic couplings between PDI and 6T are significantly larger than those with C60 for both CT and CR processes due to the closer molecular packing between PDI and 6T. For complex 6T/PDI, the electronic couplings can become as large as 100 meV, while they only reach 40 meV in a very limited region for complex 6T/C60. Similarly, slightly stronger electronic couplings appear in configuration c than in configuration b for complex 6T/C60.

For complex 6T/PDI, in general, the electronic couplings to the lowest CT state are slightly stronger for the ground state than for the lowest excited state of the acceptor (PDI), but weaker than for the lowest excited state of the donor (6T). For complex 6T/C60, similar situations are found for these couplings in the case of configuration c, while in the case of configuration b the electronic couplings between the ground state and the CT state are in most instances the strongest.

3.4. Exciton-dissociation and charge-recombination rates

The CR and CT rates for complexes 6T/PDI and 6T/C60 have been calculated by taking into account the driving forces, reorganization energies, and electronic couplings described above; their accuracy will thus be limited by the approximations that were made for the determination of the parameters entering Marcus theory. The results are shown in Fig. 4.
Fig. 4 Evolution of the CR rate from the lowest CT state to the ground state (kS0) and of the CT rates from the lowest singlet state on PDI or C60 (kS1A) and on 6T (kS1D) to the lowest CT state, as a function of the displacements of 6T along the long and short axes, for complexes 6T/PDI (configuration a) and 6T/C60 (configurations b and c). Here, EA = −3.8 eV.

The electron-transfer rates display the same alternating modulation as the electronic couplings as a function of the long- and short-axis displacements, although the trends in peak values are different. All the CT and CR processes we considered correspond to exothermic reactions, namely ΔG0 < 0. In addition, except in the case of dissociation of the S1 state of C60, all the absolute values of ΔG0 are larger than the reorganization energy (ΔG0 + λ < 0). In Marcus theory (see eqn (1)), the electron-transfer rates reach a maximum when ΔG0 + λ = 0. Since the driving force for the CT process reduces with the long- and short-axis displacements, the dissociation rates of the S1 states of 6T (kS1D in Fig. 4) and PDI (kS1A in Fig. 4a) generally increase with displacements, while the dissociation rates for the S1 state of C60 (kS1A in Fig. 4b and c) decrease. For the CR process, the combination of the ΔG0 and electronic coupling evolutions results in a faster drop in the CR rates with increasing displacements (see kS0 in Fig. 4).

Fig. 4 illustrates that in most cases the acceptor (PDI and C60)-based excitons dissociate more quickly than the donor (6T)-based excitons, even though the acceptor-based excitons have relatively smaller couplings with the CT state. This can be attributed to the value of ΔG0 + λ becoming closer to zero for the acceptor-based excitons. The CT rates for complex 6T/C60 are larger than those for complex 6T/PDI when the excitons are on the acceptor, while these rates are similar when the excitons are on the donor. For both 6T/PDI and 6T/C60 complexes, the CT rates can be very large and generally reach ∼109 to 1013 s−1 for all types of excitons. Thus, our calculations suggest that the excitons are able to dissociate efficiently in both 6T/PDI and 6T/C60 solar cells.

Interestingly, for most complex configurations, the decay of the lowest CT state to the ground state for complex 6T/C60 is substantially slower than the exciton dissociation. In contrast, for a large number of intermolecular configurations of the 6T/PDI complex, the decay of the lowest CT state is similar or even faster than the formation of the CT state. When comparing the two systems, our calculations reveal that, in general, the decay of the CT state back to the ground state in complex 6T/PDI is more efficient with respect to complex 6T/C60; this conclusion also holds if we explicitly consider other EA values in the range −3.95 to −3.5 eV, see ESI†. Thus, it appears that for complex 6T/PDI, the CR process can efficiently compete with the dissociation of the CT state into mobile charge carriers. To evaluate more generally the impact of the interfacial configurations that can be present in BHJ solar cells, we have also performed calculations where other intermolecular orientations (generated by rotating the PDI or 6T molecule, see the results in ESI†) of the 6T/PDI and 6T/C60 complexes are considered. The results again demonstrate that the CR rates are much faster in complex 6T/PDI. Therefore, the lower performance of the thiophene/PDI OPV devices could be related to this highly efficient CR process.

4. Conclusions

We have investigated the exciton-dissociation and charge-recombination processes in donor/acceptor complexes of relevance to 6T/C60 and 6T/PDI solar cells by means of quantum-chemical methods. We have evaluated the electronic couplings and the CT and CR rates for different model configurations that can be present in bulk heterojunction devices. The results show that, with comparison to the 6T/C60 complex, the decay of the lowest CT state to the ground state in the 6T/PDI complex is much faster. Importantly, in most cases, this recombination could compete with the dissociation of the CT state into mobile charge carriers. Our results thus point to the fact that the poorer performance observed experimentally for the PDI-based devices could result from this efficient CR process.

While we are acutely aware of the limitations of our calculations (consideration of a single isolated complex, no explicit account of polarization effects), we believe that they constitute a useful step in our understanding of exciton dissociation and charge recombination at the molecular level. Work is in progress to take better account of the influence of the environment.
