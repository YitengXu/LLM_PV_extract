Abstract

This review provides an overview of materials within the Bâ€“Câ€“N triangular zone. The structure, properties, synthesis routes and applications of boron carbon nitride (BCN) nanomaterials have been discussed in detail. Boron carbon nitride is a ternary compound of Bâ€“Câ€“N, which has been anticipated to possess properties in-between those of conducting graphene and insulating BN. The properties of BCN can be easily tuned with slight variation in the arrangement and composition of B, C and N atoms. Various morphologies of BCN such as nanoparticles, nanosheets, nanotubes, nanoplates, and aerogels show unique and exceptional properties to its analogous counterparts. BCN offers potential applications in energy devices, supercapacitors, nanoelectronics, optoelectronics, electrochemical sensors, catalysis, high-temperature lubricants, novel composites, biomedical applications, etc. This review provides a summary of recent developments in BCN materials.

1. Introduction

Nanoscience and nanotechnology have been crossing boundaries to revolutionize a varied range of fields. Nanomaterials are generally defined as materials having dimensions in the nano-range, i.e., 1â€“100 nm. These nanomaterials are classified as nanoparticles, nanosheets, nanotubes, etc., based on their nano-dimensional growth. In the last few years, research interest in graphene based and layered nanomaterials has flourished due to their interesting physical and chemical properties.1,2 Specific surface area and bandgap have made these nanomaterials significant for the advancement of opto-electronic devices, energy storage, gas sensors, protective layers, etc. The underlying notion of a two dimensional layered material goes back to the 1940s when the layered structure of graphite was described, which could be exfoliated to individual layers having extraordinary electronic characteristics. However, theoretically it was proposed that the monolayer product of graphite may not be thermodynamically stable. In 2004, for the first time, Novoselov et al. successfully extracted individual graphene monolayers using a simple mechanical exfoliation method, which showed high stability and superior electrical conductivity. The exceptional electrical, thermal, mechanical, and optical properties of graphene have motivated research in analogous materials such as boron nitride (BN) and boron carbide (B4C) and transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs).3â€“6 Monolayer boron and boron-based metal organic materials also show potential for application in various fields such as gas sensors, catalysis, energy storage, etc.7â€“9 One more exciting layered material is boron carbon nitride (BCN) which has gained research consideration over the past few years.

BCN exhibits tunable physical and electrical properties different from hexagonal BN (h-BN) and graphene.10,11 It is expected to combine the properties of BN, B4C, graphene and carbon nitride (C3N4) in BCN compounds. The composition variation of B, C and N atoms is responsible for the properties of BxCyNz.12 The ternary BCN consists of h-BCN rings along with BN and graphene domains which is different from B, N co-doped graphene or carbon-doped BN.13,14 Various theoretical predictions and experimental studies have revealed bandgap tuning possibility for BCN from large bandgap insulators to semiconductors.3,15 This ternary material offers potential applications in energy devices, supercapacitors, nanoelectronics, optoelectronics, electrochemical sensors, catalysis, high-temperature lubricants, novel composites, etc.16â€“22

2.1. Graphene

Graphene, composed of an sp2-hybridized single layer of carbon, is the first 2D material available. It is reported as the thinnest and strongest material.28 It exhibits high thermal conductivity (5000 W mâˆ’1 Kâˆ’1), high transparency, strong ambipolar electric field effect, superior carrier mobility, electrical conductivity, environmental compatibility, high theoretical specific surface area, and high Young's modulus (âˆ¼1 TPa), etc.29â€“36 These exceptional properties make 2D graphene promising for biological, electronic, mechanical, sensing, and catalytic applications.37â€“42 In 2004, for the first time graphene was fabricated by the â€˜peeling offâ€™ process.32 Till then, various synthetic routes such as solid exfoliation, liquid-phase exfoliation of graphite, reduction of graphene oxide, unzipping of carbon nanotubes, organic synthesis, chemical vapor deposition (CVD), epitaxial growth, etc. were employed for the synthesis of graphene.43â€“49 Graphene is a bandgap less material and oxygen reactive at high temperatures, which limits its use.

2.2. Boron nitride

BN forms a covalent structure similar to carbon allotropes as Bâ€“N and Câ€“C bonds are isoelectronic. The first synthesis of BN was reported by Balman in 1842. In the chemical reaction, boric acid (H3BO3) and potassium cyanide were used to synthesize BN, which was unstable.50 With the technological developments, the inexpensive and stable synthesis of BN became a reality. BN can be prepared by mechanical exfoliation, chemical exfoliation, CVD, substitution reaction, solid-state reaction, etc.51,52 BN can be formed in various crystalline structures â€“ hexagonal (h-BN), cubic (c-BN), wurtzite (w-BN), and rhombohedral (r-BN).53 Among these, c-BN is the most stable and is analogous to diamond having sp3-bonded phases. The atomic arrangement of w-BN is also sp3-bonded, arranged in a hexagonal network. r-BN possesses threefold layered stacking (stacking sequence of ABCABCâ€¦). h-BN, the most in demand structure, is analogous to graphite and has sp2-bonded atomic arrangements. The layers in hexagonal BN are arranged in AAâ€²AAâ€²â€¦ sequence.54 h-BN displays high chemical stability, thermal stability, electrical properties, tunable solubility, high-temperature lubricating properties, and gas adsorption properties.54â€“56 These days, boron nitride-based composites are also gaining much research interest due to improved properties than h-BN. In 1995, Chopra et al. synthesized boron nitride nanotubes (BNNTs) for the first time.57 BNNTs possess uniform electronic properties, extraordinary mechanical properties, high oxidation resistance, etc. BN has applications in the field of microwave transparent windows, semiconductors, electrodes, fuel cells, batteries, paints, cosmetics, structural material for seals, dental cement, sensors, etc.58,59

2.3. Boron carbide

Boron carbide with the chemical formula B4C is one of the hardest materials after diamond and c-BN. High hardness leads to the use of B4C in bulletproof vests, tank armor, and several industrial applications.60 Also, B4C holds the capability to absorb neutrons and is used for neutron radiation shielding applications.61 The crystal structure of B4C is rhombohedral, which comprises icosahedra (12-atom) positioned at the unit cell corners. The linear chains (3-atom) connect the icosahedra along the rhombohedral axis (111). The composition of the unit cell can be described as B6â€“Câ€“Câ€“Câ€“B6. The exchange of B and C atoms between interstitial atoms can take place (B6â€“Câ€“Bâ€“Câ€“B6).62 Boron carbide can exist in a wide range of composition (B4C to B10.5C).63 B4C can be synthesized by carbothermic reduction, vapour phase reactions, magnesiothermic reduction, from polymer precursors, by ion beam synthesis, liquidâ€“phase reactions, solid-state reactions, etc.62 The key characteristics of B4C are hardness, good strength, low density (2.52 g cmâˆ’3), wear resistance, chemical inertness and high elastic modulus.64,65 Additionally, the occurrence of defect states in B4C modifies its photocatalytic and photoluminescence properties.

2.4. Carbon nitride

Analogous to graphene, bulk g-C3N4 comprises layers held together by weak van der Waals forces. Various exfoliation methods like thermal etching exfoliation, liquid-phase exfoliation, chemical exfoliation, ultrasonic-assisted exfoliation, etc. have been employed to obtain a few sheets of g-C3N4.66 The structure comprises covalently linked (sp2-hybridized) carbon and nitrogen atoms. The bandgap of g-C3N4 is âˆ¼2.7 eV.67 The facile, environment friendly, and low-cost synthesis methods with excellent physicochemical properties have resulted in the exploration of g-C3N4 for enormous applications. The Câ€“N material possesses high hardness, good strength, wide bandgap, corrosion resistance, thermal conductivity, wear resistance, high stability in the air (âˆ¼600 Â°C), etc.68 Properties such as narrow bandgap, biodegradability, tunable bandgap, and electron-rich properties have shown the potential of nanostructured g-C3N4 in photocatalysis, energy storage, sensing, and solar cell applications.66

2.5. Boron carbon nitride

The boron (group IIIA), carbon (group IVA), and nitrogen (group VA) elements have similar atomic sizes. Also, the structures of boron nitride and carbon polymorphs are analogous, which forms a basis for synthesizing a diamond-like phase with all three elements. Boron carbon nitride is a ternary compound of Bâ€“Câ€“N, which has been anticipated to possess properties in-between those of conducting graphene and insulating BN.69 This boron carbon nitride class may be further divided into (i) simple, i.e., BN and graphene domain containing composites, and (ii) complex, i.e., Nâ€“C and Bâ€“C bonds along with Bâ€“N and Câ€“C bonding. The percentage of these bonds and the synthesis method are the two parameters that decide the properties of these materials. Various theoretical as well as experimental studies have revealed that BCN materials have numerous potential applications due to their outstanding optical, electrical, mechanical and thermal properties.70,71 These materials can be expected to find possible applications in electrical conductors, electronics, novel composites, high-temperature lubricants, hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), etc.2,17

The properties of BCN can be easily tuned with slight variation in the arrangement and composition of B, C and N atoms. The investigation of three hybrid phases of BCN has been explored â€“ h-BxCyNz, c-BxCyNz, and nanohybrids between CNTs and BNNTs.53 The insulating behaviour of h-BN and semi-metallic graphite has instigated research in the analogous h-BxCyNz hybrid phase with a honeycomb structure. h-BxCyNz offers bandgap tunability and stability of nanostructures with a mixture of h-BN and graphite domains. To develop a superhard material, research in the c-BxCyNz phase gained interest. High-temperature high-pressure synthesis methods are used for the synthesis of c-BxCyNz which is phase separated into diamond and c-BN. A detailed discussion of the properties, applications and synthesis methods of BCN is included in the subsequent sections.

3.1. Zero-dimensional (OD) BCN nanomaterials

Nanoparticles or nanospheres are referred to as 0D BCN nanomaterials. BCN nanoparticles demonstrate tunable photoluminescence properties. The increase in carbon concentration results in a narrow bandgap with PL emission shift from blue to the green region.72 BCN nanoparticles show a concentration dependent quantum confinement effect on optical properties. BCN nanospheres are also gaining a lot of interest. BC4N microspheres were synthesized with random distribution of B, C and N.73 Theoretical predictions show homogeneous distribution of B and N with high cohesive energy of configurational entropy. BCN hollow spheres with high purity were developed with h-BCN conserved in a spherical shell.74

3.2. One-dimensional (1D) BCN nanomaterials

Nanotubes, nanorods and nanoribbons are 1D BCN nanomaterials, among which BCN nanotubes (BCNNTs) have gained utmost interest.75â€“77 Similar to BNNTs and carbon nanotubes (CNTs), BCNNTs exhibit spiral or bamboo-like conformations wrapped in single-walled or multi-walled structure. A distinction from CNTs and BNNTs is that BCNNTs exhibit tunable electrical and optical properties with variation in B, C, and N atomsâ€™ arrangement and composition. The thermal conductivity of BCNNTs is improved compared to that of BNNTs. BCNNTs could be fabricated from B, N co-doped CNTs or C-doped BNNTs. Until now, both homogeneous and phase-separated BCNNTs have been reported. B, C, N intermixed homogeneous NTs with a bandgap of 2.8 eV have pyridine-like Câ€“N defective bonding structures, enabling chemical activity.78 The properties of BCNNTs depend upon wall layers, element compositions and structure alignment. Vertically aligned BCNNTs show their potential for photoelectricity and energy storage applications.79 BCN nanorods with high BET (Brunauer, Emmett and Teller) surface area and BCN nanoribbons have also been reported as 1D nanomaterials.

3.3. Two-dimensional (2D) BCN nanomaterials

Similar to 2D graphene, 2D BCN also emerged as a noteworthy layered nanomaterial due to its diverse and excellent properties. Usually, 2D BCN is synthesized as nanosheets.80 BCN nanosheets (BCNNSs) are 2D materials which comprise ternary BCN hybrid NSs, C-doped boron nitride nanosheets (BNNSs) and B, N co-doped graphene. B, N co-doped graphene demonstrates remarkable electrochemical properties because of the synergistic effects of B and N.81,82 The calculated highest-occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest-unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy of pure graphene and B, N co-doped graphene by Wang et al. showed high chemical reactivity and low kinetic stability of BCN.82 At low temperatures, graphene layers are metallic, whereas these characteristics enable BCNNSs to be semiconductors. B, N co-doped graphene also demonstrates microwave absorbing properties. But there is an issue of thermodynamic instability with phase isolation which shifts the focus to C-doped BNNSs. Theoretical calculations show the activation of BNNSs (insulators) by carbon substitution. Further intermixing in BCNNSs, rather than doping, results in fewer defect sites and increased sp2 hybridization of B, C and N. Ternary hybrid BCNNSs are homogeneous and display excellent electronic properties. BCNNSs possess high BET surface area and have been reported for lithium-ion storage. Various methods, such as solid-state reaction, microwave plasma CVD, thermal catalytic reaction, etc., have been reported for the synthesis of BCNNSs.83 In these methods, the solid-state reaction produced BCNNSs with low thermal conductivity, while h-BN and graphene possess ultrahigh thermal conductivity. So, BCNNSs can be a potential candidate for thermal energy applications. Another 2D BCN nanostructure is nanoflakes. The BCN nanoflakes synthesized by the pyrolysis method show their potential as high-temperature lubricant materials.

3.4. Three-dimensional (3D) BCN nanomaterials

The 3D graphene nanostructures have great potential in electrical applications, raising curiosity about how 3D BCN would perform. This interest resulted in the development of BCN aerogels and foams with crosslinking networks.84,85 The 3D BCN nanostructure possesses enhanced stability for gas adsorption or energy storage applications. BCN hybrid foams developed by microwave treatment showed density (0.24 g cmâˆ’3) with enhanced thermal stability, methanol desorption and conductivity. The improvement was attributed to the charge distribution of boron and nitrogen atoms with carbon and more defect density.85 BC2N and BC4N foams were also fabricated using the same method of which the latter showed incredible thermal stability (âˆ¼780 Â°C). In h-BN sheets, a decrease in layers leads to increased saturation magnetization in magnetic hysteresis. Similar to graphene and h-BN, BCN materials show ferromagnetism at room temperature. 5. Synthesis of BCN

A number of methods have been reported for the synthesis of BCN nanostructures over time. These methods can provide 0D, 1D, 2D and 3D BCN nanostructures. The reported synthesis techniques can mainly be divided into four categories â€“ chemical vapor deposition (CVD), physical vapor deposition (PVD), pyrolysis in a solid and liquid phase, and the molten salt method (MSM). 5.1. Chemical vapor deposition (CVD)

For two dimensional materials, CVD is an extensively used bottom-up synthesis approach in which solid products are formed by gaseous materials reacting on the substrate surface or in the vapor phase. Experimental parameters like pressure, temperature, gas flow rate, precursor composition, and distance between the source and the substrate play an important role in the size, orientation, morphology and layers of the 2D material.142 Kaner et al. reported graphitic Bâ€“Câ€“N, for the first time, by thermal decomposition of boron trichloride (BCl3), ammonia (NH3) and acetylene.143 After that, the CVD method for BCN synthesis has progressed and various methods such as ambient pressure CVD (APCVD), thermal catalytic CVD, gaseous catalyst assisted CVD, microwave plasma CVD, and plasma enhanced CVD (PECVD) have been reported.13,94,144â€“146 These methods result in high-quality products and controlled growth for application-oriented synthesis.

Aoki et al. synthesized BCN thin films by plasma-assisted chemical vapor deposition. Plasma of source gases nitrogen (N2) and CH4 â€“ was produced by supplying radio frequency (RF) power to the turn coil. Precursor BCl3 was carried near the substrate (kept at 730 Â°C) by H2 gas. The ratios of B, C, N and O were 43%, 30%, 24% and 3% respectively and O atoms were deliberately incorporated. The results of water-treated BCN films showed potential for a novel humidity sensor.147 Son et al. demonstrated that growth of flat graphene pads on a mechanically exfoliated h-BN surface can be achieved by APCVD. Graphene pads are grown with a high yield of âˆ¼95% with round shape, high flatness and homogeneous diameter. It has been observed that the size and layers of graphene pads are directly dependent on growth parameters (temperature and gas concentration).144

In the CVD process, single or multiple source precursors can be employed during the synthesis of 2D BCN nanosheets. Lin et al. reported BCN growth by using single layer graphene oxide (GO), NH3 and boron trioxide (B2O3) as precursors. It was observed that B and N atoms (from precursors) were fused on the substrate while B and C atoms formed Bâ€“N and Câ€“N bonds by binding with N.148 Tang et al. reported the synthesis of graphene domains on h-BN by the GACVD process in which silane was used as a gaseous catalyst. Hall measurements showed an ultra-flat dielectric substrate. This synthesis method can be used for device-based applications.145 Leardini et al. reported the synthesis of BCN layers via microwave-assisted PECVD using methylamine borane. This easy method allows reproducible and controlled growth of Bâ€“Câ€“N layers onto thin Cu foils. The obtained results showed ternary Bâ€“Câ€“N phases in the form of nanolayers as potential candidates for optoelectronics, nanoelectronics and catalysis.99

The GCA-CVD method is directly used to synthesize stacked graphene on exfoliated h-BN. To enhance the graphene growth rate (âˆ¼1 Î¼m minâˆ’1), silane is used which can offer large graphene domains on h-BN.145 This method here ties the gap in graphene growth on a metal surface and dielectric surface. Germane and silane can be utilized for this catalytic growth. Faster growth with no grain boundaries has been achieved. Zhou et al. reported the growth of ultrawide bandgap Îµ-Ga2O3/h-BCN heterojunctions on sapphire substrates by metalorganic CVD (MOCVD). h-BCN was a 2D layered structure and Îµ-Ga2O3 was of polycrystalline nature. 5.2. Physical vapor deposition (PVD)

Various PVD methods such as sputtering, ion beam assisted deposition, and pulse layer deposition have been used for the synthesis of BCN materials. In RF sputtering of BCN films, the main parameters are gas flow rate (N2), deposition temperature, sputtering power, annealing temperature and bias voltage.151 The substrate temperature and bias voltage variation provides control over bond contents in BCN films. The increase of N2 flow decreases elastic modulus, wear resistance and hardness in BCN films. The variation of annealing and deposition temperature affects the bandgap and refractive index. Zagho et al. published a detailed review on BCN films deposited by RF sputtering. This article provides a brief historical overview of BN and B4C, and recent research trends in BCN synthesis. Characterization and properties of RF sputtered BCN thin films have also been covered.151 S. D. Nehate and K. B. Sundaram prepared hydrogen doped BCN thin films and studied electrical properties. The films were prepared by sputtering boron carbide and boron nitride. The influence of temperature and hydrogen gas on the films was studied.152 Nehate et al. have summarized all growth techniques including physical vapor deposition methods for thin films in detail.118

5.3. Pyrolysis

The pyrolysis method thermally transforms B, C, and N precursors into BCN at high temperatures. The overall method is to make a B, C, and N precursor slurry which is heated at high temperature. After washing and drying, BCN is obtained. Moses et al. prepared layered boron carbon nitrides (BCN and BC5N) with varied difference in configuration using the urea route. For the first time, BCN nanosheets with a porous structure and high SSA were explored as capacitive deionization (CDI) electrode materials. High adsorption capacity with good stability was observed for BCN nanosheets.155 Zhang et al. reported preparation of ultra-light BCN using a simple precursor synthesis-based pyrolysis method. Frequency-controllable exceptional microwave absorption properties were observed. 5.4. Molten salt method

The molten salt method (MSM) is an extensively used method to produce 2D nanomaterials. In the MSM, the salt mixture is heated over its melting point to attain a liquid state environment for the reaction to dissolve precursor molecules. After the reaction, it is easy to separate the salt mixture to get pure h-BCNNSs. In this method, the salt acts as a solvent medium and also averts the self-agglomeration of BCN layers.161 The important factors in this method are melting point, solubility and stability. Precursor solubility can influence particle size, reaction rate and surface morphology. In the MSM, mass transport in the molten salt and rapid solvation of precursor molecules enable 2D BCN nanosheet synthesis. The generally used salts for BCN synthesis are ionic compounds such as lithium chloride (LiCl), sodium chloride, (NaCl), and potassium chloride (KCl) with a similar crystal structure. Lei et al. reported the synthesis of few-layered BCNNSs (2â€“6 atomic layers) by using the LiCl/KCl eutectic mixture. BCNNSs exhibited a high surface area and enhanced lithium storage capability.162 Zheng et al. reported the synthesis of modified BCNNSs by a KCl-assisted molten salt method with boric acid, urea and glucose (C6H12O6) as precursors. In comparison to bulk BCN, modified BCNNSs resulted in lower thickness, improved crystallinity and enhanced charge separation. The photoluminescence, photoelectrochemical and electron paramagnetic resonance analysis specifies the promoted separation/transfer of photoinduced charge carriers. BCNNSs can be used for application in organic transformations.163

Luo et al. fabricated BCN ceramic aerogels via a bio-hydrogel template molten salt method for use as heterogeneous photocatalysts (as shown in Fig. 4(d)). They show superior catalytic efficiency due to the synergistic effect of ceramic 3D structure with improved crystallinity and increased surface area. This BCN aerogel opens new opportunities for photocatalysis.87 Zhang et al. synthesized BCNNSs using boron oxide, glucose, melamine, and NaCl/KCl. The mixture was annealed at 1250 Â°C for 5 h (under NH3). The as-obtained BCNNS with atomic layered thickness demonstrates improved visible light H2 evolution activity in comparison to bulk BCN.161 Jiang et al. synthesized porous BCN with a simple KCl-assisted strategy. Porous BCN showed high photocatalytic performance than bulk BCN as a result of the improvement in charge separation and transmission by enhanced crystallinity.164 In summary, the MSM can be effectively used to synthesise high-quality and ultra-thin h-BCNNSs with less cost and sustainable solvent.

7. Summary and future perspectives

This review covers the synthesis, properties and applications of BCN materials. The ternary phase triangle of Bâ€“Câ€“N has been explained in which graphene, BN, B4C and C3N4 possess various unique properties for a wide range of applications. The properties of these materials can be combined and tuned in BCN nanostructures. Various theoretical and experimental studies disclosed superior optical, mechanical, electrical and thermal properties of BCN. The exceptional and tunable properties of BCN make it suitable for energy and environment-related applications. In the reported literature, mostly multiple steps and complex process synthesis routes have been used so far. Although few works have published relatively low temperature and simple synthesis routes, there is a requirement of a simple, cost-effective, single-step synthesis approach with high yield.

The unique optical and electrical properties of the synthesized BCN make it promising for energy related applications. The porous structure and enhanced surface area make this material suitable for water cleaning applications. Also, BCN shows enhanced hydrogen evolution reaction performance due to the presence of more active sites from B and N, tunable properties, chemical stability, defect sites such as sp3-C or Stoneâ€“Wales defects. The achieved superior HER performance and charge storage capacity of BCN can be further utilized to study real time supercapacitor and battery applications. BCN can be fabricated with different morphologies to have more active catalytic sites along edges and on its basal planes. The reported works show the role of defect states in the observed properties. Further detailed study of defect states by ab initio density functional theory (DFT) is required for opto-electronic and supercapacitor applications. The synthesized BCN is relatively less toxic in nature. Therefore, similar to BN nanotubes, BCN nanotubes can be explored for drug delivery applications.

BCN can be cross-linked with other 2D materials to form heterostructures. These heterostructures can be studied for HER and electrical properties. Likewise, formation of BCN nanocomposites needs to be explored. Similar to graphene integration on Si chips, BCN integration on Si chips needs to be explored for commercial applications. Theoretical studies predict the thermal conductivity and magnetic properties of BCN but detailed experimental work is required in this field. Such investigations can provide breakthrough properties and expand the horizon of BCN based nanomaterials. Although BCN shows high electrical conductivity but there is limitation in photoelectrode fabrication. So, a consistent method to construct the BCN catalyst on the substrate is required to maintain its activity over a large number of cycles. There is a need for a detailed study of BCN based van der Waals heterostructures with other 2D materials for scope in future energy storage applications.
