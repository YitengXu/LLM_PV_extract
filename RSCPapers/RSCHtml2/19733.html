Abstract

Nickel cobalt sulfide is widely applied to energy storage and electrocatalysis owing to its high electrical conductivity and multiple oxidation states. This study proposes NiCo2S4 and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4 coreâ€“shell structures as energy storage materials and electrocatalysts for the oxygen evolution reaction. A higher specific capacity of 2.22 mA h cmâˆ’2 (168.18 mA h gâˆ’1) at 10 mA cmâˆ’2 is obtained for the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4 electrode which is applied as the battery-type electrode in the battery supercapacitor hybrid, but a better electrocatalytic activity is achieved for the NiCo2S4 electrode with a smaller overpotential of 1.567 VRHE at 0.12 A cmâˆ’2 and a smaller Tafel slope of 86.1 mV decâˆ’1. The electrochemically active surface area (ECSA) is larger for NiCo2S4 due to the nanoparticle-assembled nanotube wall, whereas NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4 shows higher electrical conductivity owing to the presence of molybdenum. Different performances in energy storage and electrocatalysis for NiCo2S4 and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4 nanomaterials are caused by different potentials applied for driving the electrochemical reactions. This study for the first time proves that the trade-off between ECSA and electrical conductivity is important for designing nanomaterials applied in different electrochemical fields.

1. Introduction

Developing multi-functional materials is attractive for application in numerous electrochemical devices since the requirements for establishing efficient active materials are similar. By coupling the electrode with different active materials and the electrolyte with various components, diverse electrochemical devices with various functions could be assembled, such as sensors,1â€“3 batteries,4â€“6 supercapacitors,7â€“9 solar cells,10â€“12 photocatalytic degradation13,14 and photoelectrochemical cells.15â€“17 The electrode material for electrochemical devices requires excellent properties of transferring electrons and facilitating chemical reactions. Hence, high electrical conductivity, low charge recombination possibility, large active surface area and an efficient charge-transfer path are intensively pursued for fabricating highly effective electrode materials. These requirements can be met by suitable material selection and rational morphology design.

Recently, cobaltites (with the chemical formula MCo2O4; M = Mn, Ni, Zn, Cu, Mg, etc.) have been reported as one of the fascinating materials applied to energy storage devices and electrocatalysis, due to their multiple oxidation states and capabilities of facilitating more electrochemical reactions.18,19 Among all these oxides, spinel nickel cobaltite (NiCo2O4) is one of the most promising materials with electrical conductivity 2 times higher than those for nickel oxide and cobalt oxide.20,21 The existence of binary nickel and cobalt ions is expected to facilitate richer redox reactions than those facilitated by their single counterparts.22 Oxygen can be substituted by sulfur to further enhance the electrical conductivity of nickel cobaltite. Nickel cobalt sulfide has drawn much attention because of its much smaller optical band gap energy and much higher electrical conductivity compared to those of NiCo2O4. Nickel cobalt sulfide with high electrical conductivity and bimetallic nature for possessing abundant oxidation states has been intensively applied to electrochemical devices, such as the battery-type material for energy storage23,24 and the electrocatalyst for accelerating water splitting.25,26 Wang et al. reported nickel cobalt sulfide nanoparticles on graphene for energy storage.23 Zhang et al. deposited a nickel cobalt sulfide nanosheet array on graphene foam for energy storage.24 Jiang et al. synthesized NiCo2S4 double-shelled hollow spheres as the electrocatalyst for the HER.25 Irshad et al. prepared a nickel cobalt sulfide film as the electrocatalyst for the HER.26 To further improve the electrochemical performance of nickel cobalt sulfide, fabricating composites with other semiconductors is one of the effective strategies.27 Combining other semiconductors can not only facilitate higher electrical conductivity and extra redox reactions but also provide opportunities to design well-defined morphologies with an efficient charge-transfer path and large active surface area. Cheng et al. synthesized a NiCo2S4@NiCo2O4 nanocolumn array for energy storage.27 Beka et al. fabricated a NiCo2S4 nanotube/CoxNi(3âˆ’x)S2 nanosheet coreâ€“shell structure for energy storage.28 Recently non-noble metal electrocatalysts such as metal oxides,29,30 metal hydroxides,31,32 metal-nitrogen/carbon,33 metal carbide,34 metal sulfide35 and metalâ€“organic complexes36 have been widely reported as alternatives for Pt-based electrocatalysts, because noble Pt is noble and not stable in the environment containing CO and CH3OH.37 The active sites of electrocatalysts are important for influencing the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) performances.38 Liu et al. prepared quaternary (NiFe)PS3 nanosheets as ultra-effective OER catalysts. The (NiFe)PS3 nanosheets show an OER activity with an overpotential of 223 mV at 10 mA cmâˆ’2. It was found that only unsaturated Fe and Ni atoms located on the (010) plane of (NiFe)PS3 are active for the OER.39 Huang et al. synthesized rapidly cationic defect and anion dual-regulated CoAl layered double hydroxides as OER catalysts. The synergistic effect of Al3+ vacancies and S2âˆ’ regulation promotes the exposure of active Co sites, resulting in an amorphous and porous surface for improving the OER performance.40 Sun et al. loaded MoS2 electrocatalysts onto the Co3O4 nanosheet array to catalyze the HER with a low onset potential of 76 mV, which was attributed to the increased active sites and the electronic interactions between MoS2 and Co3O4.41

On the other hand, as for the morphology design, the one-dimensional structure is able to facilitate efficient charge transfer and enhance the electrochemical performance for active materials.42â€“44 Caverning the middle of nanorods to fabricate nanotubes is more effective to enhance the surface area for facilitating more electrochemical reactions.45â€“47 However, the nanotube structure was rarely fabricated as the active material for electrochemical devices, especially for the electrocatalysts toward water splitting. Except for the difficulty in synthesizing well-defined nanotube structures, the other probable reason may be the risk of active materials peeling off from the substrate when gases are generated from the middle of nanotubes during the water splitting process. Therefore, it is worth designing an efficient nanotube structure with good adhesion on the substrate and to investigate the feasibility of the nanotube structure as the electrode material for energy storage and water splitting applications.

In this study, a nickel cobalt sulfide (NiCo2S4) nanotube array was fabricated on Ni foam using a hydrothermal reaction and coupled with evenly grown nickel molybdenum oxide (NiMoO4) nanosheets on the outer surface of the nanotubes for fabricating a nanotube/nanosheet coreâ€“shell structure, for application in energy storage and electrocatalysis of oxygen evolution. It should be noted that the NiCo2S4 core was converted to nickel cobalt oxysulfide (NiCo2OxSy) after carrying out the hydrothermal synthesis for fabricating the NiMoO4 shell. Due to the well-defined nanotube/nanosheet coreâ€“shell structure as well as the suitable combination of NiCo2OxSy and NiMoO4, the battery-type electrode with the NiCo2OxSy/NiMoO4 shows a very high areal capacitance of 17.75 F cmâˆ’2 (1345 F gâˆ’1) corresponding to a capacity of 2.22 mA h cmâˆ’2 (168.18 mA h gâˆ’1) at 10 mA cmâˆ’2. The battery supercapacitor hybrid (BSH) with the NiCo2OxSy/NiMoO4 positive electrode also achieved a maximum energy density of 5.28 W h kgâˆ’1 at a power density of 329 W kgâˆ’1 and a CF retention of 100% after 2000 charging/discharging cycles. However, a better electrocatalytic activity was attained for the NiCo2S4 electrode, which presented a smaller overpotential of 1.553 V vs. RHE at a current density of 0.12 A cmâˆ’2 and a Tafel slope of 86.1 mV decâˆ’1 for electrocatalysis toward oxygen evolution. The reaction time-dependent morphology and the electrochemically active area were also carefully investigated in this work to fully understand the growth and the design of these promising NiCo2S4 nanotube and NiCo2OxSy/NiMoO4 coreâ€“shell structures.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Morphology and composition characterization

The morphology of the nanomaterial is of the greatest importance for determining the energy storage and electrocatalytic capabilities. To synthesize a well-defined NiCo2S4 nanotube array on Ni foam, the nanomaterials formed during the whole synthesis process need to be carefully examined.

It was reported that during the two-step hydrothermal synthesis, the morphology of the NiCo2S4 array was almost determined by the Niâ€“Co LDH array,48 which is the first product formed in the synthesis process. Hence the morphology of the Niâ€“Co LDH grown on NF should be carefully designed at the first step. The Niâ€“Co LDH was synthesized by performing a hydrothermal process for 2, 3, 4 and 6 hours, and the corresponding SEM images are shown in Fig. S1(a)â€“(d) in the ESI,â€  respectively. For the Niâ€“Co LDH prepared by performing a hydrothermal process for 2 hours, a nanosheet structure was obtained with several nanoparticles uniformly distributed on the surface. When increasing the time of the hydrothermal synthesis to 3 hours, the Niâ€“Co LDH nanosheet structure started to dissolve and the edge of nanosheets became rugged. The dissolution kept proceeding with increasing hydrothermal synthesis time, and the Niâ€“Co LDH re-formed to be soft nanowires with a thin top and a wide bottom when the hydrothermal synthesis time reached 4 hours. A solid nanowire structure with a uniform diameter of around 150 nm was obtained for the Niâ€“Co LDH prepared by performing a hydrothermal process for 6 hours. The well-dispersed nanoparticles are found to have uniformly grown on the surface of the solid nanowires. This nanoparticle-coated nanowire array is expected to be very promising for application in the electrochemical device, since an efficient one-dimensional charge-transfer route can be achieved by the nanowire and larger active surface area can be attained by the nanoparticles on the surface. Therefore, the Niâ€“Co LDH NWA fabricated by performing a hydrothermal process for 6 hours was used as the basic structure for further growing the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF.

In a typical synthesis process, the Niâ€“Co LDH NWA/NF should be further calcined to form the NiCo2O4 NWA/NF, which would be transferred to the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF using another hydrothermal synthesis. However, the NiCo2S4 NWA/NF could also be obtained by the direct vulcanization of Niâ€“Co LDH NWA/NF, as reported in the previous literature.49 Hence, it is interesting to investigate the necessity of the calcination process for transforming the Niâ€“Co LDH NWA/NF to the NiCo2O4 NWA/NF prior to carrying out vulcanization for synthesizing the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF. Fig. S2(a) and (b) in the ESIâ€  respectively show the low-magnification and high-magnification SEM images for the NiCo2S4/NF synthesized by direct vulcanization of the Niâ€“Co LDH NWA/NF. This means that there is no NiCo2O4 NWA formed in the whole synthesis process. To fairly discuss the necessity of the calcination process for synthesizing the NiCo2O4 NWA/NF, the NiCo2S4/NF fabricated by the vulcanization of the NiCo2O4 NWA/NF is shown in Fig. 1(a) for comparison. The NiCo2S4/NF synthesized without the calcination process (Fig. S2(a)â€ ) presents a hexagonal nanotube structure with a very smooth surface. However, the NiCo2S4/NF synthesized with the calcination process for forming NiCo2O4 NWA/NF as the intermediate shows a nanoparticle-assembled wall for the nanotube array. The nanoparticle-assembled wall is inferred to have larger active surface area for facilitating more redox reactions with the electrolyte. Therefore, the calcination process is thus considered to be necessary for fabricating efficient electrochemical materials.

To more clearly analyze the structure of NiCo2S4 and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4, the TEM images of NiCo2S4 and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4 are shown in Fig. 1(d) and (e), respectively. The TEM image of NiCo2S4 clearly shows a nanotube structure with an inner diameter of around 60 nm and an outer diameter of around 150 nm. It is surprisingly found that the well-dispersed nanoparticles on the surface of nanotubes observed from the SEM image (Fig. 1(a)) is not the accurate description for the morphology of NiCo2S4. Instead, the nanoparticles were assembled to construct the wall of nanotubes instead of growing on the surface of nanotubes. On the other hand, the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4 presents large nanosheets grown on the nanotube. A simple growth process is to merely deposit the NiMoO4 nanosheets on the surface of the core. This possible growth process is preferable for the strong adhesion between the core and the shell. The coreâ€“shell structure with excellent connection between the core and the shell is promising as the electrochemical material, which could survive repeated ion intercalation and deintercalation and even in the gas generation process.

3.3. Electrochemical analysis of NiCo2S4 NTA/NF and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF in electrocatalysis

Second, the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF and the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF were applied in electrocatalysis toward the OER. The linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) curves for NiCo2S4 NTA/NF and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrodes were measured to evaluate their electrocatalytic activity toward the OER, as shown in Fig. 6(a). The LSV curves of NiCo2S4/NF and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF were IR-corrected using the Rct value obtained in the EIS measurement carried out in 1 M KOH electrolyte. At potentials larger than 1.55 V vs. RHE, the current density for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode is higher than that for the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrode. Also, as observed in the figure, the overpotentials of 1.553 and 1.567 V vs. RHE were obtained at a current density of 0.12 A cmâˆ’2 (9.09 A gâˆ’1) for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF and the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrodes, respectively. It is found that the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode can present not only a higher current density but also smaller overpotential to achieve 0.12 A cmâˆ’2 (9.09 A gâˆ’1), compared to those for the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrode. The results suggest the better electrocatalytic activity of the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF. Moreover, the Tafel plots for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF and the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrodes were obtained from the LSV curves as shown in Fig. 6(b). The Tafel slopes of 86.1 and 99.7 mV decâˆ’1 were obtained for NiCo2S4 NTA/NF and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrodes, respectively. The smaller Tafel slope for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode again indicates its better electrocatalytic activity toward the OER.

The result for the electrocatalytic application is opposite to that for the energy storage application. That is, the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode shows better electrocatalytic activity toward the OER than the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrode, while a better energy storage capability was obtained for the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrode compared to that for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode. It is interesting to investigate the difference between the requirements for an efficient electrocatalyst and energy storage material. Hence, the electrochemically active surface area (ECSA) was analysed. The ECSA was estimated by using the CV measurements in the potential region without faradaic reactions. The CV curves for NiCo2S4 NTA/NF and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrodes measured at the potential window from 0.625 to 0.825 V vs. RHE using different scan rates are shown in Fig. S6(a) and (b) in the ESI,â€  respectively. There is no redox peak in the curves, suggesting a suitable potential window for estimating the ECSA. The square shape of the CV curves suggests the simple capacitance contributed from the electrical double layer (Cdl). Subsequently the difference between the oxidation current (ia) and the reduction current (ic) at a potential of 0.725 V vs. RHE was plotted as a function of the scan rate. Fig. S6(c) and (d) in the ESIâ€  respectively show the relation of the difference between ia and ic and the scan rate for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF and the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrodes. The Cdl values of 5.70 F cmâˆ’2 (431.82 F gâˆ’1) and 4.92 F cmâˆ’2 (372.73 F gâˆ’1) were obtained for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF and the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrodes, respectively. It is surprising to find that the ECSA for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode is larger than that for the NiCo2S4@NiMoO4/NF electrode. This result is probably the reason for the better electrocatalytic activity of the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode. As observed in the TEM image, the NiMoO4 shell is not simply grown on the NiCo2S4 NTA core. Instead, the nanoparticle-assembled wall of the core was dissolved and a new nanomaterial was formed by using the dissolved chemicals and the molybdenum precursor in the hydrothermal solution. The nanoparticles possess larger surface area compared to that of the nanosheets. The NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode with the nanoparticle-assembled wall could probably present larger active surface area for contact with electrolytes, as found in ECSA analysis. Since the larger active surface area has been proved for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode, it is necessary to discuss the better energy storage ability of the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrode. Other than the active surface area, the electrical conductivity and the intrinsic properties of the nanomaterial are also important factors influencing the electrocatalytic activity and the energy storage ability of the electrodes. It was reported that the addition of molybdenum in the metal compound is primary to enhance the electrical conductivity other than enhancing the redox reactions for energy storage. The NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrode could possess better electrical conductivity than the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode. Hence, the trade-off between the ECSA and the electrical conductivity may be the primary cause for the different results on the electrocatalytic activity and the energy storage ability of the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrodes. Due to the larger potential applied for examining the electrocatalytic activity, the larger driving force may induce the reaction of more ions on the active area for facilitating the electrocatalytic reactions. Hence the extra portion of the active surface area for NiCo2S4 NTA/NF compared to that for the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrode could be efficiently used for electrocatalysis. However, the smaller potential applied to carry out the faradaic reactions for energy storage may induce the migration of less ions to the electrode. Hence the active surface area could not be fully utilized and the advantage of the active surface area for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode may play a less important role in energy storage than in electrocatalysis. Due to the less important role of the active surface area, the enhancement of the electrical conductivity for the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrode could thus become more dominant in electrochemical applications. This is probably the reason for the better energy storage ability of the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrode. Also, there are more reports on the application of molybdenum for energy storage than for electrocatalysis. Hence the presence of molybdenum could be more preferable for energy storage materials, which is another probable reason for the better energy storage ability of the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4/NF electrode and the better electrocatalytic activity of the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode.

Moreover, the long-term stability of the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode was also evaluated by measuring the current density at 1.68 V vs. RHE for 10 hours in an electrolyte of 1 M KOH, as shown in Fig. 6(c). The LSV curves for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode measured before and after 10 hours of the chronopotentiometric measurement are shown in Fig. 6(d). A current density retention of 66.8% was attained for the NiCo2S4 NTA/NF electrode after a long-term test for 10 hours, and a decay occurred at potential larger than 1.3 V vs. RHE. The decay in the current density is probably due to the peeling-off of the active nanomaterial from the nickel foam substrate due to the severe generation of gases and especially to the escape of the gases from the inner of the nanotubes.

4. Conclusion

A NiCo2S4 NTA and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4 coreâ€“shell hollow structures were successfully synthesized on Ni foam using hydrothermal synthesis coupled with calcination for application in energy storage and electrocatalysis for oxygen evolution. The hydrothermal synthesis for growing the NiMoO4 shell leads to the dissolution of the NiCo2S4 core. Therefore, the composition of core was changed to NiCo2OxSy and the original nanoparticle-assembled wall of the NiCo2S4 NTA turned into a nanosheet-assembled tube. The battery-type electrode with the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4 coreâ€“shell hollow structure showed a higher areal capacitance of 17.75 F cmâˆ’2 (1345 F gâˆ’1) corresponding to a capacity of 2.22 mA h cmâˆ’2 (168.18 mA h gâˆ’1) at 10 mA cmâˆ’2 compared to that for the NiCo2S4 NTA electrode measured under the same conditions. The resulting battery supercapacitor hybrid achieved a maximum energy density of 5.28 W h kgâˆ’1 at a power density of 329 W kgâˆ’1 and a CF retention of 100% after 2000 charging/discharging cycles. On the other hand, the NiCo2S4 NTA electrode showed a smaller overpotential of 1.553 V vs. RHE at a current density of 0.12 A cmâˆ’2 (9.09 A gâˆ’1) and a smaller Tafel slope of 86.1 mV decâˆ’1 for the electrocatalytic process toward the oxygen evolution reaction, compared to those for the NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4 electrode. The results present different electrochemical activities toward energy storage and electrocatalysis for the NiCo2S4 and NiCo2OxSy@NiMoO4 electrodes. It is inferred that the electrochemically active surface area and the electrical conductivity are important factors influencing the electrochemical performances, and the electrochemical applications at different potentials are found to prefer different designs of the nanomaterials.
