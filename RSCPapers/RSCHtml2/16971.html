Theoretical insight into the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure: a promising visible-light absorber

Abstract

Metal halide perovskites have drawn considerable attention thanks to their excellent optoelectronic performances. However, further development has been severely hampered by their poor stability. Recently, it has been confirmed experimentally that the optical performance of perovskites is significantly improved by the incorporation of a CdS layer. Based on this, first-principles calculations are conducted to probe the inherent mechanism, including the electronic structure, absorption spectra, strain effect, etc. The optimized geometric structure shows that the distance between the CdS layer and the FAPbI3 layer is smaller than 3.0 , which implies that the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure is more stable than common van der Waals heterostructures. Moreover, it is revealed that the electronic states at the band gap edge are from different component layers. It is further unveiled by Bader charge analysis that the electronic charge is transferred from the FAPbI3 layer to the CdS layer, which is consistent with the charge density difference (CDD). In addition, the absorption coefficient in the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure is remarkably enhanced, compared with that of pure perovskite FAPbI3. Furthermore, the strain effect on the bandgap of the CdS/FAPbI3is also explored. These results shed new light on understanding and designing new kinds of highly efficient perovskite-based photovoltaic materials.

1 Introduction

Perovskites, as a category of emerging optical absorbers, have stood out from the numerous photovoltaic materials, thanks to facile technologies, highly cost-effective production and rapidly improved power conversion efficiency (PCE). In 1978,1 a perovskite was experimentally synthesized and characterized for the first time, but it was not until 20092 that perovskites really attracted considerable attention from all over the world. At that time, Miyasaka and co-workers originally used an organic metal halide perovskite as a light sensitized layer instead of dye molecular particles in dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), and the PCE of the resulting device was only 3.8%.2 Since then, the PCE of perovskite-based solar cells has been continually updated, and it is worth noting that recently the PCE has exceeded 25%.3 Moreover, it has been unveiled that perovskites have some unique characteristics including a long carrier diffusion length,4 a small exciton binding energy and effective mass,5 a wide optical response and a high optical absorption coefficient,6 and so on. Furthermore, it has been experimentally demonstrated that perovskites can be also exploited as either a hole transport layer or electronic transport layer in addition to an optical absorption layer.7,8 Based on the aforementioned properties, perovskites stand out from the numerous photovoltaic candidates. In addition, it has also been reported that perovskites have extensive applications including for light emitting diodes (LEDs),9 photodetectors,10 lasers,11 lenses,12 memory devices13,14 and so forth. Therefore, it is of interest to explore the intrinsic characteristics of perovskites because of their potential optoelectronic applications.

Perovskites have the formula ABX3, where A is FA (NH2CHNH2+), MA (CH3NH3+), or Cs+;B is Pb2 +, Sn2+, or Ge2+; X is a halide ion of I, Br or Cl. There are two features of perovskites: one is that the B-site atoms are surrounded by six X-site atoms, forming corner-sharing [BX6]4 octahedrons; the other is that the A-site atoms are in the geometric center of eight [BX6]4 octahedrons, which means that an A-site atom is surrounded by twelve X-site atoms. As for FAPbI3 in the present work, there are trigonal (space group, P3m1, perovskite) and hexagonal (space group, P63mc, non-perovskite) phases, determined by the synthesis conditions.18,19 The generation of FAPbI3 in the hexagonal phase is usually suppressed, because it is not favorable for transferring electrons and harvesting visible light. In addition, it has been extensively reported that the electronic orbits at the band gap edges are occupied by the atoms in the B and X sites.2022 Specifically, the valence band maximum (VBM) is composed of a X-p electronic state, while the conduction band minimum (CBM) consists of a B-p electronic state. In other words, the main occupied electronic states at the band gap edge are directly related to the electronic and optical properties of the perovskite. There are many studies which demonstrate that the main occupied electronic states at the edge of the band gap can be efficiently tuned by constructing heterostructures.2325 The heterostructures usually consist of a two-dimensional (2D) material layer and the perovskite layer.2327 For instance, graphene/perovskite,23,24 black phosphorus/perovskite25 and transition metal sulfide (TMS)/perovskite have been confirmed to have significantly enhanced photovoltaic performances compared with pure perovskites. Hence it is beneficial to incorporate 2D materials to improve the optical capability of perovskites. Among the numerous 2D materials, monolayer cadmium chalcogenides, CdX (X = S, Se, Te) have been achieved by means of colloidal templates28,29 and solvent evaporation.30 Furthermore, it has been revealed in experiments that the incorporation of a CdS monolayer can remarkably enhance the optical response of the perovskite.26,27,31,32 In particular, the CdS/perovskite heterostructure is confirmed to have a high photocurrent and responsivity compared to the pure perovskite,26,32 which implies that it is a promising candidate for photoelectric applications.

Based on the aforementioned analysis, there are currently the following issues. Firstly, is the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure stable? Secondly, how are the electronic charges transferred? Thirdly, what is the strain effect on the band gap? Therefore, in this paper the electronic and optical properties of the perovskite-based heterostructure CdS/FAPbI3 are systematically studied to address the issues via first-principles calculations based on density functional theory (DFT).

2 Computational methods

First-principles calculations based on DFT have been carried out with the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP).33,34 The electronion interaction is dealt with using the projector augmented wave (PAW),35 and the exchangecorrelation potential is in the form of GGA-PBE (PBE: PerdewBurkeErnzerhof) under the generalized gradient approximation (GGA).36 DFT-D3,37 proposed by Grimme, is adopted to characterize the dispersion correction. For the present work, the valence electrons for the component elements are configured as Cd (4d105s2), S (3s23p4), Pb (5d106s26p2), I (5s25p5), C (2s22p2), N (2s22p3), H (1s1). As for Brillouin zone sampling, a -centered 5 5 1 -point mesh is chosen for the CdS layer, and a -centered 4 4 1 -point mesh is used for the FAPbI3 layer and the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure. A cutoff energy of 500 eV is set for the plane wave basis, and the convergence criteria for the total energy change and atomic force are thresholds of 1 105 eV and 0.02 eV 1, respectively, both of which are sufficiently accurate based on the preliminary test. To ensure good convergence, the conjugate gradient algorithm is adopted for lattice optimization, while the tetrahedron method with Blchl corrections is used for the total energy calculations.

In addition, the spinorbit coupling (SOC) interaction is also taken into account here because it has serious influence on the heavy metal Pb, and the Pb-6p electronic orbital in particular.38 As is well known, the calculations of GGA-PBE based on DFT usually underestimate the band gap, and hence it is necessary to adopt the time-consumed hybrid functional of HSE0639,40 (HSE: HerdScuseriaErnzerhof), to obtain the exact electronic band structures. The exchangecorrelation energy in HSE06 is described as,

ECHSEX = ESRX() + (1 )EPBE,SRX() + EPBE,LRX() + EPBEC,(2)
with the parameters of the mixing factor and screening factor . The electronelectron interactions in the exchange term consist of two parts, a short-range part (SR) and a long-range part (LR). In particular, the LR and the correlation term are in the form of the PBE density functional, while the SR is involved in Fock exchange and the corresponding PBE counterpart.

With respect to the investigated CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure, both the lattice relaxation and self-consistent calculations are at the GGA-PBE level with a -centered -point of a 4 4 1 grid. By contrast, the optical properties and strain effect are treated at the level of GGA-PBE with a denser -centered -point of a 6 6 1 grid, while the electronic density of states (DOS) is at the HSE + SOC level with a looser -point of a 3 3 1 grid, limited by the computational resources and time limitations.

The Ei (i = CdS/FAPbI3, FAPbI3 (001), or CdS) indicates the total energy of the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure, the FAPbI3 (001) layer, or the CdS layer, respectively. In other words, it is favorable to synthesize the aforementioned heterostructures, because the reactions involved are all exothermic. The case with a rotation angle of 60 is focused on because it has the lowest binding energy among these. When comparing with the other vdW heterostructures,4144 the binding energy per unit surface (eV 2) is adopted to ensure the unification of standards. By comparison, it is more favorable to synthesize the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure due to the lower binding energy. In addition, the relaxed layer-distances for the cases with rotation angles of 0, 60 and 120 are 2.656 , 2.182 and 2.087 , respectively. It is hence substantiated that the interaction between CdS and FAPbI3 is significantly stronger compared with those in the common van der Waals heterostructures, which implies that the investigated CdS/FAPbI3 possesses good stability.

3.2 Electronic properties

In this section the electronic properties of the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure are discussed in detail, including the electronic DOS, band structure, charge density distribution in the band gap edge, and Bader charge analysis.

Moreover, the DOS in Fig. 2(b) shows that the VBM is mainly occupied by S-3p, Cd-4d and I-5p electronic states, while the CBM mostly consists of Pb-6p and I-5p electronic states. 3.4 Strain effect

In experiments it is almost inevitable for compressive or tensile strain to arise due to lattice mismatch, epitaxial growth or bending. It has been widely confirmed that the strain effect has a remarkable influence on the electronic structures and properties of materials. Hence it is interesting to study the strain effect systematically in detail, specifically including the binding energy, the DOS, the band gap and the alignment of the band gap edge. Fig. 6 shows that the band gap and the binding energy significantly vary with the interfacial uniaxial strain ranging from 6% to 6%. As for the band gap, it monotonically increases with strain on the x-direction ranging from 6% to 4%, while for the y-direction the range is from 6% to 2%. And the band gap monotonically decreases with compression strain from 4% to 6%. As for the binding energy, it is endothermic with respect to the compressive strain, while it is exothermic for tensile strain. And the binding energy always increases whether is compressive or tensile, although the compression effect is stronger than the tensile one. In other words, it is obvious that the equilibrium structure without strain corresponds to the lowest binding energy, which implies that the structure without strain is more stable than the others with strain. Additionally, the energy level alignments of the band gap edge under uniaxial strain are shown in Fig. 7. By contrast, the CBM and the VBM under x-direction strain almost keep increasing monotonically. Meanwhile, it is clearly unveiled that the y-direction strain effect on the VBM is remarkably stronger than that on the CBM, which is in line with the results shown in Fig. 6.

The biaxial stain is next discussed. In Fig. 8, it is shown that the electronic density of states (DOS) for the investigated system apparently varies with different strains, which implies that strain can effectively tune the electronic properties of the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure. Limited by computational resources and time limitations, the strain effect on the DOS is simulated in the range 4% to 4%. Specifically, the band gaps for a compressive strain of 1% to 4% are 1.04 eV, 1.27 eV, 1.13 eV and 1.40 eV, respectively. By contrast, for a tensile strain of 1% to 4%, the band gaps are 0.7 eV, 1.15 eV, 0.93 eV and 1.07 eV, respectively. It is hence demonstrated that the band gap is efficiently tuned by double-axial strain. In particular, a double-axial strain of 2% and 4% is more favorable to achieve superior optical absorption in the visible-light range of the spectrum. As for the tensile strain, it is obviously not suitable for photovoltaic application due to the narrow band gap. It is hence unveiled that the appropriate strain can be used to improve the photovoltaic performance.

4 Conclusions

In the present work, the electronic and optical properties of the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure have been systematically studied by means of first-principles calculations. The main results are listed as follows.

There are three kinds of CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure with different rotation angles of 0, 60 and 120 to be considered, and that with a rotation angle of 60 is the most stable. The layer distances between CdS and FAPbI3 (001) in the optimized structures are all less than 3.0 , which means that the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure is more stable relative to common van der Waals heterostructures. The DOS demonstrates that the VBM and the CBM are occupied by electronic states from different component layers, which means a low electronhole recombination ratio. Moreover, both Bader charge analysis and CDD show that there is significant electronic charge transfer from FAPbI3 to CdS. Meanwhile, the absorption coefficient in the visible-light range is remarkably enhanced in the CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure compared to pure FAPbI3, which is attributed to the incorporation of CdS. Additionally, it is further unveiled that the band gap of the investigated CdS/FAPbI3 heterostructure can be effectively tuned by uniaxial or biaxial stain, although the binding energy without strain is the smallest. These results are helpful to deeply understand the intrinsic characteristics of the monolayer chalcogenide/perovskite heterostructure, which is useful to achieve highly efficient photovoltaic devices.
