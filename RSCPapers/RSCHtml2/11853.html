Abstract

Solvents define pivotal properties for chemical processing and chemical reactions, and can be as game-changing as catalysts. A solvent can be the key to a good chemical process, rather than being just an asset and a reaction space. It determines the solubility, meaning the concentration at which reactants can be processed, determines the stability of excited states, and thus guides the potential-energy curves of activation. As a net outcome, solvents rule over productivity and economic/environmental benefit. Expanding the capabilities of conventional solvents, a new class of so-called master solvents has been proposed and investigated in the last two decades, which have also been termed â€œgreenâ€ or â€œdesignerâ€ solvents. They comprise first of all ionic liquids and supercritical carbon dioxide, yet also deep eutectic, thermomorphic and fluorous solvents. There is a vast literature on and experience with these solvents and their applications. However, their sustainability has been reported mostly by considering only one class of master solvent and hardly benchmarking several classes. Thus, a comparative, complete, and holistic view of their sustainability is missing. This perspective review aims to fill this gap and focuses on a holistic overview of sustainability assessments of master solvents. It encompasses green chemistry principles, life cycle assessments, and modern circularity evaluations based on the â€œ10R frameworkâ€; standing for 10 principles starting with â€œRâ€ such as recycling, reuse, repair, rethink, remanufacture, and so on. This review will show what â€œgreenâ€ actually denotes and means for each class of master solvent when it comes to a â€˜weightingâ€™ by a certified sustainability assessment.

1. Introduction

Solvents define pivotal properties for chemical processing and chemical reactions. They can be as game changing as catalysts. They are a constitution part of the 12 Green Chemistry principles,1 which have widely changed the practices of organic chemists in the last 30 years, and solvents have gained similar importance for chemical process engineers making their designs under the auspices of circularity with its â€œ10R frameworkâ€;2 standing for 10 principles starting with â€œRâ€ such as recycling, reuse, repair, rethink, remanufacture, and so on. Today, solvent recycling is the most prominent outcome here. Solvents can do much more than to be simple carriers for reactants and reaction spaces for their interaction. They determine solubility, meaning the concentration at which reactants can be processed, finally determining productivity and economic benefit. They can also support the stability of excited states, and thus guide the potential-energy curves of activation.3 They can govern the stability and activity of the catalysts, including labile catalyst classes such as biocatalysts/enzymes or sophisticated transition metal catalysts.4 Solvents usually constitute 70â€“80% of the process medium and thus are crucial in reducing waste. Solvents can be reactants in the sense of reactive solvents. A sustainable and green solvent can solve issues related to the environment, cost, and contribute to the optimisation of the overall process.5 A solvent can be the key to a good chemical process, rather than just being an asset.

Against this background, specialty solvents were introduced in the last two decades, which have been termed â€œmasterâ€ or â€œdesignerâ€ or just â€œgreenâ€; for reasons of simplicity, we will use the latter term, which is also widely used in the literature. A vast number of reviews provide an overview of the features of green solvents and their applications.6â€“9 The green solvents considered in this paper comprise ionic liquids and supercritical carbon dioxide, as well as deep eutectic, thermomorphic and fluorous solvents. Some of them are widely used, even on a production level, such as ionic liquids; some have more specialty uses such as fluorous solvents. We consider all of them in this review paper, because we found that seeming curiosity solutions such as microfluidic devices can become essential enablers for industrial transformation as a key disruptive technology after 1â€“2 decades of serious, concerted development10,11â€”in a new shape and in â€œnew clothesâ€ as â€œcontinuous-flow manufacturingâ€. Early-stage sustainability assessments can be key to translating curiosity science into attractive industrially relevant applications. This is the first motif of this perspective review.

The second motif is to demystify the overarching assignment of â€œgreenâ€ to the advanced solvents considered here and the promise behind coining them â€œmasterâ€ or â€œdesignerâ€. We are aware that our perspective paper cannot solve such complex questions or the notable consequences of doing so; thus, we are critical in our approach and cautious in our conclusions. Yet, within such limits, we feel there is value in (1) compiling and summarising sustainability assessments made for â€œgreen solventsâ€ and prudently generalising them, where justified and sensible, and (2) aiming for a direct benchmarking of those solvents in one model chemical process, an exercise which, to the best of our knowledge, is presented here for the first time. Again, benchmarking cannot be taken as a final answer; but this paper hopes to give the first answer. Those who specialise in sustainability assessments, and in particular in life cycle assessments, would agree that all conclusions that are made deal with a complexity of information and depend on the setting of system boundaries; which embrace the life cycle inventory, the methodology, and finally the question to be answered. This is to say, complexity is intrinsic to matters of sustainability, and assessments do not (always) provide simple, straightforward answers. Yet, we do our best here to give plain, understandable messages to our wide range of audience members, for example, chemical engineers and chemists who are interested in the choice of solvents, recognising their pivotal role in the control of chemical reactions.

To sum up and in simple words, the focus of this review is (only) the sustainability of green solvents, and not about green solvents themselves or their uncontradicted usefulness including some sustainability consideration; for which numerous reviews exist. Beyond just reviewing, an added original technical study translates the review into a perspective in its second part.

2. Classes of green solvent

2.1. Ionic liquids

Ionic liquids (ILs) are defined as a large category of salts that, due to differences in their cation and anion sizes, are normally liquid at a temperature less than 100 Â°C.12,13 Thermal and chemical stability along with negligible vapor pressure and low flammability coming from strong electrostatic forces between ions has turned ILs into â€œgreen solventsâ€ based on the twelve principles of green chemistry (12th principle).14 Imidazolium, phosphonium, ammonium, and pyrrolidinium cations, with halides, bis(trifluoromethane sulfonyl) amide, tetrafluoroborate anions are the most common choices in recent studies.15â€“18 Since the physicochemical properties of ILs can be simply altered by changing the type of cation and anion, ILs are described as designer solvents. Accordingly, the application of ILs has expanded in various fields, such as solvent extraction,19,20 CO2 capture,21 battery technology,22 solar cells,23 catalysts,24 and medicine.25

ILs are highly tunable so, using an appropriate design of new structures with improved processes by adding electrolytes or applying efficient reactors can be very desirable.26,27 ILs are probably the class of master solvent being mostly employed on an industrial scale and have attracted attention at commercial scales. Examples are BASF in acid scavenging technology,28 the IoLiTec/Wanders purification process,29,30 Petronas,31 G24 power in dye-sensitised solar cell processes,32 Pionics and Nohms in a lithium-ion battery, and the Degussa,33 Linde and Clariant companies.34 The largest commercial-scale use of ILs was reported in April 2021 by Chevron and Honeywell using an industrial ISOALKYâ„¢ Ionic Liquids Alkylation unit.34

2.2. Deep eutectic solvents

Deep eutectic solvents (DESs) were first introduced by Abbott et al.35 as a mixture of two or more chemicals, which had melting points much lower than their parent compounds. DESs share similar properties to ILs, such as high tunability for operation and high versatility in usage.36,37 The â€œgreenestâ€ of DESs have been further boosted with the recent discovery of natural deep eutectic solvents (NADES).38 NADES are made from plant-based primary metabolites, i.e. organic acids, sugars, alcohols, etc.39 Eutectic solvents have become greener, far more biocompatible40,41 and biodegradable42 than some unconventional solvent counterparts. Therefore, DESs have been widely applied in metal- and biocatalysed processes, in the synthesis of active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) as well as other biologically active compounds.43

2.3. Supercritical carbon dioxide

Supercritical fluids (SCFs) are fluids operated at conditions slightly above their critical temperature (Tc) and pressure (Pc), and have been receiving increasing attention. They have not only the gaseous property of being able to penetrate anything, but also the liquid property of being able to dissolve materials.44 Their thermophysical properties can be adjusted by simply changing the operating conditions in terms of pressure and/or temperature. With simple expansion, they can separate a product, while a purification process is not required in the recovery and reuse of the gas.45

Due to the aforementioned remarkable properties, many applications of SCFs have been investigated as sustainable alternatives to reduce the consumption of numerous conventional organic solvents. The applications of SCFs include chemical extraction,46 purification,47 synthetic chemical reactions,48 polymer processing,49 and biocatalysis.50 Besides, supercritical fluids are also used in drying51 and cleaning,52 and in the energy domain.53 Recently, combining the advantages of size reduction provided by microsystems with the unique properties of SCFs to form supercritical microfluidics, which can address the limitations of both macroscale SCFs reactors and conventional liquid microfluidic reactors, has attracted increasing interest.54

2.4. Thermomorphic solvents

Thermomorphic solvents are switchable multicomponent solvent mixtures that change their phase state upon temperature change to provide beneficial phase conditions in different process steps.55,56 One of the most common applications of these thermomorphic solvent systems (TMSs) is homogeneously catalysed reactions, performed under single-phasic conditions supporting fast reaction kinetics by avoiding mass transfer limitations. Subsequently, upon cooling, the mixture is split into two liquid phases, allowing the separation of the catalytic species from the products.57â€“59 In particular, the recovery of the catalyst is usually the key challenge in such processes with regard to economics and sustainability.56,60 TMSs can be established by combining suitable conventional organic solvents, but the concept can also be realised by using deep eutectic solvents,61â€“63 ionic liquids64â€“67 or fluorous solvents.68,69 Typically, commercially available homogeneous catalysts and ligands can be used without the need for tailored modification.70 Apart from TMSs, thermomorphism is considered for energy-efficient CO2 capture processes71,72 which are not further discussed here.

The most intensively studied reaction in a TMS is hydroformylation, especially for long-chain olefins in the lab73â€“76 and at mini-plant scale.77â€“80 Besides that, telomerisation,81 metathesis82 and many other reactions were investigated.59 Recently, challenging hydroaminomethylation, in which water is formed as a co-product, was successfully carried out in a continuous process.83,84 Besides transition metal catalysis, the use of enzymes in a TMS has also been reported, which is also advantageous in terms of sustainability.66,70,85,86

2.5. Fluorous solvents

Fluorous solvents (FSs) were first introduced in the 1950s by Horvath87 as highly fluorinated (or perfluorinated) solvents. They can be synthesised through the attachment of a fluorocarbon chain to an organic solvent, resulting in the formation of a biphasic system. Since the intermolecular bonding in a fluorous solvent is relatively weak, it usually has a lower boiling point than the corresponding n-alkane and can be used as an extremely low-polarity solvent. As a result, an FS is hardly dissolvable in water while it tends to absorb gases (i.e. CO2, O2, H2, etc.) much better than water or other hydrophobic organic solvents. The most important property of an FS is its temperature-dependent miscibility with organic solvents, which allow self-manifestation in very different forms. Thus, in reaction engineering, an FS is often employed to switch a reaction that is heterogeneous into a homogeneous one to improve the mass and heat transfer efficiency.

Fig. 1 shows the application of an FS to create a biphasic system in a catalytic reaction.88 In such a biphasic mixture, the soluble reagent or catalyst is in the fluorous phase while the starting materials are dissolved in the immiscible solvent phase, which could be an organic solvent, water, or non-organic solvent.89 These two distinct layers become mono-phasic upon heating, allowing the reactants to come into contact, which enables the reaction to take place. The two phases can be separated again through cooling in which the products tend to stay in the organic phase while the remaining reactants and catalysts dissolve in the perfluorous phase. This special phasic behavior allows easy separation of the reaction products and catalyst recycling without using additional organic solvents for extraction.

Nowadays, FSs are popularly produced for commercial purposes, such as refrigerants (R-134, R-227ea); greases and lubricants to be operated at high temperature (>300 Â°C); and reagents for chemical synthesis and catalysis. The major types of commercial FS include perfluoroalkanes, perfluorodialkyl ethers and polyethers, perfluorotrialkyl amines, and hydrofluoroethers. Perfluorohexane (also known as FC-72), perfluoromethylcyclohexane (PFMC), and perfluorodecalin are frequently used perfluoroalkanes.

2.6. The ideal green solvent

An ideal fully sustainable green solvent would not have an ecological impact at any stage, and it would ease process conditions and at the same time make them milder and more sustainable (Fig. 2). The production of a green solvent would be energetically and economically efficient and would come from renewable feedstock, which would not generate emissions, a carbon footprint or waste. In a further stage, the application of this solvent would be very versatile, contributing positively to process performance, and reducing solvent loads by subsequent recycling processes due to high stability. The ecological payback would be low not only in terms of the environment but also in terms of human health and safety. The solvent recovery would be simple and efficient, being suitable for easy reuse. At the end of its lifetime, it would be fully biodegradable without a trace.

3. Energy Efficiency

In each of the following sections, we focus on one sustainability category, which either refers directly to an impact category in life cycle assessment (LCA) or a 10R-framework quantity of the circularity assessment. Not all LCA and circularity criteria are covered, for the simple fact that there are no or only scattered studies in the literature concerning green solvents.

The investigation starts with energy efficiency, and heating and cooling processes are obviously crucial assets in the use of solvents and also crucial for our environment, e.g. their global warming potential. Energy efficiency has different facets: the energy needed to make designer solvents, and their power to reduce the energy needed to make a chemical process happen, in which they function as solvents.

3.1. Ionic liquids

Ionic liquids have been tested in a plethora of applications, and examples are.90â€“93 Thus, the respective potential of reducing the energy efficiency of chemical processes is well documented. This impact has positive collateral effects; the energy efficiency can also reduce the cost of the process and reduce the pollutants causing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions while necessitating the lower use of process media, i.e. water consumption, thus reducing waste.

To illustrate the impact of using IL, higher energy efficiency was demonstrated for extractive distillation using diverse azeotropic boiling mixtures, such as ethanol/water, methylcyclohexane (MCH)/toluene, and ethylbenzene/styrene using ethylene glycol (EG) and [hmim][B(CN)4], N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone and N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP), and [4-mebupy][BF4] and sulfolane.94 For the ethanol/water system, it is reported that the energy use was reduced by 16% in the presence of [emim][N(CN)2] compared to the system with EG. In this case, a purity of 99.9% for ethanol was reached. In another example, a purity of 100% for MCH was achieved with [hmim][B(CN)4], while the total energy consumption dropped by 50% compared to the process with NMP. Yet ILs do not achieve a better result in all cases, as evident in an extractive distillation of ethylbenzene/styrene system using IL, which showed no superiority over using sulfolane as a solvent.95 Recently, the energy consumption needed for the absorption of benzene, toluene, and xylenes (BTX) was compared between using a triethylene glycol (TEG) and [Emim][Tf2N] IL.93 The energy consumption for the BTX absorption process with TEG was 1.08 and 0.74 times bigger than that for the IL-based process which includes both cooling and heating energies. The total annual cost for the process based on [Emim][Tf2N] was 26% lower than for the TEG-based process, showing potential for industrialisation. The methanol/ethanol/water separation process was simulated by using an IL of [Emim][DCA], comparing it again to a performance using the conventional solvent EG.96 In this study, conventional ternary extractive distillation (CTED) and extractive dividing wall column ternary extractive distillation (EDWC-TED) were employed in presence of EG and IL separately and also in a mixture of both solvents. The presence of the IL resulted in significant energy savings. In the EDWC-TED process, the energy consumption and total annual cost (TAC) using the ionic liquid-based mixed entrainer dropped by 4.5% and 7.8%, respectively (see Fig. 3). The mixed entrainer reduced the energy consumption by 2.9% and TAC by about 6.0%.

3.2. Deep eutectic solvents

Regarding the manufacturing of DESs, only mixing and heating of readily accessible chemicals are required for the synthesis of eutectic solvents. Furthermore, in most cases, DESs can be used without the need for a post-purification process. Therefore, DESs are considered to be an energy-efficient, cost-efficient, and environmentally-friendly class of solvents.

With a focus on the processing potential of DESs, Procentese et al.98 studied the pretreatment of waste lettuce leaves using a choline chlorideâ€“glycerol DES system for bio-butanol production. Compared with different pretreatment methods, including acid/base hydrolysis, and a steam explosion process, the use of a DES reduces the energy required for the process by 28%â€“75% with efficient sugar yield and fermentation inhibitors. Haghbakhsh and Raeissi reported the application of DESs in petroleum industries.99 Three solvents, namely reline, glyceline, and the commercial Selexol, were applied for the pressure swing adsorption process. Simulated results show that the overall load of the entire plant was less when using deep eutectic solvents (âˆ’51.27, âˆ’11.13 kW for reline and glyceline, respectively, and 15.17 kW for Selexol). The obtained results prove the potential use of DESs for CO2 capture processes since they can be more energy efficient than traditional solvents.99

In another study, Zhong et al. reported a class of DESs formulated with choline chloride (ChCl), phenol (PhOH), and ethylene glycol (EG) for NH3 capture.100 They investigated the impacts of temperature, pressure, and DES composition on the capacity of NH3. The use of supercritical fluids can improve energy efficiency. Using supercritical n-butanol in recycling carbon fiber reduced polymer waste to 31% compared to the steam thermolysis method.101 In addition, energy analysis of butanol extraction shows that the energy cost of supercritical CO2 extraction is 3 MJ per kg butanol, which is much lower than that of conventional extraction (9 MJ per kg butanol).102 Tompsett et al. studied semi-batch extraction of several representative higher alcohols, including n-butanol, n-pentanol, and n-hexanol from dilute aqueous solutions using supercritical CO2.103 The results show that the energy efficiency for SFE recovery of butanol from aqueous solution is between 0.26 MJ kgâˆ’1 and 4 MJ kgâˆ’1 compared to 8 MJ kgâˆ’1 to 14 MJ kgâˆ’1 for conventional methodologies.103 For pentanol and hexanol, the benefits of supercritical CO2 extraction are even more apparent than for butanol, with energy efficiencies for supercritical CO2 extraction of n-pentanol and n-hexanol of 3.1 and 2.4 MJ kgâˆ’1, respectively.103 In addition, the energy consumed in preparing one mole of a chemical compound is compared for a variety of technologies.104 As a result, a supercritical reaction has been reported that still requires energy considerably higher than the microwave reaction but is twice as energy-efficient as the traditional thermal method.104

Homogeneous catalysis is known for its energy-saving, mild reaction conditions, i.e., reaction temperatures often below 150 Â°C and pressures below 40 bar, which also apply to catalytic reactions in TMS.56,105 As an example, Brunsch et al. performed the hydroformylation of dodecene at 100 Â°C and varied the phase splitting temperature in a subsequent decantation step from +25 to âˆ’9 Â°C. 3.5. Fluorous solvents

Since FSs have a relatively low boiling point, they are preferred for reactions with temperature sensitivity, such as enzymatic reactions, pharmaceutical synthesis, and other bio-applications. In other words, less energy is required for FS-involved reactions while they can also significantly reduce energy consumption for product separation, owing to their switchable property through temperature changes.87,88 The biphasic system, created through the application of an FS, has been proven to be a promising reaction system to reduce product inhibition, avoid substrate hydrolysis, and facilitate product separation as well as solvent recovery. For example, in a catalysis system, high-affinity catalysts, which are soluble in the organic solvent, can react in one phase at a higher temperature and then be separated in a biphasic system at a lower temperature. This improves the energy efficiency of the reactions by eliminating heating and other mixing processes. However, fluorous liquids typically require multistage syntheses, and the increased impact of the extra manufacturing and purification steps needs to be weighed carefully against the advantages of separation and reuse that these alternative media may bring to a process.

4. Global warming potential â€“ greenhouse gases

Climate change and global warming resulting from greenhouse gas emissions are classified as one the most critical environmental problems faced by the global community.110 The industrial revolution combined with the increase in the use of fossil fuels has raised the emission of hazardous chemicals in the air, escalating the global warming problem. To prevent droughts, arctic sea ice melting and the planet's temperature rising, capturing harmful greenhouse gases, such as recycling fluorinated gases and CO2, is urgently needed.111

4.1. Ionic liquids

Due to the low stability and high volatility and corrosivity of materials used in conventional CO2 capturing processes, such as amine-based absorption, water and organic liquid scrubbing, and hollow fiber membranes, they potentially infract green chemistry principles.21,111,112 Therefore, ILs have gained specific interest due to their advantages of good dissolution properties, low volatility, high decomposition temperature, energy, and cost-efficient separation of CO2 from post-combustion flue gas. A large number of papers studied imidazolium-based ILs for CO2 capture.113,114 Anions are considered to play a primary role in CO2 absorption compared with the cations of ILs. Recently, Lepre et al. have investigated the absorption of 1,1,1,2-tetrafluoroethane, commonly known as R134a which has a global warming effect hundreds to thousands of orders of magnitude higher than that of CO2 using 5 ionic liquids.115 Their research showed that the gas solubility follows the order [C2C1Im][NTf2] < [C8C1Im][NTf2] < [C8C1Im][BETI] < [C8H4F13C1Im][NTf2] < [C8H4F13C1Im][BETI], meaning ionic liquids with fluor moieties especially in anions have better gas solubility which is related to the favorable entropy of solvation.115â€“117

DESs have been studied for their ability to remove harmful gaseous substances by an absorption process. The higher the solubility of gases in the solvent the greater is the reduction in energy penalties. 5. Recyclability

The recycling of solvents is essential for the effective use of resources and environmental conservation. It is a constitutional part of the 10R framework of circularity; and also low ranked there, as it is the major industrial practice of the 10R framework today. Recyclability is one of the most important economic drivers in an industrial process, leading to the reusability of a designer solvent for multiple separations, which can decrease the initial costs of a process.

5.1. Ionic liquids

Depending on the hydrophobicity of an IL, the method of recyclability can differ. Thermal treatment can be one of the first options for the recycling and reuse of ILs, due to their low vapor pressure, as evidenced in the case of hydrophilic ILs.131 Nevertheless, thermal treatment would not be efficient for thermal-sensitive and non-volatile solutes. Adsorption,132 centrifugation,133 and membrane-based technology134 such as nanofiltration, reverse osmosis and electrodialysis are other techniques that can be used for the recycling of ILs.135

In the case of the metal extraction process with hydrophobic ILs, stripping agents such as HCl, H2SO4, NaOH, ammonia, and EDTA can be favorable.136â€“138 For instance, the recycling of Cyphos IL 104 using HCl solution in germanium oxide extraction from Zener diodes after twelve such extractionâ€“stripping cycles showed no noticeable change in extraction performance.139 Nakasu et al. studied the pretreatment of biomass using the low-cost IL of [MEA][OAc] with different acetic acid contents.140 The solvent recovery rates during the evaporation and centrifugation for [MEA][OAc] were 97% over 6 cycles and no extra solvent was needed. Meanwhile, in the presence of a higher acid content, the extraction yield increased, but the IL recovery rate decreased. While in comparison with [MEA][OAc], the recycling of [TEA][HSO4] resulted in a higher recovery rate of 99%, which could be related to the non-volatility of sulfuric acid.

5.2. Deep eutectic solvents

DESs are claimed to be easily separated from downstream products and recycled without significant changes in performance strength.141 This information is supported by studies by Di Gioia et al.142 and Cui et al.143 reported that the DES can be simply reused up to six times by adding water and simple filtration, followed by a water evaporation process. The performance of a DES after several recycles has already been widely discussed in the literature.144â€“146 Shen et al.147 reported a reduction in the recovered yield of DES per cycle, and Morais et al.148 stated that recycled DES efficiency was reduced by 5% after the first two cycles.

5.3. Supercritical carbon dioxide

Solvent recovery by simple depressurisation allows the collection of pure SCF solvent (gas). Thus, it can be recirculated into storage and is ready for reuse, leading to a reduction in total energy costs and an increase in the overall sustainability of SCF-based processes.16 The example of butanol extraction using supercritical CO2 shows that up to 97.8% of the solvent can be recycled.102 In addition, a supercritical solvent recovery of 90% can be achieved, as reported for metal oxide nanoparticle production.150

5.4. Thermomorphic solvents

The applications being considered for TMS are a key reason for calling it sustainable. TMSs are especially suitable for reactions using fats and oils, where common recycling strategies suffer from high boiling products and slow reaction rates.151 These reactants can be produced from renewables and help to reduce dependence on fossil resources.70,105,152

TMSs were invented for the recovery of homogeneous catalysts.57 Using a polyethylene glycol (PEG)/dioxane/heptane TMS and a PEG-modified ligand, Yang et al. achieved very high catalyst recovery rates of 99.4% on average in 23 consecutive recycling batch experiments for the hydroformylation of dodecene.75 Brunsch et al. performed 30 batches in a DMF/decane TMS, recording a loss of activity after 13 runs, which was compensated by longer reaction times. This loss was caused by catalyst leaching into the production phase that was quantified as 1% in the last run. It was concluded that the catalyst recovery can usually be improved by (i) lowering the separation temperature, causing an increased energy demand, (ii) reducing the catalyst concentration, leading to a slower reaction, and (iii) choosing solvents with larger polarity difference, which is limited by the requirement to be single-phasic at the reaction temperature.73 In a cyclic repeated semi-batch reactor, two sets of reaction parameters were tested for 93 and 100 runs (<90 hours each), achieving successfully stable operation. Rhodium leaching was on average 1.4% hâˆ’1 and 0.7% hâˆ’1, respectively.78 Dreimann et al. additionally closed the loop for decane recovery using a classic CSTR-decanter mini-plant and reported stable operation for 120 hours.80 For a more sustainable process, nanofiltration of the product phase after decantation was introduced, reducing rhodium leaching to 0.25% hâˆ’1.79,108 For many other examples, TMSs have been shown to work stably and can be used as a recycling concept as well.59

5.5. Fluorous solvents

Whether the purpose is to meet or exceed sustainability goals, or to improve cost efficiency, fluorinated solvents are also an excellent choice to implement solvent recycling and recovery programs.88 The reason is that fluorous solvents are extremely stable, allowing them to be utilised again and again without loss of quality.153 Additionally, since they usually have very low boiling points, FSs can be easily recovered through an evaporator while leaving very little residue.9

6. Stability

Designer solvents are more labile to chemical, thermal or other degradation, as they are typically more complex chemical molecules than conventional solvents, i.e. they are more prone to any conversion. Degradation relates to the generation of waste, and thus to one of the 12 Green Chemistry Principles.

6.1. Ionic liquids

Thermal stability, chemical stability, and electrochemical stability must be considered, and have been exemplified for ILs.154,155 High thermal stability is critically important for high-temperature applications, such as gas purification, biomass dissolution, distillation, and extraction of the organic compound, while electrochemical stability plays a considerable role in order to reach strong power density and high energy in electrochemical devices, such as batteries and fuel cells.156,157

The decomposition temperature of ionic liquids usually starts from 150 Â°C, which is related to choline-based ionic liquids.158 The anion type is the main parameter in the thermal stability of ILs, and thereafter the type of cation as well. Generally, the thermal stability of anions decreases in the following order: [PF6] > [Beti] > [NTf2] > [BF4] > [I] > [Cl] > [Br].156,158,159 Investigation of the thermal properties of carboxylate ILs by Xu and Wang has shown that introducing hydroxyl (OH), phenyl (C6H5) or ether groups to the anion considerably improves the thermal stability.160 The effect of cations on the thermal decomposition of ILs is discussed in ref. 161. Although increasing the alkyl chain length of imidazolium IL with [BF4], [PF6], [NTf2] or [Cl] anions reduces the thermal stability, this trend is reversed in the case of quaternary ammonium and choline carboxylate Ils, in which the thermal decomposition temperature decreases with increasing alkyl chain length.160 Indeed, the reduction in the thermal decomposition temperature for quaternary ammonium and choline-based ILs with carboxylate anion decreases with increasing alkyl chain length. However, adding alkoxylated side chains in the ILs with pyrrolidinium, piperidinium or imidazolium cations can reduce the thermal stability.158,160,162

Furthermore, in terms of electrochemical stability, both the cation and the anion are controlling agents on the electrochemical window of ILs.22 Generally, a higher operation temperature and higher voltage accelerate the degradation percentage of ILs. Raiguel and coworkers investigated the chemical stability of ILs in alkaline solution in detail.155 It has been reported that the nature of the cation plays a major role in the stability of ILs in alkaline conditions.155 Phosphonium-based ILs display higher thermal stabilities with lower viscosities compared to analogous ammonium-based ILs. While the chemical stability of phosphonium ILs in alkaline conditions is less than ammonium salts. Nevertheless, imidazolium, pyridinium, and sulfonium cations can be attacked by alkaline agents more easily than quaternary ammonium and 1,2,3-triazolium cations, which offer resistance to basic conditions. Hence, caution must be observed when ILs with fluorine-based anions are used because of the possibility of the formation of HF, which is toxic and very corrosive.163

6.2. Fluorous solvents

FSs are exceptionally stable and can be used again and again without losing potency. In addition, their stability precludes the need for stabilizer additives or acid acceptance testing. Another advantage resulting from the stability of these industrial solvents is their broad compatibility with a wide variety of materials. Fluorinated solvents can be used safely with most plastics, elastomers, metals, and substrates. Their cleaning action is highly efficient, evaporating quickly and leaving very little residue.9,88

6.3. Deep eutectic solvents

DESs are reported to be non-volatile and high thermal stability solvents, which can easily dissolve the majority of organic and inorganic compounds. Providing that DESs tend to be decomposed before reaching a critical temperature, the range of operating temperatures is between the degradation temperature (Tdeg) and their melting point (Tm).164,165

For hydrophilic DESs, such as natural deep eutectic solvents (NADES), Craveiro et al. reported that most NADES present a single degradation peak at temperatures above ca. 120 Â°C (Tdeg) while the minimum melting point (Tm) recorded is around âˆ’9.7 Â°C in the case of ChCl:ca (1:1).166 They also reported that water has a significant influence on the complex and the properties of NADES, interfering with the liquidus and solidus lines in the eutectic phase diagram. Results also show that Tm might suffer a depression of 1 Â°C if approximately 4 wt% water was added to the solvents, while the opposite effect and an increase in the melting temperature were recorded at higher water contents.166

In the case of the hydrophobic DESs, the melting points of most reported solvents in this category were observed below 20 Â°C, which allows them to remain in the liquid phase as extraction media at room temperature. Generally, the degradation temperatures of hydrophobic DESs are relatively higher (can be >200 Â°C) in comparison to their hydrophilic counterparts, yet they are highly dependent on the nature of the DES constituents.167

7. Waste generation/avoidance, solvent load

Solvents constitute 70â€“80% of the process medium and thus contribute majorly to process waste. While recycling can help a lot, it cannot fully eliminate this fact, as losses and inefficiency are inevitable. Yet, on the positive side, solvents can help to decrease other sources of waste, e.g. that of side products or unreacted products, by assisting the reaction process.

7.1. Deep eutectic solvents

Micropollutants captured from aqueous streams are an example of the removal of waste as an environmental gain. Besides the ability to capture gases, researchers have studied using DESs for the removal of unwanted substances from the aqueous phase, in the context of wastewater treatment processes. Van Osch et al. reported that metal ions were removed from non-buffered water with hydrophobic DESs with over 99% removal of Co2+ ions.168 Florindo et al.169 investigated tetrabutylammonium chloride-based and menthol-based DESs to remove micropollutants, i.e. pesticides, from diluted water solution.

7.2. Supercritical carbon dioxide

ScCO2-based processes provide a range of benefits as well as offering routes to overcome some limitations of conventional processes.149 One of the benefits of supercritical carbon dioxide is avoiding the use of toxic solvents and waste treatment, which lead to environmental hazard and high cost. Overall, the operating cost and environmental emissions of ScCO2-based processes are much smaller than those of conventional processes.

7.3. Thermomorphic solvents

Studies evaluating waste generation or the E factor170 quantitatively are not available to date for TMS processes. However, as a simpler criterion, atom efficiency, is often high for typical reactions in TMSs, e.g., hydroamination,171 amino-telomerisation,172 or hydroesterification;173 meaning this is an intrinsic measure to reduce waste, given that the yield is high. In one study, a TMS-based biodiesel process was developed that minimises waste generation by a reduction in the number of production steps.174

Waste generation is deeply coupled with selectivity. The more resource material is converted in the right way, the better. Due to the use of homogeneous catalysis, TMS-based processes usually show high selectivity.56 This is usually ensured by the ligand used, but the solvents of a TMS can also be optimized to improve the reaction performance.175â€“177 In hydroformylation, chemoselectivity to the aldehyde must be considered, but also regioselectivity, which distinguishes between linear (n) and branched (iso) aldehydes. Yang et al. achieved remarkably high chemoselectivities of 98%, but these were accompanied by a low n/iso regioselectivity of 52:48. Even increasing the self-prepared TMPGP ligand did not significantly improve the n/iso ratio.75 Using BiPhePhos as a ligand in lab-scale batch experiments, Brunsch et al. reported higher regioselectivities of typically 99:1 with chemoselectivity to the aldehyde of up to 90%,73 which can be classified as high.178 It should be noted that most of the by-product is olefin isomer that can be recycled into the reactor as a reactant. This recycling, consisting mainly of the alkane solvent, iso-, and n-olefins, was put into practice in mini-plant experiments.80 After a model-based reactor optimization using the elementary process function (EPF) concept,179â€“181 two TMS reactor networks were commissioned. A helically-coiled tube reactor with a subsequent CSTR and decanter provided 70% selectivity to the linear aldehyde, while a cyclically operated semi-batch reactor with a CSTR achieved up to 90%.77,78 This example of the hydroformylation of long-chain olefins underlines the potential of TMSs to enable highly selective chemical conversion.

The solvent load is another reason for waste. At first glance, the solvent load of a TMS is high. However, TMSs integrate reaction and separation, and therefore the solvent system includes media for reaction and liquidâ€“liquid extraction. For the hydroformylation of 1-dodecene, the TMS composition was studied in detail in batch experiments.73,74 It turned out that there is a trade-off between maximising the number of reactants and maximising the recovery of the catalyst. A TMS mixture of 16/42/42 wt% (1-dodecene, DMF, decane) was recommended, which was adapted in mini-plant experiments,77,78,108 while for kinetic investigations a composition of 20/32/48 wt% was favourable in terms of analytics.182 Due to this high solvent load, efforts have been made to develop â€œintegratedâ€ TMSs in which reactants take over the role of solvents.183,184

7.4. Fluorous solvents

Since an FS is often used as a co-solvent to substitute the organic solvent in order to create the biphasic system, only a small amount of FS is required at the reaction stage to maintain the advantages of the heterogeneous system. More FS is required at the separation stage to allow sufficient removal of catalyst and enable the purification of the products. Due to their extremely high stability, FSs can be easily recovered and reused in several cycles that prevent the generation of unexpected waste while eliminating the formation of other toxic compounds.

8. Toxicity and biodegradability

Reactants are generally toxic; to humans, the soil, the aqueous environment (rivers, oceans), and the atmosphere. This occurs by releasing emissions and other harmful substances to the ecosphere; as well as the response of the ecosphere against those toxic compounds. Thus, the effects of a solvent can vary if one considers human toxicity potential or aquatic toxicity potential. As aforementioned, the solvent has its own toxicity, but also influences the toxicity of the reaction medium as a whole: for example, by increasing the yield and thus reducing waste products.

8.1. Ionic liquids

Although ILs have been known as green solvents, this classification relies on some simple physical assignments, such as the negligible vapor pressure and low flammability of ILs. Yet, since this does not address holistic sustainability, it does not mean that all ILs are non-toxic and biodegradable.8 Up to now several kinds of research have been developed to investigate the toxicity and degradation criteria for ILs with a focus on EC50, LD50, dissolved organic carbon (DOC), theoretical carbon dioxide (ThCO2), and theoretical oxygen demand (ThOD), and even hepatic effects.30,154,185â€“188 Ecotoxicity inspection can be categorised into several biomodels: animals and mammalian cell lines (rat), algae, crustaceans, fungi, and bacteria, in which the toxicity results are relevant to the kind of biomodel.189,190 Based on the EC50 (mg Lâˆ’1) for Selenastrum capricornutum, the toxicities of some ILs like [C12MIM][Cl], [Chol][PF6], and [TMSiMmim]Br are much greater than for conventional solvents, for example methanol, acetonitrile or even toluene, while [bmpyr][TfMs], [bmpyr]Cl, [HOPMIM][FCH2COO] and [HOPMIM][glycolate] show less toxicity than the conventional organic solvents of chloroform and toluene.191 The acute toxicity of EC50 (mg Lâˆ’1) for [C3mim][NTf2] to Daphnia magna is favorably compared with the toxicity of conventional solvents, such as dichloromethane, methanol, acetonitrile, aniline, ammonia, chlorine, and phenol.192,193

Generally, the toxicity and biodegradability of ILs refer to their overall chemical structure; meaning the type of cation used, as well as the type of anion causing corresponding cationâ€“anion interactions, and the length of alkyl chains. Strategies to decrease the toxicity of ILs and promote their biodegradability have been developed, using morpholinium and cholinium-based cations, sulfonates, and organic acid-based anions, introducing polar groups such as hydroxyl, or nitrile ether or ester groups.30,194 However, the toxicity of ILs is increased when employing phosphonium cations within long hydrophobic alkyl chains.30

8.2. Deep eutectic solvents

DESs are commonly made from quaternary ammonium salts, which are known to be biodegradable and have relatively low toxicity.195 Many reports claim that DESs are non-toxic, environmentally-friendly, and biodegradable solvents.196,197 This was further confirmed by Hayyan et al.198 who showed no toxic effect of the studied DESs when exposed to bacterial and eukaryotic cells. MacÃ¡rio et al.199 demonstrated that choline chloride and tetramethylammonium chloride-based DESs have good biocompatibility with human skin cells. RadoÅ¡eviÄ‡ et al.200 provide evidence that three tested choline chloride-based DESs are readily biodegradable due to their high level of mineralisation of 62â€“87%. However and as a critical note, Hayyan et al.198 state that some DESs shows higher cytotoxicity levels compared to their individual components. It also has to be admitted that the bandwidth of investigations is not sufficient for the vast number of potential DESs; meaning we know just the tip of the iceberg and there might be a bias to presenting positive results.

8.3. Supercritical carbon dioxide

Many supercritical fluids are considered non-carcinogenic, nontoxic, non-mutagenic, and non-flammable solvents.201 Honestly, supercritical CO2, while being widely used, still exhibits a level of toxicity related to the concentration and time of exposure,202 especially due to being an asphyxiant at high dosage.203 The use of supercritical CO2 to extract antioxidants from rosemary caused human toxicity approximately twice as high as that of a process combining pressurised hot water extraction and powder formation online.204 It should be noted that supercritical solvents other than supercritical CO2 are in use, such as supercritical methanol,205 supercritical benzene,206 and supercritical toluene,207 and complete sustainability assessments are missing.

8.4. Thermomorphic solvents

The toxicity of TMSs depends on the solvents used, as a TMS is a mixture. Gaide et al. developed a â€œnarrow TMSâ€ (the reaction conditions are not yet single-phasic, but the reaction performance does not improve on temperature increase) consisting of water and 1-butanol for the hydroformylation of bio-based methyl 10-undecenoate using sulfoxantphos as a catalyst ligand. The system was experimentally studied in batch recycling experiments and in a continuous process with stable operation.208 This green TMS was developed based on the knowledge of chemical experts, and a systematic green TMS design was introduced by combining in-silico solvent screening using COSMO-RS and estimation of EHS properties using quantitative structureâ€“property relations.209 It was shown that two novel TMS mixtures using dimethyl succinate and tetrahydropyranone can be used for the hydroformylation of long-chain olefins, replacing the developmentally toxic solvent DMF. The approach was refined in terms of thermodynamics and EHS properties, leading to diethyl sulfoxide and other promising polar green TMS solvents.210 Based on these screening results, KeÃŸler et al. formulated a model-based process optimization approach and a computer-aided solvent and process design for the generation of green TMSs and showed that they can compete with a conventional DMF-based TMS, even in terms of total annualized costs.211,212

8.5. Fluorous solvents

Many fluorinated compounds are nontoxic, and there are abundant biomedical applications of fluorinated compounds as blood substitutes, imaging agents for ultrasound and magnetic resonance, and many applications in drug delivery on the horizon.213 Although FSs have been widely used in industry for several decades, there were not many studies investigating their negative impact on health, safety and the environment until the start of the century when their widespread and advertised impacts came to notice.130 Fluorocarbons are not flammable by themselves, and their incineration requires the input of energy and may also lead to dangerous acidic by-products that must be further treated.214 The release of perfluorocarbon formulations to the environment is of concern because of their high global warming potential and long atmospheric lifetimes, and as they do not biodegrade.

Although there is still concern about the safety of FSs, it is obvious that such solvents and their derivatives will continue to be popularly used in both academia and industry.213

12. Conclusions

The solvent is possibly the most underestimated component in the process mixture of a chemical reaction. Its function in the past has mainly been seen to fill the reaction space in order to allow operations of chemical engineering such as mixing, heating, and separation. A solvent has to dissolve reactants, often at a high or maximal concentration, and thus solubility is a major goal. Solvents have to be cheap and typically accessible in large amounts. In view of increasing environmental awareness, the recyclability of solvents has become increasingly important. In the last decade, we have seen a trend towards using â€˜green solventsâ€™, such as acetone, esters (ethyl acetate), alcohols, and water. Solvent maps were made with traffic-light assignments, meaning green (wanted), orange (not the best but no better alternative now), and red (to be banned).

With the aim of designing an â€˜ideal solventâ€™ from first principles according to the above goals, entirely new types of solvents have entered the portfolio of chemists. They have been termed â€œdesigner or master solventsâ€. They offer a very different set of functions in terms of environmental profile, safety, functionality, and process integration. Moreover, they can, for example, promote reactions by making reactants or catalysts more reactive, or making them last longer (catalyst stability). The solvents themselves can even act as reactants as shown by the class of reactive ionic liquids. The use of disruptive process intensification technologies, such as microfluidics, having set a new standard in the quality of unit operations, has increased the demand for the new class of master solvents. Evidently, a vast number of reports are available on microflow technologies using ionic liquids.

Designer solvents were termed â€œgreenâ€ from the moment they were first mentioned, and from then onwards many accolades have been given to their environmental friendliness. This has often been made from a chemist's point of view, focussing on their physico-chemical properties, such as the zero vapour pressure of ionic liquids. In the last few years, the point of view of a process and sustainability chemist has been added, and this has revealed gaps in that kind of assignment, e.g. the absence of mature technical solutions for the recycling of ionic liquids; at least at the time the reports were made.

This is where this review comes in. We have provided a critical review of the sustainability assessment of green solvents, reviewing modern life cycle and circularity assessments published in the literature. We show that some classes, e.g. ionic liquids and carbon dioxide, are quite well documented, yet still leaving notable gaps in relevant sustainability assessments. The class of thermomorphic solvents has been well and comprehensively documented for the purpose of its focused use, centering on homogeneous catalysis. Thermomorphic solvents could be used much beyond this and are simply not as â€˜popularâ€™ as other master solvents. Other green solvent classes need more environmental documentation. We have tried to deepen these impressions from the literature review by a somewhat more quantitative assessment. In Table 6 we quantify the degree of investigations, as taken from this overview, by weighting their number and breadth, classifying them as 0 (none, rare), 1 (medium), and 2 (strong). We admit that our overview is limited to the literature we covered and not made by a real statistical survey of the whole literature; yet this holds for almost all reviews, which still draw conclusions. Therefore, we aim to make careful conclusions in the following.

Table 6 confirms the impression that master solvents have not been investigated throughout concerning sustainability at the highest detail. For several solvents, some of the sustainability investigations in Table 6 have not been done (0 score), leaving a gap. In particular, Table 6 confirms that ILs have been thoroughly investigated, while being widely used. Thermomorphic solvents, despite being restricted to homogeneous catalysis applications, are actually well documented for sustainability; opening the door to wider applications. Supercritical carbon dioxide has often been used in the literature, yet in this study we see that the sustainability documentation does not reach the degree of comprehensiveness which ILs have; yet admittedly providing some holistic sustainability assessments. Fluorous solvents today fill only an application niche, and thus have not experienced advanced sustainability documentation. The same goes for deep eutectic solvents, but they are still a new solvent class.

Seeing Table 6 from the perspective of sustainability parameters, recyclability is particularly well documented, and to a somewhat lower extent energy efficiency, global warming potential, waste generation/avoidance, and toxicity/biodegradability. Stability issues have been largely addressed for ILs, and not or only rarely for the other solvents. A holistic sustainability assessment and cost analysis are only rarely available.

Apart from commenting on the state of sustainability assessment for master solvents, this review presents a direct benchmarking of green solvents, which has, to the best of our knowledge, not been given in the literature. This comparison has been done with an example of a relevant and future-pointing chemical process, converting biomass to chemicals.

13. Outlook

To sum up, this perspective paper takes the process chemist's point of view, and provides a critical assessment of green solvents, showing up their potential in process sustainability and â€˜hotspotsâ€™ to consider improving that further (to render those solvents useful in industry). The aim is to expand the dominant chemist's view beyond reaction in a glass flask toward factory-gate processing with industrial motivation. The next step would be a cradle-to-cradle analysis, leaving the border of the factory gate and covering the whole life cycle.
