Synthesis and characterization of substituted Schiff-base ligands and their d10 metal complexes: structure-induced luminescence tuning behaviors and applications in co-sensitized solar cells†

Abstract

Nine IIB group complexes, [ZnL1Cl2] (Zn1), [CdL1Cl2]2 (Cd1), [HgL1Cl2] (Hg1), [ZnL2Cl2] (Zn2), [CdL2Cl2] (Cd2), [HgL2Cl2] (Hg2), [ZnL3Cl2] (Zn3), [CdL3Cl2] (Cd3) and [HgL3Cl2] (Hg3), have been synthesized from the corresponding ortho-(6-methoxy-pyridyl)(CHNAr) (where Ar = 2,6–iPr2C6H3, L1; 4–MeC6H4, L2; 2–OMeC6H4, L3) Schiff base and structurally characterized by elemental analysis, FT-IR, 1H NMR and X-ray single-crystal analysis. Crystallographic studies reveal that the center metal of the complexes adopts a distorted tetrahedron geometry (except for Cd1 and Cd3, which display square pyramidal geometry) and C–H⋯Cl hydrogen bonds and π⋯π stacking interactions contribute to three-dimensional supramolecular structures. The series of complexes exhibit tunable luminescence from blue, through green, to light yellow by varying the temperature (298 K and 77 K), both in solution and in the solid state. Moreover, the quantum yields range from 0.027 to 0.422, and decrease according to the order of the periodic table (Zn > Cd > Hg). These results indicate that the center atom of the complexes leads to the geometry differences and hence to the tunable luminescence properties. Because Zn1–Zn3 exhibited higher molar extinction coefficients and a distinct absorption region, they were employed as co-sensitizers in ruthenium dye N719-sensitized photoanodes to deliver light-electricity efficiency enhancement, being assembled with counter-electrodes and electrolyte to prepare ZnX/N719 (where ZnX = Zn1, Zn2 and Zn3) co-sensitized dye sensitized solar cell (DSSC) devices. The prepared co-absorbent could overcome the deficiency of N719 absorption in the low-wavelength region of the visible spectrum, and offset competitive visible-light absorption of I3−. Application of these prepared complexes in N719-sensitized solar cells enhanced their performance by 10–36%, which indicated a potential application of these types of complexes in DSSCs.

1. Introduction

Recently, the design and synthesis of organic–inorganic hybrid complexes based on strong coordinate bonds and multiple weak non-covalent forces has become a rapidly expanding field of research field in coordination chemistry and crystal engineering. These complexes not only possess fascinating structural features but also show interesting properties as new functional materials, which can be used in the areas of luminescence, sensors, separation, adsorption, catalysis and biological chemistry, etc.1–4 Moreover, in recent years, researchers have begun to focus on the potential applications of organometallic complexes with optical properties in co-sensitized dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs).5,6

Zn(II), Cd(II) and Hg(II) ions often adopt different coordination modes, such as four-, five- or six-coordination modes when they react with organic ligands with N, O donors.7 The design and synthesis of new organic ligands is a key approach for the construction of metal–organic complexes with desired structures. Schiff bases still play an important role in metal coordination chemistry, even after almost a century since their discovery.8 They are considered “privileged ligands” and are the most widely used, due to the diversity of synthetic methods that are available, their remarkable versatility and their good solubility in common solvents. A large number of Schiff bases have been synthesized and extensively studied because of their characteristic properties, such as novel structures, thermal stability, relevant biological properties, high synthesis flexibility and medicinal utility.9 The possibility of structural changes and the existence of different supramolecular structures after coordination are the key phenomena responsible for the photochromic behavior of the complexes based on aromatic Schiff bases with hydrogen bonds.10 The relatively complex photobehavior of these complexes, related to their rotational flexibility, needs to be well understood.11 The introduction of different small anions can also have a significant effect on the structural construction of complexes and their properties. In coordination chemistry, halogen ions have been widely used as anions for the construction of metal coordination complexes because they can adjust the topologies of complexes through different coordinate bonds or non-covalent interactions.12

To gain insight into the influence of Schiff-base ligands with different functional groups in constructing Zn(II), Cd(II) and Hg(II) complexes, we studied the synthesis, stability, and photoelectric properties of complexes that used the following three newly-synthesized Schiff bases, namely, ortho-(6-methoxy-pyridyl)(CHNAr) (where Ar = 2,6–iPr2C6H3, L1; 4–MeC6H4, L2; 2–OMeC6H4, L3). By self-assembly, a series of novel Zn(II), Cd(II) and Hg(II) complexes were synthesized: [ZnL1Cl2] (Zn1), [CdL1Cl2]2 (Cd1), [HgL1Cl2] (Hg1), [ZnL2Cl2] (Zn2), [CdL2Cl2] (Cd2), [HgL2Cl2] (Hg2), [ZnL3Cl2] (Zn3), [CdL3Cl2] (Cd3) and [HgL3Cl2] (Hg3). The rational design of Schiff bases with functional groups in different positions not only influences structural diversity but also considerably determines the luminescent properties.13 Generally, the scale of the π-conjugated system and the electronic effect of substituents on the ligand are effective factors for controlling the emission color, as well as the photoluminescence efficiency.14 With the objective of achieving light-electricity efficiency enhancement, and taking into account the absorption of light in the low wavelength region of the visible spectrum,15 we used Zn1–Zn3 to prepare ZnX/N719 co-sensitized dye sensitized solar cell (DSSC) devices, on account of their suitable absorption and energy levels. The application of these prepared complexes in N719-sensitized solar cells enhanced the performance by 10–36%, which indicates a potential application in DSSCs.

2.3. Assembly and measurement of DSSCs

Dye-sensitized solar cells were fabricated using the following procedure. The TiO2 paste was cast onto the FTO substrate by a screen-printing method, followed by drying at 10 °C for 5 min; this process was repeated six times, and then followed by sintering at 500 °C for 15 min in air to obtain a transparent TiO2 photoelectrode with a thickness of ca. 10 μm. The co-sensitized electrodes were prepared by immersing the obtained mesoporous TiO2 photoelectrode into 0.3 mM Zn1, Zn2 or Zn3 solution in absolute ethanol for 2 h and washing with ethanol and drying with a blower, then further immersing the electrodes in 0.3 mM N719 solution in absolute ethanol for 12 h, and then washing with ethanol and drying with a blower again. The single N719-sensitized electrodes were prepared by only immersing the TiO2 photoelectrode into 0.3 mM N719 solution in absolute ethanol for 14 h. The electrolyte used in this work was 0.5 M LiI + 0.05 M I2 + 0.1 M tert-butyl pyridine in a 1:1 (volume ratio) of acetonitrile-propylene carbonate. The platinum counter-electrode was prepared by depositing H2PtCl6 paste onto the FTO glass substrates and then sintering at 450 °C for 30 min. The cells were assembled by sandwiching the electrolyte between the dye-sensitized photoanode and the counter-electrode and the assembly was held together using mini-binder clips.

Photocurrent–photovoltage (I–V) curves were recorded with a Keithley model 2400 digital source meter, using a mask with an aperture area of 0.16 cm2. The irradiance of AM1.5 global sunlight from a filtered 500 W xenon lamp light source was set at 100 mW cm−2 and was calibrated with a standard silicon solar cell (no. NIMMS1123, calibrated by the National Institute of Metrology, P. R. China). Based on the I–V curve, the fill factor (FF) was defined as: FF = (Jmax × Vmax)/(Jsc × Voc), where Jmax and Vmax are the photocurrent density and photovoltage for maximum power output, and Jsc and Voc are the short-circuit photocurrent density and open-circuit photovoltage, respectively. The overall energy conversion efficiency η was defined as: η = (FF × Jsc × Voc)/Pin where Pin is the power of the incident light. EIS were recorded with a CHI660D Electrochemical Analyzer (Chenhua, China), and the measurements were taken over a frequency range of 0.1–100 kHz under standard global AM1.5 solar irradiation. Dark currents were also recorded with a CHI660D Electrochemical Analyzer.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthesis and Characterization of ligands and complexes

Three new ligands ortho-(6-methoxy-pyridyl)(CHNAr) (where Ar = 2,6–iPr2C6H3, L1; 4–MeC6H4, L2; 2–OMeC6H4, L3) were synthesized by the condensation of 6-methoxy-2-pyridinecarboxaldehyde with the corresponding aniline in refluxing anhydrous methanol in the presence of a catalytic amount of formic acid (Scheme 1). All complexes Zn1–Zn3, Cd1–Cd3 and Hg1–Hg3 were self-assembled by the reaction of Zn(II)/Cd(II)/Hg(II) chloride and corresponding ligands at refluxing temperature. The formation of the Schiff-base complexes can be rationalized in the light of a template effect of Zn(II)/Cd(II)/Hg(II) modulated by the counter cations. A template agent (free ligand) can be said to contain the required structure to organize a collection of building blocks so that they can be linked together in a specific manner.16 The identities of ligands and complexes were established by 1H NMR, UV-vis and FT-IR spectroscopy, and satisfactory elemental analysis data. The crude products of the ligands were recrystallized from n-hexane to give a pale yellow solid (L1), a yellow solid (L2) and a brown-yellow oil (L3), respectively. X-ray-quality crystals of Zn1, Zn3, Cd1–Cd3, Hg1 and Hg3 were obtained by slow evaporation from methanol solution; Zn2 and Hg2 were obtained by slow evaporation from methanol/dichloromethane solution. The yields of ligands and complexes were within the range 77–89% and 45–78%, respectively. We confirm that all the complexes are stable in the solid state upon extended exposure to air. All ligands have good solubility in common organic solvents and the complexes are slightly soluble in very low polarity solvents, such as n-hexane, benzene and toluene, soluble in proton solvent and high polarity solvents, such as DMF and DMSO.

The FT-IR spectra of ligands (L1–L3) and complexes (Zn1–Zn3, Cd1–Cd3 and Hg1–Hg3) are shown in the ESI† (Fig. S1–S3). In the FT-IR spectra, the characteristic in-plane and out-of-plane deformation bands of the 2-substituted pyridine rings shift to higher frequencies [ca. 30 cm−1δ(Py) and 20 cm−1γ(Py)] in comparison with similar free ligands.17 Several bands in the range 2800–3000 cm−1 are assigned to the pyridine νC–H stretching vibration. The appearance of a band in the region of 1620–1647 cm−1, assigned to νCN, is slightly red-shifted by the range of 3–12 cm−1 relative to that of the free Schiff-base ligand (1628–1647 cm−1) upon coordination of the metal ions,18 suggesting that coordination through the Schiff-base nitrogen atoms weakens the CN double bonds. Extensive studies19 have shown that the s character of the nitrogen lone pair in the –N bond increases upon coordination, producing a shorter CN bond length and a greater –N stretching force constant (higher frequency of vibration). For all of the ligands and complexes, three medium to strong peaks observed in the range 1570–1454 cm−1 are likely to be associated with the pyridine rings. The νC–O stretching vibration is observed in the range of 1222–1297 cm−1. A medium to weak metal-sensitive stretching band in the 416–411 cm−1 range for all complexes can be assigned to the ν(M–N) vibration. These phenomena confirm the coordinative interaction between the nitrogen atoms and metal cations.

The 1H NMR spectra of ligands and complexes were recorded in CDCl3 solutions to probe the solution structure (see the ESI,† Fig. S4–S6). The three protons on pyridine rings do not exhibit spin structure and display low-field signals compared to phenyl protons, which appear as a broad signal at ca. δ = 6.81–8.16. Furthermore, a common trend in the position of these signals is that, as all complexes were formed by the imine–N and pyridine–N, they display a 0.01–0.75 ppm shift. This behavior causes a characteristic change of the sequence of chemical shift positions of the pyridine proton resonances compared to that in the free ligands. The 1H NMR spectra of the complexes (8.18–8.83 ppm) show that the resonances of the imine –CHN– moiety protons shift toward low field compared to those of the corresponding ligands (8.18–8.50 ppm), indicating the formation of the coordination bonds between the ligand and the M(II) center.20 For ligands and corresponding complexes, the obvious strong signal at ca. 3.99–4.27 ppm is assigned to the –OCH3 group protons of pyridine rings. Especially for L1 and the corresponding complexes (Zn1, Cd1 and Hg1), the appearance of protons of the –CH– group as a solitary signal unambiguously testifies that the ligands and complexes are magnetically equivalent. The multiplet at the higher field in the range of 2.95–3.13 ppm is assigned to the –CH– group protons. For the corresponding complexes of L1–L3, differing only in the species of the methyl substituents on the phenyl ring, the methyl resonance is observed in the region 1.08–1.18, 2.02–2.42 and 3.85–4.15 ppm, respectively.

In order to confirm the phase purity of the bulk materials, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) experiments were carried out on the nine complexes (Zn1–Zn3, Cd1–Cd3 and Hg1–Hg3) (See the ESI,† Fig. S7). Although the experimental patterns have a few unindexed diffraction lines and some are slightly broadened in comparison with those simulated from the single crystal models, it can still be concluded that the bulk synthesized materials and the as-grown crystals are homogeneous for all complexes. The differences in intensity may be due to the preferred orientation of the crystalline powder samples.21

3.2. X-ray crystal structures

The eventual goal here was to investigate the possibility that the ultimate crystal morphology could be influenced by the metallic unit at the very beginning of the crystal growth process. In order to investigate the effect of different metal ions on the crystal structures, the metal salts with the different metal cations and the same anions, namely, ZnCl2, CdCl2 and HgCl2, were used. Moreover, the ligands L1, L2 and L3 were employed in order to study the influence of different electron-donating substituents of the aniline on the construction of structures. Crystal data and experimental details for the crystals are summarized in Table 1, and selected bond lengths and bond angles are given in Tables S1–S3.† In zinc and mercury complexes, Zn(II) and Hg(II) formed tetrahedral complexes, while Cd(II) tended to form square pyramidal complexes, which can be observed from the crystal structures of Cd1 and Cd3. In the nine complexes, the structure of the ligand plays an important role in the construction of the complexes. The dihedral angles between the pyridine ring and phenyl ring in the complexes of L1, L2 and L3 are in the range of 79.58(1)°–79.92(1)°, 7.65(2)°–11.12(2)° and 10.53(1)°–15.23(2)°, respectively (see the ESI,† Table S4). The –OCH3 group in the pyridine ring does not coordinate to the metal center in these complexes because of the stereospecific blockade. However, it can serve as the donor of hydrogen bonds and still play important roles in the formation of supramolecular structures through C–H⋯Cl hydrogen bonds. Furthermore, in these complexes, there are more chances to form C–H⋯Cl hydrogen bonds (see the ESI,† Table S5) and π⋯π stacking interactions (see the ESI,† Table S6) between the ligands, which play significant roles in constructing the three-dimensional structures. All complexes are air stable and can retain their structural integrity at room temperature for a few months.

In summary, the diverse complex structures were obtained owing to the structure and coordination site of the ligand, in combination with different metal atoms.

The quantum yields of all ligands and complexes were determined in acetonitrile solution at 298 K (Table 2). It was found that the quantum yields of complexes are higher than those of the free ligands. In particular, the quantum yield of Zn3 (ΦF = 0.422) is 8.79-fold that of the L3 ligand (ΦF = 0.048). This is because the M(II) centers in complexes play a significant role in enhancing the ligand-centered π*–π fluorescent emission. The chelation of the ligand to the metal center could increase the rigidity of the ligand, and reduce the loss of energy by thermal vibrational decay. Meanwhile, luminescence quantum yields decrease according to the order in the periodic table (Zn > Cd > Hg) when the same ligand is attached. The lowest luminescence quantum yields of complexes Hg1–Hg3 can be attributed to the “heavy atom effect”.28 The sensitivity of the luminescence intensity toward Hg(II) ions made us interested in possible applications in luminescent sensors for Hg(II) ions. For complexes Zn1, Zn2 and Zn3 (ΦF = 0.422, Zn3 > 0.197, Zn2 > 0.060, Zn1), the steric configuration and the electron donating nature determine the luminescence properties. Similarly, Cd1–Cd3 and Hg1–Hg3 also possess the same regularity.

In the solid state, all complexes emit green luminescence at λmax = 509–543 nm when irradiated at 365 nm (Fig. 9). As mentioned above, in the complexes M1–M3 (M = Zn, Cd and Hg), the zinc complexes show the greatest emission intensity and luminescence quantum yields, both in the solid state and in solution. This indicates that the central metal and the structure of the ligand affect the luminescence properties. Then, in order to investigate the influence of the external environment on the luminescence properties, we chose complex Zn3 as an example to provide some luminescence measurements in acetonitrile solution at different temperatures (from 298 K to 343 K). The thermal quenching of luminescence is an important technological parameter for materials because it has considerable influence on the light output.30 The temperature-dependent luminescent properties of Zn3 excited at 375 nm are presented in Fig. 11. With an increase in the temperature, the maximum emission wavelength remains basically unchanged and the full width at half-maximum (FWHM) of the emission band increases from 57.5 to 59.9 nm. Curves A and B in the inset of Fig. 11 represent the temperature dependence of the integrated emission intensity and the emission peak height normalized with respect to the values at room temperature (298 K), respectively. When the temperature increases to 343 K, the normalized integrated emission intensity and emission peak height are found to be 75% and 73% of the values at 298 K, respectively. These phenomena can be explained by the interaction between the electron and thermally active phonon. The excited luminescent center is thermally activated through phonon interaction, and then thermally released through the crossing point between the excited state and the ground state. This probability of non-radiative transition by thermal activation leads to the decrease of emission intensity, which is strongly dependent on temperature. The thermally activated luminescent centers interact strongly with thermally active phonons. At higher temperature, the population density of phonon is increased, and the electron–phonon interaction is dominant. Consequently, FWHM of emission spectrum is broadened.31

Hence, we discovered that the luminescence emission intensities and wavelengths of coordination complexes with d10 metal centers can be easily tuned by each of the following factors: central metal, state of aggregation, solvent, and temperature. The results lead to the conclusion that subtle changes in the structure of the ligand and the central metal give rise to significant differences in the structure of their coordination complexes, which will generate dramatic changes in their functional properties.

The luminescence decay profiles of ligands L1–L3 and complexes Zn1–Zn3, Cd1–Cd3 and Hg1–Hg3 were measured at their optimal excitation wavelengths in the solid state and in acetonitrile solution at 298 K and 77 K. The detailed data are listed in Table S7 and S8, ESI.† A general trend is that the luminescence lifetimes for M(II) (M = Zn, Cd and Hg) complexes either in the solid state or acetonitrile solution at 298 K and 77 K are mostly longer than those of the corresponding ligands L1–L3. The most obvious observation is that the lifetime of Zn3 (τ = 13.33 μs) is 2.55-fold that of the corresponding ligand L3 (τ = 5.22 μs) in acetonitrile solution. This is attributed to the more stable structure and interaction upon coordination.32 Meanwhile, the luminescence lifetimes of the ligand and corresponding complexes in the solid state are longer than those in acetonitrile solution, which can be ascribed to the less polar nature of the environment in solid state.33 The luminescence lifetimes of complexes Hg1–Hg3 (8.82–14.95 μs at 298 K; 6.39–9.45 μs at 77 K) are shorter than those of Zn1–Zn3 (11.41–19.82 μs at 298 K; 7.62–14.27 μs at 77 K) and Cd1–Cd3 (10.79–15.37 μs at 298 K; 6.53–10.75 μs at 77 K), which may be a result of the heavy-atom effect of the Hg(II) ion.34 Luminescence lifetimes for ligands and complexes at 298 K are mostly longer than those at 77 K, as shown in Tables S7 and S8, ESI.† This phenomenon can be attributed to the quenching of the center metal cation at a lower temperature. It should be pointed out that the lifetime of Zn3 (τ = 19.82 μs) is longer than that of other complexes.

3.4. Application in DSSCs

The complexes Zn1–Zn3 have the highest luminescence quantum yields, compared to the other corresponding metal complexes. Meanwhile, the absorptions of Zn1–Zn3 are all in the region of 300–450 nm (Fig. S15, ESI†), which could compensate for that of N719 in the low-wavelength region of the visible spectrum. Besides the optical properties discussed above, energy-level matching is a crucial factor in selecting a sensitizer. The HOMO and LUMO energy levels of Zn1, Zn2 and Zn3 were investigated by cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurement in a three-electrode cell and an electrochemistry workstation. The experimental data are summarized in Table 4. As estimated from the intersection of absorption and emission spectra, the excitation transition energies (E0–0) of Zn1, Zn2 and Zn3 are 3.04, 3.01 and 2.94 eV, respectively. The HOMO values of Zn1, Zn2 and Zn3 corresponding to their first redox potential are −5.18, −4.86 and −5.02 eV, respectively. The estimated excited-state potentials corresponding to the LUMO levels of Zn1, Zn2 and Zn3, calculated from EHOMO + E0–0, are −2.14, −1.85 and −2.08 eV, respectively.35 For better electron injection, this LUMO level should lie above the conduction band (CB) of the TiO2 semiconductor (−4.40 eV vs. vacuum) and for effective dye regeneration, the HOMO energy level should lie below the I−/I3− redox electrolyte (−4.60 eV vs. vacuum), which is further improved negatively by about 0.3 V by adding additives such as 4-tert-butyl pyridine (TBP) to the I−/I3− redox electrolyte.36 This gives sufficient driving force for dye regeneration.37 It shows that the energy levels of Zn1, Zn2 and Zn3 are appropriate for DSSC systems containing TiO2 (Fig. 12). Thus the complexes Zn1–Zn3 were employed as co-sensitizers in photoanodes used to prepare ZnX/N719 co-sensitized DSSC devices.

EIS is a powerful technique for the analysis of interfacial electronic and ionic transport processes in an electrochemical device.38 It is a steady-state method to measure current response based on application of an AC voltage at different frequencies. Herein, we utilized EIS to analyze charge carrier dynamics in the interfacial regions of solid–liquid layers. The Nyquist plots of EIS for DSSCs based on different photoelectrodes were measured under standard global AM1.5 solar irradiation, applying a forward bias of −0.75 V. The three semicircles located in the high-, middle- and low-frequency regions (left to right) are attributed to the electrochemical reaction at the Pt–electrolyte interface, the charge transfer at the TiO2–dye–electrolyte interface and a Warburg diffusion process of the I−/I3− in the electrolyte.39 Under light illumination, EIS was utilized to analyze the charge transport resistance at the TiO2–dye–electrolyte interface for its significance on the efficiency of DSSCs.40 As shown in Fig. 14, the radii of the large semicircle located in middle-frequency regions in the Nyquist plot decrease after being co-sensitized with zinc complexes, and the values are in the order of Zn3/N719 < Zn2/N719 < Zn1/N719 < N719, which indicates a decrease of the electron transfer impedance (Rct) and an increase of charge transfer rate at this interface after co-sensitization. This is of benefit for the enhancement of DSSC performance.

Dark-current measurement of DSSCs has been considered as a qualitative technique to describe the extent of the back electron transfer.41 A comparison of dark current between the investigated cells can provide useful information regarding the back electron transfer process. Fig. 15 shows the dark current–voltage characteristics of the DSSCs based on different photoelectrodes with the applied bias from 0 to +0.80 V. The onset of the dark current for individual N719-sensitized DSSC occurs at a bias of about +0.30 V, with a subsequent dramatic increase of dark current with the increase of potential. In contrast, for the co-sensitized DSSCs, the onset potential shifted to about +0.45 V; furthermore, the dark current of the co-sensitized DSSCs increased much more slowly than that of N719-sensitized DSSC when the potential was greater than +0.45 V. In other words, under the same potential bias, when the potential was ≥0.45 V, the dark current for the co-sensitized DSSCs was noticeably smaller than that for the N719-sensitized DSSC. The increase of the onset potential and the reduction of the dark current demonstrated that Zn1, Zn2 and Zn3 successfully suppress the electron back reaction with I3− in the electrolyte. This is critical to enhance the efficiency of DSSCs.

When the molecular structures of Zn1–Zn3 are taken into account, it is found that introduction of the electron-donating substituent group in aniline also has some effect on the performance of DSSCs. The enhancement of co-sensitized DSSC performance compared with that of single N719 sensitized DSSC is in the order of Zn3/N719 > Zn2/N719 > Zn1/N719 > N719. This indicates that the η values increase according to the electron-donating ability of substituent groups (–OCH3 > –CH3 > –iPr) in aniline. For the introduction of the –OCH3 substituent, the absorption shows a red shift and the largest molar extinction coefficient (Table 4). The longer wavelength shift is better for compensating the absorption of N719 and overcoming the competitive light absorption of I3−. Moreover, the dihedral angle between the pyridine ring and the phenyl ring is 15.23(2)° (Zn3), which is approximately parallel. The good co-planarity is better for electronic energy transfer. Therefore, the performance of co-sensitized cell is in the order of Zn3/N719 > Zn2/N719 > Zn1/N719 > N719.

4. Conclusions

In summary, nine new transition metal (M = Zn, Cd and Hg) complexes with ortho-(6-methoxy-pyridyl)(CHNAr) ligands were synthesized and characterized and their luminescent properties were studied. Crystal structure analysis reveals that M(II) (M = Zn, Cd and Hg) complexes adopt distorted tetrahedral geometry (except for Cd1 and Cd3, which display distorted square pyramidal geometry) and have C–H⋯Cl hydrogen bonds and π⋯π stacking interactions in the solid state. The series of complexes exhibit tunable luminescence from blue, through green to light yellow on varying the temperature (298 K and 77 K) in the solution and in the solid state. Among the nine transition metal complexes, the complexes Zn1–Zn3 exhibit the brightest luminescence. The luminescence emission intensities of coordination complexes with d10 metal centers can be easily tuned by the center metal or the structure of the ligand. Subtle changes in the structure of the ligand or the center metal give rise to significant differences in the structure of their coordination complexes, which will generate dramatic changes in their functional properties. The complexes Zn1–Zn3 were employed as co-sensitizer in photoanodes to assemble with counter electrodes and electrolyte to prepare ZnX/N719 co-sensitized DSSC devices. The results show that, upon co-sensitization, the performances of cells are all improved. In particular, the Zn3/N719 device exhibited a higher Jsc of 16.59% mA cm−2, corresponding to an overall conversion efficiency of 6.94%, which is ca. 36% higher than that of devices sensitized by single N719, indicating that these complexes possess wonderful potential in DSSC applications.
