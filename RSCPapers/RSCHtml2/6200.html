Optimized molecular aggregation and photophysical process synergistically promoted photovoltaic performance in low-regularity benzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole-based medium-bandgap copolymers via modulating π bridges†

Abstract

π-Bridges of D–π–A conjugated copolymers (CPs) not only determine the optical absorption, bandgap, energy levels, miscibility, molecular configuration, and aggregation but also affect the dipole moment, electrostatic potential, and photophysical process. Herein, we judiciously regulated the octylthienyl π bridge on both sides of electron-deficient benzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole (BT) core, and three medium band gap copolymers, irregular PClBDT-TBT, regular PClBDT-BT, and PClBDT-DTBT were synthesized. DFT calculations demonstrated that the irregular models (ClBDT-TBT-1)3 and (ClBDT-TBT-2)3 had larger dipole moment (Δμ) and reduced exciton binding energy (Eb), but the irregular models (ClBDT-TBT-3)3 and (ClBDT-TBT-4)3 possessed decreased Δμ and elevated Eb. Irregular PClBDT-TBT not only effectively depressed excessive aggregation and altered molecular orientation but also regulated the molecular crystallinity to improve the charge mobility. Moreover, femtosecond transient absorption (fs-TA) spectroscopic analyses suggested that low-regularity PClBDT-TBT:Y6 film had a prolonged exciton diffusion time so as to suppress recombination and ameliorate the charge transfer. Consequently, the low-regularity PClBDT-TBT-based device yielded excellent power conversion efficiency (PCE) as high as 13.04% when paired with Y6. Moreover, regular PClBDT-BT- and PClBDT-DTBT-based devices obtained PCEs of 5.54% and 11.12%, respectively, under identical testing conditions. Our work demonstrates that regulating π bridges in D–π–A type CPs is an easy and effective strategy aimed at further boosting the device performance by tuning the molecular aggregation and photophysical processes.

1. Introduction

Thanks to their desirable features of easy manufacturing, flexibility, semitransparency, and molecular structure tunability, bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) organic solar cells (OSCs), which can directly convert solar energy into electricity have attracted extensive and persistent attention in the past two decades.1–6 The weak light-harvesting ability, deeper and inflexible energy levels, and high cost, have limited the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of fullerene-based OSCs to a value of 11.7% in single junction binary solar cells.7 However, non-fullerene-based OSCs have achieved a great breakthrough and promoted the PCEs to outstrip over 18%.8–12 Among these numerous electron acceptors, non-fullerene (NF) small molecular electron acceptors featuring acceptor–donor–acceptor (A–D–A) architecture and low optical bandgap (<1.60 eV), e.g., indacenodithieno[3,2-b]thiophene-based ITIC,13 fluorinated IT-4F,14,15 and a C-shaped Y6 featuring the fused benzothiadiazole (BT) aromatic ring,8,9,16 have been especially focused on and gained conspicuous successes.17–20 Therefore, it was important and highly desirable to exploit high-performance medium band gap (MBG) polymeric donors paired with the aforesaid electron acceptors.21,22

Historically, an effective strategy for designing efficient photovoltaic (PV) conjugated copolymers (CPs) assisted by alternating electron-rich and electron-deficient building blocks to build up the D–π–A structure has been widely employed because it could easily modulate absorption, energy levels, molecular stacking, and microstructural morphology of the resulting CPs by elaborately choosing suitable D, π-bridge, and A subunits and delicately regulating the intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) procedures from the D unit to A moiety. With this in mind, some typical building blocks, for instance, benzo[1,2-c:4,5-c′]dithiophene-4,8-dione (BDD),23–27 oligothiophene,16,17,28–31 and BT32–37 were selected to explore the high-performance MBG polymeric donors in order to well match the NFAs. It is well known that because of its planar and rigid geometry, strong o-benzoquinoidal structure, easily functionalized by fluorine, chlorine and alkyloxy substituent groups, and easy preparation, BT was considered to be one of the most excellent electron-deficient units (Chart 1 and Table S1, ESI†).36–42 Early in 2011, You's group designed a fluorinated BT-based PBnDT-DTffBT, exhibiting an optical band gap (Eoptg) of 1.7 eV, the deeper highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energy level (EHOMO) of −5.30 eV, and thus obtained 7.2% PCE when pairing with PC61BM.37 Subsequently, Yan et al. investigated an oligothiopnene-containing PffBT4T-2OD, the resulting PffBT4T-2OD:PC71BM-based device achieved a PCE of 10.8%.38 Then, they further optimized the molecular structure via side chain and boosted the PCE up to 11.7%.7 In 2018, Peng et al. prepared a series of BT-based CPs to investigate the synergistic effect of sulfuration and fluorination onto the alkylthienyl side chain in benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b′]dithiophene (BDT), and found a combination of sulfuration and fluorination in PBDTSF-FBT obtained the best light-harvesting ability, the lowest EHOMO of −5.41 eV, and thus high open circuit voltage (VOC) of 1.03 V as well as best PCE of 11.66% paired with ITIC.32 Thereafter, Li et al. selected chlorinated BDT and 4,7-bis(thienyl)-5,6-dialkyloxy-BT to construct PBT-Cl, and the simper chlorination strategy impelled to achieve the deeper EHOMO of −5.51 eV, good short circuit current density (JSC) over 20 mA cm−2 and thus PCE of 11.60% when blending with IT-4F even in the adverse condition of slightly narrowing absorption range.33 Meanwhile, Guo et al. reported two D–A1–D–A2 types of MBG phthalimide-involving PhI-ffBT and ffPhI-FFBT, exhibiting excellent hole mobility of 1.42 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 s−1, fill factor (FF) of 76% and remarkably increased PCE of 13.31%.34 In 2020, Bo et al. also selected a BT-based MBG copolymer PBDT-AFBT as a donor to realize an excellent JSC larger than 20 mA cm−2 and PCE of 12.33% that matched with IDTCN-O.36 Evidently, the development of high-performance BT-based MBG polymeric donors was encouraging, however, it still lagged behind the well-developed BDD- and oligothiophene-based ones, and were overlooked to some extent.

Apart from regulating the D and A subunits in the D–π–A type copolymers, the π bridge played a momentous role in lengthening the conjugation, adjusting the absorption, energy levels, molecular backbone and degree of crystallinity of the corresponding PV materials.43–45 Within the numerous D–π–A copolymers, the extensively used conjugated π bridge ranged from the simple ethylene46 and ethynylene47 to the aromatic thiophene,45,48–53 selenophene,54,55 furan,56 thiazole,44,57,58 and larger conjugation thieno[3,2-b]thiophene (TT).59,60 Of these, thiophene was regarded as the most common and successful application unit, due to its medium electronegativity, tunable distortion angle with the bilateral-linked groups, weak intermolecular S⋯X (X = N, O, S, and halide) non-bonding interaction61 and easily decorated by alkyl-,62 alkyloxy-,63 alkylthio-,61,64 fluoro-,45 chloro-,51 ester65 and siloxane-terminated40etc. substituents. For instance, when the flexible side chain of the thiophene π bridge in naphtho[1,2-c:5,6-c′]bis[1,2,5]thiadiazole (NT)-based CPs was shortened from 2-dodecyltetradecyl (DT) in NT1014 to 2-octyldodecyl (OD) in NT812, the anticipated face-on orientation, and ideal microstructural morphology was formed, which impelled the relevant PCE, remarkably increasing from 3.03% to 10.33%.62 In thieno[3,4-b]thiophene-based CPs, the broadened absorption, good planarity, and 11.9 times enhanced hole mobility, and thus 2.17 times increased PCE was observed after the alkyl was substituted with alkylthio chain.61 As for BDD-based CPs, the red-shifted absorption, deepened the EHOMO, closed molecular stacking, and more obvious fibrillar network morphology was obtained and the PCE was elevated from 10.9% to 12.7% after incorporating the chloro group onto the thiophene bridge.51 Easily modified thiophene π bridge was of critical importance and gained more and more attention, thereupon, there were many works devoted to the modification of the thiophene π bridge. Nevertheless, only limited efforts have shown solicitude for the asymmetric thiophene π bridge.50,52,53,66

Given the aforementioned consideration, three MBG BT-based CPs, hereinafter called PClBDT-BT, PClBDT-TBT, and PClBDT-DTBT were synthesized, by regulating the number of π bridges using chlorinated benzodithiophene (ClBDT) as the D moiety and BT as the A unit, (Chart 2), to deeply investigate the impact of the octylthienyl bridge on the absorption, energy level, molecular dipole moment and electrostatic potential (ESP), molecular stacking, charge transfer, microstructural morphology, and photovoltaic properties. It was found that low-regularity PClBDT-TBT:Y6 film not only possessed optimized molecular orientation and aggregation but also yielded prolonged exciton diffusion time in order to reduce the recombination. Accordingly, when pairing with Y6, low-regularity PClBDT-TBT gained the sterling PCE of 13.04% (Chart 1), which outperformed those of regular PClBDT-BT (5.54%) and PClBDT-DTBT (11.12%). This work demonstrates that preciously tuning the π bridge in the D–π–A structure is easy and tried, and is the true scenario to advance the device performance.

2. Results and discussion

2.1 Materials synthesis and characterization

Molecular structures of PClBDT-BT, PClBDT-TBT, and PClBDT-DTBT involving different numbers of octylthienyl π bridges are exhibited in Chart 2 and the corresponding synthetic procedure is shown in Scheme S1 (ESI†). These BT-based CPs were obtained by the Stille polycondensation reaction between chlorinated bistin 2,6-bis(trimethyltin)-4,8-bis(4-chloro-5-(2-butyloctyl)thien-2-yl)benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b′]dithiophene (ClBDTSn) and dibromides 4,7-dibromobenzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole (BTBr2), 4-bromo-7-(5-bromo-4-octylthien-2-yl)benzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole (TBTBr2) and 4,7-di(5-bromo-4-octylthien-2-yl)benzo[c]-[1,2,5]thiadiazole (DTBTBr2) using Pd2(dba)3 and P(o-tolyl)3 as the catalyst.67 The chemical structures and purities of the comonomers were verified by 1H NMR spectra etc. (Fig. S1–S5, ESI†). The end-capping procedure was performed to enhance the stability.49 For removing oligomers and residual catalysts, further treatment was carried out considering our reported method.49 All BT-based CPs formed as black solids with yields spanning from 63% to 83% and their chemical structures were checked by 1H NMR in deuterated o-dichlorobenzene (o-DCB-d4) at 85 °C (Fig. S6–S8, ESI†). Furthermore, these resulting copolymers possessed enough solubility in chloroform (CF) to satisfy the requirement for device fabrication. The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) exhibited a 5% weight-loss decomposed temperature (Td) varied from 371 to 416 °C (Table S2, ESI†), which meets the need for solar cell fabrication.

2.2 Optical and electrochemical properties

Normalized UV-vis absorbance spectra of PClBDT-BT, PClBDT-TBT, and PClBDT-DTBT in diluted chloroform solution and films were measured and are shown in Fig. 1(a) and (b), and the detailed optical data are listed in Table 1. In dilute solution, three copolymers exhibited absorption ranging from 300 to 780 nm, the weak peak placed in the high-energy region was attributed to the π–π* transition of the π-conjugated backbone, and the ones situated at low energy consisted of two vibrational peaks 0–1 peak originated from ICT interaction from D to A and the 0–0 peak was related to the aggregation.44,49,66,67 It was seen that as the number of octylthienyl π bridges increased, the maximum absorption peak (λmax) was firstly kept unchanged and then blue-shifted, and this trend was close to that found in DFT calculations discussed later (Table 2). Moving from the solution to film, red-shifted values were found to be 3 nm for PClBDT-BT from 639 nm to 642 nm, 14 nm for PClBDT-TBT from 639 nm to 653 nm, and 2 nm for PClBDT-DTBT from 608 nm to 610 nm, respectively. The optical band gap (Eoptg) calculated from the equation Eoptg = 1240/λfilmonset, where λfilmonset refers to the onset absorption band, was 1.79 eV for PClBDT-BT, 1.75 eV for PClBDT-TBT and 1.68 eV for PClBDT-DTBT, respectively. Moreover, the corresponding maximum peak absorption coefficients in the solution were measured (Fig. S10, ESI†) and estimated to be in the range of 3.24 × 104 to 3.58 × 104 M−1 cm−1 (Table S2, ESI†), indicative of the π-bridge barely impacting on sunlight-harvesting capability. Importantly, these copolymers can well match with Y6 and form complementary absorption, leading to a desired JSC. Additionally, the EHOMO and ELUMO are estimated on the basis of the equation: EHOMO = −e(φonsetox + 4.70) (eV) and ELUMO = −e(φonsetred + 4.70) (eV) were obtained by cyclic voltammetry (CV) method,49 and the CV curves of polymer donors and Y6 under the identical testing condition were shown in Fig. S11 (ESI†), as well as the corresponding energy levels diagram is elucidated in Fig. 1(c). We can find that the EHOMO/ELUMO levels were both gradually upshifted from −5.69/−3.60 eV to −5.57/−3.54 eV and then to −5.47/−3.52 eV, when the number of octylthienyl π bridges was increased, resulting in the reduced electrical bandgaps of 2.09, 2.03 and 1.95 eV, verified by the later DFT predication. Obviously, introducing the octylthienyl π bridge in the D–π–A structure was harmful to enlarge VOC, however, the maximized offset of ELUMO between the polymer donors and Y6 could facilitate the photo-generated excitons split into free charges.

Temperature-dependent absorption (TD-Abs) spectra with temperature scope from 25 to 105 °C in an increment of 10 °C in the diluted chlorobenzene (ca. 10−5 M) were recorded to probe into the impact of octylthienyl π bridge on aggregation, in Fig. 1(d)–(f). Obviously, strong 0–1 and 0–0 optical transitions were observed both in PClBDT-BT and PClBDT-TBT, whereas, clear 0–1 but very weak 0–0 peaks were found in PClBDT-DTBT, indicating that the bisoctylthienyl-bridge-containing polymer exhibited the weakest aggregation. 2.3 Density functional theory (DFT) simulations

To deeply investigate the influence of the octylthienyl π bridge on the molecular geometry and intrinsic properties of polymeric donors, DFT and time-dependent DFT (TD-DFT) simulations were performed under the B3LYP-(D3)BJ/6-31g(d,p) level and CAM-B3LYP/def2svp level, respectively.71,72 To enhance the calculation efficiency, the alternating regular polymer backbones for PClBDT-BT and PClBDT-DTBT were simplified into three repeating units (ClBDT-BT)3 and (ClBDT-DTBT)3, respectively. To better match the actual polymer, the low-regularity PClBDT-TBT was replaced with the possible linking fashions, i.e., regular (ClBDT-TBT-1)3 and (ClBDT-TBT-2)3, and irregular (ClBDT-TBT-3)3 and (ClBDT-TBT-4)3. Alkyl side chains 2-butyloctyl and octyl were replaced with iso-butyl and methyl, respectively. The optimized molecular geometries are shown in Fig. 2(a). According to Table S3 (ESI†), little differences in the molecular planarity in these model molecules were observed. The electron density of HOMO levels was preferentially distributed onto the whole polymer backbone and that of LUMO levels was predominantly situated on the electron-deficient BT units. The calculated EHOMO/ELUMO/Eecg were observed to be −5.09/−3.05/2.04 eV for (ClBDT-BT)3, −4.89/−2.96/1.93 eV for (ClBDT-TBT-1)3, −4.89/−2.97/1.92 eV for (ClBDT-TBT-2)3, −4.88/−2.99/1.89 eV for (ClBDT-TBT-3)3, −4.87/−2.98/1.89 eV for (ClBDT-TBT-4)3, and −4.78/−2.92/1.86 eV for (ClBDT-DTBT)3, respectively, which well tallied with the above variation trend of energy level from the CV test. We can find that the irregular model in (ClBDT-TBT-3)3 and (ClBDT-TBT-4)3 was conducive to reducing the bandgap and harvesting more sunlight.

The dipole moments for all model molecules were calculated and the detailed information is listed in Table S5 (ESI†). The overall change in the ground and excited state dipole (Δμg–e) was obtained considering the changes of dipole along each coordinate via the equation: Δμg–e = [(μgx − μex)2 + (μgy − μey)2 + (μgz − μez)2]1/2.73 It was seen that Δμg–e values of two regular models (ClBDT-TBT-1)3 and (ClBDT-TBT-2)3 in low regularity PClBDT-TBT were 9.40 and 8.78 Deby, respectively, which were obviously higher than those of regular (ClBDT-BT)3 and (ClBDT-DTBT)3 (7.24 and 8.18 Deby, respectively). However, the decreased Δμg–e values of 7.84 and 8.06 Deby in the irregular model (ClBDT-TBT-3)3 and (ClBDT-TBT-4)3, respectively, were found. Correspondingly, a reduced Eb of 0.307 eV in the regular model (ClBDT-TBT-1)3 and (ClBDT-TBT-2)3 but enlarged Eb of 0.320 and 0.340 eV in the irregular models (ClBDT-TBT-3)3 and (ClBDT-TBT-4)3 were also observed (Table 2). The smaller Eb was helpful in promoting the exciton dissociation efficiency.53 Besides, simulation maximum absorption peak (λmax) and oscillator strengths (f) were calculated and are summarized in Table 2. The calculated λmax values for (ClBDT-BT)3, (ClBDT-TBT-1)3, (ClBDT-TBT-2)3, (ClBDT-TBT-3)3, (ClBDT-TBT-4)3 and (ClBDT-DTBT)3 were found to be 557.06, 554.18, 557.06, 560.67, 558.68 and 569.47 nm, respectively, which were consistent with S0 → S1 transition primarily originating from single one-electron excitation from HOMO to LUMO.74 Note that the actual molecular structures of low-regularity PClBDT-TBT were complicated, however, the DFT prediction still disclosed the variation tendency.

2.4 Photovoltaic properties

To determine the influence of the conjugated octylthienyl π bridge on the device performance, BHJ devices with a configuration of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/polymer donors: Y6/PDINO/Al were fabricated. The PV parameters were optimized under different conditions, including screening out the D:A weight ratio, thermal annealing (TA), and using 1,8-diiodooctane (DIO) and 1-chloronaphthalene (CN) additives, and tested under the conditions of the AM1.5, 100 mW cm−2 illumination, as shown in Fig. 3, Fig. S14, S15 (ESI†), and Table 3 and Table S6 (ESI†). After searching, it was found that the best D:A weight ratios for three polymers were all to be 1:1.2 (Fig. S14 and Table S6, ESI†). In detail, the PClBDT-BT-based device exhibited a VOC of 0.88 V, JSC of 9.23 mA cm−2, FF of 52.62%, and PCE of 4.26%, and PClBDT-TBT-based device revealed a VOC of 0.83 V, JSC of 18.61 mA cm−2, FF of 67.54% and PCE of 10.42%. In contrast, the PClBDT-DTBT-based device yielded a VOC of 0.75 V, JSC of 22.97 mA cm−2, FF of 59.72%, and PCE of 10.29%. Following, optimal TA treatment had a positive effect on enhancing PCE and its treatment temperatures were 100 °C, 120 °C, and 110 °C for PClBDT-BT-, PClBDT-TBT- and PClBDT-DTBT-based devices, respectively. Thus, the corresponding PCEs were optimized as 4.86%, 13.04%, and 11.12%. Then, additives 3% DIO and 0.5% CN (volume content) played a negative impact on the device performance except that the PClBDT-BT-based device increased from 4.86% to 5.54% after using 0.5% CN (Fig. S15 and Table S6, ESI†). As a consequence, the optimized J–V characteristics are shown in Fig. 3(a), and the related PV data are summarized in Table 3. The optimized PClBDT-BT-based device acquired a VOC of 0.84 V, JSC of 12.04 mA cm−2, FF of 52.88%, and PCE of 5.54%, and the best PClBDT-TBT-based device afforded a VOC of 0.81 V, JSC of 23.72 mA cm−2, FF of 67.63% and PCE of 13.04%, whilst, the first ranked PClBDT-DTBT-based device got a VOC of 0.74 V, JSC of 23.88 mA cm−2, FF of 62.94% and PCE of 11.12%. After incorporating the octylthienyl π bridge, VOC gradually down-shifted from 0.84 to 0.81 then to 0.74 V, evidenced by the above-discussed slowly elevated EHOMO from −5.69 to −5.57 then to −5.47 eV observed in the CV tests and up-shifted step by step from −5.09 to −4.89 then to −4.78 eV divined in DFT simulations, and JSC was drastically increased from 12.04 to 23.70 mA cm−2, then kept stable, while FF was firstly raised from 52.88% to 67.63% then dropped to 62.94% which was verified by the anti-V type varied shunt resistance (RSH) from 447 to 878 then to 714 Ω m2 and the V-type changed series resistance (RS) from 8.85 to 3.66 then to 4.30 Ω m2 (Table 3). Apparently, PClBDT-TBT-based device exhibited the highest PCE up to 13.04%. The external quantum efficiency (EQE) curve under the best fabrication conditions is exhibited in Fig. 3(b). 2.5 Charge recombination behavior and transport properties

To determine the reasons why these optimized devices exhibited different JSC and FF, the exciton dissociation and charge collection efficiencies were examined by measuring the photocurrent (Jph) relative to the effective applied voltage (Veff).75,76 Note that Jph was acquired according to the equation Jph = JL − JD, in which JL and JD represent the current density under illumination and in dark, respectively. Veff was gained from the equation Veff = V0 − Vappl, where V0 stands for voltage at Jph = 0 and Vappl is applied voltage. As seen in Fig. 4(a), when the Veff was larger than 2.5 V, the Jph attained the saturated values (Jsat) of 13.89, 24.17, and 24.85 mA cm−2 for PClBDT-BT:Y6-, PClBDT-TBT:Y6- and PClBDT-DTBT:Y6-based devices, respectively. The exciton dissociation probability (ηdiss) and charge collection probability (ηcoll) can be estimated according to Jph/Jsat under the short-circuit condition (Jsph) and maximal power output condition (Jmph), respectively. From PClBDT-BT to PClBDT-TBT, values of ηdiss and ηcoll were notably increased from 75.5% to 98.6%, and from 53.0% to 84.9% (Table 4), respectively, consistent with markedly increased JSC and FF. However, the PClBDT-DTBT-based device showed a slightly declined ηdiss of 95.6% and ηcoll 78.0% compared to those of PClBDT-TBT, tallying with declined FF.

When n is close to 1, trap-assisted recombination can be effectively inhibited at the open-circuit condition. As seen in Fig. 4(c), (d) and Table 4, from PClBDT-BT to PClBDT-TBT, both α (0.970 vs. 0.995) and n (1.124 vs. 1.049) exhibited approaching 1, demonstrating that obviously depressed charge recombination at both short-circuit and open-circuit conditions upon incorporating the octylthienyl π bridge, consistent with 96.84% increased JSC and 27.89%-enhanced FF. 2.7 Photophysical properties

To further discern the origin of low regularity PClBDT-TBT:Y6 system acquiring champion PV performance, the photo-carrier kinetic processes of pure and blend film were investigated using femtosecond transient absorption (fs-TA) spectroscopy with a 400 nm pump light, as shown in Fig. 9 and 10. To eliminate the disturbance of thickness, the thickness for all films was controlled to 110 nm. In principle, the TA spectra were mainly classified into two features: ground state bleaching (GSB) peak with a negative ΔA signal and excited state absorption (ESA) peak with a positive signal.82 Pure PClBDT-BT film exhibited the GSB signal ranging from 520 to 680 nm, corresponding to the electron transition from the ground state to the excited state excited by pump light, which was identical to the above-mentioned steady-state absorption (Fig. 1(b)) and ESA signal spanning from 690 to 890 nm with photoinduced absorption (PA) in the range of 900–1000 nm induced by singlet exciton. Meanwhile, PClBDT-TBT and PClBDT-DTBT films exhibited similar GSB signals (520–700 nm and 520–715 nm) and ESA signals (705–930 nm and 720–900 nm) with PA signals (940–1000 nm). To reveal the difference in ultrafast dynamics among these pure films, the decay times fitted by the tri-exponential decay function based on GSB peaks were obtained and shown in Fig. 9(g)–(i). It was seen that three decay times τ1, τ2, and τ3, were 0.37, 6.11, and 129.41 ps, respectively, for PClBDT-BT at 655 nm, 0.43, 6.18, and 167.00 ps, respectively, for PClBDT-TBT at 665 nm, and 0.41, 4.39 and 140.85 ps for PClBDT-DTBT at 675 nm, respectively. These observed approaching decay time data demonstrated that the three BT-based CPs experienced a similar physical process after being excited.83

3. Conclusions

In summary, three MBG D–π–A type BT-based polymeric donors, PClBDT-BT, PClBDT-TBT, and PClBDT-DTBT were synthesized by adjusting the number of octylthienyl π bridges in order to investigate how the π bridge affects the molecular intrinsic characteristic, physical properties, and the ultimate photovoltaic performance when pairing with Y6. It can be found that the regular model (ClBDT-TBT-1)3 and (ClBDT-TBT-2)3 had larger molecular polarity and reduced exciton binding energy, but the irregular model (ClBDT-TBT-3)3 and (ClBDT-TBT-4)3 possessed decreased molecular dipole moment and maximized exciton binding energy. Low-regularity PClBDT-TBT not only effectively depressed strong aggregation and altered the molecular orientation from mixed-fashion of PClBDT-BT-based film to face-on, but also regulated the molecular crystallinity to improve the charge mobility. Furthermore, fs-TA absorption spectra demonstrated that the low regularity PClBDT-TBT:Y6 blend had a prolonged exciton diffusion time that suppressed recombination and ameliorated charge transfer. As a result, low regularity PClBDT-TBT:Y6-based device afforded a VOC of 0.81 V, JSC of 23.72 mA cm−2, and FF of 67.63%, and thus champion PCE of up to 13.04%. 135% Increased PCE relative to PClBDT-BT mainly benefited from remarkably increased JSC and FF even sacrificing VOC, proofed by desired predominant face-on orientation and higher and balanced charge mobility. In comparison with PClBDT-DTBT, 17% enhanced PCE was mostly attributed to collectively improved VOC and FF, evidenced by the descended EHOMO level and slightly enhanced crystallinity. Therefore, our work demonstrated that skillfully regulating the π bridge in D–π–A type CPs can effectively tune the molecular aggregation and photophysical process, and further promote photovoltaic performance.
