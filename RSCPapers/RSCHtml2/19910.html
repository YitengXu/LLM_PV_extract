Solution processed perovskite incorporated tandem photovoltaics: developments, manufacturing, and challenges

K. D. G. I. Jayawardena *, S. M. Silva and R. K. Misra
Advanced Technology Institute, Department of Electrical and Electronic Engineering, University of Surrey, Guildford, Surrey GU2 7XH, UK. E-mail: i.jayawardena@surrey.ac.uk

Received 20th March 2020 , Accepted 7th May 2020

First published on 1st June 2020

Abstract

Photovoltaics based on organic–inorganic as well as all inorganic semiconducting perovskites have emerged as a high performing technology at a lower cost. Intense research carried out in this regard over the last decade has resulted in single junction power conversion efficiencies that now exceed 25%. Furthermore, combining wide-bandgap perovskites with other narrow-bandgap absorbers such as silicon has enabled efficiencies exceeding 24%. Such tandem architectures provide the possibility of increasing the power conversion efficiency with either a little increase in cost or even at a lower cost. In this review, we discuss the emerging tandem concepts that incorporate perovskites in at least one sub-cell and can also be printed on roll-to-roll manufacturing lines. Initially, we discuss the progress in the field of perovskite/silicon tandem architectures followed by a discussion on perovskite/copper indium gallium selenide tandem devices. Then, recent progress in all-perovskite tandem devices is discussed. This is then followed by developments combining perovskites and organic bulk heterojunction absorbers. Subsequently, we discuss roll-to-roll and sheet-to-sheet printing techniques that can be used for scaled-up manufacturing of the above tandem architectures, which is then followed by reported work on perovskites that have utilised these printing techniques. Finally, we discuss prospects and future directions focusing on material stability and elimination of toxic solvents that are typically used in lab-scale perovskite solar cell fabrication processes and on less well investigated applications such as energy harvesting in space.

1. Introduction

Generation of energy through renewable sources and through sustainable means is one of the most important challenges humanity will have to overcome in the next few decades.1 Among the key technologies that can enable renewable energy generation, photovoltaic (PV) energy harvesting is gaining momentum as a major contributor. Currently, photovoltaic modules based on silicon stand by-and-far the widely deployed PV technology. However, there is still the need for developing PV technologies that enable high efficiencies at a low increase in cost. Furthermore, technologies at lower costs compared to the current technologies with low specific power output2 also open new market opportunities for non-grid based applications such as in building integrated photovoltaics,3etc.

Perovskite solar cells (PSCs), a thin film solar technology based on solution processable precursors, have rapidly emerged as both a competing and supporting technology for Si PVs. Based on the ABX3 formula (Fig. 1(a)), there have been a number of compositions for perovskites reported with the A-site consisting of either an organic cation such as methylammonium4 (MACH3NH3+) or formamidinium5 (FACH(NH2)2+), or an inorganic cation such as cesium6 (Cs+) or a combination of these,7,8 the B-site consisting of lead (Pb2+)7 or tin (Sn2+)9 or a mixture of these10–12 and the X-site consisting of halides, iodide (I−), bromide (Br−), or chloride (Cl−)13 or a combination of these.4,7,14 Since the first demonstration of perovskites as photovoltaic materials in 2009, the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of PSCs has seen a rapid increase from below 4%15 to 25.2%.16 Such impressive performances have been enabled due to the promising characteristics such as high optical absorption coefficients, long carrier diffusion lengths exceeding 1 μm,17 excellent defect tolerance18 as well as low exciton binding energies.19 Recent efforts have also seen developments in pathways for improved device stability.20,21 However, the performance of single junction PSCs is limited by the Shockley–Queisser limit of ∼33%.22 A potential route towards bypassing this limit is through the formation of tandem architectures which is enabled by the bandgap (EG) tunability23 of perovskite absorbers through compositional engineering of the halides14 or the B-site12 enabling the formation of both wide bandgap and narrow bandgap absorbers (Fig. 1(b) and (c)). This in turn has resulted in impressive developments not only in perovskite/Si tandem architectures with PCEs exceeding 29% (certified),16 but also in other perovskite incorporated tandem devices with efficiencies approaching 25%.12,16,24

As highlighted above, one of the key attractions of PSCs has been their solution processability. This can potentially enable high-throughput production based on roll-to-roll (R2R) and sheet-to-sheet (S2S) fabrication methodologies based on low cost, high throughput printing techniques such as slot die coating, ink jet printing, spray coating, and doctor blading. In fact, there are ongoing industrially driven research and development activities towards the realization of printed perovskite solar technologies from companies such as Panasonic,25 Saule Technologies,26 and more recently, Swift Solar27 to name a few.

With the developments in large area printed single junction perovskites, it is timely and important to consider the possibility of incorporation of such printed perovskite layers to manufacture tandem architectures. While the tandem device architectures can result in an increase in cost, this increase is often argued to be offset by the potentially higher PCEs possible. In fact, the possibility of developing low cost tandem architectures based on printed technologies was a key goal for organic photovoltaic (OPV) technologies towards realizing low cost renewable energy generation.28,29 With the advent of perovskites it is evident that the possibilities for such all printed tandem technologies have also dramatically increased. Some of the possible combinations in this regard include PSC (wide EG)/silicon (narrow EG) or PSC (wide EG)/copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS, narrow EG) for semi-printable approaches or PSC (wide EG)/PSC (narrow EG) or PSC (wide EG)/OPV (low EG) or even organic photovoltaics (OPV, wide EG)/PSC (low EG) combinations for all-printed approaches.

This review is structured as follows (Fig. 1(d)): initially, we will discuss the benefits of tandem architectures. This will be followed by developments in perovskite incorporated 2-terminal (2T) tandem technologies. Initially, we discuss recent developments in perovskite/silicon and perovskite CIGS tandem devices. This is followed by a discussion on potential all-solution processable 2T architectures which include all-perovskite tandem architectures, perovskite–OPV tandem architectures, and other emerging systems such as those based on quantum dots. We then discuss developments made in R2R and S2S printing techniques for printing perovskite absorbers. Finally, we outline the current challenges and future directions that can enable such printed, perovskite incorporated tandem architectures to be realized.

2. 2T tandem technologies

One of the principal bottlenecks of single junction technology is the thermalization losses31 (Fig. 2(a)) due to the absorption of photons with energies higher than EG, which restricts the maximum possible cell voltage to (EG − 0.3)/e V, where e is the electronic charge and the 0.3 eV term is due to fundamental thermodynamic losses.32,33 One potential route towards bypassing this limit is through combining sub-cells in a series connection31 (Fig. 2(b)) where the photocurrent generated in each cell is matched or balanced to minimize losses. This has in fact resulted in III–V multijunction cells consisting of six junctions with PCEs exceeding 39%.16

The theoretical maximum for all PV devices is estimated based on the detailed balance limit principle.32 For single junction devices, this often results in a theoretically possible maximum of 33% for a single junction cell which is increased to ∼46% for a 2T 2-junction (2J) (Fig. 2(c)) architecture. This can be further increased up to 52% for a triple junction (3J) architecture, and up to 68% for an infinite number of junctions.34 With regard to perovskites incorporated in tandem architectures, Hörantner et al.35 estimated the impact of fabrication of all perovskite tandem architectures based on the 2T-2J concept by combining absorbers with EG's of 0.9–1.35 eV and 1.5–2.0 eV which was shown to enable PCEs of ∼32% while a 2T-3J scheme combining sub-cells with EG's of 1.22 eV, 1.50–1.90 eV and 1.90–2.30 eV was shown to enable PCEs of 33.1% (Fig. 2(e) and (f)). It was noted that the appropriate matching of EBG's is crucial for obtaining the best PCE. In terms of combining perovskites with absorbers based on other semiconductors (e.g. OPVs), it is noted that appropriate losses that are over and above the losses typically observed for crystalline systems should be also taken into consideration. For example, in OPVs, the fundamental voltage loss during a large part of their early development was identified to be at 0.6 V due to the energy level alignments required for exciton dissociation. However with the recent resurgence in the field of development of new donor and acceptor systems, this loss has been reduced,36 thereby enabling the previously identified target of 15%29 for the 2T-2J architecture to be surpassed.37

Beyond the benefit of minimizing thermalization losses, the use of multijunction concepts can also enable the balancing of optical absorption with efficient charge extraction. For example, while increasing the thickness of an absorber is beneficial to enable a higher generation of carriers, this may not be matched with the diffusion lengths of the absorber material. Therefore, the stacking of the same absorber to form multijunction architectures is also seen as a favorable route towards minimizing losses.38 This has been implemented in both OPVs and all-perovskite architectures.

3. Perovskite incorporated tandem architectures

We now proceed to discuss tandem technologies that have been reported based on the incorporation of a perovskite absorber.

(a) Perovskite/silicon tandem photovoltaics

Among the photovoltaic materials that have been studied and developed so far, crystalline silicon (c-Si) has been the most successful in terms of commercial implementation. Consisting of a low EG of 1.1 eV, high PCEs exceeding 23% over large areas, non-toxicity and excellent stability, c-Si has been the benchmark for all emerging single junction PV technologies.16 As the efficiency of single junction cells approaches the theoretical limit for this material,39 there have been significant efforts to identify an appropriate wide EG absorber to form tandem architectures. Among the materials that enable the formation of wide EG sub-cells, perovskites have so far been the most promising due to the bandgap tunability over 1.6–1.8 eV through compositional engineering, low processing costs, and high efficiency.40,41 As a result, an industrial demonstrator with a PCE of 28% based on a 2T perovskite/silicon tandem cell has been reported by Oxford PV which was recently exceeded by a 2T perovskite/silicon architecture with a PCE of 29.2%.16 The latter is the first demonstrator where the efficiency of a single junction c-Si cell has been exceeded based on the incorporation of a printable wide EG cell. In terms of the c-Si cell, both homojunction and heterojunction architectures have been used in conjunction with a perovskite sub-cell. Si heterojunction technology (SHT) offers improved device performance and cost advanatges,42 although the former, which accounts for >80% of the market share for photovoltaics,43 enables processing at higher temperatures, enabling compatibility with the mesoporous perovskite architecture.44 In the following sections, we will first discuss developments based on homojunction Si technologies followed by developments using SHT. The first reported work on a 2T perovskite/silicon tandem cell based on a silicon homojunction architecture was by Mailoa et al.45 The perovskite absorber used was based on the MAPbI3 composition which was identified to possess an EG of 1.61 eV. Utilizing a mesoscopic architecture for the perovskite cell in combination with a c-Si cell resulted in a PCE of 13.7%. The low PCE observed was attributed to several issues including the use of a non-textured surface for fabrication of the perovskite sub-cell and lack of passivation. Indeed, the use of a non-textured surface has been a favoured approach for a majority of developments in perovskite/silicon tandem architectures due to the difficulties in achieving conformable perovskite coatings on textured surfaces. However, we note recent reports that identify routes towards the deposition of perovskites on textured surfaces.46,47 Following the above work, Werner et al. reported48 a tandem architecture comprising of a high temperature processed mesoscopic perovskite sub-cell together with a c-Si cell. A PCE of 16% was achieved, which is higher than that reported by Mailoa et al.45 This was enabled by incorporation of passivation strategies for the silicon sub-cell and also the use of better optimized recombination layers. Silicon oxide (SiOx) was used as a passivation layer on both surfaces of the n-type silicon wafer which enabled a higher Voc to be achieved compared to that reported by Mailoa et al.45 The hole-collecting emitter (through boron implantation) as well as the local contact and back surface field (BSF) layer (through phosphorus implantation) was formed through laser patterning. Improvements in PCE were also driven by optimization of the recombination layer thickness. This was also noted to influence the optical interference pattern in the Si cell which occurs due to the refractive index mismatch between the compact titanium oxide (c-TiO2) electron transport layer in the perovskite sub-cell and the zinc tin oxide (ZTO) recombination layer. As noted above, optimization of the optical coupling due to the influence of each layer used in tandem architectures is another key point for PCE gains. Further improvements in the PCE for the above work were noted to be limited by the interfacial resistance which limited the fill factor (FF) of the device. A significant improvement for perovskite/silicon tandem architectures incorporating a mesoscopic perovskite sub-cell was reported by Wu et al.49 where a PCE of 22.5% was obtained. The authors introduced several notable modifications. Firstly, passivation of the front and rear surface was carried out which also enabled optimization of the optical coupling. An alumina (Al2O3)/silicon nitride (SiNx) bilayer was used on the front surface for better matching of refractive indices and suppression of reflection losses while the rear SiNx layer enhanced light trapping (and as a result, the current). To minimize resistive losses due to the contact resistance between the indium tin oxide (ITO) recombination layer and the boron emitter, a factor which restricted the performance of the cells developed by Werner et al.,48 an array of Cr/Pd/Ag metal stacks was used for the contact openings. The authors also used a quadruple cation composition50 of the form Cs0.07Rb0.03FA0.765MA0.135PbI2.55Br0.45 which when processed with the vacuum flash assisted solution process (VASP) as reported by Bi et al.51 improved reproducibility.

In most of the above reports, the use of a relatively high conductivity metal oxide recombination contact has been a common factor. Zheng et al.52 demonstrated a tandem device based on a planar MAPbI3 cell where such a recombination contact was removed. The resulting devices demonstrated steady-state PCEs of 20.5% over 4 cm2 and 17.1% over 16 cm2. In place of the widely used TiO2 electron extraction contact for the perovskite, tin oxide (SnO2) based on a colloidal solution was used, enabling compatibility with printed manufacturing routes. Rear texturing of the bottom silicon cell enabled improved light trapping while the use of a metal grid together with the top ITO transparent contact also minimized sheet resistance losses, thereby improving device FF. Shen et al.43 developed a similar concept where c-TiO2 was deposited using atomic layer deposition (ALD) on both homojunction and passivating contact heterojunction cells which together with Cs0.05Rb0.05FA0.765MA0.135PbI2.55Br0.45 as the perovskite absorber resulted in stabilised PCEs of 22.8% and 24.1% respectively (Fig. 3(a) and (b)).

One of the limitations of perovskite incorporated tandem architectures has been the parasitic absorption losses due to 2,2′,7,7′-tetrakis[N,N-di(4-methoxyphenyl)amino]-9,9′-spirobifluorene (Spiro-OMeTAD), the most widely used hole transport layer in PSCs. To mitigate this issue, Zheng et al.53 used a textured polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) film that incorporated a down-shifting phosphor (Ba,Sr)2SiO4:Eu2+ in front of a monolithic perovskite/silicon tandem cell. The phosphor converted near UV light to visible light, thereby bypassing parasitic absorption losses in Spiro-OMeTAD. Based on the above approach, a further improved PCE of 23.1% on a 4 cm2 cell with a high FF of 81% was reported (Fig. 3(c)).

In the above reports, n-type silicon was widely adopted. However, c-Si cells based on p-type Si account for a large market share.54 Therefore, in this regard, it would be advantageous to develop perovskite/silicon tandem architectures based on p-type Si wafers. In this regard, Kim et al.55 reported design rules for planar monolithic perovskite/p-type Si tandem solar cells based on control of the thickness and EG of the perovskite absorber as well as the selection of an appropriate hole transport layer. Using a double cation (FAPbI3)0.8(MAPbBr3)0.2 composition, a PCE of 21.19% was achieved.

The first perovskite/silicon tandem architecture based on the SHT concept was reported in 2015 by Albrecht et al.56 The authors reported the use of atomic layer deposited SnO2 to form the electron extraction contact, enabling a stabilized PCE of 18.1% to be achieved together with a high Voc of 1.78 V. The use of ALD for SnO2 continues to be the preferred route for the formation of a metal oxide electron extraction layer in a majority of perovskite based tandem cells to date. Almost immediately following the above work, Werner et al.57 reported a perovskite/silicon tandem cell with an improved PCE in excess of 21%. The most notable feature of the work was the incorporation of an indium zinc oxide (IZO) window as the recombination layer together with a solution processed polyethyleneimine (ethoxylated) (PEIE)/phenyl-C61-butyric-acid-methyl-ester (PC61BM) bilayer for electron extraction from the perovskite sub-cell which facilitates low temperature processing. The authors also employed a two-step fabrication process for the MAPbI3 perovskite absorber with MAI being solution deposited on thermally evaporated PbI2, a route that shows promise for perovskite deposition on textured surfaces.46 Through optimization of the IZO thickness, they achieved a stable PCE of 21.2% over an aperture area of 0.17 cm2 and 19.2% over an aperture area of 1.22 cm2. One of the key issues in the above work by Werner et al.57 was the limited near infrared response of the Si sub-cell due to the non-textured cell used. This was reduced in a subsequent study by Werner et al.58 where a PCE of 20.5% was reported for an area of 1.43 cm2. The rear-side texturing on the bottom cell led to an enhancement of the photocurrent response of the Si sub-cell in the near-infrared region. Zhue et al.59 demonstrated the possibility of obtaining high efficiencies following careful optimization of the top MoO3 buffer layer and ITO transparent electrode stack. Through careful tuning of the thickness and the deposition conditions to reduce optical and electrical losses, they achieved a further improved PCE of 22.8%.

While most of the above reports on SHT based work used MAPbI3 as the absorber, this has a lower EG than is typically desired for tandem architectures. Based on this, Fan et al.60 investigated the impact of EG tuning in mixed A-site cation compositions by the use of mixed halides (iodine and bromine). In their work, MA0.37FA0.48Cs0.15PbI2.01Br0.99 with an EG of 1.69 eV was identified to be optimum. They fabricated a monolithic perovskite tandem solar cell with a steady state PCE of 18%. Qiu et al.61 followed a similar approach and identified MA0.38FA0.50Cs0.12PbI2.04Br0.96 as possessing an optimum EG of 1.69 eV for perovskite/silicon tandem solar cells. Through further optimization of the SnO2 electron transport layer thickness, the authors achieved a steady state PCE of 20.6%, similar to Fan et al.60

One of the key concerns in the above perovskite compositions has been the use of methylammonium which is typically regarded to introduce device instability under heating.62 This has led to the development of compositions consisting of Cs and FA.8,14 Based on this, Bush et al.63 developed Cs0.17FA0.83PbI0.83Br0.17 based perovskite/silicon tandem cells with a PCE of 23.6% over 1 cm2 featuring a rear textured SHJ. SnO2/ZTO deposited using ALD/pulsed-chemical vapour deposition enabled a hole blocking window layer with lower parasitic absorption. The photocurrent loss in the infrared region was reduced by using a Si nanoparticle incorporated rear reflector which allowed appropriate refractive index matching. Bush et al.64 later reported further improvements in PCE through reduction of the optical loss that was present in their previous work.63 This was achieved by optimizing the perovskite composition to achieve a wider EG, reducing front reflection losses by thinning of the ITO electrode and introduction of metal fingers to minimize sheet resistance losses due to the thinner ITO layer, and the incorporation of a polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) stamp with a random, pyramidal texture to further reduce front side reflection losses. They achieved 25% efficiency on a 1 cm2 cell.

In addition to its use in the rear electrode, nanocrystalline Si (nc-Si) has also been used in the recombination layer. For example, Sahli et al.65 utilized a hydrogenated nanocrystalline Si (nc-Si:H) layer, deposited by plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition (PECVD) at low temperature, as a recombination junction. This led to a decrease in the parasitic absorption and reflection losses leading to PCEs of 22%, 21.2% and 18% for active areas of 0.25, 1.43 and 12.96 cm2. Sahli et al.46 used a similar concept on the first perovskite/silicon tandem architecture where the perovskite was deposited on a textured silicon bottom cell (Fig. 3(d)–(f)). For this purpose, a hybrid two-step deposition method was used combining sequential co-evaporation of PbI2 and CsBr2 and spin coating of the FABr and FAI mixture to form the conformal perovskite absorber layer. A certified steady-state PCE of 25.2% over an aperture area of 1.419 cm2 was achieved based on the above architecture and processing. Nogay et al.54 adopted a similar approach with a p-type, textured Si based bottom cell where a steady-state PCE of 25.1% and a reverse scan PCE of 25.4% were achieved for devices with an active area of 1.42 cm2.

Other than nanocrystalline Si layers, optimized hydrogenated nanocrystalline silicon oxide (nc-SiOx:H) layers66 have also been reported as a suitable interlayer. Ren et al.67 improved the near infrared response of SHJ solar cells by using a wide band gap and low refractive index hydrogenated nanocrystalline silicon oxide (nc-SiOx:H) layer on a smooth c-Si wafer together with an n-type back surface field (BSF) layer resulting in a PCE of 20.1%. Mazzarella et al.68 also reported a monolithic perovskite/silicon cell which employed nc-SiOx:H as the interlayer between the silicon and perovskite sub-cells. An increased bottom cell current density due to reduction in reflection losses as a result of nc-SiOx:H enabled a certified PCE of 25.2% (stabilized). The use of nc-SiOx:H together with the incorporation of a textured light management (LM) foil on the front-side of a tandem solar cell processed on a planar front-side wafer consisting of a textured back-side was reported by Jošt et al.69 The resulting improvement in optical management led to an increase in PCE from 23.4% to 25.5% for tandem devices consisting of perovskites with Cs0.05(FA0.83MA0.17)0.95Pb1.1(I0.83Br0.17)3. Hou et al.70 utilized a nc-SiOx:H layer which together with a PDMS film as an antireflective foil resulted in improvement in PCE from 19.38% to 21.93%.

Processing of the perovskite layer is another key aspect that drives high performing perovskite/silicon tandem PVs. Zhu et al.71 reported the importance of optimization of dimethylformamide (DMF) and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) as a means of optimizing the properties of the perovskite layer, which in turn impacts light absorption and transmission within a tandem device structure. This approach was demonstrated for both FACs based and FAMACs based perovskite compositions, with the latter resulting in a PCE of 22.8%. Other than solvent engineering, the incorporation of additives is now rapidly becoming a preferred approach towards grain engineering and modification of morphology in perovskite absorbers. For example, Chen et al.72 incorporated methyl ammonium chloride (MACl) and MAH2PO2 into the perovskite precursor. These additives were shown to modify the crystallization process, resulting in a PCE of 25.4% for perovskite/silicon tandem devices. More recently, Xu et al.13 reported the incorporation of chlorine into a FACs based perovskite composition as a means of maintaining an EG of 1.67 eV (suitable for tandem architectures), while reducing the bromine content. While high bromine loadings enable wider bandgaps to be achieved, this often results in light induced halide segregation,73 leading to solar cell performance drift. Based on the above approach, the authors reported an impressive PCE of 27% over 1 cm2. However, it is noted that the excess chloride was added to the perovskite precursor in the form of MAPbCl3 and as of present, the role of the excess MA in the above device characteristics remains unclear. Mitigation of halide segregation has also been reported to be possible through solvent post-treatment. For example, Hou et al.47 reported a PCE of 25.7% via post treatment of the perovskite film with 1-butanethiol (Fig. 3(g) and (i)). The resulting self-limiting passivation layer formed on rough and micrometer thick perovskite surfaces was reported to increase the carrier diffusion length while mitigating phase segregation. More importantly, this work also reported the first example of an entirely solution processed perovskite layer on a textured Si surface (Fig. 3(h)). The shunting of devices which is usually anticipated when solution processed layers are deposited on textured surfaces with high peak-to-trough dimensions was avoided through the use of thick perovskite layers. Approaches to passivation of grain boundary recombination in tandem solar cells have also followed trends utilized in perovskite single junction cells. In this regard, Kim et al.74 reported the grain boundary passivation of a 1.68 eV perovskite absorber through the incorporation of phenethylammonium (PEA)-based two-dimensional (2D) additives together with a thiocyanate (SCN) which enabled a PCE of 26.7% to be achieved (Fig. 3(j)–(m)).

(b) Perovskite/CIGS tandem photovoltaics

Among the emerging thin film photovoltaic materials, I–III–VI type compound semiconductors consisting of copper, indium, gallium, sulphur and selenium are of significant interest. These materials, which consist of a chalcopyrite crystal structure, display a number of key advantages including high energy yield, low temperature coefficient of power loss, and low sensitivity to shadowing (i.e. good performance under low illumination intensities as well as short energy payback times).75 Presently, CIGS PVs have achieved a certified Power Conversion Efficiency (PCE) of 23.35 ± 0.5% over areas of 1.043 cm2 for lab scale devices while a certified PCE of 19.2 ± 0.5% over an aperture area of 841 cm2.76

One of the important optoelectronic properties of CIGS is the tunability of its EG,77 with EG in the range of 1.1–1.2 eV being the most widely developed. As a result, this allows the possibility of developing tandem solar cells when combined with absorbers with EG in the range of 1.7–1.8 eV as allowed by perovskites.14 Despite this, there has been very little activity in developing 2T perovskite/CIGS tandem architectures as evident from the following discussion (related device performances are given in Table 2).

The first example of a 2T perovskite/CIGS tandem device was reported by Todorov et al.78 The authors evaluated the impact of both MAPbI3 and MAPbBr3 as the perovskite on the overall performance of the tandem device. As the perovskite was observed to degrade when deposited on ZnO and annealed at 60 °C, the usage of an i-ZnO layer on the CIGS sub-cell was avoided. Furthermore, the perovskite sub-cell processing temperature was kept below 120 °C to avoid the degradation of the CIGS sub-cell due to the interdiffusion that takes place at the cadmium sulfide (CdS)/CIGS interface. While the CIGS sub-cell on its own demonstrated a PCE of 11.6%, the authors noted a drop in the PCE to 4.6% with a Jsc of 16 mA cm−2 when shadowed using an MAPbI3 sub-cell. On the other hand, a higher PCE of 7.7% with a Jsc of 25 mA cm−2 for the CIGS sub-cell was observed when shadowed with MAPbBr3. Based on the above, the authors developed a perovskite with an EG of 1.72 eV for the 2T tandem architecture. The perovskite was fabricated based on a vapour based halide exchange process where the existing halide was replaced by MABr vapour. Test devices formed using a 10–15 nm thick Al layer as the transparent conducting electrode (with 50% transmission) resulted in a PCE of ∼8%. The authors pointed out a number of issues in this structure including the high resistance of the perovskite sub-cell, the high ideality factor of the overall tandem cell and the low optical transmission of the Al. Replacing the Al with a thin layer of Ca enabled a higher optical transmission of 70–80% which led to a PCE of 10.98%.

The next example of a 2T perovskite/silicon tandem device was reported in 2018, possibly due to the difficulty in the fabrication of a perovskite cell on the highly textured CIGS which typically demonstrates a high σRMS of ∼50–200 μm.79 In their work reported in 2018, Han et al.80 utilised a boron doped ZnO (BZO) layer on top of the CIGS cell which minimised the σRMS to ∼60 nm although structural features with heights of ∼250 nm were still observed. In order to address this, the authors deposited a thick layer of ITO which was then polished using a chemical–mechanical polishing (CMP) method (Fig. 4(a)) which reduced the height of the maximum vertical features observed to ∼40 nm. However, the thickness of the ITO layer was such that the CMP processing did not polish the BZO layer. As BZO has a work function of ∼4 eV in comparison to the PTAA hole transport layer (work function of −5.1 eV) used in this work, the use of an ITO layer reduced the barrier potential for holes, thereby enabling a more effective recombination junction to be formed. Following the CMP process, the CIGS cell was reported to show a slight decrease in Jsc to 34.34 mA cm−2 from an initial value of 37.10 mA cm−2 for the unpolished cell. This was attributed to the parasitic absorption due to ITO over the wavelength region of 400–500 nm (the parasitic absorption of ITO does not affect the absorption over longer wavelengths). The FF was also reported to decrease from 74.7% (unpolished) to 72.4% (polished), which was attributed to the higher sheet resistance (Rsheet) of the polished ITO. As a result, the CMP polishing process resulted in a decrease of the PCE to 16.76% from an initial value of 18.73% for the unpolished cell. The authors also noted a Jsc loss of 1.386 mA cm−2 over the wavelength range of 750–1250 nm for the CIGS cell. For the perovskite sub-cell, the authors employed Cs0.09FA0.77MA0.14PbI2.58Br0.42 (with an EG of 1.59 eV) as the absorber. In order to mitigate the detrimental influence of the prevailing surface roughness of the polished ITO, the authors optimized the thickness of the PTAA layer in combination with dopants. Following the above, the authors reported the fabrication of a 2T tandem cell where a certified PCE of 22.43% was achieved over a cell area of 0.042 cm2 (Fig. 4(b)). The CIGS cell based on this structure showed photocurrent losses over the wavelength range of 800–1100 nm (Fig. 4(c)) due to reflection losses at the ITO/air interface at the top cell, which was reduced using a MgF2 antireflection coating, leading to a PCE of 20.8%. The stability of unencapsulated cells was observed by ageing the devices under ambient conditions for 500 h under 1 sun continuous illumination while maintaining the cells at 30 °C. The performance was observed to drop to a value of >88% from an initial PCE of 22%. However, this loss was observed to be recoverable following storage of the cell in the dark for 12 hours. The high stability of the unencapsulated device under ambient conditions was attributed to the use of ZnO and ITO which prevent moisture ingress.81

Despite the success of the above architecture developed by Han et al.,80 one of the shortcomings was the requirement of a thick ITO layer which can result in parasitic absorption losses. To alleviate this shortcoming Jošt et al.79 developed a bilayer HTL (Fig. 4(d)) for the perovskite based on a conformal NiOx coating deposited using ALD (Fig. 4(e)) and PTAA. While NiOx deposited using ALD requires annealing at 300 °C, this was avoided on the architecture reported as annealing at such high temperatures invariably results in interdiffusion at the CIGS/CdS interface. 2T tandem devices were based on the Cs0.05(FA0.83MA0.17)0.95Pb(I0.83Br0.17)3 composition reported by Saliba et al.7 Tandem devices based on the bilayer concept for the perovskite sub-cell showed a PCE of 21.6% with no hysteresis while devices that used only PTAA or NiOx showed lower PCEs of 3.1% and 18.2% respectively (Fig. 4(f)). External Quantum Efficiency (EQE) measurements carried out indicated a current mismatch of ∼2 mA cm−2 indicating room for further improvement. Additionally, a further Jsc loss of 2.62 mA cm−2 was identified due to reflection losses. Low parasitic absorption was observed between 400 and 1000 nm enabled by the highly transparent n-type contact. Parasitic absorption in the UV region dominated by absorption of C60 and IZO and also absorption loss in CIGS near its band edge were identified as additional sources of losses. In order to understand the losses in more detail, the authors fabricated single junction cells of CIGS consisting of a Ni/Al/Ni grid as the front contact which demonstrated a PCE of 16.3%. The authors noted alkali postdeposition treatment and light trapping schemes for improving Voc and Jsc respectively. Furthermore, based on the estimation of the quasi Fermi level splitting (QFLS) through hyperspectral PL imaging the authors noted that a Voc of 1.70 V should be possible on elimination of loss mechanisms.

More recently, Al-alshouri et al.24 reported the development of a new class of hole transport layers based on self-assembled monolayers (SAMs) for perovskites which enabled a stable, high performance for CIGS-LHP 2T tandem cells. The SAMs reported, (2-{3,6-bis[bis(4-methoxyphenyl)amino]-9H-carbazol-9-yl}ethyl)phosphonic acid (V1036), [2-(3,6-dimethoxy-9H-carbazol-9-yl)ethyl]phosphonic acid (MeO-2PACz) and [2-(9H-carbazol-9-yl)ethyl]phosphonic acid (2PACz), were based on carbazole derivatives that enable transportation of holes while allowing the localization of electrons. Additionally, the phosphonic acid groups were incorporated into these SAM materials which enables a self-limiting adhesion process to ITO. The authors evaluated the above hole transport layers in perovskite/CIGS tandem PVs, using the AZO/SAM (formed by dip coating) recombination junction. The resulting tandem devices showed an impressive certified PCE of 23.26% over an area of 1.03 cm2 which exceeded the PCE of 21.6% for perovskite/CIGS tandem devices incorporating the NiOx/PTAA bilayer.79 Notably, the bottom cell alone demonstrated a PCE of 15–16% while the Voc was improved compared to the previous work from 1.59 V79 to 1.68 V. However, the FF was reduced to 72% compared to 76% obtained for the NiO2/PTAA double layer.

The development of perovskite/CIGS tandem architectures has also been expanded to the fabrication of flexible 2T tandem architectures where the MAPbI3 perovskite sub-cell was processed on a CIGS cell that had been fabricated on a polyimide foil.82 A thin PTAA layer that was doped with 1 wt% F4TCNQ was used as the hole transport layer for the perovskite sub-cell. Unlike the work reported by Jošt et al.,79 the authors reported the formation of a conformal coating of PTAA on the somewhat rough CIGS cell. A Ni–Al metal grid was also utilized on the AZO top transparent contact to enable better charge collection, while a MgF2 anti-reflection coating was incorporated to reduce reflectance losses. A steady state PCE of 13.2% was reported with very little Voc loss indicating the formation of a good recombination junction. However, the overall performance was limited by the sub-optimal photocurrent generation in the MAPbI3 layer due to its thickness of <300 nm which does not enable all the incident photons above its EG to be absorbed. Additionally, a lower charge collection efficiency was observed for the LHP in the tandem configuration compared to its single junction variant indicating a collection loss. One possible source for this loss was attributed to the small grain size which is lower than the film thickness. Interestingly, despite the potential for such perovskite/CIGS flexible devices with improved performances, especially in light of recent developments in multi-cation perovskite absorbers, there has been very little work published so far in this regard.

Beyond terrestrial applications, Lang et al.83 evaluated perovskite/CIGS tandem PVs for space-based energy harvesting (Fig. 4(g)). The resulting devices retained 85% of their initial performance, even after irradiation under 68 MeV protons with a cumulative dose of ∼1012 protons per cm2 (Fig. 4(h) and (i)). The degradation in performance was attributed to the increase in carrier recombination in the CIGS sub-cell and the NiOx layer used in the bilayer hole transport system used for the perovskite sub-cell. In comparison, the PCE of perovskite/silicon tandem devices was observed to decrease to 1% of their initial value under similar irradiation conditions. The high radiation stability of the perovskite is in agreement with the application of perovskites for direct conversion radiation detection84,85 and points towards a new field of application for these tandem devices.

(c) All perovskite tandem photovoltaics

As mentioned previously, one of the key advantages of perovskite semiconductors is their bandgap tunability. While the development of wide EG has attracted significant interest, there is now a growing activity in terms of developing narrow EG perovskites through the partial or complete replacement of Pb2+ with Sn2+. The slow development of this technology has been mainly due to the undesirable oxidation of Sn2+ to Sn4+, even when processed under glove box conditions. However, recent activities as discussed later on have identified routes to mitigate this issue to some extent, enabling high performing single junction low band EG perovskite devices as well as all-perovskite tandem architectures to be developed, hinting at the possibility for an all printed perovskite PV technology. Due to the early development in EG tunability in lead-only PSCs, 2T tandem architectures incorporating Pb-only perovskites were the first reported all-perovskite 2T tandem architecture. For this, Heo et al.87 used a top cell made of MAPbBr3 (EG = 2.25 eV) and a bottom cell made of MAPbI3 (EG = 1.55 eV) where the tandem cell was fabricated by simply sandwiching the two substrates containing the top and bottom cells together by pressing with a double clip and subsequent drying. Based on this simple methodology, they were able to fabricate a monolithic tandem cell with a PCE of 10.8% and a Voc of 1.95 V. One of the biggest challenges faced in the fabrication of a 2T tandem PV is the optimization of the charge recombination junction or the interconnecting layers (ICLs) between the top and bottom cells so as to (a) avoid voltage losses, (b) act as a barrier that prevents the dissolution of the bottom cell when depositing the top cell and (c) minimise optical losses. In the above work by Heo et al.87 the clipping of the cell prevented the dissolution of one cell. However, in terms of manufacturability and performance optimisation, it would be preferable for such an approach to be avoided. The first bottom-up fabricated 2T all-perovskite tandem PV was demonstrated by Jiang et al.88 where a solution processed charge recombination layer made of Spiro-OMeTAD/poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene)–poly(styrene sulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS)/polyethyleneimine (PEI)/(PC61BM):PEI was used to serially bridge the top and bottom cells. By using these recombination layers and top and bottom cells made of MAPbI3, they were able to achieve a PCE of 7% and a Voc of 1.89 V, with the Voc being almost equal to the sum of Voc's of the two sub-cells.

Forgács et al.89 later reported a 2T tandem architecture based on the double cation Cs0.15FA0.85Pb(I0.3Br0.7)3 as the wide bandgap absorber (with EG = 2 eV) and MAPbI3 as the low bandgap absorber (EG = 1.55 eV). In their work, N4,N4,N4′′,N4′′-tetra([1,1′-biphenyl]-4-yl)-[1,1′:4′,1′′-terphenyl]-4,4′′-diamine (TaTm) doped with 2,2′-(perfluoronaphthalene-2,6-diylidene) dimalononitrile (F6-TCNNQ) and C60 doped with N1,N4-bis(tri-p-tolylphosphoranylidene)benzene-1,4-diamine (PhIm) were used as the interconnecting layer (ICL). The resulting devices demonstrated a champion PCE of 18.1% with an impressive Voc of 2.1 V, most likely due to the improved optoelectronic characteristics in the dual cation 2 eV wide EG cell used.

One of the major milestones in the field of all-perovskite 2T tandem architectures was reported by Eperon et al.90 where they combined a wide bandgap FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I0.5Br0.5)3 (EG = 1.8 eV) with a narrow bandgap sub-cell consisting of the Pb–Sn mixed perovskite system FA0.75Cs0.25Pb0.5Sn0.5I3 (EG = 1.22 eV) to form tandem architectures. The resulting monolithic devices also employed a recombination junction consisting of SnO2 and ZTO together with sputtered indium tin oxide (ITO) which acted as a barrier that protects the underlying perovskite from any solvent damage that may occur during subsequent processing. The resulting 2T tandem device demonstrated an impressive PCE of 17% enabled by a high Voc > 1.65 V. The authors noted the impressive stability of the Pb–Sn mixed composition reported here, where the device stability was identified to be on par with that of Pb-only devices with no reduction in performance when held at the maximum power-point (MPP) in an inert (nitrogen) environment for more than 18 hours. Following this, Leijten et al.91 reported a 2T tandem architecture where a wide EG Cs0.4FA0.6Pb(I0.7Br0.3)3 was combined with the FA0.75Cs0.25Pb0.5Sn0.5I3 low EG absorber enabling a PCE of 19.1% to be achieved (Fig. 5(a) and (b)). The improvement in PCE was achieved through better current matching between the two sub-cells and the post deposition treatment of the narrow bandgap absorber with methylammonium chloride (MACl) of the low bandgap absorber, which improved perovskite crystallization and increased the grain size, ultimately leading to an increase in Voc and FF.

Rajagopal et al.92 developed a tandem architecture based on MAPb0.5Sn0.5I3 as the low bandgap absorber (with EG = 1.2 eV) and MA0.9Cs0.1Pb(I0.6Br0.4)3 as the wide bandgap absorber (with EG = 1.8 eV). Among the key developments of this work were (a) the identification of the indene-C60 bis-adduct as a more matching electron transport layer (ETL) for the Pb–Sn mixed absorber and (b) the development of an ICL based on Bis-C60/ITO/PEDOT:PSS which enabled a high Voc of 1.98 V which is 80% of the value predicted by the Shockley–Queisser limit resulting in a PCE of 18.5%.

The next notable improvement in 2T all perovskite tandem solar cells was reported by Zhao et al.93 In their work, they utilised a composition of (FASnI3)0.6(MAPI3−3xCl3x)0.4 for the low bandgap cell (EG = 1.25 eV). The incorporation of chloride ions, similar to Leijten et al.,91 resulted in an increase in the grain size which is compatible with the film thickness of ∼750 nm, as well as a reduction in the trap density. This low bandgap cell when combined with a wider bandgap absorber based on the FA0.8Cs0.2Pb(I0.7Br0.3)3 composition (EBG = 1.75 eV) through a Ag(1 nm)/MoO3(3 nm)/ITO(120 nm) ICL led to the first demonstration of a PCE exceeding 20% (20.6%) for all-perovskite 2T tandem architectures (Fig. 5(c)). These cells also demonstrated an impressive stability with 80% of their initial PCE being maintained after 80 hours of continuous illumination under AM1.5G conditions in air without encapsulation while the cells retained 94% of their initial PCE after 45 days of storage in ambient air (Fig. 5(d)).

Subsequent to this, there have been several rapid developments within a short span that have enabled further improvements in 2T tandem architectures consisting of a wide bandgap Pb only cell in combination with a narrow bandgap Pb–Sn mixed sub-cell. Among the developments made are processing optimisation for thicker Pb–Sn mixed perovskite sub-cells and reduction of the background carrier density. For example, Tong et al.94 reported the incorporation of guanidinium thiocyanate into (FASnI3)0.6(MAPbI3)0.4 which enabled a significant improvement in the carrier lifetimes to >1 μs and also the reduction of the background carrier concentration by an order of magnitude, enabling the fabrication of thick absorber layers for increased light harvesting, especially in the near-infrared portion. This low bandgap cell when coupled with an EG = 1.63 eV wideband gap sub-cell with a composition of Cs0.05FA0.80MA0.15PbI2.55Br0.45 enabled a champion PCE of 18%. By changing the wide bandgap absorber to a perovskite with EG = 1.75 eV, a significantly improved PCE of 23.4% was obtained while more than 88% of the initial PCE was maintained under continuous AM1.5G illumination for 100 hours. These devices demonstrated an impressive FF exceeding 80% as well as a Voc of 1.942 V.

While the above activities enabled PCE improvements in all-perovskite tandem devices, the oxidation of Sn2+ (the preferred oxidation state for PSCs) to Sn4+ remains a challenge with approaches such as the inclusion of SnF2, use of appropriate anti-solvent washing and use of appropriate annealing temperatures11 being utilised to mitigate this issue. Recently, Lin et al.12 outlined a comproportionation process where Sn powder is added to the precursor mix enabling the reduction of Sn4+ to Sn2+ thereby minimising the detrimental impact of Sn2+ oxidation. Based on this method, a single junction PSC based on the FA0.7MA0.3Pb0.5Sn0.5I3 absorber achieved a PCE of 21%, while combining this with a wide bandgap Cs0.2FA0.8PbI1.8Br1.2 absorber to form a monolithic 2T tandem solar cell (Fig. 5(e)) resulted in a certified PCE of 24.8% for an aperture area of 0.073 cm2 (Fig. 5(f)) and 22.3% for an aperture area of 1.03 cm2. These are the highest reported for a 2T all perovskite tandem solar cell as of present. These devices also demonstrated an impressive stability where 90% of their initial performance was maintained after 463 hours of operation (Fig. 5(g)).

Perhaps one of the first demonstrations of fabrication of all perovskite tandem solar cells fabricated on flexible substrates was reported by Palmstrom et al.95 In their work, ALD was used to grow the recombination layers of the monolithic tandem cell in order to reduce shunting and damage caused to the underlying layers due to solvents used in subsequent processing of the tandem cell. Hence, an ALD grown aluminium doped zinc oxide (AZO) layer was grown on top of C60 using an ultra-thin (1 nm) nucleation layer in the form of poly(ethylenimine) ethoxylated (PEIE). However, they also discovered that a thin layer of transparent conductive oxide (TCO) is required to prevent FF losses due to damage caused to the AZO layer by acidic PEDOT:PSS. 2T tandem architectures were fabricated on a flexible IZO coated polyethylene naphthalate (PEN) substrate with the configuration of PEN/sputtered IZO/poly-TPD/PFN/FA0.6Cs0.3DMA0.1PbI2.4Br0.6/LiF/C60/PEIE/AZO/IZO/PEDOTT:PSS/FA0.75Cs0.25Sn0.5Pb0.5I3/C60/BCP/Au which resulted in a PCE of 21.3% (with 23.1% on a glass substrate) with a Voc of 1.82 V and a FF of 75%. The devices also maintained more than 95% of their initial performance after 100 bend cycles (bend radius of 1.5 cm). Furthermore, no degradation was observed when continuously illuminated under AM1.5G for 13 hours as well as when stored in the dark at 85 °C for 50 h.

In terms of other developments with regard to appropriate ICL layers, Chang et al.86 developed an ICL based on a cross-linkable p-doped PTAA layer and PCBM. As the cross-linking agent, 1,2-bis[4-(azidomethyl)phenyl]-1,2-diphenylethene (TPE-MN3) was employed while molybdenum tris-[1-(trifluoroethanoyl)-2-(trifluoro-methyl)ethane-1,2-dithiolene] (Mo(tfdCOCF3)3) was incorporated as the p-type dopant. The incorporation of TPE-MN3 enabled cross linking of PTAA under UV irradiation, resulting in a dense blocking layer that has better solvent-resistance, thereby protecting the underlying perovskite absorber when processing the top perovskite absorber. The devices based on a regular architecture (as opposed to the inverted architecture widely used for all perovskite 2T tandem structures) which combines MAPbI3 as the wide bandgap absorber and MAPb0.75Sn0.25I3 as the low bandgap absorber resulted in a champion PCE of 18.69%, with a stabilized PCE of 18.92%. Most notably, the Voc of the tandem architecture (1.79 V) was exceptionally close to the sum of the Voc's of individual sub-cells (1.80 V) indicating the effectiveness of the ICL developed.

Going beyond the widely explored 2T-2J concept, McMeekin et al.96 demonstrated a 2T-3J (triple junction) architecture based on 3 perovskite light absorption layers, namely, FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I0.3Br0.7)3 (EG = 1.94 eV) in the top cell, MAPbI3 (EG = 1.57 eV) in the middle cell and MAPb0.75Sn0.25I3 (EG = 1.34 eV) in the bottom. While optical modelling showed that the cell had the potential of achieving a PCE of around 26.7% and a corresponding Voc of 3.16 V, the fabricated device only showed a PCE of 6.7% and a Voc of 2.7 V. Despite the measured values being significantly lower than the anticipated values, it can be envisioned that significant improvements in performance are possible following the optimisation of interconnecting layers to reduce parasitic absorption, etc. This work so far remains the only reported work on 2T-3J all-perovskite tandem devices.

(d) Perovskite/organic tandem photovoltaics

Organic BHJ PVs are perhaps one of the earliest PV technologies to adopt printing technologies. Despite this, perovskite–organic bulk heterojunction (BHJ) tandem devices are a relatively less well explored concept compared to the more widely investigated perovskite incorporated tandem architectures which employ either Si, copper indium gallium selenide or even a perovskite as the low bandgap absorber. While the initial tandem device concepts were based on combining two BHJ absorbers, the field was somewhat restricted in terms of the possible performance due to voltage losses as high as 0.6 V100,101 and the lack of efficient infrared absorbers; recent developments in the field102,103 has resulted in the development of high performing single junction architectures with absorption up to ∼1000 nm104,105 while the voltage losses have now been significantly reduced.101,106 This has led to significant improvements in single junction device efficiencies which now exceed 16%.107,108 As a result, there is now growing interest in the formation of tandem architectures by combining organic BHJs with perovskites as discussed below. In terms of the perovskite/organic BHJ 2T tandem architecture, the first work in this regard was reported by Chen et al.109 In their work, the authors used MAPbI3 as the wide EG absorber while a BHJ consisting of PBSeDTEG8 (EG = 1.28 eV) as the donor and PC61BM as the acceptor was used as the narrow EG absorber. For the perovskite absorber, the authors reported the use of an additive assisted anti-solvent deposition method which enabled the formation of smooth pin-hole free absorber layers which resulted in a PCE of 9.1% for single junction PSCs. The use of the PBSeDTEG8 block copolymer-based donor was found to be beneficial in enabling better thermal stability for the BHJ absorber. Furthermore, this absorber did not necessitate the use of 1,8-diiodooctane (DIO), a commonly used additive in high performing organic BHJ devices, which can also result in poor shelf-life.110 The single junction organic BHJ sub-cell resulted in a PCE of 7%. Compared to the conventional 2T tandem architectures where the incident light first passes through the wide EG absorber, this work employed an architecture where the light first passes through the narrow EG BHJ absorber, a concept previously reported111 for all organic BHJ tandem architectures. However, it is noted that such a device architecture is likely to result in undesirable absorption from the narrow EG absorber and result in sub-optimal performance. By employing an ICL consisting of poly[(9,9-bis(3′-(N,N-dimethylamino)propyl)-2,7-fluorene)-alt-2,7-(9,9-dioctylfluorene)] (PFN)/PEDOT:PSS (high conductivity, PH1000)/PEDOT:PSS (low conductivity, PVP AI 4083), the authors successfully demonstrated a 2T tandem device with a PCE of 10.2%.

Following this, Liu et al. reported112 the development of a 2T perovskite–organic tandem device where the efforts were focused on the development of an ICL based on the stacking of poly[(9,9-bis(3′-(N,N-dimethylamino)propyl)-2,7-fluorene)-alt-2,7-(9,9-dioctylfluorene)] (PFN)/Al or Ag doped MoO3 (step-layer)/MoO3. The authors noted an improvement in Voc upon the incorporation of the step-layer. For example, when doped with Al, the Voc was improved from 1.38 V to 1.45 V while this was further improved to 1.55 V upon using Ag as the dopant under optimized doping conditions. Furthermore, the use of dopants was also reported to result in the elimination of an S-shaped J–V characteristic for the tandem device which is indicative of a more balanced charge transport through the device. Following a 2T tandem device architecture incorporating MAPbI3−xClx as the wide bandgap absorber and poly{2,6′-4,8-di(5-ethylhexylthienyl)benzo[1,2-b;3,4-b]dithiophene-alt-5,5′-dibutyloctyl-3,6-bis(5-thiophen-2-yl)pyrrolo[3,4-c]pyrrole-1,4-dione} (PBDTT-DPP):[6,6]-phenyl C71 butyric acid methyl ester (PC71BM) as the narrow bandgap absorber, a PCE of 8.62% was achieved based on further optimization of the individual layers. In the above, the removal of S-shaped J–V characteristics following the inclusion of Ag as a dopant indicates the need for careful optimization of individual layers as well as the recombination junction to achieve balanced currents from individual sub-cells.

A significantly improved PCE of 16% for perovskite-BHJ 2T tandem devices was subsequently reported by Liu et al.113 In this work, the authors used the MAPbI3 absorber processed using the acetate route114 on a heated substrate and poly([2,6′-4,8-di(5-ethylhexylthienyl)benzo[1,2-b;3,3-b]dithiophene]{3-fluoro-2[(2-ethylhexyl)carbonyl]thieno[3,4-b]thiophenediyl}) (referred to as PCE10 or PTB7-Th or PBDTTT-EFT or PBDTT-FTTE):PC71BM as the BHJ based absorber (Fig. 6(a)–(c)). We note several key developments that enabled the high PCE for this 2T tandem architecture. Firstly, an ICL based on tris(sulfobetaine)-substituted fulleropyrrolidine (C60-SB)/ultra-thin Ag/MoO3 was used. The C60-SB layer processed using 2,2,2-trifluoroethanol as the solvent does not dissolve the underlying layers in the perovskite sub-cell and in combination with the ultrathin Ag and MoO3 layers acts as a barrier against solvent induced dissolution of the perovskite sub-cell when processing the BHJ sub-cell. Additionally, based on Kelvin Probe Force Microscopy (KPFM) analysis, a favourable potential gradient was identified enabling electron extraction from the perovskite sub-cell into the C60-SB layer and the extraction of holes from the BHJ sub-cell into the MoO3 layer. While the PCE10:PC71BM sub-cell utilized DIO, the detrimental impact of this additive110 was eliminated through vacuum based removal of DIO in the BHJ layer. Finally, the thickness of the perovskite absorber was tuned in the range of 70–160 nm (significantly lower than that used for single junction PSCs) while maintaining a constant thickness of 100 nm for the PCE10:PC71BM layer enabling an improvement in Voc and Jsc to be achieved.

Zeng et al.115 utilized the concept of an all inorganic perovskite absorber coupled with a BHJ to form a 2T tandem device. In their work, the CsPbI2Br perovskite sub-cell was coupled with the high performing ternary BHJ system of PTB7-Th:2,2′-[[4,4,11,11-tetrakis(4-hexylphenyl)-4,11-dihydrothieno[2′,3′:4,5]thieno[2,3-d]thieno[2′′′′,3′′′′:4′′′,5′′′]thieno[2′′′,3′′′:4′′,5′′]pyrano[2′′,3′′:4′,5′]thieno[2′,3′:4,5]thieno[3,2-b]pyran-2,9-diyl]bis[methylidyne(5,6-difluoro) (or COi8DFIC):PC71BM, a BHJ system used to demonstrate the highest all-organic 2T tandem architecture reported so far.37 Using an ICL of MoO3/Au/ZnO, a PCE of 15.04% was reported with the overall performance being limited due to low current in the perovskite sub-cell. However, considering recent developments in all-inorganic perovskite single junction devices,6,116,117 further improvements in the above tandem architectures can be anticipated.

Compared to the above device architectures where an ICL layer is incorporated in forming a 2T tandem architecture, there have also been reports on an “integrated” concept where the ICL layer is eliminated (Fig. 6(d)). Not only does this enable complementary absorption to be incorporated during the design, but it also eliminates the need for complicated processing for the deposition of an ICL, thereby simplifying the overall stack. Although this device architecture is perhaps not a tandem configuration as per the conventional usage of the term, we nevertheless discuss important developments in this area as it presents an opportunity to enhance the spectral response and perhaps improve tandem architectures based on Pb-only compositions.

The first reported work in this regard was by Liu et al.118 where a small molecule organic BHJ based on DOR3T-TBDT:PC71BM was spin coated on an MAPbI3−xClx perovskite absorber. Compared to semiconducting polymers, small molecule systems have been identified as an alternative that minimises performance variations due to factors such as the broad distribution in molecular weight as prevalent in semiconducting polymers.119,120 The device developed Liu et al.118 resulted in an overall device PCE of 14.3% (Fig. 6(e)). As the absorption of the BHJ used overlaps with the perovskite absorber thereby making it difficult to identify the exact influence of the above architecture, the authors utilised a low bandgap BHJ architecture based on PBDTT–SeDPP (the selenised form of PBDTT-DPP):PC71BM as the BHJ which also demonstrated a PCE of 12% (Fig. 6(f)). In the above architecture, the authors attributed the observed performance as being due to the donor phase in the BHJ acting as a hole transport layer for the perovskite while the perovskite acts as an electron accepting system for the charges generated in the BHJ. The charge generation within the BHJ was identified to be important in terms of the overall device performance as a lower PCE was observed when only the donor polymer was used.

Following this, Kim et al.121 reported a perovskite and organic ternary BHJ based integrated device with a PCE of 16.36% as compared to an efficiency of 14.7% for perovskite cells without a BHJ. The ternary BHJ was based on a blend consisting of the low bandgap polymer DT-PDPP2T-TT(TT) as the donor and poly{[N,N′-bis(2-octyldodecyl)-naphthalene-1,4,5,8-bis(dicarboximide)-2,6-diyl]-alt-5,5′-(2,2′-bithiophene)} (N2200) as well as PC71BM as the acceptors. The authors also fabricated flexible integrated devices, taking advantage of the low temperature processing steps involved in this study, resulting in a champion PCE of 12%.

Cheng et al.122 utilised a small molecule BHJ architecture with a (FAPbI3)0.85(MAPbBr3)0.15 perovskite absorber. Compared to semiconducting polymers, small molecule systems have been identified as an alternative that minimises performance variations due to factors such as the broad distribution in molecular weight as prevalent in semiconducting polymers.119 The authors evaluated several small molecule systems designed based on the acceptor–donor–acceptor (A–D–A) concept where the phenoxazine flanked benzo[1,2b:4,5b′]-dithiophene backbone was used for all molecules, but capped with different electron-withdrawing units: N-ethylrhodanine (for molecule M1), N-(2-ethylhexyl)pyridinium (for molecule M3), and (2-(3-cyano-5,5-dimethylfuran-2(5H)-ylidene)-malononitrile) (for molecule M4) which enabled the adjustment of the energy levels. While PSCs were tested through incorporation of the above small molecules as the HTL, an increase in Jsc was observed when PC71BM was incorporated into the organic material to form a BHJ. A champion PCE of 17.3% in reverse (average PCE of 16.2%) was achieved for the M3:PC71BM BHJ incorporated device.

Gao et al.105 reported the performance of integrated devices with MAPbI3 as the perovskite and porphyrin donor based bulk heterojunction layer consisting of 5,15-bis(2,5-bis((2-ethylhexyl)thieno)-3,6-dithienyl-2-yl-2,5-dihydro-pyrrolo[3,4-c]pyrrole-1,4-dione-5′-yl-ethynyl)-10,20-bis(5-(2-ethylhexyl)thienyl)porphyrin zinc(II) (DPPZnP-TSEH):PC61BM. Similar to the above Guo et al. reported123 the use of an organic BHJ blend based on PBDTTT-E-T (PTB7):2,2′-((2Z,2′Z)-((5,5′-(4,4,9,9-tetrakis(4-hexylphenyl)-4,9-dihydro-s-indaceno[1,2-b:5,6-b′]dithiophene-2,7-diyl)bis(4-((2-ethylhexyl)-oxy)thiophene-5,2-diyl))bis(methanylylidene))bis(3-oxo-2,3-dihydro-1H-indene-2,1-diylidene))dimalononitrile which enabled an extended absorption up to 930 nm and an external quantum efficiency of 50% in the near-infrared range. This BHJ in combination with MAPbI3 resulted in a high Jsc of 24.77 mA cm−2, although the PCE was 14.57% (lower than the perovskite only device) due to a low FF. More recently, a ternary BHJ consisting of two donors Y6 and S1 together with PCBM to enable absorption up to 980 nm (Fig. 6(g) and (h))124 was coupled with a triple cation perovskite CsFAMA resulting in a champion PCE of 20.61%, mainly due to the high Jsc of 28 mA cm−2 as a result of the extension of absorption (Fig. 6(i)). Both these reports indicate the possibility of the concept of spectral enhancement in Pb-only perovskite PVs by incorporation of newly emerging NIR absorbing organic semiconductors.

(e) Other perovskite based tandem concepts

Beyond the above-mentioned solution processed tandem architectures, there have also been other concepts reported that are not as widely investigated as those discussed above. For example, Karani et al.125 investigated theoretically as well as experimentally the potential of coupling a MAPbI3 perovskite absorber together with lead sulfide (PbS) colloidal quantum dots (CQDs) as the low bandgap absorber. They identified the possibility of achieving a PCE of 43% with a CQD absorber with EG = 1 eV if radiative coupling between the two sub-cells is considered while a maximum PCE of 37% was identified to be possible with a CQD absorber with EG = 0.8 eV. They further fabricated a tandem architecture consisting of PbS CQDs with EG = 1.03 eV in combination with an MoO3/(Au or Ag) tunnel junction between the two sub-cells. However, a rather low PCE of 1.01% was reported.

An alternative to the use of 3D perovskite absorbers, processed based on the previously discussed routes, involves the use of perovskite quantum dots. While the initial performances of these quantum dots remained low (i.e. <10%), developments in the modification of quantum dot–quantum dot junctions126 and ligand exchange for compositional stability127 have enabled PCEs in excess of 16% for perovskite CQD PVs. Furthermore, the dimensionalities of such CQDs also enable the formation of more stable inorganic perovskite absorbers128 in comparison to the conventional synthesis routes reported where rapid degradation of the inorganic absorber is often observed. In this regard, Christians et al.129 investigated the potential of utilizing CsPbI3 CQDs for tandem architectures. However, a demonstration of perovskite CQDs has so far not been reported and remains an interesting area for future developments.

4. Approaches to manufacturability

4.1 Limitations of spin coated processing

Historically, spin coating has remained the preferred method for fabrication of small area, lab scale, solution processed photovoltaic devices where substrate sizes typically do not exceed 2.5 cm × 2.5 cm. Indeed, in all the work discussed above, the fabrication of the perovskite absorber layer has relied on spin coating. Spin coating can be carried out via static or dynamic dispensing where the ink is deposited on the substrate followed by the spin process in the former, whereas the ink is dispensed on an already spinning substrate in the latter. The resulting centrifugal force results in a thin wet film. In situations where <100 nm thick films are required, this often results in relatively dry films. On the other hand, during the fabrication of thicker films, the spin coating process often results in a semi-dry film which is often dried under thermal annealing to form the desired micro or nanostructure.130

For perovskites, spin coating has been the widely adopted methodology in terms of fabricating high-performing devices. In terms of single cation systems such as MAPbI3, this often involves the careful control of the precursor composition (e.g. the acetate route)114,131 and the ambient conditions (e.g. the chloride route)4,132 which enables formulations that in principle can be adopted for scale-up coating techniques. However, in terms of the overall compositional space enabled by perovskites, the anti-solvent route133 which enables uniform nucleation and crystallization has been the more widely preferred methodology for perovskite fabrication. While the anti-solvent route was first implemented for MAPbI3133 to enable the fabrication of pin-hole free absorber layers, this was adopted early-on for the multi-cation7 systems which have demonstrated better stability, higher efficiency, and a larger processing window in glove box environments.

In industry (and specially the microelectronics industry), spin coating is somewhat implemented over wafer scales where wafer diameters approach 12′′. However, this often leads to a large wastage in terms of the solutions/inks used which is not attractive in terms of developing low-cost photovoltaic technologies. Furthermore, spin coating is somewhat restrictive in its throughput as it does not enable a high production rate that is targeted for low cost photovoltaic technologies. We do note however that there have been instances where the spin coating process has been used for the fabrication of lab scale modules with areas of ∼100 cm2.134 Alternative technologies that enable the coating of perovskites and indeed solution processed tandem architectures include blade coating, slot die coating, spray coating and ink jet printing (Fig. 7).

With regard to scaling up and manufacturability, Galagan et al.135 highlighted the following key points which should be addressed to enable manufacturability of perovskites on a R2R or S2S system:

(1) Solvent choice enabling control of viscosity, boiling point, toxicity, and price.

(2) Controlled crystallization enabling pin-hole free films.

(3) Uniformity of layer properties.

(4) Performance.

In achieving the above key goals, one of the critical points which require careful attention is the development of appropriate crystallization routes that can be implemented on a R2R or S2S system that will facilitate the formation of pinhole-free films.

4.2 Enabling compact pinhole free films

As indicated above, in the scaling up of the perovskite coating process, one of the key challenges lies in the fabrication of a dense, pinhole free film. In this regard, the most attractive starting point in terms of forming a dense film is through the modification of the precursor composition. For example, while precursors consisting of PbI2 and MAI are perhaps the most widely used for fabrication of MAPbI3, replacing PbI2 with Pb(Ac)2 (lead acetate under the acetate route) has enabled compact pinhole free perovskite solar cells to be formed.114 Similarly, replacing PbI2 entirely with PbCl24 in combination with appropriate control of ambient humidity,132 or incorporating it with Pb(Ac)2131 also enables the formation of compact films.

An alternate route to enabling heterogeneous nucleation in perovskites involves the deposition of the “semi-wet” perovskite film in a bath of the antisolvent136 that is typically dripped during the anti-solvent based spin coating method. However, it has been highlighted that the processing window that enables the incorporation of the above is fairly limited in the conventionally used DMF:DMSO solvent mixtures. This led Yang et al.136 to modify the original solvents used in the perovskite precursor to incorporate 1-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) which slowed down the drying dynamics enabling an anti-solvent bath to be used to enable heterogeneous nucleation and crystallization to proceed.

From an overall scaling point-of-view, it would still be attractive to eliminate the need for an anti-solvent step. One potential route towards achieving this is through coating of the material on a heated substrate.137,138 In addition to the use of acetates or chlorides highlighted above, the incorporation of additives into the precursor is an attractive route towards enabling compact films. This has already enabled the demonstration of modules incorporating additives139 such as didodecyldimethylammonium bromide, polyethylene glycol sorbitan monostearate, and sodium dodecyl sulphate. Additionally, gas quenching methodology136,140 which was first implemented for spin coating methods in place of anti-solvent treatment is a route that enables homogeneous nucleation during R2R or S2S coating of perovskites. In this process, the blowing of a gas (often N2) results in speeding up of the (precursor) solvent evaporation that leads to rapid saturation and precipitation of nucleation species which enables the formation of a dense pin-hole free film. This technique has already been adapted in R2R and S2S coating techniques for perovskites where the use of an air-knife141 facilitates the desired saturation, precipitation and crystallisation to take place, enabling dense films.

Blade coating has been a widely used technique in organic photovoltaics in terms of developing small area devices148 as well as for demonstrating scaling up of printed photovoltaics.29,149 However, as discussed previously, appropriate modifications need to be implemented with regard to either the precursor or the coating process to enable the formation of a compact film.

Among the methodologies utilized for obtaining a compact absorber layer based on perovskites, blade coating has involved the use of substrate heating as a means of both solvent removal and initiation of perovskite crystallisation. For example, Wu et al.150 reported a module efficiency of ∼11% achieved through blade coating of a precursor mixture consisting of MAI and PbI2 in DMF on a heated substrate (temperature varied from 60 to 160 °C) which resulted in a PCE of ∼11%. This work was subsequently modified by Peng et al.151 where the solvent used in the precursor was modified to a DMF:DMSO solvent mix and through the incorporation of excess MAI the formation of a more optimized absorber was enabled resulting in an average PCE of 10.16% (champion PCE of 10.92%) for an aperture area of 0.09 cm2.

With regard to the impact of the substrate temperature on the formation of perovskites during blade coating, Zhong et al.152 reported insights into the crystallisation dynamics of perovskites under different substrate temperatures based on in situ grazing incidence wide-angle X-ray scattering (GIWAXS) studies (Fig. 8(a)–(d)). For a precursor solution consisting of MAI and PbI2 in DMF, they observed the formation of a highly disordered and heavily solvated film for periods exceeding 200 s when the precursor is blade coated at room temperature. Increasing the substrate temperature resulted in a faster transition from the highly disordered and solvated film condition to a more ordered condition. For example, at 80 °C, this transition was observed to take place at ∼13 s. When the temperature was further increased, the time for this transition was even further reduced (e.g. to ∼2.4 s at 135 °C). The resulting device performances also demonstrated a strong correlation to the transition from a highly disordered state to a more ordered state where the PCE increased with increasing substrate temperature.

Li et al.153 utilised a combination of solvents in the precursor and investigated the impact of the blade coating temperature on the conversion of the precursors in forming the final perovskite absorber layer. Using GIWAXS studies, it was observed that film formation by blade coating of the perovskite precursor in a solvent mixture of DMSO:gamma butyrolactone (GBL) at room temperature, followed a similar route to that of spin coated samples without an anti-solvent. However, due to the slow solvent drying process, the resulting film formation also proceeded at longer time scales which is not an attractive feature in terms of high-throughput manufacturing. However, when the blade coating temperature was increased to 150 °C, a direct transition from a disordered sol–gel phase to a crystalline phase was observed, enabling higher film quality. Optimization of the above conditions enabled a champion PCE in excess of 18% for devices with an active area of 0.09 cm2 while a PCE of 17.06% was observed for 1 cm2 cells. In addition to the above, the blade coating speed has a significant impact on the formation of compact films. For example He et al.154 reported high efficiencies of 19.81% for (FAPbI3)0.85(MAPbBr3)0.15 over active areas of 0.1 cm2 and 18.02 cm2 for aperture areas of 0.98 cm2 by employing a slow coating speed in combination with high boiling point solvents. However, the use of a slow coating speed is less preferable in terms of high production rates. At higher blade coating speeds (>20 mm s−1), Deng et al.139 noted the need for a surfactant that would minimize microscale fluid flow that results in the formation of islands (Fig. 8(e)). In their work, surfactants such as didodecyldimethylammonium bromide, polyethylene glycol sorbitan monostearate, and sodium dodecyl sulphate were incorporated into the MAPbI3 perovskite precursor which enabled the formation of a dense film at high coating speeds of 50 mm s−1. This enabled PCEs of 20.3% for small area cells (0.075 cm2) and 14.6% for a module with an aperture area of 57.2 cm2 (Fig. 8(f) and (g)). It should be noted that in the case of forming a dense perovskite film, the hydrophilic/hydrophobic nature of the underlying layer also plays a critical role. For example, modification of PEDOT:PSS used as an HTL in blade coated devices by incorporating poly(4-styrenesulfonic acid) into the mixture was reported to enable scalable coating of MAPbI3 based on using PbCl2 as the Pb2+ source, resulting in an average PCE of 8.29% and a champion PCE of 10.15%. It is noted that in the case of the above, the authors utilized DIO in perovskite precursors as well as a high coating speed between 2.5 and 10 cm s−1, although substrate heating was not used.

Beyond the more widely adopted approaches discussed above, we note that increasing the processing window (Fig. 8(h)) to enable anti-solvent bath deposition as a means of uniform crystallization has been reported for blade coated samples. For example, Yang et al.136 utilised a solvent mix of DMF and NMP which enabled the blade coating of perovskite precursors consisting of MAI, PbI2 and PbCl2 followed by dipping in a bath of diethyl ether. This enabled stabilized efficiencies >19% for 0.12 cm2 cells while a PCE of >17% was achieved for 1.2 cm2 cells. The technique was further extended to a small area module with an active area of 11.09 cm2 which resulted in a PCE of 13.3% (Fig. 8(i) and (j)). We note that somewhat of a similar approach was adopted by Eperon et al.90 in fabricating the low band gap Pb–Sn mixed absorber, although the film was fabricated through spin coating. However, careful control as well as optimization of humidity and temperature is important depending on the precursor used (e.g. PbCl2 in the perovskite precursor mix) as has been demonstrated for spin coated132 as well as blade coated155 PSCs.

(b) Slot-die coating. Slot-die coating can be considered as being somewhat similar to the blade coating method discussed above, with the exception of a continuous ink flow which enables R2R coating as opposed to S2S coating (only). A reservoir containing the ink is fed to the slot-die head which is applied to the substrates through a thin slit (shim) (Fig. 7(b)). The ink viscosity, precursor concentration,156 and the relative speed between the blade and the substrate are used to control the thickness of the final film while a heated bed is often preferred to enable uniform drying on a macroscopic scale. Slot die coating is a technique that has been widely adopted in the field of organic photovoltaic development activities focusing on high throughput R2R manufacturing.157–159

As per the case of blade coating of perovskites, precursor solutions can be coated either at room temperature or on substrates held at elevated temperatures, while an air knife can be used in order to control the crystallization during the coating process (Fig. 9(a)), enabling R2R fabrication on flexible substrates (Fig. 9(b)). Furthermore, this technique is compatible with either the single-step (where all the components of the perovskite are mixed in a single precursor) or the two-step (where the lead precursors and the organohalide precursors are prepared in separate solvents and are sequentially coated) process. For example, Qin et al.160 utilized a two-step process involving the slot die coating of PbI2 in DMF at temperatures between 25 and 30 °C followed by dipping the coated substrate in a bath of MAI. The work resulted in PCEs of 11.7% and 14.75% over active areas of 0.1 cm2 for devices with slot die coated Spiro-OMeTAD and 2,2′,7,7′-tetrakis(N,N-di-p-methoxyphenyl)amine-9,9′-bifluorenylidene (Bifluo-OMeTAD) respectively.

In comparison to the 2-step approach, the single step coating is perhaps more favoured, especially in terms of potentially reducing the processing time on a R2R manufacturing process. For example, Cotella et al.141 adopted a single step coating process for a perovskite based on MAI and PbCl2 in DMF. The precursor was coated on preheated substrates held at 20, 65 or 90 °C which was achieved by placing the substrate in an oven for 30 min prior to coating. The optimum coverage was observed following preheating at 65 °C while combining this with a cold air knife enabled the formation of a smoother film. Devices optimized in this manner resulted in an average PCE of 7% and a champion PCE of 9.2% for an aperture area of 0.0625 cm2. A similar approach was reported by Ciro et al.,161 who reported the fabrication of flexible perovskite solar cells through coating of a precursor mix similar to that used by Cotella et al.141 on a heated ITO/polyethylene terephthalate (PET) substrate. The formation of a compact perovskite film was observed to depend on both substrate temperature and precursor concentration; an increased concentration at low temperatures results in the reduction of pinhole formation while increasing the temperature at all concentrations studied enabled the formation of compact films. Following further optimization of the PC61BM coating (used as the electron transport layer) thickness, a champion PCE of 2.91% was achieved.

Giacomo et al.162 used a modified precursor consisting of PbCl2 and Pb(Ac)2 as the lead sources and MAI in DMF for coating over 6′′ × 6′′ substrates (Fig. 9(c)–(e)). For devices with an active area of 0.09 cm2 tested with an aperture area of 0.04 cm2, an average PCE of 16.4% was achieved. Lee163et al. utilized a similar precursor mix as that of Giacomo et al.,162 but studied the influence of the ratio between the Pb2+ sources. A gas blowing method was implemented while the slot die coating was carried out under ambient temperature. The authors focused on the lower end of PbCl2 loading as the increase in PbCl2 results in longer annealing times. The incorporation of PbCl2 was noted to be beneficial in terms of increasing the perovskite grain sizes. In terms of devices, a champion PCE of 13.3% was reported for Pb(Ac)2:PbCl2 of 8:2. The use of both lead sources enabled pinhole free film formation as opposed to Pb(Ac)2 only. In order to demonstrate the possibility of scaling up, the authors investigated the performance of a module with an area of 10 cm2 which enabled a champion PCE of 8.3%.

Kim et al.164 combined the gas blowing technique and substrate heating to improve slot die coated device performance. For a precursor consisting of MAI:PbI2 mix in DMF, a needle like structure formation was observed under gas blowing (only), while inclusion of substrate heating enabled more dense film formation. The authors also investigated the incorporation of additives DMSO, ammonium chloride (NH4Cl) and N-cyclohexyl-2-pyrrolidone (CHP) where the latter two enabled better film quality for slot die coated films. The authors evaluated the use of P3HT and Bifluo-OMeTAD as HTLs in combination with different substrate heating temperatures for the perovskites where the best performance was observed for Bifluo-OMeTAD resulting in a champion PCE of 12.73%. The latter aspect also indicates the importance of optimization of the transport layer materials to improve PCE for printed PSCs.

In terms of manufacturing, there is a strong need for elimination of toxic solvents such as DMF as highlighted by Galagan et al.135 In their work, a solvent mix consisting of DMSO and 2-butoxyethanol (2BE) was utilised where the latter solvent enabled better wetting of the precursor, formation of a uniform perovskite layer and acceleration of crystallization. When evaluated using precursors based on MAI and PbI2, needle-like structures were obtained when a slow heat up ramp was followed in the slot die coating process while a mixture of plate-like large crystals and some needle-like features were obtained when the temperature ramping rate was increased. To obtain better crystallization, the perovskite composition was changed to Cs0.15FA0.85PbI2.85Br0.15. Using the same solvent composition as above, plate-like structures were obtained under slow as well as fast temperature ramping rates (following the precursor coating) which enabled PCEs of 15.2% to be obtained for active areas of 0.09 cm2 on an ITO/PET substrate (Fig. 9(f) and (g)). When a similar device was fabricated using DMF as the solvent on glass using spin coating, a PCE of 15.8% was obtained while 13.5% was obtained for the DMSO:2BE solvent mix. This difference in performance between the spin coated samples and the slot die coated samples was attributed to the sheet resistance of ITO in the PET/ITO substrates used for the slot die coated samples.

(c) Spray coating. Spray coating is another technique that can be used for the scaling up of perovskite photovoltaic coatings for R2R or S2S fabrication. Under the spray coating method, a pressurized gas system or an ultrasound head is used to break down the precursor into droplets which are deposited on the substrate (Fig. 7(c)) which can be either heated or at room temperature.165 The resulting semi-dry films can then be further processed either under vacuum166 or in a solvent bath167 followed by thermal annealing168,169 to enable the formation of the final layer.

In terms of spray coated perovskite devices, one of the earliest reported studies was by Barrows et al.,169 who used ultrasonic spray coating to deposit MAPbI1−xClx. The coverage of the perovskite layer was observed to be dependent on the substrate temperature where ∼85 ± 5% coverage was achieved at a substrate temperature of 75 °C. Furthermore, the authors reported that a lower post annealing temperature required a long crystallization time while annealing temperatures above 110 °C resulted in a de-wetting effect (Fig. 10(a)–(f)). An average thickness of ∼340 nm was observed to be ideal for the spray coated perovskite solar cell performance resulting in an average PCE of 7.8% and a champion PCE of 11.1%. Das et al.170 reported the use of ultrasonic spray coating of precursors based on MAI and PbCl2 in DMF on glass and ITO/PET substrates. Substrate heating was employed to enable rapid drying of the films. The authors reported an optimum substrate temperature of ∼75 °C, similar to that reported by Barrows et al. Devices fabricated on glass substrates resulted in a champion PCE of 11.4 ± 0.4%. Following this, the authors translated the process to a flexible ITO/PET substrate where high density infrared pulses were used to cure the TiO2 layer which resulted in a significantly poor performance of 0.4%.

Mohamad et al.168 demonstrated a spray coated perovskite solar cell based on MAPbI1−xClx where the HTL and ETL (PEDOT:PSS and PCBM) were also spray coated resulting in an average PCE of 7.1% and a champion PCE of 9.9% over an aperture area of 0.025 cm2 while cells where only the perovskite absorber was spray coated enabled an average PCE of 9.8% and a champion PCE of 11.1%. Also, humidity was observed to have an impact on the performance where an increase in humidity led to a reduction of PCE.

Heo et al.171 developed a unique approach for spray coating of perovskite films by balancing out the outgoing solvent flux with the sprayed perovskite precursor enabling a longer spray coating window. MAI and PbCl2 were first reacted in IPA which were then purified and dissolved in a DMF/GBL solvent mix. Balancing of the DMF:GBL ratio was identified to be crucial in enabling the balancing of the outgoing solvent flux with the spray coated precursor mix. This resulted in spray coated cells with a PCE of 18.03% over 0.16 cm2. They further demonstrated the possibility of fabricating a spray coated module with an active area of 40 cm2 resulting in a PCE of 15.5%.

Bishop et al.142 modified the previously mentioned process reported by Mohamed et al.168 to enable spray coating of the compact TiO2, mp-TiO2, the MAPbI1−xClx absorber and spiro-OMeTAD. Hydroiodic acid was used as an additive to enable better solubility of PbCl2 in DMF while also promoting better surface coverage. The work enabled an average PCE of 9.2% over a 4 mm2 pixel (aperture area of 0.0256 cm2) while cells with an active area of 1.51 cm2 enabled an average PCE of 6.59 cm2 (Fig. 10(f)–(h)).

An alternate route towards the fabrication of perovskite absorbers is based on the 2-step method.172,173 For example, Huang et al.173 developed a 2-step spray coating process to fabricate MAPbI3 based solar cells. Initially, PbI2 in DMF was spray coated followed by spray coating of MAI in 2-propanol (IPA). The temperature was identified to be a key parameter as too-high temperature(s) did not enable the infiltration of MAI into the dense PbI2 layer while a too-low temperature resulted in residual DMSO. The optimized coating process resulted in a PCE of 13.09% for a 1 cm2 pixel.

In comparison to the organic–inorganic hybrid perovskite spray coating reported in the above work, Lau et al.174 developed spray coated all inorganic perovskite solar cells, an important composition in terms of forming thermally stable perovskites.116 In that work, a “semi” spray coated method was adopted where CsI was spray coated on a spin coated PbBr2 film enabling the formation of an all inorganic perovskite absorber. The substrate temperature was identified to have a crucial impact on perovskite formation with substrates kept at room temperature enabling the desired perovskite phase while heating of the substrate to either 160 °C or 350 °C resulted in the formation of an undesirable CsI phase. The best PCE of 5.4% was achieved for the room temperature spray coated samples while PCE <3% was achieved for those prepared using heated substrates.

The use of spray coating also enabled the development of rapid compositional screening. For example, Tait et al.175 utilised concurrent spray coating of mixtures of PbCl2:MAI and Pb(Ac)2:MAI in DMF on substrates held at a temperature of 40–60 °C (40 °C being identified as the optimum). Based on the above method, the optimum PbCl2:Pb(Ac)2 ratio was identified to be 3:1 which facilitated low pinhole formation as well as good photovoltaic device performance resulting in a PCE of 13.7% for device areas of 0.13 cm2 and a PCE of 10.4% for module areas of 3.8 cm2. The authors also investigated concurrent spray coating of PbCl2:MAI and PbBr2:MABr to enable bandgap tuning which resulted in a PCE of 6.8% for a perovskite with an EBG of 1.8 eV. A more traditional approach towards compositional screening was reported by Chou et al.,176 who reported the spray coating of MA1−xFAxPbI3, where x = 0, 0.25, 0.50, 0.75 and 1. The optimum performance was realized for x = 0.25 which resulted in a champion PCE of 15.61% when the precursor was spray coated on a heated substrate kept at 70 °C. The work highlights the role of spray coating techniques as a means of compositional optimization of perovskites, an area that is receiving significant attention in terms of utilizing machine learning approaches.177,178

Cai et al.179 utilised hot air blowing during the fabrication of spray coated cells. In their work, this methodology was reported to result in better reproducibility of MAPbI1−xClx (in DMF) based PSCs resulting in a PCE of 13.5% (reverse scan) for an aperture area of 0.13 cm2 and 9.8% for an active area of 1 cm2.

With regard to the drying of the spray coated films, an alternate approach to the conventionally used thermal annealing process involves vacuum drying of the spray coated samples that is followed by thermal annealing,166 analogous to VASP reported by Bi et al.51 Based on this approach, Bishop et al.166 spray coated the triple cation perovskite Cs0.05[(FAPbI3)0.85(MAPbBr3)0.15]0.95 in a DMF:DMSO = 4:1 solvent mix onto a substrate held at 40 °C followed by a vacuum drying and subsequent thermal annealing process which enabled a higher Voc compared to samples that did not undergo the vacuum drying process. The resulting devices showed an average PCE of 17.8% in the reverse direction with more spatially homogeneous optoelectronic characteristics compared to non-vacuum dried films (Fig. 10(i)–(k)).

(d) Ink jet printing. In terms of printed solar cells, ink jet printing is another attractive technology that bridges the gap between lab scale evaluation and manufacturing through R2R or S2S processes. In fact, perovskite based solar cells fabricated through ink-jet printing (Fig. 7(d)) are already being evaluated as window facades.180 Ink jet printing is based on 2 approaches: continuous printing and drop-on-demand printing146 (Fig. 11(a) and (b)). In continuous printing, the ink is forced through a nozzle where it results in a column of droplets due to the surface tension. The droplets then pass through a nozzle where a charge is imparted to them. The direction of printing is steered by deflection plates which steer the direction of the droplets. In the drop-on-demand printing method, a pressure pulse is generated in the head either through a piezoelectric transducer or through rapid heating of the ink which results in the formation of a bubble at the surface of a heater. The resulting pressure pulse drives the ink through the nozzle resulting in the refilling of the ink. It is noted here that ink jet printing has so far produced some of the best performing large area perovskite modules with the collaboration between Panasonic and Japan's New Energy and Industrial Technology Development Organization (NEDO) reporting25 a PCE of 16.09% for an aperture area of 802 cm2 (Fig. 11(c)) while Saule Technologies, a company that is developing ink jet printed perovskite solar cells, has tested a module on Skanska's Spark office in Warsaw.181

As in the case with many other manufacturing techniques, in inkjet printing of perovskites also, the substrate temperature plays a key role in terms of forming a compact perovskite film. For example, Li et al.182 demonstrated the inkjet printing of a perovskite layer consisting of MAI:PbI2:MACl = 1 − x:1:x in GBL where substrate heating was implemented to enable rapid ramping of the temperature from 25 °C to as high as 60 °C within a couple of minutes which was identified to facilitate homogeneous crystallization, similar to that of anti-solvent treatment8,11,12,133 and hot casting techniques.137,138 A temperature of 50 °C together with a MAI:MACl ratio of 4:6 (i.e. x = 0.6) was identified as being the optimum condition to achieve the best performance of 12.3% where MACl was identified to result in homogeneous crystallization of the perovskite.

Mathies et al.183 utilised inkjet printing for the coating of MAPbI3 based on a solvent system consisting of DMSO and GBL which enabled slow solvent evaporation, thereby resulting in the formation of smooth, thin films. Optimum performances were observed to be obtained following the incorporation of a vacuum annealing step over a duration of 2 or 5 min, which resulted in the best performances of ∼4.5% and 4.3% for a single coating (i.e. perovskite thickness of ∼180 nm). However, the performance was observed to decrease if vacuum annealing was carried out for shorter or longer periods. Applying three coatings to enable a thickness of ∼480 nm in combination with vacuum annealing resulted in a higher PCE of 11.3% for a device area of 0.09 cm2 (Fig. 11(d)–(f)). Following this initial work on MAPbI3,183 Mathies et al. reported184 the use of a triple cation perovskite C0.10(FA0.83MA0.17)0.90Pb(I0.83Br0.17)3 in combination with the vacuum annealing process optimized at 2 min. The drop spacing was altered from 60 μm to 25 μm enabling the increase in thickness from 180 nm to 780 nm. A champion PCE of 15.3% (Fig. 11(g)) was obtained for a thickness of 520 nm. The triple cation printed devices also demonstrated an improved thermal stability compared to MAPbI3 with only 10% loss in PCE when kept at 80 °C for 100 min.

Liang et al.185 reported the printing of MAPbI3 precursors prepared in a DMSO:GBL mix to print perovskite films in combination with vacuum annealing. They achieved PCEs of 17.04% for 0.04 cm2 and 13.27% for 4 cm2. The improvement in performance was enabled by deposition of a 5 nm thick C60 layer on the TiO2 ETL where the C60 coating enabled larger grains to be formed as well as an increased recombination resistance.

Gheno et al.186 evaluated the importance of the halide composition on methylammonium based perovskites in conjunction with the impact of printing on metal oxide films (TiO2 compact or mesoporous films (mp-TiO2), or tungsten oxide (WOx) films). Four perovskite compositions were evaluated: MAPbI3, MAPbI3−xClx, MAPbI3−xClx with DIO as an additive and MAPbI3−xBrx. ITO substrates stored in air enabled a more hydrophilic surface for printing MAPbI3−xClx while WOx in nitrogen enabled a similar outcome. This work enabled the identification of the importance of controlling the overall perovskite concentration such that the jettability is not impacted, although it should be noted that it is likely that additives such as 5-ammonium valeric acid iodide (5-AVAI) would also reduce the clogging of the printing head as reported by Hashmi et al.187 The influence on jettability is most likely due to the presence of colloids in the perovskite precursors.188 The authors evaluated the performance of a PSC incorporating printed WOx as an ETL.186 A champion PCE of 10.7% under reverse scanning was achieved, although a large hysteresis was also observed.

Huckaba et al.189 reported inkjet printed PSCs where both the perovskite and mp-TiO2 were inkjet printed. In order to minimize the coffee-ring effect when drying, a solvent combination of DMF/DMSO/GBL/NMP was used together with phosphatidylcholine as a surfactant. The perovskite composition followed a quadruple cation system based on Cs0.10Gu0.05(FA0.83 MA0.17)0.85PbI2.63Br0.37. In contrast to the above conditions, the authors dipped the coated film in an anti-solvent bath of diethyl ether to enable homogeneous crystallization. Decreasing the drop spacing during inkjet printing enabled the formation of a compact, thick films. A champion PCE of 14.1% was obtained with an average cell efficiency of 12.15% for devices with an area of 0.16 cm2 which was much higher than that obtained for inkjet printed MAPbI3 cells.

Hashmi et al.187 utilised inkjet printing for fabrication of the perovskite absorber and the insulating ZrO2 spacer layer on a porous carbon layer to fabricate HTL free solar cells (Fig. 11(h)). The precursor used consisted of a mix of MAPbI3 and 5-AVAI which has previously been reported190 to result in one of the most stable device architectures. While 5-AVAI enables slow crystallization, it also prevents the clogging of the inkjet head, enabling optimized devices with PCEs of 8.47% (in the reverse for an aperture area of 0.16 cm2) to be achieved. Initial investigations on device stability where the cells were kept under illumination at 35 °C for 1046 h showed very little decrease in performance. When stored in the dark for 3 weeks, the performance was observed to improve by 13% to a value of 9.53% for the champion cell (Fig. 11(i)). This work was later expanded191 to identify the stability of the cells under UV irradiation where the devices when encapsulated with an epoxy showed no performance degradation under UV irradiation equivalent to 1.5 sun illumination conditions over 1002 hours.

An interesting approach in terms of “colouring” of perovskite solar cells was reported by Schlisske et al.,192 who incorporated ink jet printed luminescent down shifting layers (mixtures of Violet 570, Yellow 083, and Red 300 Lumogen-F series, BASF in polymethylmethacrylate) together with a solar cell consisting of ink-jet printed perovskite layer Cs0.10(FA0.83 MA0.17)0.90Pb(I0.83Br0.17)3. However, the PCE was observed to decrease from 11.5% to 9.4% following the incorporation of the down-shifting layers. Such an approach is likely to be attractive in terms of developing perovskites for building integrated PVs where variation in colours is preferred for aesthetic purposes.

Inkjet printing has also been used for rapid compositional screening of perovskites. For example, Bag et al.193 used in situ mixing capabilities of multichannel ink-jet printers to explore the compositional space based performance of ink-jet printed perovskite solar cells where the CMYK (cyan magenta yellow black) channels consisted of MA+, FA+, water, water. By variation of the CMYK values, different MA:FA combinations were evaluated. The resulting printed devices indicated the best performance to be obtained from MA:FA = 2:1 resulting in a champion device performance of 11.1%. As noted previously for similar activities discussed under spray coating, inkjet printed compositional screening provides an avenue that supports machine learning based perovskite composition optimization activities.

5. Prospects and future directions

One of the key issues in the development of perovskite absorbers has been the inherent instability of perovskites under certain compositions. For example, MAPbI3, the fruit-fly of perovskite research, has been identified to demonstrate inherent instability under high temperature (>85 °C) conditions which can result in the conversion of CH3NH3I into CH3I and NH3.8 As MAPbI3 often requires annealing temperatures closer to 100 °C, there is a need for screening compositions which minimise or eliminate this risk.

As discussed in this review, one of the key attractions in the development of PSCs has been the bandgap tunability of perovskites, which enables the formation of low bandgap absorbers based on Pb–Sn mixed perovskites. However, the oxidation of Sn2+ (the preferred oxidation state for PSCs) to Sn4+ remains a key challenge, especially considering that it would be preferable to carry out the printing of low bandgap perovskites under ambient conditions, which accelerates the oxidation of Sn2+. In terms of minimising oxidation, a number of routes have been developed such as the inclusion of SnF2,195 use of appropriate anti-solvent washing such as with toluene (Fig. 12(a)) and use of appropriate annealing temperatures11 as well as the inclusion of tin powder12 (Fig. 12(b)) in the precursor mix. In terms of R2R or S2S printing capability, we do not foresee any significant barrier towards utilising the above methodologies. However, more in-depth studies are required in this regard to ensure that stable, low bandgap absorbers can indeed be printed on high throughput manufacturing systems.

Another significant impediment towards the high throughput manufacturing of perovskite based tandem structures is the use of toxic solvents such as DMF, chlorobenzene (CB), dichlorobenzene (DCB), etc.7,10,12 For example, solvents such as DMF are noted for their harmful nature (when in contact with skin).196 Similar obstacles have been highlighted in the manufacturing of organic photovoltaics which use solvents such as CB or DCB in lab scale device optimization. This has in fact resulted in studies for the optimization of this technology based on green solvents.197,198 With regard to perovskites, there have been demonstrations towards the development of precursors based on more environmentally friendly solvents. For example, Gardner et al.199 have discussed several environmentally friendly solvent combinations for the perovskite precursor while Galagan et al.135 have demonstrated slot die coated examples of perovskites where DMF has been eliminated. Noel et al.200 (Fig. 12(c)) have also reported clean, low boiling point solvents that can enable the fabrication of perovskites in a manner that can be implemented on a manufacturing line. This approach was more recently reported201 to be utilised for the development of slot die coating in conjunction with an air knife for the fabrication of perovskites on ITO/PET which demonstrated a champion PCE of 14.7%. However, there remains significant room for further improvement that will require future activities on lab-scale processing to focus on the promotion of environmentally friendly manufacturing methodologies to ensure that the promise of perovskites as a printed PV technology is realised.

In terms of future applications beyond contribution to the grid, it is anticipated that perovskites will be integrated into building components with early developments already being reported. While a current limitation lies in terms of its aesthetics (i.e. colour of the module), the approach reported by Schlisske et al.192 is likely to enable this issue to be bypassed. Other than this, perovskite-based tandem architectures are also likely to lead to space-based energy harvesting applications. The reported high radiation tolerance is likely to make this technology a favourable candidate. This could also be considered as a new application space for all-perovskite tandem solar cells where module degradation due to oxygen and moisture is of less concern. Additionally, the satisfactory performance of perovskites under concentrator applications202 can also lead to broadening of the device configurations developed in this area.
