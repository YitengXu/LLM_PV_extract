Improving photoanodes to obtain highly efficient dye-sensitized solar cells: a brief review

Abstract

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) feature low cost, stability, and environment friendliness and are thus a promising substitute for traditional silicon solar cells. DSSCs have received intensive research attention and have been rapidly developing in the last two decades. The efficiency of DSSCs should be increased to promote their commercialization and large-scale application. This brief review summarizes the major progress in advanced nano/micromaterials to improve photoanodes and enhance the conversion efficiencies of DSSCs. Commonly used methods to improve photoanodes include semiconductor film nanoarchitecture, light-scattering material application, compositing, doping, interfacial engineering, and TiCl4 post-treatment. This review provides insights into DSSC improvement and development of other photovoltaics, such as perovskite solar cells and photoelectrochemical cells.

1. Introduction

Energy demand is increasing dramatically with the development of human society. Fossil fuels, such as raw oil, natural gas, and coals, are the major energy sources used to meet our current need. However, these sources are non-renewable, and their increasing consumption rate contributes to exhaustion of fossil fuels. The use of fossil fuels has led to serious environmental issues, such as CO2 emission, global warming, and air pollution. Therefore, scholars have focused on identifying renewable, abundant, and clean energy sources as substitutes to traditional fossil fuels.

Solar energy is one of the most promising alternatives to fossil fuels. Sunlight seems endless and clean; conversion of 0.1% of the sunlight reaching the Earth surface by using photovoltaics, with a conversion efficiency of 10%, can generate sufficient energy to meet our current demands.1 Thus, photovoltaic development is critical to future energy generation.

Solar cells are photovoltaic devices that convert sunlight to electricity; researchers have investigated and developed generations of solar cells, some of which are already commercialized. Most commercial solar cells are fabricated using silicon and exhibit high light-to-electricity conversion efficiency of more than 20%, but the relatively high cost of these cells limits their large-scale application. Multi-junction solar cells achieve conversion efficiency higher than 40%; however, the harsh conditions and complexity of the preparation render the commercialization impractical. Other types of solar cells, such as organic solar cells, copper indium gallium diselenide (GIGS), and CdTe solar cells, possess low conversion efficiency and toxic properties. In recent years, perovskite solar cells (PSCs), which are typically based on organic–inorganic lead halide perovskites, have been widely used because of their high conversion efficiency, low cost, and easy fabrication.2 Practical application of these cells is hindered by the presence of toxic Pb in perovskites, moisture instability, and poor reproducibility. Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), from which PSCs evolved, should be reconsidered and studied in a new perspective because of their low toxicity, excellent stability, and good conversion efficiency. After two decades of research, the highest certified conversion efficiency of DSSCs achieved is more than 11% (Fig. 1).

Fig. 2a shows the structure of the first successful DSSC fabricated in 1991 by O'Regan and Gratzel.3 An actual dye-sensitized solar cell contains five components:3,4 (1) a transparent conductive substrate, normally fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) or indium tin oxide (ITO) glass; (2) a semiconductor film, usually based on TiO2 or ZnO nanomaterials which are low cost, abundant and non-toxic; (3) a sensitizer (dye) adsorbed onto the surface of the semiconductor; (4) an electrolyte containing a redox mediator, conventionally based on triiodide/iodine (cobalt(II/III) complex redox couple has also been popular in recent years5); and (5) a counter electrode, such as platinum and carbon, which can regenerate the redox mediators. Fig. 2b presents the basic working principle of DSSCs based on a TiO2 semiconductor film. The photosensitizer (dye) absorbs a photon and excites an electron from the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the dye (process 1). This excited electron enters the conduction band of TiO2 (process 2) and flows though the TiO2 film to the conductive substrate; the electron then flows via the external load to the counter electrode (process 3) to reduce the redox mediator (process 4), thereby regenerating the dye and completing the whole circuit. In addition to the desired electron transfer pathways, undesired pathways occur and lead to electron recombination (processes 5, 6, and 7 in Fig. 2b). Process 5 is the direct recombination of the excited photoelectron, reflected by the excited state lifetime. Processes 6 and 7 show the possible recombination of the electron injected into the conduction band of the semiconductor with oxidized dyes and acceptors in the electrolyte, respectively. The back transfer of electrons from the uncovered conductive substrate to the electrolyte may occur, resulting in the loss of the photo-excited electrons. The aforementioned recombination processes can deteriorate the efficiency of electron transfer in the device and thus mainly restrict the performance of DSSCs.


Fig. 2 (a) Schematic and (b) working principle of dye-sensitized solar cells. CB, conduction band; VB, valence band; LUMO, lowest unoccupied molecular orbital; HOMO, highest occupied molecular orbital; Red., reduced; Ox., oxidized.

Photocurrent density–voltage (J–V) curves and incident photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE) are used to investigate the performance of DSSCs. Short-circuit photocurrent density (JSC), open-circuit photovoltage (VOC), and fill factor (FF) of a DSSC can be determined from the J–V curve. JSC is determined based on the amount and molecular structure of the dyes and the electrochemical properties of the mesoporous network (e.g., mesoporous TiO2 film) in the electrolyte.6,7VOC is the difference between the Fermi level of the metal oxide semiconductor electrode and the redox potential of mediators in the electrolyte (Fig. 2b); this parameter is also influenced by the recombination rate and adsorption mode of the sensitizer.8 FF is defined as Imax × Vmax/(JSC × VOC), where Imax and Vmax are the current density and voltage at which maximum power output can be obtained, respectively. FF is related to the series and shunt resistances of the device. IPCE is used to describe the efficiency of light-to-electricity conversion at a given wavelength. The most important evaluation parameter for a DSSC is the overall solar-to-electrical energy conversion efficiency (η), η = JSC × VOC × FF/Pin, where Pin is the intensity of the incident light. Based on this equation, high JSC, VOC, and FF at low Pin can lead to high conversion efficiency. The performance of DSSCs depends on the intensity and spectrum of incident light; in this regard, 100 mW cm−2 light intensity with AM 1.5G spectrum (1 Sun) is typically used as the benchmark.9

Since the first reported DSSC with a moderate conversion efficiency of ∼7%,3 researchers have increased the conversion efficiency of DSSCs up to 13%;10 hence, DSSCs remain an alternative renewable photovoltaic device to replace fossil fuels. The twofold increased in the conversion efficiency (from ∼7% to 13%) could be due to various methods employed for improving the DSSC performance. Technically, the performance of DSSCs can be improved by altering any single or multiple component(s) of the cell. Researchers have developed numerous methods to enhance the component semiconductor films, dyes, electrolytes, and counter electrodes in DSSCs, leading to enhanced conversion efficiency. Because of the relatively enormous number of reports, this paper focuses only on approaches developed to improve the semiconductor films of photoanodes and their influence on the performance of DSSCs.

2. Improvement of photoanodes

A semiconductor film (normally TiO2 or ZnO) plays an essential role in DSSCs. The film not only supplies sufficient surface area for loading photosensitizers, but also acts as an electron pathway to transfer the excited electron from the LUMO of the photosensitizer to the back contact of the conductive substrate via the conduction band of the semiconductor. Therefore, the energy levels between the semiconductor film and photosensitizer should match the synergetic requirement. The light pathway should also be considered when the incident light passes through the semiconductor film; in this case, the semiconductor structure must be rationally designed to reduce the loss of the illuminating light. Furthermore, the electron that transferred to the semiconductor film can recombine with holes, defects, and crystal boundaries; thus, the recombination processes 5, 6, and 7 in Fig. 2b should be considered. Consequently, the morphology and crystal phase of the semiconductor significantly affect the performance of DSSCs. Improving the semiconductor films is the most straightforward and probably the most efficient approach to enhance the performance of DSSCs. Given that TiO2 is the most commonly used semiconductor for DSSCs, this review mainly focuses on TiO2 nano/micro-materials unless noted otherwise.

Numerous studies have reported on improving the semiconductors of photoanodes in DSSCs. These approaches can be categorized according to mechanisms employed and results (Fig. 3).

2.1.1 One-dimensional nanostructures. TiO2 nanoparticles with diameter of ∼20 nm are traditionally employed to construct the active layer of a photoanode for DSSCs; these particles can supply the required energy levels and large surface area to absorb sufficient amount of dyes. However, these nanoparticle-based structures induce the electron transfer through a “random walk” direction; thus, many grain boundaries, defects, and traps in the film are encountered (Fig. 5). As a result, electron recombination during this process occurs to a dramatic extent and decreases the DSSC efficiency. Researchers have focused on one-dimensional nanostructures, such as nanotubes, nanowires, nanofibers, and nanorods, with large aspect ratio of length/diameter. One-dimensional nanomaterials generally possess higher diffusion coefficients than non-ordered nanoparticles and yield longer electron diffusion length than film thickness and better light absorption.12–14 In contrast to traditional nanoparticles, well-ordered one-dimensional array nanostructures can offer a direct transfer pathway (known as electron highway) for electron transfer from the semiconductor film to the conductive substrate, leading to less recombination and improved photoelectric performance of DSSCs (Fig. 5).15,16
Fig. 5 Different electron transfer pathways in traditional nanoparticles (random walk) and one-dimensional nanomaterial arrays (direct transfer) in the photoanode film of DSSCs.

2.1.1.1 Nanotubes. The TiO2 nanotube array is a one-dimensional nanomaterial intensively employed in DSSCs because of its unique morphology. Self-organized TiO2 nanotube arrays are prepared by electrochemical anodic oxidation of Ti foil. This simple electrochemical process can be performed in an electrochemical cell with a two- or three-electrode system, where Ti foil and Pt are used as working electrode (the anode) and counter electrode, respectively; this system requires a solution containing fluoride ions and a standard voltage source or a high-voltage potentiostat. Vertically aligned TiO2 nanotubes can be sensitized by dyes not only on the outer walls but also on the inner walls; this feature indicates that the TiO2 nanotube array can supply higher surface area than other one-dimensional nanomaterials (e.g., TiO2 nanowire, nanorod, and nanofiber). Moreover, TiO2 nanotubes can be synthesized through a simple and facile electrochemical method and exhibit tunable length and diameter and excellent electron diffusion length; these properties promote the application of these nanostructures in DSSCs.17–20

Schmuki and co-workers developed the first dye-sensitized solar cells using TiO2 nanotubes in 2005, although the conversion efficiency was very low (0.036%) and inferior to nanoparticle-based cells.21 Since then, TiO2 nanotubes have been applied in DSSCs. Researchers have developed the use of TiO2 nanotubes on Ti foil for DSSCs, exhibiting an efficiency of more than 7%,22 which is higher than those of reported TiO2 nanoparticle-based DSSCs under the same conditions;23–25 these findings illustrate the enhancement of DSSCs by using TiO2 nanotubes.

TiO2 nanotube arrays synthesized on Ti metal substrates exhibit tunable nanotube length; the length can be adjusted by altering the electrochemical parameters in the synthesis. Diau and co-workers reported that the conversion efficiency of TiO2 nanotube-based DSSCs will be improved by increasing the length of the nanotubes. In this study, the prepared TiO2 nanotube arrays possessed tunable length from 15 μm to 57 μm (Fig. 6). Moreover, the corresponding efficiency of DSSCs increased from 4.5% to 6.1% because of the large surface area and great light-scattering effect of the elongated TiO2 nanotubes.22 However, if the length of TiO2 nanotube arrays is too long, the anodized TiO2 nanotube arrays will be inclined to detach from the Ti foil because of the local stress at the interface of TiO2 nanotube arrays/Ti foil;26 hence, preparation of ultra-long TiO2 nanotube arrays on Ti foil remains challenging. Craig and co-workers synthesized highly-ordered TiO2 nanotube arrays with length of ∼220 μm; however, the resulting DSSC can obtain the highest conversion efficiency of 6.9% when the length is only ∼20 μm, and arrays longer or shorter than this value will reduce the performance of the DSSC.27 Therefore, the effect of the length of TiO2 nanotube arrays on the performance of DSSCs remains unclear.

The incident light on TiO2 nanotube-based DSSCs can only illuminate from the transparent Pt counter electrode side because of the opacity of the Ti metal substrate normally used to grow and support TiO2 nanotube arrays; this process is known as back illumination (Fig. 7). By contrast, for a traditional FTO-glass-based DSSC, the incident light illuminates from the FTO-glass side, known as front illumination. The major drawback of back illumination is the loss of light-harvesting capability of the photoanodes; this phenomenon is due to light scattering and absorption when the incident light passes through the Pt layer and electrolyte before reaching the TiO2 nanotube photoanode. This limitation hinders the high performance of TiO2 nanotube-based DSSCs with Ti metal substrates.28,29 In FTO-glass-based DSSCs, back illumination causes ∼40% efficiency loss compared with front illumination.23 Therefore, the conversion efficiency can be significantly improved by making front illumination feasible for TiO2 nanotube-based DSSCs.


Fig. 7 Schematic of front and back illumination for DSSCs and their light-harvesting loss.

To address the limitation of back illumination of TiO2 nanotube-based DSSCs, researchers used a transfer method for preparing TiO2 nanotube arrays on transparent FTO-glass substrate, rendering front illumination feasible.26,30–34Fig. 8a shows a typical transfer method. TiO2 nanotube arrays are typically prepared on Ti metal foil by anodization. The TiO2 nanotube arrays are then peeled off from the Ti substrate and transferred to a transparent FTO-glass substrate with an adhesive layer containing TiO2 nanoparticles. The fabricated TiO2 nanotube-based DSSCs can be illuminated from the FTO-glass side and are predicted to exhibit improved performance by avoiding back illumination. Lamberti et al. applied this transfer method to fabricate DSSCs, with a promising conversion efficiency of 6.26%; a higher efficiency (7.56%) can be obtained by further TiCl4 post-treatment.30 The obtain conversion efficiencies are higher than those of reported back illuminated DSSCS.35–39 Wang and co-workers also used a similar transfer method and optimized the length of TiO2 nanotube arrays (33 μm) for FTO-glass-based DSSCs (Fig. 8a), achieving a very high conversion efficiency of 9.02%;40 then, they introduced TiO2 nanoparticles of appropriate particle size ∼20 nm into TiO2 nanotube arrays and 9.86% conversion efficiency of the corresponding FTO-glass-based DSSC could be obtained (Fig. 8b–d);41 to the best of our knowledge, this is the highest efficiency ever reported for TiO2 nanotube array-based DSSCs. ZnO nanotube arrays have also been developed but are not widely used for DSSCs because of their relatively low conversion efficiency (lower than 3%) compared with that of TiO2 nanotubes.42–45

2.1.1.2 Nanowires/rods. Nanowire and nanorod arrays are also commonly used one-dimensional nanomaterials for fabricating DSSCs. In contrast to nanotubes, nanowires and nanorods can only supply the outer walls as the support of the dyes, resulting in relatively small surface area for dye loading.46–51 The insufficient surface area and low light harvesting of these nanomaterials limit their application in solar cells.16,52–56 As such, the surface area of these one-dimensional nanostructures should be increased to improve the performance of DSSCs. Lin and co-workers prepared rutile TiO2 nanorod arrays on FTO glass, which were selectively etched to porous nanorod arrays by hydrothermal treatment (Fig. 9a–e); the product exhibited large surface area and a high conversion efficiency of 7.91%.49 Increasing the length of one-dimensional nanomaterial arrays is another straightforward and efficient strategy to enlarge the surface area, thereby increasing the scattering effect.49,57,58 Kuang and co-workers employed multiple hydrothermal steps to grow ultra-long anatase TiO2 nanowire arrays (the length was ∼50 μm) on FTO glass. The resulting DSSC achieved 9.40% conversion efficiency, which is not only higher than that of short TiO2 nanowire-based DSSCs but also superior to TiO2 nanoparticle P25-based ones (7.23%) (Fig. 9f–i).57 Researchers have also extensively studied extending the length of ZnO nanowire arrays to increase their surface area and thus improve the performance of DSSCs.15,16,53,59–63 Gao and co-workers synthesized multilayer assemblies of vertical ZnO nanowire arrays, with lengths from ∼10 μm to ∼40 μm, on conductive glass (Fig. 10a–e); the efficiency of DSSCs increased with increasing length of the nanowire. In particular, ∼40 μm long ZnO nanowires exhibited a high conversion efficiency of ∼7% (Fig. 10f),61 which is higher than those of most ZnO nanoparticle-based DSSCs.42,45 Similar to nanowire arrays, nanorod arrays with an appropriate length possess better performance than shorter ones and can outperform nanoparticle counterparts in DSSCs because of reduced recombination and high surface area.49,64 However, very long nanorod arrays will reduce the VOC of the DSSCs because of cross-linking and fast recombination rates.57,64

One-dimensional nanomaterial (nanotube, nanowire, and nanorod) arrays exhibit interesting properties, such as adjustable length, low recombination, and facile electron transfer, and thus can outperform traditional nanoparticles in DSSCs. However, the specific surface area of these one-dimensional nanomaterials remains insufficient, and dye loading should be increased to significantly contribute to DSSC improvement.

2.1.2 Hierarchical materials. Hierarchical materials have been increasingly used for DSSC photoanodes in recent years. These materials consist of primary nanostructures, such as ZnO or TiO2 nanocrystallites; the primary structures form a secondary structure, which is either spherical in shape, low dimensional, or three dimensional.65 Compared with conventional nanoparticles and one-dimensional nanoarrays, hierarchical materials can supply larger specific surface areas to load sufficient dyes by the primary nanostructure; these materials also exhibit higher light-harvesting efficiency, lower electron recombination, and facilitated diffusion of electrolyte because of the secondary structure.66,67 Studies have reported the use of photoanodes made of hierarchical materials to improve DSSC performance (Table 1).
2.1.2.1 Hierarchical spheres/beads. Various TiO2 or ZnO hierarchical spheres/beads have been employed as alternatives to nanoparticles in the photoanodes of DSSCs.68–77Fig. 11 shows several typical TiO2 and ZnO hierarchical spheres for DSSCs. Highly porous hierarchical TiO2 spheres/beads, with surface areas larger than 100 m2 g−1, have been successfully prepared; when these particles are applied as the photoanode of DSSCs, the conversion efficiency can be more than 10%, which is significantly higher than those based on standard P25 TiO2 nanoparticles with a similar film thickness (Fig. 11a–f).68,71,78,79 Cao and co-workers also synthesized ZnO hierarchical spheres (100 nm to 500 nm diameter) aggregated by ZnO nanocrystallites with 15 nm diameter (Fig. 11g and h). The use of ZnO hierarchical spheres as photoanode film in DSSCs exhibits twice higher conversion efficiency than those with ZnO nanoparticles.70 Nanoribbons/belts/wires/rods/sheets assemble as sphere-like hierarchical materials and can also be utilized as the active film of photoanodes in DSSCs; the fabricated cells exhibit higher conversion efficiencies than those based on their nanoparticle counterparts.78,80–83 This finding could be due to enhanced light harvesting and electron transfer within the electrodes without sacrificing the accessible surface area for dye loading of the photoanodes.

Hierarchical hollow spheres with single or multiple shells have been developed as advanced materials for improvement of the DSSC performance. These materials exhibit high surface area and facile electron transfer as well as superior light-scattering effect because of significantly enhanced light harvesting as a result of reflective light by the shell(s) (Fig. 12a).84 When hierarchical hollow spheres are applied in DSSCs, the shell number is an important consideration for improving the performance of devices.84–88 Increasing the shell number of TiO2 hollow spheres will result in high conversion efficiencies of DSSCs because of an enhanced multi-reflectance effect (Fig. 12a–f); the obtained conversion efficiency can be as high as 9.4%, as reported by Jang and co-workers.84 ZnO hollow spheres, with shell number ranging from one to four, can be accurately controlled by heating rate and time (Fig. 13); the DSSC performance will also be enhanced (from 3.7% to 5.6%) by increasing the shell number,87 similar to the TiO2 hollow sphere-based DSSCs. Compared with TiO2 and ZnO nanoparticles, hierarchical hollow spheres show obvious superiority and thus exhibit potential for improvement of DSSC performance.

2.1.2.2 Hierarchical (quasi) one-dimensional nanomaterials. The direct pathway of (quasi) one-dimensional nanomaterials for electron transfer can reduce the electron recombination. However, the relatively small surface area of these materials limits their application in DSSCs. As such, (quasi) one-dimensional nanomaterials with hierarchical structures have been developed for DSSCs. The (quasi) one-dimension can offer a direct pathway for electron transfer, and the hierarchical structure provides a sufficient surface area for dye loading. Furthermore, in many cases, the light-scattering effect can be enhanced simultaneously. Various hierarchical (quasi) one-dimensional nanomaterials with different morphologies have been synthesized for DSSCs; these nanomaterials include hierarchical nanotubes,29,89–95 nanowires and nanorods,96–99 nanotrees,100–102 and nanoforest arrays.103,104 Most of these nanomaterials not only possess (quasi) one-dimensional nanostructures, but also form coarse faces and several branches along the one-dimensional axis (Fig. 14). These nanostructures exhibit the following advantages: (1) the coarse faces and several branches increase the surface area of the photoanodes to enable loading of more dye molecules; (2) the coarse faces and several branches enhance the light scattering in the films, thereby increasing the light-harvesting efficiency significantly; (3) the electron can transfer directly through the (quasi) one-dimensional nanomaterials to the substrates with reduced recombination compared with conventional nanoparticles; and (4) the engineered growth of (quasi) one-dimensional nanomaterials renders them adjustable to match different DSSCs. Therefore, hierarchical (quasi) one-dimensional nanomaterial-based DSSCs can outperform nanoparticle-based ones in many cases. For example, Kim and co-workers applied hierarchical anatase pine tree-like TiO2 nanotubes as the photoanode in a solid state DSSC; the resulting device exhibits an outstanding conversion efficiency of 8.0%, which is twofold higher than that of commercially available TiO2 nanoparticle-based paste (4.0% Dyesol) and one of the highest values obtained for solid state DSSCs.101 Thus, hierarchical anatase pine tree-like TiO2 nanotubes exhibit great potential for improving the DSSC performance.

2.1.3 Exposed facet. Anatase TiO2 is commonly used as the semiconductor for DSSCs; in this material, the (101) facet dominates more than 94% of the total surface area of the crystals105 and becomes the main location for dye molecular absorption. The (001) and (010) facets106 are also important because of their high energy and activity in catalysis and photocatalysis.107–109 The (001) facet possesses negative flat-band potential,110 and the (010) facet is the most active among the facets of (101), (001), and (010).105 Thus, engineering (001) and (010) facets to expose the content can improve the performance of DSSCs by increasing VOC and/or JSC. For instance, anatase TiO2 nanosheet-based hierarchical spheres with over 90% (001) facet can outperform P25 nanoparticles and exhibit 43% improvement in efficiency when used as photoanodes for DSSCs; the anatase (001) facet has high surface energy and strong ability to dissociatively absorb the (COOH) group of the dye molecules.111,112 The percentage of the exposed (001) facet in the TiO2 crystal can be deliberately adjusted, resulting in different morphologies, such as nanosheets,107,111,113,114 spheres111,115 and nanorods,109,116 (Fig. 15). The conversion efficiency of DSSCs increases with increasing percentage of the exposed (001) facet, which not only is favorable for dye adsorption but also effectively retards the charge recombination process.116 Making the (010) facet dominant in the TiO2 crystal leads to effective adsorption of dyes, promotes electron transfer from the excited dye molecules to TiO2, and increases the VOC of DSSCs.107,117 Moreover, rationally exposing the facet influences the crystal phase and morphology, resulting in facile electron transfer, high light-scattering effect, and preferable pore volume distribution.83,113,115 These benefits along with the intrinsic advantage of highly exposed facets could improve the efficiency of DSSCs.

2.2.1 Double-layer structure. In the double-layer structure, an extra film composed of large particles, known as the scattering layer, is placed on the top of the active film, which usually consists of nanoparticles to construct the basic absorbing layer of the DSSCs. Compared with the size of nanoparticles (usually ∼10 nm to 50 nm) used to supply sufficient surface area for dye loading, the size of scattering particles in the scattering layer is larger and always comparable to the wavelength of light (e.g., 200 nm to 500 nm); this feature enables the reflection of incident light that passes through the active nanocrystalline film, consequently decreasing the loss of light by transmission (Fig. 17). Therefore, the size of the scattering particles in the scattering layer is essential for light reflection and scattering effects.124–126 The particle size of the scattering layer ranges from several hundred nanometers to few micrometers, resulting in different scattering effects; this finding was confirmed by computer simulations of light scattering and absorption in DSSCs.127,128 The scattering layer consists of various materials. Large TiO2 nanoparticles (diameter ca. 100 nm to 500 nm) are commonly used in the scattering layer (Fig. 18a);129–132 the resulting conversion efficiency of DSSCs exceeds 10%.130,132 In recent years, TiO2 sphere and sphere-like scattering materials have been intensively studied and used in scattering layers (Fig. 18b–h).89,124,125,133–145 Interestingly, most TiO2 spheres used for scattering layers are hierarchically composed of primary structures, such as small nanoparticles, leading to their multi-functions in DSSCs. For instance, Yu and co-workers synthesized multiple hierarchical TiO2 spheres and applied them as scattering layer for DSSCs with different nanostructures;146,147 the efficiencies of the DSSCs significantly increased compared with the cells without scattering layers. The improvement is not only derived from the scattering effect, but also from the large surface areas and pore volume of TiO2 spheres, resulting in more adsorption of the dye molecules and better transfer of the electrolyte, respectively.146,147 These advantages render TiO2 spheres promising materials for DSSCs; even without an underlayer of nanocrystalline particles, the DSSCs can obtain high efficiency (can be over 10%) in some cases because the TiO2 spheres can exhibit light-scattering and high intrinsic surface area for loading sufficient dyes to replace nanocrystalline layers (as discussed in Section 2.1.1.2 Hierarchical materials).68,69,71

2.2.2 Mixture structure. In the mixture structure, large scattering particles are embedded into a nanocrystalline film, which actively absorbs the dyes. The large particles embedded into the nanocrystalline film act as light scatterers to generate different degrees of light scattering and extend the travelling distance of light within the photoelectrode film; this phenomenon leads to increased probability of photons being absorbed by the sensitized semiconductor film.158 Similar to scattering materials used in double-layer structures, various nanomaterials, such as nanowires and spheres, can be employed as light scatterers into the nanocrystalline films of DSSCs. Tan & Wu fabricated DSSCs by using composites of anatase TiO2 nanoparticles and single-crystalline anatase TiO2 nanowires; solar cells with TiO2 nanowires offer a light-scattering effect and a highway for rapid electron transport, resulting in an enhancement of conversion efficiency from 6.7% (without TiO2 nanowires) to 8.6%.159 Jang and co-workers fabricated SiO2/TiO2 core/shell nanoparticles with different diameters and investigated the influence of size on light scattering. After mixing the SiO2/TiO2 core/shell nanoparticles as light scatterers in the TiO2 nanocrystalline film, the conversion efficiency of the DSSCs increased by 20%.160 Míguez and co-workers mixed TiO2 spheres as light scatterers with mesoporous TiO2 for the photoanode of DSSCs, which achieved 33% efficiency enhancement.161 These results indicate that the mixture structure with light-scattering materials can efficiently enhance the performance of DSSCs.

Both double-layer and mixture structures can improve the DSSC performance through a similar scattering mechanism. However, in the double-layer structure, the introduced large-sized-particle-based scattering film will increase the internal resistance and hinder charge transport within the solar cells. Meanwhile, in the mixed structure, the large-size particles will unavoidably cause a loss of internal surface area of the photoelectrode film.158,162 Therefore, the structure of DSSCs using scattering materials requires a carefully rational design. Several factors, such as morphology, film thickness, and scatterer contents, must be considered to obtain the optimal synergetic improvement; otherwise, the performance of DSSCs may deteriorate.

2.3.1 Carbon materials. Carbon-based materials are commonly used to form composites with TiO2 in the photoanodes of DSSCs. Graphene and carbon nanotubes are the most popular carbon-based materials because of their excellent electron mobility, high specific surface area, and superior mechanical properties.164–166 Graphene cannot only reduce the resistance at the electrolyte–electrode interface and the charge recombination rate, but also enhance the transport of electrons from the semiconductor films to the conductive substrates. Incorporating graphene in photoanodes also improves light harvesting, thereby increasing the number of photoinduced electrons.76,167–176 Yu and co-workers fabricated a TiO2 nanosheet/graphene composite film photoanode for DSSCs with 0.75 wt% graphene; the conversion efficiency of the composite film was increased by 25% compared with the bare TiO2 nanosheet photoanode.167 Carbon nanotubes can also enhance the transport of electrons from the films to the FTO substrates, reduce the charge recombination, and enhance the conversion efficiency.177–181 Hence, these materials can be used to significantly improve the performance of DSSCs.

Graphene exhibits better performance than carbon nanotubes when used for DSSCs because of the two-dimensional structure of the former. In two-dimensional graphene bridges, TiO2 can anchor in the graphene and the photoinduced electrons can be captured and transferred by the graphene; by contrast, in one-dimensional carbon nanotube composites, the fewer intermolecular forces and connections between TiO2 and the one-dimensional nanomaterial lead to a large transfer barrier and easy recombination174 (Fig. 19a–f). The amount of graphene or carbon nanotubes in the photoanode must be carefully controlled to lower than 1 wt% in the film. High loadings of graphene or carbon nanotubes will shield the visible light from being adsorbed by the dyes, decreasing the number of photogenerated electrons and the efficiency of the DSSCs.

2.3.2 Au and Ag with plasmon resonance effect. Surface plasmon resonance from nanostructured gold and silver contributes to unique properties, such as intense absorption and enhanced local electromagnetic field, and is utilized in photocatalysis,182,183 and silicon184 and organic photovoltaic devices.185 The utilization of the plasmon resonance effect of Au and Ag nanomaterials is a promising pathway to increase the efficiency of DSSCs (Fig. 20). The excitation of localized surface plasmon resonance can enhance the spectral absorption and light harvesting;186 hence, compositing Au or Ag with TiO2 in the photoanodes of DSSCs can increase the photocurrent density. Furthermore, Au and Ag plasmon can increase the energy levels of TiO2, enhance charge transfer processes in DSSCs, and reduce the electron recombination (Fig. 20a–c); thus, the use of Au and Ag plasmon can considerably improve the DSSC performance;187–195 in some cases, the improvement can even double the efficiency of the counterpart without Au or Ag. These core–shell nanostructures not only take advantage of the plasmon effect of Au and Ag, but also have the merits of enhanced light scattering and low electron recombination;86,196–198 as such, the use of these structures can enhance the performance of DSSCs with more than 10% conversion efficiency.196,197 Belcher and co-workers significantly enhanced the broadband light harvesting of DSSCs by simply blending the plasmonic nanoparticles of the photoactive multiple-core-shelled TiO2–Au–TiO2; the conversion efficiency of the panchromatic DSSC increased from 8.3% to 10.8%.196 Moreover, a small amount of Ag@TiO2 nanoparticles can obviously improve the conversion efficiency while decreasing photoanode thickness for improved electron collection,198 indicating less material requirement and potential low cost for DSSC fabrication (Fig. 20c and f).

2.3.4 Others. Application of emerging nanomaterials to DSSCs has gained increased research attention, particularly for compositing photoanode materials. Yu and co-workers fabricated DSSCs by using graphite carbon nitride (g-C3N4) modified TiO2 nanosheets as photoanode (Fig. 21d–f); the conversion efficiency of DSSCs increased by 28% in the sample with g-C3N4 decoration because the g-C3N4 layer acted as the blocking layer for electron recombination and contributed additional electrons to enhance the performance of DSSCs.203 Light-harvesting efficiency can be increased by converting UV (which is normally filtered out by the glass substrate and cannot be utilized by photoanodes) and near-infrared light (which normally cannot excite electron from the dyes) to visible light by coating with luminescent materials204,205 and incorporation of upconverting phosphor nanoparticles,206 thereby improving the performance of DSSCs. Moreover, hybrids of hydrogels,207 CaF2,208 SiO2,209 SiC,210 Li2SiO3211 and CdS212 have been applied in TiO2 composite photoanodes, resulting in enhanced conversion efficiency. New nanomaterials for composite photoanode film for DSSCs are still emerging and are predicted to be very promising for future applications in photovoltaics.

2.4 Doping

2.4.1 Nitrogen and boron doping. Some oxygen deficiencies are present in the crystal structure of pure TiO2.213 In DSSCs, oxygen deficiencies can create electron–hole pairs; the oxidizing holes can either react with the dye, or destroy it, and/or be scavenged by iodide ions in the electrolyte.214 Therefore, oxygen deficiency shortens the lifetime of DSSCs. Doping nitrogen atoms to replace oxygen deficiency in TiO2 can induce TiO2 to become visible-light-active, stabilize the photoelectric properties, and enhance the IPCE and conversion efficiency of the DSSCs.213–215 Ma and co-workers fabricated a highly efficient DSSC by using a nanocrystalline N-doped TiO2 electrode; the DSSC showed enhanced IPCE and 8% conversion efficiency. The stability test showed that the DSSC fabricated by the N-doped TiO2 exhibited great stability, which was superior to P25 and pure TiO2-based ones.215 This example demonstrates the superiority of TiO2 with N doping. Several technologies have been developed to prepare N-doped TiO2; these technologies include sputtering and implantation,216 sintering TiO2 under N-containing atmosphere (NH3, for instance)217 and wet methods involving sol–gel and solvothermal methods.218,219 Of these methods, synthesis of N-doped TiO2 nanocrystals through wet techniques using ammonia, triethylamine, and urea as nitrogen dopants has been well studied. Comparison showed that the ammonia nitrogen dopant is more efficient than the two other nitrogen dopants, resulting in higher efficiency derived from the synergistic effects of high dye uptake and efficient electron transport by N doping.219 Specifically, an enhanced photocurrent of ca. 36% in N-doped DSSCs derived was from the synergistic effects of the high dye uptake and the efficient electron transport due to nitrogen doping, resulting in high efficiency of 8.32%.219 This work showed that N-doped TiO2 electrodes are suitable candidates for highly efficient DSSCs.

Doping with boron is a popular method used to improve the efficiency of ZnO-based DSSCs. The visible behavior of ZnO can be improved by boron doping, and the light-harvesting efficiency and photon-to-electron transfer process of the DSSCs will be enhanced. Park and co-workers applied B-doped ZnO nanoporous nanosheets in DSSCs and obtained a conversion efficiency of 6.75%, which is higher than that of undoped ZnO-based DSSCs (2.62%).220 Furthermore, Sung and co-workers fabricated a double-layered B-doped ZnO film-based DSSC, where both the top layer of the light-scattering ZnO particles and the underlayer of nanoporous ZnO were doped by boron. Moreover, the double-layered B-doped ZnO-based DSSC can achieve a high conversion efficiency of 7.2%; the obtained efficiency was higher than the 4.1% efficiency obtained by double-layered undoped ZnO films and the 2.1% achieved by monolayer nanoparticle N-doped ZnO films.221 This work demonstrated a novel structural design by combining doping and light-scattering materials for DSSCs. 2.4.2 Metal doping. Various metals, mainly transition and rare earth metals, can be doped into semiconductors of photoanodes to enhance the DSSC performance (Table 4). These transition metals include Ti(III), W, Mg, Sn, Nb, Ni, Ta, and Zn.7,222–232 These metal dopants make the energy levels or electron recombination preferable for DSSCs. For instance, Sn-doped TiO2 can negatively shift the flatband of TiO2 and facilitate the electron transfer in the TiO2 film, thereby increasing VOC and JSC of DSSCs, respectively.225 Nb-Doped TiO2-based DSSCs showed higher conversion efficiency than undoped ones; intensity-modulated photovoltage spectroscopy and photocurrent spectroscopy data indicated that the beneficial effect is due to the improved recombination.227 Thus, doping with appropriate transition metals to substitute Ti in TiO2 can offer additional advantages such as extended band edges, increased electron density of states, and high Fermi level.

Doping with rare earth metals (e.g., Y, Yb, Er, Lu, Tm) is also mainly used to engineer the electron density of states and energy levels. Most rare earth ions are positive trivalent cations. When these ions are applied to dope TiO2 semiconductor, a p-type doping effect occurs, resulting in increased energy level of the photoanode and enhanced photovoltage of the DSSCs. Wu and co-workers performed a series of work and revealed that doping of rare earth metals affects not only the energy levels of the photoanode, but also the light-harvesting efficiency by an up-conversion/luminescence process.233–236 Therefore, the incident light harvesting and the photocurrent of the DSSC can be enhanced by doping rare earth metals into TiO2.233,234

2.5 Interfacial engineering

The performance of DSSCs depends on electron back recombination, which mainly occurs at two interfaces, namely, FTO substrate/electrolyte and TiO2 photoanode/electrolyte. Electron back recombination results in reduced VOC and JSC, and negatively affects the conversion efficiency of DSSCs. Correspondingly, the back charge transfer from FTO to electrolyte at the FTO/electrolyte interface can be reduced by using a thin TiO2 compact layer to minimize the exposed FTO surface not covered by the nanoporous TiO2 film or a blocking material, which is commonly deposited on FTO-glass substrates to passivate the FTO substrate (Fig. 22a). The electron recombination at the TiO2/electrolyte interface can be reduced by coating the mesoporous TiO2 film with a thin layer of a wide bandgap semiconductor with more negative conduction band than that of TiO2, or using a material with an electronic insulating coating to form an energy barrier at the interface (Fig. 22b). The surface barrier is supposed to reduce the back charge transfer recombination rate, thereby increasing the device performance.237

For the FTO/electrolyte interface, a thin TiO2 compact (blocking) layer, which minimizes the exposed FTO surface to electrolyte, can efficiently suppress the back electron recombination from the FTO glass to the electrolyte (Fig. 22a).238–240 Compact TiO2 layers can be prepared on conductive substrates through spray pyrolysis,241–243 sol–gel,244 DC-magnetron sputtering,245 electrochemical deposition,246 and atomic layer deposition (ALD).247–250 Kavan and co-workers prepared TiO2 compact layers on FTO glass by thermal oxidation of Ti, spray pyrolysis, electrochemical deposition, and ALD to improve the performance of DSSCs;239 the effects of these different methods on the DSSC performance were investigated. Besides the influence of the thickness of the TiO2 compact layer on the DSSC performance, the preparation method plays an important role for the optimal fabrication of DSSCs. TiO2 compact layers made by thermal oxidation of Ti, films made by electrodeposition (thickness ∼60 nm), and ALD films (thickness >5 nm) exhibit satisfactory blocking function and provide less than 1% effective area of pinholes in the total film area. However, the blocking effect of the electrodeposited and ALD films is reduced upon calcination at 500 °C. The blocking function of spray-pyrolyzed films is poor and less sensitive to calcination. Thermally oxidized Ti shows good blocking function and insensitivity to calcination but cannot be used in DSSCs if optical transparency of the cathode is required. Spray pyrolysis remains the first choice for practical DSSC production. Electrodeposition and ALD can be employed for fabrication of TiO2 photoanodes in cases when post-calcination is not required.239

Different metal oxide thin layers with wide bandgaps are coated on TiO2 nanoparticles in the photoanode of DSSCs to reduce the back electron transfer/recombination at the TiO2/electrolyte interface (Fig. 22b). With the wide bandgaps of the semiconductors with conduction bands more negative than that of TiO2, the coating layers of MgO251,252 on TiO2 can restrain the surface states of the TiO2 electrode and substantially suppress the back charge transfer from TiO2 to triiodide in the electrolyte; this phenomenon results in reduced interfacial charge-transfer and solution resistance to enhance the performance of DSSCs. Other metal oxide thin layers are applied as insulating layers to coat TiO2; these layers include Al2O3,253–255 ZrO2,255–257 Nb2O5,99,256 Ga2O3,256,258 Ta2O5256 and SiO2.255,259 These compounds can block the electron recombination from TiO2 to the electrolyte, leading to increased VOC and conversion efficiency of DSSCs. However, a recent study revealed that the insulating property of these metal oxides alone is not sufficient to suppress the unwanted back reaction. Other properties such as conduction band position and oxidation state should be considered as the primary criteria. Meanwhile, the surface isoelectric property of the insulating layer should also be considered with regard to the charge of the electrolyte redox species (Co(II)/Co(III) or iodide/triiodide electrolyte).256 These requirements indicate that the interface engineering of the TiO2/insulating layer has to be designed and tuned carefully to fabricate DSSCs. 2.6 TiCl4 treatment

TiCl4 post-treatment of the TiO2 film of the DSSC photoanode is a well-known method for improving the performance of DSSCs (Table 4); in this method, an extra layer of TiO2 is grown onto the TiO2 nanoparticles to form a film. Typically, the sintered TiO2 film is immersed in a moderate-concentration TiCl4 solution at a mild temperature for a certain period, and the electrodes are sintered again to obtain TiCl4-treated film. This method not only is applied to TiO2 nanoparticle-based photoanodes,260–263 but also can be used for other nanostructured TiO2-based photoanodes, such as TiO2 hierarchical beads,71 nanorods264 and nanotube arrays.22,26 Several hypotheses have been developed to explain the improvement effect of TiCl4 post-treatment on DSSCs; these conjectures include increased surface area,265 improved electron transport,265 light scattering,266 TiO2 purity,267 and dye anchoring,268 indicating the multi-functionality and the complicated effect of TiCl4 post-treatment. Thus far, the use of TiCl4 treatment to improve the performance of DSSCs presents many disputable points.

Lee and co-workers reported on the effects of treating TiO2 nanocrystalline films with different concentrations of TiCl4 (5 mM to 500 mM) on the film morphology, charge carrier dynamics, and performance of DSSCs; frequency-resolved modulated photocurrent/photovoltage spectroscopy analysis was used to investigate the transport and recombination of electrons in the TiCl4-treated films (Fig. 23a–c).261 This study indicated that intermediate TiCl4 concentrations (15 mM to 50 mM) can lead to increased surface area of the films, resulting in enhanced light harvesting and conversion efficiency. Sommeling and co-workers studied systematically the influence of the TiCl4 post-treatment on nanocrystalline TiO2 films in DSSCs at an early stage.260 The results indicated that the increase in photocurrent is not primarily caused by increase in light absorbed or decrease in light loss during transport, but is related to the downward shift in the band edge; this phenomenon leads to high quantum efficiency of injection and high photocurrent. The mechanism of TiCl4 post-treatment in improving the DSSC performance remains unclear and must be investigated in future research.

Normally, if the band edge of TiO2 is at the correct potential to maximize the quantum efficiency of injection, which means high photocurrent density and conversion efficiency have been already obtained (e.g., over 10%) from the untreated photoanodes, then the improvement to DSSCs by TiCl4 post-treatment will be very limited (Fig. 23d and e).71,269,270

3. Conclusion and perspectives

This review on materials and techniques for improvement of photoanodes provides a basis for large-scale and practical applications of DSSCs. Effective protocols are not always utilized individually; instead, rationally or unintentionally two or more of them are combined to enhance the performance of DSSCs. For example, one-dimensional TiO2 nanotube or nanorod arrays can cooperate with relatively large-sized scattering particles; the former are beneficial for the electron transfer from photoanode film to FTO-glass substrate and reduce electron recombination, and the latter lead to an increased light-harvesting efficiency.89,144,147 In many cases, an individual material with a special nanostructure can exhibit multi-function to enhance the performance of DSSCs. Some of the most remarkable examples are the hierarchical materials, such as TiO2 hierarchical spheres69,71,163 and nanotrees,101 which have high surface area, facile electron transfer, and effective light scattering simultaneously, making them competitive candidates to conventional nanoparticles. These single materials can yield conversion efficiencies higher than 10%,71,78,163 demonstrating the advantages of multi-functional materials for photoanodes of DSSCs. The presented strategies not only can be applied for improvement of the DSSC performance, but also are suitable for the enhancement of other solar energy conversion related areas, such as perovskite solar cells,271–273 photoelectrochemical cells,274,275 and photocatalysis,276 showing a considerable universality.

However, a few issues should be pointed out in this research area. (1) In consideration of the huge technical and measurement differences among various research groups, a fair comparison between the above-described DSSCs is almost impossible. A careful judgment should be made when a “better” DSSC with “higher” conversion efficiency than another one is announced. A benchmark protocol is urgently desired. (2) The record of conversion efficiency for DSSCs that employ traditional TiO2 nanoparticles, porphyrin SM315 dye and Co(II/III) tris(bipyridyl) electrolyte has been beyond 13% so far (not certified),10 obviously still surpassing those of the DSSCs fabricated using the aforementioned materials and treatments. Therefore, whether these nano/micromaterials with special and elaborately engineered morphologies can outperform their nanoparticle counterparts intrinsically in DSSCs remains unclear. Furthermore, commercially available P25 nanoparticles (normally ∼25 nm in size, 70% anatase and 30% rutile) are widely used for comparison with synthesized nano/micromaterials to show the advantages of the latter in DSSCs. The “tricky” part is that P25 is not an ideal candidate nanoparticle for use in DSSCs because of its non-optimal particle size and mixture of anatase and rutile phases, which reduce the amount of dye loading and photoactivity. Therefore, materials that outperformed P25 cannot be readily declared as “better” than other nanoparticles for DSSCs. However, this declaration had been made in many reports, which may be considered as misconceived.

Tables 1–4 show that the above-mentioned approaches can improve the performance of DSSCs, at least compared with the pristine ones, although the extent of the improvement is limited when the pristine DSSCs already possess a high conversion efficiency (e.g., over 10%). Apparently, there is still a lot of room for further improving DSSCs in the future by novel design. One of the most possible directions is the development of efficient photocathodes of p-type DSSCs, and fabricating them with the above introduced n-type DSSCs to construct a tandem configuration, which is predicted to capture a wide range of the solar spectrum and improve the performance. Some efforts had been made, e.g., Li and co-workers fabricated a p-type DSSC based on NiO, giving 64% IPCE and 5.48mA cm−2JSC.277 Nattestad and co-workers made a tandem DSSC constructed of an n-type TiO2-based photoanode and a p-type NiO-based photocathode. Particularly, the VOC of this tandem DSSC (1.079 V) was higher than that of the individual n-type DSSC (0.905 V) and p-type DSSC (0.186 V).278 This value of VOC of over 1 V is very attractive and promising for practical applications, although the overall conversion efficiency is still relatively low (1.91%).278 These attempts pave a route for novel design for further improvement of DSSCs, which is believed to be one of the directions in the future.

Moreover, the development of DSSCs on flexible substrates and with various colors, which make them suitable for a roll-to-roll way of manufacture and artwork shows great potential for low-cost and large-scale production for industry and decoration.279 Recently, the Chinese Academy of Sciences achieved the technology transfer of its self-developed DSSCs to a Shenzhen company with a transfer fee of 100 million Chinese Yuan (∼US$ 15 million), which is considered a big step to the commercialization of DSSCs.280 Nowadays, the urgent requirement is the efficiency breakthrough of DSSCs to compete with the emerging new types of solar cells, such as perovskite solar cells with conversion efficiency higher than 20%.281 With the emergence of newly advanced materials and the development of DSSC device fabrication, we are confident that DSSCs with relatively satisfactory performance can be achieved, and their commercialization can be completed in the near future.
