Abstract

Nanoparticles probably constitute the largest class of nanomaterials. Nanoparticles of several inorganic materials have been prepared by employing a variety of synthetic strategies. Besides synthesizing nanoparticles, there has been considerable effort to selectively prepare nanoparticles of different shapes. In view of the great interest in inorganic nanoparticles evinced in the last few years, we have prepared this perspective on the present status of the synthesis of inorganic nanoparticles. This article includes a brief discussion of methods followed by reports on the synthesis of nanoparticles of various classes of inorganic materials such as metals, alloys, oxides chalcogenides and pnictides. A brief section on coreshell nanoparticles is also included.

1. Introduction

Synthesis of nanomaterials constitutes a crucial aspect of nanoscience and technology. Several strategies have been employed for the synthesis of nanomaterials of different dimensionalities. In particular, zero-dimensional nanoparticles (NPs) have received greater attention because of the wide-spectrum of uses. Chemical methods have been very useful in the generation of nanoparticles of various inorganic materials such as metals, oxides, chalcogenides and pnictides and some of the reviews cover various aspects of these nanocrystals.1,2 A perspective article that appeared a few years ago in this journal3 covered the synthesis of inorganic NPs, nanotubes and nanowires. Since then, there has been an explosion in publications dealing with the synthesis of nanomaterials, specially of NPs. We, therefore, considered restricting ourselves to the synthesis of inorganic NPs in this article. This perspective provides an up-to-date report on the synthesis of inorganic NPs covering the period 20072011. We have briefly introduced some of the important methods employed for the synthesis of NPs in Section 2, citing typical examples from the literature. We then discuss the synthesis of NPs of metals, alloys, metal oxides, metal chalcogenides and metal pnictides in separate sections. We have included a brief section on the synthesis of coreshell NPs. We trust that practitioners as well as beginners will find this article useful in their efforts to prepare inorganic NPs. We apologize for any omissions and errors in judgment in citing articles from the literature.

2. Some synthetic methods

2.1. Use of microemulsions

Microemulsions consist of a ternary mixture of water, a surfactant or a mixture of surface-active agents and oil. Micelles are aggregates of surfactant molecules formed above a critical micellar concentration (CMC).4,5 Micelles are responsible for the enhancement of the solubilization of organic compounds in water (oil-in-water (o/w) emulsion) or hydrophilic compounds in the oil phase (water-in-oil (w/o) emulsion). This process has been used for the synthesis of various NPs by mixing two microemulsions containing appropriate reactants (see schematic representation in Scheme 1).

Au NPs have been prepared by the reduction of HAuCl4 with NaCNBH3 in sodium 1,4-bis(2-ethylhexoxy)-1,4-dioxobutane-2-sulfonate (AOT) water-in-hexane microemulsion.6 Ag NPs are prepared by mixing reverse micellar solutions of 30% Ag(AOT), 70% Na(AOT) and NaBH4 respectively.7 Cu NPs are obtained by the reduction of a micellar solution of Cu(DS)2(DS, dodecyl sulfate) with NaBH4. Below the CMC, CuO2 NPs occur along with the metal NPs where as above the CMC, only metallic nanostructures are obtained.8 Pd NPs are obtained by the inverse micelle formed by AOT in isooctane, by the addition of capping agents to the suspension.9 Bimetallic AuAg NPs are synthesized in a waterAOTisooctane microemulsion by the co-reduction of HAuCl4 and AgNO3 with hydrazine at room temperature.10 SnO2 NPs are prepared by mixing microemulsions formed by cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB), 1-butanol, isooctane, SnCl45H2O and 0.1 M NH3 solution, followed by heating the precipitate formed at 500 C.11 Addition of aqueous methylamine, to mixed micelles of Co(DS)2 and Fe(DS)2 results in the formation of CoFe2O4 NPs.12 Cu2S NPs are prepared by the reaction of the copper ammonia complex with an equimolar thiourea solution in Triton-X 100cyclohexane water-in-oil microemulsion.13

2.2. Thermal decomposition

Thermal decomposition of organometallic precursor compounds and metalsurfactant complexes in hot surfactant solutions has been used to synthesize inorganic NPs. Metal NPs are generated by the thermal decomposition of molecules containing zero-valent metals, such as metal carbonyls.14 Thus, Pd NPs with particle sizes in the 3.57 nm range are prepared by the thermal decomposition of Pdtrioctylphosphine (TOP) complex at 300 C for 30 min.15 Monodisperse Co NPs can be synthesized via a high-temperature thermal decomposition of Co2(CO)8 in the presence of oleic acid (OA) and triphenylphosphine at 220 C.16 Bimetallic FePt and CoPt NPs are obtained by reacting Pt(acac)2 with 1,2-hexadecanediol (HDD) and trioctylphosphine oxide (TOPO) at 100 C followed by the addition of Fe(CO)5 or Co2(CO)8 at 286 C for 30 min respectively.17 Thermal decomposition of metaloleate precursors in high boiling solvents produces monodisperse NPs (Fig. 1).18 Thus, the ironoleate complex in octadecene (ODE) is slowly heated to 320 C and aged at that temperature for 30 min to generate iron oxide NPs. Thermal decomposition of copper cupferronate (Cu(cupf)2), on a silicon substrate at 250 C for 10 min produced an assembled film of Cu NPs which on oxidation transformed to Cu2O with retention of the assembly.19 Cadmium complexes of N,N-diethyl-N1-benzoylthiourea and N,N-diethyl-N1-benzoylselenourea are used as the precursors for the synthesis of CdS and CdSe NPs respectively in TOP and hexadecylamine at 200 C for 60 min.20 Thermal decomposition of Cl2GaP(SiMe3)2 prepared from GaC13 with P(SiMe3)3 produces GaP NPs at 300 C for 3 hours.21 Green and O'Brien22 report the synthesis of InP and GaP NPs by the thermal decomposition of M(PtBu2)3 (M = Ga, In). The decomposition of Fe(CO)4[PPh2CH2CH2Si(OMe)3] in a silica xerogel matrix results in the formation of Fe2P nanoclusters.23 Thermal decomposition of long-chain iron carboxylates such as Fe(III) oleate, palmitate, and myristate influences the morphology of iron oxide NPs varying from spheres to nanostars.24 Synthesis of metal oxide nanoparticles by combustion synthesis is well documented.25 CoFe2O4 NPs are synthesized by the decomposition of a mixture of Fe(acac)3 and Co(acac)3 in a mixture of ODE, OA and oleyl amine (OAm) at different temperatures ranging from 150300 C.26 GaN, AlN and InN NPs are readily prepared by decomposing the urea complexes of the corresponding trichlorides in trioctyl amine under refluxing conditions.2730

2.3. Hydrothermal and solvothermal synthesis

Hydrothermalsolvothermal synthesis is a common method to synthesize inorganic NPs. Properties of the reactants, such as their solubility and reactivity change in the solvent or water at elevated temperatures and pressures. Taking advantage of the high reactivity of metal salts and complexes at elevated temperatures and pressures, inorganic nanomaterials are conveniently prepared at temperatures considerably lower than in solid-state reactions. Reaction parameters such as time, temperature, pressure, reactant concentration, pH, reaction cell fill volume can be tuned to attain satisfactory nucleation rates and particle size distribution. NPs of metals, oxides, chalcogenides, pnictides and various materials have been prepared by the hydrothermal or solvothermal synthesis. Thus, an aqueous solution containing HAuCl4, trisodium citrate, and CTAB is heated at 110 C for 6, 12, 24, 48, and 72 h to obtain Au octahedra with average sizes of 30, 60, 90, 120, and 150 nm respectively.31 Nanoanatase TiO2 is obtained by using tetrabutyl titanate as the titanium precursor, acetic acid as an inhibitor, and diethyl ether anhydrous as a solvent at a temperature 100 C.32 Monodispersed ZnO nanorods with an average diameter less than 50 nm are prepared solvothermally by using zinc nitrate, NaOH, ethanol and ethylenediamine at 180 C for 20 h.33 ReO3 NPs (diameter range of 8.532.5 nm) can be prepared by the decomposition of the Re2O7dioxane complex at 200 C for 4 h under solvothermal conditions.34 To synthesize PbS and CdS NPs, the molecular precursors, 2,2-bipyridyl(Pb(SC(O)(C6H5)2) and 2,2-bipyridyl(Cd(SC(O)(C6H5)2) respectively are heated in aqueous media at 100 C for 30 min.35 Monodisperse 3 nm CdSe particles are prepared solvothermally using Cd(sterate)2, Se, tetralin, dodecanethiol and toluene in a stainless steel autoclave heated to 250 C for 5 h (Fig. 2).36 -Fe2O3 (10 nm dia.) and CoFe2O4 (7 nm dia.) are prepared by hydrothermal heating of the reaction mixtures containing Fe(cupf)3Co(cupf)2, n-octylamine and toluene respectively at 220 C for 1 hour.37

2.4. Solgel method

The solgel method is generally employed for the synthesis of metal oxide NPs as well as oxide nanocomposites. The solgel process involves the hydrolysis and condensation of metal precursors.38,39 Further condensation and polymerization lead to three-dimensional metal oxide networks forming the gel. The solgel process can be either in aqueous or non-aqueous medium. In the aqueous solgel process, oxygen for the formation of the oxide is supplied by water molecules. In the nonaqueous process, oxygen is provided by a solvent (ethers, alcohols, ketones, or aldehydes) or by a organic constituent of the precursor (alkoxides or acetylacetonates).40

The aqueous solgel process can be represented as follows:

M(OR)x + mH2O M(OR)xm(OH)m + mROH(OH)

If x = m, the reaction is said to be of total hydrolysis. This can happen in two stages, either by water or alcohol condensation.4

2M(OR)xm(OH)m (OH)m1(OR)xmMOM(OR)xm(OH)m1 + H2O(OH)
2M(OR)xm(OH)m (OH)m1(OR)xmMOM(OR)xm1 + ROH(OR)

The total process is expressed as follows

M(OR)x + x/2H2O MOx/2 + xHOR(OR)

The condensation process in non-aqueous solgel synthesis involves metaloxygenmetal bonds formed either by alkyl halide elimination, ether elimination, ester elimination, or by aldol-like condensation.

del Monte et al.41 obtained -Fe2O3 NPs in a size range between 6 and 15 nm by direct heat treatment of gels at 400 C. Ceria NPs are prepared by a reverse micelle solgel technique by using cerium isopropoxide in cyclohexane.42 A nonhydrolytic solgel method to prepare TiO2 NPs, involving condensation of titanium halide with titanium alkoxide has been reported by Trentler et al.43 Various metal oxides such as ZnO,44 ZrO2,45 iron oxide,46 CeO247 and ferrites48 have been synthesized by a nonhydrolytic solgel method. Niederberger et al.18 have developed a general nonaqueous solgel route to prepare NPs of binary metal oxide such as -Ga2O3, ZnO and cubic In2O3 by the reaction between the corresponding metal acetylacetonates and benzylamine.

2.5. Phase-transfer method

Phase-transfer mediated synthesis is widely used for the preparation of various NPs. This method involves the transfer of reactants from a polar medium to a non-polar medium, followed by further processing in a non-polar medium. NPs prepared in a nonpolar medium can also be phase-transferred to a polar medium. One of the earliest reports of this method is by Brust et al.49 for the synthesis of Au NPs. The method involves the phase-transfer of chloroaurate ions into toluene using a phase-transfer reagent such as tetraalkylammonium bromide, followed by the reduction of gold ions using sodium borohydride, giving rise to Au NPs capped with alkanethiol molecules. The gold ions from the aqueous solution are transferred to toluene due to electrostatic interaction with tetraoctylammonium bromide. The movement of aqueous gold NPs into non-polar organic solvents requires hydrophobization of the NPs. Yang et al.50 report a general protocol for the phase transfer of various metal ions from an aqueous to an organic medium for the synthesis of NPs. Here, phase-transfer of metal ions from water to an organic medium is carried out by dodecylamine by using ethanol as solvent. Using this method, it was possible to synthesize noble metal NPs, semiconductornoble-metal composite NPs. Heterogeneous deposition of noble metals on semiconductor NPs, and homogeneous growth of semiconductors on noble metal NPs could also be accomplished. Fig. 3 shows TEM images of various NPs prepared by the transfer of metal ions from water to organic media. Thiol-derivatized NPs of Au, Pt, and Ag have been prepared as organosols by the acid-facilitated transfer of the NPs from a hydrosol to the toluene layer containing an alkanethiol.51 Phase-transfer of nanostructures from aqueous medium to fluorous solvent has been carried out.52 By using this approach, it has been possible to phase-transfer Au NPs from water, and semiconductor NPs such as CdSe and CdS from toluene, to perfluorohexane using perfluoroalkanethiol as capping agent. A one-step method for synthesis and phase-transfer of NPs from hydrocarbon medium such as toluene to a fluorous medium such as perfluorohexane has been reported recently.53 This method is based on the fact that fluorous and organic solvents become miscible at elevated temperatures (Fig. 4). In this manner, NPs of metal chalcogenides such as CdSe, CdS, PbSe, ZnSe and metal oxides such as -Fe2O3 and ZnO and bimetallic FePt have been transferred to fluorous media.

2.6. Microwave synthesis

Microwave synthesis is commonly employed for the synthesis of inorganic NPs since it is fast, simple and energy-efficient. Microwaves are generally generated using a magnetron. Microwave energy is absorbed by the reactants, which is then converted to heat thereby causing the rapid decomposition of the precursors and creating highly supersaturated solutions, where nucleation and growth occur to produce the desired nanocrystalline products.54 Au NPs (spherical, hexagons, truncated prisms) have been prepared in a microwave using HAuCl4 in HCl as the gold precursor and OAm and OA as capping agents (Fig. 5).55 CdSO4, Pb(Ac)2, and CuSO4 are reacted with Na2SeSO3 in water in the presence of potassium nitrilotriacetate to synthesize of CdSe and PbSe NPs, or using triethanolamine to obtain Cu2xSe NPs, in 10 minutes.56 Coreshell AuPd NPs are prepared by the simultaneous reduction of palladium(II) chloride and hydrogen tetrachloroaurate(III) in ethylene glycol (EG) for 1 hour.57 FePt NPs are obtained from microwave process using Fe(acac)3 and Pt(acac)2 as metal precursors and tetraethyleneglycol (TEG) as solvent as well as reducing agent at 250 C.58 Amorphous Fe2O3 NPs have been synthesized by microwave heating of an aqueous solution containing ferric chloride, polyethyleneglycol (PEG) and urea.59

2.7. Use of the liquidliquid interface

The liquidliquid interface is conveniently used for the preparation of assemblies or thin films of inorganic NPs. The interface is a nonhomogeneous region with a thickness of a few nanometers. NPs are highly mobile at the interface and rapidly achieve an equilibrium assembly due to reduced interfacial energy. The parameters that influence assembly process at the liquidliquid interface are: (i) nature of the interface, (ii) surface modification of NPs at the interface, and (iii) the effective radius of NPs, also smaller NPs generally attached more weakly to interface than larger ones.60 To synthesize inorganic nanomaterials, an organic precursor of relevant metal is taken in the organic layer and an appropriate reagent in the aqueous layer. The product formed by the reaction at the interface contains ultra-thin nanocrystalline films of the nanomaterial.61 This technique has been used to prepare NPs of metals such as Au, Ag, and Pd, of metal chalcogenides such as CdS and NiS, as well as extended ultrathin single crystalline films of CuO, ZnO, CuS, PbS, and ZnS.62 In a typical preparation of AuCuAg nanocrystalline film, a solution of Au(PPh3)ClCu(PPh3)Cl or Ag2(PPh3)4Cl2 in toluene is allowed to stand in contact with aqueous alkali in a beaker at 300 K. Once the two liquid layers are stable, tetrakishydroxymethylphosphonium chloride (THPC) is injected into the aqueous layer using a syringe with minimal disturbance to the toluene layer.63 By taking mixtures of the corresponding metal precursors in organic layer, nanocrystalline films of binary AuAg and AuCu alloys and ternary AuAgCu alloys are prepared.64 Polycrystalline thin films of CdSeCuSe have been prepared at the organicwater interface by reacting Cd(cupf)2Cu(cupf)2 in the toluene layer with dimethylselenourea in the aqueous layer.65 By reacting Cu(cupf)2 in the organic layer with an aqueous NaOHNa2S solution, a single-crystalline film of CuOCuS is formed at the organicaqueous interface.66Fig. 6 shows electron microscopy images of Au, Ag, CdS and CuS NPs formed at the liquidliquid interface.

2.8. Use of ionic liquids

There have been several attempts during last five to six years to use ionic liquids as media for the synthesis of inorganic nanomaterials.67,68 Ionic liquids are organic salts with low melting points and no measurable vapour pressure, and do not evaporate. They remain as liquids over a wide range of temperatures, in some cases in excess of 400 C. The low interfacial tension and the associated high nucleation rate as well as high thermal stability make ionic liquids attractive to carry out the synthesis of nanostructures. Ionic liquids have received much attention due to their potential use as green recyclable alternatives to traditional organic solvents. Polar reactions can be carried out in these liquids in the absence of water, and crystalline NPs can be synthesized conveniently at relatively low temperatures. The pronounced self-organization of a solvent is made use of in synthesis of self-assembled, highly organized hybrid nanostructures. Synthesis and functionalization of gold NPs in ionic liquids has been reported, wherein the color of the gold NPs can be tuned by changing the anion of ionic liquid.69 NPs of Rh and Ir have been prepared by the reduction of appropriate compounds in 1-n-butyl-3-methylimidazolium-hexafluorophosphate, ([BMIM][PF6]), in the presence of hydrogen.70 RuO2 NPs of 2 to 3 nm in diameter have been obtained by NaBH4 reduction of RuCl3 dissolved in the ionic liquid, 1-n-butyl-3- methylimidazolium-hexaflurophosphate.71 Biswas and Rao72 successfully synthesized nanostructures of CdS, CdSe, ZnS, ZnSe and PbS in ionic liquid media. They have examined effects of changing the anion of the ionic liquid as well as addition of surfactants and capping agents on the size and shape of nanostructures.

2.9. Other methods

There are many other methods employed for the synthesis of inorganic NPs.13 Some of these are: photochemical syntheses as typified by radiation-induced decomposition and reduction of gold compounds, electrochemical synthesis of metal NPs, arrested precipitation, the sonochemical method and solvated metal atom dispersion involving trapping of metal atoms in a cold matrix of a liquid followed by growth on warming the matrix. Biological media and reagents have been used to prepare NPs of metals and other inorganic materials.

3. Metal nanoparticles

The first scientific study of metal NPs was by Michael Faraday73 in 1857. Enstn and Turkevich74 in 1963 prepared spherical NPs of Au and Ag of tunable sizes by the reduction of gold or silver salts by citrate in aqueous solutions. During the past three decades research on NPs has greatly intensified due to the development of improved synthetic procedures which enable precise control over the size and shape.1,2,75 Metal NPs can now be made in large quantities at low cost, there by expanding the possible range of applications. Besides the methods described earlier, metal NPs have been prepared by employing many variants and by using a variety of reducing agents ranging from alcohols to fungi.76

3.1. Gold

The popular citrate route to synthesize Au NPs was first reported by Hauser and Lynn.77 The procedure involves the addition of chloroauric acid to a boiling solution of sodium citrate,74,78 and enables one to obtain NPs with an average diameter in the 10100 nm range by varying the concentration of the reactants.64,69 Au NPs with diameters between 1 and 2 nm are obtained by the reduction of HAuCl4 with alkaline tetrakis(hydroxymethyl)phosphonium chloride (THPC) which also acts as a capping agent.79 Brust and co-workers49 developed a general method to obtain organic-capped metal NPs in biphasic mixture of an organic solvent and aqueous solution of a metal salt in the presence of a phase-transfer reagent. Ji et al.80 have studied the growth kinetics and temporal size/shape evolution of the Au NPs by citrate reduction in boiling water. Kanehara et al.81 report the size control and evolution of decanethiol-protected Au NPs at room-temperature using a quaternary ammonium salt and proton acid (Fig. 7). Ohyama et al.82 have used a one-phase method for the reduction of HAuCl4 in a DMF solution containing a porphyrin-cored tetradentate passivant giving horizontal porphyrin monolayer-coated gold NPs.

8. Coreshell nanoparticles

Coreshell nanoparticles exhibit unique properties with several possible applications. In the case of fluorescent semiconductor NPs, coreshell NPs help to increase the robustness and enhance the photoluminescence quantum yield as well as the probability of radio-active recombination. NPs with magnetic, plasmonic and semiconducting properties can be used as cores or shells for manipulating the properties of these hybrid structures. Properties of each component within the hybrids can be modulated through a conjugating component or interface.

Au@Ag coreshell nanostructures are prepared at room temperature through the selective reduction of Ag+ ions onto the surface of Au nanorods, using hydroquinone as the reducing agent.510 A modified two-step seed-mediated growth procedure for synthesizing Au@Pd nanocubes, has been reported.511 Here Au NPs of about 3 nm in diameter are used as the seeds for growing about 30 nm Au nanooctahedra as the cores (Fig. 27). Uniform Au@Pd nanocubes have been generated by reducing H2PdCl4 on octahedral Au cores with ascorbic acid in the presence of CTAB. Feng et al.512 carried out the synthesis of Au@Pt nanostructures by Au nanorod seed mediated growth wherein Pt nanodots are decorated on Au nanorods by reducing K2PtCl4 with ascorbic acid. NPs composed of magnetic cores with continuous Au shell layers have been synthesized. The procedure converts hydrophobic iron oxide NPs to water-soluble Au coated bifunctional NPs.513 Zhang et al.514 report nonepitaxial growth of hybrid Au@CdS coreshell nanostructures with large lattice mismatch by controlling the soft acidbase coordination reaction between the molecular complexes and the colloidal nanostructures (Fig. 28).

9. Conclusions

The discussion in the previous sections should clearly indicate the great interest evinced in the last few years in the synthesis of inorganic NPs. In spite of this progress, we still need well-established protocols for the synthesis of many of the important inorganic NPs of desired sizes and shapes. In this context, it is necessary to develop methods, which are simple and do not use expensive and poisonous chemicals. A green chemical approach to NP synthesis is therefore important. We believe that there will be continued effort to prepare nanoparticles of various inorganic materials of different sizes and shapes in the years to come. Functionalization and chemical manipulation of inorganic nanoparticles are of considerable importance in using these materials in the preparation of composites and such applications. We have not dealt with these aspects in this perspective, since we had to limit the scope only to synthesis. We do hope that the article will serve as a ready guide to all those pursuing synthesis of nanoparticles.
