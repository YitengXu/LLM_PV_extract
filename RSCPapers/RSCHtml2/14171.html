Abstract

The development of stable noble metal-free photocatalysts with efficient separation and transportation of the photogenerated electronsâ€“holes is of crucial importance for promoting the application of photocatalysis technology. Herein, we propose an electron transfer strategy by reasonable design and fabrication of novel 0D NiSx nanosheets as a co-catalyst on the surface of 1D CdS nanorods (CdS-NRs) to enhance photocatalytic hydrogen evaluation and contamination (Cr(VI), rhodamine B and bisphenol A) removal in water. Under visible light irradiation, the 0Dâ€“1D NiSx/CdS-NR nanocomposite with 1.5% NiSx loading gave a hydrogen evolution rate of 5.98 mmol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1, which is about 5.3 times and 1.9 times higher than that of the native CdS-NRs and the optimal 1% Pt/CdS-NRs, respectively. Notably, good stability in the recycling test and a high apparent quantum efficiency of about 69.9% at 420 nm were also obtained. The 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs exhibited enhanced photocatalytic contamination degradation efficiency of about 2 times higher than pure CdS-NRs. In this hybrid photocatalyst, 0D NiSx nanosheets came into intimate interfacial contact with the surface of 1D CdS-NRs and played a similar role as noble metals, which could effectively improve the separation, transportation efficiencies and lifetime of photogenerated charge, and thus enhance the photocatalytic performance of CdS-NRs with more efficient conversion of solar energy. This work shows not only a possibility for the utilization of noble metal-free NiSx as a co-catalyst in the photocatalysis, but also provides new insight into the design and fabrication of high-performance composite photocatalysts (such as NiSx/g-C3N4 and NiSx/Zn3In2S6).

1. Introduction

The increased consumption of fossil-derived oil and serious environmental pollution as well as global climate change have become worldwide issues.1â€“3 For example, the total world consumption of petroleum and other liquids reached 96.95 million barrels per day in 2016 and continues to climb annually (98.5 million barrels per day in 2017).2 Emissions of CO2 had increased to 41.5 Â± 4.4 billion tones in 2017.3 To address the above current problems, semiconductor photocatalysis as a promising method has attracted increasing interest due to the possibility of converting solar energy into green chemical energy and environmental remediation.4â€“8 Since Fujishima and Honda9 reported that H2 could be produced on TiO2 by photoelectrocatalytic splitting of water, various photocatalysts such as metal sulfides, metal oxides, metal oxynitrides and metal-free semiconductors have been explored,10â€“14 while the development of highly efficient, visible-light driven and stable noble metal-free photocatalysts is still considered to be central to promoting the application of photocatalysis.

Recently, CdS is of increasing interest in applications such as solar energy conversion and photocatalysis due to its optimal band gap (2.4 eV) for efficient utilization of visible light, suitable band structure for the reduction of protons (H+) to H2, low-cost and simple preparation method.15â€“21 However, the individual CdS exhibited very low photocatalytic performance due to poor photostability, high activation energy (over-potential) and rapid recombination of photoexcited electrons (eâˆ’) and holes (h+).17â€“21 High-efficiency photocatalytic splitting of water into H2 is indeed a hard process. To obtain highly active photocatalysts for H2 evolution, tremendous efforts have been devoted to the designing of CdS-based photocatalysts in the last few decades.18â€“28 Loading noble metals as the co-catalyst onto the photocatalyst has proven to be an effective method,25â€“28 which can dramatically lower the over-potential and improve the separation and transportation of photoexcited electronsâ€“holes, thus remarkably enhancing the photocatalytic H2 evolution activity. Nonetheless, the scarcity and the high price of noble metals greatly limit their large-scale application as co-catalysts in practical photocatalytic H2 evolution.17â€“20 Therefore, seeking highly efficient and inexpensive Earth-abundant materials as co-catalysts is of great urgency at present.29 Fortunately, some co-catalysts based on Earth-abundant transition-metal elements including Co,30,31 Mo,18,32 Ni,19â€“21,33â€“40 Cu,41 and W42,43 and their corresponding oxides, sulfides or phosphides with low-cost have been explored and demonstrated high activity for assistance to enhance the photocatalytic H2 evolution of the host photocatalyst. Among them, NiS, a low bandgap semiconductor, has been considered as a potential alternative to noble metals due to its friendliness to the environment, high electrical conductivity and high power conversion efficiency, such as on CdS,33â€“38 g-C3N4,40 and ZnxCd1âˆ’xS.44

Despite remarkable progress, the efficiency of NiS-based composites reported so far is very limited because NiS is a p-type semiconductor, while photocatalysts (such as CdS and g-C3N4) are generally n-type semiconductors. It is easy for NiS and the host photocatalyst to generate pâ€“n junctions, which can facilitate the separation of photoexcited electronsâ€“holes due to the presence of built-in electric fields from the host photocatalyst to NiS. Specifically, the built-in electric field can promote the transportation of photoexcited electrons from NiS to the host photocatalyst, thus improving the photocatalytic activity.33 However, the photoexcited electrons produced on the host photocatalyst cannot be transferred, and the high electrocatalytic H2 evolution activity of NiS cannot be expected from photocatalytic H2 evolution. Consequently, the photocatalytic H2 evolution activity of such pâ€“n heterostructures could only be enhanced to a limited degree.19,33 That is to say, the ideal structure model of the NiS-based composite photocatalyst is that the photoexcited electrons can transfer from the host photocatalyst to NiS. In order to achieve this, Î²-NiS has been brought up because of its remarkable electrical conductivity. For example, Peng et al.36 prepared Î²-NiS film modified CdS nanoflowers with a high apparent quantum efficiency (AQE) of 43% at 420 nm at 25 Â°C. However, the crystalline phase of NiS in the reported NiS/CdS photocatalysts19,33 and other NiS-based composites40,44 is commonly Î±-NiS or a mixture of Î±-NiS and Î²-NiS because of the difficult preparation of pure Î²-NiS. Alternatively, NiSx may be a promising candidate to replace Î²-NiS because: (1) it is easy to prepare NiSx;34,38,45â€“47 (2) NiSx possesses remarkable electrical conductivity and higher work function than CdS (so the photoexcited electrons produced on CdS could be transferred to NiSx);46 (3) NiSx shows the lowest activation energy to form a Niâ€“H bond which is conducive to the adsorptionâ€“reductionâ€“desorption process of H2.45,47 However, a moderate AQE was observed on CdS/NiSx photocatalysts. Qin et al.34 first prepared CdS/NiSx photocatalysts for photocatalytic H2 evolution, showing a high AQE of 60.4% at 420 nm but at 35 Â°C. Later on, Xu et al.38 proposed a self-template synthetic strategy for the fabrication of CdS/NiSx nanohybrids by using Cd(II)â€“Ni(II)-(4,4â€²-bipyridine) coordination polymers as the template and precursor, exhibiting an AQE of 16.8% at 420 nm at 20 Â°C. In the previous reports,34,38 NiSx particles are prone to precipitate onto the surface of CdS (Na2S and NaOH used in the preparation process might accelerate the precipitation of NiSx), and the reported NiSx/CdS composites are usually composed of bulk-shaped morphology or building blocks. These greatly limit the contact area and the transfer of photoexcited electronsâ€“holes, and thus the photocatalytic H2 evolution activity. Therefore, it remains a challenge to obtain highly active NiSx/CdS hybrid photocatalysts for photocatalytic H2 evolution under visible light irradiation at room temperature.

To address these problems, we herein develop a simple and efficient hydrothermal process to fabricate a composite photocatalyst (NiSx/CdS-NRs) with 0D ultrafine NiSx nanosheet modified 1D CdS nanorods (CdS-NRs). Herein, 1D CdS nanorods other than bulk or building blocks were selected as the scaffold, because 1D nanostructures have unique advantages such as a short radial distance for the separation and transportation of photoexcited electronsâ€“holes.48 To control the growth of NiSx on the surface of CdS-NRs, thioacetamide (CH3CSNH2) instead of Na2S used in the previous report38 was chosen as the sulfur source of NiSx. The optimally prepared NiSx/CdS-NRs exhibit high efficiency and photostability for photocatalytic H2 evolution in lactic acid solution under visible light (Î» > 400 nm), reaching an H2 evolution rate of about 5.98 mmol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1 at 10 Â°C (the AQE is approximately 69.9% at 420 nm). Notably, this approach is versatile for the application of other NiSx/semiconductor photocatalysts (such as NiSx/g-C3N4 and NiSx/Zn3In2S6) with high photocatalytic H2 evolution activity. Moreover, a significant enhancement of photocatalytic Cr(VI) reduction and organic pollutant (rhodamine B (RhB) and bisphenol A (BPA)) degradation could also be obtained over NiSx/CdS-NR nanocomposites. The probable mechanism was further determined by the photoelectrochemical method, time-resolved photoluminescence (TR-PL) spectra and electron spin resonance (ESR), which indicates that NiSx is an efficient co-catalyst for photocatalytic H2 evolution and contaminant removal in water under visible light irradiation.

2.1. Preparation of photocatalysts

CdS-NRs were prepared via a solvothermal method. In the experimental procedures, 10 mmol Cd(NO3)2Â·4H2O and 30 mmol CS(NH2)2 were dissolved in 60 mL C2H4(NH2)2 by ultrasonic treatment for 10 min. After being cooled to ambient temperature naturally, the precipitates were collected by centrifugation, washed with distilled water and anhydrous ethanol several times, and then dried at 60 Â°C in a vacuum oven.

NiSx/CdS-NR samples were prepared by a hydrothermal method. Typically, 0.08 mmol Ni(CH3COO)2Â·4H2O, 0.08 mmol CH3CSNH2 and 0.5 g CdS nanorods were dispersed in 60 mL distilled water. The obtained precipitates were collected by centrifugation and rinsed with distilled water. The final product was dried in a vacuum oven at 60 Â°C.

(3) A Pt loaded CdS-NR (Pt/CdS-NR) sample was prepared by an in situ photodeposition method using a CdS-NR and H2PtCl6 mixed suspension solution under visible light irradiation.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Characterization of the NiSx/CdS-NR composite

Fig. 1 shows the results of microstructural characterization of the as-prepared CdS-NR sample. The as-prepared CdS-NRs with nanorod-like morphology could be clearly observed in Fig. 1a and b. The XRD pattern of the CdS-NRs as shown in Fig. 2 also indicates that the prepared CdS-NRs are in a high crystallinity structure and the diffraction peaks are in agreement with hexagonal CdS with lattice parameters of a = 4.1 Ã… and c = 6.7 Ã…. 3.2. Evaluation of the photocatalytic activity

3.2.1. Photocatalytic H2 evolution. The photocatalytic H2 evolution activity of the prepared CdS-NR and NiSx/CdS-NR composites with a different content of NiSx (0.25%, 0.5%, 1%, 1.5%, 2%, 3% and 10%) was investigated under visible light irradiation (Î» > 400 nm). Fig. 4 shows the average rates of H2 production during the first 3 h reaction over CdS-NR, NiSx/CdS-NR and NiSx samples in 1.22 M lactic acid solution. Clearly, the pure CdS-NRs have a relatively low activity with a H2 evolution rate of only 1120 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1, although it has strong visible light response. It is similar to the previous reports for photocatalytic H2 evolution over the CdS photocatalyst.18â€“20,33â€“39 The poor activity of the pure CdS greatly limits its applications. Although the NiSx sample was not active for H2 evolution, a significant enhancement of the activity for H2 evolution with a volcano-like distribution could be obtained by loading NiSx onto the CdS-NR photocatalyst.
Fig. 4 Activity comparison of different samples. Reaction conditions: 0.1 g photocatalyst, 110 mL of aqueous solution of lactic acid (1.22 M), a 300 W Xe-lamp with a 400 nm cutoff filter.

The highest H2 evolution rate (5890 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1) was achieved on the 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NR sample, which is approximately 5.3 times that of the pure CdS-NR sample. When the weight ratio of NiSx was further increased, the H2 production activity decreased. For 10% NiSx/CdS-NRs, the H2 production rate was just about 2010 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1. It has also been reported that an optimum loading of the co-catalyst is necessary to produce maximum activity in composite photocatalysts.18â€“20 This phenomenon in this work could be attributed to the following factors: (i) excessive amounts of NiSx nanosheets covered on the surface of CdS-NRs may shield the incident light and result in a decrease in the surface active sites of CdS-NRs, thus hindering the generation of electrons from the inside of CdS-NRs and photoexcited electronâ€“hole separation, respectively;33 (ii) pure NiSx shows no H2 production activity under the same conditions.

It has been reported that 1D nanostructures can provide direct pathways for charge transport and facilitate photoexcited charge separation, thus exhibiting high photocatalytic activity.48 To illustrate this point, CdS nanoparticles (CdS-NPs) prepared by a hydrothermal method and commercial CdS (CdS-C) were used to prepare NiSx/CdS-NPs and NiSx/CdS-C composite photocatalysts. From Fig. S3 and S4,â€  it is clear that both CdS-NPs and CdS-C exhibit an irregular nanoparticle-like shape. Apparently, NiSx/CdS-NRs show much higher photocatalytic activity than NiSx/CdS-NP (179 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1) and NiSx/CdS-C (1560 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1) samples (Fig. 4). This result clearly demonstrates that the unique 1D nanostructure of the CdS is important for the high H2 evolution rate of the NiSx/CdS nanocomposite. Previous reports17,18 have also demonstrated that the hexagonal CdS possesses a higher H2 evolution rate than the cubic CdS and the mixture of cubic and hexagonal CdS. Since CdS-NPs are in a mixture phase of cubic and hexagonal and CdS-C is in a pure hexagonal phase (Fig. S5â€ ), no wonder a low H2 evolution rate is observed over the NiSx/CdS-NP sample.

It is reported that noble metal co-catalysts (e.g. Pt,26â€“28 Au25) loaded on the photocatalyst can effectively promote the separation of photoinduced electrons and holes, providing active sites, and therefore significantly enhance the H2 production rate. Pt is considered as the most effective co-catalyst for CdS because of its high work function and high redox potential.26â€“28 Therefore, we chose the optimal 1% of Pt to be used as a reference model.33,35 The Pt was in situ deposited onto the CdS-NRs (Pt/CdS-NRs) by injecting H2PtCl6 aqueous solution into the suspension through the irradiation of visible light (Î» > 400 nm). To better evaluate the photocatalytic activity, the 1.5% Pt/CdS-NR sample has also been prepared to correspond to the 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs. As shown in Fig. S6a,â€  it can be observed that tiny Pt nanoparticles are deposited on the surface of CdS-NRs, and the deposition amount of Pt is about 1.38% (determined by ICP-MS). From Fig. 4 and Fig. S6b,â€  both 1% Pt/CdS-NRs and 1.5% Pt/CdS-NRs show significant enhancement of H2 production activity (3120 and 2721 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1, respectively), while it is still lower than that of 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs. Furthermore, 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NR nanocomposite does not show any loss of activity like Pt/CdS-NRs after photoreaction for 9 h (Fig. 5). However, the activity of pure CdS-NRs is decreased because of the known photocorrosion of CdS. Additionally, the used 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs (Fig. S7â€ ) still have a rod-like structure with almost the same shape as the fresh one (Fig. 3a). These results indicate that NiSx is an efficient co-catalyst for CdS-NRs and the NiSx/CdS-NR nanocomposite is a highly efficient and stable catalyst for visible-light-driven photocatalytic H2 evolution.

3.3. Influencing factor and the mechanism

Generally, the catalytic activity of a photocatalyst is mainly influenced by three crucial factors: light absorption, surface area (or active site), and photogenerated charge separation and transportation, among which photogenerated charge behavior has been demonstrated as the most crucial factor.56â€“58 Therefore, a series of characterization methods have been performed to understand the origin of the higher photocatalytic activity of the NiSx/CdS-NR nanocomposite for visible-light-driven photocatalytic H2 evolution in comparison with that of the individual CdS-NRs.

First, the optical properties of the prepared CdS-NR and 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NR samples were studied by UV-vis diffuse reflection spectroscopy (DRS). As shown in Fig. 8, both of them exhibit similar absorption at Î» < 540 nm, which corresponds to the intrinsic absorption of CdS (the bandgap energy is about 2.3 eV). Compared to CdS-NRs, it is clear that the absorption band edge of the NiSx/CdS-NR composite is not shifted. It indicates that nickel ions are not doped into the lattice of the CdS-NRs by cation exchange during the hydrothermal process, but rather precipitated on the surface of the CdS-NRs. This result is in line with the results of the XRD and TEM analysis. It can be explained by the solubility product constant in which CdS has a much lower constant (8.0 Ã— 10âˆ’27) than NiS (3.2 Ã— 10âˆ’19).35 The absorption level in the visible light region after 540 nm is substantially increased for NiSx/CdS-NRs, which can be observed distinctly by the color change of both samples (the insets in Fig. 8).


Fig. 8 UV-vis DRS spectra and images (inset) of CdS-NRs and 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs.

Second, the Brunauerâ€“Emmettâ€“Teller (BET) surface areas of CdS-NR and 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NR samples were measured and the N2 adsorptionâ€“desorption isotherms are shown in Fig. 8. Type IV isotherms with a H3 hysteresis loop according to the Brunauerâ€“Demingâ€“Demingâ€“Teller (BDDT) classification can be seen, suggesting the presence of mesopores (2â€“50 nm) in both samples.59 The wide pore size distributions (inset in Fig. 9) could be attributed to the irregular aggregation (cross and overlap) of CdS-NRs.33 The surface areas of CdS-NRs and 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs are about 21.2 and 18.8 m2 gâˆ’1, respectively. The slightly lower BET surface area and pore volume of 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs than those of CdS-NRs clearly indicate that the surface area cannot account for the different activity between 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs and CdS-NRs.

Third, the photogenerated charge behavior was characterized by a photoelectrochemical method and a photoluminescence technique.60â€“64Fig. 10a illustrates the electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) Nyquist plots of 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NR and CdS-NR electrodes, and the charge separation and transfer resistance across the solid-state interface layer can be reflected from the semicircles of the EIS plots.60,61 Clearly, both high- and medium-frequency semicircles of 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs are smaller than those of CdS-NRs, which implies more effective separation of photoexcited electronsâ€“holes and faster interfacial electron transfer. Fig. 10b shows the transient photocurrent response of 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs and CdS-NRs. The much higher photocurrent of 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs than that of CdS-NRs under visible light suggests a higher separation efficiency and longer lifetime of photoexcited electronsâ€“holes. Moreover, open circuit photovoltage decay measurement shown in Fig. 10c also indicates the lower recombination rate of the photoexcited electronsâ€“holes and the longer lifetime of the photoexcited electrons of 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs than those of CdS-NRs (Fig. 10d). These mutually compatible results are in good agreement with the photocatalytic activity of 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs and CdS-NRs.

Additionally, the charge recombination and lifetime over 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs have been further verified through physical means (photoluminescence (PL) and time-resolved PL techniques).63â€“65 Generally, a higher PL intensity indicates a higher recombination rate of photoexcited electrons and holes.63,64 As shown in the inset of Fig. 11, the CdS-NR sample shows an intense PL peak, while the PL intensity of 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs is much weaker. This demonstrates that the recombination of photoexcited electronsâ€“holes on the individual CdS-NRs is rapid but can be efficiently restrained by the deposition of the NiSx co-catalyst. That is an efficient charge transfer process occurred from CdS-NRs to NiSx. The charge transfer behavior was further proved by time-resolved PL spectra.65 The fitting decay curves are shown in Fig. 11. The lifetimes of the photoexcited charge are determined to be about 0.65 and 1.2 ns for CdS-NRs and 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NRs, respectively, confirming efficient charge transfer from CdS-NRs to NiSx.

Based on the above characterization and analysis, the promising photocatalytic H2 evolution performance and photocatalytic contamination removal over the NiSx/CdS-NR nanocomposite could be attributed to the introduction of NiSx nanoclusters decorated on CdS-NRs. As a result, the photoinduced charge can be separated and transferred effectively, leading to the enhancement of the photocatalytic H2 evolution rate and photocatalytic degradation efficiency under visible light irradiation. Thus, mechanisms for the photocatalytic H2 production and photocatalytic contamination removal over the NiSx/CdS-NR nanocomposite are proposed and shown in Fig. 12. Under visible light irradiation, the electrons (eâˆ’) in the valence band of CdS-NRs can be excited to the conduction band, and form holes (h+) in the valence band. Then, the photoexcited electrons could effectively transfer from CdS-NRs to NiSx through the interfacial contact between the CdS-NR and NiSx nanoclusters, and the holes could be consumed by lactic acid molecules,35 suppressing the recombination of the photoexcited electronsâ€“holes and promoting their separation and transportation. Finally, NiSx will catalyze the reduction of H+ to H2, resulting in the enhanced photocatalytic performance of the CdS-NRs for H2 evolution. On the other hand for photocatalytic contamination removal, the electrons on CdS-NRs and NiSx would be trapped by Cr(VI) or O2 to produce Cr(III) or Ë™O2âˆ’, while the holes could oxidize organic pollutants directly and oxidize OHâˆ’ to form Ë™OH. The active species Ë™O2âˆ’, h+ and Ë™OH have strong oxidative ability,5 and thus can degrade environmental contamination.

4. Conclusion

In summary, a 0Dâ€“1D NiSx/CdS-NR nanocomposite was successfully synthesized by a hydrothermal method. Under visible light irradiation, the optimal 1.5% NiSx/CdS-NR photocatalyst exhibits the highest photocatalytic activity with a H2 production rate of 5890 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1, which is about 5.3 times higher than that of the individual CdS-NRs and even higher than a conventional 1% Pt/CdS-NR photocatalyst. Notably, this versatile approach could also be applied in the fabrication of other NiSx/photocatalyst (such as g-C3N4 and Zn3In2S6) nanocomposites with high photocatalytic H2 evolution activity. Moreover, a significant enhancement of photocatalytic Cr(VI) reduction and organic pollutant (RhB and BPA) degradation could also be obtained over NiSx/CdS-NR nanocomposites, and the superoxide radical (Ë™O2âˆ’) is the major active species followed by photogenerated holes (h+) and hydroxyl radicals (Ë™OH). These results indicate that NiSx is a good noble metal-free co-catalyst to improve the photocatalytic activity under visible light. The existence of NiSx nanoclusters and the close contact between NiSx and basal CdS-NRs confirmed by XPS and TEM techniques could effectively improve the separation and transportation efficiencies of photoexcited electrons and holes, thus greatly enhancing the photocatalytic H2 production and contamination removal. This study shows a possibility for the utilization of inexpensive and Earth-abundant transition-metal sulfides (TMS) as a substitute for noble metals in the visible-light-driven photocatalysis.
