Recent development of pyridine based charge transporting materials for organic light-emitting diodes and perovskite solar cells

Manju Sheokand , Yogajivan Rout and Rajneesh Misra *

First published on 20th April 2022

Abstract

In recent years, donor-acceptor based pyridine derivatives have significantly been used in organic solar cells (OSCs), thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF), organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs), perovskite solar cells (PSCs) and many more. Pyridine derivatives are used as charge carriers i.e. hole transporting materials (HTMs) in perovskite solar cells (PSCs) to improve the power conversion efficiency and stability of photovoltaic devices due to the enhanced open circuit voltage, film uniformity and improved hydrophobicity which further increase the long term durability. Pyridine derivatives are suitable electron transporting materials (ETMs) used in organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs) to improve the current efficiency, external quantum efficiency and thermal stability of the optoelectronic devices through high triplet energy to avoid the exciton quenching, low operating voltage, and smooth film formation ability. The frontier energy levels of pyridine based ETMs are controlled by aromatic π-conjugated moieties at the ortho, meta, and para positions of the pyridine unit, which provides deep highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) levels to block the holes/excitons and appropriate lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) levels for smooth electron injection. We have explained the intermolecular and intramolecular interactions in the pyridine derivatives, which facilitate charge injection and enhance the electron transporting mobility of the ETMs used in organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs). In the review, we provide a better outlook of the recent developments in the design and synthesis of various donor-acceptor based pyridine derivatives as 4-tert-butylpyridine (tBP) additive free and dopant free hole-transporting materials (HTMs). The pyridine could form a Lewis acid-base adduct in the HTM and tris(pentafluorophenyl)borane (BCF) interacted with methylammonium lead iodide (MAPbI3), resulting in the MAPbI3/HTM interface becoming more selective for holes in the perovskite solar cells (PSCs).

1. Introduction

The emerging global energy crisis is driving the development of smart energy-efficient display and lighting systems.1–3 Energy-efficient optoelectronic technologies, particularly future energy-saving illumination sources, are considered to be one of the most competitive choices for next-generation smart displays.4–6 Emerging optoelectronic technologies such as perovskite solar cells (PSCs), organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs), and phototransistors have received a lot of attention recently because of their promising power conversion efficiencies and ease of fabrication in a variety of colors on arbitrary substrates.7,8 Organic semiconducting materials that transport charge are essential in electronic and optoelectronic devices (such as OLEDs, organic thin-film transistors, and photovoltaic cells). The fabrication of OLED and PSC devices in general demands engineering the mobilities and recombination processes of holes and electrons in an organic solid in such a way that radiative emission is maximized. Extensive research over the last few decades has revealed that organic solids with high electron and hole charge mobilities can be designed and synthesized to further improve the efficiency of OLEDs and PSCs.9–13 Under an applied voltage, the single layer device made of organic materials emits light via electron–hole recombination.14–18 However, due to the imbalance of the charge carriers, these devices suffer from a fundamental disadvantage of differing charge mobilities, in which the hole mobility is significantly larger than the electron mobility, resulting in reduced recombination of holes and electrons.19–23 The device structure of OLEDs and PSCs was improved by adding an electron transporting layer into a multilayer architecture to overcome this problem. Depending on whether an electron-transport layer (ETL) or a hole-transporting layer (HTL) is deposited over the transparent conducting oxide (TCO), PSCs are classified as normal (n–i–p) or inverted (p–i–n) devices.24 The highest PCE of 20.0%97 attained by PSC without HTL is still far lower than for devices with HTL, which indicates that device interface modification is required for effective extraction of holes from the perovskite layer. Proper alignment of HOMO and LUMO energy levels of HTMs with the energy levels of perovskite layer for better hole extraction and transportation through the HTL are important requirement for minimizing potential loss of perovskite device and charge recombination loss. 2,2′,7,7′-Tetrakis[N,N-di(4-methoxyphenyl)amino]-9,9′-spirobifluorene (C81H68N4O8) spiro-OMeTAD is the most studied HTM for PSCs to date, with a power conversion efficiency of 25%.25 Unfortunately, spiro-OMeTAD synthesis is time-consuming and low-yielding, and purification needs costly sublimation processes, making it excessively expensive. In its natural state, it has low conductivity and hole mobility, therefore it needs addition of chemical p-dopants such as bis(trifluoromethane) sulfonamide lithium salt (LiTFSI), 4-tert-butylpyridine (TBP), and cobalt(III) complexes to boost conductivity and hole mobility.26–29 However using an ionic salt to boost charge-carrier mobility degrades the perovskite layer due to their low boing temperature and seen as a compromise for device thermal stability. A range of organic small molecules, conducting polymers, organometallic complexes, and inorganic semiconductors have been explored as HTMs for PSCs with remarkable solar characteristics to solve these disadvantages.30–35 In comparison to the development of several high-performance light-emitting materials (LEMs) and hole-transporting materials (HTMs), there are few reports on good electron-transporting materials (ETMs) for use in OLEDs. After three decades of research, in 1987, Tang and VanSlyke reported, the first bright OLEDs using tris(8-hydroxyquiniolato)aluminum(III) (Alq3) as an electron transporting material (ETM).36–40 Despite of its slow electron mobility, it is still commonly used as an ETM due to its relatively good working durability. To improve electron charge balance, ETMs should have good hole blocking and electron transport capability.41–43 Because of Alq3 poor hole-blocking ability, another traditional hole-blocking material is frequently required for use with it to improve electron charge balance.44–46 This would inevitably increase the complexity of device fabrication. There are currently just a few materials that meet these criteria. One such substance is 1,3,5-tris(phenyl-2-benzimidazolyl)benzene (TPBI), which has good electron-transporting and hole-blocking characteristics. Unfortunately, in an amorphous film, TPBI molecules prefer to crystallize, which might cause stability issues.47 Another extensively used ETM with good hole-blocking properties is 4,7-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline BPhen (C24H16N2). As a result, a lot of effort has gone into developing ETM with good electron transport and hole blocking capabilities.48

Pyridine is an electron-deficient heterocyclic ring and its derivatives play diverse roles in medicinal as well as material applications. Pyridine derivatives are mainly introduced in medicinal chemistry due to their excellent properties such as small molecular size, better water solubility, and also form hydrogen bond with different receptor. In the recent years, pyridine derivatives are often used in optoelectronic applications due to its strong electron withdrawing properties. The pyridine based derivatives are also used in acid sensors due to presence of lone pair of electron on nitrogen atom.49–51 In 1994 Yamamoto and his colleagues discovered polypyridine (PPy) and polypyridine vinylidene (PPyV) having n-type charge carrier properties.52 These studies prove the importance of pyridine heterocycles in OLEDs. In the past few years, a series of multifunctional pyridine-containing electron transport materials (ETM) have been developed as hole/exciton blocking layers for electron transporting layer (ETL) and phosphorescent OLEDs, thereby simplifying the device structure and improving external quantum efficiency (ηext). In addition, the simultaneous introduction of other heterocycles to produce compounds containing hybrid heterocycles is also an attractive method to adjust the electron and hole transporting properties of pyridine based derivatives via adjusting the highest occupied molecular energy level. Hence it is important to consider pyridine for the design of HTM/ETM. Combining the pyridine unit with continuous-conjugation and using the electron-donating fragment to create prospective HTM/ETM is the most efficient technique for designing ETM/HTM. To improve the stability and efficiency of HTM for use in PSCs, it is also necessary to identify suitable non-hygroscopic dopants. The Lewis acid tris(pentafluorophenyl)borane (BCF) is the most effective organic dopant in the HTM design strategy, which intended to improve the performance and stability of PSC devices.53

In this review we have addressed the development of pyridine based charge carriers in PSCs and OLEDs to provide a better clarification on the various limitations (low yielding synthetic processes, requirement of additional dopants, thermal stability and low charge carrier mobility etc.), and delivers a future outlook in the field of charge carriers. We provide an overview of recent advances in the design and development of pyridine based organic small molecules/polymer as HTM/ETM materials. The pyridine based organic small molecules are classified based on their different active positions (ortho, meta and para positions), effect of intermolecular and intramolecular interactions on film formation ability and charge carrier mobility. A relative study of photophysical, electrochemical and thermal properties is described for better understanding the structure–property relationship of the different pyridine derivatives.

2. Applications

2.1. ETM for Organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs)

OLED is a double charge injection device that provides both electrons and holes to the electroluminescent (EL) material sandwiched between the cathode and anode at the same time in the device of OLED (Fig. 1). In organic semiconductors the electron mobility is several orders lower in magnitude compared to the hole mobility. To overcome this limitation, electron transport materials (ETM) with high electron mobility and good hole blocking characteristics are needed. To prevent hole leaking from emitting layer while simultaneously promoting electron injection from the cathode to the electron transport layer (ETL). In order to improve the performance of OLED, ETMs should have low highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy levels. Despite the instability of Alq3 cations (Hq), the well-known ETM tris(8-quinolinyl)aluminum Alq3 is still employed in OLED which causes OLED device degradation. As a result, ETMs with excellent electron transport and hole blocking capabilities as well as strong thermal stability should be investigated. To improve the material's hole-blocking performance, extensive efforts have been made to raise its HOMO energy level. Aza-aromatic compounds like phenanthroline, quinoline, pyridine, and triazole have been introduced into the conjugated system to increase the electron mobility of organic semiconductors.54–60 In the past few years, a series of multifunctional pyridine-containing electron transport materials (ETM) have been developed as hole/exciton blocking layers for ETL and phosphorescent OLEDs, thereby simplifying the device structure and improving the external quantum efficiency (ηext).61–66 The ETM properties demonstrated that the position of the nitrogen atom of the substituted pyridine ring is a key factor in determining the electron injection and transport properties and the performance of the OLED.67,68 Hence, the synthesis of pyridine-containing compounds with substitution at different positions in a single molecule is of high importance to further regulate the characteristics of the pyridine-containing ETM.

In 2008 Kido et al. reported two pyridine based ETMs 1 and 2 for application in the OLEDs due to their suitable triplet energy levels and high electron mobility.69 ETMs 1 and 2 were synthesized via the Pd-catalysed Suzuki cross-coupling reaction of the pyridyl boronate ester with 1,3,5-tri(m-bromophenyl)benzene to prepare the ETM 1 and similarly with 1,3,5-tri(p-bromophenyl)benzene to form the ETM 2 (Scheme 1). The ETM 1 showed absorption band at lower wavelength region around 254 nm, whereas the ETM 2 showed a red shifted absorption band at wavelength region around 296 nm due to better π-conjugation. To evaluate the turn-on voltage, both the ETMs were fabricated with FIrpic (C28H16F4IrN3O2)(bis[2-(4,6-difluorophenyl)pyridinato-C2,N](picolinato)iridium(III) and Ir(PPy)3 as phosphorescent emitters. The device system of ETM 1 exhibits a low turn-on voltage of 2.85 V and a low drive voltage of 3.25 V at 100 cd m−2 and 1000 cd m−2, respectively. In contrast, ETM 2-based devices exhibit a lower turn-on voltage of 2.64 V at 100 cd m−2, and lower drive voltages of 2.95 V and 3.30 V at 100 cd m−2 and 1000 cd m−2, respectively. The low driving voltages for ETMs 1 and 2 could be ascribed to the higher electron mobility values of 7.0 × 10−4 cm2 V−1 and 3.4 × 10−3 cm2 V−1, respectively.

Li et al. reported two polyaryl-substituted pyridine derivatives 3 and 4 (Chart 1) for application in ETM as well as in hole blocking layer.70 The ETMs 3 and 4 were synthesized via cyclo-condensation reaction. The ETMs 3 and 4 possess suitable HOMO and LUMO values for effective electron transport in the device. The high triplet energy ET value of HTM 3 (3.04 eV) as compared to 4 (2.99 eV) was due to the presence of more electron withdrawing cyano group at the para position which can lower the value of triplet energy level. The calculated LUMO value (>−3.50 eV) and HOMO at −6.87 eV was reasonable for effective electron injection (Fig. 2(a)). The organic layers included α-napthylphenylbiphenyl diamine (NPB) as the hole-transporting layer, 9,10-di(2-naphthyl)anthracene (ADN) as the light-emitting layer (LEL), and 3 or 4 as the ETL with a hole-blocking property. The ETL facilitates the electron injection from the cathode (Mg/Ag) to the organic layer owing to the comparable LUMO energy levels of ETM 3 and 4. Simultaneously, the HOMO energy level are higher than standard ETMs which helps to prevent the movement of holes from light emitting layer (ADN), hence act as hole blocking layer (Fig. 2(a)). By introduction of a methyl group at pyrimidine, unfavorable impacts due to the sub-atomic growth and crystallization can be avoided during the film formation which causes both the ETMs to show great electron-mobility and hole blocking capacities. The device fabricated with ETM 4 as the ETL showed a maximum current efficiency of 2.51 cd A−1 at 20 mA cm−2, although the current efficiency at 80 mA cm−2 is slightly lower i.e. 2.40 cd A−1. The external quantum efficiency of the corresponding devices based on 3 and 4 were 2.44% and 1.28%, respectively.

Kido et al. reported a series of pyridine-containing ETMs 5–7, in which pyridine was substituted at 2-methylpyrimidine skeleton and the effect of pyridine was studied on the optical properties and charge mobility of ETMs.71 For the synthesis of ETMs 5–7, the precursor a′ was prepared by the Pd-catalyzed regioselective Suzuki cross-coupling reaction between a and boronic acid of dichlorobenzene in 81% yield. The obtained tetrachloride intermediate a′ was further coupled with excess amount of 3-pyridine boronate ester and 4-pyridine boronate ester to obtain 5 and 6 with a yield of 77% and 67%, respectively. On the other hand, the tetraborate (a′′) was prepared by the Miyaura Borylation reaction of a′ and bis(pinacolyl)diborane). The tetraborate (a′′) was obtained with a yield of 61%. The ETM 7 was prepared by the Pd-catalyzed Suzuki–Miyaura reaction between (a′′) and 2-bromopyridine in 84% yield. The synthetic route of all the derivatives are shown in Scheme 2. The DSC and TGA measurements of ETM 7 showed a weight loss of 5% (Td) at 465 °C, and glass transition temperature (Tg) of 7 was at 107 °C. The melting point of ETM 6 was higher than ETM 5 and 7 due to the stronger H-bonding interactions. All the ETMs differ only in the position of the substituted pyridine rings, which results in the various degree of H-bonding interactions and follows the order 7 < 5 < 6. The strength of H-bonding was explained with help of thin layer chromatography (TLC) and DSC studies. The value of the calculated electron mobility of 5, 6 and 7 was about 10−5 cm2 V−1 s−1, 10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1, and 10−6 cm2 V−1 s−1, respectively. From the estimated values of the electron mobility, it was observed that ETM 7 shows higher mobility due to positional difference of N-atom of pyridine.

Further, Yokoyama et al. studied the effect of C–H⋯N hydrogen bonding which induced the molecular stacking, leading to higher carrier mobility in the vacuum deposited organic films using reported ETMs 5, 6 and 7.72 The authors have discussed the importance of various position of nitrogen atom in the pyridine ring. They have also prepared a series of ETMs 8–10 (Chart 1) via replacing methyl unit by phenyl ring to confirm the versatility of reported HTMs. The existence of H-bonding was ensured by performing IR studies of vacuum deposited films of ETMs 5–7 The results explained that the molecular planes of 5, 6, 8 and 9 ETMs were parallel to the surface of substrate, and in the molecular film singular orientation is caused due to the intermolecular C–H⋯N hydrogen bonding interactions. Compared to ETM 7 film, the anisotropy of 5 and 6 film was much larger, which can only be explained by the C–H⋯N hydrogen bonds in the para and meta substituted pyridine derivatives. In ETMs 5 and 6, the out facing nitrogen atoms in the pyridine ring are more feasible for intermolecular C–H⋯N hydrogen bonding interactions (Fig. 2(b) and 1(c)). The electron mobility of the ETM 5 film is one order higher than that of the 7 film, and the electron mobility of the 6 film is further one step higher as compared to ETM 5.

Lee et al. reported the application of spirofluorene-benzofluorene derivative as ETM in blue fluorescent OLEDs with an increased efficiency up to 20%.73 The pyridine-substituted spirofluorene-benzofluorene HTM (11) (Chart 1) was synthesized via the Pd-catalysed Suzuki cross-coupling reaction between pyridine boronic acid (a′) and bromo derivative of spirofluorene (a) in 99% yield (Scheme 3). The UV-Visible absorption spectra of HTM 11 showed strong absorption band at 298 nm with a shoulder peak at 318 nm which arises from the collective π–π* transition in the spiro(fluorene-benzofluorene) unit. The HOMO and LUMO energy levels of HTM 11 was determined by the cyclic voltammetry at −6.35 eV and −2.88 eV, respectively. The electrochemical studies of HTM 11 showed that HOMO and LUMO was much affected by insertion of pyridine on spirofluorene-benzofluorene moiety, and a shift of about 0.13 eV was observed in the HOMO and LUMO levels of HTM 11 due to the additional pyridine unit. The OLEDs based on ETM 11 exhibit maximum quantum efficiency of 7.72% at luminance of 3000 cd m−2, which was comparatively higher than the device based on Alq3.

Ichikawa et al. developed two ETMs 12 and 13 (Chart 1), which enhance OLEDs power efficiency due to their better electron-transporting and hole-blocking properties. The ETMs 12 and 13 were synthesized by the Suzuki–Miyaura cross-coupling reaction of bipyridine or terpyridine bromides with tris(pinacolboryl) benzene, respectively.74 The neat thin-film absorption spectra of ETM 12 and ETM 13 exhibited the absorption maxima at 274 nm and 293 nm, respectively. The optical band gap was calculated from recorded absorption spectra in the neat film and further HOMO and LUMO energy levels were determined from UV absorption spectra. Owing to the three additional acceptor pyridine units, ETM 13 attains a low lying LUMO energy level at −3.00 eV in the solution state, while the solid-state LUMO of ETM 13 was obtained at −2.85 eV, which is suitable for the transfer of electrons to the metal electrode. In addition, for these ETMs, the HOMO energy level (−6.45 eV for 12, and −6.60 eV for 13) was deep enough to prevent holes from leaking from the emitting layer of the OLED device. In the differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) curve, multiple numbers of heating scans were performed, and the glass transition temperature (Tg) was observed to be at 75 °C for ETM 12 and at 110 °C for 13. These findings indicate that both the molecules can form a stable glassy film under vacuum processing of device fabrication. The luminous efficiency (4.0 cd A−1) exhibited by the device with ETM 12 was almost the same as that of the reference device with Alq3. Due to the deeper HOMO level, the device with 13 as an ETM showed an excellent luminous efficiency (5.0 cd A−1). Electron mobility calculated as a function of electric field (μ = Dd/tTV) for ETM 12 and 13 (10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1) was comparatively higher than that for Alq3 (Fig. 3).

Sun et al. reported symmetrical pyridine substituted anthracene ETM 14 (Chart 1) for application in OLEDs in place of widely used tris (8-quinolinolato aluminum) Alq3.75 The ETM 14 was synthesized via the Pd-catalysed Suzuki cross–coupling reaction between 9,10-bis(3-bromophenyl) anthracene with pyridin-3-ylboronic acid (Scheme 4). The UV-visible absorption spectra of ETM 14 exhibited a strong absorption band at 375 nm and 381 nm in solution and solid state, respectively, which may be caused by the collective π–π* transition of the pyridine ring and the benzene ring connected to the anthracene core. There was red shift of 11 nm in the photoluminescence (PL) spectra of the film with respect to the emission spectra in the solution state. The HOMO value calculated from the anodic oxidation potential (σ) was at −5.9 eV, and the LUMO level of the compound was estimated to be at −2.6 eV. ETM 14 has bipolar transport properties, and both the electron and the hole mobilities were at 10−3 cm−2 V−1 s−1; that was about 2 times higher than Alq3. The OLED devices based on 14 as ETM were very reliable: ETM 14-based devices have a half-life of 67000 h (longer than those based on Alq3), and the basic brightness was 5000 cd m−2.

Liu et al. reported a series of pyridine-containing ETMs 15–20, in which pyridine was substituted through different positions (ortho, meta, and para) at the central pyrimidine ring, and all the pyridine derivatives were synthesized via the Pd-catalyzed regioselective Suzuki cross-coupling reaction between different pyridine boronic pinacol esters and aryl halide (I) (Scheme 5).76 The UV-Visible absorption spectra for both the ETM 15 and 16 exhibited strong absorption band at 262 nm, similarly both the ETM 17 and 18 showed absorption peak at 254 nm which could be from the collective π–π* transition of the benzene ring and pyridine ring. Shoulder peaks were observed at longer wavelength of 326 nm and 346 nm due to the n–π* transition. The calculated HOMO and LUMO energy levels of 15–20 are at −6.68, −6.42, −6.57, −6.51, −6.65, and −6.62 eV, and −2.68, −2.42, −3.10, −3.01, −3.21, and −3.17 eV, respectively (Table 1). Among these ETMs, 17-based devices attained the highest current efficiency (CEs) of 102 and 94.9 cd A−1 at luminescence of 100 and 1000 cd m−2, respectively, and the corresponding ηext was 28.5% and 26.4%. In contrast, 18–20 based devices showed relatively low CEs values of ∼97 and ∼88 cd A−1 at 100 and 1000 cd m−2, respectively.

By taking ETM 1 as a reference, Ye et al. reported a series of ETMs 21–27 (Chart 2) containing pyridine rings with different orientations of nitrogen atoms on the periphery and in the central core of the ETMs.77 Three additional phenyl rings were inserted at the periphery of ETM 1 to yield ETM 21, and three more pyridine rings with different nitrogen atom orientations were inserted with ETM 1 to give ETMs 22, 23, 24, 25 and 26, respectively. 2.2. HTM for perovskite solar cells

The power conversion efficiencies of Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) based on conventional hole-transporting materials (HTMs) are comparable to those of typical inorganic solar cells; however, the dopants used to increase the hole mobility and film-forming ability, reduce the long-term stability of these devices, preventing PSCs from being commercialized. Therefore, for potential HTM, effective design strategies must be adopted in the emerging areas of the current PSCs. Organic HTMs play a vital role in promoting effective charge transport and collection in the device and reduces the cell degradation (as a protective layer on top of the perovskite crystals). In the conventional architecture of n–i–p perovskite solar cell (Fig. 11), hole transporting layer (HTL) is deposited on a transparent conducting oxide (TCO) and a perovskite layer is sandwiched between the electron transporting layer (ETL) and the HTL. Therefore, the function of HTM in PSC is very important to improve the device performance and stability. In most high-efficient PSCs, triphenylamino derivatives 2,2′,7,7′-tetra(N,N′-di-p-methoxyaniline)-9,9′-spirobifluorene (C81H68N4O8) (Spiro-OMeTAD) and poly[bis(4-phenyl)(2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)amine] ((C21H19N)n) (PTAA) is still an obvious choice for HTM due to their high PCE of 20.8%100 and 20.2%,101 respectively. However, synthesis of spiro-OMeTAD is time-consuming and low-yielding, and its purification needs costly sublimation processes, making it prohibitively expensive. In its natural state, it has low conductivity and hole mobility, therefore, it needs addition of chemical p-dopants such Lithium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (LiTFSI), 4-tert-butylpyridine (TBP), and cobalt(III) complexes to boost the conductivity and hole mobility. A range of organic small molecules based on heterocyclic molecules i.e. phenothiazine, carbazole, triazine, triphenyamine, imidazole, quinoxaline and benzothiadiazole etc. have shown remarkable hole transporting characteristics in the PSCs. In recent years, the development of organic HTMs containing pyridine rings showed significant improvement in the device performance of PSCs due to the electron deficient character of pyridine and free lone electron pairs at the nitrogen atom. The pyridine based HTMs have attained more than 20% PCE in the PSCs even without using additional dopants.

In recent years, great progress has been made toward highly efficient and long lifetime OLEDs. High-performance OLED devices can be obtained by building the multilayer structure105 comprising of an ITO glass substrate anode, a hole transporting material (HTM), an electron-transporting material (ETM), and a cathode. One approach that has been widely used to achieve an efficient OLED device is the effective hole-transporting layer, which decreases the energy barrier between the anode and an organic emitter in a multilayer structure.102–106 Therefore, there has been a strong focus on developing new hole-transporting materials to achieve good OLED devices. During the past few years, several carbazole, triphenylamine, phenoxazine, and tolylamino cyclohexane-based compounds have been studied as hole-transporting materials in electronic devices because of their good electron-donating properties and suitable hole mobility.107–115

In 2021, Kumar et al. developed pyridine incorporated pyrene based HTMs 82–85 and studied their optical, thermal and hole transporting properties for application in the OLEDs. The desired HTMs were synthesized via the cyclization reaction of pyrene aldehyde with acetophenone derivatives in the presence of ammonium acetate and further purified by column chromatography. The optical band of HTMs 82, 83, 84, and 85 are observed at 3.28, 3.28, 3.30, and 3.26 eV, respectively, calculated using the onset value of absorption spectra. The HOMO energy levels were evaluated using oxidation potentials obtained from cyclic voltammetry. The estimated HOMO energy levels of 82, 83, 84, and 85, are −5.63, −5.61, −5.63, and −5.65 eV, respectively, and their corresponding lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy levels are −2.35, −2.33, −2.33, and −2.39 eV, calculated from the subtraction of HOMO energy levels from the optical band gap values. In TGA studies, HTMs 82, 83, and 84 exhibited high thermal stability with decomposition temperature (Td5) of 357 °C, 355 and 367 °C, respectively and irrespective to that HTM 85 show lower Td5 of 249 °C due to the presence of C–Br bond. The absence of π–π stacking in solid-state, donor–acceptor properties of molecules (83 and 84), and the less π-electron flow from pyrene to pyridine might help in achieving the balanced carrier transport, which results in obtaining high stability and improved device performance at a higher voltage. All of the compounds were tested as hole-transporting layers in multilayer OLED devices, and it was found that the device with a hole-transporting layer of 85 offered the highest performance (a maximum luminance of 17300 cd m−2, a maximum current efficiency 27.6 cd A−1, and a maximum EQE 9% at 3500 cd m−2 with 7% roll-off from 1000 to 10000 cd m−2). Among all of the devices, a 85-based device exerted the highest EQE of 9% followed 83 (8.8%), 82 (8.5%), and NPB (6.7%) (Chart 5).

3. Conclusion

Emerging optoelectronics such as PSCs, OLEDs, and phototransistors have received a significant attention due to their promising power conversion efficiencies, ease of fabrication, low turn on voltage and high mobility of charge carriers which improves the driving voltage, and thermal stability. In OLEDs and PSCs, the charge carriers (ETM and HTM) play a vital role to enhance the power conversion efficiency as well as the external quantum efficiency. Although a number of organic/inorganic based ETMs/HTMs derivatives (small molecules and polymer) have been explored, still there is a wide scope to improve the power conversion efficiency as well as thermal stability of OLEDs and PSCs device. In this review, we have discussed various pyridine based derivatives used as charge carriers (ETMs/HTMs) in OLEDs and PSCs. The pyridine unit has three reactive positions (ortho, meta, and para) which are more feasible for substitutions of various donor/acceptor moieties to tune the photophysical, electrochemical and photovoltaic properties. We have summarized the development of donor-acceptor based derivatives with pyridine unit at central core as well as at end-capping positions of the ETM/HTM in OLEDs and PSCs. The advantages of various active positions, lone pair of nitrogen in pyridine, and the ability to form intramolecular/intermolecular hydrogen bonding in the pyridine derivatives on the optical properties of OLEDs and PSCs are:

1. The incorporation of electronically active pyridine unit in the molecular design favors the multiple strong intermolecular interaction and electronic coupling between the Frontier Molecular Orbitals (FMOs) of the adjacent molecules and consequently benefits charge transport.

2. The appropriately designed pyridine ring containing molecules to improve intermolecular interaction, which arises from hydrogen bonding, can control the molecular stacking. It has been demonstrated that intermolecular stacking helps to improve the charge carrier ability of HTM/ETM and improves the film forming ability. The lone pair of pyridine ring with N-atom at para and meta positions are more feasible for hydrogen bonding to the adjacent molecules compared to the ortho position due to the inner facing of lone pair of N-atom in the pyridine ring. These hydrogen bonding interactions helps to increase the electron mobility of ETMs in the following order i.e. para > meta > ortho.

3. The widely used ETM Alq3 possess good electron mobility but possess poor hole blocking properties and needs additives to improve the hole blocking capacity. However, pyridine based ETMs show excellent electron mobility and better hole blocking properties.

4. The HTMs used in PSCs possess high cost and low thermal stability due to the use of hygroscopic dopants i.e. (lithium bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)imide (Li-TFSI), tert-butyl pyridine (TBP), and [tris(2-(1H-pyrazol-1-yl)-4-tert-butylpyridine)-cobalt(III)] tris(bis(tri-fluoromethylsulfonyl)imide). Therefore, the introduction of pyridine in the development of HTMs can avoid the use of external dopant due to the ability of pyridine lone pair to make the Lewis acid-base adducts with the Pb of CH3NH3PbI3 in the PSC device.

5. The incorporation of pyridine in HTMs provides a new strategy to develop dopant free small organic derivatives with high external quantum efficiency and better thermal stability for PSCs.

6. The electron-withdrawing nature of pyridine lowers the HOMO level of the respective derivatives and enhances the open-circuit voltage of perovskite solar cells.

7. The efficiency and steady state performance of perovskite solar cells (PSCs) can be improved via using 4-tert-butylpyridine (tBP) as an additive in triarylamine-based organic hole-transporting materials (HTMs). However, the long term stability and device performance of PSCs is significantly impacted due to the low boiling point of tBP (196 °C).

8. We speculate that the Lewis acid-base adduct (LABA) formation of pyridine in the HTM and tris(pentafluorophenyl)borane (BCF) interacted with methylammonium lead iodide (MAPbI3), resulting in the MAPbI3/HTM interface which becomes more selective for holes. This also enhanced the film uniformity and afforded a smoother morphology with improved hydrophobicity that further increased the long-term durability.

This review provides an outlook on the importance of pyridine based charge carriers, their photophysical, electrochemical, thermal and photovoltaic properties of the OLEDs and PSCs. The structure property relationship gives a better strategy to design and synthesize pyridine derivatives with improved charge mobility and better device performance for the future research.
