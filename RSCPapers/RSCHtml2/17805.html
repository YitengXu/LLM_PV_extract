Abstract

Phosphorus, one of the most abundant elements in the Earth (∼0.1%), has attracted much attention in the last five years since the rediscovery of two-dimensional (2D) black phosphorus (BP) in 2014. The successful scaling down of BP endows this ‘old material’ with new vitality, resulting from the intriguing semiconducting properties in the atomic scale limit, i.e. layer-dependent bandgap that covers from the visible light to mid-infrared light spectrum as well as hole-dominated ambipolar transport characteristics. Intensive research effort has been devoted to the fabrication, characterization, functionalization and application of BP and other phosphorus allotropes. In this review article, we summarize the fundamental properties and fabrication techniques of BP, with particular emphasis on the recent progress in molecular beam epitaxy growth of 2D phosphorus. Subsequently, we highlight recent progress in BP (opto)electronic device applications achieved via customized manipulation methods, such as interface, defect and bandgap engineering as well as forming Lego-like stacked heterostructures.

1. Introduction

The past decade has witnessed an explosion of interest in two-dimensional (2D) materials, a group of materials of only one-layer or few-atomic layer thickness. Due to the extreme scaling down in the out-of-plane direction as well as the weak van der Waals (vdW) interlayer interaction, 2D materials are ideal candidates for exploring the fundamental quantum physics and tackling the technological bottlenecks such as the scaling limit of bulk semiconductors.1–5 The most widely investigated 2D materials are graphene and transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs). Graphene is a semimetal lacking an intrinsic bandgap, leading to the poor on/off ratio in electronic devices.6,7 TMDCs are a family of materials in the form of “MX2”, where M is a transition metal such as Mo or W and was recently expanded to Pt, Sn, etc., and X represents S, Se or Te.8 Most TMDCs, e.g. MoS2 and WS2, are semiconductors, but the relatively large bandgap (typically >1.5 eV), the low charge carrier mobility and the poor semiconductor–metal interface (Fermi level pinning) severely hinder their practical (opto)electronic applications.3,4,9,10

Black phosphorus (BP) was first synthesized over one century ago as a semiconducting phase allotrope of the phosphorus family.11 It was not until 2014 that Li et al. exfoliated BP down to the few-layer form by the Scotch tape method.12 The scaling down of BP gives rise to many intriguing semiconducting properties, including a moderate and thickness-mediated direct bandgap that covers from the visible to mid-infrared light spectrum, as well as hole-dominated ambipolar transport characteristics, making it a promising building block for next-generation electronic and optoelectronic devices.13–19 The orthorhombic lattice structure of BP with reduced symmetry (D2h18) leads to the in-plane anisotropy, which enables the crystal orientation control over fundamental optical and electronic properties.20,21 Intensive research efforts have been devoted to revealing the fundamental physical and chemical properties of BP as well as the phosphorus allotropes via both theoretical and experimental methods.22–25 Meanwhile, it is of broad interest to design high-performance BP (opto)electronic devices and functional devices with versatile applications via customized manipulation methods, such as interface, defect and bandgap engineering as well as by forming stacked heterostructures.23,26–28

The aim of this review is to present a thorough perspective of this rapidly developing material. We begin with the findings on the fundamental atomic and electronic structures of BP. A detailed description of the fabrication of both bulk and few-layer BP, in particular the growth mechanism and recent achievements in the molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) growth of 2D phosphorus, is given. As the poor stability of BP under ambient air conditions severely limits its further application, we discuss the oxidation mechanism of BP under various environmental conditions, together with providing a summary of reliable and effective surface passivation methods. The functionalization of BP via interface, bandgap and defect engineering, along with current applications in versatile functional devices like diodes, inverters, memory, photodetectors, solar cells and spin valves based on customized engineering methods, is presented in the subsequent sections. Finally, the remaining challenges and future opportunities of BP are outlined.

2. Properties of few-layer BP

2.1. Crystal and band structure of BP

BP is the first discovered single-element layered material with semiconducting properties beyond group IV family (graphene, silicene, etc.). Each P atom is connected to three adjacent atoms, forming a honeycomb structure with an interlayer distance of ∼5 Å (Fig. 1a).12,24,25,30,31 The semiconducting BP belongs to the D2h18 point group with reduced symmetry compared to graphene (D6h).1 Unlike graphene, where carbon atoms are located on the same atomic plane to form the hexagonal honeycomb structure, the individual layers of BP show two kinds of P–P bonds with different bond lengths. The P–P bond length is 2.224 Å in the same plane and 2.244 Å between the top and bottom plane P atoms within a single layer (top view in Fig. 1b).30

The band structure of bulk BP can be verified by angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES) measurements in combination with ab initio calculations.12 As plotted in Fig. 1c, the dashed and solid lines represent filled and empty bands, respectively. The bulk BP crystal features a direct bandgap of ∼0.3 eV at the Z point. The bandgap can dramatically increase with the shrinking of layer thickness, even reaching 2 eV in the monolayer form according to theoretical calculations. However, the numerical value of the calculated bandgap depends on the theoretical method used, showing a variation from 0.8 to 2 eV (Fig. 1d).29 Different from the indirect to direct bandgap transition in TMDCs from bulk to ML, BP features a direct bandgap for any number of layers.21,32 But the bandgap minimum point will shift to the Γ point in a few-layer form. Such a layer-independent direct bandgap has been verified by theoretical calculations, UV-Vis absorption, photoluminescence (PL), infrared spectra, etc.18,21,22,33–37 The tuneable bandgap of 2D BP shows great potential in a wide range of photoresponse devices from the visible to mid-infrared light region, which is complementary to gapless graphene and TMDC materials with a relatively large bandgap, as illustrated in Fig. 1e.24,30 Different from graphene and MoS2, BP has an orthorhombic lattice structure with D2h18 symmetry, leading to the in-plane anisotropic property:18,38 the effective mass of carriers along the zigzag direction (y) is about 10 times larger than that along the armchair (x) direction.30 Hence 2D BP exhibits strong orientation dependence in optical, electronic and phonon properties, which will be introduced in the following part.

2.2 Phonon properties

When scaling down from bulk down to ML, the band structure of BP, especially the bandgap, changes a lot. In the synergy of the quantum confinement effect and the interlayer interactions, the phonon and optical properties of few-layer BP are greatly influenced, which have been investigated by Raman, absorption and PL spectroscopy in combination with theoretical calculations.24,25,31

The D2h18 space group of bulk BP permits 12 lattice vibrational modes while 6 of them are Raman-active (Fig. 2a). According to the symmetry selection rule,39 three of them labelled Ag1 (∼365 cm−1), B2g (∼440 cm−1) and Ag2 (∼470 cm−1) can be detected when the laser is incident perpendicular to the sample plane. As plotted in Fig. 2b and c, Wang et al. used a linear polarized laser to measure the Raman spectra of BP.20 Since the Ag2 mode represents the atomic vibration along the armchair (x) direction (Fig. 2a), the measured Ag2 peak intensity exhibits a strong angle dependence on the polarized laser direction, with the strongest and weakest intensity at 0° (polarized along the x axis) and 90° (polarized along the y axis), respectively.20 Such a relationship between the peak intensity and angle can be well described by a cos2θ function, as shown by the solid line in the polar plot (Fig. 2c). The inhibition of Ag2 intensity at 90° results from the symmetry-forbidden optical transition via the y axis. Other than the crystal orientation, the Raman peak intensity also strongly depends on the polarization angle between the incident laser and the scattered light, which determines the periodicity of Raman mode intensity.39 These strong angle-dependent properties enable the quick and precise crystalline orientation identification of BP. Other properties like PL, absorption, and transport properties have similar angle dependence as Raman spectra.21,24,41

The evolution of Raman spectra with the BP layers ranging from ML to bulk is shown in Fig. 2d. A clear shift of the vibration modes, especially the Ag2 mode, can be distinguished on different layers, which can be used to identify the layer number. However, because of the accelerated oxidation under light illumination42–44 (which will be discussed later) as well as the strong angle dependence, this method is not as effective as that in TMDCs.45 Meanwhile, with the thinning of the layer thickness, the full-width at half-maximum (FWHM) broadens to the maximum at the trilayer, followed by shrinking on further thinning down to ML. This feature is ascribed to the Davydov splitting similar to TMDCs.46,47 Another interesting phenomenon is the splitting of the Ag2 peak in bilayer BP. As the number of atoms in a bilayer unit cell doubles that of ML, the larger number of atoms results in a larger degree of freedom. Correspondingly, a new Raman-allowed mode in the component of two out-of-phase ML infrared vibration modes can be distinctly revealed, as shown in Fig. 2d. Such an effect is a signature characteristic of bilayer 2D materials and has been previously observed in other 2D materials such as Bi2Te3,48 MoSe2,46 WSe249 and MoTe2.50 Therefore, this special feature can be utilized to identify bilayer BP.

2.3 Excitons and charged excitons

Besides phonons, exciton properties are of particular interest and can be investigated by the combination of various spectroscopy techniques like PL and absorption spectroscopy. Fig. 3a summarizes the evolution of PL peak energy with layer number, which exhibits a clear red shift with the increase of layer number, indicating the shrinking of the bandgap at thicker forms.51 Thus, controlling the thickness of BP can make it cover a wide spectrum from the visible to mid-infrared region (Fig. 1e). Several groups have investigated the exciton properties of BP in detail, especially the exciton binding energy (Eb). The inset of Fig. 3a is a schematic energy diagram showing that Eb can be quantitatively extracted from the equation: Eb = Eg − Eopt, where Eg and Eopt represent the quasiparticle (electronic) bandgap and optical bandgap, respectively. In a pioneering study, the Eg of BP (ML BP on SiO2) was identified by photoluminescence excitation spectroscopy (PLE), where the PL intensity undergoes a sharp enhancement at an excitation energy of ∼2.2 eV (Eg, Fig. 3b). Since the ground-state exciton energy is ∼1.3 eV, the calculated Eb is as high as 900 meV (ref. 20) (compared to ∼370 meV for ML WSe252). In another report, however, the absorption spectrum (differential reflection) was recorded to obtain the Eg of BP, and much lower values of Eg ∼ 1.73, 1.15, and 0.83 for mono-, bi- and tri-layer were obtained for hexagonal boron nitride (h-BN) encapsulated BP on SiO2 (Fig. 3c–e).21 The PL energies are only slightly lower than the absorption energies in all numbers of layers, indicating a small Eb of ∼100 meV.21 Very recently, Zheng et al. systematically investigated the layer-dependence of Ebvia infrared absorption spectroscopy (BP on PDMS/SiO2). It was found that Eb decreases from 213 meV (2-layer) to 106 meV (6-layer). Intrinsic (free-standing) ML BP was extrapolated to have a large Eb of ∼800 meV.36 Two main factors lead to the deviation between these experimental results, including (1) the different methods to extract Eg and (2) the different dielectric environments. The first factor, i.e. accuracy of the specific method employed in each case, is hard to evaluate. For the second one, as the screening effect from the dielectric environment has a significant influence on the exciton properties, the h-BN protecting-layer would lead to underestimation of the intrinsic Eb.3,52–54 Therefore, to rule out the environmental impact (especially the dielectric), it is now an important research topic to obtain direct experimental evidence of the intrinsic Eb on free-standing BP samples.

Due to the quantum confinement effect and reduced dielectric screening in 2D systems, the excitons are in a tightly bound form. Further interaction with free charge carriers (h+/e−) can form charged excitons (2h+ + e−/h+ + 2e−), i.e. trions (labelled X+/X−).4 Extra binding energy is required to bind the free carrier; therefore the emission energy for a trion is always smaller than that for the exciton.55 The modulation of the trion/exciton has been widely investigated in TMDCs.55,56 Electrostatic modulation and chemical doping were proved to be effective at modulating the formation of trions, thereby having a deep impact on optoelectronics.3 In a typical field-effect transistor (FET) configuration with a BP channel, the exciton/trion modulation by an electrostatic field was measured by PL spectroscopy.34 As shown in Fig. 3f, the two predominant peaks at ∼1100 and 1300 nm are ascribed to the exciton (X0) and positive trion (X+) peaks, respectively. They are sensitive to the applied voltage and can be gradually tuned from X+ dominated to X0 dominated photoemission by switching the gate voltage from negative to positive.34 When applying a negative voltage, BP is p+ doped by injected holes. The excess free holes are easily bound to excitons and form X+; therefore trion emission dominates the emission process. In contrast, when applying positive voltages, the injected electron would exhaust the intrinsic holes in the p-doped sample, converting the PL emission process to exciton available. Notably, the binding energy of the trion can be calculated by the energy difference of X0 and X+ peaks, with a value of ∼100 and 160 meV obtained for ML and trilayer, respectively. Note that trion possesses a longer lifetime and a slower relaxation rate, which may strongly influence the optoelectronic performance such as photodetection.56 However, approaches to effectively modulate the trion/exciton properties via a non-volatile method, e.g. chemical doping, have rarely been reported and need further investigation.

3. Preparation of BP

The preparation of high-quality few-layer BP samples is essential for investigating their fundamental properties and practical applications. The typical synthesis methods of 2D material can be classified into bottom-up and top-down methods, respectively. Bottom-up methods include chemical synthesis of 2D samples from precursors directly by chemical vapor deposition (CVD) or MBE, which have been widely applied in graphene,57 TMDCs58–60 and h-BN.61 However, there is still no high throughput bottom-up method to grow BP due to the absence of a suitable substrate with appropriate interactions and lattice constants. Therefore, to obtain high-quality few-layer BP flakes, most samples are prepared by top-down methods, i.e. exfoliation from bulk crystals by adhesive tape or liquid phase exfoliation via sonication. In the following part, we will first review growth methods of bulk BP crystals and several typical top-down strategies for preparing few layer BP flakes. Next, the growth of 2D phosphorus allotropes by MBE will be emphasized.

3.1 Bulk BP crystals

Bulk BP crystals were first synthesized in 1914 under 200 °C and 1.2 GPa with ∼30 min reaction time from white phosphorus (WP) by Bridgman.11 This new phosphorus allotrope features relatively high density (2.69 g cm−3), much higher than WP (1.83 g cm−3) and red phosphorus (RP, 2.05 g cm−3). Unlike amorphous WP, this new allotrope of phosphorus features a highly polycrystalline form.11 As a result, BP shows much better air stability and cannot catch fire in air. Keyes et al. then found that the phase transition process from WP to BP happens within a few minutes at 200 °C under 1.3 GPa, with a typical sample size smaller than 100 μm.62 Brown et al. achieved a needle or rod-like BP crystal by heating the WP/bismuth mixture at 400 °C for 20 h, with a typical sample size of ∼5 × 0.1 × 0.07 mm3.63 In 1981, Akimoto et al. for the first time achieved a large single crystal of BP from RP at 270 °C under a high pressure of ∼3.9 GPa.64 After that, mercury or bismuth was used for “low pressure” synthesis of BP.63,65,66 In 2007, a low pressure synthesis method for a BP crystal was developed by Nilges et al. via the reaction between RP and Sn by using an Au and SnI4 mixture as a mineralizer. Based on this method, Kopf et al. further developed a new method only with Sn/SnI4 as a mineralization agent67 and this method has also has been applied to synthesize a P–As alloy recently.68

3.2 Few-/single-layer BP

3.2.1 Top down isolation. Large-area high quality few-layer BP is necessary for the fabrication of functional devices. However, mechanical exfoliation by adhesive tape suffers from the low production rate of large-area few-layer samples. Herein, several other “top-down” methods assisted by external forces have been developed. The first strategy is to exfoliate the sample with the assistance of a liquid, i.e. liquid-phase exfoliation. Several groups have demonstrated that BP crystals can be exfoliated down to few- or even mono-layer by sonication in solvents such as N-cyclohexyl-2-pyrrolidone (CHP),69 dimethylformamide (DMF) and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO).70 In the exfoliation process, a bulk BP crystal was first immersed in aprotic and polar solvents, followed by sonication to produce uniformly dispersed solutions. The solutions were then centrifuged to separate supernatants and residual bulk samples. A typical high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) image of the exfoliated sample is presented in Fig. 4b, with the crystal structure in perfect match with the BP lattice structure.70 This method can be further improved by using surfactant-assisted exfoliation and post-processing in deoxygenated water in order to get rid of the organic residues and, meanwhile, improve the exfoliation yield, as demonstrated by Kang et al.71

Since liquid-exfoliation involves long-time agitation by sonication or shear-mixing, the exfoliated sample size is quite limited, usually smaller than 1 μm2. Electrochemical exfoliation is a promising alternative due to the high efficiency (only lasts for several minutes) without the agitation process. A typical set-up is shown in Fig. 4c; a BP crystal was clamped in a cathodic Pt clamp for nonoxidative exfoliation under the simultaneous monitoring by a microscope.72 The applied negative voltage drives the intercalation of the organic electrolyte consisting of tetraalkylammonium (TAA) in aprotic polar solvents, leading to the fast expansion of the interlayer distance of bulk BP and the exfoliated layers eventually dispersed uniformly in solvents.72 The sample prepared in this way is of high quality with a hole mobility of ∼60 cm2 V−1 s−1, a thickness of ∼5 layers, and a sample size of ∼10 μm2 and can be used in inkjet printing to fabricate large-area BP photodetectors.72

In addition to exfoliation, an alternative strategy is to etch the larger-area thick BP flake down to the 2D limit by Ar+ plasma etching.74 The etching rate is adjustable and thereby even ML BP can be achieved by this method. Another layer-by-layer thinning method is to use conductive atomic force microscopy (CAFM) via anodic oxidation, as shown in Fig. 4e.73 The thick BP was transferred onto doped Si in order to facilitate the direct current (DC) transfer between the substrate and the AFM tip. By controlling the bias voltage on the tip, the selective area of BP can be oxidized with the formation of a phosphorous oxide (POx) by-product, which could be removed by a water rinsing process.73 After patterning, the contact mode height image (Fig. 4f) demonstrates the retraction features in the as-patterned region, confirming the effective thinning in the selective area.73 Moreover, this method is also applicable to insulating substrates, e.g. silicon dioxide (SiO2) by the alternating current (AC) method. The BP FET with a channel region patterned by this method shows a 50 times enhancement of the on–off ratio without much sacrifice of the on-current.73 Therefore, this method is of great potential in directly thinning the fabricated devices on the selective area and can be widely applied to other 2D materials.

3.2.2 Bottom-up growth.
Theoretical predication. In addition to BP (termed black P in this section in order to differentiate it from other phosphorus allotropes), there exists many other 2D allotropes of phosphorene with no bulk counterpart, but there is blue phosphorene that can only be epitaxially grown on Au(111). Meanwhile, although single layer or few-layer black P has been isolated via mechanical or liquid phase exfoliation; there is still no high throughput epitaxial method for the growth of large area black P with high quality (the mobility of CVD grown few-layer BP is as low as ∼0.5 cm2 V−1 s−1).75 Thus, it is highly desirable to understand the growth mechanism and explore the suitable substrates for the bottom-up growth. Zeng et al. performed density functional theory (DFT) calculations to study the interaction between black/blue P and different substrates, as summarized in Fig. 5a.76 Black P has minimal lattice mismatch when placed on the GaN(001) along the armchair direction, while the GaN(001) substrate also presents the minimal lattice mismatch for blue P. Further examining the binding energy of black and blue P to different substrates gives the largest value on Cu(111), Au(111) and GaN(001). The GaN(001) was selected because of the convenience of further device fabrication. Fig. 5b presents the structures of black and blue P on GaN(001) after 1 ps and 50 ps ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulations at 400 K, respectively. Black P was severely distorted while blue P retained its original structure; therefore, GaN(001) would be suitable for blue P growth.76

As demonstrated in Fig. 5d, the surface structures of P atoms on GaN(001) depend on the coverage (θ). At low coverage, P atoms tend to be isolated from each other instead of forming dimers. Trimers start to evolve after the coverage reaches 2/9 ML; however, these trimers will not stitch into a large island but pack into ordered arrays when θ = 1/2 ML, as shown in Fig. 5d(III). Furthermore, if an extra P atom is added into the trimer pattern, it can diffuse from one trimer to another, and finally climb down from the edge of a trimer to the bare GaN surface, with a barrier of 0.71 eV (Fig. 5c). This growth behavior is different from that of graphene, silicene or other elemental 2D materials, on which nucleation into a small island or cluster is preferred. On further increasing the coverage, the P adatoms on the top of the trimer pattern also prefer to be isolated from each other to induce the structural distortions (Fig. 5c(II)). When the coverage reaches 5/6 ML, the P adatoms will break the former trimers and form an initial state that mimics the blue P structure, as shown in Fig. 5d. Therefore, a half-layer-by-half-layer growth mechanism was proposed for blue P on GaN(001) (Fig. 5c(IV)). The adatom deposited on the ML blue P can readily diffuse (barrier is 0.29 eV) and climb down (1.07 eV) to the top of the first half-layer.

As demonstrated above, the interaction between the substrate and phosphorus atoms plays key roles in determining the final phases. To explore the appropriate substrates for engineering the growth of different phosphorenes, Ding's group investigated four phosphorene allotropes on different metal substrates widely used in graphene and h-BN growth.77Fig. 6a and b summarize the formation and binding energies of four phosphorenes on seven metal substrates. It is clear that all the allotropes have the lowest formation energy and the smallest binding energy on the β-Sn(100) surface, while they have the highest values on the Ni(111) surface. Furthermore, charge transfer between blue phosphorene and the substrates is stronger than for black P due to its planar structure. Consequently, blue P on Au(111) and Ni(111) is more stable and favorable than black P. In the case of γ-P, it is predicted to be the most stable on Ru(0001) and Ni(111) due to its largest binding energy. Therefore, it is possible to fabricate certain phosphorene allotropes by selecting an appropriate substrate. As phosphorene is unstable and can barely be transferred to other substrates by dissolving the substrates in chemical solution, low interaction between phosphorene and the substrate would be preferred to enable dry transfer.78

Theoretically, Han et al. studied the nucleation mechanism and growth behavior of P clusters on the Au(111) substrate at the initial stage by ab initio calculations.79 Isolated P atoms randomly dispersed on Au(111) when N ≤ 3, and were linked into a linear zigzag chain for 4 ≤ N ≤ 10, as depicted in Fig. 6c. Increasing the number of atoms to 11 induces the transformation from a linear chain into a straight network comprising 4-, 5-, and 6-membered rings. It is noteworthy that the cluster preserves the straight configuration instead of circling into a 2D large island, strikingly different from the growth behavior of graphene at the initial stage.80,81 The phosphorus atom number dependent cluster morphology results from the competition effect between the P–P and P–Au bonds. The driving force for the P aggregation when N ≥ 4 is the Mulliken bond population difference of P–P and P–Au. It will bond into chains when P–P interaction is strong enough to overcome the coupling with the Au substrate (P–Au interaction). With the increase of P atom numbers, the formation energy of P clusters decreases monotonically, as shown in Fig. 6d (square and triangle). To evaluate the formation possibility of black P on Au(111), Han et al. also calculated four PN clusters (N = 10, 13, 19, 24) which are truncated from black P (hexagonal in Fig. 6d). The formation energy is obviously much higher than that of blue P, which is mainly due to the fewer P atoms bonded with Au substrate. Extending to ML black P and blue P on Au(111), the vertical distance between ML black P (blue P) and the Au(111) surface is 2.627 Å (2.419 Å). Fig. 6e and f present the charge density difference (CDD) between ML blue P/black P and the Au substrate. According to Bader analysis, each P atom in blue P accepts 0.039e, while that in black P accepts only 0.019e. It is obvious that only half-layer black P interacts with the Au(111) surface while almost all the atoms in blue P can attract electrons from gold; therefore, blue P is energetically more favorable than black P on Au(111).

Furthermore, to explore a suitable substrate to grow black P, Gao et al. performed ab initio energy optimizations and molecular dynamics simulations of P27 nanoflakes on Cu(111), h-BN and modified h-BN.82 They found that the stability of phosphorene strongly depends on the interaction strength between the nanoflake and the substrate. The strong interaction (0.75 eV per P atom) with Cu(111) breaks down the phosphorene nanoflake, while the weak interaction (0.063 eV per P atom) with h-BN fails to stabilize the structure of black P. In particular, the edge construction of the flake on h-BN has an energy barrier of only 17.5 meV, indicating a fast edge reconstruction that can barely retain the structure. Modulating the h-BN substrate to have a moderate interaction of 0.35 eV per P atom, the edge reconstruction barrier dramatically increases to 1.31 eV. Therefore, the edge wrapping of black P flake can be prohibited, while the inner part of P27 preserves the pristine structure of black P. Although theoretical calculations have provided guidelines for the selection of substrates with appropriate interaction strength, the experimental realization of the epitaxial growth of ML black P is still a big challenge.

4. Oxidation and surface protection

4.1 Surface oxidation mechanism

Besides the lack of a high throughput fabrication method of large area BP thin films, one major challenge restricting its application is its poor stability against an ambient atmosphere, particularly its severe degradation under illumination.42,43 The lone pairs at the surface of phosphorus make it active to oxygen. It is essential to identify how the oxygen interacts with BP and whether the active oxygen species are energetically favorable or not. In the Heyd–Scuseria–Ernzerhof (HSE) band structure of ML BP (Fig. 9a), the valence band maximum (VBM) and the conduction band minimum (CBM) are located at −5.46 and −3.95 eV with respect to the vacuum level, respectively, which shows a direct bandgap of 1.51 eV.42 The redox potential of O2/O2− is located in the bandgap; therefore, the BP has the potential to dissociate O2. Fig. 9b summarizes several possible configurations of isolated oxygen on BP based on theoretical calculations.19 The dangling oxygen binding on top of the phosphorus atom with a bond length of 1.5 Å has the highest binding energy. The second stable configuration is the interstitial bridged oxygen, which penetrates into the lattice, forming a bridge between two P atoms. The formation of this configuration is highly exothermic (Eb = −1.6 eV).19 Besides these two stable configurations, oxygen has other metastable binding sites on phosphorus, including a diagonal bridge and a horizontal bridge, as shown in the table of Fig. 9b. These chemisorbed oxygen atoms introduce an acceptor state near the conduction band and perturb the valence band state. However, if only considering oxygen itself, the energy barrier of oxygen dissociation is ∼0.56 eV (Fig. 9c) and, thus, the dissociation requires external energy excitation such as light illumination. Ziletti et al. proposed an indirect way that oxygen molecule physisorbs at 3.2 Å parallel to the phosphorene surface, while the oxygen is in the triplet ground state. When the oxygen molecule gets closer to the surface, the doubly degenerated π*2p orbitals are lifted due to the hybridization with the lone pairs of phosphorene.19 As depicted in the inset of Fig. 9c, the crossing of the triplet to singlet state occurs when the oxygen molecule is 1.9 Å above the phosphorene surface. Then, the oxygen molecule bridges the neighboring phosphorus atoms to form a diagonal bridge. The dissociation of this bridge O2 to dangling oxygen needs only 0.15 eV. It can be concluded that the oxidation of phosphorene is restricted by both the high energy barrier to dissociate the oxygen molecule and also the triplet-to-singlet conversion. However, it is possible to induce surface oxidation of phosphorene under light illumination, as proposed in Fig. 9d.

Following previous research, Zhou et al. proposed that the degradation of BP in O2 and H2O under light involves three steps.42 The first step is the generation of O2− under light (eqn (1)), and then the O2− dissociates after landing on phosphorus to form two P–O bonds. Finally, the hydrogen bond in the H2O molecule interacts with the dangling oxygen and pulls the P atoms out of the surface, resulting in the breakdown of the surface BP, as demonstrated in the snapshots of AIMD simulations (Fig. 9e).42,86 However, it is noteworthy that the fully oxidised layer can stabilize the BP surface (Fig. 9f). The top panel is partially oxidized without the formation of P–O–P bonds, where the H2O interacts with the oxygen to break the P–P bond. For comparison, in the case of the fully oxidized layer with the P–O–P bonds, although the surface is also terminated with dangling oxygen to attract the H2O molecules near the surface, the formation of the intralayer P–O–P bond is much stronger than the P–P bond, and hence no bonds can be broken. This oxidized layer can serve as a protective layer to inhibit further degradation.87

The aforementioned theoretical studies demonstrated the role of oxygen, water and light in the surface degradation of BP, focusing on the surface oxidation mechanism. However, owing to their practical application in electronic devices, such as FETs, it is vital to explore the cooperative effect of oxygen, water and light on the BP degradation mechanism under in operando conditions to stimulate ambient environments. The transport properties of BP under different environments (O2, N2, or O2 under illumination, air, dry air, humid O2, or humid N2) and the polarization effect of water on BP oxidation were explored based on the combination of the FET, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and DFT approaches (Fig. 10).44,88 Exposure of the as-fabricated BP FET to pure O2 largely attenuates the electron conductance by nearly three orders of magnitude while it keeps hole conductance intact at the same time (Fig. 10a). It is noteworthy that annealing under an inert atmosphere like Ar could nearly recover the on-current of the electron branch to the pristine state, implying the physisorptive nature of oxygen on the BP surface. This assumption can be well supported by in situ XPS spectra, where neither the shift of the P 2p peak nor the appearance of phosphorous oxide can be observed.44 The striking difference between electron and hole mobilities after oxygen exposure is mainly ascribed to the fact that the physisorbed O2 molecules can serve as electron trapping centers and then scatter negative charge carriers, thus resulting in severely reduced electron mobility, but the scattering of holes is minimal.44 In contrast, exposure to O2 under laser illumination (515 nm, ∼1.5 W cm−2) attenuates both hole and electron conductance, which cannot be recovered by annealing. The XPS P 2p core level clearly shows a new peak at ∼134.5 eV, corresponding to the phosphorous oxide (POx) species. The severe degradation of the device properties after oxygen exposure under light illumination is consistent with the previously proposed photoinduced oxidation of BP.42

Besides the light induced oxidation in oxygen, a new degradation channel in a dark environment via water-catalyzed oxidation was proposed.88 Firstly, to determine the role of water alone, BP was exposed to humid N2 in a dark environment. The as-fabricated device only shows a slight n-doping after water exposure, with both the electron mobility and concentration slightly increased. In situ XPS spectra under different exposure times and after annealing revealed that the BP is not oxidized in contact with water in a dark environment. However, upon exposure to humid O2, both the electron and hole conductance decreased significantly (Fig. 10d), showing a dramatic on-current decrease by over two orders of magnitude after 19.5 h exposure (inset of Fig. 10d).88 The hole mobility dropped from 163.7 to 1.3 cm2 V−1 s−1 after 23.5 h exposure, while the electron mobility sharply decreased from 5.7 to 0.2 cm2 V−1 s−1 only after 3 h exposure. Such degradation can be ascribed to the oxidation of BP in humid oxygen as further confirmed by the in situ XPS study (Fig. 10e).88 The peak at 134.5 eV is exactly the same as the light-induced oxidation case and can be ascribed to POx. In further theoretical studies, it is found that the strong polarization effect of H2O molecules leads to the downward shift of the energy levels of degenerated π*2p orbitals of O2 (3Σg−), i.e. the electron affinity of O2 is enhanced in the presence of H2O (Fig. 10f). Thereby, electrons in BP could spontaneously move to the O2–H2O composite and form P–O bonds. Water molecules then form hydrogen bonds with O atoms in P–O bonds and further destroy the BP lattice.42,88

4.2 Surface protection

As demonstrated above, oxygen itself can barely induce the oxidation of BP; either water or light illumination can stimulate the formation of active oxygen species and further induce the POx to degrade the device performance. Therefore, effective surface protection methods are necessary for long working lifetimes of BP based devices. Typical surface protection layers including noncovalent and covalent bonding will be reviewed in detail in this section.

The most widely used protection method is to passivate the as-fabricated device with Al2O3 due to its compact structure and easy access via atomic layer deposition (ALD). However, the BP device with a 30 nm Al2O3 capping layer (with sidewalls fully covered) cannot retain well the pristine device performance, resulting from the slow diffusion of adsorbates through the capping layer (Fig. 11a).89,93 In comparison, double-layer protection, which consists of 25 nm Al2O3 and a layer of spin-cast DuPont Teflon-AF fluoropolymers, could achieve good long-term stability (Fig. 11b). The improved stability can be attributed to the hydrophobic nature of the fluoropolymer to prevent the water penetration to the BP surface, thereby avoiding the water-catalyzed oxidation.89 h-BN is another promising capping layer due to its chemically inert, pinhole free and atomically flat surface, and has been widely used to reduce the charged impurity scattering in 2D materials.3,94,95 As shown in Fig. 11c and d, Avsar et al. used graphene and h-BN as the contact and the top dielectric, respectively.90 The atomically thin graphene contact allows the full sealing of the BP channel and gets rid of the surface adsorbates and the charge trap induced hysteresis, thus achieving robust device performance with over 2 month air exposure.90

Self-assembled monolayers (SAM) are another promising choice due to their easy access by solvent immersion or physical vapor deposition (PVD), as well as their ability to self-assemble into stable and well-ordered MLs on numerous substrates.96–98 The effect of self-assembled perylene-3,4,9,10-tetracarboxylic dianhydride (PTCDA) on BP was investigated by Zhao et al. from a theoretical perspective.91 PTCDA neither forms chemical bonds nor breaks the original electronic properties of BP as it physically adsorbs on BP via vdW epitaxy.91 The discrete nucleus islands form large area films with herringbone patterns due to the intermolecular hydrogen bonding (C–H⋯O, Fig. 11e). Molecular dynamics simulation of PTCDA's passivation ability revealed that ML PTCDA cannot isolate well the air atmosphere (simulated density is ∼500 times higher than the atmosphere), while 7 MLs or thicker are good enough to protect the underlying BP. Besides PTCDA, organic molecules like octadecyltrichlorosilane (OTS),99 7,7,8,8-tetra-cyano-p-quinodimethane (TCNQ),100 and ligands like titanium sulfonate (TiL4)101 have also proved to be effective noncovalent surface protection layers.

Covalent passivation utilizes the chemical reactivity of BP to form stable chemical bonds with adsorbates. Ryder et al. found that covalent aryl diazonium (4-nitrobenzene-diazonium and 4-methoxybenzenediazonium) functionalization not only induces p-type doping, resulting in enhanced hole mobility and on/off ratio, but also suppresses the chemical degradation, with the morphology unchanged after three weeks ambient exposure.102 A thorough theoretical investigation of covalent functionalization was then performed by Li et al.92 The optimized structure of the phenylenevinylene (PV)–BP covalent bond is presented in Fig. 11g. With the formation of the P–C covalent bond between BP and PV on the top layer, a new dangling bond appears at the sublayer (or a singly occupied molecular orbital lies between the bandgap, Fig. 11h), which is unstable and can easily react with the cations (H+) or anions in solution (Fig. 11h).92 In the hydrogenation process, the midgap state disappears while the top valence band is flattened and the curvature decreases. As a result, the effective hole mass increases from 6.23m0 to 9.48m0, hence attenuating the hole mobility. However, as plotted in Fig. 11i, the CBM is still above the redox potential of (O2/O2−), implying the possibility of superoxide anion generation, i.e. not a promising surface protecting method (Fig. 11i). Moving forward, the stability of different poly-phenylenevinylene (PPV) derivative functionalized BP was simulated. The modulation strength of decoration groups is determined by their electrophilic ability. As shown in Fig. 11i, OCH3− and F− slightly decrease the CBM of PPV-BP by 0.04 and 0.19 eV, respectively, but O2/O2− still lies below the CBM. CN− and NO2− can significantly shift the CBM of PPV-VP downward to be lower than O2/O2−, indicating the formation of an effective protection layer.

5. Functionalization

Surface charge transfer doping by introducing functional adlayers on the surface of semiconductors is a convenient way to realize effective and non-destructive n- or p-type doping. It has been demonstrated that the transfer properties of BP can be effectively modulated via in situ surface functionalization with cesium carbonate (Cs2CO3, n-doping) and molybdenum trioxide (MoO3, p-doping).27 Other species such as Cu, Al, K, benzyl viologen, self-assembled layers and cross-linked poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) have been demonstrated to be effective dopants.103–108 Besides manipulating the charge carrier type and concentration, a tuneable bandgap is highly desirable in (opto)electronic applications. Various methods have been developed to manipulate the bandgap of BP and will be reviewed in the following section. Meanwhile, customized intercalation and defect engineering enable the effective functionalization of BP devices, thus opening up a fast-emerging research field.

5.1 Bandgap engineering

In 2015, Kim et al. reported the widely tuneable bandgap of few-layer BP with in situ potassium doping as discussed below.109 In pristine BP, the valence band (Γ2+) and the conduction band (Γ4−) of BP are located at the Brillouin zone center with a predicated Eg of 0.7 to 1.6 eV (ref. 110) (with a vertex in VB at −0.15 eV). After introducing K atoms, the BP is slightly n-doped (Fig. 12b), with a small downward shift of VB (Eg ∼ 0.6 eV, Fig. 12d). On further increasing the dopant density (0.36 ML), the bandgap gradually reduces to zero, indicating a transition from a moderate-gap semiconductor to a band-inverted semimetal (Fig. 12c). When VB and CB come into contact with each other, the band dispersion in kx is linear, whereas that in the ky direction is still parabolic (schematic drawing in Fig. 12c). Fig. 12e is a 3D representation of ARPES spectra as a function of dopant density. The diamonds and circles denote the positions of VBM and CBM, respectively. With the increase of dopant density, the Γ2+ and Γ4− get closer to each other and cross at a critical point of 0.36 ML, where band inversion occurs. The bandgap as a function of the dopant density is plotted in Fig. 12f; it is clearly shown that the bandgap of BP can be continuously tuned in the range of +0.6 to −0.2 eV via the potassium doping. They further performed DFT calculations based on four-layer BP with a single K atom on each 2-by-2 surface unit cell to elucidate the origin of this bandgap variation. At a distance (d) of ∼3.67 Å (2.76 Å at equilibrium), the calculated bandgap is ∼0 eV. In the meantime, the uniformly spatially distributed Ev and Ec states in pristine BP are strongly separated in opposite directions in the presence of a K atom, resulting from the strong local electric field generated by K anions. This phenomenon is termed the giant Stark effect, as previously reported in h-BN111,112 and TMDCs113,114 and further confirmed in BP from STM37 and double-gated115 FET perspectives.

Recently, a theoretical study placed K atoms at the opposite site of the BP layer, which is supposed to have a minimal effect on the bandgap due to the neutralization of double-sided electric fields.121 However, simulated results show that it can still change the bandgap when varying the distance between the dopant and the BP surface. Thus, they believed that it was surface charging of donated electrons from K dopants and its screening of the K ion potential rather than the giant Stark effect that leads to the variation of bandgap, but more experimental study of this standpoint is needed.121 Besides alkali doping and electrical field, it was also demonstrated by Zhang et al. recently that strain could modulate the bandgap of BP.122 Different from the monotonic redshift of the bandgap by the electrical field, tensile and compressive strain enables the blueshift and redshift of the bandgap, respectively, thus enabling a much wider modulation range.122

5.2 BP intercalation compounds (BPICs)

The interlayer spacing of BP is about 5.3 Å, which is roughly 2 Å larger than that in graphite, making it possible to be intercalated by alkali metal atoms.123 However, BP will be corroded when it comes into contact with the alkali metal vapor, and, thus, it is challenging to prepare stable intercalation compounds.124 To overcome this issue, Grigorieva's group employed a liquid ammonia intercalation method by immersing BP in a metal–liquid ammonia solution at a temperature of −78 °C in a dry-ice/isopropanol bath, which has been used to prepare intercalated MoS2 with superconductivity.125 As shown in Fig. 13a and b, several alkali and alkali-earth metal intercalated BP compounds have been successfully prepared which exhibit superconductivity with a Tc of 3.8 ± 0.1 K.116 Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) was used to determine the chemical composition of the intercalated compounds, which present average concentrations of 20, 17, 18 and 8 at% for K, Rb, Cs and Ca intercalated crystals, respectively (Fig. 13a).116 The elemental distribution is uniform based on the mapping data. Fig. 13b shows the temperature dependence of dc magnetic susceptibility for Li, K, Rb, Cs and Ca intercalated BP compounds under zero-field cooling (ZFC) and field-cooling (FC) conditions. It is obvious that all the curves show a sharp change at 3.8 K, a typical feature of the superconducting transition. The inset in Fig. 13b is a plot of dχ/dT as a function of T, exhibiting a Tc = 3.8 ± 0.1 K for all compounds. This property is strikingly different from the alkali intercalated graphite, where the Tc strongly depends on the chemical composition, ranging from 0.02 K for CsC6, 11.5 K for CaC6 and non-superconducting for Li intercalated graphite.126 Based on the controlled experiments, superconductivity is ascribed to electron-doped phosphorus, whereas the intercalated layers mainly play the role of charge reservoirs.116

The modification of BP with alkali and alkali-earth atoms can modulate the electronic structure, but these compounds are very sensitive to ambient atmosphere exposure. Therefore, it is necessary to prepare intercalated BP with high mobility and stability.

Very recently, Loh's group demonstrated an approach to intercalate BP with alkali metal hydride.119Fig. 13g schematically shows the two-step intercalation processes. Lithium was first evaporated from a getter source under ultra-high vacuum (UHV), followed by annealing in hydrogen (2 × 10−5 Torr, 450 K) to stabilize the Li cation. The final product is a quasi-ML LiH intercalated BP.119 To confirm the quasi-ML feature of the final product, PL spectra were measured on the bulk BP and the intercalated region (Fig. 13h). No PL peak can be detected on the bulk surface, while the intercalated area shows a PL emission at 729 and 748 nm, which is close to the previously reported oxygen etched86 or mechanically exfoliated ML BP.130Fig. 13i plots VBM as a function of the atomic ratio of the deposited LiH. It is obvious that there are two stages, corresponding to two distinct phases. For the typical K doping (Fig. 12), the doping induced VBM shift changes monotonically with the atomic ratio of dopant. However, for LiH, when the atomic ratio of LiH is larger than 0.15, the VBM abruptly shifts to 1.23 eV, implying the formation of quasi-ML phase. To evaluate the stability of the LiH-intercalated BP, AFM was used to directly monitor the surface morphology. Small bumps can be seen after the exfoliation of BP under ambient conditions, which is ascribed to the formation of phosphoric acid.131 However, as shown in Fig. 13j (top panel), LiH-intercalated BP exhibits no change after the ambient atmosphere exposure for one month, as evidenced by the smooth line profile at a thickness of 5 nm.119 In a control experiment, hydrogenation of BP alone barely has any protection against degradation. Fig. 13j (bottom) shows the AFM image of the hydrogenated BP after 3 days exposure to air. Bright bubbles with high density and average height above 60 nm can be found on the whole surface. The nontrivial stability of LiH-intercalated BP has several origins, which is the same as the increased stability of the 1T′ phase in LiH-intercalated MoS2.132 First, LiH is less reactive than Li with air and water. Second, LiH can act as an effective electron donor to donate electrons to the electron trap sites or neutralize the hole oxidizers in BP. To evaluate the quality and stability of LiH-intercalated BP, a FET device was fabricated. As shown in Fig. 13k, the sample maintains a high mobility of up to ∼800 cm2 V−1 s−1 and an on/off ratio of >103 even after 18 days’ exposure to air (20 °C, 56% humidity, natural indoor light illumination). In contrast, the FET device with pristine BP died after 2 days’ air exposure (inset in Fig. 13k). The LiH intercalation method, which has been proved to be an effective way to both tune the electronic properties and protect the materials from degradation, should also be applicable for other vdW layered materials.

Alkali metal atoms possess low electron negativity and small atom radius and, thus, are easy to intercalate, but how to intercalate large organic molecular remains a challenge. Very recently, Duan's group reported an electrochemical molecular intercalation approach, where a standard BP FET was immersed in a saturated molecular solution (cetyl-trimethylammonium bromide in N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone) coupled with a platinum counter-electrode and an Ag/AgCl reference electrode. By controlling the electrochemical voltage, the intercalation ability could be easily controlled by the driving voltage (1.4–2 V for 4 to 10 layers and >3 V for ML BP).127Fig. 14a (top gate transfer characteristic) shows a largely increased on/off ratio (from <10 to >105) of BP FET after several intercalation processes, implying the ‘thinning’ of bulk BP. Further in situ PL investigation reveals a gradual evolution from the absence of PL to a prominent emission peak at 898 nm (1.38 eV), 710 nm (1.75 eV) and 548 nm (2.26 eV) during the intercalation process, corresponding to trilayer, bilayer and ML BP exciton emission, respectively (Fig. 14b). The relatively large emission energy can be attributed to the strain-induced bandgap expansion and broken orbital symmetry.133 The intercalation nature was then investigated by various methods such as AFM, XRD and cross-section TEM, which clearly demonstrate an interlayer distance expansion from 5.24 Å to 11.21 Å (Fig. 14c and d). This method is of great universality and can be applied for intercalating various molecules into different kinds of 2D materials such as semiconductors (MoS2), thermoelectric materials (SnSe), semimetals (NbSe2) and topological insulators (Bi2Se3).127

5.3 Characterization of defects in BP

Both interface and defect engineering are promising strategies for the modulation of fundamental physical properties of low dimension materials.3,134 To achieve desired functionalities by defect engineering, it is of great significance to understand the effect of defects first. In the early work on BP, a variety of theoretical studies have been carried out to reveal different types of defects, and their effects on the electronic properties as well as the surface instability of BP, but experimental characterization studies of the defects in BP are few in number. Benefiting from its weak interlayer van der Waals interactions, BP can be cleaved along the [001] direction in UHV, which gives rise to an atomically clean and flat surface for in situ STM studies.

As seen from the STM image (Fig. 15a), the puckered structure of BP exhibits a zigzag pattern along the [010] direction, leading to periodic zigzag rows which correspond to the upper plane of BP.129 Scanning tunnelling spectroscopy (STS) measurements on it illustrate a bandgap of 0.317 ± 0.036 eV (Fig. 15b). Fig. 15c summarizes the positions of VBM and CBM for 48 individual measurements, showing that the Fermi level resides within ±30 mV of the valence band edge. This means that BP is strongly p-doped, consistent with another STM study.128Fig. 15d is a large-area STM image of BP at the sample bias of −0.1 V (near the valence band edge). Several elongated, dumbbell-shaped protrusions which exhibit charge distribution along the [100] direction can be found, but show different brightnesses. The inset of Fig. 15d highlights the measured heights of these protrusions, giving a value ranging from 0.01 to 0.2 nm. These differences are ascribed to the defects beneath the surface with various depths.129Fig. 15e shows two examples of such defects with different depths. Examining the STM topographies reveals that both the size and brightness of the dumbbell are mirror-symmetric along the [010] direction. It is noted that these images were taken at −0.1 V (sample bias close to the valence band edge). Tuning the bias presents different information of the defects, as shown in Fig. 15f and g. At negative bias, the defects are surrounded by a bright halo, while positive bias gives rise to a dark halo surrounding.129 The dark halo at positive sample bias is due to the repulsive interaction between the negatively charged defect and electron tunnelling into empty states, while the bright halo at negative sample bias is ascribed to the attraction between the defect and hole tunnelling into occupied states.129 The tip bias induced morphology variation has also been observed in the Si impurities on the GaAs semiconductor surface, which is ascribed to the ionization of the donor.135 The charge state of the Si donor in GaAs was manipulated with a STM tip that was fully controlled by the position of the tip and the voltage applied. In the current case, to elucidate the electronic properties of the defect in BP, STS measurements were performed, as shown in Fig. 15h. The green curve is the STS taken on BP far away from the defects, shown as a reference. The dI/dV spectrum taken on the defect shows two weak peaks at −0.13 eV (P1) and −0.21 eV (P2) and a sharp peak at 0.31 eV (P3). This sharp peak implies a tip-induced deionization of shallow acceptor states. As schematically depicted in Fig. 15i (top panel), at negative sample bias, downward band bending shifts the acceptor state below EF, thus facilitating the formation of stable negatively charged acceptor states. In contrast, the tip-induced band bending at positive bias shifts acceptor states above EF, resulting in the deionization of the acceptor. Therefore, the P3 peak corresponds to the tip-induced deionization of acceptor states.129

To further elucidate the nature of the electronic states, DFT calculations were performed to show the simulated STM images. It is noted that only taking the Coulomb interaction of the hole with the negatively charged acceptor center and the respective hydrogen-like states into account can reproduce the features. Vacancies are the most common defects in BP.137–139 Their crystal and electronic structures have been characterized by STM and STS, interpreting the origin of intrinsic p-doping of BP. Similar to graphene, engineering BP with vacancies or foreign atoms can also modulate their electronic properties,68,140,141 or even induce magnetism,142–144 thus broadening their applications. In addition, an atomic scale study on the reaction of oxygen and water with the defects would be helpful to understand the degradation mechanism of BP. However, experimental characterization at the atomic scale is still rarely reported, mainly due to the challenges in high-quality sample preparation under UHV.

6. Applications

6.1 Electronic devices

2D BP was rediscovered in 2014 as an attractive channel material for FETs because of its semiconducting nature, such as high on/off ratio ∼105, hole dominated ambipolar transport characteristics and planar surface in favor of the integration with other low-dimension materials.12,14 The typical BP FET demonstrated by Li et al. is plotted in Fig. 17a–d.12 Few-layer BP (∼5 nm) was exfoliated from the bulk crystal and further configured as a FET device with Cr/Au contact. The fabricated device showed clear hole-dominated ambipolar conductance. The hole mobility of the sample is as high as 984 cm2 V−1 s−1, which is comparable to or even larger than TMDC based FETs.56,145 Meantime, the electron branch in this device is also clearly distinguishable in the positive Vg range.12 The bandgap of BP is strongly layer-dependent and approaches 0.3 eV in this 5 nm-thick sample, which makes it easy to tune the Fermi level from below VBM to above CBM with the electrostatic field. Contact performance of this device is then demonstrated by measuring the Ids–Vds curve (Fig. 17b), which shows good/poor linearity in the hole/electron branch, representing the ohmic and Schottky contact performance in p and n branches, respectively.12 Furthermore, to reveal the dominated scattering mechanism on BP's transport characteristics, temperature dependent measurements were performed (Fig. 17c). The extracted hole mobility (μh) shows a clear temperature dependence: μh decreases with reducing the temperature down to 100 K and saturates at lower temperature (Fig. 17d), consistent with charge impurities dominated scattering.12,146 Meanwhile, Hall mobility clearly increases with charge carrier concentration at low temperature (squares in Fig. 17d), which can be attributed to the enhanced screening effect with larger carrier concentration. Therefore, the mobility in this range is dominated by charge impurity scattering, resulting from the residual ions in the underlying SiO2 dielectric. Hall mobility at temperatures >100 K fits well with μ ∼ T−0.5, consistent with the electron–phonon dominated scattering process.146

Owing to the strong in-plane anisotropy as discussed previously, exploring the electrical performance with different in-plane orientations is of wide interest. As shown in Fig. 17e, Liu et al. fabricated a device with multiple Cr/Au contacts with 45° included angle in the adjacent contacts.14 The drain current at Vg = 30 V, Vd = 0.5 V in the perpendicular direction is ∼85 mA mm−1 (at 45° and 225°) and ∼137 mA mm−1 (at 135° and 315°), respectively, demonstrating the strong anisotropy of ∼50% between the orthogonal directions. Utilizing such a strong anisotropy of BP, BP based orientation-induced diodes can be fabricated, showing a remarkable rectification ratio as high as 115 and an ‘ideal’ diode property with an ideality factor (n) of ∼1.05.147 Another possible application of the in-plane anisotropy property is in thermoelectric devices, which rely on the Seebeck effect to convert heat into electricity. In particular, the conversion efficiency is proportional to the ratio between electrical conductance and thermal conductance. To achieve a high Seebeck coefficient, it is desirable to obtain a high electrical conductance and a low thermal conductance simultaneously. In anisotropic BP, electrical and thermal conductance peak along the armchair and zigzag directions, respectively. Consequently, the conversion efficiency shows a ZT value (thermoelectric figure of merit) of ∼2.5 along the armchair direction, which is comparable to commercial materials.148

In 2D material electronic devices, semiconductor/metal contact plays a major role in determining the transistor polarity and contact resistance. The formation of ohmic contact is crucial to achieve devices with high performance. Therefore, a systematic investigation of the BP device performance with different metal contacts is highly desirable. The contact between ML BP and a variety of metals is theoretically investigated and plotted in Fig. 18a.149 ML BP forms chemical bonds with deposited metals; therefore it undergoes metallization. Two different methods, band calculation and transport simulation, were used to predict the contact performance. In consideration of the interaction between metal electrodes and semiconductor channels, the latter approach shows much higher accuracy and is more consistent with experimental results.149 It was found that Schottky barriers are formed at almost all ML BP/metal interfaces. The electron Schottky barrier heights (SBHs) between ML BP and Au, Cu, Cr, Al and Ag are around 0.30, 0.34, 0.37, 0.51, and 0.52 eV, respectively; meanwhile the hole SBHs between Ti, Ni, and Pd and ML BP are 0.30, 0.26, and 0.16 eV, respectively.149

BP FETs with different metal contacts have been experimentally investigated by several reports.41,150–152 The contact resistance can be extracted with the transfer length method (TLM), as Rtotal = 2Rcontact + Rchannel and Rchannel strongly depends on the channel length. Based on this method, Rcontact between Ni/Pd and few-layer BP is extracted to be 1.75 ± 0.06 and 3.15 ± 0.15 Ω mm at the electrostatic doping limit, respectively.41 Notably, the contact resistance for both holes and electrons is much smaller in the short-channel configuration. As shown in the schematic of Fig. 18c and d, the effective SB height only depends on gate voltage in the long-channel configuration.41 If the channel length is reduced, not only gate voltage but also source drain bias would modulate the SB height, i.e. a large source drain bias would turn the thermionic emission into a tunnelling process at the metal/semiconductor barrier.

Apart from basic electronic devices like FETs, functional devices like diode and logic inverters have been demonstrated in many studies.103,106,153–155 Replacing traditional SiO2 with a better dielectric, e.g. h-BN, quantum oscillations,156 quantum Hall effects95 and spin valves157 have been achieved at ultra-low temperature. Using a ferroelectric material dielectric, poly(vinylidenefluoride-trifluoroethylene) (P(VDF-TrFE)), a non-volatile memory device has been demonstrated by Lee et al.158

6.2 Optoelectronic devices

An important application of semiconductors is photodetectors. Owing to the tunable direct bandgap from 0.3 (bulk) to 2 eV (ML) as well as the high charge carrier mobility, BP is a promising candidate for photodetection over a wide range, from the visible to infrared regime. A schematic plot of the early studied BP photodetector is shown in Fig. 19a.159 For a photodetector, the figure of merit photoresponsivity (R) is defined as photocurrent triggered by a unit power of incident light on the effective area of the photodetector: R = ΔI/PAs, where ΔI is the excited photocurrent (ΔI = Ilaser,ON − Ilaser,OFF), P is the intensity of the light, and As is the effective area under light illumination. Upon illumination, BP FET shows a fast photoresponse speed of ∼1 ms, a large R of ∼4.8 mA W−1 in the visible light region and expanding to the near-infrared (NIR) region at 940 nm (Fig. 19b).159 The relationship between R and wavelength is plotted in Fig. 19c, which decreases linearly when the excitation wavelength is increased. A BP photodetector shows a wider operation range compared to a TMDC photodetector,162–166 with an estimated cutoff wavelength (λcutoff) of ∼997 nm (1.24 eV), owing to the much smaller bandgap of BP (Fig. 19c).159 Interestingly, there is an abnormal significant enhancement of R in the near ultraviolet (UV) range, reaching a record high value of ∼9 × 104 A W−1 at Vsd = 3 V (Fig. 19d).160 Different from the fast response (ms) in the visible to infrared range, the photoresponse at 330 nm is as slow as ∼100 s (Fig. 19e). Such a colossal but slow UV photoresponse is ascribed to the resonant-interband transition between a pair of specially nested valence and conduction bands (Fig. 19f), which is known as the band-nesting effect.167 Density of states (DOS) on the nested bands are extremely high, leading to this highly efficient absorption in the UV light region.160

The mid-infrared (MIR) photodetector performance has been systemically investigated by Guo et al.161 To enhance the photoresponse, multiple interdigitated electrodes were used to optimize the photocarrier collection efficiency (Fig. 19g).168 This device demonstrates an ultrawide operation range up to the mid-infrared region of ∼3.39 μm, as plotted in Fig. 19h. When the incident power increases, R of the device drops simultaneously, revealing a photogating effect dominated photoresponse. In particular, if photo-generated electrons (holes) are trapped by defects,169 impurities170 or hybrid structures like quantum dots,171 the lifetimes of the photo-generated holes (electrons) are generally prolonged. The trapped carriers can act as a local gate to effectively modulate the concentration of photo-generated free carriers. As a result, the photogain (G) of the device is enhanced according to G = τ/τT, where τ is the excess minority carrier lifetime and τT is the carrier transit time.161 Here, some intrinsic defects like vacancies, dislocations and grain boundaries act as the trap state, thus leading to the high G = 104 and R ∼ 82 A W−1 at 500 mV bias and 1.6 nW incident power (Fig. 19h).161 The frequency dependent photocurrent is shown in Fig. 19i. The 3 dB cutoff frequency (the point where the photocurrent becomes half of the one under continuous illumination) ranges from 2.2 to 1.1 kHz for incident power from 5.6 μW to 1.6 nW, demonstrating a fast-response and low-power-sensitive mid-infrared photodetection properties. The high frequency (>10 kHz) cutoff point is ∼60 mA W−1 for all the powers, originating from the photovoltaic effect.172

7. Summary and outlook

Recent progress in the preparation, characterization, and application in (opto)electronics of BP has been reviewed. Bulk BP features a small bandgap ∼0.3 eV and can be gradually increased to ∼2 eV by simply scaling down the thickness. Unlike TMDCs, which involve a direct-to-indirect bandgap crossover in the bilayer or thicker case, the bandgap of BP is always direct, thus holding promise in high-efficiency light emission optoelectronics, especially in the infrared regime. However, achievements in BP based light emission devices are surprisingly rare, possibly resulting from the poor air stability induced challenging device fabrication process. Furthermore, the theoretically predicted hole mobility can be as high as 10000 cm2 V−1 s−1, much superior to TMDCs and can be applied to next-generation electronic devices. Besides these, the unique crystal symmetry makes BP highly anisotropic, a new dimension that can both control the physical properties and design some conceptually new logic devices. In the sample preparation part, we traced back the bulk BP crystal synthesis to the last century. To fabricate few-layer BP, versatile top-down (exfoliation or etching) methods are summarized and compared. Meanwhile, the detailed progress of phosphorene allotrope prepared via MBE is given, with an explicit description of the substrate effect from both theoretical and experimental perspectives. The oxidation mechanism of BP under various environment and several noncovalent/covalent protection methods is summarized. The effect of water, which was usually overlooked, is now identified to ‘catalyze’ the oxidation of BP even in a dark environment. How to effectively prevent water penetration is vital for keeping the BP surface intact. In the device application part, customized engineering methods, including interface, bandgap and intercalation engineering, have been outlined. Particularly, we highlighted recent achievements in characterizing the type and effect of defects in a BP crystal, which is the basis for future defect engineering. Functional electronic and optoelectronic devices based on BP are summarized. In optoelectronics, the relative low infrared light absorption of BP severely limits the device performance. A recent study has found that Ag–P hybridization could induce an abnormal near-infrared absorption, thus improving the near-infrared photoresponsivity.174 To further improve the infrared optoelectronic performance, future studies should be directed to integrating surface plasmonic structures and micro-cavities or precisely controlling the defect states,175 thus achieving optoelectronic devices with both high and fast responsivity.
