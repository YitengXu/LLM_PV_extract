Abstract

The research and development of advanced energy-storage systems must meet a large number of requirements, including high energy density, natural abundance of the raw material, low cost and environmental friendliness, and particularly reasonable safety. As the demands of high-performance batteries are continuously increasing, with large-scale energy storage systems and electric mobility equipment, lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries have become an attractive candidate for the new generation of high-performance batteries due to their high theoretical capacity (1675 mA h gâˆ’1) and energy density (2600 Wh kgâˆ’1). However, rapid capacity attenuation with poor cycle and rate performances make the batteries far from ideal with respect to real commercial applications. Outstanding breakthroughs and achievements have been made to alleviate these problems in the past ten years. This paper presents an overview of recent advances in lithiumâ€“sulfur battery research. We cover the research and development to date on various components of lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries, including cathodes, binders, separators, electrolytes, anodes, collectors, and some novel cell configurations. The current trends in materials selection for batteries are reviewed and various choices of cathode, binder, electrolyte, separator, anode, and collector materials are discussed. The current challenges associated with the use of batteries and their materials selection are listed and future perspectives for this class of battery are also discussed.

1. Introduction

The increasing use of fossil fuels has inevitably generated environmental pollution issues, and therefore alternative sustainable and clean energy options need to be researched and developed. Recently, much attention has been paid to solar and wind energy; however, these are both intermittent and uncontrollable. In this context, and with the continuous development of science and technology and improvements in people's living standards, higher demands are being made with respect to the performances of batteries. Among various types of batteries, lithiumâ€“sulfur (Liâ€“S) batteries have received particular attention because of their fairly low cost, and high theoretical specific capacity (1675 mA h gâˆ’1) and energy density (2600 Wh kgâˆ’1).1,2

An Liâ€“S battery generally comprises a cathode, binder, separator, electrolyte, anode, and collector. Elemental sulfur is the main cathode material of batteries, and this is closely related to its structure and electrochemical properties. Elemental sulfur has a crown-like structure comprising eight sulfur atoms and possesses outstanding thermodynamic stability.3,4 The high capacity and chargeâ€“discharge performances of elemental sulfur are achieved through Sâ€“S bond electrochemical fracture and linkage in the S8 molecule.5 In the discharge process, every sulfur atom transfers two electrons, which is a greater quantity compared to a metal ion (one or less than one electron per atom). Furthermore, sulfur has many other advantages, such as abundance in nature, non-toxicity, and low cost. Hence, elemental sulfur is considered to be one of the optimal cathode materials for a rechargeable battery.

Lithium is a metal in nature and is considered to be a suitable battery material candidate due to its low standard potential (âˆ’3.04 V vs. SHE), density (0.53 g cmâˆ’3), and electrochemical equivalent (0.26 g Aâˆ’1 hâˆ’1), and high theoretical specific capacity (3861 mA h gâˆ’1). A conventional Liâ€“S battery is mainly composed of a sulfur composite cathode, a lithium metal anode, an organic liquid electrolyte and a polyolefin separator. Although an Liâ€“S battery has a very high specific capacity and energy density in theory, capacity attenuation is extraordinarily quick during the cycle process. This makes Liâ€“S battery commercialization a real challenge. The issues causing capacity attenuation in Liâ€“S batteries include the low electrical and ionic conductivity of elemental sulfur and the discharge product, Li2S, the â€œshuttle effectâ€ caused by the dissolved polysulfide species (PS), lithium anode deterioration due to surface passivation, an unstable solid-electrolyte interphase, and the huge volume change in the conversion reaction and associated safety concerns. Because of these limitations and challenges, further development is needed to improve the electrochemical performance of Liâ€“S batteries.7â€“9 In recent years, significant progress has been made through the development of strategies for the material selection of the cathode, binder, separator, electrolyte anode and collector, the structural design and modification, and configuration innovation for Liâ€“S batteries. Nanomaterials are considered to be ideal materials in Liâ€“S battery applications due to their unique structural features and high surface area, superior electric and thermal conductivity, excellent chemical stability, and short path lengths for electronic or Li+ transport, possessing especially suitable exposed active sites and fast reaction kinetics, which facilitate electrochemical energy storage, enhance energy and power density, and improve cycle life and rate capability. Nazar's group is best known for their 2009 Nature Materials paper, presenting the feasibility of an Liâ€“S battery using nanomaterials. They applied a nanostructured carbonâ€“sulfur cathode,10 bimodal mesoporous carbon nanofibers,11 magneli-phase nanoparticles (Ti4O7),12 nanosheets of manganese dioxide (MnO2),13 and other nanomaterials for Liâ€“S batteries. These batteries demonstrated an ultra-low capacity decay rate and a stable cycling performance. Especially in addressing the â€œshuttle effectâ€ of PS, nanomaterials could be directly applied in the cathode, binder, and electrolyte, or indirectly used in the separator, anode, and collector by methods such as coating, doping, and magnetron sputtering. And these nanostructures could buffer against volume changes, reduce the strain on the material and store more active substances. Recently, the flexibility of nanomaterials has also attracted widespread attention and was applied in an Liâ€“S battery. For example, when a flexible and robust paper electrode, consisting of carbon nanotubes (CNT) and activated carbon nanofibers (ACNF), loaded with MnO2 nanosheets, served as an efficient sulfur host for an Liâ€“S battery,14 the battery exhibited a high rate capacity of 780 mA h gâˆ’1 at a rate of 2C and a high capacity retention of 64% over 300 cycles. Meanwhile, this will significantly enhance the practical applications of flexible Liâ€“S batteries.

In this review, the principles and characterization of chargeâ€“discharge processes for Liâ€“S batteries are presented and discussed. Particular attention is paid to the selection of cathode materials, as well as the other components, and the structural designs for Liâ€“S batteries. Various strategies for structural design and modification, as well as configurational innovations of Liâ€“S batteries, are presented and introduced. Finally, some future developmental directions in the area of Liâ€“S batteries are discussed. In these discussions and presentations, besides the latest progress in the common contents of Liâ€“S batteries, significant and novel attention is paid to the polysulfide cathode and the structural designs of the cathode, multifunctional and free battery binders, safer electrolytes, pre-lithiated anodes, and other novel anodes and collectors, especially the large number of nanomaterials applied to improve the electrochemical performances of the batteries due to the above-mentioned advantages.

3. Cathode materials of lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries

Sulfur is one of the main cathode materials in Liâ€“S batteries, because it has an excellent theoretical specific capacity (1675 mA h gâˆ’1) and energy density (2600 Wh kgâˆ’1). Although sulfur is a suitable cathode material, it is an insulator of electrons and ions, which makes the cathode conductivity poor. Furthermore, the charge-transfer resistance of sulfur is quite large, and a serious â€œshuttle effectâ€ of PS still exists in the utilization of Liâ€“S batteries. As a result, suitable conductive materials are necessary to enhance the electronic conduction of battery electrodes.23

3.1 Sulfur/carbon composites

Since early research, carbon materials have been regarded as conductive agents. Various types of carbon have received wide attention. To date, the types of carbon in Liâ€“S battery research mainly include conductive carbon black, aerogel carbon, graphene, carbon nanotubes, carbon nanofibers, and hybrid carbon (e.g. graphene-carbon nanotube (CNT) conductive composites,24â€“28 graphene/CNT carbon composites,29â€“32 and combined graphene sheets, as well as micro/mesoporous carbon (SCNMM) composites33). They focused on raw material selection of carbon blacks, including Printex-A, Super C65, Printex-XE2, and Ketjen black EC-600JD and standard (Super C65 and Printex-XE2 in a ratio of 1:1), mixed with sulfur. There is considerable interest in using sulfurâ€“carbon composites. In early 2009, in a study by Nazar et al., a high-performance Liâ€“S battery was demonstrated. Based on the above research, the structural designs of sulfur/carbon composite cathodes have also been a research focus, besides the various carbon materials. Several porous carbon materials with a large surface area and pore volume for PS species accommodation have been the latest hot topic in the preparation of battery cathodes. Wang and his co-workers40 used microporous carbon to encapsulate and confine short-chain sulfur species for an Liâ€“S battery. A new type of thiocarbonate-like SEI could be formed in the C/S cathode, which improved the sequestration of sulfur. The most outstanding electrochemical reversibility could be obtained at almost 100% coulombic efficiency, even over 4020 cycles. Huang and his co-workers41 prepared a sulfur cathode with ordered microporous carbon for an Liâ€“S battery. The microporous carbon, with a small enough pore size, could prevent penetration of the solvent molecules and interaction between PS and carbonates, so the â€œshuttle effectâ€ could be effectively suppressed. Thus, a battery with the cathode developed in this study presented excellent rate capability and cycling stability. Xiong and his co-workers42 prepared a microporousâ€“mesoporous carbonaceous nanotubes/sulfur cathode for an Liâ€“S battery. The hierarchical structure not only promoted electron and ion transfer during the charge/discharge process, but also efficiently suppressed PS dissolution. More importantly, the inner hollow cavity could hold even more sulfur, thus buffering the large volume changes in dischargeâ€“charge processes. Even at a high current density of 1600 mA gâˆ’1, capacity decay was only âˆ¼0.13% per cycle after 150 discharge/charge cycles. In another study, porous nitrogen and phosphorous dual-doped graphene was prepared using a thermal annealing and subsequent hydrothermal reaction route, and the graphene/sulfur composite was used as the cathode.43 The porous and dual-doped (nitrogen and phosphorous) structure could accommodate the volume expansion and suppress the PS â€œshuttling effectâ€ in an Liâ€“S battery. Meanwhile, a higher cathode conductivity compared to single N or P doped was achieved. Zhang and his co-workers44 prepared a dual-spatially-confined reservoir within micro@meso-porous dense graphene for a sulfur cathode. The outer part was a mesoporous structure, which could act as a reservoir to stabilize the over-flowed PS and enhance the Li+ transportation. The microporous inner structure was helpful in enhancing the electrical/ionic conductivity, improving the utilization of sulfur, and suppressing PS dissolution, because of strong interactions with the pore structure.

Recently, Zhang and his co-workers45 prepared hierarchical porous graphene for a sulfur cathode by a CaO-templated CVD growth method. The graphene presented a large number of macro-sized strutted cavities, meso-sized wrinkled pores, and micro-sized in-plane vacancies. This offered several advantages, including short ion-diffusion channels, low interfacial resistance, strong surface entrapping for PS, and a stable structure.

Due to being ultra-lightweight, and having a high specific surface area and a stable and open framework, leading to intimate contact between the sulfur and highly porous base material, porous three-dimensional carbonâ€“sulfur cathodes are the focus of many studies in battery research. Jiang et al.46a synthesized three-dimensional porous graphene aerogels/sulfur nanocrystals. The size of the sulfur crystals in the synthesis process was on the nanoscale, and the framework of the graphene aerogel was stable and open, leading to intimate contact between the sulfur and graphene conductive matrix. Thus the strong adsorption ability of functional groups of graphene was demonstrated. A battery with this cathode presented an excellent rate capability and outstanding cycle performance. Xu et al.46b and Jia et al.46c loaded sulfur into porous three-dimensional (3D) graphene by a metal-ion-assisted hydrothermal method and a drying strategy. The 3D graphene has a highly porous structure, and the pathway of rapid electron and ion transportation was stable and continuous. The highly porous inner space restrained soluble PS and accommodated the volume changes. As a result, a significant improvement in the electrochemical performance was obtained. However, the battery developed by Jia's group had better electrochemical properties than that developed by Xu's group due to controllable characteristics. Recently, porous three-dimensional carbon was applied to encapsulate sulfur, and the wrapped layers were used to encapsulate the electrode.47a Xi et al.47b prepared a hierarchical porous three-dimensional sulphur/few-layer graphene foam cathode for an Liâ€“S battery. The few-layered graphene had an interconnected 3-D network and a suitable hierarchical pore structure, which facilitated rapid electron/ion transportation, and effectively retained the electro-active sulphur. At the same time, the cathode material was free of binder and conductive additives, thus enhanced electrical conductivity could be obtained for the cathode. Even at a high current density of 3200 mA gâˆ’1, the capacity decay was only âˆ¼0.064% per cycle after 400 discharge/charge cycles.

The structure of the cathode allowed a sulfur loading as high as 80.1%. The 3DCG was composed of two-dimensional graphene nanosheets and one-dimensional (1D) highly conductive carbon nanotubes (CNT). The cathode structure, especially the one-dimensional (1D) highly conductive CNTs, not only improved the conductivity, but also appropriately tuned the mesoporous structure, which could achieve bimodal mesopores with sizes of 3.5 and 32.1 nm. Meanwhile, the mesopores could provide a large number of reaction sites, store abundant sulfur and facilitate the migration of electrolyte and charge. Recently, many nanomaterials, such as nanocubes, nanocages, nanosheets, nanoshells, nanospheres, nanotubes, nanofibers, and nanowires, have attracted widespread attention, as illustrated in Fig. 5. This is because the physical and chemical properties of nanomaterials greatly affect the electrochemical performance of the electrode.

Secondly, in porous cathode structures, porous material can provide a high surface area, porosity, and pore volume, thus they can store adequate sulfur, achieve high utilization of the active material, and facilitate the diffusion of lithium ions and electrolyte penetration. And attention also needs to be paid to the design of porous structured materials for cathodes. Thirdly, in some structures of natural biological shapes, because the biological shapes of cathode materials usually have the above features of nanostructured materials or porous materials, these design principles are suitable. Meanwhile, the novel shape designs of cathode materials aim to better suppress the shuttle of PS through the synergistic effect of structures or a convenient modification treatment.

By combining the physical and chemical adsorption of PS in battery cathodes, the doping or special chemical properties (forming chemical bonds between structural materials and PS) of materials, such as doping inorganic nanoparticles and metal salts in battery cathodes, have attracted huge attention by researchers.

4. Binders of lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries

The binder also plays an important role in the electrochemical performances of Liâ€“S batteries. The applied binder must be securely attached to the electrode and must build a network structure between the sulfur and conductive carbon. 6. Separators of lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries

At present, many research reports focus mainly on the cathode and electrolytes. However, the separator is also an important part of batteries, as it affects their electrochemical performance and safety. Separators mainly have two functions: (1) a separator should have a microporous functional polymeric membrane, which plays an important role in avoiding a short circuit between the positive and negative electrodes. (2) Separators must have excellent strength, flexible bending or shrinkage, suitable ionic conductivity, proper porosity, and wettability. As a result, they can inhibit PS migration and protect the electrodes from being destroyed or losing functionality. Traditional separators are mainly polyolefin separators, such as polypropylene microporous membranes, polyethylene microporous membranes, and multi-layer composite membranes. Although there are some advantages, such as low cost, appropriate mechanical strength, good chemical and electrochemical stability, and controllable porosity, some disadvantages are significant: (1) the chargeâ€“discharge performance is poor at high temperatures or high currents, leading to short-circuiting and serious safety hazards; (2) it is easy to restrain intermediate PS diffusion in the batteries. Therefore, developing new separators with higher quality and multifunctionality has become one of the most important directions for improving battery performances.

7.1 The protective layer for anodes

The main issues that need to be overcome to make a battery feasible are the formation of a passivating layer and corrosion associated with dendrite formation, which can damage the structure of the lithium cathode during the chargeâ€“discharge of batteries, or even hamper the safety of rechargeable batteries based on metallic lithium. Zhang et al.125a and Kim et al.125b used amorphous carbon by a magnetron sputtering method, and multilayer graphene by coating on the surface of metallic lithium, respectively. Because the compact carbon film and graphene coating inhibited the growth of dendritic lithium in the chargeâ€“discharge process, the electrochemical performances of batteries with both of these novel coatings were significantly improved. The coulomb efficiency of the former was maintained above 80%, while the coulomb efficiency of the pure Li electrode was reduced to 40% after 50 cycles. The electrochemical performance of the latter was also obviously improved, with 81.0% capacity retention after 200 cycles. But the thick coating led to a larger impedance for Li+ delivery. So, a moderate coating thickness was very important in the sputtering process. Xiong and his coworkers126a,b applied the additives LiNO3 and LiBOB in electrolyte solution to modify the electrode/electrolyte interfaces in lithium batteries. A smooth and compact surface film was formed on lithium metal, which effectively prevented the penetration of solvents and PS into the lithium anode. A fluorinated ether electrolyte with neither cathode confinement nor additives was also beneficial for both the efficient use of electrolytes and the safe operation of the battery. An SEI layer with a hierarchical chemical composition of LiF and sulfate/sulfite/sulfide was identified on lithium anodes, which suppressed parasitic reactions and helped to preserve the anode quality.127

8. Collectors

The collector is also an important part of Liâ€“S batteries. Primarily, it is regarded as the active material carrier of the cathode, and collects electrons, transferring them to the active material through the external circuit. Moreover, the collector can easily contact and react with the electrolyte solution. So, the electrolyte stability and the compatibility of the collectors can also affect the performance of a battery.142a When the battery is overcharged, a variety of complex side effects will occur, such as the decomposition of the electrolyte, structural destruction, transformation of the electrode material, and the electrochemical corrosion of the metal collector. When the collector is corroded or dissolved, the contact between the active substance of the electrode and the collector will be reduced, which can cause the active material to fall off, the voltage of formation to be delayed, and the resistance to increase. Furthermore, the metal ions will pollute the electrolyte solution, and the presence of impurity ions will reduce the reactivity of the ions. All of these factors will cause the performance of the battery to drop sharply. Collectors are mainly composed of aluminum foil, stainless steel mesh, foam nickel, or copper foil. But the performances of the collectors can be improved to some extent to adapt them to high-performance Liâ€“S batteries. Previously, some researchers only paid attention to the micro-network substrate and microporous current collector for various rechargeable battery systems.142b,c

In terms of Liâ€“S batteries, the collector not only needs to have excellent conductivity, but also needs a large space to store the active material mixtures. The design of the collector is vital for improving the electrochemical performance and the stability of the cathode. The novel structure of collectors has also attracted huge attention. 9. Battery structure design and modification of lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries

The overall performances of Liâ€“S batteries fundamentally depend on the features of the cathode, binder, separator, electrolyte, anode, and collector. Meanwhile, the structure of the electrodes also has a significant influence on the electrochemical properties of the battery. Because the reaction system of the battery is different from that of traditional secondary lithium batteries, the common structure and assembly process of traditional secondary lithium batteries do not adapt to the new battery systems. In addition to structural design and modification by various chemical or physical methods, building a new battery structure is also attractive for research and exploration.

10. Summary and future directions

A comprehensive review of the current state of research and development in Liâ€“S batteries is presented and discussed. The Liâ€“S batteries are believed to be the most promising candidates for high-performance energy storage materials. Ideally, if a complete reaction between sulfur and lithium occurs, the theoretical specific capacity of an Liâ€“S battery could be as high as 1675 mA h gâˆ’1 and the energy density could reach 2600 Wh kgâˆ’1. There are several challenges and limitations for Liâ€“S technology with respect to cell materials and configuration. Firstly, the resistance of sulfur is as high as 5 Ã— 10âˆ’30 S cmâˆ’1, and the structure and morphology changes of the intermediate products (lithium polysulfide Li2Sx) during cycling lead to unstable electrochemical contact within the electrode. Rapid fading of the capacity is another challenge that occurs as a result of the â€œshuttle effectâ€, where dissolved polysulfide causes loss of active materials. The volume expansion occurring in the cathode can reach about 80%, leading to pulverization of the cathode with severe reduction in capacity and contact between collectors and electrodes. Also, dendrite formation in the lithium metal anode leads to short circuits or fire, which causes safety concerns. Last but not least, the insulated Li2S forming on the surface of the cathode increases the impedance of the electrode, which leads to the loss of capacity and poor cyclability.

The material choice of the cathode plays an important role in the electrochemical performances of Liâ€“S batteries. To date, sulfur/carbon composites, sulfur/conductive polymer composites, sulfur/metal oxide composites, sulfur/multiple sulfur-based compound materials, and even various non-conductive metalâ€“oxide nanoparticle-decorated carbon flakes, have been trialled to replace conventional pure sulfur. The main forms of carbon in the cathodes of batteries include conductive carbon black, aerogel carbon, carbon nanotubes, carbon nanofibers, and hybrid carbon, especially graphite-based materials (e.g. graphene-CNT conductive composites, graphene/CNT-porous carbon composites or combined graphene sheets and micro/mesoporous carbon (SCNMM) composites). The combinations of these materials have great potential to significantly improve the performance of batteries. There are a large number of theoretical calculations and related experiments, showing that many functional groups (e.g. O-, B-, N-, S-, Se-, Co-, Si-, I- and C3N4-) can significantly enhance their electronic conductivity and effectively trap polysulfide by chemical interactions between doped or co-doped atoms and sulfur/PS. The novel structures simultaneously contribute to greatly immobilizing migrating PS. Some other conductive polymers can also be considered in the cathode research of Liâ€“S batteries. Moreover, polysulfide cathode materials, including Li2S, FeS2, and Cu3BiS3, could potentially attract wide concern with respect to lithium dendrites.

At the same time, the design of cathode structures is important in maximizing the sulfur loading, by increasing the surface area to hold more PS, providing convenient channels for electron transport, reducing the irreversible loss of active material, and improving the capacity retention of Liâ€“S batteries. Generally, cathode designs can be divided into two types, given the material composition: carbonaceous materials and non-carbonaceous materials; they can also be divided into three forms, including nanostructures (nanocubes, nanocages, nanosheets, nanoshells, nanospheres, nanotubes, nanofibers, and nanowires); porous structures (microporous, micro/mesoporous, disordered mesoporous, activated bamboo-derived porous, hierarchical porous, tunable porous, coreâ€“shell tubular porous, and cylindrical and gyroidal mesoporous) and structures of natural biological shapes (e.g. coreâ€“shell, yolkâ€“shell, shrimp shell, coralloid shape, cauliflower, honeycomb, coaxial-cable structure, sponge, Aspergillus flavus, split-half-tubular, layered structure, paper, foam, pie, and nest-like structures) for the improvement of sulfur cathodes. In the future, more composite structures should be explored for cathode designs to realize multi-functionality. In practical Liâ€“S cells, the use of cathodes with high areal sulfur loading is of high importance, and is the ultimate goal of cathode design. Meanwhile, the percent of the cathode, especially sulfur, in the whole battery, such as the optimized electrolyte/sulfur ratio, should also be considered.

As to the reaction, binders can become inactive sites, leading to the deterioration of cell performances. Therefore, the development of novel and appropriate binders is extremely important in Liâ€“S batteries. Multi-functional and free battery binders should also be considered. But the binder-free cathode still has some drawbacks, including difficult synthetic steps, and low connectivity between the conductive agent and sulfur, except for certain designs. The binder percentage still needs to be further researched for the development of high-performance Liâ€“S batteries.

Making the optimal choice of electrolyte in Liâ€“S batteries is not easy. Liquid electrolytes are widely used in Liâ€“S batteries because they have high ionic conductivity. But liquid-state electrolytes still have some drawbacks, such as lithium dendrite formation, PS dissolution and shuttle effects. In order to solve these drawbacks, solid-state electrolytes can be good candidate materials. However, these also have disadvantages, such as low ionic conductivity and an unstable interface, which can be damaged by some polysulfide ions. Complex hydride-type or conducting polymer-type solid electrolytes will provide some guidance due to their improved conductivity and comparatively stable interface. Gel polymer electrolytes can integrate the advantages of the above electrolytes, but the electrolytes still have a negative impact on battery capacity and self-discharge, due to their high viscosity. In addition to the above three types of electrolytes, ionic liquid electrolytes are promising solvents for Liâ€“S batteries. Although ionic liquid electrolytes have many merits, such as non-flammability, high ionic conductivity, a wide electrochemical window, and the ability to protect lithium metal anodes, some disadvantages still exist, such as irreversible reducibility in anodes and unexpected byproducts with polysulfide anions. Therefore, the choice of functional electrolyte is also vital in conjunction with the choice of cathode materials. Meanwhile, safe composite polymer electrolytes must be realized so that Liâ€“S batteries can be commercialized.

Separators are as vital as the cathodes and electrolytes. Their main functions are to separate the anode and cathode and to permit the free flow of lithium ions. Furthermore, a large amount of research shows that the microporous structure and thermal dimensional stability of separators considerably affect the battery performances. To date, the separators include modified polyolefins, PEO matrix, PVDF matrix, lithiated Nafion ionomers, and some blend polymer membranes. There are some drawbacks associated with these separators. The former three kinds of separators cannot effectively restrain PS ions passing through them, which leads to a significant reduction in the content of active substances. As a result, a sharp drop in the capacitance and cycle performance can take place. Although lithiated Nafion ionomer separators can mitigate the above disadvantages, their low strength cannot resist the formation of dendrite on the lithium electrode. At the same time, their electrolyte absorption is considerably low, and they embody high internal resistance. Using blended polymer membranes can address these problems to some extent. But the present research lacks systematic investigation. In developing separators for batteries, some nanofiber membranes made by the electrospinning method, the solution injection method, and electro-blown spinning have been used by our groups. The design of the cathode and separator structure is as follows: in the co-doped hierarchical porous carbon nanofiber-sulfur cathode, PVA was used as the carbon precursor, PTFE was used as the surficial polymer, and some tree-like polymer separators were used in the Liâ€“S battery. The co-doped hierarchical porous carbon nanofiber-sulfur cathode can achieve an increased sulfur loading and effectively suppress the â€œshuttle effectâ€ with the synergistic role of chemical interactions between doped or co-doped atoms and sulfur/polysulfides. Meanwhile, the tree-like polymer separators can provide a large number of sites to trap the PS. Furthermore, the thickness of the separator should be reduced due to the requirement for denser cathodes. In addition, the anode materials cannot be ignored. The development of a stabilized lithium metal anode that is compatible with the cathode needs to be further investigated. To date, some novel cell configurations have been developed through the application of interlayers, collectors and even new Liâ€“S battery structures.

Nanomaterials are an exceedingly important constituent of Liâ€“S batteries due to their unique structural features and performances. Advances in nanoscience and nanotechnology have provided great opportunities to design and fabricate new energy storage materials for the development of high-performance Liâ€“S batteries. For cathodes, one-dimensional nanorods, nanobelts, nanowires and nano-dots; two dimensional nanosheets, nanoplates, nanoplates, nanowebs, nanodiscs, and nanoflakes; and three dimensional hollow spheres, nanotubes, nanocages, nanoparticles, nano-sized grains, nanorods, nanofibers and other hierarchical nanostructured composites have been successfully applied in the fabrication of batteries. Meanwhile the popularity of nanoscaled sulfur as a cathode material is rising among researchers, and nanosized binders, such as helical dendrimers, have been used. PEO-based crystalline nanocomposite polymer electrolytes have been prepared and applied in batteries. The major feature of nanostructured electrolytes is high stability towards the lithium metal electrode. Meanwhile, this unique property makes the electrolyte quite promising for the development of rechargeable Liâ€“S batteries. With regard to separators, nanofiber membranes produced by the electrospinning method, the solution injection method, and electro-blown spinning have played an important role in improving the electrochemical performance of batteries. Nano-scale coatings of polymer nanocomposite, oxide, and inorganic nanoparticles are also successfully used in battery separators. For the anode, a nanostructured aluminum alloy is a good choice. Otherwise, the nano-sized metallic clusters as lithium hosts permit and enhance Li+ reversibility, which can compensate for the limitations inherent within one material alone. At the same time, various electrodeposited nanostructures as current collectors (e.g.: copper, nickel) have also been used in Liâ€“S batteries.

Further research into nanodimensional materials is still needed for further improvement in the electrochemical performance of batteries. There are some challenges in this area that need to be addressed: firstly, compared with bulk materials, the synthesis or preparation processes focusing on nanoscale storage materials are complex. Therefore, the development of low cost, large-scale, and environmentally benign synthesis techniques for nanocomposites are of significant concern and need to be developed, and it is imperative to spread awareness in society in order to boost the commercial application of batteries. Secondly, work needs to be done in refining some of the processing techniques used, such as high-energy ball milling, solid-phase synthesis, and other imprecise machining methods. The control of morphology, particle size and dimensional distribution by these methods is not easy. Meanwhile, the structures and morphology of nanomaterials and their effect on the lithium storage performance need to be further discussed. Finally, extremely scarce theoretical calculations associated with nanomaterials, which affect the capacity retention, stability, and cycling performance of the batteries, still remain an important challenge. And the lithium insertion mechanism into these nanomaterials also needs to be investigated.

Although the significant development of Liâ€“S batteries has given some hope for advanced rechargeable batteries with high energy storage, most research is focused on experimental efforts, while a small amount of theoretical research is considered.
