Defects in metal triiodide perovskite materials towards high-performance solar cells: origin, impact, characterization, and engineering

Abstract

The rapid development of solar cells (SCs) based on organic–inorganic hybrid metal triiodide perovskite (MTP) materials holds great promise for next-generation photovoltaic devices. The demonstrated power conversion efficiency of the SCs based on MTP (PSCs for short) has reached over 20%. An MTP material is a kind of soft ionic solid semiconductor. The intrinsic optoelectronic properties of MTP are greatly determined by several factors, such as the crystalline phase, doping type, impurities, elemental composition, and defects in its crystal structure. In the development of PSCs, a good understanding and smart engineering of the defects in MTP have been demonstrated to be a key factor for the fabrication of high-efficiency PSCs. In this review, we start with a brief introduction to the types of defects and the mechanisms for their formation in MTP. Then, the positive and negative impacts of defects on the important optoelectronic features of MTP are presented. The optoelectronic properties mainly include charge recombination, charge transport, ion migration, and structural stability. Moreover, commonly used techniques for the characterization of the defects in MTP are systematically summarized. Recent progress on the state-of-the-art defect engineering approaches for the optimization of PSC devices is also summarized, and we also provide some perspectives on the development of high-efficiency PSCs with long-term stability through the optimization of the defects in MTP.

1. Introduction

In the past few years, perovskite solar cells (PSCs) based on organic–inorganic hybrid metal triiodide perovskite (MTP) materials (e.g., methylammonia lead iodide (MAPbI3)) have been considered to be one of the most promising types of third-generation solar cells (SCs). This is mainly because their power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) have reached over 20%, starting from 3.8% in 2009.1–6 Compared to the traditional commercialized SCs, it is very impressive to obtain such a high PCE in such a short period. The superior optoelectronic performance of PSCs is mainly attributed to the high absorption coefficient, balanced ambipolar carrier transport, long charge diffusion length, high charge mobility, low exciton binding energy, and tunable direct band gap of MTP.7–9 Moreover, the simple and cost-effective film fabrication process and the flexibility of MTP films also make MTP very promising for the large-scale commercialization of PSCs and their application in integrated devices in the near future.10

Despite the overwhelming advantages in the fabrication of MTP film for PSCs (e.g., solution spin-coating and vacuum thermal evaporation technologies), the inherently “soft” structure of MTP, with a large and significant level of unintentional defects, still significantly affects the optoelectronic performance of PSCs in terms of the PCE and long-term stability.11 For example, a well-defined single crystal of MAPbI3 shows a much lower defect density (1010–1011 cm−3) than a solution deposited MAPbI3 film (1016–1017 cm−3).12 As expected, MTP single crystals with low defect density have displayed higher charge mobility, longer charge diffusion length, and higher structural stability. Moreover, efficient control and engineering of defects distributed in well-established inorganic light-absorbing semiconductor materials (i.e., crystalline silicon,13 GaAs,14 CdTe,15 chalcopyrite Cu(In,Ga)Se2,16 and kesterite Cu2ZnSnS417) have also been proven to be an effective way to accelerate the development and commercialization of high-performance SCs.18 Although the reported PCE of PSCs was over 20%, it is still far below the thermodynamic PCE limit of ∼31% for MAPbI3.19 This is most likely due to the unavoidable shallow- or deep-level defects formed in MTP film during crystal formation. Such defects are considered to be responsible for the hysteresis,20 non-radiative recombination,21 charge trapping,22 charge scattering,23 and ion-migration24 effects in MTP film. As a result, these factors inevitably lead to inferior performance and long-term instability issues in the resultant PSC devices. Therefore, it is highly desirable to develop high-performance PSCs with efficient control of the defects in MTP.

In this review, we pay close attention to recent progress on the topic of defect science and engineering in MTP for PSC applications. We begin with an overview of the established theoretical studies on the origins of the defects in MTP. Then, we move forward to a discussion on the negative and positive impacts of defects on the optoelectronic properties of MTP. Thereafter, the typical characterization methods for the defects in MTP are systematically summarized and critically discussed. To improve the performance of PSCs, the state-of-the-art experimental efforts on the control and engineering of defects are also presented in this review. Finally, we also provide some perspectives on the development of high efficiency PSCs with long-term stability through optimization of the defects in MTP.

2. Defects in MTP

2.1 Origin of the defects in semiconductors

In the ideal crystal structure of a semiconductor, every atom is located on its prescribed site without any defects or impurities (Fig. 1a). In a real semiconductor, however, there is often a large amount of unavoidable defects generated from the crystal growth or/and post-treatment process, and thus present in the crystal structure (Fig. 1b). This is due to imperfect lattice alignment over long distances or to trace foreign atoms. The presence of such defects in semiconductors (especially, traditional inorganic compounds) has commonly been recognized as fatal, such that the semiconductor properties (e.g., conductivity, free charge mobility, and charge lifetime) are significantly decreased.18 Nevertheless, these property variations might be beneficial in some cases, when they are used intentionally for their “unique” properties required in certain applications.25–27 Some examples include alkali-metal atom doping of C60 (i.e., Cs3C60) as a superconducting material,28 N doping of carbon to make efficient catalysts,29–31 metal doping of layered transition metal dichalcogenides for energy applications,32 and the use of alkali-metal atom doping for the fabrication of high performance PSCs.33 To expand the field of application of the semiconductor, a good understanding of the science of defects should take priority before their control and engineering.34 Considering single defects, as shown in Fig. 1c, there are three common kinds of intrinsic point defect that are formed in the crystalline structure of a semiconductor (i.e., vacancies, interstitials and antisites). These point defects can be formed intrinsically or by introducing foreign atoms (impurities or dopants) intentionally, and the possibility of the presence of a defect is determined by its formation energy. The presence of a point defect could create a charge recombination centre within the band gap of the semiconductor to attract and trap free charges. At the same time, these defects will form shallow or deep electron transition levels in the forbidden region, namely, defect trap states. These states are largely determined by the location of their energy levels relative to the conduction band minimum (CBM) or valence band maximum (VBM) edge.18
Fig. 1 Ideal structure (a) without defects and (b) after introducing defects. Possible defects existing in the crystal structure of a semiconductor: (c) point defects, (d) defect pairs, and (e) higher dimensional defects.

Single point defects are basic units in theoretical study, and they are closely related to the natural properties of the crystal structure of the semiconductor. Apart from point defects, the defects in most semiconductors often exist in array configurations. Fig. 1d shows two types of neutral defect pairs, commonly recognized as Frenkel defects and Schottky defects. The former is an ion vacancy that involves the same ion as an interstitial while the latter involves anion and cation vacancies occurring together.36 Moreover, a series of dimensional defects is formed by the combination of point defects and defect arrays. As shown in Fig. 1e, the 1-dimensional (1D) defects, namely, dislocations, takes the form of lines along edges in which the crystal pattern is broken. The 2-dimensional (2D) defects are found on the crystal surface (i.e., the external surface and the grain boundaries (GBs)), in which differently oriented crystallites are joined together. The 3-dimensional (3D) defects induce the volume changes in the crystal pattern. 3D defects include precipitates (small volumes with a different crystal structure), large voids, and second-phase domains. Whatever types of defect exist in a semiconductor (point, array, configuration, and combinations thereof), they lead to considerable localized structural variation and thus significantly affect the photovoltaic properties of the semiconductor, as well as the performance of SCs. Therefore, better understanding and identification of the defects existing in MTP is an important step towards the fabrication of high-performance PSCs. It should be noted that the formation of intrinsic defects in MTP follows the same patterns as in a traditional semiconductor, as is discussed above. In the following sections, we will use the classical and most-studied organic–inorganic hybrid lead-based MTP (i.e., MAPbI3), as an example to demonstrate research progress on defect science and engineering in MTPs.

2.2 Defects in MTP

It is well-known that the formation of a substance is thermodynamically governed by its formation energy, so that the substance with the lowest formation energy exists in its most stable state. Because crystal growth of MTP is always fast and requires high temperature, the wide existence of defects in MTP is inevitable. Theoretical calculations are a powerful tool to investigate the photovoltaic properties of MTP as influenced by defects, as well as providing valid guidance to defect engineering in material design and the corresponding PSC fabrication. With the help of theoretical calculations, the formation and function of simple point defects in MTP can be deeply investigated. A classical method to study the origin of a defect is to calculate its theoretical formation energy by first principle calculations. This method has been widely used to investigate the characteristics of point defects in semiconductors according to their inherent properties on the atomic scale.36–39 As an example, the ideal MAPbI3 crystal structure (Fig. 2a) has twelve possible intrinsic point defects: three vacancies (VMA, VPb, VI), three interstitials (MAi, Pbi, Ii), and six substitutions (MAPb, PbMA, MAI, PbI, IMA, IPb).35 All these point defects create a transition level, which is a Fermi level position located near the CBM (VBM) or within the forbidden band of MAPbI3. The transition level introduces charge recombination centres (traps) to trap free electrons or holes. A defect-induced transition level near the CBM (VBM) is called a shallow-level defect trap. In this defect trap, the trapped charges have a high probability of detrapping into the CBM or VBM, indicating that a shallow-level defect trap with a low charge recombination rate could influence the charge mobility to some extent.40 A transition level located within the middle third of the forbidden band is called a deep-level defect trap, where the trapped charges are doomed to be recombined via a non-radiative recombination process. A defect trap in the deep-level state is also known as a Shockley–Read–Hall (SRH) recombination centre, which is suggested to be detrimental for efficient charge extraction in PSCs.41 According to the energy level location of these point defects in the band gap of MAPbI3, all the point defects could be further divided into electron acceptors (Ii, MAPb, VMA, VPb, IMA, and IPb) and electron donors (MAi, PbMA, VI, Pbi, MAI, and PbI), as shown in Fig. 2b and c, respectively.

The formation of the point defects is largely determined by the environment. Under different film fabrication conditions (e.g., precursor concentrations, precursor ratio, solvents, temperature, and doping), all the point defects have different formation energies. Here, the chemical potential of each constituent in MAPbI3 under various conditions plays a decisive role in the formation of the MAPbI3 structure. Under thermodynamic equilibrium growth conditions, the chemical potential for the formation of a stoichiometric MAPbI3 phase should follow this equation:42

 
μMA + μPb + 3μI = ΔH(MAPbI3) = −5.26 eV(1)
where μ stands for the chemical potential of each constituent in its most stable phase and ΔH(MAPbI3) is the formation enthalpy of MAPbI3. To obtain a pure MAPbI3 phase, the formation of secondary phases (MAI and PbI2) must be restrained, and the chemical potentials of MAI and PbI2 should be satisfied as follows:35
 
μMA + μI < ΔH(MAI) = −2.87 eV(2)
 
μPb + 2μI < ΔH(PbI2) = −2.11 eV(3)

On satisfying eqn (1)–(3) simultaneously, the formation of MAPbI3 can be realized in the red region, as shown in the chemical potential chart of Pb vs. I (Fig. 2d). In other words, MAPbI3 will decompose into PbI2 and MAI when the formation energy is outside of this red region. This narrow formation window indicates relatively strict equilibrium conditions for the formation of MAPbI3. The formation energy difference between MAPbI3 and its precursors (i.e., PbI2 and MAI) is only ∼0.1 eV, further suggesting the possible presence of MAI and PbI2 in MAPbI3.43 On the other hand, the long shape of the red region indicates that it is possible to tune MAPbI3 from n-type to p-type. This is in agreement with the reported result that neutral defects in MAPbI3 did not form a deep-level state, so that the charge diffusion length and lifetime of MAPbI3 are seldom affected by neutral defects.36

Despite the progress achieved, there are still some controversial results in which defects did introduce deep-level trap states in MAPbI3.12,36,45–47 It is commonly suggested that VI is a shallow donor point defect.35,36,45,47 Agiorgousis et al.46 reported, however, that VI is an amphoteric point defect with a deep transition energy level in the band gap. Moreover, Du et al.45 reported that Ii is a low energy deep-level trap and non-radiative recombination centre. This is in contrast to other results.35,46 The discrepancies in these studies resulted partly from the different phases of MAPbI3 used in their calculations (i.e., α-MAPbI3,35 β-MAPbI3,45–47 and γ-MAPbI336), as well as partly from the inconsistent calculation methods. Du et al.39 used a hybrid DFT method involving spin–orbit coupling (SOC) to calculate the point defects in a β-MAPbI3 phase (stable at room temperature). They found that only VI and its complexes create deep-level electron/hole trap states within the band gap of MAPbI3.39 Despite the discrepancy in the results of these theoretical studies, there is consensus that the deep-level defect traps present in MTP are undisputedly fatal to the photovoltaic performance of the material. Generally, for obtaining high-performance PSCs, the formation of deep-level defects should be effectively avoided. Therefore, the prediction and identification of the possible deep-level defects in MTP is fundamentally crucial.

In addition to MA-based MTP, a formamidinium (FA) based MTP material in PSCs has recently achieved a high PCE of 22.1%.48 So far, there have been a few theoretical studies on the properties of intrinsic defects in FAPbI3. Very recently, Liu et al.49 performed DFT calculations to study the intrinsic point defects in FAPbI3. The energy levels of the defects in FAPbI3 are similar to those in MAPbI3. FA-related defects (e.g., VFA, FAI, and IFA), have the lowest formation energies, however, and antisites (e.g., FAI and IFA) create deep levels in the band gap. This is different from the MA system and is most likely attributable to the weak van der Waals interactions between FA cations and PbI6 octahedra.49 To avoid the formation of deep-level FAI defects, it is suggested that the FAPbI3 material should be grown in an I-rich environment and that the electrochemical potential should be controlled in the n-doped region. This result is consistent with the experimental report using excess iodide ions (I3−) as an additive in an FA-based system.48

3. The impacts of defects on the properties of MTP

As discussed above, the presence of point defects in MTP can create higher dimensional defects (e.g., dislocations, GBs, and precipitates) in films (Fig. 1b).11 Although intentional introduction of defects could sometimes be advantageous for a semiconductor material, the higher dimensional defects are considered to lead to serious damage to the optoelectronic properties of MTP and subsequent performance failure in PSCs. In order to effectively fabricate high-performance MTP based PSCs, further understanding and identification of the negative and positive impacts of the defects in MTP are critically urgent. We now briefly overview current research progress on understanding the negative impacts of defects on the optoelectronic properties of MTP, such as ion migration and structural instability. On the other hand, we also summarize some recent emerging studies related to the positive impacts of defects in MTP for PSCs and other applications.

3.1 Role of defects in the optoelectronic properties of MTP

3.1.1 Charge recombination. Fig. 3 shows the recombination pathways of charges (electrons and holes) in a semiconductor. As can be seen, in an ideal structure after photo-excitation, the electrons from the ground state at the VBM will be activated into excited states at the CBM and simultaneously form free charges. The free charges are transported within the semiconductor, transfer out through the interface, or recombine via band-to-band recombination. The “band-to-band” radiative recombination process involves the transition of excited electrons at the CBM directly back to the ground state, where they are recombined with holes at the VBM through relaxation in the MTP (Fig. 3a). This process is intrinsic, often occurs at a location where no defects are present, and results in the photoluminescence (PL) phenomenon (Fig. 3d). Since it directly relates to the reverse process of light absorption, the “band-to-band” radiative recombination process doesn’t significantly depend on the defect levels of the MTP.50 Actually, the energy difference between the photo-excited free electrons and holes is the origin of the open-circuit voltage (VOC) of the PSCs. Therefore, the “band-to-band” radiatively recombined charges should be significantly preserved in MTP.
Fig. 3 Charge recombination pathways in a semiconductor. (a) “Band-to-band” radiative recombination, (b) shallow-level defect assisted radiative recombination, and (c) deep-level defect assisted non-radiative recombination. (d) “Band-to-band” radiative recombination occurs at a location where no defects are present, (e) shallow-level defect assisted radiative recombination occurs at a location where shallow defects are present, and (f) deep-level defect assisted radiative recombination occurs at a location where deep defects are present.

Because defects will insert an energy transition level within the band gap of the semiconductor, however, the presence of a defect could trap the free charges and greatly change the charge recombination pathways and influence the optoelectronic process. During the transportation of the free charges, a part of the free charges is likely to be captured or trapped by the defects. Such a process breaks the ideal photophysical properties of the MTP, thereby reducing the optoelectronic properties of the PSCs containing it. The charges trapped by the defects with different energy levels have different types of behaviour. It is well-known that the shallow-level defects can create transition energy levels in the band gap close to the CBM or VBM of an MTP. In this regard, the trapped charges could radiatively recombine or detrap back to the CBM (or VBM) band via phonon absorption.40 The former could lead to a substantial enhancement of the PL quantum yield but in the form of PL emission, while the latter could enhance the optoelectronic properties of the MTP.51 Moreover, the charges trapped by the shallow-level defects could result in a small energy loss, which leads to radiative recombination with red-shifted emission (Fig. 3b and e).52,53 In the case of the deep-level defects, the trapped charge would need to annihilate with an opposite charge before it could be emitted, as shown in Fig. 3c. This process is the so-called non-radiative recombination, which releases a part or all of its electronic energy as in the form of phonons to the adjacent lattice (Fig. 3f), and this process dramatically decreases the valuable optoelectronic properties of the MTP.54

Shallow-level defects are not a severe problem for MTPs. In the case of MAPbI3, despite the detrapping of charges captured by shallow-level defects, even if radiative recombination assisted by shallow-level defects is by far the most dominant process, the recombination rate can be very high without compromising the performance of PSCs.55 This is because the emitted photons would probably be reabsorbed by the MTP. As expected, the photons get “recycled” before charge extraction by the respective electrodes.56 Moreover, the charge diffusion length of MAPbI3 is reported to cover a large range from several hundreds of nanometers to micrometers.57–59 This demonstrates that, as long as the free charge is not trapped and non-radiatively recombined via deep-level defects, it can be fully extracted from the MTP and yield high PCE.

Since deep-level defects are the origin of the non-radiative recombination process, they are the biggest problem. For MTP, the dynamics of charge recombination is closely correlated with the depopulation rate of the charges on defects and with the defect density. The deep-level defects in MTP are considered to be the major non-radiative recombination centre that eliminate the charges and limit the performance of PSCs.60 The non-radiative recombination process in MTP could reduce the steady-state electron/hole density, charge lifetime, and charge diffusion length, thereby leading to a severe loss of PSC performance.55 Higher dimensional defects in MTP films, such as GBs and surface traps, are considered to be composed of deep-level defects and thus are significantly responsible for the non-radiative recombination process.61 As a result, an MTP with abundant GBs and grain surface area exhibits low PL intensity, further revealing the faster non-radiative recombination rate occurring at these places.62 Conductive atomic force microscopy (c-AFM) clearly detected intra-grain heterogeneity in the VOC and short circuit current density (JSC), together with low VOC and JSC at GBs in MTP film,63 which further suggests that the state densities of facet-dependent defects at GBs and surfaces are primarily responsible for the reduced photovoltaic performance of PSCs.

3.1.2 Charge trasportation. Free charges in a semiconductor are transported rapidly through the crystal lattice under an electric field until they interact with scatterers (e.g., phonons, impurities, and defects) or are trapped by vacancies. When the charges eventually reach their respective electrodes, photon-to-current conversion is performed. As expected, the efficient transportation of free charges (electrons/holes) largely determines the photovoltaic performance of SCs. To achieve high PCE values for the SCs, the free charges must be transported far enough to be collected by the electrodes before they are trapped or scattered at a defect. This requires charge transport in a semiconductor with high charge mobility and a long charge lifetime. Because of their low charge effective mass (0.104 me, where me refers to the mass of an electron),64 high charge mobility,65 and long charge diffusion length,66 MTP based PSCs have displayed impressive optoelectronic performance. Nevertheless, dispersive transport of charges in MAPbI3 was identified in PSCs using mesoporous titanium oxide (TiO2) as the electron transporter.67 Whereas, no such behaviour was observed in planar PSCs containing a [6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) passivation layer as an electron transporter.67 This was suggested to be due to the considerable presence of defects at the MAPbI3 interface due to the use of mesoporous TiO2. In addition, deep-level defects at GBs in MTP could also create potential barriers to reduce the conductivity and charge transport, and increase the charge scattering.68 This is evidenced by the much lower defect density (1010–1011cm−3) in single-crystal MAPbI3 than in polycrystalline MAPbI3 film (1016–1017cm−3).12 The former exhibits much higher charge mobility and longer charge diffusion lengths.12,69,70

3.2 The negative impacts of the defects in MTP

As discussed above, the presence of defects in MTP significantly influences the free charges drifting within the crystal structure. In addition to this, the presence of defects could also lead to some detrimental impacts on the characteristics of MTP. In this part, we will introduce the negative impacts of defects in MTP, involving ion migration and structural instability.
3.2.1 Ion migration. Ion migration in inorganic halide-based perovskite materials was observed 30 years ago. The migration of halide-ion vacancies with low activation energies (EA) was considered to be the intrinsic mechanism.73 The hysteresis74–76 and giant switchable photovoltaic effect20 of MTP based PSCs have made the ion migration within MTP the focus of recaptured attention. For example, based on the new finding of ferroelectricity and trapping effects in MTP film, it was proposed that ionic defect migration under an electric field might be the possible origin of these phenomena, effectively resulting in the photocurrent hysteresis effect.71,72 Moreover, it was suggested that the ion migration could induce polarization of the MTP, leading to hysteretic behaviour in PSCs.77 The ion migration process was also considered as a main factor in several physical phenomena, including the giant dielectric constant,78 diminished transistor behaviour at room temperature,79 photo-induced phase separation and the self-poling effect,80 and electrical-field driven reversible conversion between PbI2 and MAPbI3 phases.81 Thus, many efforts have been made to further investigate the inherent mechanism of ion migration in MTP. It is well known that the ion migration is hard to realize in an ideal MTP without any defects, so study of the ion migration by means of defect-mediated hopping is an important direction.82

To deeply understand defect-mediated ion migration, many theoretical studies have been carried out.71,72,83–86 As a representative MTP, in MAPbI3, all the ions (MA+, Pb2+, and I−) could migrate through point defects (i.e., vacancies and interstitials). As shown in Fig. 5a–c, vacancy-mediated ion migration involves an ion moving from one lattice site to its adjacent site. The interstitials use the ion on the regular lattice site as a bridge instead of diffusion by hopping along different interstitial sites (Fig. 5d). This is because the close-packed structure of the MAPbI3 crystal lacks sufficient space for ions to move between two interstitial sites.85 In order to initiate the defect-mediated ion migration, each defect in MTP needs to be activated in the first place. Defects with lower EA are easy to move. As shown in Fig. 5e and f, Eames et al.72 calculate three vacancy transport pathways related to conventional hopping between neighbouring positions: (i) I− migration along an octahedron edge, (ii) Pb2+ migration along the [110] diagonal of the cubic unit cell and (iii) MA+ migration into a neighbouring vacant A-site cage. They found that the EA of I− migrations through VI mediated diffusion (0.58–0.68 eV) is lower than that of MA+ migration through VMA (0.84 eV) and Pb2+ through VPb (2.31 eV).72 Haruyama et al.84 showed that VI and VMA have EA of 0.32–0.45 eV and 0.57–0.89 eV, respectively. Azpiroz et al.71 reported that the EA of VI, VMA and VPb is calculated to be 0.08, 0.46, and 0.80 eV, respectively. Among these studies, EA values at different defects are quite discrepant. This is due to the different MAPbI3 phase and calculation models used. Despite this, it reaches a consensus that VI and VMA have the highest possibility for mobility among all the ions under external electric poling.72,86 In addition, recent work found that H+ migration in MAPbI3 along a transient hydrogen bond connecting two equatorial iodides only requires an EA of 0.29 eV.83

Despite the progress achieved on theoretical calculations, experimental observations of ion migration are still limited. In 2015, Xiao et al.20 observed a colour and morphology change close to the anode side of PSCs when the electrode was intentionally poled for 2 hours. They speculated that it originated from the drift of the charged Pb and MA vacancies. Later, Yuan et al.87 presented visual images of MA+ migration for the first time, using advanced photo-thermal induced resonance (PTIR) microscopy integrated with Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) chemical signal acquisition and AFM imaging. After the poling of a lateral MAPbI3 based PSC in a small electric field (∼0.3 V μm−1), the uniform distribution of MA+ was destroyed. This clearly indicated that MA+ accumulated around the cathode region and left MA vacancies behind at the original sites. The accumulation of MA+ near the cathode induced n-type doping, and the MA vacancies near the anode induced p-type doping. The p-i-n configuration from this fabrication resulted in enhancement of the PCE of PSCs from 4.31% to 9.08%.80 In 2016, the migration of I− through vacancy-mediated diffusion was directly visualized by optical microscopy and elemental mapping. Under a strong electric field (∼3 V μm−1) at 330 K, a reversible conversion between MAPbI3 and PbI2 phases was observed.81 Under external bias, however, Pb2+ was found to not be mobile. This is in agreement with the theoretical prediction for Pb2+ with high EA.72 Delugas et al.88 employed first-principles calculations to investigate the molecular dynamics of all the atoms in MAPbI3. On the nanosecond scale and at room temperature, the iodine defects (both vacancies and interstitials) were found to be very mobile and detectable, while both Pb and MA defects were found to be difficult to move. This is because the time scale of MA+ and Pb2+ diffusion is several orders of magnitude larger than that of I−.88 Thus, it is proposed that the migration of Ii occurs when one of the two I–Pb bonds rotates around the Pb in the plane of the dumbbell. This process will kick off a neighbouring I− and generate a new dumbbell at a neighbouring crystal site.88

The presence of a high density of defects in MTP could result in unfavourable vacancy-mediated ion migration and structural transformation, leading to decreased performance of PSCs. Therefore, more experimental observations of ion migration with the help of theoretical calculations are needed.

For example, Karunadasa et al.94 reported a highly stable 2D layered (PEA)2(MA)2[Pb3I10] (phenethylamine, PEA = C6H5(CH2)2NH+) that was exposed to moisture for 46 days with negligible degradation. Moreover, a mixture of halides (e.g., I and Br) in MTP was also found to increase the stability of the PSCs in the presence of humidity.95

Despite the progress achieved, several major drawbacks responsible for the degradation of MTP have reasons beyond its intrinsic structure, such as external environment (e.g., moisture, oxygen, temperature, and illumination) and internal intrinsic factors (e.g., ion migration, electron migration, and interfacial reactions).96 Essentially speaking, all these factors are associated with the defects present in MTP. In this section, the degradation mechanism of MTP originated from defects, in terms of moisture, oxygen, illumination, and thermal effects, are discussed.


3.2.2.1 Air instability. Moisture is one major reason for the degradation of MTP that diminishes the performance of unsealed PSCs. The reversible deprotonation as a result of hygroscopic MA exposure to moisture is mainly responsible for triggering the structural decomposition.97 Recently, Choi et al.98 reported that the charges preferentially trapped by the defects at GBs provide charge accumulation sites and infiltration pathways for water vapour. This is responsible for the irreversible moisture-induced degradation of MTP regardless of the polarity of charge.98 The degradation of MTP was found to be initiated from the GBs, which then gradually led to decomposition to irreversible products. The overlapping images of the topology and the potential distribution of the MTP clearly indicate the charges trapped along GBs.98 It was also proposed that the trapping of charges with any polarities at defects together with the induced local electric field could lead to deprotonation of organic cations.98 Such a deprotonation process could lead to the evaporation of volatile molecules (MA and FA) at room temperature. As a result, controlling the defect density in MTP, especially the surface defects, can increase its moisture stability to a great extent.

Similar to the case of moisture, the instability resulting from MTP exposure to oxygen is also a major obstacle. For example, exposure of MAPbI3 film to oxygen together with light leads to the formation of reactive superoxide (O2−) species. The O2− can deprotonate MA+ of photo-excited MAPbI3, leading to the formation of PbI2, H2O, MA, and I2 (Fig. 6a–d).99 DFT calculations revealed that VI defects in the crystal structure as reaction mediators play a key role in facilitating the superoxide formation.100 Moreover, it is found that negatively charged VI binding with O2 has the lowest formation energy to form superoxide. The thus-formed superoxide species could lead to oxygen-induced degradation from the surface into the interior of the MTP. In contrast, the grains with larger size, corresponding to low GB density and low defect density show lower yield of superoxide formation and thus display better stability compared to the grains with smaller size. This further suggests that higher defect density more easily promotes the decomposition rate of MTP through the penetration of O2 at GBs.100 Moreover, the formation of H2O when degraded by O2 could also promote the decomposition rate of MTP.

3.2.2.2 Light irradiation instability. The optical instability of MTP under ultraviolet (UV) light irradiation was already observed in the early stages of PSC research. The photocatalytic effect of TiO2 was found to generate defects at the interface.101 This is considered as one of the main reasons for MTP degradation.102 Later, Leijtens et al.103 confirmed this proposed mechanism. They found that using a UV filter or replacing TiO2 with photo-stable Al2O3 could significantly enhance the UV stability of MTP. Inverted TiO2-free PSCs using UV filters, however, also displayed a decreased JSC and fill factor (FF), as well as the presence of light-activated metastable defects, leading to performance degradation of the PSCs.104 These results show the possibility of light-driven degradation within the MTP. Recently, Xing et al.105 observed that sunlight irradiation could greatly reduce the energy barrier for ion migration and promote ultrafast vacancy-mediated ion migration within polycrystalline MTP films. Instead, ion migration within a single crystal is difficult to detect, because an MTP single crystal with much lower defect density shows a much higher energy barrier for ion migration, therefore, the density of the intrinsic defects in MTP plays a crucial role in the light-driven structural degradation process.105

The degradation through vacancy-mediated ion migration was further observed in MTPs containing mixed halides. One superiority of MTP is that its bandgap (Eg) can be tuned by controlling the composition.106 For example, the Eg of (MAPbBrxI3−x (x = 0–3)) can be continuously tuned between 1.55 and 2.43 eV by adjusting the ratio of I to Br.107 The substitution of Br for I not only yields a higher VOC, but also considerably enhances the stability of the MTP by stabilizing its crystal lattice.95,109 It was found, however, that the light irradiation could lead to phase segregation and the growth of I-rich and Br-rich domains.110–112 Very recently, it was proposed that the segregation was attributable to the vacancy-influenced halide ion migration and the differing affinity of Pb2+ towards Br− and I−.108 As shown in Fig. 6e, vacancies play a major role in achieving stable I-rich and Br-rich phases after photoirradiation.108 A similar study also indicated that ion segregation takes place via halide defects, resulting in I-rich low-band-gap regions close to the illuminated surface.113 The localized strain induced by a single photo-excited charge interacting with the soft and ionic lattice of an MTP with mixed halides was sufficient to promote halide phase separation and nucleate a I-rich cluster.114 Although this segregation effect was reversible under short-term illumination, the defect-assisted light-driven ion migration from long-term irradiation could cause rapid degradation of MTPs with mixed halides, leading to poor performance of PSC devices.115


3.2.2.3 Thermal instability. The defects in MTP also influence the thermal stability of PSCs. The decomposition of MTP at GBs or surfaces can occur at much lower temperature and accelerate at elevated temperature. As expected, the decomposition of MTP begins from the GBs and interface.61 Theoretical work has shown that the iodine defects (both VI and Ii) are very mobile, especially at higher temperature. Fig. 6f shows snapshots of Ii diffusion during annealing.88 There is also indirect evidence for the thermal instability of MTP caused by defects. For example, single crystal MAPbI3 with a very low defect density (109–1011 cm−3) shows no signature of thermal decomposition, but with a small amount of HI and MA beginning to volatilize at 240 °C.116,117 Moreover, Stoumpos et al.57 reported that the decomposition of MAPbI3 single crystal started at >300 °C under N2 flow. Supasai et al.118 and Dualeh et al.119 demonstrated, however, that the well-studied MAPbI3 polycrystalline films with higher defect density by several orders of magnitude (1015–1017 cm−3) normally decompose at the low temperature of 150 °C. Although several lines of experimental evidence indicated that the thermal instability of an MTP was related to the defects in the MTP, further theoretical insights and deeper investigations into the defect-assisted thermal instability of MTP are still needed.

3.3 The positive impacts of the defects in MTP

It is well-known that the intentional introduction of defects through element doping into a semiconductor can endow the material with superior electrical properties. This strategy has been intensively applied in the smart and rational design of semiconductors to be used in energy conversion and storage systems, including rechargeable ion batteries,29,30,120,121 hydrogen evolution,122 fuel cells,31 and supercapacitors.123 As mentioned in the section above, however, the defects in MTP have been found to be greatly detrimental to the performance of PSCs. Nevertheless, the intentional introduction and manipulation of defects in MTP might bring some potential benefits and even enable the design of MTPs for a broader range of applications. In this section, some studies dealing with the potential advantages of utilizing defects in MTP are discussed.
3.3.1 Intentional manipulation of defects. Although there is a general consensus that defects in MTP film need to be reduced as much as possible to realize efficient PSCs, some emerging studies have proposed that defects could play very vital functions relating to the electrical properties of MTP and the performance of PSCs.36 As noted previously, the intentional introduction of interstitial defects into inorganic perovskite oxide semiconductors could modify their physical and electrical properties.124,125 Recently, it was reported that the intentional introduction of potassium cations (K+) into MAPbI3 is beneficial for improving the performance of PSCs (Fig. 7a).126 The doped K+ mainly occupied the interstitial sites in the MAPbI3 lattice and simultaneously created MA vacancies. The presence of K+ in an optimized amount (20 mol%) could lead to increased crystallinity, red-shifted PL emission, and decreased surface potential of the MAPbI3 film, thereby resulting in enhanced charge separation and reduced charge recombination in the MTP film. As shown in Fig. 7b and c, PSCs based on K0.2MA0.8PbI3 exhibited the highest PCE of 19.3% with stabilized power output. Very recently, Son et al.33 again confirmed that K+ is the valid dopant to occupy the interstitial sites in MTP by introducing a series of alkali metal iodides (LiI, NaI, KI, RbI, and CsI), which prevent the formation of iodide Frenkel defects. In this regard, the hysteresis effect can be greatly reduced by K+ doping. These studies should motivate more attention to the engineering of defects in MTP to lead to enhanced performance of PSCs and other applications.

3.3.2 Potential application utilizing the defects in MTP. The presence of defects in MTP often results in decreased performance of PSCs due to easy polarization, ion migration, charge trapping, and scattering. The disadvantages induced by the defects in MTP may be advantageous, however, in other applications. For example, the use of MTP has been extended to the field of resistive switching (RS).127 For MTP, VI with the lowest EA was considered to be the main cause of RS behavior.128 Lee et al.128 designed a non-volatile memory memristor taking advantage of the defect migration in MAPbI3−xClx film. The designed device shows a remarkable RS effect. As shown in Fig. 7d, under an applied forward bias voltage, the randomly distributed VI could be initialized to be positively charged and drift toward the negatively charged electrode (indium tin oxide, ITO). When the alignment of VI took place, the electron transport rate was enhanced by trap-to-trap hopping via charged VI sites (Fig. 7e). This is also called the “set” process (Fig. 7f), which originates from the well-known space-charge limited conduction (SCLC) of semiconductors. When electron detrapping occurs during the reset process, the conduction paths will be ruptured, and the material will be switched from the low-resistance state (ON) to the high-resistance state (OFF). During the “set-reset” process, MTP displays obvious bipolar RS characteristics.127

Very recently, an application of MAPbI3 as an O2 gas sensor has been developed.129 It was proposed that VI in an MAPbI3 crystal provided the active sensing sites for the O2 filling, because VI defects can act as charge traps. The decreased number of VI after O2 filling immediately translates into a higher number of charges, which are available for electrical transport with higher current. Moreover, to verify this “trap healing mechanism”, MAPbI3 film was treated with CH3I, in which an I atom is capable of filling a VI (Fig. 7g).130 After CH3I treatment, two effects can be observed in the current response measurement. As shown in Fig. 7h, the absolute current measured in pure N2 increased from 40 to 120 nA, which could be explained as a trap healing effect induced by the CH3I-mediated vacancy filling. What is more, the sensor response to O2 is lower after CH3I treatment. This is evidenced by the relative change in the current measured in N2 and air decreasing from 68 times without CH3I treatment to 45 times with CH3I treatment, which can be assigned to the lower number of VI active sites available for O2 sensing after CH3I treatment.

To date, applications utilizing the defects in MTP, such as memristors and O2 sensors, are still rare. It is highly desirable to explore more applications based on the defects in MTP.

4. Measurement methods for the defects in MTP

Although it is beyond the existing technology to visually see the defects in the crystal structure of MTP experimentally, it is still of great importance to understand the features of the defects (density and energy level) in MTP experimentally. Efficient techniques to study the defects in MTP are necessary and powerful in terms of providing useful information on the defects. In this part, we summarize the advantages and disadvantages of frequently-used technologies that characterize the defects in MTPs. These technologies include space charge limited current (SCLC), thermal admittance spectroscopy (TAS), thermally stimulated current (TSC), steady-state PL emission, and deep-level transient spectroscopy (DLTS), which are explained as follows.

4.1 Space charge limited current (SCLC)

It is well known that the defects in semiconductors can capture and trap the free charges, and thus, the electrical properties of the material can be changed after all the defects are filled. In this regard, the SCLC method has been widely utilized to measure the relevant properties of defects (i.e., conductivity, carrier concentration, carrier mobility, and defect density).131

The inherent mechanism of the SCLC method is as follows: when a semiconductor is subjected to a sufficiently large electric field through Ohmic contacts, charges will be injected into the material and generate a current flow. When defect sites are present, they will capture a part of the injected charges and thus reduce the density of free charges. As expected, the current is limited by the space-charge effect. The defect density in the semiconductor can be obtained from the curve of the current vs. voltage (J–V) in the region of space-charge-injected current.135

4.2 Thermal admittance spectroscopy (TAS)

Charged defects are equivalent to a localized capacitor, so that the trapped charges not only influence the electrical properties of the semiconductor, but also the capacitance characteristics. By tracing the changes in capacitance during the alternating current (AC) voltage, the features of a defect can be deduced. As an established and effective technique, TAS has been broadly applied to measure the defect density in SCs, including both shallow- and deep-level defects, by tracing the junction capacitance,140,141 because the defect density and its effectiveness as a dopant are related to the junction capacitance. In order to reveal the defect density and energy distribution within the band gap in a semiconductor, TAS measurements at various temperatures are commonly used.134

In typical p–n junction SCs, the capacitance mainly originates from the charging–discharging of defects with changes in the frequency of the AC voltage. Defect occupancy of the semiconductor is determined by the position of the Fermi level. As shown in Fig. 8d, electron traps below the Fermi level are assumed to be filled by photo-generated electrons.133,134 When the frequency of the AC voltage changes, the corresponding traps can release their charge within the AC period and thus contribute to the admittance. The release of the charge depends on not only the energy depth of the defect, but also the temperature, because the charge could be thermally activated and released, and then contribute to the admittance signal. Therefore, the defect traps with different EA at different modulated AC frequencies and temperatures are related to the junction capacitance.134

The TAS method is a valid technology to qualitatively compare the defect density in MTP deposited under different conditions. Nevertheless, TAS has its own limitations in principle, as follows. (i) Admittance spectroscopy cannot distinguish between valence band and conduction band states. If valence and conduction band states are similar in shape and density, the analysis of admittance data would lead to an overestimated defect density.140 (ii) Only the defect traps below the energy demarcation can capture or emit charges and contribute to the capacitance signal. In this case, the defects with deeper levels are easily omitted, leading to incomplete estimation of the defect density. (iii) TAS measurement is a temperature dependent technique. A trapped charge with a long thermal emission time cannot contribute to the capacitance signal, which also reduces the accuracy of the measured defect density.

TSC has obvious limitations. Inequality (12) only estimates the lower limit of the defect density in the MTP. During the TSC measurement process, the incomplete trap filling, partial detrapping of charges during thermalization, and recombination of the detrapped charges with opposite charges could lead to undetected defects. Moreover, similar to TAS, only those detrapped photo-generated charges could contribute to the TSC response. Under these conditions, the defect traps with long thermal emission times will not be counted in the TSC measurement, and this reduces the accuracy of the estimation.

4.5 Deep-level transient spectroscopy (DLTS)

The release of the charges captured by shallow-level defects can influence the electrical properties of a semiconductor. The charge trapped by deep-level defects is difficult to detect, however, using the techniques mentioned above. DLTS analysis has improved features from the aspects of its sensitivity, range of observable defect depths, and facile operation.149 The essential feature of DLTS is the ability to set an emission rate window. The instrument only responds when it detects a transient with a rate within this window. Thus, if the emission rate of a defect varies in this window at different temperatures, the instrument will show a response peak at the specific temperature. The main idea behind DLTS is to use the variation in defect emission rates with temperature to determine the difference in energy between the defect states and one of the band edges as well as to determine the capture cross-section of the defects.150

In the typical DLTS measurement, the capacitance transients are investigated by measuring the capacitance variations with variations in temperature, usually in the range from liquid nitrogen temperature to room temperature (300 K) or above. During the initial bias pulse at low temperature, defects could be filled by the bias-generated charges. In this process, the capture rate of a defect trap is much larger than its emission rate, and thus, the latter can be neglected. When the temperature increases, DLTS peaks will appear if the emission rate of charge trapped by the defects is within the rate window. The defect density can be obtained directly from the capacitance change, corresponding to complete filling of the defect traps with a saturating injection pulse or the largest possible majority-carrier pulse. Even if such a defect level could be saturated during the injection pulse, it might also be able to empty during the fall time of the pulse.149 Moreover, if the thermal emission rate is much larger than the optical rate (shallow centres such as donors, acceptors, and bound excitons), the defect can no longer be filled between pulses, and the transient disappears. 5. Defect engineering for highly efficient MTP based PSCs

As described above, the presence of defect trap states located in the forbidden gap of an MTP is one of the main limiting factors obstructing the fabrication of high-performance PSCs. Suppressing the formation of the defects in the MTP is an effective way to improve the PCE and operational stability of PSCs. To date, many research efforts have been made, and much progress has also been achieved. In this section, we will give a brief overview of the state-of-the-art effective defect engineering techniques for highly efficient PSCs, including defect passivation, GB density reduction, and structural dimensionality reduction.

5.1 Defect passivation

Although most vacancy defects create shallow transition state levels near the band edges, deep-level defects (e.g., interstitial and antisite defects) have been identified as non-radiative recombination centres that are fatal to the optoelectronic properties of MTP. The defects in MTP could possibly exist on the surface, at the GBs, and in the grain interior. Theoretical results suggest that the non-radiative recombination centres for charges in MTP are mainly related to the presence of I-rich chemical species (e.g., I interstitials or I antisites).43,134 In order to eliminate these species of deep-level defects, passivating them through chemical interactions is the most facile and effective strategy.
5.1.1 Additive passivation. To passivate the defects at the GBs and on the surface of MTP film, numerous small-sized molecules have been used as additives. This is because these molecules are able to bond with the point defect sites in MTP. As a classic electron transport layer in inverted PSC devices, PCBM coated on MAPbI3 film after thermal annealing can permeate into the MAPbI3 layer along the GBs, effectively passivating the defects and eliminating the notorious photocurrent hysteresis phenomenon in PSCs.161 The reduction of the defect density significantly reduces the recombination of interface charges and prolongs the lifetime and mobility of charges, leading to improved performance of PSCs. Based on DFT calculations, it was found that the introduction of PCBM can interact with the Pb–I antisite defect, in which I occupies the Pb site and forms a trimer with neighbouring I atoms, as shown in Fig. 11a. This interaction leads to a hybridized ground state wave function between the PCBM and the MAPbI3 surface (Fig. 11b). Because the bond between PCBM and I− is thermodynamically favourable, the formation of deep-level defects is significantly suppressed (Fig. 11c).161 The bond between PCBM and I− results from the charge transfer from PCBM to the buckyball and the van der Waals force between I− and the butyric acid methyl ester group, which is further evidenced by liquid-state 1H nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) measurements.162 Moreover, our work revealed that the introduction of ordered PCBM nanorods into MAPbI3 film was also found to be useful for defect passivation and simultaneous incorporation of “electron transport highways” within the MAPbI3 film.163 This is due to the formation of bulk heterojunction structures between the MAPbI3 and the PCBM nanorods.163 Apart from fullerene derivatives, CuI and Cu(thiourea)I [Cu(Tu)I] have been recently reported to effectively passivate the defects in MAPbI3 film.164 A defect can interact with the under-coordinated metal cation and the halide anion at the surface of MAPbI3. Such interaction not only provides I− to fill the halide vacancies and coordinate the under-coordinated metal cations (red arrows in Fig. 11d), but also chelates the under-coordinated halide anions with the electropositive Cu atoms (red dashed lines in Fig. 11d). The incorporated p-type defect passivators can form bulk heterojunctions with MAPbI3, which can further facilitate hole transport and decrease the recombination of charges. As a result, the passivated MAPbI3 film demonstrated a high PCE of 19.9% with no hysteresis in inverted PSCs (Fig. 11e).164 In addition, other small molecules, such as pyridine,62,165 triiodide,48 thiocyanide ion (SCN−),166,167 iodopentafluorobenzene (IPFB),168 1,2-ethanedithiol (EDT),169 guanidinium (GA),170 N-doped graphene,171 and Lewis acid and Lewis base172 have also been used to passivate the defects in MTP, leading to improved performance of PSCs.

5.1.2 Surface passivation. Apart from the reduction of defects by the use of small molecules, the elimination of defects at the interface between the MTP active layer and the electron transport layer (ETL) in PSCs has recently also attracted great interest. In 2014, Noel et al.173 found that the treatment of the crystal surface of MAPbI3−xClx with the Lewis bases thiophene and pyridine could significantly reduce non-radiative electron–hole recombination and prolong the photoluminescence lifetimes up to 2 μs. This is attributed to the electronic passivation of under-coordinated Pb atoms in MAPbI3−xClx.173 Zhao et al.174 treated the surface of MTP film with long-chain C8 diammonium iodide. The passivation was confirmed by scanning Kelvin probe microscopy (SKPM) and time-resolved PL (TRPL) measurements. Recently, Lin et al.157 introduced an n-type π-conjugated Lewis base, indacenodithiophene end-capped with 1,1-dicyanomethylene-3-indanone (IDIC), as an interlayer in PSCs. As shown in Fig. 12a, IDIC with n-type semiconducting properties enabled the extraction of electrons from MTP, while the Lewis base end groups in IDIC simultaneously passivated the under-coordinated Pb ions and Pb clusters in MTP (namely, the Lewis acid traps).157 The energy diagram results (Fig. 12b) clearly indicated the reduction of the defect density after the surface passivation by IDIC. Compared to the control device without IDIC, the PSCs with IDIC yielded a 45% PCE enhancement (19.4% for the champion cell). Zheng et al.175 reported quaternary ammonium halides as an efficient interfacial passivation layer to reduce the defect density and prolong the recombination lifetime of charges in MAPbI3 film. As shown in Fig. 12c and d, quaternary ammonium ions were expected to only passivate MA+ vacancies at cuboctahedral sites to compensate the loss of MA+ on the film surface. The authors found that, compared with the PCBM passivation layer, choline chloride passivation can yield higher photovoltaic performance with a PCE of 21.0%, JSC of 23.7 mA cm−2, VOC of 1.14 V, and FF of 78.0% (Fig. 12e). Moreover, treating the TiO2 electron transport layer with ionic liquid137 or Cl155 is another efficient method to passivate the defects at the interface between the TiO2 and the MTP layer. Without Cl treatment, the Pb–I antisite defects will lead to localized states near the valence band edge, where these states can capture holes and become non-radiative recombination centres, leading to decreased performance of the PSCs.155 When the TiO2 is treated with Cl, the formation energy of the Pb–Cl antisite at the PbCl2-terminated interface is improved, indicating that the antisite defect is suppressed (Fig. 12f). Theoretical calculations also showed that the Pb–Cl antisite defect has little effect on interface recombination, and it becomes much shallower and more delocalized.155 The improved performance of PSCs containing a Cl-treated TiO2 layer was also evidenced by a high laboratory PCE of 21.4% (VOC = 1.189 V, JSC = 22.3 mA cm−2, FF = 0.806), without hysteresis in J–V sweeps (Fig. 12g) and having excellent reproducibility within the narrow distribution of PCE values (average PCE of 19.6%) in over 204 devices (Fig. 12h).

In addition to the defect passivation of MTP film, other interface issues (e.g., energy level alignment, band bending, pinned Fermi level) could be affected by the density of surface defect states (DoSDS) of MTP film during the ETL deposition. It is generally suggested that the MAPbI3 films deposited under different preparation conditions could generate various DoSDS on the film surface, thus leading to different energy level alignments at the interface. For example, Wang et al.176 prepared an MAPbI3 film using a two-step solid reaction method and found upward band bending in the MAPbI3 layer when C60 with an upward shift of the energy levels was deposited on the MAPbI3 film. Schulz et al.,177 however, prepared an MAPbI3 film using a one-step spin-coating method, and reported that no band bending was observed at the MAPbI3/C60 interface after C60 deposition. This is because almost flat band conditions are created with the formation of the MAPbI3/C60 interface. Moreover, the DoSDS of the MTP film could lead to a pinned Fermi level. For example, the n-type MTP single crystal shows a pinned Fermi level that moves downward to the VBM, which is caused by donor-type surface states.178 Very recently, it was also proposed that the interface issues are highly correlated with the initial DoSDS of the MTP film.179 In particular, only small changes in the surface band bending are observed when the initial DoSDS of the MTP film is high. This is because the Fermi level is strongly pinned close to the CBM. When the DoSDS is low, the MTP film after ETL deposition displays strong band flattening at the MTP/ETL interface, which has resulted from an upward band bending of the MTP.

5.1.3 Self-passivation. In addition to the extraneous molecule-assisted defect passivation discussed above, MTP films can also be self-passivated by control of the component ratio of the MTP. The PbI2 residual left in the MAPbI3 film has been demonstrated to be an important factor that can influence the performance of PSCs. For example, excess PbI2 can induce the self-passivation of MTP, giving it a prolonged charge lifetime and reduced non-radiative recombination at both the GBs and the interface,180–187 although the film stability was found to be slightly decreased.188 Chen et al.181 proposed that prolonged annealing time for MTP film would force the MAI to escape from the GBs and generate a PbI2 rich region (Fig. 13a and b). As a result, the thus-formed p-i-n structures (TiO2–PbI2–MAPbI3 and MAPbI3–PbI2–2,2′,7,7′-tetrakis(N,N-di-p-methoxyphenyl amine)-9,9′-spirobifluorene (spiro-OMeTAD)) effectively reduced the probability of charge recombination at both interfaces. As shown in Fig. 13c, at the TiO2/perovskite interface, the presence of wide band-gap PbI2 species helps to reduce the probability of charge recombination. At the perovskite/spiro-OMeTAD interface, the presence of PbI2 changes the band alignment bending from downward to upward, which reduces the charge recombination between the electrons from perovskite and the holes from spiro-OMeTAD, as shown in Fig. 13d. At the optimized annealing time of 60 min, the PSCs achieved a PCE of 12.0%, with VOC, JSC, and FF of 0.959 V, 18.05 mA cm−2, and 69.32%, respectively (Fig. 13e). It was also found that the hysteresis effect could be significantly reduced by prolonging the film annealing time up to 100 min.189 It was due to the passivation of GBs by precipitated PbI2. In order to confirm the influence of the PbI2 dosage, Jacobsson et al.53 compared MTP films with different compositions, ranging from a large deficiency of PbI2 (i.e., MAI-rich) to a large surplus of PbI2 (i.e., PbI2-rich). It was interesting that the MAI-rich condition yielded a higher VOC value of 1.20 V, although the PbI2-rich condition gave a higher PCE in PSC devices. They suggested that a surplus of MA+ could lead to the complete conversion of PbI2 into MTP phase, enabling MTP with high crystal quality, longer charge lifetimes, and high PL and external electroluminescence quantum efficiency.53 Also, they pointed out that thick PbI2 layers with the wrong energy matching may insulate individual grains and block charge transfer instead. Moreover, they also found that the excess MAI present at GBs could obstruct the ion migration, leading to decreased J–V hysteresis and increased photostability.53

5.2 GB density reduction

Considering the high crystallinity of MAPbI3, intrinsic defects might have a good chance to occupy sites at the surface and GBs. Many efforts have been devoted to increasing the size of the grains (i.e., reduction of GB density), as well as their crystal quality. In this part, we mainly focus on the film fabrication strategies that are able to enlarge the grain size of MTP film,52,191–196 though the grain size can be enlarged through the introduction of some small molecules as additives to MTP.161,171,197,198

5.3 Structure dimensionality reduction

Recently, reduction of the dimensionality of MTP has been demonstrated to improve its stability.199 This is mainly due to defect passivation at the GBs of low-dimensional MTP. As a result of its lower defect density, as well as higher exciton binding energy, the low-dimensional MTP showed competitive PL and electroluminescence (EL) efficiency.200,201 From the 3D to the 2D MTP structure, the small MA+ is replaced by a much bulkier organic primary ammonium cation. This replacement makes the MTP 2D because of steric effects.

The family of 2D layered MTP has Ruddlesden–Popper phases with the formula A2(MA)n−1PbnI3n+1 (A = phenylethylammonium (PEA), butylamine (BA), n = 1, 2, 3, 4…∞). As n increases, the band gap of the A2(MA)n−1PbnI3n+1 changes from 2.24 eV (BA2PbI4 (n = 1)) to 1.52 eV (MAPbI3 (n = ∞)).201 As shown in Fig. 16a, compared to 3D MTP, the low-dimensional MTP with a lower defect density exhibits enhanced ambient stability, high formation energy for defects, and high structural stability.202,203 In other words, the photocurrent hysteresis, photo-induced poling effect, film decomposition, and non-radiative recombination centres in 2D layered MTP with long-chain cations could be largely suppressed.204 In addition, the presence of long-chain cations in 2D MTP also prevents moisture and oxygen from entering into the MTP structure and thus enhances the PSC stability.204 Smith et al.,94 for the first time, fabricated a high-quality 2D MTP film with robust moisture resistance based on PEA+. DFT calculations suggested that MTP layers intercalated by PEA could introduce quantitatively appreciable van der Waals interactions. Such van der Waals interaction increases the formation energy and material stability.203 When the optimized PEA content (n = 60) was used, the hysteresis-free PSCs showed a certified PCE of 15.3% (Fig. 16b) and improved moisture and thermal stability. As shown in Fig. 16c, Tsai et al.205 employed the hot-casting technique to fabricate 2D layered (BA)2(MA3)Pb4I13 (n = 4) with strong preferential out-of-plane alignment with respect to the contacts. More importantly, the 2D layered (BA)2(MA3)Pb4I13 shows a lack of Urbach tails in the optical absorption, a very small Stokes shift, and strong absorption and photoluminescence, indicating that (BA)2(MA3)Pb4I13 has high crystallinity, few defects, and a disorder-induced density of states in the band gap. As a result, (BA)2(MA3)Pb4I13 based PSCs demonstrated a promising PCE of 12.51% (Fig. 16d), and un-encapsulated devices retained over 60% of their efficiency for over 2250 hours under operation conditions, with great tolerance to 65% relative humidity (Fig. 15e and f). It should be noted that the encapsulated devices did not show any degradation under the same operation conditions (Fig. 15g and h).205

6. Summary and outlook

Despite the rapid development of highly efficient PSCs based on organic–inorganic hybrid MTP, the large-scale application of commercial PSCs with high PCE and long-term operation stability is still in the future. Here, we give an elaborate review to provide insight into and understanding of the state-of-the-art research progress on defect science and engineering in MTP, which is a fundamental issue for guiding the further development of MTP based PSCs, as well as other SCs. Beginning with the well-established understanding of the defects in traditional inorganic semiconductors, we have discussed the defects in MTP in detail as follows: our theoretical understanding of the origins of the intrinsic defects, the disadvantages and advantages of defects, frequently used techniques for the characterization of defects, and the latest research progress on defect engineering toward high-performance PSC devices.

Although the GBs in MTP film are broadly considered to be detrimental, a few recent reports have claimed that GBs may not be the dominant non-radiative centres and may be rather benign or even beneficial for collecting charges efficiently.206,207 Such conflicting conclusions are most likely caused by the different features of GBs (e.g., surface termination, electronic state and resulting charge carrier behaviour). The different features are highly correlated with several factors (e.g., the film fabrication strategy, material composition, and illumination conditions).208–212 Therefore, further theoretical and experimental studies on the photo-physical properties of GBs in different environments are necessary to fully unravel the key role of GBs in PSCs.

At present, there are several defect characterization techniques to estimate the density and energy level of the defects. The results from these measurement techniques have low consistency, however. When the defect characterization largely depends on the material preparation conditions and the characterization techniques used, detailed comparisons of the same MTP sample characterized by various techniques are welcome. Apart from the challenges arising from the measurement techniques, defect identification in the MTP structure also remains a challenge. Although many theoretical studies have predicted which type of defect has the highest possibility to exist in MTP crystals, visual observation of the defect structure (i.e., specific atom vacancies, atom interstitials, atom dislocations, etc.) has not been reported to date. Emerging studies have attempted to show mapping images of the electronic trap states using PL mapping in combination with scanning electron microscopy (SEM),62 atomic force microscopy (AFM),213 and femtosecond transient absorption microscopy (TAM).214 These are still only methods for the detection of defect positions, however, instead of their identification. Therefore, the next big step forward for defect identification may lie in the identification of defect types with the help of extremely high-magnification microscopy with resolution down to the atomic-scale as well as theoretical calculations. It should be noted that high-magnification microscopy requires a high energy electron beam, which could destroy the MTP material. Successful image acquisition from MTP crystals using atomic-resolution transmission electron microscopy (TEM) with extremely low beam doses could provide an important research direction.215

Inspired by the chemical self-healing effect of CuInSe2,216 the unravelling of the mechanism for self-healing of defects in MTP is exhilarating. This would be a facile, highly efficient, and low-energy-cost way to obtain ideal crystals of MTP. Pioneering theoretical work has demonstrated the light-induced annihilation of Frenkel defects in MTP.217 When the presence of large amounts of defects in lead-free MTP materials greatly limits their development in PSCs applications,218 effective strategies for defect engineering in lead-free MTP are also urgently required. Therefore, further investigations and deeper insight into the formation mechanism and methodology for manipulating defects in MTP could enable MTP based PSCs with enhanced performance.

In addition, there is a new concept that a promising MTP absorber should exhibit not only high dimensionality in its structure, but also high electronic dimensionality.219 An optoelectronic semiconductor may exhibit high structural dimensionality, but low electronic dimensionality. It may hold less promise as a light absorber, mainly due to the high barriers for the transport of isotropic current flow (electrons/holes), and the presence of deeper-level defects. Nevertheless, the defects in MTP play indelible roles, as mentioned above. Therefore, deeper insight into defect science and engineering could open up new chapters in our essential understanding and the practical engineering of MTP towards highly efficient optoelectronic devices in the future.
