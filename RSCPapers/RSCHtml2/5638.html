Abstract

Over the past decade, electrochemical energy storage (EES) devices have greatly improved, as a wide variety of advanced electrode active materials and new device architectures have been developed. These new materials and devices should be evaluated against clear and rigorous metrics, primarily based on the evidence of real performances. A series of criteria are commonly used to characterize and report performance of EES systems in the literature. However, as advanced EES systems are becoming more and more sophisticated, the methodologies to reliably evaluate the performance of the electrode active materials and EES devices need to be refined to realize the true promise as well as the limitations of these fast-moving technologies, and target areas for further development. In the absence of a commonly accepted core group of metrics, inconsistencies may arise between the values attributed to the materials or devices and their real performances. Herein, we provide an overview of the energy storage devices from conventional capacitors to supercapacitors to hybrid systems and ultimately to batteries. The metrics for evaluation of energy storage systems are described, although the focus is kept on capacitive and hybrid energy storage systems. In addition, we discuss the challenges that still need to be addressed for establishing more sophisticated criteria for evaluating EES systems. We hope this effort will foster ongoing dialog and promote greater understanding of these metrics to develop an international protocol for accurate assessment of EES systems.

Introduction

Due to limited crude oil resources and growing concerns about their negative environmental impacts, particularly global warming and the emission of toxic chemicals, there is an increasing interest in renewable energy. Switching from fossil-based energy to renewable energy offers the possibility of saving energy and simplifying energy distribution by sending electrons over the grid, or even generating the energy from renewable sources on-site, instead of transporting fossil-based fuels. However, intermittency of renewable energy sources such as sunlight, wind, and tide calls for energy storage to ensure efficient, continuous, reliable and affordable energy supplies. Electrochemical energy storage (EES) technologies along with materials development constitute essential elements to tackle the challenges to provide a stable supply of energy. Over the past decade, steady progress has been made towards this goal through the development of advanced electrode active materials. Thus, high power supercapacitors and high-energy rechargeable batteries have been developed. However, each of these stand-alone technologies by itself lacks the requisite qualities to meet the commercial needs that have been shaped by the consumption of fossil fuels. Consequently, hybrid energy storage devices that strive to advance along the diagonal of a so-called Ragone plot, by combining the high power characteristics of supercapacitors with the high energy content of rechargeable batteries, have been explored1 (Fig. 1).

The introduction of advanced nanostructures for a new generation of energy storage systems has contributed to the blurring of the distinctions between batteries and supercapacitors based on two fundamentally different energy-storage mechanisms. However, increasing the energy content of supercapacitors to approach that of batteries, or improving the power performance of batteries to approach that of supercapacitors, usually comes at the cost of losses in power and/or cyclability of supercapacitors and deteriorations in the energy of batteries.2,3 Thus, the outstanding properties reported for some devices, e.g. supercapacitors with energy densities that approach or even exceed that of batteries,4 do not necessarily translate into reality. Although some metrics seem to support the claims of having both high power and high energy densities, the actual performance of a given device almost always contradicts the assertions being made. Thus, in spite of many attempts that have been made to standardize the evaluation methods of EES systems, there is still a lack of clear rules for evaluating the performance of EES systems in a fair and systematic way.

Herein, we first present an introduction to the basic principles of different energy storage systems. Then we describe some of the most important and commonly employed characterization techniques to guide newcomers of the EES community as well as scientists and engineers who may not be familiar with the electrochemical terms through the steps that are necessary to effectively and accurately evaluate their EES systems. We hope this effort will serve as a resourceful starting point, and as a step forward along with the efforts that others have made, to sharpen the distinction between different charge storage modalities. We also expect that the push into the evaluation of EES systems with appropriate metrics that give a real picture of the performance of EES systems will help in reaching a point where we can all speak the same language.

Overview of energy storage technologies

Electrical energy storage technology can be classified by the form of storage into a variety of categories including mechanical energy storage (e.g. flywheels), chemical energy storage and conversion (e.g. water splitting, fuel cells), thermal and thermochemical energy storage (e.g. heat storage, solar hydrogen, solar thermal energy), as well as electrical energy storage (e.g. physical capacitors, electrical double layer capacitors) and electrochemical energy storage (e.g. pseudocapacitors, batteries).6 Herein, we keep the focus on capacitors, supercapacitors, and batteries that are three different classes of energy storage devices, which share some similarities and have several key differences. These are different technologies with different capabilities and of course different applications. These energy storage devices are generally complementary (rather than competing) and combining them, especially a supercapacitor and a battery, should be considered an opportunity to help guarantee that the needs of our energy future can be met.7,8

Physical capacitors represent the first and simplest form of an energy storage device. They are usually made up of two parallel plates separated by a thin insulating material known as the dielectric (Fig. 2a). One plate of the capacitor has a positive charge, while the other maintains a negative charge. Thus, in a capacitor, the charge becomes stored in the form of an electric potential energy between the two plates. Electrolytic capacitors are considered as the latest evolutionary step of capacitor technology. In electrolytic capacitors the dielectric is replaced with an ionic (but not electronic) conductive medium, which is also often called the electrolyte.9 In an electrolytic capacitor the ions are freely mobile in the bulk of the electrolyte, thus providing a much greater charge density at the electrode/electrolyte interface than is possible at the electrode/dielectric interface.9 Therefore, the capacitance of electrolytic capacitors is often in the mF range, but only in the μF range for dielectric capacitors.


Fig. 2 Schematic representation of a conventional capacitor (a) and a supercapacitor (b). The detailed structure of the electrical double layer is presented as insets.

Supercapacitors (also known as electrochemical capacitors or ultracapacitors) do not use conventional dielectrics between the plates; instead, they consist of two electrodes separated by an ion-permeable separator (such as cardboard, ceramic, glass, plastic or paper) and an electrolyte that ionically connects the two electrodes (Fig. 2b). In a supercapacitor, the charge becomes stored either by separation of charge in a Helmholtz double layer at the electrode–electrolyte interface (known as the electrical or electrochemical double layer capacitance, EDLC) or by Faradaic charge-transfer via redox reactions (known as pseudocapacitance).10,11

Batteries are another intriguing class of energy storage devices. Batteries consist of two electrodes separated by an electrolyte. However, batteries do not actually store electrical energy. Batteries are devices that store energy in the form of chemical energy inside electrode active materials via chemical bonds, and convert the chemical energy into direct current electricity via redox reactions or via intercalation as with Li-ion batteries.12 In a battery, the energy is stored in a particular compound or compounds that make up the negative and positive poles, as well as the electrolyte.

Along with the two most popular research areas focusing on batteries and supercapacitors, in recent years, some supercapacitor-type electrode materials have been coupled with battery-type materials to produce hybrid energy storage systems, for which the term supercapattery (= supercapacitor + battery) has been coined.1,13,14 In hybrid energy storage devices, two electrodes with different charge storage behaviors, e.g. one capacitive (supercapacitor) and one Faradaic (battery), are combined, where the former serves as the power source, while the latter serves as the energy source. The behavior of a hybrid system resembles that of a rechargeable battery with a higher power and/or cycle life, or a supercapacitor with a higher energy.1 Such an integration offers opportunities for further advancement in the energy storage realm, and performance of such devices is generally in between that of a supercapacitor and a battery.

In general, electrical energy storage devices, like a hydroelectric power plant (Fig. 3), give potential energy to the charges.

A pumped storage hydropower technology gives gravitational potential energy to water, while electrical energy storage devices give electrical potential energy to their charges. In a pumped storage plant, water is pumped from a lower reservoir to an upper reservoir. Then, water flows through the pipes from the upper reservoir to the lower one. While passing through the pipes, the gravitational potential energy of water is changed into kinetic energy by turning a rotor, and then the kinetic energy is partially converted into electrical energy using a generator. In an energy storage device, charges flow through wires and their potential energy is converted to e.g., light energy or thermal energy, by a bulb or a resistor, respectively. Analogous to a water pump, the charges that have lost all their potential energy receive a potential boost from a power provider (like the pump) and the cycle begins again.

Generally, electrical charge can be stored either on the surface (double layer charging) or in the bulk of a material (Faradaic reactions). Thus, an ideal material for an EDLC must have a highly accessible effective surface area. For example, the charge storage in carbon-based materials is founded on the accumulation of electrical charge at the interface between the electrode active material and the electrolyte, giving rise to an EDLC (Fig. 4a). In this process, no Faradaic reactions take place across the electrode/electrolyte interface and no electrochemical reactions take place inside the bulk of the electrode active material. In rechargeable batteries, charge storage occurs through reversible Faradaic reactions throughout the bulk of the electrode materials in combination with the electrolyte (Fig. 4b). Thus, the material must be designed to allow the easy insertion/de-insertion of the reacting species throughout the material. Charge storage in pseudocapacitors is based on Faradaic redox reactions on the surface or near the surface of the electrode active materials, without ion diffusion within the bulk of the electrode active materials (Fig. 4c). As the name implies, hybrid energy storage systems are a hybrid between batteries and supercapacitors, with a behavior intermediate between EDLC and Faradaic processes as the two extremes (Fig. 4d).

It should be pointed out that both EDLC and pseudocapacitive charge storage processes are capacitive in nature and/or behavior, with near-rectangular cyclic voltammograms (CVs) or quasi-triangular galvanostatic charge/discharge (GCD) profiles, and the prefix “pseudo-” just reflects the difference in the mechanism of charge storage. On the other hand, well-defined peak-shaped CVs or nonlinear GCD profiles with one or more obvious potential plateaus are characteristics of rechargeable batteries and/or hybrid devices. Thus, different charge storage mechanisms can be categorized as (1) capacitive non-Faradaic charge storage (CNFS), (2) capacitive Faradaic charge storage (CFS), and (3) non-capacitive Faradaic charge storage (NCFS). The correct metrics for characterization of capacitive charge storage processes is capacitance (F, i.e. q/V) and that of non-capacitive Faradaic processes is the specific charge capacity (mA h, i.e. q/3.6). Thus, if the correct evaluation criteria had been used, many of the extremely high specific capacitance values derived from peak shaped CVs that have been reported in the literature would not have been exciting at all.

Now, the question is how to differentiate among the three charge storage mechanisms. It is clear what EDLCs are and how they differ from Faradaic charge storage processes. The confusion arises, however, when researchers try to distinguish between batteries and pseudocapacitors, both of which are Faradaic processes in nature. Two key features that help differentiate between the two mechanisms are15–17 (1) phase transformation(s) of the host material(s) in batteries that translates into peak-shaped CVs and plateaus in GCD profiles, where the pseudocapacitive material(s) either present rectangular shaped CVs or CVs with significantly broadened peaks; (2) intrinsic kinetics of the Faradaic processes where the redox reactions in batteries are diffusion-controlled processes (i.e. I ∝ ν0.5), while in pseudocapacitors the current is directly proportional to the scan rate (I ∝ ν). Hybrid energy storage systems show a behavior in between a capacitor and a battery. Contributions from each of the above-mentioned categories can be identified by analyzing the CV data, which is explained in detail subsequently. Contributions from EDLC and pseudocapacitance to the total capacitive charge storage can also be deconvoluted by step potential electrochemical spectroscopy techniques.

We can utilize system theory for analysis of the energy storage process, since this provides an analytical framework for viewing an energy storage device as a unified whole (Fig. 5).

In such a system, the input could be the voltage or current that is applied to the energy storage device and the output could be the current or voltage that can be readily monitored. The feedback of the output to the input could provide information about the system under study, e.g. the minimum and maximum cut-off voltages.

A capacitor is an energy storage device. It accumulates energy by storing electric charge on two sides of a break in a conductor. The most basic model is that of a parallel plate capacitor. As electric charges (electrons) move from one plate to another, a positive charge, +q, builds up on one plate and a negative charge, −q, builds up on the other. In the initial stage of charging, electrons move freely from the positive plate to the negative plate. As time passes, the electrons that accumulate on the negative plate repel the incoming electrons. These later electrons encounter even more repulsion. Thus, as the time continues, the potential across the plates rises. Now, consider what happens when the direction of the applied current is reversed. The electrons stored on the capacitor flow through the external circuit and this process continues until the capacitor is fully discharged and the voltage across the plates of the capacitor drops to zero.

Electrochemical energy storage devices

Historical background of electrochemical energy storage

Batteries are by far the most common form of energy storage devices. The ancient Persians had probably known about electricity before it was officially discovered.57 The “Parthian Battery”, which was found in 1936 during archaeological excavations near Baghdad, appeared to be a prehistoric battery that dates back to the Parthian empire (247 B.C.–224 A.D.).58 The battery consisted of a clay jar filled with a vinegar solution into which an iron rod surrounded by a cylinder made of a rolled copper sheet was inserted. The jar might have been used to generate electricity (1.1 to 2.0 V) for the purpose of silver and gold plating. The history of modern batteries can be traced back to around 1800 when Alessandro Volta developed the so-called voltaic pile, consisting of alternating discs of zinc and copper separated by cardboard.59 The Volta cell evolved into the two-electrolyte Daniel cell in 1836, and then into the Leclanche cell in 1866 which used a zinc anode and a carbon cathode.60 The next major milestone was the development of rechargeable (secondary) batteries which began with the invention of the lead–acid battery in 1859 and then evolved over the years through nickel–cadmium (1899) and nickel–metal hydride (patented in 1986) to Li-ion batteries59 (announced in 1972 by Whittingham; turned into a breakthrough in energy storage by Goodenough in the 1980s; and commercialized in the 1990s).

The beginning of capacitor technology is generally attributed to the invention of the Leyden jar in 1745.7 Next, paper capacitors, made by sandwiching a strip of wax-impregnated or metalized paper between two metal foils and rolled up tightly into a cylinder, were invented in 1876.61 Aluminum and tantalum are the so-called “valve metals” that develop a stable passivating oxide layer (Al2O3 and Ta2O5) when immersed in an appropriate electrolyte.62 Valve metals are able to conduct electricity in one direction, but impede the current flow in the opposite direction as a result of the formation of oxide dielectrics. On account of this property, the first electrolytic capacitor, based on a high-purity etched aluminum foil with aluminum oxide as a dielectric, was patented in 1896.62 Other notable capacitors include the mica dielectric capacitor invented in 1909, the polyethylene terephthalate based capacitor patented in 1941, and plastic dielectric capacitors developed beginning in 1959. The first patent describing the concept of electric double-layer charge storage was filed by General Electric in 1957 using porous carbon coated on a metallic current collector in a sulfuric acid solution. However, the device was highly impractical and never commercialized.61 The first commercial double layer capacitor (1.0 F, 5.5 V) was introduced in 1971 by Nippon Electric Corporation based on a patent filed by Standard Oil of Ohio.3 This can be considered as the starting point for the development and use of electrochemical capacitors in commercial devices. It was NEC, Inc. (Tokyo, Japan) that coined the term “supercapacitor” for the first commercially successful EDLC, and Pinnacle Research Institute (USA) that termed the device “ultracapacitor”,63 although currently electrochemical capacitors are generally referred to as supercapacitors or ultracapacitors (Note: within this review, the technology will be generally referred to by the term “electrochemical capacitors”, but we also use the term “supercapacitor” for the purpose of being consistent with the majority of the current literature). The concept of pseudocapacitance was first reported by Trasatti and Buzzanca in 1971,64 which opened up a new world of opportunities to enhance the specific capacitance of different systems. The first planar interdigitated micro-supercapacitor was reported in 2003 with polypyrrole electrodes.65 After the first micro-supercapacitor report, more and more innovative strategies,66 such as lithographic microfabrication,67 inkjet printing,68 layer-by-layer assembly,69 laser scribing,70,71 and screen printing,72 were developed. Despite many revolutionary developments from the Volta pile to lithium-ion batteries as well as advanced capacitive energy storage devices, each of the individual technologies still lags behind the demands of industry. A combination of the high energy of batteries and the high power and quick charging of supercapacitors offers opportunities for improved properties with respect to the individual components. Thus, many battery–supercapacitor hybrid devices have been developed during the past two decades.73–76 Later on, high-tech energy storage devices with more advanced functions and properties, such as being flexible, bendable, stretchable, foldable and wearable, were fabricated.77 This continuous research and development on battery-type and supercapacitive materials has led to today's high-performance energy storage devices. However, before getting down to the details of feature identification, a deeper understanding of the physico-chemical nature of the charge storage processes and the type of the electrode active material will help us steer a straight course and select the proper criteria for assessment.

Classification of electrochemical energy storage systems

Electrochemical energy storage systems are currently classified into three main categories: (1) batteries (including primary and secondary batteries), (2) supercapacitors, and (3) hybrid systems. Each energy storage cell/device contains a positive electrode, a negative electrode, an electrolyte, and a separator as well as current collectors and a casing (Fig. 7). Electrodes are active components of the energy storage system and are used for loading the active materials. They allow electrons to pass from one half-cell through an external circuit to the other. The electrolyte is a chemically stable liquid, gel or solid substance that provides conduction for the ions in the cell. In modern supercapacitors, the inert electrolytes can be replaced with redox electrolytes; either redox-active electrolytes which are inherently able to undergo Faradaic electron transfer reactions or redox-additive electrolytes in which redox active species are included to enable Faradaic electron transfer reactions at the electrode/electrolyte interfaces.78,79 A separator is a permeable membrane placed between the positive and negative terminals of the cell. The separator prevents instant self-discharge and electrical short circuits between the two electrodes, while allowing for the transport of ionic conductors that are essential for closing the circuit during the flow of current in the cell. However, by the use of either solid-state, polymer or gel electrolytes, the separator is no longer needed, which is considered to be an additional improvement in energy storage technology. Current collectors are used for the efficient transfer of electrons from the electrodes to the terminals of the battery. However, the development of highly conductive and freestanding electrode active materials, such as well-interconnected carbon nanotubes, graphene or 2D metal carbides with metallic conductivity, can eliminate the need for current collectors.15 The casing provides mechanical stability, protects the electrolyte from evaporation, and prevents negative influences from outside of the system.

In many papers in the field of energy storage, the negative electrode is called the anode, while the positive electrode is called the cathode. Such a notation is probably an adaptation from primary batteries that can only be discharged, for which the anode is always the negative electrode and cathode is the positive one. A secondary cell, for example, a rechargeable battery, is a cell in which the chemical reactions are reversible. When a secondary cell, such as a rechargeable battery or a supercapacitor, is being charged, the anode becomes the positive terminal and the cathode the negative terminal. Thus, by calling an electrode the “anode”, it is impossible to know if it is the positive terminal or the negative terminal of the system. Note that the positive terminal always remains the positive end, and the negative terminal always remains the negative end. It is the anode/cathode designations that switch depending on whether the cell/device is charging or discharging. During the charging process, the negative pole of the cell/device is the cathode. During the discharge process, the situation reverses and the negative pole of the cell/device is the anode. To avoid misleading the readers, we use positive and negative notation throughout this article.

Batteries

Batteries are one of the energy storage devices that convert the chemical energy stored in their active electrode materials directly into electrical energy through electrochemical redox reactions or charge-transfer reactions. The reaction at the negative electrode gives up electrons to the external circuit and the reaction at the positive electrode accepts them. In a cell, the electrolyte provides a medium for the transfer of charge, as ions, between the positive and negative electrodes.
Classification of batteries. There are two fundamental types of batteries: (i) primary cells, commonly referred to as non-rechargeable batteries, and (ii) secondary cells, also known as rechargeable batteries.80 Within this classification, other sub-genres are also used to better classify batteries based on particular structures or designs.81

(a) Stationary vs. flow batteries

Conventional batteries employ non-flowing stationary electrolytes, whereas flow batteries contain two electrolyte solutions in two separate reservoirs that flow through or along the stationary electrodes. It should be noted that in stationary batteries, the charge is stored on solid electrode active materials, whereas in flow batteries the charge is stored on redox moieties dissolved in solution phases. The most common flow batteries are Zn–Br2 and V2+/V3+–V4+/V5+ batteries. One of the most advantageous properties of flow batteries is that they can be almost instantly recharged by simply replacing the electrolyte and recovering the spent material for re-energizing.

(b) Dissolution/deposition- vs. intercalation/de-intercalation-based batteries

Dissolution/deposition-based batteries store energy as a result of chemical reactions occurring inside the cell. The basic mechanism of intercalation/de-intercalation-based batteries is that charged ions (e.g. Li+ or Na+) are shuttled back and forth between the positive and the negative electrodes during charge and discharge processes.

(c) Closed vs. open batteries

A closed battery contains all of the active materials within the cell and the battery is sealed forming a closed system, whereas in an open battery the active material (e.g. oxygen) is not part of the electrodes and is provided from the atmosphere.

(d) Aqueous vs. non-aqueous batteries

The most commonly used aqueous batteries are lead–acid, nickel–cadmium, and nickel–metal hydride batteries that use a water-salt solution as an electrolyte. Non-aqueous batteries use polar-aprotic electrolyte solutions that have no reactive protons or hydrogen atoms. Most Li-ion batteries use lithium salts such as LiPF6, LiAsF6, LiN(SO2CF3)2, or LiSO3CF3 dissolved in solvents such as acetonitrile, ethylene carbonate, diethyl carbonate, propylene carbonate, 1,2-dimethoxyethane, DMSO, THF, or a binary or ternary combination of these to form the electrolyte and are classified as non-aqueous batteries. Non-aqueous electrolytes may be further divided into organic electrolytes, polymeric electrolytes, ionic liquid electrolytes, and solid-state electrolytes.

(e) Solid electrolyte (dry) vs. liquid electrolyte (wet) batteries

Most batteries utilize a liquid as the electrolyte between the two electrodes to regulate the flow of current. However, in some cases, e.g. Li-ion batteries, the liquid electrolyte can lead to safety hazards. Thus, all-solid-state batteries are being developed that have both solid electrodes and solid electrolytes. Solid electrolytes are very good bulk ion conductors but are essentially insulating toward electrons. Examples of such materials include Ag4RbI5 for Ag+ conduction and LiI/Al2O3 mixtures for Li+ conduction. All solid-state batteries can be stacked in a bipolar configuration for better space savings. However, solid-state batteries typically suffer from higher ion-transfer resistance at the electrode–electrolyte interface.

(f) Acidic vs. alkaline batteries

Aqueous batteries can also be classified based on the type of electrolyte used in their construction. There are three common classifications: (i) acidic batteries, such as lead–acid batteries, (ii) near neutral batteries, such as zinc-air batteries, and (iii) alkaline batteries, such as nickel–cadmium and zinc–manganese dioxide batteries.

Advanced electrode materials for batteries. As indicated earlier, charge storage in batteries occurs through reversible Faradaic redox reactions throughout the bulk of the electrode active material(s). The amount of electrical energy that a battery delivers is a function of the cell potential that follows certain well-established physico-chemical (thermodynamic and kinetic) principles as explained before. A battery is composed of a negative electrode, a positive electrode, and an electrolyte that allows for ionic conductivity, along with a separator. Thus far, a variety of metal-based species and conductive polymers have been studied to realize advanced technologies in various types of non-lithium ion batteries.87–93 However, Li-ion batteries are considered the best among all battery types because of their superior performance across multiple characteristics. Generally, Li-ion batteries, as state-of-the-art batteries, are based on intercalation and extraction of Li ions for both positive and negative electrodes. When a Li-ion battery is charging, the positive electrode active material gives up some of its Li ions, which flow through the electrolyte to the negative electrode and remain there, thus the energy is stored during this process in the battery. The reverse processes occur when the battery is discharging. The chemical potential of Li in the two electrodes drives Li ions to diffuse out of the negative electrode, thus generating a flow of Li ions from the negative electrode to the positive electrode through the electrolyte. At the same time, to keep the electrodes electrically neutral, the electrons move through an external circuit and travel toward the positive electrode. So, the flow of ions through the electrolyte and the movement of electrons via the external circuit are interconnected processes, and if either stops so does the other. The energy that a battery provides depends on the charge storage capability (Li-ion release and uptake) of the electrode active materials and the redox reactions that take place at the two electrodes.94 Thus, the active material must be designed to allow the easy intercalation/de-intercalation of the reacting species throughout the material.

A number of nice reviews addressing advancements from synthesis to development of the battery electrode active materials have been published.95–98 The positive pole of the Li-ion batteries are typically made of LiMO2 (M = Co, Ni, Mn),99,100 LiMn2O4,101 LiMPO4 (M = Fe, Co, Ni, Mn).102,103 However, the major limitations of LiCoO2 are high cost and capacity fading during deep cycling.96 The main limitation of LiNiO2 is its thermal instability that Ni3+ is readily reduced at high temperatures. LiMnO2 suffers from low cycling performance, because of the conversion of the layered structure of LiMnO2 into a spinel structure during the Li-ion de-intercalation, as well as the leaching of Mn out of the LiMnO2 structure during cycling.96 The major weakness of LiMPO4 compounds is their poor electrical and ionic conductivity or low energy and power density.96 To circumvent these limitations, the pure phases of the electrode active materials are doped with other elements to further improve their energy storage performance. As such, many advanced electrode active materials including LiNi0.5Mn0.5O2,104 LiNi0.5Mn1.5O4,105 LiNixCoyMnzO2 (x + y + z = 1),106,107 LiNi0.5Co0.5PO4,108 Li3V2(PO4)3,109,110 and LiMnxFeyCozPO4 (x + y + z = 1)111,112 are being developed. Mechanisms of charge storage in supercapacitors

As indicated before, the charge/discharge processes in supercapacitive active materials are fundamentally different from those in batteries. Charge storage processes in supercapacitors are, ideally, 2-D and proceed via two main mechanisms: (i) accumulation of static charge within the double layer (that results in EDLC), and (ii) charge storage based on Faradaic redox reactions on the electrode surfaces.125,126

Electrical double layer capacitance

The energy storage mechanism in EDLCs is based on the electrostatic adsorption of the electrolyte ions on the surface of the electrode active materials. Thus, the key to the high capacitance of EDLCs is in using a highly accessible surface area and electronically conducting electrode materials. Nanostructured carbon-based materials with a high specific surface area and good electrical conductivity are the materials of choice for EDLCs. Carbon-based materials3,127,128 including graphene derivatives,34,129–134 activated carbon,128,135 carbon nanotubes,136–138 carbon nanohorns,139–142 carbon nanocoils,143,144 carbon onions,145,146 carbon fibers,147–149 carbon fabrics,150–152 carbon cloth,153,154 carbon aerogels,128,155,156 carbide-derived carbons,67,157,158 zeolite-templated carbons,159,160 and porous carbons161–164 with electrical conductivity, electrochemical stability, and highly accessible surface area as well as low cost satisfy all the requirements for this application. The double layer capacitance of the carbon-based materials is governed by their structure, especially the porosity and specific surface area.

Simon and Gogotsi experimentally investigated the relationship between the capacitance and the pore size in solvent-free ionic liquid electrolytes.165,166 Their studies reveal that the maximum capacitance can be achieved when the pore size optimally matches the ion size. Generally, EDLCs offer great power performance and cycle life and are characterized by nearly rectangular CVs and triangular GCD profiles. The rectangular-shaped CV for EDLCs stems from the fundamental nature of the double-layer charge storage mechanism. In EDLCs, adsorption and desorption of ions at the electrode–electrolyte interface, in the absence of diffusion limitations, is almost instantaneous, so that dV/dt is a constant.15 The first and simplest EDL model is the Helmholtz model. Based on this model, under an applied cell voltage, an electrode immersed in an electrolyte attracts counter-ions, while repelling co-ions. Thus, two compact layers of charges form at the electrode–electrolyte interfaces, which are called the “electric double layer”. The double layer capacitance, Cdl, at each electrode–electrolyte interface resembles that of a conventional parallel plate capacitor, eqn (32). In contrast to the Helmholtz assumption, the charged ions are mobile in the electrolyte solutions and are under the influence of both diffusion and electrostatic forces. Consequently, the Gouy–Chapman model was developed, in which the ions are treated as point charges and the concentration of the ions in the solution near the surface follows a Boltzmann distribution. Although this makes a better approach to reality, the Gouy–Chapman model fails for highly charged double layers.178 The most sophisticated post-Helmholtz EDL model is the Gouy–Chapman–Stern model, which combines the Helmholtz and the Gouy–Chapman ideas (Fig. 15). On this account, the Gouy–Chapman–Stern model more accurately describes the detailed structure of the electric double layer.179 Based on the Gouy–Chapman–Stern model, the EDL has, in fact, a multi-layer structure, including the inner Helmholtz plane (IHP), outer Helmholtz plane (OHP), and a diffuse layer that is next to the bulk electrolyte solution. Pseudocapacitance

In spite of the outstanding improvements in properties of carbon-based materials, their limited energy generally restricts EDLC-based supercapacitors to applications requiring power delivery over only a few seconds at a time. Consequently, many research efforts are focused on designing other electrode active materials to overcome the challenge of the low energy of supercapacitors. Supercapacitors with higher energy densities can be achieved by using pseudocapacitive materials. RuO2 was the first material to demonstrate pseudocapacitance.64 RuO2 has a wide potential window (1.2 V), high proton conductivity, good thermal stability, and metallic-type conductivity. Hybrid energy storage systems

An ideal energy storage device is one that exhibits both high energy and high power. In spite of significant improvements in increasing the energy content of supercapacitors, their energy is still low. Hybrid energy storage systems aim to accomplish comparable performance to batteries in energy content, and to maintain the high power characteristic and good cycle life of supercapacitors. A hybrid energy storage system can be achieved by hybridization at either the device or the materials level, which is also known as external and internal hybrids, respectively.75

At the device level, an EDLC and a battery can be integrated in tandem in modules. In addition to an EDLC and a battery, a device level hybrid energy storage system requires power conversion electronics to independently manage the two energy storage devices. Because of these requirements, device level hybrid systems are multicomponent and typically suffer from manufacturing complexity, higher cost, and higher weight or volume. Current research on energy storage aims to accomplish battery level energy and supercapacitor level power and cyclability in a single device. Materials level hybridization is a step forward to achieve something better than the externally hybridized systems. In materials level hybridization, a hybrid system involves one battery-like charge storage process and one capacitive (either EDLC-type or pseudocapacitive) charge storage process (Fig. 21). For example, we have previously reported hybridization of an asymmetric supercapacitor based on activated carbon (which can be used alone as a supercapacitor electrode material) with zinc (which is an electrode active material for batteries) in a parallel combination.76 In the fabricated hybrid energy storage system, activated carbon is the negative electrode of the asymmetric supercapacitor, Zn serves as the negative electrode of the secondary battery, and self-doped polyaniline performs as a shared positive electrode. Since the operating potential window of the supercapacitive material matches that of the battery material, the hybrid system benefits from the high-energy advantages of the battery and the high-power capabilities of the supercapacitor.

Capacitance from redox electrolytes. As already indicated, the main charge storage mechanism to increase the energy content of supercapacitors beyond electric double layer capacitance is the incorporation of solid-state redox-active electrode materials that employ fast Faradaic redox reactions for charge storage (known as pseudocapacitive materials). Inspired from pseudocapacitive charge storage processes, hybrid energy storage systems can utilize liquid-state redox-active electrolytes to supply extra-capacity. Note that redox electrolytes contribute to charge storage via Faradaic non-capacitive processes, thus, are not part of supercapacitors. Redox electrolytes have made substantial progress as a means to boost Faradaic charge storage in hybrid systems, and several excellent reviews on redox electrolytes can be found elsewhere.78,317,318 Redox electrolytes are classified under two main categories; (a) redox-active electrolytes which are inherently able to undergo redox reactions, and (b) redox-additive electrolytes in which redox active species are introduced into the electrolyte to enable fast Faradaic reactions at the electrode/electrolyte interface.78 The utilization of redox electrolytes has several advantages:317 (1) the approach is readily scalable because redox active species are simply dissolved into the existing electrolyte. (2) Synthesis of solid-state electrode active materials, some of which involve complicated multi-step processes, can be avoided. Instead, simple electrodes, such as commercially available carbon-based materials, can be used. (3) High-power performance is maintained because the redox reactions are not limited by the kinetics of solid-state diffusion. (4) The redox potential of the system, consequently, the sloping plateaus of the discharge profile of the device, can be tuned by the design of custom organic redox-active molecules. (5) The dead weight of the electrochemically inert electrolyte of the conventional energy storage systems is utilized as an active component to augment energy storage performance of the device. To date, different classes of redox additives have been introduced including halides (especially iodide and bromide),319,320 ionic liquids,321 transition metal cations (Cu, V, Fe, etc.),78 inorganic and organometallic complexes (such as metal bipyridine complexes, ferro/ferricyanide, etc.),322–324 and organic ring compounds (viologens, dihydroxybenzenes, quinone derivatives, etc.).27,32,79,294 Redox additives should have (i) high stability, (ii) reversible redox reactions, (iii) chemical and electrochemical stability in the investigated voltage window, (iv) compatibility with other cell components, and ideally (v) a standard redox potential close to the oxygen evolution potential for the positive electrode electrolyte and near the hydrogen evolution potential for the negative electrode electrolyte. The latter characteristic shifts the voltage plateau of the hybrid device to a higher voltage value, resulting in a huge increase in energy content of the device.

We have recently introduced another paradigm into the subdomain of Faradaic charge storage based on redox electrolytes, that is catalytic interactions between electrode active materials and solution-phase redox species.27 Thus, charge storage mechanisms can be generally categorized as:

 
qtot = qNon-Faradaic + qFaradaic = (qElectric + qElectrolytic) + (qSurface + qInterface + qSurf.–Interf.interaction)(82)
In this relation, qNon-Faradaic is composed of contributions from electrical charge storage (as in physical capacitors and EDLCs). qFaradaic is composed of contributions from surface reactions of the immobilized active materials (as in batteries and pseudocapacitors), and reactions of redox electrolytes at the interface (as in flow batteries), along with the q provided via the interactions between electrode active materials and solution-phase redox species.

The voltage provided by a battery drops quickly at the beginning of the discharge. After this period, the voltage remains relatively constant (which is also called the discharge voltage plateau) until the battery is near complete exhaustion, then the voltage drops off sharply. Since batteries deliver a relatively constant operating voltage over an extended period of time, it is often difficult to precisely determine the state-of-charge of a battery. Whereas batteries deliver a steady voltage in the vast majority of their whole discharge time (∼95%), the voltage output of a supercapacitor, especially EDLCs, decreases nearly linearly as current flows, thus preventing the use of its full energy spectrum (Fig. 24).

In charge storage based on the EDLC mechanism, the active materials will not be decomposed, thus the device can survive up to a million charge/discharge cycles. At high rates of charge and discharge, where redox reactions cannot proceed to any great extent, EDLC can provide the required energy. Thus, charge storage based on the EDLC mechanism allows the system to be charged and discharged much faster than batteries. In addition, the equivalent series resistance of EDLCs is negligible, allowing them to work at close to 100% efficiency.

Unlike EDLCs, energy storage and delivery in batteries rely on chemical reactions. Redox-active materials are prone to decomposition; thus, the lifetime of the system will be continually diminished. In addition, the kinetics of the redox reactions limit the applicability of the device where high rates of charge and discharge are required. However, at slower rates of charge and discharge, the Faradaic charge storage mechanism provides much more energy for the system compared to that of EDLC based materials.

The energy and power of energy storage devices can be significantly boosted by combining positive and negative electrodes of different chemical natures into a hybrid configuration. In a hybrid energy storage system, battery-like materials, as energy sources, are combined with supercapacitor-like materials, as power sources. The energy density or specific energy demonstrates how long a device can be used and the power density or specific power represents how quickly a device can deliver energy. Integrating the two energy storage technologies into a single device can assure the best of both worlds and is considered as a practical key to unlocking the full benefits of energy storage. In addition, in a hybrid/asymmetric configuration, the positive and negative electrodes operate in different potential ranges, thus the operating potential window of the device can be significantly extended.125

2E and 3E cell setup

Generally, the electrochemical characteristics of electrode active materials are evaluated in either a 2E or a 3E system. A 2E cell setup is composed of a pair of closely spaced electrodes containing the electrode active materials and separated by an ion permeable, but electrically insulating, separator. Electrochemical performance of a 2E cell configuration depends on both the electrodes in a given electrolyte. Depending on the type of the electrode active material on each of the electrodes, supercapacitors are grouped into two major categories known as symmetric and asymmetric supercapacitors31 (Fig. 26). Electrodes in a symmetric device contain the same active materials with the same mass loading (m+ = m−) for both the positive and the negative electrodes. Symmetric supercapacitors are generally made of EDLC-based materials or materials consisting of contributions from both EDLC and Faradaic capacitances. Symmetric supercapacitors based on just Faradaic materials are rarely used since the redox reactions only take place either at the positive voltages or the negative voltages, but not at both.327 Asymmetric supercapacitors bring two different electrode active materials, i.e. one EDLC and one pseudocapacitive, together in the same electrolyte. Supercapacitors with the same type of electrode active material, but different masses of materials (m+ ≠ m−) on each electrode, are also considered as asymmetric supercapacitors (Fig. 26).

As explained earlier, when the time of charging and discharging is the same, the coulombic efficiency of the device is 100%. However, the energy efficiency of the system is not necessarily 100%. This is especially the case in systems that show considerable pseudocapacitive behavior. Coulombic efficiency just looks at the x-axis of a GCD profile, whereas the energy efficiency uses the information that is obtained from both the x- and y-axes. Consequently, the area under the V–q plot that integrates the information of both the axes provides a more precise criterion for the real characterization of a system.

Note also that the quality of the delivered energy is not a state function (that depends only on the starting and ending conditions), instead it is a path function (that depends on the way from the initial to the final value). Thus, although the net values of energy and power of a system are extremely important, the way that the device delivers the energy is also crucial. In other words, two devices may have the same energy, but different performances for specific applications, since the ways of delivering the energy in the two devices are different. This is especially the case for Faradaic supercapacitors and hybrid devices in which the charge/discharge curves deviate considerably from linearity. Consequently, depending on whether a voltage plateau appears at a higher or a lower voltage, performance and real applicability of the system will be different. For example, the performance of the two devices with the same energy content could be different in lighting up a set of blue, green, yellow and red light-emitting diodes (LEDs) each of which is driven within a specific potential range. The device with a high voltage plateau can light up a blue LED for a prolonged time, whereas the one with a low voltage plateau could light up a blue LED for just a moment.

Cell/device notation

There is a large body of literature reporting on electrode active materials for both positive and negative electrodes, and therefore, there are many possible cell configurations. It becomes very cumbersome to indicate the positive and negative electrode active materials each time you would like to discuss a cell or device. As such, a shorthand notation, which electrochemists commonly use to describe electrochemical cells, should be followed. Operation of an energy storage device requires at least a negative electrode, a positive electrode and a separator that serves as a salt-bridge. To implement shorthand notation we move from left to right, starting from the solid material of the negative electrode on the far left, to the solution-phase components of the negative electrode half-cell, then we denote the solution-phase components of the positive electrode half-cell, and finally we indicate the solid materials of the positive electrode on the far right. In this notation, the phases are separated by a single vertical line “|” and the salt bridge or separator is shown by double vertical lines “‖”. Thus, an electrochemical cell may be represented by a negative electrode | a negative electrolyte ‖ a positive electrolyte | a positive electrode. For example, the overall reaction of the Daniell cell, which consists of Zn and Cu, is:
 
Zn(s) + Cu2+(aq) ↔ Zn2+(aq) + Cu(s)(96)
for which the cell notation is: Zn(s)|Zn2+(aq)‖Cu2+(aq)|Cu(s)

For simplicity, researchers in the energy storage community can content themselves with the illustration of just the electrode active materials, i.e. the negative electrode ‖ the positive electrode such as Zn‖Cu, or even more simply Zn//Cu, which seems to be known to the general public for this field of research. Reading this simple and most generic symbolic cell notation we know that the left side of the cell notation, i.e. Zn, implies the negative pole and the right side of the cell notation, i.e. Cu denotes the positive pole of the cell. It should be noted that, by convention, the left-hand electrode in this cell notation is always taken to be the anode, and the electrode written on the right side is therefore always the cathode. Having this point in mind, the cell notation is a shorthand description of galvanic cells, equivalent to the discharge process of any energy storage device.

Measurement of the key parameters

Electrochemical performances of energy storage devices are commonly characterized by cyclic voltammetry, galvanostatic charge/discharge, and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy. These methods are essentially used to measure the three fundamental parameters: current, voltage, and time. All the other parameters including energy, power, capacitance/capacity, equivalent series resistance (ESR), and the operating potential window can be obtained from them. Aside from the above-mentioned parameters, there are some other tests, namely, retention capability, cycling stability, coulombic efficiency, self-discharge, and time constant that provide insights into the performance of an energy storage system. There are still some other parameters that have not yet been well formulated, such as shelf life and temperature effects. Thus, it is essential to establish an improved process for their assessment, to support continuous improvement, and to unify the evaluation tests. However, these two parameters are better suited for field-testing and industry rather than laboratory experiments, thus they are not considered here. Additionally, testing a supercapacitor material (i.e. a 3-electrode cell setup) differs from a supercapacitor device (i.e. a 2E cell configuration), and the differences are clearly indicated here. It should also be noted that although almost all of the three main electrochemical techniques can be used for the measurement of each of the key parameters, each of these techniques has a particular advantage in determining some of these parameters. For example, EIS is better suited for the measurement of ESR, GCD profiles for the determination of capacitance, energy, and power, and CV for the differentiation between capacitor-type and battery-type characteristics. Before going deeper into the measurements of the parameters that are mainly used to show the performance of energy storage devices, we first define exactly what each of these terms means.

The operating potential window/cell voltage

The operating potential window is the maximum potential range that guarantees (i) chemical stability of the electrolyte against anodic and cathodic reactions, and (ii) stability of the electrode active materials against decomposition. A potential window is typically used for electrodes and a voltage window is commonly used for devices.329 The concept of potential/voltage window for batteries and supercapacitors differs slightly. In batteries, the potential/voltage window is defined as the potential range in which the changes of the electrode/device during the charging and discharging processes are reversible, whereas in supercapacitors in addition to being reversible during the charging and discharging processes, the behavior of the electrode/cell must be capacitive (with a rectangular CV or triangular GCD profile).329 Unlike batteries that display a discharge voltage plateau until the battery is nearly completely exhausted, the voltage of a supercapacitor is proportional to the amount of stored electrical charge. The maximum voltage of a supercapacitor is provided when the supercapacitor is in its fully charged state. The decomposition of solvent and irreversible reactions on one of the electrodes (i.e. over-oxidation or over-reduction of the electrode active materials) are among the parameters that restrict the operating potential window of an energy storage system. Let's denote the lower and upper potential limits of the negative electrode as EN1 and EN2, and that of the positive electrode as EP1 and EP2, respectively. The potential windows of the negative and positive electrodes are thus, EN2–EN1 and EP2–EP1, respectively.329

In many cases, especially in supercapacitors, the operating potential window of the positive electrode overlaps with that of the negative electrode, i.e. EN2 > EP1, which means the operating voltage window of the supercapacitor is narrower than the sum of the operating potential windows of the two electrodes. In an extreme case where the operating potential window of one electrode completely covers that of the other electrode, the operating voltage window of the supercapacitor is then restricted by the electrode with the narrower operating potential window. Referring to Fig. 31, this happens when EN1 < EP1 and EN2 > EP2 for a wider operating potential window of the negative electrode or EP1 < EN1 and EP2 > EN2 in the case of a wider operating potential window for the positive electrode. On the other hand, if the operating potential windows of the two electrodes are not connected, i.e. EN2 < EP1, which is commonly the case in batteries, the device would then have a minimum discharging voltage.

Equivalent series resistance

Equivalent series resistance (ESR) is often defined as the sum of the resistances of the bulk electrolyte, contact resistance between the electrode and the current collector, as well as the resistance of the electrode.340 ESR is an important parameter that limits the speed at which a supercapacitor can be charged or discharged, thus a high ESR leads to a low power performance of the supercapacitor.341

Energy and power

Energy and power are the two most important parameters for characterization of electrochemical energy storage devices. Specific energy (W h kg−1) or energy density (W h L−1, or W h cm−2) is the amount of energy stored in an energy storage device per unit mass or volume/area of the electrode active materials or the whole device, respectively. Specific power (W kg−1) or power density (W L−1, or W cm−2) is the amount of power an energy storage device can deliver per unit mass or volume/area of the electrode active materials or the whole device, respectively. The relationship between these two important parameters can be expressed by the so-called Ragone plot, which is a very important plot for performance comparison of different energy storage devices.

Rate capability

Rate capability is defined as the capability of an energy storage device to preserve the capacitance/capacity as the current density or specific current increases.342 A system with fast kinetics of electrode processes (i.e. short diffusion pathway and fast electron/ion transport) exhibits a high rate capability and demonstrates a high power performance.343 Adequate control over the specific surface area and pore size distribution of the electrode active materials is crucial to attain a high rate capability. In a hierarchically structured porous material, the macropores shorten the ion diffusion distances to the interior surfaces, the mesopores provide low resistant pathways for the ions to diffuse through the porous particles, and the micropores are strongly desired to reinforce the electric double layer capacitance.344,345 As a result, three-dimensional hierarchical porous carbon demonstrates a better rate capability along with an enhanced electrochemistry performance compared with single-sized pore carbon-based materials or flat graphene sheets, because of the effectiveness of the processes occurring in a porous structure. Note that although a macroporous structure is beneficial for the high rate capability of the system, the large pore volume that is induced by the macropores reduces the packing density and the volumetric characteristics of the devices.345 Rate capability of batteries is assessed by the C-rate system, which is widely used to scale the charge and discharge current for batteries.123

Similar to the well-stablished C-rate system for batteries, a 60 s charge/discharge time has been recommended for supercapacitors.17,123 This 60 s charge/discharge time, referred to as a discharge rate of 60C, can remedy the inconsistencies that arose from the different test conditions in GCD experiments, i.e., A g−1, A cm−2, A cm−3, etc. In addition, it can be used to identify the proper scan rate in CV tests as ν = potential window/60 V s−1: ν = 1/60 V s−1 (∼16.7 mV s−1) for a 1 V system; ν = 2.3/60 V s−1 (∼38.3 mV s−1) for a 2.3 V one.123

Cycle life/stability test

Generally, the capacitance/capacity of an energy storage device decreases as it continues charging and discharging. There is not a generally accepted definition for cycle life of a device, and it strongly depends on the application. In some cases, cycle life is defined as the number of complete charge–discharge cycles before capacitance/capacity of the device falls below 80% of the beginning-of-life value.264,346,347 The death state of a battery for an electric or hybrid electric vehicle is also defined as 80% of its initial capacity.348 Note that in ordinary applications, we generally recharge the battery after using 70–80% of the full charge capacity, which increases the cycle life of the battery by several fold. Cycle life of batteries are typically limited to a few hundred to a few thousand cycles, due to the lack of stability of materials and reversibility of redox reactions during the consecutive discharge–recharge cycles, whereas supercapacitors can undergo millions of cycles, which makes them experimentally burdensome (if not impossible) to directly measure.123

As an equivalent to cycle life, cycle stability is a measure of how well an energy storage device deals with being consecutively charged and discharged. Cycle stability is commonly reported as a certain percentage of the initial capacitance/capacity that the device preserves after a specified number of cycles. In spite of the fact that redox reactions are involved in the pseudocapacitive charge storage, cycle life of pseudocapacitors far exceeds that of rechargeable batteries. This is because batteries store energy based on redox reactions in the bulk or through the intercalation of ions into the layered structure of active materials via bulk reactions of the electrodes. The intercalation process and the bulk redox reactions, however, are severely limited by the diffusion kinetics of the ions. Redox reaction-induced phase transformations may degrade the layered structure of the electrode active material with cycling, particularly at high rates.349,350 The non-ideal reversibility of redox reactions in electrode active materials is due to slow reaction kinetics, particularly if the reactions are driven at high rates, complicating the achievement of an ultra-long cycle life and a stable performance in batteries.349 In redox supercapacitors, electrochemical redox reactions take place at the surface or near the surface of the electrode active materials, but not through the bulk materials; thus, the negligible destruction of the electrode active materials structure ensures longevity of the redox supercapacitors.73 However, although research studies have shed some light on the main characteristics of the energy storage mechanisms of various systems, their molecular aspects remain mostly unknown. There is, therefore, an explicit need to conduct in situ experiments and simulations that can provide valuable mechanistic information, although conducting these experiments is a problematic task due to the difficulty in obtaining well-defined interfaces in redox supercapacitors.351

During a cycling test, most of the time is consumed at voltages that are not critical for device stability. Therefore, a more demanding test to evaluate the stability of a system is a constant voltage held at the maximum cell voltage known as the voltage-hold test.352 Typically, the voltage hold test takes a shorter time than the cycling test before a clear result can be obtained. In this test, the critical voltage is applied to the cell and by occasional (e.g. every 10 h during the voltage hold procedure) cycling the cell in a potential window that is harmless to the cell, capacity/capacitance of the cell is measured. Performing this test on energy storage devices, especially hybrid systems that utilize solution-phase redox species, gives more information on the long-term stability of the system because the voltage-hold test allocates more time for charged redox probes to diffuse away from the electrode surface and interact with other solution-phase redox species or the opposite electrode. Coulombic/energy/voltage efficiency

Coulombic efficiency (also called charge efficiency) is defined as the ratio of the charge drawn out from an energy storage device/electrode during the discharging step to the amount of charge that is stored in the device/electrode during the charging step. In a typical battery, coulombic efficiency is defined as the ratio of the discharge capacity to the charge capacity in one cycle.353,354 In Li-ion batteries, coulombic efficiency is also defined as the ratio between the Li ions that are stripped from the working electrode and the Li metal that is replated.355 For a system with 100% coulombic efficiency, charging and discharging times are the same in the GCD profile (if of course the rates of charging and discharging are the same).

Energy efficiency is a critical parameter to evaluate the performance of energy storage devices. While coulombic efficiency is deduced from the data along the x-axis of the i–V curve, energy efficiency utilizes the information of both the x- and the y-axes. Energy efficiency is defined as the ratio of electrical energy drawn out from a device to the electrical energy required to put the energy storage device back to the same state of charge. Voltage efficiency is defined as the ratio of discharge voltage to charge voltage of the energy storage system. Self-discharge and leakage current

In an ideal energy storage device, the electrons just flow from the negative pole to the positive pole when the circuit is closed. However, in reality, under an open circuit condition, a slow discharge does occur which is known as self-discharge. This is because of an effective thermodynamic driving force that tends to move the charges back into the equilibrium condition. Often the self-discharge rate in supercapacitors is higher than that in batteries, thus this can have a severe adverse effect on the performance of a supercapacitor. From a practical point of view, it means that replacing, for instance, a vehicle's lead–acid battery with a supercapacitor is currently impractical since self-discharge can result in a supercapacitor having insufficient voltage to start the vehicle. The chemical and physical processes responsible for self-discharge are often complex. However, self-discharge processes are commonly classified under three main categories (Fig. 34):356,357 (i) charge redistribution, (ii) parasitic Faradaic reactions, and (iii) ohmic leakage of current between the electrodes. If one of the self-discharge mechanisms or a combination among them is available, self-discharge takes place.

Movement of ions between the Stern layer and the electrolyte and subsequently across the electrolyte due to movement of charge through the electrode active materials causes self-discharge in supercapacitors. (b) Self-discharge in redox pseudocapacitors, as well as in hybrid systems, is mainly caused by redox shuttling between charged redox species in solution (b, top magnified panel) or via cross-diffusion of charged redox couples between the oppositely charged electrodes (b, bottom magnified panel). Charge redistribution. With many electrode active materials (e.g. porous carbon-based materials as well as pseudocapacitive materials), the outer portion of the electrode active material charges more rapidly than the interior of the materials. As a result, the surface-exposed portion of the electrode active material reaches the desired potential before the interior parts of the material. Inequality in the potential between the interior and exterior surfaces of the electrode active material is a driving force leading to movement of charge through the material to equalize charge propagation, called charge redistribution.356 In a supercapacitive energy storage system, the electrode potential is measured at the outer surface of the electrode active materials. Thus, as the charge moves deeper into the material, the measured voltage at the outer surface drops, i.e. the electrode self-discharges (Fig. 34a). Note that one of the characteristics of self-discharge by charge redistribution is that the self-discharge process will be attenuated by repeated charge/self-discharge cycles since with repeated charging more of the interior parts of the pseudocapacitive materials will be in higher oxidation states and/or more of the pores will be available for the electrolyte.
Parasitic Faradaic reactions. Self-discharge is also one of the critical challenges facing the realization of high-performance hybrid energy storage systems.317 In a hybrid system, solution-phase redox species diffuse across the cell and shuttle electrons between oppositely charged electrodes by exchanging electrons with either the electrode surfaces or other solution-phase redox species (Fig. 34b). Thus, one of the big advantages of a hybrid energy storage system, i.e. the storage of energy with the aid of solution-phase redox species, presents a major challenge for practical applications. Cross-diffusion of redox species also weakens the structure of the double layer. For example, on the positive electrode, the positive charge of the electrode is balanced by electrolyte anions in the double layer. The potential of the positive electrode may be higher than the thermodynamic oxidation potential of species on the electrode surfaces or at the electrode–electrolyte interface. The redox species that undergo oxidation reactions on the positive electrode pass their electrons across the double-layer into the electrode surface, thus, reducing the positive charge on the surface and releasing the anions from the double layer. In self-discharge due to Faradaic reactions, the processes are controlled by either an activation-controlled or a diffusion-controlled self-discharge mechanism. In a self-discharge process based solely on an activation-controlled Faradaic reaction, the reacting species are at high concentrations or are attached to the electrode, thus, transport limitations are removed. The self-discharge profile under this circumstance results in a linear voltage drop as a function of log time after a plateau.358 If the self-discharge profile is governed purely by impurities (e.g. O2, H2O2, Fe2+/Fe3+, etc.) at a nonporous electrode, the voltage would decay with t1/2, to satisfy the semi-infinite, diffusion-controlled Faradaic reaction.359
Ohmic leakage. In an energy storage device (full cell), there is the possibility of a short circuit between the positive and negative electrodes. The self-discharge due to charge transfer through this short circuit is called ohmic leakage. For a self-discharge process resulting from ohmic leakage, a plot of ln voltage versus time would give a linear relationship.359

Operating potential window/cell voltage

The maximum operating potential window is determined by gradually increasing the potential window and recording the voltammograms to reach a potential close to the stability limits of the electrode active materials, or until the polarization trend of the solvent becomes very obvious. Moreover, the stability of the potential window is examined over extended times by the shape and intensity of the CVs. A decrease in intensity of the CVs may indicate that the electrode active materials are dissolving or losing contact with the substrate surfaces such as is seen in polymeric materials upon consecutive cycling. The change in shape of the voltammograms may indicate that the electrode active materials are being decomposed. In addition, gas formation could also block the pores of porous materials, thus shifting the shape of the CVs from a capacitive to a resistive curve. The type of electrolyte, configuration of the cell, and the electrode active materials significantly influence the operating voltage of a cell. An overwhelming advantage of non-aqueous electrolytes over their aqueous counterparts is their much wider stable operating potential window that spans experimentally up to 4 V in ionic liquids (and theoretically operate at up to 6 V).376 To avoid limited ionic conductivity, poor safety and toxicity of organic electrolytes, researchers have adopted another strategy that is moving from a symmetric to an asymmetric configuration. With a proper selection of the electrode active materials for both positive and negative electrodes and fabricating the device in an asymmetric configuration, one can make full use of the complementary potential windows of the electrode materials, thus achieving a maximum potential window in an aqueous cell system.329,377 Two- and three-electrode cell setups provide different information about the operating potential window. A three-electrode cell setup provides information about the electrode active materials (since the reference electrode provides a constant potential).378 To know what the redox behavior of each electrode looks like individually and how they contribute to the overall operating potential window of the cell, three-electrode studies of the electrode active materials are necessary. From a two-electrode cell setup, in which one electrode is assigned as a working electrode and the other electrode serves as both the counter electrode and the reference electrode, only general information about the operating potential window of the cell can be obtained (Fig. 37). However, from a practical point of view, a 2E cell setup is a straightforward way to obtain the practical operating voltage of the cell.

Rate capability

In CV studies, rate capability is a measure of capacity/capacitance as a function of scan rate. Rate capability can be evaluated by plotting the variation of capacitance with increasing scan rate (Fig. 39b and d). As can be seen, the highest specific capacitance is obtained at the lowest scan rate, and the capacitance diminishes with increasing scan rate. A high rate capability demonstrates that most of the surface area and pores of the electrode active materials are accessible to the electrolyte ions even at high scan rates.392 Thus, the large specific surface and suitable porosity are extremely important to attain a high rate capability. However, in practice, the penetration distance of the electrolyte ions inside the electrode active materials diminishes with increasing scan rate, so that the inner portions of the active materials do not play a significant role in the charge storage process at high scan rates, thereby leading to a moderate or poor rate capability.390,393

Galvanostatic charge–discharge measurements

GCD is the most widely used method for analysis and prediction of the energy storage performance of the electrode active materials under practical operating conditions. In a GCD test, a constant current is applied to the electrode and the electrode is charged and discharged between two potential limits, while the change in voltage vs. time is recorded. Analogous to CV plots, supercapacitors, hybrid systems, and batteries demonstrate different responses in a GCD plot. The voltage output of EDLC and some pseudocapacitive materials increases and decreases linearly as current flows, while some other pseudocapacitive materials, as well as hybrid systems, demonstrate nonlinear charge and discharge curves with one or more tiny sloping voltage plateau regions. The GCD profiles of batteries exhibit a long steady voltage plateau derived from common thermodynamic principles. Because of this discrepancy among the GCD profiles of different energy storage devices, each type of material should be handled in a unique way to calculate its characteristic metrics.

Similar to CV for which the contributions from capacitive and Faradaic currents depend on scan rate, the current density of GCD testing affects the contributions from capacitive and Faradaic components to the total capacitance/capacity of the system. At high current densities, only the external surfaces of the active materials contribute to charge/discharge processes. Thus, the response of a hybrid device is dominated by the EDLC component, which results in a considerable decrease in its energy. However, at a sufficiently low current density, both the outer and inner sites of the active material(s) will be accessible to the electrolyte ions, leading to a higher value of the specific capacitance. A GCD test is also conveniently used to study the cycling stability of the device. Note that CV and GCD methods should both give the same result for analysis of an energy storage device, provided that ν for CV and i for GCD are given appropriate values. However, generally, CV analysis of an energy storage device is typically carried out in a shorter time interval than a GCD test. It should be noted that an extremely low current density should be avoided since this induces self-discharge.

Cycling stability

Cycling stability, which is a crucial factor for characterization of an energy storage device, can be readily measured by continuously repeating GCD cycles at a given current density for a few hundred (in batteries) to several thousand (in supercapacitors) cycles.401 The result is often reported as the percent of the initial capacitance/capacity that the device retains after a specified number of cycles. This test can also be performed by recording the GCD cycles at ascending current densities (for tens to thousands of cycles each) that finally return to the initial value (Fig. 43d). The cycle life of a rechargeable energy storage system depends heavily on the operating conditions such as the depth of discharge, current of charge and discharge, and operating temperature. Choosing a standard cycling lifetime value depends entirely on the nature of the energy storage system. Non-Li-ion batteries deliver 500 full charge/discharge cycles, Na- and Li-ion batteries can on average last ∼2000 cycles, for supercapacitors ∼10000 cycles have been proven feasible by the literature, and cycling lifetime of the hybrid system is in between (∼5000).316,402 We recommend this as a standard protocol for consistent assessment of energy storage systems.

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy

EIS is a widely used and non-invasive technique for characterization of a variety of energy storage systems, especially supercapacitors (since batteries are inherently non-linear systems, i.e. they demonstrate a non-linear i–V relationship, and, on the other hand, linearization is inherent to the EIS technique, EIS studies of batteries may be prone to degeneracy).408 EIS testing measures the impedance of a system as a function of frequency at a fixed potential. EIS can be used to measure the capacitive behavior of either an individual electrode or a device comprising two or more electrodes.409 In an EIS measurement, a small amplitude sinusoidal voltage (normally from 5 to 10 mV) is superimposed on a steady-state voltage and the resulting impedance is recorded as a function of the modulation frequency. The read-out signal of an EIS study is conveniently represented as a Bode plot (magnitude and phase response vs. frequency) or a Nyquist plot (imaginary vs. real part of the impedance). Practically, EIS testing can be used to study the impedance, specific capacitance, equivalent series resistance, and charge storage mechanisms for electrode active materials.123 More interestingly, adjusting the frequency makes it possible to act on the different conduction paths in an equivalent circuit. It means that depending on the applied frequency the physio-chemical characteristics of the material can be tuned so that the material behaves like a pure resistor, capacitor, etc.

A rewarding approach to analyzing EIS data is fitting the experimental data to a suitable equivalent circuit model. However, scientists may know little about the processes or mechanisms that are involved in the system. As such, the relative ease with which a set of experimental impedance data can be fit to different equivalent circuits makes the quantitative EIS analysis a challenging task. Thus, scientists should take care so that when they do EIS analysis, the equivalent model and consequently the resulting data make sense. Challenges of normalization

As mentioned before, the charge storage ability of supercapacitors is normalized to different metrics, mainly mass, volume and area. The resulting capacitance, C, is named gravimetric or specific capacitance (F g−1), volumetric capacitance (F cm−3), and areal capacitance (F cm−2), respectively. However, sometimes C is normalized by the weight or volume of the active materials, but at other times by the weight or volume of the whole device. On the other hand, there are two types of area-normalized capacitance: (1) the true area normalized capacitance, and (2) the geometric area normalized capacitance. The true areal capacitance, which is the well-known EDLC, is actually the ratio of the capacitance to the specific surface area of the materials. The true area normalized capacitance is a difficult metric to obtain due to the variability in surface area measurements, especially in the case of porous active materials. Thus, inconsistencies arise when researchers prefer one type of normalization in advance, but use a different one when the time of implementation arrives. Moreover, the reported value for the capacitance strongly depends on the evaluation technique (CV or GCD), charging rate, the maximum operating potential window, as well as the mode of charge and discharge (i.e. constant current or constant voltage), in GCD. The lack of a consistent normalization parameter along with discrepancies among the results of different electrochemical testing methods makes comparisons between different energy storage devices rather challenging. Supercapacitors can deliver more energy than state-of-the-art batteries can release in short timeframes (<10 s). Thus, supercapacitors are high power energy storage devices with virtually unlimited cyclability, but generally suffer from lower energy compared to batteries. Trying to boost the energy of supercapacitors usually comes at the cost of turning high power supercapacitors into low energy batteries. In recent years, some researchers have argued that supercapacitors can approach or even outdistance batteries in energy performance. Such outstanding properties do not necessarily translate into real-world effectiveness, for which Gogotsi and Simon have provided a specific example (Fig. 55).2

Thus, with a huge rise in the number of researchers who have focused on boosting the energy of supercapacitors as well as developing hybrid energy storage devices, the challenge of selecting an illustrative normalization parameter is more urgent than ever.426 The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) model is commonly used to calculate surface areas; however, this method only provides a qualitative measurement of the surface area of porous materials with small pores. Moreover, the entire surface area is not necessarily accessible to the electrolyte under all operational conditions. Gravimetric and volumetric energy/power densities are more common in the literature. However, considering a very porous carbon material, such as a graphene aerogel that has recently gained considerable attention, the gravimetric energy/power (normalized solely to the mass of the active material) would be extremely high. But the electrolyte fills in the pores of the aerogel and considerably increases the total weight of the material. Therefore, the volumetric energy/power density of such a material will be very low. Thus, particular attention should be paid to each normalization parameter. Preferentially, a combination of two or three normalization methods, based on mass/volume/area of both the active material and the whole device should be reported to paint the whole picture of the energy storage device in a more accurate way.

Performance metrics of energy storage devices are obtained from CV, GCD or EIS methods. It is generally accepted that a higher scan rate in CV or a higher current density in GCD results in a lower energy/power density. Zuliani et al. studied the electrochemical performance of two types of activated carbon; one derived from coconut shells and the other from fluid petroleum coke.426 The results show that the rate performance of the two samples differs so that a single correction factor or correlation cannot be used to explain the trend. Thus, to back up claims with facts, the materials/devices should be compared at a comparable scan rate or current density. Moreover, CV and GCD methods operate on fundamentally different mechanisms, thus the metrics derived from each of the methods are only comparable when the sweep rate of the CV curve and current density of the GCD profile are low. Generally, to compare various energy storage devices, it is strongly recommended that the criteria of assessment be limited to similar normalization factors to ensure a reasonable comparison. To provide a more realistic picture of the energy storage device, both the gravimetric and volumetric normalization factors should be presented. The gravimetric and volumetric energy/power not only should be presented based on the mass/volume of the active material(s), but also for the whole package, because all the energy storage devices need supporting components, such as current collectors, binders, separators, electrolytes, and casing.

The mass of active materials in supercapacitors is usually very low. The contribution of EDLC significantly diminishes with increasing mass of the active materials since the EDLC is limited to the accessible surface of the materials. Typically, in a supercapacitor, 100% of the active material contributes to energy storage, whereas in batteries the contribution is significantly lower. In a typical commercial supercapacitor based on activated carbon, the thickness of the electrode is about 100–200 μm or about 10 mg cm−2 of a carbon film. In such a case, the weight of carbon accounts for only 30% of the whole package, thus dividing by a factor of 3 to 4 is needed to extrapolate energy and power of the active material to those of the device. Such an extrapolation is only valid for electrodes with a comparable thickness or loading. Thus, mass normalizing the energy and power in a micro-supercapacitor, a thin film supercapacitor or the one with a negligible mass loading is erroneous compared to the energy normalized by the volume and area of the electrode. Since limited space is a key constraint, volumetric/areal capacitance, energy and power are more meaningful evaluation criteria.

Mechanical flexibility of electrodes and devices

The ever-growing demands for flexible electronics in applications of consumer electronics, activity trackers, implantable/wearable healthcare devices, environmental monitoring and many more require concerted mechanical flexibility in energy storage devices.77,427–430 Significant progress has therefore been made in the fabrication of bendable, stretchable, shape-memory, transparent and self-healing energy storage devices over recent years (Fig. 56).71,430–437 Flexible energy storage devices have been made as 1D fibers,433,438–440 2D films,70,300,428 and 3D foams173,441–443 with ordered porous structures. For applications in portable and wearable electronics, performance stability of the energy storage devices under different mechanical bending, knotting, twisting, compressing, and stretching conditions, as well as harsh environmental situations (including extremely high or low temperature, high humidity levels, exposure to radiation and vibration), is an important performance metric.431 The main purpose of mechanical testing in the energy storage realm is to get a qualitative sense of flexibility and to ensure that products meet or exceed the minimum requirements for modern electrical equipment. For example, a common test of a material's mechanical properties involves fracturing a cylinder of the material. The load on the cylinder is increased gradually until the test specimen breaks or fractures suddenly into two or more pieces. For example, besides a quantitative analysis, Ling et al. reported hollow MXene/PVA cylinders (6 mm in diameter and 10 mm high) made by rolling Ti3C2Tx-based films with a thickness of 4–5 μm that were capable of supporting ∼15000 times their own weight.444 However, a detailed and quantitative description of a specimen's flexibility cannot be obtained by just one such routine test. For a quantitative assessment, the mechanical properties of electrodes/devices must be evaluated using (1) stress–strain curves, (2) monitoring the in situ change in resistance of the system, (3) studying the in situ electrochemical performance of the system under different mechanical deformations, and (4) recording spectroscopic information or microscopic images of the materials at different levels of applied strains.431

Stress–strain curve

The stress–strain test is used to determine mechanical properties of a material, including the tensile, compressive, shear, or torsional strength. An object under tensile stress experiences an increase in length, whereas an object under compressive stress undergoes a decrease in length. If instead of applying a force perpendicular to the surface, we apply pairs of equal, but opposing forces on the two opposite sides of an object, we are applying a shear stress. Torsional stress is the twisting of a straight object that tends to produce rotation about the longitudinal axes of the object. In situ resistance measurements

In situ changes in resistance have also been employed to test the flexibility of electrodes.443,461,462 For example, we reported 3D macroporous laser-scribed graphene (LSG)–MnO2 electrodes and evaluated the foldability of the LSG–MnO2 electrodes by in situ measurements of the electrode resistance under successive bending cycles (Fig. 58).173 The change in resistance of the electrode was negligible up to a bending radius of 5.0 mm and was completely recovered after straightening no matter whether the bending was positive (convex) or negative (concave). Notably, after 1000 cycles of bending and straightening in a concave configuration, with a radius of curvature of 5.0 mm, the resistance was increased by only ∼2.8%, demonstrating excellent mechanical integrity under large mechanical deformation. This is in contrast to MnO2 by itself, which undergoes serious strain-induced performance degradation because of its rigid structure.

If a device has a radius of curvature between zero to twice the thickness of the device, it can be regarded as a foldable device.

In situ electrochemical study

Mechanical tests of the energy storage materials/devices are generally performed by recording electrochemical performances under different mechanical deformations. For example, Zang et al. reported supercapacitor electrodes based on crumpled graphene paper that exhibited extremely high stretchability with a uniaxial strain of ∼300% and a biaxial strain of 200% × 200%, which is equivalent to 800% areal strain (Fig. 59).453 They have demonstrated that the capacitances of the electrodes are insensitive to large deformations of the electrodes. The Hamedi research group introduced a flexible and compressible three-dimensional supercapacitor based on layer-by-layer self-assembly of interdigitated thin films that is fully functional at a bending angle of 90° and compressions up to 75%.442 To demonstrate this flexibility, in situ CV experiments were performed while the device was under operation.

Spectroscopic and microscopic characterization

A few other methods have also been used for studying the mechanical flexibility of materials. For example, Lee et al. estimated the Young's modulus of single- and bilayer graphene by Raman spectroscopy as 2.4 ± 0.4 and 2.0 ± 0.5 TPa, respectively,463 while the Hone research group used atomic force microscopy (AFM) nanoindentation and measured the Young's modulus of a freestanding monolayer of graphene as 1 TPa.464

Conclusions and perspective

Personal electronic devices and transportation technologies are now more dependent on energy storage systems than at any previous time in history, with a strong demand for enhanced energy and power densities. Therefore, a vast variety of advanced electrode active materials for batteries, supercapacitors, and hybrid energy storage systems have been reported. However, when critically evaluating energy storage systems under realistic conditions, it appears that in some cases, the characteristics assigned to an energy storage system (especially those based on the gravimetric performance of the active materials) have been overestimated. This is because of the low mass loadings of the electrode active materials and the dead volume in cells that prevent material-based gravimetric characteristics to be translated into realistic device-level volumetric performances. These common challenges of energy storage systems call for an attempt towards clarifying the importance of a balanced view of the active materials and devices.

In this Review, a comprehensive survey of the basic principles of energy storage systems and the protocols for their evaluation are presented. We have briefly described the physico-chemical principles of conventional capacitors, electrical double-layer capacitors, pseudocapacitors, hybrid energy storage devices, and batteries. In parallel with improvements in advanced engineering and the development of high-performance electrode active materials, a fundamental understanding and further development of the criteria for their proper evaluation are also needed to meet the challenges of new energy storage technologies. In this continuum, from conventional capacitors to supercapacitors to batteries, (super)capacitors are generally exploited to meet high-power demands, while batteries are generally employed to meet high-energy needs. Unlike conventional capacitors, the concomitant Faradaic reactions in pseudocapacitors and hybrid energy storage devices result in nonlinear galvanostatic charge–discharge behavior, and cyclic voltammograms that significantly deviate from the rectangular-shaped voltammograms expected for capacitors. Characterizations of pseudocapacitors and hybrid energy storage devices with metrics of capacitors or electric-double layer supercapacitors sometimes lead to false negative or positive results for the energy content and power performance of the device. We have emphasized here that the metrics for evaluation of energy storage devices vary depending on the type, since each energy storage device sets out specific characteristics with special use cases.

While desirable advancements have been achieved for the evaluation of energy storage devices, drawbacks still exist. For example, when pseudocapacitive materials are used in an energy storage device, the mass loading and thickness of the electrode active materials must be indicated. When the mass loading or thickness of the electrode active material is very low, or the charge–discharge processes are conducted using a very low current density, the energy performance of the material may be erroneously regarded as high, while finding a correction factor for this overestimation may not be easy. In this regard, the combination of computational modeling and experimental investigations should become an effective method for extrapolating and predicting the properties of both materials and devices. We hope that this Review will provide a useful contribution to the energy-storage community as a roadmap for ameliorating the hurdles for evaluating electrochemical energy storage systems.
