Abstract

Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes are exceptional one-dimensional transition metal oxide materials with low density, large surface area, promising electrochemical and photoelectrochemical properties, which are widely investigated in lithium-ion batteries, photoelectrochemical devices, gas sensors, and catalysis. They have drawn significant attention to the fields of energy storage and conversion, and environmental sensing and remediation due to the increase in the global energy crisis and environmental pollution. Many efforts have been made toward controlling the morphology or impurity doping to improve the intrinsic properties of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes. In this review, we introduce the synthesis methods and physicochemical properties of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes. The fabrication conditions, which are important for the physicochemical properties of materials, are also listed to describe the synthesis processes. Furthermore, the development and breakthrough of various applications in batteries, supercapacitors, photoelectrochemical devices, environmental remediation, and sensors are systematically reviewed. Finally, some of the current challenges and future perspectives for Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes are discussed. We believe that this timely and critical mini-review will stimulate extensive studies and attract more attention, further improving the development of the Î±-Fe2O3 (hematite) nanotube structure.

1. Introduction

The rapidly growing demand for clean energy has driven researchers to widely investigate new materials with improved electrochemical/physicochemical properties.1â5 As the key technology for the possibility of fully utilizing visible light, various semiconductor materials have drawn great research interest.6â8 Transition metal oxides have attracted considerable scientific and technological attention, due to their intrinsic catalytic properties, unique crystal structure, and tunable surface morphologies.9 Most of them have been widely used in electrochemical energy conversion and storage, electrochemical sensors, and photocatalytic hydrogen production, which are considered as alternative sustainable renewable energy and environmental technologies.10â12

The Î±-Fe2O3-based nanomaterials display exceptional characteristics,13 including (i) attractive photo(electro)catalytic properties that provide alternatives to precious nanomaterials for numerous energy conversion reactions; (ii) superior energy storage capabilities; (iii) reduced operation cost of industrial processes due to their high earth abundances; (iv) long-term sustainability due their low cost, availability, comparatively low toxicity, wider chemistry, and chemical reactivity. The advantages of Î±-Fe2O3, such as low toxicity, low cost, high availability, and high theoretical specific capacity, make it one of the materials with the greatest potential for energy and environmental applications. In the energy storage field, Î±-Fe2O3 can react with six moles of Li, leading to theoretical gravimetric and volumetric lithium storage capacities of about 1006 mA h gâ1 and 5331 A h Lâ1, respectively, which exceed the theoretical values of graphite in terms of both weight (372 mA h gâ1) and volume (837 A h Lâ1).14 The remarkable electrochemical capacities and superior electrical conductivity make it better for clean and reliable energy storage devices.15 Besides, the bandgap of hematite is only about 2.0â2.2 eV, which ensures that it can absorb over half of the visible light (>600 nm), harvest up to 40% of the incident solar spectrum energy, and is stable in most aqueous solution (pH > 3).16 However, Î±-Fe2O3 nanomaterials experience a large volume change during the charge/discharge process, leading to unstable electrochemical properties.17 The unmatched hole diffusion length and light penetration depth and the poor conductivity are also drawbacks of Î±-Fe2O3 materials, causing the fast recombination of electronâhole pairs inside the semiconductor.18 As such, the structure of the Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes was designed to overcome the above-mentioned issues.

In comparison to the bulk material, various morphologies of Î±-Fe2O3, such as nanowires, nanorods, nanotubes, nanorings, and nanoporous structures,19 have been investigated to enhance the cyclic stability and rate capability for energy storage or catalytic performance for energy conversion. While these nanostructures are attractive for practical applications, the nanotube geometry provides a greater surface area than others for a given diameter, as the tubes possess both inner and outer surfaces. Furthermore, unlike the case of wires, the structural integrity of the tubes can be maintained even if the wall is thinned to a few nanometers. For applications such as photocatalysis and chemical sensing, thin walls help the charge carriers to remain close to the surface and take part in surface reactions.20,21 The vertically aligned nanotube geometry is particularly useful as it could help to improve the optical properties of the material and provide facile pathways for the conduction of charge carriers along the length to the substrate region. Compared to the bulk material, a nanotube array of length 20 Î¼m, pore diameter 100 nm, and wall thickness 20 nm could enhance the surface area by about a thousand times.22 Among the mentioned structures, the nanotube structure could improve the electrochemical performance due to the minimized volume change from hollow structures, shorten the path of photogenerated carriers and increase the efficiency of photoelectrons.23 In particular, highly ordered vertical nanotubes, prepared by facile anodic oxidation, with extremely large specific surface area as compared to dense planar film, have been considered as a promising structure to improve the properties in energy storage, adsorption and desorption of gas molecules, and photodegradation.24,25 Î±-Fe2O3-based nanocomposites have also been doped to enhance the conductivity and stability of materials. Some impurity elements (such as Ti, Si, and C) can be doped into the Î±-Fe2O3 structure, causing great enhancement in the catalytic performance (i.e. photocurrent).26,27

A great number of studies on the synthesis of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes have been reported, including hydrothermal, template-assisted, electrospinning, and electrochemical anodization methods.12,28 Similar to Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, TiO2 nanotubes possess unique chemical and physical properties such as chemical inertness, gas sensitivity, large surface area, biocompatibility, high photocatalytic and electrochemical activities.29,30 The synthesis methods of TiO2 nanotubes can be used for reference and adopted in the synthesis of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes. Among the synthetic methods, hydrothermal synthesis is an easy-operation and highly productive route for preparing the Î±-Fe2O3 nanotube structure. Sacrificial templates (e.g. ZnO) can be used for the preparation of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes because they can be easily removed via chemical etching or combustion, leaving the resultants with a pre-set porosity and reversely duplicated morphology. Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes synthesized by electrospinning have been investigated extensively due to the simple and efficient processes for achieving well-defined one-dimensional nanostructures with designed components.31 Alternatively, vertically ordered Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes can be grown on an iron substrate through direct electrochemical anodization processes.32

Recently, many significant breakthroughs on Î±-Fe2O3 nanotube materials have been made for improving their electrochemical, photoelectrochemical, magnetic, and other properties. The Î±-Fe2O3 nanotube structure, including its synthesis, characterization, and properties, is the focus of this review. We summarize the fabrication methods for Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes and then review their properties in structure, composition, electrochemistry, photocatalysis, and magnetism. We also discuss their energy and environment-related applications, including Li-ion batteries, solar-light-driven water splitting, supercapacitors, water treatment, and sensors, from the aspects of function-oriented structure design and control.

2.2 Electrospinning method

The electrospinning method is suitable for the construction of a one-dimensional nanostructure. In the electrospinning process, typical precursor solutions (e.g. iron salt and polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP)/polyethylene oxide (PEO)) are pumped through a nozzle with a high applied voltage relative to grounded aluminum foil (acts as a collector) to form an electrically charged jet of solution.45 For the fabrication of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, the product obtained after the electrospinning process has to be annealed at a high temperature. Eid et al. fabricated Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes derived from PVP/iron acetate nanofibers by the electrospinning method through annealing in the air.46 Zhang et al. discussed the effect of the spinning solution composition on the Î±-Fe2O3 morphology, including nanotubes, nanorods, and nanobelts, by changing the precursor weight ratio between Fe(NO3)3 and PVP.47 An experiment scheme for the process with the corresponding expected nanostructures by the electrospinning method is shown in Fig. 7. Liu et al. synthesized original and La-doped Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes by electrospinning with Fe(NO3), La(NO3)3, N-dimethylformamide, and ethanol as the precursors.48 To obtain the highly crystalline Î±-Fe2O3 nanomaterial, the as-collected electrospun nanofibers were dried to evaporate the residual solvent, and calcined at high temperature (higher than 500 Â°C). The as-synthesized material has excellent electronic and gas sensing properties, providing helpful direction on synthesizing the controllable Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes. However, the yields by the electrospinning method are very limited. There are still many problems that need to be solved in the large-scale preparation of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes by the electrospinning method.

2.3 Templating method

The templating method is also applied as a useful route for preparing one-dimensional nanostructures. Guo et al. used ZnO nanowire arrays as the sacrificial template for the synthesis of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotube arrays.49 The ZnO template, fabricated by thermal evaporation on different substrates (including sapphire wafer, Si wafer, ITO/glass), was immersed in a dilute FeCl3 solution to form the ZnFe2O4 nanotubes. Lin et al. further synthesized Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes on the nickel foam substrate via chemical transformation of the ZnO nanowire template to obtain a conductive additive-free and binderless electrode for supercapacitors.50 For the templating method, the residual element from the template is an issue that can be easily ignored, which may affect the properties of the Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, especially the catalytic performance. Other disadvantages include the complicated fabrication process and the destruction of tubes that may occur during the fabrication process.

Interestingly, Chen et al. reported porous Fe2O3 nanotubes with controllable pore structure and phase junction by molecular layer deposition (MLD) and atomic layer deposition (ALD) approaches using carbon nanofibers (CNFs) as a template.51 MLD and ALD have been considered to be appealing technologies for constructing nanocoating on various substrates via a bottom-up approach with molecular or atomic level control, due to their self-limiting nature, excellent conformality, chemical selectivity and low reaction temperature.52â54 Iron tert-butoxide and EG were used as the precursors for MLD. Two novel MLD procedures were designed to prepare porous Fe2O3 nanotubes with tunable pore structure and phase junction. The organic fractions of the obtained Fe-hybrid MLD films act as soft templates to generate nanopores in nanotube walls and also play a key role in the formation of the phase-junction. Under annealing, the Fe-hybrid films/CNFs can convert into porous Fe2O3 nanotubes with controllable pore size and Î±âÎ³ phase junction. Azevedo et al. reported the synthesis of double-walled Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes using porous anodic alumina templates via selective chemical etching and the Kirkendall process.55 The approach is based on the electrodeposition of metallic iron nanowires in porous alumina templates, followed by a selective chemical etching, nanoscale Kirkendall effect, fast oxidation and out-diffusion of the metallic core structure during thermal annealing.

2.4 Hydrothermal method

Due to its simplicity and high yield, the hydrothermal process is commonly used for the crystal formation and the growth of the metal oxide nanotube structure.56 Fu et al. coated Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes on the micro-fiber interferometer by a simple and reliable hydrothermal method.57 FeCl3 and NH4H2PO4 were used as the precursors and were placed in an autoclave for heating at 220 Â°C for 60 h. Apart from the pure Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, the hydrothermal method is usually applied for the synthesis of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotube-based composites. For example, Wang et al. proposed a facile one-step hydrothermal method for the synthesis of a three-dimensional (3D) graphene aerogel embedded with hollow Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, which presents as a high-performance anode for the lithium-ion batteries (LIBs).58 Furthermore, Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes are usually used as the substrate to support metal oxides or quantum dots. Jiao et al. successfully fabricated the Au/Fe2O3 hollow nanotube arrays on the fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) glass surface by using a polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) reactor and a stainless-steel autoclave.59 The NaNO3, FeCl3, and NaF were dissolved as the precursors. The crystal structure of the product changed from iron oxyhydroxides to Î±-Fe2O3 after annealing at 550 Â°C for 2 h. After that, Au nanoparticles were grown on the Î±-Fe2O3 nanotube array surface by the photoreduction process. Similarly, Duan et al. developed a novel and efficient in situ epitaxial growth strategy to fabricate hetero-nanostructures of Ag3PO4 quantum dots on the external surface of hematite nanotubes (via hydrothermal method),60 by intentionally employing chemically adsorbed phosphate anions on the surface of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes to control the reaction kinetics of phosphate anions and Ag+ ions in aqueous solution. Chen et al. further reported that single-crystalline nanotubes of Î±-Fe2O3 can be fabricated through the hydrolysis of FeCl3 in the presence of phosphate (at 220 Â°C), developed from Ostwald ripening of nanocrystallites in spindle- and nanodisk-shaped polycrystalline particles.61 However, for the hydrothermal synthesis of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, some drawbacks should be taken into consideration, including non-uniformity, short length, and long preparation time. For the synthesis of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes on the metal/carbon substrate to form a 3D electrode, the adhesion strength of the material prepared by the hydrothermal method is lower than that by electrochemical anodization.

3. Physicochemical properties of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes

The physicochemical properties of the Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, including crystal structure, surface energy, surface charge, morphology, and chemical composition, play a vital role in their individual characteristic properties. Reckoning with these facts, in this review, an attempt has been made to analyze the correlation of these physicochemical properties with the specific properties. In other words, it is also an efficient way to improve the properties of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes by controlling the morphology with the size or crystal structure. With high surface area, Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes show remarkable specific capacitance and charge/discharge rate, which provides smooth and short superhighways to ions and electrons. It is reported that the electrodes made by Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes exhibit a much higher reversible capacity than that of nanorods.15 The improved lithium storage performance of the nanotubes can be attributed to the unique tubular structure, which offers enough room for the volume variation during lithiation/delithiation processes and shortens the pathways of lithium transportation. 4.4 Application in water treatment and environmental remediation

In the heterogeneous photocatalytic process, Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes can be excited by light irradiation, which promotes electrons from the valence to the conduction band and triggers a series of oxidationâreduction reactions involving the excited electrons and generated holes. However, the poor electron mobility and catalytic efficiency of the Î±-Fe2O3 nanotube nanostructure remain to be further improved.

In terms of increasing the intrinsic photocatalytic activity of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, Chen et al. reported porous hematite nanotubes with Î±âÎ³ phase junction for enhanced charge separation and photocatalytic properties.51 The well-defined nanotube-like morphology presents a wall thickness of about 20 nm. Taking the photodegradation of Rhodamine B (RhB) as a model reaction, the porous Î±-Î³ Fe2O3 nanotubes heterostructure produced by the two-step MLD exhibited the highest photocatalytic activity, which was more than a 6.5-fold and 20-fold enhancement as compared with the nonporous pure Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes and commercial Î±-Fe2O3 nanoparticles, respectively.

5. Conclusions and perspectives

The development of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes materials with unique properties and architectures has led to great advances in the energy and environmental field. This minireview has highlighted fundamental and experimental progress for the preparation of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes toward several state-of-the-art applications including electrochemical energy storage, photoelectrochemical energy conversion, and environmental remediation and monitoring. Intensive researches have greatly deepened our mechanistic understanding of the electrochemical/PEC/sensing behavior of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, which serves as a useful guideline for the design of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotube-based materials with high performances.

We highlight several remaining challenges in the fundamental studies relevant to the Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes that need to be carefully addressed in the future. The first challenge is how to simplify the technology for the large-scale production in the industry with extending practical operability and how to precisely and efficiently control the nanotube geometry by varying the operational parameters (e.g. anodic potential) to obtain optimized properties. Secondly, since the modification is key for improving the performances of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, we need to explore more methods for modification and take full advantage of the self-organized nanostructure. The third challenge is to understand the underlying mechanisms and pathways of the electrochemical/PEC reaction on the Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, where the primary task is to identify the surface-adsorbed species, intermediates, and active sites, and further guide the structural design of advanced materials. Furthermore, the stability of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotube-based electrodes is uniquely challenging (especially in acid media), not just for electrochemical energy conversion and storage but also for light-absorbing applications.

We propose the following research opportunities to potentially address the above issues and challenges in this area, resulting in further performance improvements and ultimately widespread applications of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes:

(1) The synthesis procedures are expected to be simplified and engineered for large-scale and low-cost production. Even though significant progress in the shape-controlled synthesis of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes has been achieved in the last decade, demands for even higher performance and lower costs call for the urgent development of advanced synthetic approaches that enable rational and precise structural tuning. One of the big research opportunities is to scale the yield of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes from the current laboratory level to the industrial level.

(2) Modification and nanocomposite design strategies need to be further investigated for increasing the performance of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes. Except for the conventional methods, ALD appears to be an option to homogenously and precisely coat the Î±-Fe2O3 nanotube layers from the bottom to the tube mouth, resulting in many advanced functionalities of the newly prepared nanotube layers. Nevertheless, further optimization of the ALD process toward coatings and inner fillings is required.

(3) Recently, single-atom catalysts with atomically dispersed metals on solid supports represent the ultimate limit of nanoscale catalysts that bridge the gap between heterogeneous catalysts and homogeneous catalysts to deliver the combined merits of both. In this regard, Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes, with high surface area and abundant anchoring sites, could be utilized as a potential substrate to support the single atoms, while the metalâsupport interactions and coordination environment may be systematically tuned.

(4) It is highly desirable to develop advanced characterization techniques that enable the direct in situ probing of reaction intermediates along with advanced DFT calculations and facile approaches for discovery and breakthrough in Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes. Further development of the computationally-assisted design and preparation of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes is urgently needed for the prediction of the structureâactivity relationship and the reduction of the synthesis attempts. In situ characterization techniques, such as in situ Raman, XPS, XAS, SEM, and TEM, would be very helpful for understanding the growth of Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes and reaction mechanism.

(5) Through self-assembling inorganic, organic, metallic, and magnetic nanoparticles into or onto the Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes as composites with a broad spectral response to visible light, high quantum efficiency, and stabilizing properties, applications could be widened. For example, Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes with low toxicity have great potential for development in biomedical directions, especially in photodynamic therapy. Besides the unique light-absorbing and response properties, Î±-Fe2O3 nanotubes could also be separated by a magnetic field.

(6) It is necessary to design and construct Î±-Fe2O3 nanotube-based materials that can maintain the initial activity/performance under real practical operations. Several available strategies are proposed to address this problem, such as the introduction of carbon or polymer coatings, inert metal oxide coating, atomic ordering, and elemental doping.
