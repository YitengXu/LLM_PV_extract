Abstract

Bismuthene has opened up a new avenue in the field of nanotechnology because of its spectacular electronic and thermoelectric features. The strong spinâorbit-coupling enables its operation as the largest nontrivial bandgap topological insulator and quantum spin hall material at room temperature, which is unlikely for any other 2D material. It is also known to be the most promising thermoelectric material due to its remarkable thermoelectric properties, including a substantially high power factor. However, an in-depth understanding of the mechanical and thermal transport properties of bismuthene is crucial for its practical implementation and efficient operation. Employing the StillingerâWeber potential, we utilized molecular dynamics simulations to inspect the mechanical strength and thermal conductivity of the monolayer Î²-bismuthene for the first time. We analyzed the effect of temperature on the tensile mechanical properties along the armchair and zigzag directions of bismuthene nanosheets and found that increasing temperature causes a significant deterioration in these properties. The material shows superior fracture resistance with zigzag loading, whereas the armchair direction exhibits an improved elasticity. Next, we showed that increasing vacancy concentration and crack length notably reduce the fracture stress and strain of Î²-bismuthene. Under all these conditions, Î²-bismuthene showed a strong chirality effect under tensile loading. We also explored the fracture phenomena of a pre-cracked Î²-bismuthene, which reveal that the armchair-directed crack possesses a higher fracture resistance than the zigzag-directed crack. Interestingly, branching phenomena occurred during crack propagation for the armchair crack; meanwhile, the crack propagates perpendicular to loading for the zigzag crack. Afterward, we investigated the effect of loading rate on the fracture properties of bismuthene along the armchair and zigzag directions. Finally, we calculated the thermal conductivity of bismuthene under the influence of temperature and vacancy and recorded a substantial decrement in thermal conductivity with increasing temperature and vacancy. The obtained results are comprehensively discussed in the light of phonon density of states, phonon dispersion spectrum, and phonon group velocities. It is also disclosed that the thermal conductivity of Î²-bismuthene is considerably lower than that of other analogous honeycomb structures. This study can add a new dimension to the successful realization of bismuthene in future (opto)electronic, spintronic, and thermoelectric devices.

1. Introduction

Two-dimensional (2D) nanostructures have triggered a great deal of attention both in academia and industry owing to their astounding dimension-tunable thermophysical1 and chemical2 properties, rendering them an excellent harbinger for high-performance nanoelectromechanical systems (NEMSs), (opto)electronics, catalysis, and energy storage devices.3 The fairly large variation in intra- and interlayer bonding enables 2D nanomaterials to exhibit extremely anisotropic mechanical and thermo-electrical behaviors including high optical and UV adsorption, mechanical flexibility, superior strength, superconductivity as well as high carrier mobility.2,4

The successful synthesis and the phenomenal mechanical,5 optical,6 electrocatalytic,7 and extraordinary electronic8 characteristics of the first discovered honeycomb graphene render it an outstanding contender as a building block for future nanotechnology. However, despite having extremely high electronic transport features owing to their massless Dirac fermions as charge carriers, excellent noise immunity, and many other exotic physical properties, its semi-metallic characteristics obstruct its implementation in several high-performance electronic devices. In particular, graphene has a poor on/off-current ratio (below 10 near room temperature) and a low Seebeck coefficient resulting from its null bandgap, which is not suitable for logic circuit operations.9,10 Moreover, graphene-based devices have low energy conversion efficiency due to its tremendously high thermal conductivity.11 Thus, the integration of graphene in digital applications leads to high power consumption, preventing its successful realization in several semiconductor and thermoelectric applications, which has stimulated the quest for an appropriate alternative 2D honeycomb from group IV or group V. Nevertheless, several fascinating properties of the group-V compounds regarded commonly as pnictogens, allow them to be more appropriate for functional applications relative to the group-IV materials. In contrast to the semi-metallic nature of the group-IV elements, the 2D monolayers of pnictogens are insulators or semiconductors covering a broad range of electronic bandgaps,12,13 and their band topology can be effectively tuned by applying external electric fields, lattice strain,14,15 and chemical adsorption,16 which are ideal for broadband photo-response applications.17

The recent extraction of phosphorene from black phosphorous,18 its successful realization in field-effect transistors (FETs),19 as well as its outstanding properties such as a tunable bandgap (200 meV), large on/off ratios (>105), high carrier mobility (103â104 cm2 Vâ1 sâ1), and strong in-plane anisotropy4,17,20 have sparked curiosity in discovering 2D allotropes of the following pnictogens. Owing to their superior chemical, electronic and thermophysical characteristics,4,12 novel 2D pnictogens are expected to be a suitable competitor of graphene. Among all pnictogens, the 2D monolayer bismuthenes are of special importance due to their extremely versatile electronic properties arising from a greater atomic number (Z = 83) and a highly buckled topology of 1.74 Ã.21 By the virtue of a strong spinâorbit-coupling (SOC), the buckled honeycomb bismuthene is reported to be the highest nontrivial bandgap (>600 meV) 2D topological insulator (TI) in its natural state,22,23 as compared to other pnictogens that are traditional semiconductors (nitrogen is an insulator).13 The electronic bandgap of bismuthene can be tuned within a wide range of 0â900 meV by configuring its structural and chemical properties like thickness and phase.12,24 Most interestingly, by engineering the SOC strength and lattice strain, the topological phases of the single-layer bismuthene can be transformed over a large region in the phase diagram including a zero bandgap metal, a topological semimetal, a null-gap Dirac semimetal, TI, direct and indirect bandgap semiconductors, and conventional insulators.21 In fact, bismuthene and its alloys such as Bi2Se3, Bi2Te3, BixSb1âx, Bi1.1Sb0.9Te2S, etc. possess the most promising topological insulator properties,25,26 due to their considerably high SOC which scales as the fourth order of the atomic number.25 The large bandgap of 900 meV12,24 of bismuthene resulting in a strong SOC, ultimately enables them to be a quantum spin hall material at the practical operating conditions,25 which is a big challenge for the group-IV elements as their predicted bandgaps are smaller than 100 meV25 due to substantially weaker SOC, thus limiting their room temperature operation as FETs.

Although recently 2D single-layer bismuthene was experimentally synthesized over various substrates including NbSe2,27 SiC25 and graphene,28 free-standing stable bismuthene has not been realized so far. However, there are several predictions and theoretical studies available exploring its electronic, structural, and thermoelectric properties. Bismuthene possesses a large number of other fascinating characteristics such as highly anisotropic transport properties,29 the highest resistivity and Hall coefficient among all materials,29â31 sizeable SOC, a semimetal band structure,32 high stability at room temperature,33 broadband optical response, topological nontriviality,34 a high carrier mobility ranging between 5.7 Ã 106 (in the bulk) to 300 cm2 Vâ1 sâ1 (monolayer),35 a moderate bandgap, metalâsemiconductor transition under strain,36 easy transferability from the parent to another substrate without altering its optical, electrical, and structural properties,35 and robust TI states under strain and electric field.37 All these fascinating properties render bismuthene a remarkable candidate for all-optical switchers,38 light modulation and photodetectors, light-emitting devices, solar cells,26 electrochemical sensing,39 cooling and power generation, spintronics, optronics, and many other electronic applications.40,41

Meanwhile, intensive research has been focused on increasing the efficiency of thermoelectric materials in recent years, as such materials can convert waste heat to electricity through the Seebeck effect, thereby mitigating issues associated with global warming and the scarcity of natural energy resources. Materials having a low thermal conductivity and/or a high power factor, that is the product of electrical conductivity and Seebeck coefficient, are desired for achieving an increased thermoelectric figure of merit (ZT),36 which is a non-dimensional quantity for evaluating the conversion efficiency of thermoelectric materials. Bismuth-based compounds, for instance, Bi2Te2X (X = S, Se, Te), (Bi2Te3),42 and (BiSb) alloys41 are known to be the most promising materials for thermoelectric applications near room temperature43,44 due to a number of interesting features including low thermal conductivity because of its large atomic mass causing increased anharmonic phonon scattering and small group velocity of phonons,42 highly anisotropic Fermi surface,29 long carrier mean free path,45 high carrier mobility, low carrier density,29 long electron wavelength and negligible carrier effective mass.32 In addition, the ZT value of bismuthene monolayers is 2.4 at 300 K, which is substantially higher than that of bulk Bi-based compounds. More interestingly, upon increasing the temperature to 500 K, this value can be increased up to 4.1,41 rendering bismuthene a promising candidate for thermoelectric operations.

Although a lot of endeavors have been devoted towards analyzing the structural and electronic properties of 2D monolayer bismuthene, its mechanical properties, thermal transport and failure mechanisms remain unexplored. There are very few investigations in the literature reporting the mechanical properties and almost no studies are available regarding the thermal conductivity of monolayer bismuthene nanosheets. Utilizing first-principles calculations, Akturk et al.46 confirmed the stability of an asymmetric washboard bismuthene monolayer at high temperature and showed that by transforming from the 3D crystal to the monolayer honeycomb structure, the semimetal nature of bulk bismuth can be converted into that of a semiconductor. They also evaluated the response of Poisson's ratio and in-plane stiffness under strain. Aghdasi et al.47 investigated the effects of introducing H, F, Cl, and Br atoms as adsorbents on the elastic and bulk moduli of a bismuthene nanosheet subjected to uniaxial and biaxial loading, and reported a notable decrease of the elastic and bulk moduli of the adsorbed nanosheet compared to its pristine state.

It is crucial to investigate physical properties such as stressâstrain response, Young's modulus, ultimate tensile strength, thermal conductivity, effects of various defects, and fracture mechanism of Î²-bismuthene both in theory and in experiments for ensuring better performance, life-time, efficient heat dissipation, and thermal management in bismuthene-based (opto)electronic, thermoelectric, and spintronic devices. To shed some light on the above discussed facts, the current work, therefore, aims to investigate the mechanical, thermal transport, and fracture characteristics of monolayer Î²-bismuthene nanosheets both in the armchair and zigzag loading directions. The effects of temperature, mono-vacancy, crack length, and loading rate on the stressâstrain behavior, UTS, fracture strain, Young's modulus, and thermal conductivity of Î²-bismuthene have been thoroughly investigated. Finally, the fracture phenomenon of pre-cracked Î²-bismuthene has been explored as well. Although there is another stable phase of bismuthene that is the asymmetric washboard (Î±-bismuthene),26 our investigation is limited to the Î²-phase (hexagonally buckled honeycomb phase) only. Note that Î²-bismuthene would be denoted as bismuthene in the following sections (Fig. 1).

2. Computational methods

To calculate the mechanical and thermal properties of bismuthene, we conducted molecular dynamics (MD) simulations using the Large Scale Atomic/Molecular Massively Parallel Simulator (LAMMPS) package.48 We have utilized the potential of StillingerâWeber (SW)49 to describe the interatomic interactions. The parameters of the SW potential for bismuthene are provided in the ESI.â 

In both mechanical and thermal analyses, a 40 nm Ã 40 nm monolayer bismuthene nanosheet (BiNS) is used. The influence of the size of the BiNS on the tensile properties has been provided in the ESI.â  To predict the tensile properties, first, the structure is minimized using the conjugate gradient scheme, followed by constant energy (NVE) simulations for 50 ps with a time step of 1 fs. After that, NPT (constant pressure, temperature) simulations are conducted at atmospheric pressure for 50 ps to ensure that no residual stress remains in the system. The temperature and pressure damping parameters are considered to be 0.5 ps. Canonical NVT (constant volume, temperature) simulations are then conducted for 20 ps. Eventually, uniaxial tensile strain is imposed along with the armchair and zigzag paths at various strain rates such as 108 sâ1, 5 Ã 108 sâ1, 109 sâ1, 5 Ã 109 sâ1, 1010 sâ1 under NVT ensembles. A periodic boundary condition is applied along the loading direction, and during the simulation process, a 5 nm gap is maintained in the simulation box along the z-axis to avoid any interaction across the periodic boundary. This implemented strain rate is much higher than that of actual situations; nevertheless, such high strain rates are very frequent in MD simulations in order to overcome the limitations associated with computational power and simulation time.50â52 Atomistic inspections of the generated data are done using OVITO.53 Note that we report the stress and the Young's modulus in terms of force per unit length (GPa nm or N mâ1) instead of force per unit area. The results corresponding to the temperature dependent tensile strength, defects, and crack length on the tensile properties of BiNS are provided for a strain rate of 109 (sâ1).

2.1 Heat flux control method

In this method, a constant heat flux is established throughout the simulation domain in a particular direction and the corresponding temperature gradient is calculated after attaining the steady-state equilibrium conditions.55 For this reason, a system consisting of one heat source and two heat sinks are considered.55 As shown in Fig. 2, the 2 nm thick heat source is situated in the middle, and two heat sinks of the same thickness are positioned at both ends of the simulation domain. A consistent heat flux was established until a constant temperature profile with time was achieved, representing an equilibrium condition throughout the computational domain. To calculate the thermal conductivity with high spatial and temporal precision, temperature samples each averaging over 2 nm wide segments along the x-direction are used to set sample data,55 and thus, a statistically reasonable temperature profile (Fig. 2) was obtained to quantify the thermal conductivity of the BiNS.
Fig. 2 Snapshot of initial coordinates of 40 nm Ã 40 nm Î²-bismuthene with a magnified view. It is evident that heat is flowing along the armchair direction from the source to the sink. Temperature gradients are calculated using the linear segments of the temperature profile.

It is worth mentioning that the second heat sink is located outside the simulation domain, due to the fact that periodic boundary condition is applied along the flow direction. The simulation domain is divided into 20 bins, and consequently, the temperature of the second heat sink (21st bin) is the same as that of the first heat sink (1st bin). As the temperature of the 21st bin is not present in the temperature profile, an asymmetric nature can be observed in the profile. In the calculation of thermal conductivity, both sides of the temperature profile have been considered by taking the average of slopes computed from both sides, marked by a solid black line in the temperature profile of BiNS shown in Fig. 2. Therefore, the final slope can be described as = (slope of the left side + slope of the right side)/2. Because of the presence of non-linear regions in the proximity of the hot and cold bins, stemming from the strong inelastic scattering in the respective bins, we considered only the linear segments of the profile in the calculations of temperature gradients. Such an approach has been widely used in previous literature studies as well.60â64 Note that contrasting procedures are also found in the literature. Hu et al.65 obtained the temperature gradient directly dividing the temperature difference by the length. However, such an approach may introduce inaccuracies in the gradient due to the non-linearity in the profile.

It is important to note that the thickness of the monolayer BiNS considered for calculating the thermal conductivity was 0.65 nm, as reported by Yang et al.66 As mentioned earlier, no data are available for the thermal conductivity of BiNS therefore, we could not make a comparison of the data with any reference. However, the SW potential and its optimized versions are successfully used for predicting the thermal conductivity of other 2D materials such as silicene,10 polycrystalline silicene,67 MoS2,68 WSe269 and phosphorene.70 Besides, we found that our predicted thermal conductivity is reasonably consistent with that of other 2D materials (Table 1). 3. Results and discussions

3.1 Effect of temperature on the tensile mechanical properties of Î²-bismuthene

We investigated the effects of temperature on the uniaxial tensile-mechanical properties such as ultimate tensile stress (UTS), fracture strain, and YM of the BiNS both in the armchair and zigzag loading directions. Fig. 3(a and b) illustrates the stressâstrain response of the BiNS in both directions for a temperature range of 100â500 K. From the stressâstrain response, it is apparent that, when the applied strain is very small (less than 4%), stress increases linearly with increasing strain, thereby, exhibiting an elastic nature of the nanosheet within this limit.50,51,71,72 Further increase in strain causes a non-linear rise of stress up to the UTS point, and then immediately decreases with a slight increase in strain, representing a brittle-type failure of the monolayer BiNS. Such a brittle-type failure is also reported for other analogous 2D honeycomb lattice structures such as graphene,73 h-BN,73 silicene,73 germanene74 and stanene.75
Fig. 3 Effect of increasing temperature on the characteristic stressâstrain profile of Î²-bismuthene subjected to tension along (a) armchair and (b) zigzag directions. Prediction of (c) fracture stress and (d) fracture strain with increasing temperature in the cases of tension along armchair and zigzag edges.

We note that no plastic deformation region, and hence, no brittle to ductile transition temperature is recorded for the BiNS under uniaxial tensile loading conditions in the entire temperature region, which is again in agreement with the aforementioned elemental analogous structures. It can also be observed that with an increase in temperature, the region under the stressâstrain curves shows a gradual reduction. Consequently, the total energy absorbed by the material up to the fracture, i.e. the fracture toughness reduces monotonically with the increasing temperature. These findings are translated further into Fig. 3(c and d), representing variations in the UTS and fracture strain as a function of temperature. Both the UTS and fracture strain demonstrate a linear inverse relationship with temperature. Increasing temperature from 100 K to 500 K causes a significant decrease in the UTS (28.13% in the armchair, 20.30% in the zigzag) and fracture strain (36.17% in the armchair, 10.20% in the zigzag).

This reduction in the mechanical properties can be attributed to the fact that as the temperature rises, the chemical bonds between BiâBi atoms are subjected to larger atomic mobility and vibration, which weakens the chemical bonds and eventually, increases instability in the structure.50,51 Therefore, the atomic bonds can attain their critical bond length at a lower strain, causing material failure much earlier. Another important observation is that the zigzag direction is more resilient to deformation than the armchair direction as reported earlier.73,75,76Fig. 3(d) shows that the failure strain in the zigzag direction has a gentle dependence on temperature while it is much more sensitive in the armchair direction. This could be due to the influence of bond alignment along the direction of loading. There are six atomic bonds per unit cell carrying the load in the armchair direction, and two of them are parallel while the remaining four render a relative angle of 60Â° along the armchair direction.73 On the other hand, there are four bonds per unit cell carrying the load in the zigzag direction, which are aligned at an angle of 30Â° and the remaining two bonds are perpendicular to the loading direction. Therefore, when tension is applied in the zigzag direction, these inclined bonds allow the structure to stretch more before reaching critical bond length thus, experiencing much lower local stress. Nevertheless, in the case of armchair loading, the existence of the two additional bonds parallel to the direction of loading leads to greater local stress under loading conditions. This enables the zigzag direction to tolerate larger global strain until failure in the entire temperature regime. This kind of behavior is also evident in the case of other analogous 2D materials like graphene, silicene, stanene, germanene, and h-BN which also possess the honeycomb lattice structure.73â76 Note that, although zigzag loading is always supposed to show higher fracture stress and strain, at low temperature from 100â200 K, Fig. 3(c) manifests slightly improved fracture stress for armchair loading compared to the zigzag one, thus giving rise to an anomaly though the difference is insignificant. This could be induced from temperature fluctuation during MD simulations and high strain rates. This kind of anomaly is reported for stanene as well.75 When temperature increases beyond 200 K, zigzag directed bismuthene starts to show higher fracture stress as expected, and the difference becomes notable at 500 K.

We further analyze the failure behavior of bismuthene and found that the failure properties at 100 K is quite similar to those of silicene and stanene. For the armchair direction, silicene fails at a strain of 25%77 whereas, for stanene, the failure strain is 18%.75 The present study shows that at 100 K temperature, the predicted fracture strain of the BiNS is â¼23.58%. Among these three 2D materials, silicene is more flexible to deform and harder to break. Therefore, it can be said that in terms of mechanical properties, bismuthene is relatively weaker as compared to silicene but stronger than stanene. Moreover, YM is defined by the gradient of the linear portion of the uniaxial stressâstrain diagram which represents elastic deformation. In the present investigation, the YM was calculated by evaluating the slope on the initial linear portion (strain value less than 4%) of the stressâstrain curve.75Fig. 4 manifests a strong inverse relationship of YM with temperature. As the temperature increases, larger atomic vibrations are experienced by the bonds which in turn increases chemical instability and also the mean distance between atoms increases owing to high atomic mobility which causes the BiâBi bonds to become weaker. A lower value of stress is, therefore, required in order to cause the same amount of deformation which is responsible for lowering the value of YM at higher temperatures, as reported earlier.50,51,73,75 Another observation is that the mechanical properties of bismuthene show strong directional dependency under uniaxial tension. Likewise, other 2D materials, the properties in the armchair and zigzag directions are considerably different and the difference continuously increases with temperature. At all temperature regimes, bismuthene shows higher elasticity when a tensile load is applied along the armchair direction compared to the zigzag counterparts, which is consistent with other analogous materials such as stanene75 and germanene.74 The underlying reason can be explained again from the effect of the bond angle with the loading direction. For armchair loading, the major portion of the applied load is carried by the two bonds parallel to loading, as described earlier. A slight tension thus increases the local stress tremendously whereas for zigzag loading, the bond angles are at either 30Â° or 90Â° to the loading direction. Therefore, a much larger global strain is needed to induce the same stress, resulting in a comparatively lower YM.73,75 It is worth mentioning that bismuthene demonstrates a strong chirality effect at the entire temperature regime in the case of YM, which is evident from Fig. 4.

3.2 Effect of vacancy defect on the stressâstrain behavior of Î²-bismuthene

It is well known from Griffith's theory that compared to the bonding strength, the type, size, and shape of defects more significantly dictate the fracture strength of materials.78 Nevertheless, vacancy defects are the most frequent and have a marked impact among various defects in governing the mechanical behaviors,79,80 as they are often generated in functional materials during fabrication and processing as well as by various physical phenomena including irradiation, sublimation, and stress.81 In fact, a â¼40% reduction in the UTS of graphene is reported by introducing just 2% of monovacancy defects.82 Therefore, in the present investigation, the mechanical properties of the BiNS are studied under the consideration of randomly dispersed monovacancy defects. Fig. 5 displays the defected state of the BiNS with 1% of random monovacancy concentration.
Fig. 5 Initial coordinates of 1% defected Î²-bismuthene with a magnified view. Bismuth (Bi) atoms are removed randomly from the nanosheet.

The effects of randomly removed Bi atoms from the nanosheet on its tensile mechanical properties as a function of vacancy concentration are illustrated in Fig. 6. Fig. 6(a and b) clearly indicates that vacancy defects greatly deteriorate the mechanical properties of the BiNS both in the armchair and zigzag directions. The fracture toughness is reduced to a large extent just by eliminating 0.2% of Bi atoms. These variations are more profoundly explained in Fig. 6(c and d) in terms of UTS and fracture strain. As the concentration of vacancy is increased, both UTS and fracture strain decline exponentially and furthermore, the elimination of just 1% of atoms cause a significant reduction in the UTS (41.81% in the armchair, 36.01% in zigzag), and fracture strain (45.8% in the armchair, 42.44% in zigzag). These deteriorations of mechanical properties in the presence of vacancies can be attributed to the localized stress concentration that breaks the integrity of the stress field in the structure. When an atom and the corresponding chemical bonds are eliminated from the structure, some dangling bonds and temporary weak bonds are formed by the under-coordinated atoms around the void.83 When a load is applied, these weak and dangling bonds experience a large local vibration, giving rise to an accumulation of stress and chemical instability in the structure.84 Also, the mechanical equilibrium state of the structure degenerates greatly owing to the large local fluctuation of mass and strain, which promotes the destruction of chemical linkages of the neighboring atoms, and hence, the formation of cracks.80 Therefore, as the concentration of vacancies increases, UTS and fracture strain of the material drastically reduces as the bonds in the void region can break a lot earlier.

It can be seen from Fig. 6(c and d) that as the percentage of defects increases from 0.4% to 0.6%, both fracture stress and strain slightly increase in the zigzag loading thus rise to an anomaly. As mentioned earlier, Bi atoms are removed randomly. Thus, when vacancy concentration becomes 0.6%, there is a possibility of the creation of a large void due to the removal of more than one atom from the same region, and it is reported earlier that an increased radius of voids delays the fracture of materials.85 This kind of anomaly is also found for stanene.85 As mentioned earlier, even in the presence of defects, the zigzag direction manifests more resilience to deformation compared to the armchair direction. For a 1% defect concentration, the fracture occurs at about â¼12.5% of strain for the zigzag direction and â¼10.56% strain for the armchair direction. Also, it is important to note that the fracture strength and strain present a sharp decrease at a relatively low concentration of defects. As the concentration increases further, the failure stress and strain values become independent of the defect concentration. This might be attributed to the vacancy defects primarily facilitating only the initial crack formation.86 Fracturing mainly starts from the location where the initial crack is nucleated due to the maximum stress concentration near the vacancy. So, after forming a crack, the introduction of more vacancies does not play any significant role in further reduction of the material strength.

3.3 Effect of crack length on the tensile mechanical properties of Î²-bismuthene

In this section, we simulated the uniaxial tensile properties of a pre-cracked BiNS considering different crack lengths. The pre-cracks are generated by deleting a line of atoms in the armchair and zigzag directions and, in each case, loads are applied perpendicular to the crack extent direction. Crack lengths are varied from 1 nm to 3 nm. Fig. 7 manifests a magnified view of the BiNS with a 3 nm pre-crack. The variation of fracture stress and strain resulting from increasing crack lengths are depicted in Fig. 8. It is apparent from the figure that for both the armchair and zigzag loading conditions, the UTS decreases with larger crack lengths and the material fails at relatively lower strain values. 3.6 Thermal transport phenomenon of Î²-bismuthene

In this study, the consequences of temperature and vacancy on the thermal conductivity (TC) of monolayer BiNS are studied and found that both increasing temperature and vacancy concentrations have detrimental effects on the thermal transport of the BiNS which have been depicted in Fig. 11(a and b). In fact, Fig. 11(a) exhibits a great reduction in TC by â¼57% for the pristine bismuthene, while the temperature is increased from 100 K to 600 K. Similar decreasing behaviors in TC were observed for graphene, silicene, bismuth nanowire, bismuth telluride (Bi2Te3) thin films, stanene nanoribbons, graphene/stanene hetero-bilayer nanoribbons, hBN nanoribbons, defective graphene nanoribbons, stanene/silicene hetero-bilayer nanoribbons, MoS2 nanoribbons and carbon and silicon doped stanene nanoribbons.92â103 For the SW potential, the thermal conductivities for defect free bismuthene nanosheets obtained in the present investigation at 100 K and 600 K temperatures are â¼2.00 W mâ1 Kâ1 and â¼0.88 W mâ1 Kâ1, respectively.
Fig. 11 Variations of thermal conductivity of Î²-bismuthene with (a) temperature and (b) vacancy concentration at 300 K.

The TC of crystals, in general, decreases with increasing temperature owing to the increased phononâphonon and phononâelectron scattering that hinders the energy transfer.104 At high temperatures, due to the anharmonic nature of interatomic potentials, inelastic three-phonon scattering viz., the normal (N-process) and Umklapp (U-process) scattering processes become significant, which results in frequency modification and phononâphonon anharmonic interactions.105 In particular, the Umklapp scattering (U-process) is mainly responsible for acceleration of the thermal resistivity at elevated temperatures. As the temperature increases, the frequency of phonons increases as well, and the dominance of the U-process increases proportionally to the square of the phonon frequency resulting in a strong phononâphonon scattering, thereby greatly suppressing the phonon momentum and heat transfer.92,106,107 Below room temperature, the TC of bismuthene exhibits a much higher value than that of high temperature, as the U-process is almost insignificant at low temperatures.108

Again, it is reported that the acoustic phonons play a greater significance in carrying the thermal energy compared to the optical phonons.1 Of all the acoustic phonons, however, the in-plane phonons, viz., the longitudinal acoustic (LA) and the transverse acoustic (TA) phonons primarily dictate the TC of 2D structures.106 When the temperature is relatively low, LA phonons carry the major portion of heat.55 However, the relative contribution of TA phonons in transporting heat becomes more prominent at elevated temperatures as compared to the LA phonons, which again results in the decrease in TC. It can also be observed from Fig. 11(a) that as temperature increases, the rate of reduction in TC continuously declines due to the excitation of more and more high-frequency phonons that aid in the conduction of thermal energy.54 In addition, introducing various point defects is known to be a novel strategy for tuning lattice thermal conductivity by localizing the phonon vibrations without hindering the electrical conductivity as the mean free path of electrons is much lower than that of phonons.94 Numerous studies also disclosed that the anisotropic behavior and the magnitude of TC in graphene and silicon nanomaterials can be successfully manipulated by engineering the boundary shape, concentration, position, size, and shape of such vacancies.109â112 To be more specific, it is reported that it is possible for the TC to reduce by â¼80% by introducing only 3% of monovacancy defects in the In2Te3 lattice,113 whereas, up to a thousand-fold reduction can be achieved with only 8.25% of such vacancies in graphene.110 These reports pave the route for a similar investigation in BiNS, which can reveal a general outline for regulating the TC of bismuthene, and thereby, increasing the energy conversion efficiency of thermoelectric devices.

The consequence of randomly induced monovacancy defects on the TC of monolayer BiNS at 300 K temperature is manifested in Fig. 11(b) and the result suggests that vacancy defects can also play a prominent role in dictating the thermal transport phenomena in bismuthene. It has been found that TC of BiNS can be reduced up to â¼43% by introducing only 1% vacancy defects. The reduction of TC with increased vacancy concentration can be attributed to the localization of energy carriers and phonon scattering around the defected regions. In a homogenous lattice, the thermal energy is transported in a harmonic manner through the atomic vibration from one atom to the next atom. Nevertheless, removing an atom from the chain impedes the energy transport process between the nearby members which leads to a reduction in the phonon thermal conductivity. The removal of an atom gives rise to a large local fluctuation in mass and strain between the atomic linkages near the void, which causes the scattering of an energy carrier while traversing the interface.114 This interfacial mismatch gives rise to the effect called thermal-boundary resistance or kapitza resistance.115 Because of this phenomenon, the group velocity and the mean free path (MFP) of phonons get dramatically suppressed,94,116 which eventually leads to the localization of some phonons around the vacancy. Besides, Klemens theory117 and the Tamura model118 well agree with this decrease in TC with growing vacancy concentration, which also indicates that the point disorders created by mass contrast in a crystal can stimulate phonon scattering, which increases the thermal resistance and this anharmonic interaction in the phonon transport maximizes when the induced imperfection is a vacancy.119

It can be also observed from Fig. 11(b) at high vacancy concentration, say 0.8â1%, the TC of bismuthene endows a relatively low reduction rate as compared to the low vacancy concentration. At a low concentration of vacancies, the voids and thereby, the localized zones remain far apart from one another. Therefore, the number of localized zones increases as more voids are created, which can effectively hinder the phonon transport. Contrarily, voids are fairly close to each other at a high vacancy concentration, and multiple voids altogether form a combined localized center. Consequently, increasing vacancy concentration further can no longer effectively contribute to the TC reduction.116 The key finding of the present study is that the TC of bismuthene is considerably lower than almost all analogous 2D materials, which can be seen from the comparison presented in Table 1. This substantially low TC of bismuthene can be attributed to its heavier mass, larger interatomic bond length, larger buckling heights, lower in-plane stiffness, increased phononâphonon scattering effect, lowering of phonon group velocity and MFP with increasing atomic radius.12,68,120,121 This considerably low TC of BiNS can enable its applications as a high-efficiency thermoelectric material, as the energy conversion efficiency of thermoelectric devices is inversely related to their TC and could be predicted by the following equation where Ï, S, K and T denote the electrical conductivity, Seebeck coefficient, thermal conductivity of the material at room temperature in Kelvin.42

4. Conclusions

In summary, we have investigated the mechanical and thermal transport properties of monolayer Î²-bismuthene nanosheet utilizing MD simulations. We employed the SW potential to define the interatomic interactions. Tensile properties such as fracture strength, fracture strain, and the Young's modulus are calculated for both the armchair and the zigzag orientations. Temperature studies suggest that increasing temperature facilitates the failure at a relatively lower fracture stress and strain. The obtained room temperature fracture stress and fracture strain are â¼4.21 N mâ1 and â¼19.50%, respectively, along the armchair direction, and â¼4.22 N mâ1 and â¼25.5% along the zigzag path. At all temperatures, the zigzag direction is found to be more resilient to fracture and less vulnerable to temperature change than the armchair direction. The Young's modulus along both directions demonstrate a strong negative dependence on temperature, and armchair direction provides slightly superior elasticity compared to its zigzag counterpart. At 300 K, the Young's moduli are reported to be â¼26.1 N mâ1 and â¼25.5 N mâ1, respectively, along the armchair and zigzag directions. It is also found that increasing vacancy defects and crack length have a marked impact in deteriorating the mechanical properties of the Î²-bismuthene. Fracture study of pre-cracked bismuthene reveals that armchair directed crack is more resilient to fracture than the zigzag directed crack. Besides, a branching phenomenon occurs during crack propagation for the armchair crack, whereas crack propagation along the zigzag direction is found to be an extension of the original crack length. Both along the armchair and zigzag directions, the fracture stress and strain increase with increasing strain rates. Finally, we have calculated the thermal conductivity (majority carrier is the phonon) of the bismuthene nanosheet under the influence of temperature and monovacancy defects. We observe a decrease in thermal conductivity with increasing temperature and vacancy concentrations. Notably, introducing only 1% of randomly dispersed mono vacancy is seen to reduce the thermal conductivity by â¼43%. Also, the predicted thermal conductivity of â¼1.3 W mâ1 Kâ1 at 300 K is considerably lower than other analogous 2D honeycombs and could be engineered to â¼0.75 W mâ1 Kâ1 by removing only â¼1% bismuth atom from the domain, thus, rendering Î²-bismuthene a promising material for high-performance thermoelectric operations. To support the explanation for reduced thermal conductivity with increasing temperature and vacancy, phonon properties such as phonon density of states, phonon dispersion spectrum, and phonon group velocities are also analyzed, which exhibit excellent conformity with the obtained results. This investigation of the mechanical properties and thermal conductivity analysis unravel a new horizon in the practical utilization of Î²-bismuthene, a material with spectacular electronic and thermoelectric properties which also demonstrates topological insulator properties and quantum spin Hall effect at room temperature.
