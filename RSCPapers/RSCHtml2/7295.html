Abstract

Metal–support interactions (MSIs) have become one of the research directions of great interest in the field of heterogeneous catalysis and are significant for the design of supported metal catalysts with improved catalytic activity and controlled product selectivity. Depending on the interactions of the loaded metal or metal oxide, MSIs can mainly be classified as strong metal–support interactions (SMSIs), medium metal–support interactions (MMSIs) and weak metal–support interactions (WMSIs). Furthermore, SMSI can be divided into covalent metal–support interactions (CMSIs) and electronic metal–support interactions (EMSIs) based on the principles of their action. An extensive discussion of the recent theoretical developments is included in this review, as well as the factors influencing these developments. Additionally, the methods for the characterization of the fundamental properties, the fabrication and applications of MSIs and their wide application in the chemical industry, energy conversion and other fields are presented. Conclusively, the challenges and directions for future research and perspectives are discussed.

1. Introduction

Energy plays a pivotal role in the progression of life.1 Simultaneously, the traditional fossil energy resources are limited, and thus under the continuous exploitation and utilization of these resources presently, humans will inevitably face their depletion in the future, such as coal, oil and natural gas.2,3 Furthermore, the process of obtaining energy from natural fossil energy results in the emission of several harmful gases, such as CO2 and SO2, inevitably causing severe environmental pollution. At present, solar energy, wind energy, and geothermal energy are among the clean and renewable energy sources being developed.4 In promoting the application of new energy industries, the innovative advancement of electric energy conversion and storage technology plays a vital role.5,6 For the progression of new energy technologies and the conversion of new energy, it is essential to design and develop heterogeneous catalysts with excellent performances.

Supported metal catalysts (SMCs), i.e., nanostructured metal active substances immobilized on the boundary of a support, have become a hot topic in heterogeneous catalysis because the metal–support interactions (MSIs) confer positive catalytic activity and better stability to SMCs compared with pure metal nanoparticle (NP) catalysts.7,8 The carriers in SMCs not only play a physical role, but also interact with the active metals. Among the interactions, the MSI effect is revolutionizing the field of catalysis.9,10

As a significant concept in heterogeneous catalysis,11 MSIs often lead to phenomena such as charge transfer, molecular adsorption modulation and metal structural change,10 ultimately strongly affecting the performance of catalysts. Thus, MSIs have become one of the research directions highly sought after in the domain of heterogeneous catalysis. In the late 1970s, Tauster et al. discovered a phenomenon known as strong metal support interaction (SMSI), which is mainly used to study the loss of the ability of TiO2-loaded Pt-group precious metals to adsorb smaller molecules after high-temperature reduction.12 Thereafter, Hu et al. observed the chemical adsorption of CO on a Pt/TiO2 catalyst using infrared spectroscopy (IR).13 They proposed hydrogen overflow and reverse overflow in SMSI through the red-shift and violet-shift of the CO-infrared absorption peaks. Later, other reducible metal oxides were found to exhibit similar phenomena. The “blue shift” of the CO band is caused by the interaction of CO with the hydrogen provided by the TiO2 support, and the strong band in the IR spectrum of the adsorbed CO gradually experiences a “blue shift” with the corresponding change in conductivity. Today, MSIs are widely recognized to play a decisive role in stabilizing carrier active metals. Also, they are extremely important in studying the reaction mechanism and regulating the catalytic activity.14 MSIs in heterogeneous catalysis mostly occur on reducible metal-based supports.15 With the development of advanced material characterization techniques, such as in situ high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM),16 new insights have been gained to enable our understanding of the MSI behavior. Simultaneously, we note that the concept of MSIs has been inaccurately used in some cases, and general MSIs are considered SMSIs.17 Consequently, in this critical and systematic overview of MSIs, we look at their basic features and recent developments, the identification of different MSIs, and the understanding and application of MSIs, which are necessary and desirable for heterogeneous catalyst development.

Although the research on MSIs has made great progress, their concept is mostly applied to oxide supported/modified metallic catalysts. According to recent reports, the synergy between suboxides and alloys can enhance the performance of catalysts.18 On the one hand, alloying may cause strain, and then change the position of the d-band center of the metal. On the other hand, in the process of alloying, the electronic transfer between metal elements with different electronegativity will change the electronic structure of a single metal.19 Then, the adsorption and desorption of the reactants, intermediates and products on the active sites will be changed by the change in the position of the center of the d-band and the modification of the electronic structure.20 Therefore, the synergistic effect between the alloy and suboxide in alloy-suboxide catalysts may greatly affect their catalytic performance.

In this review, we discuss the theoretical evolution, characterization techniques, and applications of MSIs based on recent developments. In situ TEM and XPS are among the advanced characterization techniques that can be used to better understand the evolution of MSIs. In addition, MSI systems with different geometric shapes have different effects, which can be used for the construction of heterogeneous catalysts, such as particle size effect and spacing effect. For example, metal particle size usually promotes the catalytic activity of the metal species after they form MSIs.21 Therefore, it is noteworthy to understand the behavior of MSIs to regulate the catalytic performance of metal species. Here, we summarize the history and evolution of MSIs, starting with the exploration of the theoretical and experimental characterization of MSIs, which is helpful for a deeper understanding of MSIs. Then, the recent discoveries and applications of SMSIs are described in terms of charge transfer, intermetallic bonding, special morphological structures and carrier encapsulation of metal clusters, and the effects of weak metal–support interactions (WMSIs) and medium metal–support interaction (MMSIs)22 on catalytic performance are again elaborated (Fig. 1). Finally, we present our personal views on the future application areas and research directions of MSIs.

2. The formation and influencing factors of MSIs

2.1 The formation of MSIs

Presently, MSIs have been gaining increasing attention and a wider range of applications with the explosion in the work on heterogeneous catalytic materials, and it is hard to believe that there has been nearly 90 years of research into MSIs. Adadurov reported the presence of high-charged cations on the surface of a carrier polarized metal supported on a support as early as the 1930s,23 and in the mid-1950s, Schwab et al. proposed the collective-electron theory of MSIs (i.e., this theory of ferromagnetism states that the electrons that produce ferromagnetism move around the crystal more or less freely, and they interact with each other aligned due to the exchange) based on the solid-state energy theory and research techniques developed in the field of semiconductor physics.24 Schwab believed that the different Fermi energy between the metal and support caused electron transport across the interface. Back in 1978, the findings of Tauster et al. implied that if TiO2-supported group VIII metal catalysts were applied, then the metals (e.g., Pt/TiO2, Pd/TiO2, Ru/TiO2, and Rh/TiO2) were reduced at high temperature. Furthermore, the metals would be encapsulated by TiO2, thereby losing the adsorption capacity of gas molecules (such as O2).12,26 Based on these experimental phenomena, they proposed that under high-temperature H2 reduction conditions, interactions will occur between the group VIII metals and TiO2 beyond the ordinary MSIs, which are SMSIs. Increasing experimental and theoretical work has gradually recognized and accepted this phenomenon, and it can be used to explain many phenomena in other catalytic systems as well. After decades of accumulation, the concept of SMSIs has been clearly defined.27 The metal may still adsorb H2 if it is supported on a reducible metal support, but its reactivity with H2 will decrease during reduction at high temperatures, which is the result of the strong interaction between the reducible support and metal, and the support will release an electron. Subsequently, it is transferred to the metal, thereby reducing the chemisorption capacity of H2.

The weak and medium and strong metal carrier interactions (WMSIs, MMSIs and SMS1s) were distinguished by Burch.28 The van der Waals interactions between metals act as WMSIs. MMSIs occur between metals in the zeolite channel, while SMSIs occur on metals loaded on reducible oxides.12

Subsequently, Tang's team discovered the SMSI effects of platinum group metal heterogeneous catalysts.30–33 In single-atom catalysts (SACs), the single atoms should be more affected by the interaction of the metal support than supported catalysts as the active centers. Then, Wang and others reported a slight spacing effect, emphasizing the degree of matching between the distance between adjacent Cu atoms (dCu1–Cu1) and the molecular size of the reactant peroxydisulfate (PDS) (Fig. 2c), and determined the activity of the Fenton-like reactions on carbon-loaded SAC.34 Understanding the inter-site interaction properties in SACs is monumental for their application in various catalytic reactions, particularly for SACs with a high site density.35 As scientists continue to work on MSIs, the knowledge and application of MSIs are becoming more comprehensive.

2.2 Factors influencing MSIs

A loaded metal catalyst produces both dispersion and stabilization of metal NPs, as well as MSIs, which frequently result in interfacial charge transfer, change in metal structure, molecular adsorption modulation, and other phenomena, affecting the activity, selectivity, and stability of the catalyst. As we proceed through this section, we investigate how elements such as the carrier, metal particles, and temperature affect MSIs.37 Depending on the catalyst and reaction, different factors may play a different leading role.
2.2.1 Support. It is important to understand how MSIs are influenced by the composition, morphology, doping, and oxygen vacancies of the carrier. The appearance, composition of the support (e.g., degree of hydroxylation), electronic structure (particularly important for heterogeneous catalysis or photocatalysis), reactivity of the carrier, ion mobility (e.g., composition of oxygen vacancies and cation diffusion), and the possibility of cations remaining behind to form intermetallic compounds affect the operator properties of MSIs.38 In the case of oxygen vacancies in the carrier, the creation of stable oxygen vacancies at the metal–carrier interface should contribute to the more likely occurrence of MSI effects, and using a pre-reduced TiO2-x carrier, Zhang et al. prepared and characterized Cu catalysts.36 The oxygen vacancies that are present in the TiO2-x carriers bolster the SMSIs between TiO2 and Cu and enhance the adsorption and activation of CO2 (Fig. 2d). Jiang et al. stabilized Pt single atoms on the oxygen vacancy of CeO2 on different exposed surfaces through SMSIs,39 thus realizing the maximum deformation of Pt1–CeO2 {100} Pt–O–Ce. This deformed interface improved the adsorption capacity of O2 and methanol.

For carriers with different structural forms, Lan et al. achieved highly dispersed Ag NPs by taking advantage of the distinctive capturing influence offered by various forms of hydroxyapatite (HAP) as well as the protective advantage of the hydroxyl groups on the surface of HAP.40 The preparation of this highly dispersed Ag/HAP catalyst was simple. Herein, MSIs of different strengths and weaknesses were generated with Ag NPs at different positions using a novel structure of HAP as a carrier. Among them, the Ag/HAP-s catalyst layered on a nanosheet structure achieved 100% conversion at 160 °C, showing remarkable catalytic activity. By controlling the Ag NPs, their agglomeration and SMSI effects were reduced, and their dispersion in the catalytic reaction was significantly improved. Furthermore, it was concluded by Rios-Escobedo et al. that TiO2 or TiO2–ZrO2 is an essential carrier in anisole hydrodeoxygenation (HDO).41 Depending on the carrier surface acidity, the oxygen defects dispersed on the surface and metal phases dispersed on the surface of the SMSIs, and these differences lead to a change in the product distribution and high performance on the catalyst. However, SMSIs were observed for the reconstructed Cu when sputtered on flame-produced LaTiO2 but not for Cu NP loaded on commercial TiO2 at 500 °C.42

Transition metal NPs and their carriers may interact mainly through the movement of absorbed substances between the metal and oxide surfaces in heterogeneous catalysts. After encapsulating NPs in an oxidizing environment, Frey et al. found that redox-active environments caused the overlayer to be removed, followed by the appearance of particle dynamics.43 Therefore, the classical SMSI state disappears once the surface is exposed to a reactive atmosphere. MSIs play a crucial role in the reaction pathway by modifying the catalytic properties of the metal phase. Martínez et al. showed that the surface hydroxyl groups of the carriers were important for the activity and selectivity of Ru/TiO2 catalysts for CO activation.44 Also, as the crystallinity of the carrier changed, the SMSIs changed as well, affecting the metal phase dispersion and size (Fig. 3a).

When a metal reacts with another metal, the carriers are responsible for controlling its size, shape, dispersion, and structural stability. MSIs are often optimized throughout the synthesis of catalysts,45 and best MSIs will increase the quantity of chemical process active sites and stop metal sintering, thus increasing the chemical process performance.46

2.2.2 Metal nanoparticles. The MSIs of a catalyst can also be affected by the morphology, loading of metal and the particle size, in addition to its inherent properties. In the case of the size of metal particles with different loads, an effective method for enhancing the catalyst performance is to reduce the size of metal structures. By incorporating aerosol-assisted evaporation-induced self-assembly (EISA) method in alumina catalysts, Seo and colleagues synthesized nickel-loaded alumina catalysts.47 Because the nickel particles were smaller than 10 nm, Ni/Sr-MA had the highest coke resistance. As a result of the uniform and broad particle size in the catalyst, the SMSIs were distributed uniformly and effectively, promoting the thermal stability and coke resistance of the catalyst. It was discovered by Nakayama et al. that Mg2+ and Ce3+ can effectively stabilize Au agglomerates with diameters of less than 2 nm by adding them to Ca HAP and Sr HAP.48 Sr(Ca)HAP substituted with Au/Mg and Ce exhibited partially oxidative SMSIs, which accelerated the composition of positively charged Au species, although the SMSIs reduced the surface-exposed Au enrichment, showing higher catalytic activity compared to the SMSI one. However, Du et al. synthesized an Au/TiO2 catalyst with different particle size distributions by the colloidal precipitation method and studied their SMSI performance.29 It was found that the larger-size Au/TiO2 (∼9 nm and 13 nm) was more prone to SMSIs, and the CO adsorbed on Au evaporated comprehensively after reduction at 400 °C. Subsequently, the complete coating of Au NPs by the carrier could be achieved, while for the smaller-size Au/TiO2 (∼7 nm and 3 nm), the reduction temperatures for complete SMSI were 500 °C and 600 °C, respectively. Therefore, the implications of metal particle size in different catalysts on SMSIs have to be discussed specifically.

Furthermore, Ni/SiO2 catalysts prepared by various methods were tested by Chen et al., and the samples attained by ammonia evaporation had outstanding catalytic property.53 Based on the characterization results, in addition to SMSIs, the catalyst had undersized Ni particles and an abundance of Ni–O–Si units, which contributed to its excellent performance. Significant improvements were observed in the anti-sintering/coking properties of the catalysts. Nevertheless, as a result of the impregnation method, the nickel species were unevenly distributed in the Ni/SiO2-IM catalysts, the particle size was large, and the MSIs were weak, performing poorly in methane dry reforming.

The influence of metal particles with different loading amounts on MSIs is also significant, where metal particles of smaller sizes are typically formed when a smaller amount of metal species is loaded on the carrier, which results in the formation of more particles and exposure of more active sites, favoring the promotion of the catalyst activity. However, Yu et al. demonstrated the highest activity by increasing the loading of Ir on a titanium dioxide carrier, with the catalysts with significantly higher loading levels (6 wt%) displaying the highest activity.49 In the process of hydrogen reduction, higher loading levels may inhibit the SMSIs between the Ir species and the Ti dioxide carrier, thus maintaining higher partial reduction characteristics (Fig. 3b).

2.2.3 Temperature. It is well known that adjusting the reduction temperature is one of the commonly used methods for regulating MSIs at present.54 Therefore, when it is necessary to package or decorate metal particles to different degrees, we can use a reducible carrier thin layer. The temperature largely decides the formation process of MSIs. The surface energy of metallic NPs decreases at elevated temperatures due to the increased surface entropy. Because of the increase in entropy on smaller metallic NPs, they have a lower surface free energy.55 Therefore, SMSIs can only form at a higher temperature on smaller metal NPs. The classical SMSIs between Pt and sulfur-doped carbon (S–C) carriers were reported by Yin et al. with electron transfer, inhibition of H2/CO adsorption, and encapsulation of carbon carriers at high temperatures.50 Using a carbon matrix doped with Pt and sulfur atoms, the SMSIs between Pt and sulfur inhibited the overgrowth of Pt in the temperature range of 300 °C to 700 °C, followed by the formation of SMSI packaging structures on Pt NPs at a higher temperatures of 900 °C (Fig. 3c). The study by Han et al. showed that single-atom-modified TiO2 exhibits SMSIs at high reduction temperatures than Pt NPs.51 In general, SMSIs on TiO2-supported Pt catalysts occur at reduction temperatures over 400 °C. By reducing the Pt NPs at 250 °C, the CO adsorption was eliminated, clearly demonstrating the reversible encapsulation and indicating that in the presence of Pt NPs and TiO2 (B) nanosheets, SMSIs can occur at a lower reduction temperature (250 °C) (Fig. 3d).

Besides the above, a new Pt/TiOx interface was created by traditional wet impregnation of Pt NCs by Seo et al.52 During wet impregnation relating to the atmosphere thermal treatment process, TiOx voluntarily populated and partially overlapped the Pt surface, and thus the Pt particles were protected from blocking or coking at higher temperatures of CH4 or even under oxidizing conditions (Fig. 3e). Their SMSI phenomenon achieved by oxygen heat treatment remained stable even in an oxidation and reduction atmosphere. The study by Fu et al. found that SMSI growth occurs mainly on metals whose work functions exceed 5.3 eV and surface energies exceed 2 J m−2, while for group IB metals, when incorporating Cu (4.46 eV) and Au (5.3 eV),56 it is difficult to package them owing to their low surface energy or low work function.

3. Characterization

Many characterization techniques have been developed to delve into the MSI of SMCs, which is another significant part of MSIs. With the rapid development of science and technology, a variety of excellent characterization methods has been expanded, and available technology has greatly helped in realizing the application of MSI. Thus far, the techniques employed for the characterization of MSIs include X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), X-ray adsorption spectroscopy (XAS)57,58 and scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM).59 These techniques start investigating MSIs from various angles, resulting in an improved understanding of their mechanism. A deeper understanding of MSI characterization in heterogeneous catalysis can assist researchers in researching and implementing heterogeneous catalysts, which also perform well in a range of interactions.

3.1 XPS

In the early 1960s, Siegbahn et al. first utilized the concept of differential pumping in XPS.60 Also, they reported the construction of a solid XPS system for studying up to 1 mbar, which was eventually commercialized in the late 1970s.61 XPS is an advanced analytical technology, which depends on the energy of photoelectrons emitted by the measured atoms when they are irradiated by soft X-ray photons (1–2 keV).62 Since it was reported in 1970, this technology has provided scientists with a lot of useful information, which has a strong impact on our views in the field of surface chemistry.63 This technique has already been frequently used to measure the electronic structure of heterogeneous catalysts.

In addition, Fu et al. prepared NiCeAl catalysts with various CeO2 NPs decentralized at a higher CeO2 support loading (≥20 wt%) using EISA, impregnation and precipitation, and they observed the process for the formation of SMSIs through in situ XPS spectroscopy.64 NiCeAl-EISA (Fig. 4a and d), NiCeAl-WI (Fig. 4b and e) and NiCeAl-CP (Fig. 4c) possessed smaller amounts of Ce3+ at room temperature, owing to the composition of the CeAlO3 perovskite phase, which in turn increased the number of Brønsted acid sites (Fig. 4e) and Ni NPs dispersed by shortening the Ni–Al stronger interaction. The reinforced SMSIs accelerated the composition of rich oxygen vacancies and interface positions at 100 °C (Fig. 4f). NiCeAl catalysts with SMSIs exhibited high resistance to clotting and possessed stronger and dispersed Ni sites for superior performance. Zhang et al. analyzed the changes in MSIs caused by charge transfer by measuring the binding energy of electrons using XPS.65 As shown in Fig. 4g, the 3 anionic catalysts showed a clear tendency of peak drift. The supported Pd NPs transferred electrons to N, which decreased the conjunction strength and the MSI effect became weaker. Dai et al. probed the electron transfer behavior of MSIs induced in Ir/Mo2TiC2Tx catalysts by XPS.66 As shown in Fig. 4h, the three samples also transferred to lower binding energies with an increase in the reduction temperature, indicating enhanced electron transfer from the substrate to the metal. The results showed that the highest electron enrichment of the Ir atomic layer at the boundary and the electron transfer from the Mo2TiC2 carrier to Ir atoms.

3.2 XAS

As a synchrotron-based technique for characterizing materials, XAS studies the X-ray absorption near edge structure (XANES) and extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS).67 Due to its atomic-level resolving power and higher productiveness, XAS has become a practical tool for studying the electronic states between metals and carriers in heterogeneous catalysts.68 Several research groups have noted the usefulness of XAS in probing charge jumps between metal atoms and carriers. A metal atom can be investigated through its electrons in different orbitals using this probe.69 For instance, the highest peak in XANES, often known as the white line (WL) peak, is a significant aspect for detecting unoccupied orbitals of the determined elements. The intensity, shape and parallel capabilities of the WL peaks can be very helpful for MSI studies.70 In addition, operational XAS can provide electronic information throughout the reaction to investigate the additional changes that occur in the MSIs during the reaction.71

For example, Eggart et al. prepared two groups of 0.5–1% Pt-loaded CeO2 catalysts with unequal Pt dispersion and oxidation states.72 Operational XAS combined with theoretical simulations demonstrated an increase in Pt–Ce interaction when reacting with CH4 at 975 °C. Analysis of the extended EXAFS spectra demonstrated the modification and packaging of Pt the particles by the CeO2/Ce2O3 carriers or partial Ce–Pt alloy formation due to SMSIs. Furthermore, Zhou et al. focused on Prussian blue analogues (PBA) with the molecular formula of Na2Co2+[Fe2+(CN)6]·nH2O after post-treatment at 400 °C.73 The observed ligand coordination changes (Fig. 5a) were further confirmed by the O K-edge spectra, as shown in Fig. 5d and e. In the N K XAS spectrum, and N K-edge of CF-PBA-400 had a strong frontier of 398.6 eV, which was related to the covalency of the metal 3d-N 2p. Therefore, a larger strong front produces a strong covalent interaction. The characteristic peak of LS Co3+ of Co–C disappeared in all the samples after the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) (Fig. 5b and c), indicating the loss and shortening of the Co–C bond or the possible absence of MSIs.

3.3 Electron microscopy

Heterogeneous catalysts have been extensively characterized using electron microscopy since its invention by Ruska in the early 1930s.74 For instance, in 1940, Ardenne et al. emphasized the usefulness of TEM in detecting the morphology and structure of SMCs.75 Later, with the further advancement of electron microscopes and imaging theory, by using electron microscopy, atomic-level information about MSIs on catalysts could be obtained.76

For example, Frey et al. observed the particle dynamics and directional particle migration under the dynamic interaction between noble metal particles and reducible metal oxide carriers by transmission electron microscopy.43 Lattice mismatch-induced interface strain minimization drives the preferential orientation and repetitive step-like flow in the Pt (111) plane. In addition, Shi et al. synthesized Pd–Cu bimetallic catalysts with different loading sequences and annealing temperatures based on γ-Al2O3 and used STEM to characterize the catalysts, demonstrating that the loading method affects the dispersibility of the catalysts.77 As shown in Fig. 6a, the majority the Pd–Cu catalysts existed in the shape of undersized NPs (0–15 nm), but the spread of these small and roundish NPs on the γ-Al2O3 was non-uniform. As shown in Fig. 6b, the scattering of Pd–Cu catalysts on the γ-Al2O3 surface was poor and large particles (5–30 nm) were produced. The Pd–Cu particles with a smaller size (about 0–6 nm) could be uniformly dispersed on the γ-Al2O3 surface (Fig. 6c). It was observed that the distribution of the interaction between Pd–Cu and γ-Al2O3 was even. As shown in Fig. 6d, by loading Cu first, and then Pd, a smaller size (∼1–5 nm) and more uniformly dispersed Pd2.5%/γ-Al2O3 (Cu1%) catalyst (900 °C) could be obtained.

3.4 Raman spectroscopy

Raman spectroscopy is a non-destructive examination technique that uses the interaction of light and materials. Raman spectroscopy can provide details about the chemical structure, phase and morphology, crystallinity, and molecular interaction of a sample.78 The Raman spectrum is a type of scattering spectrum. We can use Raman spectroscopy to analyze the scattering spectra of different incident light frequencies to obtain the rotational and vibrational information of the detected molecules, which can also be used to study the molecular structure. This method is based on the Raman scattering effect discovered by the Indian scientist Raman.79 Also, because of these advantages, Raman spectroscopy is increasingly used in MSI research to better reveal the close relationship between the metal and carrier.

For instance, Grabchenko et al. used Raman spectroscopy to study the content of lattice oxygen defects and oxygen vacancies.80Fig. 7a shows the Raman spectra of CeO2 and Ag/CeO2 samples before and after CO oxidation. The red shift in the observed band showed that there were defects in the structure of cerium dioxide. Jia et al. used Raman spectroscopy to determine the structure of carbon products (as shown in Fig. 7b).81 According to the typical D band at 1340 cm−1 and G band at 1570 cm−2 in the spectrum, it can be concluded that the defect density is the highest among the carbon products synthesized on Ni/Mg and the lowest on Ni/Sr. Chytil et al. clearly identified a monoclinic phase (m-ZrO2) in the spectral profile, which has a prominent peak at 480 cm−1 and a peak corresponding to the tetragonal phase (t-ZrO2) at 315 and 640 cm−1 (Fig. 7c).82 After immersion in Mn, the spectral characteristics of the carrier almost disappeared, and a poorly defined spectrum (Mn15–ZrO2) was obtained. The broad peaks with centers at 350, 460, 550 and 640 cm−1 may be the contribution of the carrier and manganese oxide.

3.5 Infrared spectroscopy

At the beginning of the 20th century, scientists began to study IR.62 By 1940, after the commercial application of infrared spectrometers, IR was widely used in material and chemical research.

At present, IR is also widely used in the study of MSIs. 4. SMSI

Heterogeneous catalysis is one of the major concepts that involve SMSIs, which include metal structure changes, molecular adsorption modulation, and many other phenomena, resulting in reversible nanoscale structural changes. The changes ultimately impact the catalytic reaction performance, and most of the studies on the role of SMSI involve metal/oxide catalytic systems. Among them, SMSIs can be divided into CMSIs89 and electronic metal support interactions (EMSIs).90 In CMSIs, a covalent bond is formed between the metal and the carrier, whereas in EMSIs, strong electronic interactions generate the bond between the metal and the carrier. CMSIs must accompany EMSIs, but EMSIs do not necessarily accompany CMSIs.91 The SMSI effect in heterogeneous catalysis has been explained in terms of four aspects including metal-to-metal bonding, specific morphology, charge transfer, and carrier-to-metal coating. In addition, we describe the application of SMSIs in heterogeneous catalysts and their effects on catalyst activity, selectivity and stability.

4.2 EMSI

According to Campbell et al. (2012), EMSIs first emerged as a result of interactions between platinum and cerium.90 To explore the interaction in depth, they conducted DFT calculations and showed that metal oxide surfaces are redistributed by EMSIs. An important contribution of EMSIs is that they bridge the gap between studies of electronic theory and the design of heterogeneous catalysts.181 The interaction between metal and carrier can be exploited to improve the catalytic efficiency by exploiting their structural and synergistic effects.
4.2.1 Charge transfer. With the strong interaction between the metal NPs and the carrier, the electronic structure of the metal catalyst site can be controlled during heterogeneous catalysis. By utilizing EMSIs generated by charge transfer, theoretical calculations and the like may be applied to enhance the activity of the catalyst. Yang et al. proposed a catalyst designed by EMSIs, which was proven to be a reliable strategy for catalysts.182 They first performed density functional theory calculations to design an acceptable catalyst. The M1–M2–X (X = C, N, O) model was implemented throughout the calculation and the Rh1–TiC catalyst was prepared by wet impregnation. As one of the catalysts they selected, Rh1TiC has the EMSI Rh1–Ti2C2, which makes it best-suited for the HER. Experiments demonstrated the feasibility of the EMSI strategy via the catalytic potency of SACs of Rh1–TiC. 4.2.2 Bonding between metals. Intermetallic bonds in heterogeneous catalysis are related to electronic interactions, and the positive effects dramatically enhance their activity for catalytic applications. Nowadays, many studies use metal substrates as carriers to study the M–M interaction (i.e., EMSIs) of adjacent different metal atoms. Most of them use active elements such as Pd, Ru, Rh and Mo to load on selective and inert metals or metal oxides.198 This is mainly because the M–M bond has a higher level of d orbital overlap. Song et al. prepared Pt–Co(OH)2/SiO2 catalysts by the wet impregnation method,182 and demonstrated the EMSIs between Pt NPs and Co(OH)2 sheets by in situ diffuse reflection infrared Fourier transformations spectroscopy (DRIFTS) and XAFS technology, revealing the formation of Pt–Co intermetallic bonds, electron transfer from Co to Pt, and the negative charge of Pt atoms lowered the d-band center and weakened CO adsorption, resulting in a significant enhancement in catalytic activity.

Moderate temperature treatment will promote the charge transfer between metals or metal oxides, and the increase in active electrons will also promote the formation of metal bonds. Zhang et al. found that when the heat treatment temperature was 200 °C, the charge of partially reduced ZnO will be transferred to Rh, which may be attributed to the EMSIs between Rh and ZnO.57 Simultaneously, an increase in the number of electrons at the active site promotes the formation of metal bonds with the reactant, which ultimately improves the efficiency and selectivity of the catalyst.

5. Other interactions

5.1 WMSI

Compared with SMSIs, WMSIs have less intensity and narrower scope of action, but their application cannot be ignored. WMSIs can be constructed by changing the morphology and structure of the carrier to improve the heterogeneous catalytic performance. For example, Sun et al. successfully synthesized Pt/Ti4O7-(5, 10, 20) with different loadings (Pt and Ti4O7 were 0.05, 0.1, and 0.2, respectively) using an improved polyol technique.208 The uniformly distributed Pt NPs showed different morphologies, with Pt/Ti4O7-5 being rod-like and Pt/Ti4O7-10 and Pt/Ti4O7-20 being approximately spherical. The lattice distortions and dislocations in the high-loading samples imply WMSIs. In this regard, Wang et al. prepared a catalyst by wet impregnation. The nickel ions supported in the catalyst will preferentially occupy the γ-tetrahedral vacancy of the Al2O3 carrier, whereas WMSIs were found in the γ-Al2O3 support, indicating an attainable mechanism to clarify the various morphologies and different MSIs.209 As observed in the κ-Al2O3 carrier. Once redundant hydrogenation sites were present, the Ni/γ-Al2O3 catalyst with SMSIs had a higher isoprene conversion, higher stability and lower property. The weak interaction encompasses a positive impact on the formation of coke, which is especially associated with hydrogenolysis, resulting in a higher property for monoolefin within the main reaction and a marked decrease in isoprene conversion. Anchoring platinum catalysts on suitable carriers, such as MXenes,210 is a viable way to achieve ideal anodes for direct methanol fuel cells. A number of factors affect the catalytic activity of platinum, including its occupation of active sites, poisoning, weak interactions with carriers, and dissolution. Zhu et al. constructed three-dimensional crinkled Ti3C2Tx MXene spheres with abundant Ti vacancies to confine platinum by spray drying.211 The Pt cluster/Ti3C2Tx (Ptc/Ti3C2Tx) catalyst had a low over potential, high tolerance to CO poisoning and high stability, showing good electrocatalytic methanol oxidation reaction (MOR) activity.

The weak interaction is additionally affected by the loading level, temperature and size impact, which suggests that the interaction is monitored by adjusting these factors to attain the aim of enhancing the activity and stability of the catalyst. Yahya and Amin improved the catalytic performance of palm shell-based activated carbon (AC) catalyst by using hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) and Ni, Co support.212 H2O2-modified AC possesses increased surface chemical reaction practical groups and ultra-micropores, resulting in extremely dispersed active metal sites with uniform distribution and high acidity after Ni–Co impregnation. Among the catalysts, 10% Ni–10% Co/ACP had the highest catalytic activity, with 93.8% H2 yield and 90.2% toluene conversion, with a stability of 325 h. This can be attributed to the higher turnover frequency and smaller grain size, WMSIs and synchronal Ni single bond Co reducibility. WMSIs can result in the formation of carbon nanotubes with a tip growth mechanism, and can effectively inhibit catalyst deactivation. Yang et al. prepared two different types of Pt/CeO2 catalysts by a surfactant-assisted strategy for CO and toluene oxidation (Fig. 19a).213 The results showed that the synthesized Pt/CeO2–NH catalyst is beneficial for the oxidation of toluene, while the comparative Pt/CeO2-AH was favorable for the oxidation of CO. Simultaneously, the Pt/CeO2–NH catalyst had good thermal stability at a high temperature (such as 400 °C) (Fig. 19b–e). Different characterization techniques elucidated that the Pt/CeO2–NH catalyst possessed a higher surface Pt0 ratio, WMSI and more oxygen vacancies. Ratkovic et al. prepared two catalysts by calcining, and completely different degrees of WMSIs determined their different single behaviors.214 When calcining Fe/Al2O3 samples, WMSIs caused the metal particles to become thin and join when they are reduced or used, eventually forming giant particles with sizes surpassing the micromillimeter scale. These particles result in very poor activity and property in CNT growth, additionally to being deactivated by graphene layer deposition. Alternatively, the Fe–Ni/Al2O3 catalyst samples developed a special degree of interaction when calcining, thus maintaining the best size of metal particles stable throughout the subsequent steps of their reduction. Kumar et al. synthesized Ni/Al2O3, Ni/TiO2, Ni/SBA-15 and Ni/MgO catalysts by wet impregnation.215 By controlling the oxidization temperature of the catalyst, the strength of the MSIs was different. The formation of metal-supported compound catalysts at higher reaction temperatures resulted in stronger MSIs, whereas the nickel oxide species at the positioning of SBA-15 and the underlying MgO resulted in stronger MSI for Ni/SBA-15 and Ni/MgO, severally. For the catalysts with Ni/TiO2, Ni/MgO, and Ni/SBA-15, a significant and sophisticated relationship was found between the MSI strength and activity, where the weaker the MSI, the higher the initial alkane series, carbonic acid gas, and water conversion.

5.2 MMSIs

MMSIs are a force between WMSIs and SMSIs, which are expressed as the interaction between metals in zeolite channels. This can be seen from the bonding between metals, for instance, Jia et al. used pyrolysis and in-line catalysis to convert plastic into value-added CNTs.81 The result showed that the decreasing order of nickel catalyst activity was Ni/La, Ni/Sr, Ni/Mg, which was closely associated with the degree of metal compatibility interaction. The sturdy metal organic compound port interaction within the Ni/Mg catalyst is not responsible for the dissolution of a large quantity of metal Ni, leading to a low yield of carbon nanotubes. Alternatively, weaker metal interactions in Ni/Sr do not seem to be conducive to high coverage of Ni NPs, resulting in the coincident growth of carbon nanorods and carbon nanotubes. Because of the optimization of the amount, size, and surface coverage of Ni NPs, the Ni/La catalysts with moderate metal–compatible interactions produced the best CNT yields.

As the research continues to deepen, we know that one of the reasons for the increase in oxygen vacancies is the enhanced transfer of active electrons, and MMSIs are also generated. Grabchenko et al. synthesized Ad/CeO2 catalysts with varying degrees of MSIs for the oxidation of CO at low temperatures and the combustion of soot at low temperatures. The enhanced MSIs increased the number of oxygen vacancies and the extent of cerium reduction, leading to higher catalytic activity in soot combustion and CO oxidation.80 Gao et al. also observed a similar phenomenon. They believed that the excellent activity and stability of the Ru/Mn6Ce1OY catalyst were attributed to the strong interaction between the Ru metal and Mn6Ce1OY support, which led to the aerobic oxidation of 2,5-furanedicarboxylic acid by 5-hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF).216 Contrary to the above experimental results, Ni/Y2O3 catalysts with MMSIs converted to better activity and stability in the CO2 methanation reaction. Also, the catalysts with WMSIs showed poor stability, while that with SMSIs had poor activity.217 According to Jiang et al., for the methane triple reaction, neither strong nor WMSIs were beneficial for the performance of Ni/MgxTi1−xO catalysts. MMSIs contributed the most to the high stability of the catalysts.218

6. Summary and outlook

In summary, heterogeneous catalysts have been developed rapidly, which has highlighted the importance of the fundamental interaction force between various metal supports. The MSIs will not only be effectively explained by charge transfer, metal-to-metal bonding, special topographical structure and coating of metal clusters by carriers, but also the improved dominant metal loading, metal size, temperature, etc. At present, diverse supports have been developed, together with carbon-based materials and non-carbon-based materials. Metals or metal oxides are anchored on the supports in numerous coordination and process ways, benefitting from the variations fashioned between them. An essential element that boosts the stability and activity of chemical reactions is the interaction force between the metal supports. In addition, a mixture of theoretical style and experiments are also projected to accelerate the catalytic applications of MSIs in the OER, HER, ORR, UOR, MOR, CO2RR, etc. Despite the progress made in applying SMSIs in advance carrier materials, further analysis is required; nevertheless, MSIs are used for a variety of applications, and future analysis directions are projected.

Firstly, advances in preparation methods and treatment methods are being considered to broaden the variety and possibilities of catalyst preparation and MSI generation methods. The innovation of preparation methods and treatment methods focus on innovative thinking, according to previous methods, combined with new methods, and further exploration of MSI of catalysts. At present, there are many commonly used methods, such as impregnation, hydrothermal and precipitation methods, and new methods, such as magnetron sputtering on carbon fiber cloth (CFC) are necessary. Wang et al. synthesized FeCoNiCuPd films with an underlying core-cubic (FCC) structure for alkaline oxygen and hydrogen evolution reactions, achieving excellent results.231

Secondly, a variety of factors, such as metal load, size, and temperature, can be controlled to achieve precise MSI control in heterogeneous catalysis. In heterogeneous catalytic materials, factors such as the level of metal loading, the size of metal particles, and the optimal reaction temperature corresponding to the catalytic material will have an effect on the strength of MSIs. The purpose of studying MSIs is to better apply and materials, how to accurately control the size of its force has become particularly important, which has important future research value for whether MSIs can develop by leaps and bounds. At present, there are relatively few reports about WMIs and MMSIs. We know that not all MSIs that promote catalytic performance are as strong as possible. Finding suitable MSIs can not only improve the catalytic performance, but also greatly improve our efficiency in synthesizing effective catalysts and our understanding of the mechanism.

Thirdly, further exploration of the mechanisms of MSI in heterogeneous catalysis. Since SMSIs were proposed in 1978, great strides have been made in the study of the mechanism of strong interactions between metal carriers. With the rapid development of the modern scientific and technological levels, both the means of representation and theoretical research are steadily advancing. The structural information and characteristics of the catalytic material itself are explained, and then the content implied in the material is explained by text and three-dimensional model. At present, the representation methods are becoming more abundant, such as in situ XRD, FTIR and in situ Raman spectroscopy,232 which is of great meaning for the in-depth exploration of the MSI mechanism. To date, the causation between the encapsulation reaction and the charge transfer is not clear and the boundaries of the type of catalyst that MSIs can form need further exploration.

Finally, strengthening the wide applications of MSIs in more heterogeneous chemical fields. The significance of scientific research is very university-level in being able to put it into practice and benefit the human public. At present, MSIs have been used for a long time in many heterogeneous chemical directions, such as OER, HER, ORR, CO2RR, UOR and MOR, but there are many directions that need to be expanded. For example, materials science,233 electronics,234 environment,37 and aerospace.235 In the field, the application of MSIs has also received the attention of many engineers and researchers around the world. It is of great importance to use the MSI effect to develop more catalyst performance regulation strategies and expand their application areas.
