Abstract

Non-precious bifunctional electrocatalysts for the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) play a pivotal role in regenerative fuel cells and rechargeable metalâ€“air batteries. In this contribution, less active CeO2 is introduced for regulating the ORR/OER bifunctional activity of Co3O4@N-doped carbon (NC). The introduced CeO2 plays essential roles in increasing the surface contents of Co2+, Co3+ and N species in CeO2/Co3O4@NC for ORR/OER processes, while still maintaining hierarchical porosity and large specific surface area. Furthermore, the surface acidâ€“base properties for the adsorption of oxygen molecules (for the ORR) and activation of water molecules (for the OER) and the charge transfer efficiency are also improved by the introduced CeO2. As a result, significantly enhanced ORR/OER bifunctional activity is achieved for the optimal and desired electrocatalyst, i.e., CeO2/Co3O4@NC. Its bifunctional activity is comparable or even superior to that of the benchmarked 20 wt% Pt/C (for the ORR) and IrO2 (for the OER), respectively. In the applications of Znâ€“air batteries, the battery with CeO2/Co3O4@NC still exhibits higher performance than the battery with mixed 20 wt% Pt/C + IrO2, due to its outstanding bifunctional activity toward the ORR and OER.

1. Introduction

Development of sustainable and renewable energy is gaining momentum. Although the global installed capacity of renewable energy is rapidly growing, the real challenge comes when integrating renewable energy sources onto the grid due to their intermittent and variable nature. Advanced energy storage and conversion technologies including water splitting, fuel cells and metalâ€“air batteries are thus needed to balance and utilize renewable energy on a large scale. The oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) are the core electrochemical processes in these aforementioned technologies. However, the ORR and OER are kinetically sluggish, limiting the energy conversion efficiency and hindering their practical applications.1 Therefore, highly efficient electrocatalysts are urgently required to promote the ORR and OER.2 Platinum (Pt) based and iridium (Ir) based electrocatalysts are commonly utilized owing to their excellent electrocatalytic activity toward the ORR and OER, respectively.3,4 Unfortunately, the scarcity and high prices of these noble-metal-based electrocatalysts have hampered their large-scale applications. Another drawback associated with Pt and Ir is the poor durability caused by complicated degradation mechanisms during long-term electrochemical operation.5 As a result, non-precious metal electrocatalysts for both the ORR and OER with high efficiency, durability and low cost are highly desired.

For rechargeable metalâ€“air batteries and regenerated fuel cells, a bifunctional oxygen electrocatalyst that can efficiently catalyze the ORR process during discharging and the OER process during charging is of great importance.6 In the past few decades, earth-abundant non-precious metal oxides (Co3O4, MnO2, Fe2O3, etc.) have been widely studied as ORR and/or OER electrocatalysts in alkaline electrolytes due to their low cost and environmental merits.7â€“9 Among them, Co3O4 with mixed valences of Co cations (i.e., Co2+ and Co3+) is considered to be a promising candidate for promoting both the ORR and OER.10,11 The Co2+ ions reside on 1/8 of the tetrahedral â€˜Aâ€™ sites and the Co3+ ions occupy 1/2 of the octahedral â€˜Bâ€™ sites.12,13 The presence of mixed valences of Co cations in the crystal structure could provide donorâ€“acceptor chemisorption sites for the reversible adsorption of oxygen, thus favoring both the ORR and OER processes.14 However, the ORR/OER bifunctional activity of pure Co3O4 is still far from meeting the requirements of replacing precious metal based electrocatalysts due to the insufficient conductivity, relatively low SSAs and porosity.15 More importantly, the intrinsic ORR/OER activity of Co3O4 is lower than that of precious metals.16,17 Therefore, regulating active structures or increasing density of active sites is urgently required, and it is still challenging to further enhance the ORR and OER activity of Co3O4 simultaneously.

Herein, we constructed a Co3O4@NC composite to improve the conductivity, SSAs and porosity by combining the Co3O4 core with a N-doped carbon shell. Most importantly, CeO2 was then introduced to regulate the structure of Co3O4@NC due to its unique features, including high oxygen storage capability (OSC), tunable vacancies and redox properties.18 In our practical work, the Ce2+ and Co2+ cations were first used to coordinate with 1,2,4-triazole and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid, thus constituting the precursor (CoCe) through simple reflux, which ensured the intimate contact of Co and Ce species. The precursor (CoCe) was then annealed at 600 Â°C in N2 to obtain the desired CeO2/Co3O4@NC nanocomposite. Electrochemical and physicochemical characterization techniques demonstrate that although CeO2 exhibits rather low ORR and OER activity, it plays essential roles in boosting the ORR/OER bifunctional activity for CeO2/Co3O4@NC. The bifunctional activity of CeO2/Co3O4@NC is comparable or even superior to that of the benchmarked 20 wt% Pt/C (for the ORR) and IrO2 (for the OER), respectively. When applied in Znâ€“air batteries, the battery with CeO2/Co3O4@NC still exhibits higher performance than the battery with mixed 20 wt% Pt/C + IrO2. The important roles of CeO2 are also discussed specifically in the following text.

2. Experimental

2.1 Synthesis of electrocatalysts

The precursor (CoCe) was prepared using Co(NO3)2 6H2O and Ce(NH4)2(NO3)6 as metal sources. In a typical synthesis, 6.7 mmol of Co(NO3)2 6H2O and 3.3 mmol of Ce(NH4)2(NO3)6 were dissolved in a mixed solvent containing 10 mL of deionized water and 10 mL of N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) as solution A. 1,2,4-Triazole (30 mmol), 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid (10 mmol) and polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP, 2.40 g, Mw = 58000) were dissolved in another mixed solvent containing 20 mL of anhydrous methanol and 110 mL of DMF as solution B. Then, solution A was added dropwise to solution B under vigorous stirring. After stirring for another 30 min, the resulting mixed solution was refluxed at 90 Â°C for 4 h. After naturally cooling to room temperature, the resulting precursor (CoCe) was filtered and washed with anhydrous ethanol several times and then dried at 60 Â°C overnight. For comparison, the reference precursors, the precursor Co and the precursor Ce were prepared with similar procedures. To obtain the electrocatalysts, the three precursors were annealed at 600 Â°C in N2 for 2 h and labeled Co-600N2, CoCe-600N2 and Ce-600N2, respectively. For further comparison, the three precursors were also annealed at 600 Â°C in air for 2 h. The products were referred to as Co-600Air, CoCe-600Air and Ce-600Air, respectively.

3.4 Brief discussion on the important effects of CeO2

The results discussed above demonstrate that the less active CeO2 plays important roles in the enhanced bifunctional activity of CeO2/Co3O4@NC. Fig. 3, Tables 1 and 2 show that Ce-600N2 delivers a much lower ORR/OER apparent activity and mass activity than Co-600N2. Similar results are observed in their counterparts prepared in air (Fig. S4 and S7, Tables S4 and S6â€ ). A much lower ORR and OER activity was observed for Ce-600Air than that for Co-600Air. These results indicate that Ce-600N2 and Ce-600Air are relatively inert toward the ORR/OER compared with Co-600N2 and Co-600Air, respectively. The reported studies have shown promising bifunctional electrocatalytic performance of Co3O4 during the ORR/OER.14,50â€“52 The outstanding ORR/OER bifunctional activity has been ascribed to that the surface Co2+ and Co3+ cations in Co3O4 could serve as the electronic donorâ€“acceptor reduction sites for accelerating ORR and OER processes simultaneously.53â€“57 Thus, based on this hypothesis, increasing the amount of surface exposed Co2+ and Co3+ sites may be a feasible approach to further enhancing the bifunctional activity of Co3O4. The TEM and HRTEM observed coreâ€“shell structures of Co3O4@NC indicate that the Co3O4 may not directly come into contact with the reactants in the reaction. Recent studies regarding coreâ€“shell hybrids have implied that the Co3O4 core may change the arrangement of charges on the surface of NC shell through Mottâ€“Schottky contacts and probably indirectly participate in the ORR/OER reaction.58â€“61 A more detailed study is needed and ongoing to clarify the specific active sites in the Co3O4@NC and CeO2/Co3O4@NC during the ORR/OER. In this study, the total surface content of Co2+ and Co3+ in CoCe-600N2 (âˆ¼11.6 at%) is significantly increased as compared with that of Co-600N2 (âˆ¼1.96 at%) after the introduction of CeO2 (Fig. 1e and Table S3â€ ). Accompanied by this, the metallic Co in Co-600N2, which is usually regarded as the inert and unstable phase in the ORR/OER,62 is transformed to Co3O4 in CoCe-600N2, as confirmed by the XRD (Fig. 1a), Raman (Fig. 1b), HRTEM (Fig. 2b and d) and XPS (Fig. 1e and Table S3â€ ) results. Similarly, the total surface content of Co2+ and Co3+ in CoCe-600Air (âˆ¼12.98 at%) is also higher than that in Co-600Air (âˆ¼10.97 at%) after introducing CeO2. The increased surface contents of Co2+ and Co3+ are in line with the increased oxygen vacancies and the Ce3+/Ce4+ ratios in CoCe-600N2 (âˆ¼9.16 at% and âˆ¼0.26, respectively) and CoCe-600Air (âˆ¼6.22 at% and âˆ¼0.28, respectively) in comparison with those in Ce-600N2 (âˆ¼7.90 at% and âˆ¼0.19, respectively) and Ce-600Air (âˆ¼4.58 at% and âˆ¼0.21, respectively), respectively. This suggests that the increased Co2+ and Co3+ may be associated with the redox processes between Co and Ce species (i.e., Co0 converts into Co2+/Co3+ and Ce4+ converts into Ce3+).63 These results demonstrate that although the ORR/OER activity of CeO2 is rather low, the introduced CeO2 could regulate the surface contents of Co2+ and Co3+ and promote the transformation of metallic Co to the active Co3O4. As a result, the enhanced ORR/OER bifunctional activity of CoCe-600N2 and CoCe-600Air is achieved through the introduction of less active CeO2, as compared with Co-600N2 and Co-600Air, respectively.

It is noteworthy that the pyrolysis in N2 is important to improve the ORR/OER activity, because an inert atmosphere is necessary to form the nitrogen-doped carbon shell and interconnected porous carbon matrices derived from metalâ€“organic precursors, which are beneficial to charge transfer64 and could promote charge delocalization.65 Moreover, as mentioned in Section 3.1, the pyrolysis in N2 is also necessary for achieving hierarchical porosity and large BET SSAs. The numerous micropores and large BET SSAs are favorable for the formation and exposure of abundant active sites for the ORR/OER.66 The presence of mesopores can facilitate the mass transport of the electrolyte and oxygen during the ORR/OER process.67,68 Furthermore, the doped N species in NC including pyridinic-N, pyrrolic-N, quaternary-N and oxidized-N may also act as the active species and lower the overpotentials for both the ORR and OER.29,44,45,69â€“78 All of these features are favorable for accelerating ORR/OER processes. But the inert atmosphere may suppress the formation of Co3O4 and high valence Co species (Co2+ and Co3+). As mentioned in Section 3.1, the main Co species in Co-600N2 is metallic Co, in contrast to Co3O4 in Co-600Air. The total surface content of Co2+ and Co3+ in Co-600N2 (âˆ¼1.96 at%) is naturally much lower than that in Co-600Air (âˆ¼10.97 at%, Table S3â€ ). However, unfortunately, Co-600Air without NC has a quite low BET SSA and low porosity (Table S1â€ ), which is unfavorable for the ORR and OER activity. Fortunately, by introducing CeO2, on the one hand, the formation of the active Co3O4 is promoted (Fig. 1a and b) and the surface contents of Co2+ and Co3+ are also increased (Fig. 1e, Table S3â€ ) even in a N2 atmosphere due to the high OSC and redox properties of CeO2.22,63 On the other hand, the obtained CoCe-600N2 with NC still has hierarchical porosity and large BET SSA (even slightly higher than that of Co-600N2) in addition to the formed Co3O4 and the increased surface contents of Co2+ and Co3+. Furthermore, the surface content of active nitrogen species in Co-600N2 (âˆ¼4.83 at%) has been also increased to âˆ¼7.59 at% in CoCe-600N2 mainly due to the emerged oxidized-N species (Fig. 1g and Table S3â€ ) after the introduction of CeO2, which may facilitate the ORR/OER processes.43,69 Thus, the introduced CeO2 benefits the formation of active Co3O4 having high surface contents of active Co2+, Co3+ and N species during pyrolysis in a N2 atmosphere, without losing the hierarchical porosity and large BET SSA.

As discussed above, the introduced CeO2 plays a role in improving the acidâ€“base properties of Co3O4 (Fig. 1iâ€“j, S1h and iâ€ ), which might be ascribed to the intimate coupling and interactions between Co and Ce species (Fig. 1e and f).70 Recent literature has noted the importance of the surface acidâ€“base properties for the ORR/OER, since the oxygen and water molecules could be regarded as the Lewis base and Lewis acid during the ORR and OER, respectively.70,71,79 The increased basic sites and acidic sites may facilitate the chemisorption of oxygen molecules during the ORR and the activation of water molecules during the OER, respectively.70,71,79 Thus, the ORR/OER bifunctional activity could be enhanced.

Furthermore, the EIS results (Fig. S8â€ ) show that the charge transfer resistances of Co-600N2 and Co-600Air have been lowered by the introduction of CeO2 probably due to the interactions and redox processes between Co3O4 and CeO2.80 As it is known, low charge-transfer efficiency due to low conductivity for an electrocatalyst is bad for ORR/OER processes. In other words, increasing charge-transfer efficiency can facilitate the transport of electrons and protons between electrocatalysts and reactants, thus facilitating the ORR/OER processes.

Accordingly, based on the discussion above, the benefits derived from introducing less active CeO2 for simultaneously boosting the ORR and OER can be summarized as follows: (i) regulating the surface contents of active Co2+, Co3+ and N species; (ii) facilitating the formation of active Co3O4, having high surface contents of active Co2+, Co3+ and N species during pyrolysis in N2, while still maintaining hierarchical porosity and large BET SSA; (iii) improving the surface acidâ€“base properties for the facilitated adsorption of oxygen molecules (for the ORR) and activation of water molecules (for the OER); (iv) increasing the charge transfer efficiency. These favorable factors cause synergistic effects and thus result in significantly enhanced ORR and OER bifunctional activity in the optimized CoCe-600N2 (i.e., CeO2/Co3O4@NC).

4. Conclusions

In summary, the less active CeO2 was introduced for regulating the ORR/OER bifunctional activity of Co3O4@NC in this work. The essential roles of introduced CeO2 were figured out. Specifically, the introduced CeO2 can increase the surface contents of Co2+ and Co3+ in both CeO2/Co3O4 and CeO2/Co3O4@NC. Additionally, the introduced CeO2 benefits the formation of active Co3O4, having active Co2+, Co3+ and N species with high surface contents during pyrolysis in a N2 atmosphere, while maintaining hierarchical porosity and large BET SSA. Furthermore, the surface acidâ€“base property for the adsorption of oxygen molecules (for the ORR) and activation of water molecules (for the OER) can be enhanced by the introduced CeO2. The charge transfer efficiency is also improved by the introduced CeO2. As a result, significantly increased ORR/OER bifunctional activity is achieved in the optimal CoCe-600N2 (i.e., CeO2/Co3O4@NC). Its bifunctional activity is comparable or even superior to that of the benchmarked 20 wt% Pt/C (for the ORR) and IrO2 (for the OER), respectively. When applied in Znâ€“air batteries, the battery with CeO2/Co3O4@NC still exhibits higher performance than the battery with mixed 20 wt% Pt/C + IrO2, due to its outstanding ORR/OER bifunctional activity. This work paves an efficient way to optimize the bifunctional activity of oxygen electrocatalysts via the introduction of less active materials.
