Abstract

Tungsten oxide, as a typical functional transition metal oxide semiconductor, has been widely and intensively investigated for various applications. This review focuses on the recent progress of nanostructured tungsten oxide thin film devices, ranging from optoelectronics and ionics to iontronics. The recent research results on structural, defect, and ion intercalation aspects of tungsten oxide are first introduced in the review, as they are fundamental to the understanding and design of tungsten oxide devices. This part stresses the correlation between these aspects of tungsten oxide and its electronic structures. Then nanostructured tungsten oxide thin film devices are reviewed, especially photodetectors, electrochromic and supercapacitive devices, memristors and electrolyte-gated transistors. The figures of merit of the reported tungsten oxide photodetectors are comprehensively compared, analysed and summarised. New fabrication methods, novel configurations, multiple functions, and mechanism investigations of tungsten oxide electrochromic and supercapacitive devices are emphasized. In the exciting development of emerging iontronics, which bridges electronics and ionics, the operating mechanisms and performance of tungsten oxide-based memristors and electrolyte-gated transistors are underlined. The development trends, challenges, and opportunities of nanostructured tungsten oxide thin film devices are also analyzed and proposed.

1. Introduction

Tungsten oxide is one of the typically and widely explored n-type transition metal oxide semiconductors. The interest in tungsten oxide can be traced back to the 18th century for wolframite, and this interest is still intense even after more than two hundred years owing to the structural diversity of tungsten oxide and its derivatives.1 Tungsten oxide possesses a widely tunable optical bandgap of ca. 2.5–3.7 eV, and this characteristic has led to the intensive investigation of tungsten oxide for photo(electro)catalysts, photochromics and phototherapy, which are timely and thoroughly reviewed.1–17

The structural diversity and defect variety of tungsten oxide as a functional film also initiate widespread applications in fields from optoelectronics and ionics to iontronics, such as photodetectors,18 electrochromic devices,19 supercapacitors,20 memristors,21 and electrolyte-gated transistors,22 which hold great promise and opportunities for next-generation devices.

In the aspect of optoelectronics, tungsten oxide has generally emerged in the field of UV photodetection as a promising candidate.23 A fast response time and high photoresponsivity have been achieved in tungsten oxide UV photodetectors in recent years.24,25 However, the functionality of tungsten oxide for UV detection is rarely reviewed and summarised.

The ionic property of tungsten oxide has long been sought for electrochromic devices.26 Due to the configuration and mechanism similarity, tungsten oxide supercapacitors also attract considerable attention. Substantial progress has been made recently including an in-depth understanding of working mechanisms, novel device configurations and applications.27–29 The development in these fields is rapid and need to be timely reviewed.

Based on the excellent electronic and ionic properties, the concept of iontronics offers a fresh new approach for device design, which brings more functionalities to tungsten oxide. The exploration of tungsten oxide memristors and electrolyte-gated transistors represents the most exciting frontline in the development of novel tungsten oxide devices for the next generation.30–32

This review offers a progress report on nanostructured tungsten oxide thin film devices from optoelectronics and ionics to iontronics. The review first introduces recent findings on the structural, defect, and ion intercalation aspects of tungsten oxide, which are fundamental to the understanding of tungsten oxide devices and the development of novel devices. The review focuses on reviewing nanostructured tungsten oxide thin film devices from optoelectronics and ionics to iontronics with emphasis on the recent work of photodetectors, electrochromic and supercapacitive devices, memristors, and electrolyte-gated transistors. The development trends, challenges, and opportunities are analysed and proposed. We envisage that the review will be beneficial not only for research on tungsten oxide but also for that on optoelectronic, ionic and iontronic devices and other functional metal oxide semiconductors such as TiO2, MoO3 and V2O5.

2. Structural and morphological diversity

Tungsten trioxide crystals consist of WO6 octahedra linked by corner and edge sharing. The corner-sharing phases account for most of the tungsten trioxide phases, such as monoclinic II (ε-WO3), triclinic (δ-WO3), monoclinic I (γ-WO3), orthorhombic (β-WO3), tetragonal (α-WO3), and cubic WO3, as shown in Fig. 1.33 The different phases can be mutually transformed under different temperatures, while γ-WO3 appears to be the most common and stable phase at room temperature. As the ε-WO3 phase only exists at very low temperature, monoclinic WO3 normally refers to γ-WO3 unless specified.
Fig. 1 Tilt patterns and stability temperature domains of the different polymorphs of WO3. Reproduced with permission.33 Copyright 2000, International Union of Crystallography.

Another commonly studied tungsten trioxide is hexagonal WO3(h-WO3), which has aroused fast-growing interest in electrochemical applications.34 It is stable at room temperature and can be transformed into the monoclinic phase after annealing at a temperature of over 400 °C. The h-WO3 phase also constitutes of corner-sharing octahedral units but forming hexagonal and trigonal tunnels with six- and three-grouped units at the ab plane and repeated layers coordinated by corner-oxygen along the c axis. This structure offers a high surface area originating from 3.67 Å diameter hexagonal tunnels along the (001) direction, which creates easy access for H+ and Li+ and facilitates ion transfer (Fig. 2).35

In addition to the aforementioned tungsten trioxide structures, tungsten trioxide hydrate is also a big group of the tungsten oxide family. Tungsten trioxide hydrate includes a variety of crystal structures and phases with different water contents, from orthorhombic WO3 2H2O to different semi-dehydrated structures and phases, and all can be transformed to monoclinic WO3 with heating over 400 °C (Fig. 3).36 It is noteworthy that most of the tungsten trioxide hydrates possess layered structures.

Tungsten oxide can be synthesized into many different morphologies like other transition metal oxides, which will not be comprehensively introduced here.11,14,37 Moreover, many synthesis techniques from other transition metal oxides were also adapted to produce various tungsten oxides, such as the sol–gel,38 hydro/solvothermal,39–41 exfoliation,42 sputtering,43 chemical vapour deposition,44 and atomic layer deposition techniques.45–47 We specifically introduce three promising morphological engineering approaches for functionalization of tungsten oxide: two-dimensional (2D) flakes, quantum dots (QDs) and porous structures. 2D tungsten oxide should be particularly mentioned, as it holds great potential for tuning the electronic structures of tungsten oxide.48–50 Compared to the bulk counterpart, 2D tungsten oxide has a larger bandgap and more positive conduction and valence band edges due to the quantum confinement effect.51–53 The annealing process of 2D tungsten oxide could also result in the variation of band structures (Fig. 4a and b).54 Both the hydration and crystallinity contribute greatly to the electronic properties of 2D tungsten oxide.55 It's worth noting that not much work has been carried out so far on 2D tungsten oxide compared to other common morphologies due to the intrinsic 3D network structures of tungsten oxide. However, high-quality 2D tungsten oxide can be exfoliated from layered tungsten oxide hydrates. The work on tungsten oxide QDs is even less.5,56–58 Wang et al. reported tungsten oxide QDs hydrothermally prepared with an average size of 3.25 ± 0.25 nm (Fig. 4c and d). The tungsten oxide QDs can be dispersed in aqueous medium and exhibit excellent photoluminescence properties.59 The strong capability of quantum dots to modulate the optical properties endorses their applications in tungsten oxide optoelectronic devices.

The pore-rich morphology of tungsten oxide is of critical importance for electrochemical applications. Liu et al. synthesized pore-rich WO3 ultrathin nanosheets with a ca. 3.8 nm thickness (Fig. 5a and b).60 The charge transfer resistance (240 Ω) for pore-rich WO3 was decreased dramatically compared to those of nonporous nanosheet (680 Ω) and bulk (1200 Ω) WO3, respectively. The flat band potential and carrier density were derived from the Mott–Schottky plots (Fig. 5c). The positive slopes in the plots indicated the n-type nature of the WO3 samples. The donor concentration of 1.34 × 1020 cm−3 was three times higher than that of bulk WO3, promoting the charge transport efficiency. From the band structure (Fig. 5d and e), the flat band potential position was approximately equal to the position of the conduction band for the n-type semiconductor. The negatively shifted flat band potential depressed the charge carrier recombination by enhancing band bending at the surface. The space charge layer thickness was calculated to be 4.17 nm at 1.0 V versus Ag/AgCl, exceeding the thickness of the WO3 nanosheets, proving the full depletion of nanosheets and thus promoting efficient photoexcited carrier separation under an applied voltage (Fig. 5f).60

3. Various defects and defect-induced phenomena

The electronic properties of tungsten oxide are strongly dependent on its stoichiometry, i.e., the concentration of oxygen deficiency. A higher concentration of oxygen vacancies induces semiconducting-to-metallic properties. A vacancy concentration of 4% (WO3−x, x = 0.12) results in the metallic state of tungsten oxide, as compared to the semiconducting state for tungsten oxide with a vacancy concentration of 2% (WO3−x, x = 0.06).61 Oxygen vacancies in tungsten oxide crystals have an anisotropic nature: W6+/Vo(2e−)/W6+ with two trapped electrons along the x-direction, W4+/Vo(0e−)/W6+ along the y-direction, and W5+/Vo(0e−)/W5+ along the z-direction (Fig. 6a).61 The anisotropic oxygen vacancies give rise to the anisotropic electronic properties of tungsten oxide.

Oxygen vacancies with charge states of 0 and +1 were proposed to create optical transition levels within the bandgap, while the defect states of doubly charged oxygen vacancies lie in the CB band, thus leading to different light absorption spectra (Fig. 6b).61 Additionally, the migration of oxygen vacancies was also calculated to be anisotropic.62 More calculation and experimental work on the anisotropic effects of oxygen vacancies in WO3 needs to be conducted to give more detailed information on the variation of the electronic and optical properties.63–67

The surface of monoclinic WO3 was predicted to have 2D delocalized excess charges at the very first layer due to the surface vacancies naturally originating from the termination of the crystal structure (Fig. 7).68 The 2D excess charges were concluded to be 2D large polarons with a diameter of ca. 1 nm, and their localization is dependent on the vacancy concentration on the WO3 surface (Fig. 7b). The polaronic state on the surface could promote the formation of reactive OH˙ at the WO3/water interface, which is conducive to water catalysis. Density functional theory calculations proved that doping Mo atoms on the (200) surface of tungsten oxide also favours water catalysis.69

The defects in tungsten oxide can induce many phenomena, such as localized surface plasmon resonances (LSPRs). LSPR is the phenomenon that free charge carriers dielectrically confined to a nanoparticle at sufficient density participate in resonant and collective oscillations. LSPR in transition metal oxides arising from the unique character of the outer-d valence electrons is more appealing than that in noble metals due to the adjustable LSPR energy from tuning the doping or stoichiometry degree.70 The electrical and optical properties of WO3−x with 0 < x < 0.1 result from the localized electrons involved in polarons, which are quasiparticles consisting of a charge carrier and its polarization field in a lattice. As x > 0.1, WO3 undergoes an insulator-to-metal transition due to the overlapping of the localized polaronic wave functions, forming delocalized states of electrons.71

However, the investigation of WO3−x at the nanoscale is still controversial; WO2.72 nanowires were proved to be semiconducting and the blue colour was due to polaron hopping, whereas WO2.83 nanowires appeared to be metallic and the blue colour was proved to originate from strong LSPR with an absorption peak centred at ca. 900 nm (Fig. 8a).72 LSPR was also observed in ordered mesoporous WO2.83 featuring an absorption peak at ca. 650 nm with a free charge density of 9.79 × 1021 cm−3 calculated according to the Drude model (Fig. 8b).73 The absorption peak wavelength of W18O49 originating from the LSPR effect could be tuned by the electromagnetic oscillation coupling between the metallic ultrafine W18O49 nanowires.74 WO18O49 nanowire bundles with highly oriented ultrafine nanowires showed a blue shift of the LSPR peak as the nanowires aggregated into thicker bundles. The LSPR peak centred within the IR range (1025 nm) can shift to the visible light range (561 nm) (Fig. 8c), which could be beneficial for the sensitivity improvement of non-noble metal surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy.

Actually, many behaviours of tungsten oxide are attributed to its defects (including ion intercalation, which will be specifically elaborated in the next section), even at a very low concentration. The negative differential resistance (NDR) phenomenon, in which the current decreases upon the increase of voltage, was found in WO3 and WO3·H2O.75 WO3·H2O and WO3 exhibited NDR peaks at 2.4 and −2.3 V with a peak to valley ratio of ca. 1.3 and ca. 1.5 at positive and negative bias, respectively (Fig. 9a and b). The phenomenon was explained by the reversible charge trapping/detrapping mechanism at the bridged oxygen atoms (Fig. 9c). The generation and annihilation processes of the defects were explained by band theory, where an applied bias matching the valence band and conduction band produces increased current flow, whilst an applied bias mismatching the band structure causes decreased current flow. However, this phenomenon has not been thoroughly investigated due to the complexity of defects and their low concentration. Whether it is related to intrinsic defects or voltage-induced defects is still unknown.

Lattice mismatch-induced strain could also result in the formation of an oxygen vacancy gradient in tungsten oxide perpendicular to the interface as proved in WO3/SrTiO3 with comparison to WO3/LaAlO3.76 Water molecules are preferably absorbed on the WO3 surface in an undissociated form as a result of the low-coordinated W ions acting as Lewis acid sites on the surface.77 Many other defect-controllable phenomena via ion intercalation will be introduced in the following section along with the resultant tungsten bronzes.

4. Ion intercalation and tungsten bronzes

An insulator to metal transition could be induced through two main approaches: oxygen deficiency as introduced above, through either surface metallization from loss of surface oxygen, or oxygen migration from the interior to the surface, and ion intercalation as introduced in this section, through the formation of tungsten bronzes.78–80

Ion intercalation in hexagonal tungsten oxide hydrate was verified to be more complex than other phases.34 Proton intercalation in hexagonal WO3·0.6H2O started from the insertion of water molecules upon its immersion in the electrolyte at open-circuit voltage and during initial cycling. Subsequently, 0.25 protons per WO3 unit intercalated simultaneously accompanied by the expelling of 0.25 lattice waters. Later, 0.30 protons are inserted. After this stage, 0.17 H3O+ were inserted. The series of different intercalation stages resulted in consecutive anisotropic structural changes. The lattice first contracts along the c-axis but later expanded along the ab plane horizontally.

Ion intercalation leads to the formation of various tungsten bronzes. Superconducting properties were found in tungsten bronzes. The KxWO3 tetragonal tungsten bronze has the highest superconducting transition temperature of 2.1 K at x = 0.38, while the transition temperature decreased as the K content increased (Fig. 12).83 The hexagonal tungsten bronze InxWO3 has a similar pattern with the highest transition temperature at the lowest ion intercalation level, whereas the transition temperature of the cubic tungsten bronze was almost independent of the ion intercalation level.83 The superconducting properties in other tungsten bronzes AxWO3 (A = Rb, Cs) were also studied.84

5. Optoelectronic devices

The tungsten trioxide crystal has a relatively large intrinsic bandgap of around 2.7 eV, which causes its photosensing of almost only UV light and nearly no response to visible light. The relatively wide bandgap makes tungsten trioxide very suitable for visible-blind UV photodetection. Photodetectors based on tungsten oxide are reviewed in this section, and the status and problems in the development of tungsten oxide-based photodetectors are analysed.

A WO3 UV photodetector based on few-layer nanosheets was fabricated via chemical vapour deposition (Fig. 13).86 The photodetector has a 13 μm channel with ultrathin WO3 nanosheets of a 12 nm thickness in between. A fast response of 40 ms, high responsivity of 293 A W−1, ON/OFF ratio of 2000, and EQE of 997% were achieved.

A UV photodetector assembled from a single polycrystalline WO3 nanobelt exhibited an extremely high responsivity of 2.6 × 105 A W−1 and an external quantum efficiency of 8.1 × 107 % at 405 nm (Fig. 15).25 The photodetector also showed a high ON/OFF ratio up to 1000 with a photocurrent of ca. 12 nA and a very low dark current of 12 pA. The low dark current was attributed to the crystalline grain boundary-trapping photoresponse mechanism in the synthesized polycrystalline WO3 nanobelt, which was different from the typical absorbed oxygen-trapping photoconduction mechanism in single crystalline WO3. In a typical single-crystalline WO3 photoconductor, the photocurrent arises from the detrapped electrons upon illumination, which were trapped by the absorbed oxygen molecules on the surface in the dark (Fig. 15d). This mechanism normally results in a high dark current due to thermal activation. As compared to the typical mechanism, the boundaries of the crystalline grains in polycrystalline WO3 offer a higher energy barrier for carrier transport in the dark, leading to a much lower dark current. Upon UV illumination, the photoexcited holes migrate to the grain boundaries under the internal electric field and lower the barrier height, facilitating electron transport (Fig. 15e).

The nanojunction-interlinked structure with nanoparticle size comparable to the Debye length can take full advantage of the electron-depletion effect with minimized charge recombination, and thus leads to excellent photodetection performance.

6. Ionic devices

Compared to the application of photodetectors, tungsten oxides as active materials for electrochromic devices have been through a very long history. Even today, it is still one of the main research topics in the exploration of high-performance electrochromic devices.123 Electrochromic devices are based on ion intercalation/deintercalation, which is very similar to electrical energy storage processes. Therefore, tungsten oxide is also utilized for supercapacitor and battery applications with even more quickly growing interest after the interlayer water molecules were identified to facilitate ion transfer. In this section, the controversial electrochromic mechanisms are introduced first. In the main part, through the selection of recent high-impact work, new fabrication methods, novel configurations, multiple functions, and mechanism investigations of electrochromic and supercapacitive devices are stressed.

Despite the considerable amount of work that has been done on tungsten oxide-based electrochromic devices over the past several decades, the chromogenic mechanism remains controversial.124 Several mechanisms have been proposed such as intervalence charge transfer (IVCT), polaronic absorption, interband excitations, and transitions from the valence band to split off the W5+ state.125 In the improved IVCT model (Fig. 20), V0o (W4+ or 2W5+) defect states are created inside or near the valence band possessing two electrons. V+o (W5+) color centers form once V0o defect states lose one electron in this energy level and are pushed into the bandgap due to the coulombic repulsion of the neighbouring displaced W ions. If two electrons of V0o defect states are delocalized, W6+ states form in the conduction band. The optical transition between V+o (W5+) and V2+o (W6+) contributes to the coloration phenomena.125

With the high similarity of its working mechanism, tungsten oxide was also employed for supercapacitor applications due to its low cost, abundant ion channel structures, and multiple oxidation states.145–150 However, its poor conductivity renders unsatisfying electrochemical performance. To summarize, the development of ionic devices based on tungsten oxides is at quite a high level. Nevertheless, compared to the performance of ionic devices based on other materials (for instance, Co3O4, V2O5 and MnO2 for supercapacitors,157 and fast-growing organic electrochromic materials158), tungsten oxide-based ionic devices lack very strong competitiveness. To break the development bottleneck, the following aspects need to be considered: (1) the water content in tungsten oxide hydrates and multivalent ions can be further explored to improve tungsten oxide-based ionic devices. (2) In order to meet the trend of flexible devices, tungsten oxide films are required to be very thin and may consist of ultrathin tungsten oxide nanosheets or quantum dots, the large-scale synthesis methods of which need to be perfected. A configuration change for supercapacitor and electrochromic devices based on tungsten oxide is highly demanded. (4) The various properties of tungsten oxide enable its multiple functions. Bifunctional electrochromic supercapacitors are intensively investigated. Nevertheless, its photoelectric, gas sensing, insulating-to-metallic transition, catalysis, and other properties have not been merged into ionic devices to reach synergistic effects and to achieve smarter applications. There are still plenty of opportunities for exploration in this direction.

7. Iontronic devices

Since the concept of iontronics was proposed, tungsten oxide has found a new platform to exhibit its versatilities. It has been applied to memristors and electrolyte-gated transistors, which are reviewed in this section.

Flexible resistive random access memory (RRAM) devices were fabricated based on WO3−x nanoparticles (NP) prepared via anodic treatment on a 40 nm W thin film (Fig. 33).32 The Cu/WO3−x NP/ITO configuration exhibited a bipolar switching characteristic with an ON/OFF ratio of ca. 105, a retention time of more than 5 × 105 s, and bending endurance over 103 cycles. The working mechanism was proposed to be reversible Cu oxidation/reduction and Cu ion migration under bias voltage control, which leads to the formation/annihilation of conductive filaments and thus causes high/low-resistance states (HRS/LRS) (Fig. 33d).

In summary, the research on tungsten oxide iontronic devices is currently focusing on the fundamental mechanisms and basic performance. The IL-gated WO3 transistor, which is found to mainly work through electrostatic accumulation and depletion, is analogous to classic field effect transistors.171 However, most of the iontronic devices based on tungsten oxide operate through the ion migration mechanism. The concept of iontronics offers a fresh-new perspective for tungsten oxide devices. Tungsten oxide has been proved to be very sensitive to defects. Traditionally, the defect profiles in tungsten oxide and other semiconductor-based electronic devices are usually treated as “static” states, which are intentionally introduced via doping or other processes. Moreover, the functionalities of the semiconductor devices are traditionally modulated by an alterable electric field, magnetic field, and stress (Fig. 40a).172 The diffusion of defects, primarily oxygen vacancies in oxide semiconductors or other ions (doped or naturally occurring), is always overlooked due to the relatively large semiconductor size, or seen as a detriment if the rate of diffusion is over the threshold. However, as electronic devices enter into the nanoscale level, the diffusion length of the defects under routine controlling and environment parameters such as electric fields, magnetic fields, and temperature, is comparable to the device scale. The variation of defects could, therefore, induce new functionalities such as emerging resistive switching in memristors. The defect profile, captured with the chemical potential as the dynamic variable, could lead to the discovery of more novel phenomena and functionalities via interacting with electric fields, stress and other physical variables (Fig. 40b).172 Along with this opportunity, techniques for dynamically and atomically manipulating the concentration and profile of vacancies and ions in oxide semiconductors are highly demanded. How to multi-functionalize iontronic devices and introduce new physical stimuli into the system are new directions worthy to explore. Furthermore, the stability of new devices in ambient air also needs to be carefully evaluated.

8. Conclusion and outlook

In this review, the diversity of tungsten oxide in terms of structure, morphology, defects, and ion intercalation is introduced first with an emphasis on their correlation with electronic structures. Tungsten oxide thin film devices from optoelectronics and ionics to iontronics are reviewed, highlighting the recent progress in photodetectors, electrochromic and supercapacitive devices, memristors and electrolyte-gated transistors. The development status, trends, challenges, and opportunities are summarised, analysed, and proposed. It is clear that tungsten oxide's scale, water content and defects are of great importance for its electronic and electrochemical properties. The research on different types of tungsten oxide thin film devices is at different levels. The development of tungsten oxide optoelectronic devices is still at an early stage, the investigation of ionic devices meets a bottleneck, and the work on emerging iontronic devices is thriving.

Considering the challenges and opportunities based on the development of tungsten oxide thin film devices, the following aspects are worthy to be further explored:

(1) Defect profile and water content. More microscopic and other investigation approaches are required to establish quantitative relations between the defect (oxygen vacancies and ion intercalation) profile and electronic behaviours in tungsten oxide nanofilms. Besides, the effects of the water content in tungsten oxide nanofilms on the defect migration and electron mobility are also unclear. Moreover, the introduction of other stimuli, such as external magnetic fields and forces, to manipulate the defect profile in tungsten oxide nanofilms could be intriguing.

(2) Electrolyte. The performance of many tungsten oxide devices is highly electrolyte-dependent. Developing higher quality and effective electrolytes is one possible solution, such as adoption of multivalent ions. Replacing the normal electrolyte with new forms of ion reservoirs may be also possible. However, little effort has been made in this respect.

(3) Scale. Ionic devices are commonly fabricated at the eye-visible scale. If the active materials and devices scale down to eye-invisible size, tungsten oxide may exceed other materials in performance and new phenomena may be disclosed. Considering that few-layered tungsten oxides and quantum dots have not been intensively investigated, it poses great opportunities for nanoscale research.

(4) Configuration. Since the invention of electrochromic devices, the configuration of the devices has not been apparently changed as well as the supercapacitor configuration. Innovation in terms of the configuration could lead to fresh-new devices with new functionalities.

(5) Multiple functions. Tungsten oxide possesses various properties even within a single tungsten oxide piece with no more extra modifications. The multiple properties existing simultaneously, however, are normally intentionally overlooked or eliminated to acquire a single stable signal as wished. For example, one two-terminal device based on a piece of tungsten oxide could respond to light, temperature, humidity, gas, solutions and other physical and chemical stimuli at the same time. Further integration of tungsten oxide with other materials such as heterostructures based on WO3/2D layered materials may yield more functionalities. With exquisite design and multiple controls, high-performance multifunctional (not limited to two functions) devices based on tungsten oxides could be achieved.
