Photovoltaic effect in a few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure

Chulho Park a, Ngoc Thanh Duong ab, Seungho Bang ab, Duc Anh Nguyen a, Hye Min Oh *a and Mun Seok Jeong *ab
aDepartment of Energy Science, Sungkyunkwan University (SKKU), Suwon 16419, Korea. E-mail: ohmin@skku.edu; mjeong@skku.edu

First published on 9th October 2018

Abstract

Two-dimensional transition-metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs) are notable materials owing to their flexibility, transparency, and appropriate bandgaps. Because of their unique advantages, TMDC pn diodes have been studied for next-generation electronics and optoelectronics. However, their efficiency must be increased for commercialization. In this study, we demonstrated a heterostructure composed of few-layer ReS2 and WSe2. This few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure exhibits a pn junction and an nn junction in different gate-bias regimes. In the pn junction regime, the heterostructure shows outstanding rectification behavior. Additionally, we identify three carrier-transfer mechanisms direct tunneling, FowlerNordheim tunneling, and the space charge region depending on the drain bias. Furthermore, the photovoltaic effect is observed in this few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure. As a result, a high fill factor ( 0.56), power conversion ( 1.5%), and external quantum efficiency ( 15.3%) were obtained. This study provides new guidelines for flexible optoelectronic devices.

Introduction

The conventional diode structure is extensively employed by Si, group IIIV and other semiconductors.14 The diode structure is used for various devices such as rectifiers, photovoltaic devices, and light-emitting diodes.14 Bulk Si and IIIV semiconductors have high-quality electronics and optoelectronics but are not flexible or transparent.14 Therefore, it is difficult to apply them to next-generation devices such as flexible displays and transparent solar cells. Unlike ordinary semiconductors, two-dimensional transition-metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs) have excellent electrical and optical properties, besides being flexible and transparent.58 Thus, TMDCs are emerging semiconductors in future electronics and optoelectronics.

In photovoltaic devices, the organicinorganic hybrid perovskite material, which has been studied extensively in recent years, has a high efficiency, flexibility, and transparency.911 However, the organicinorganic hybrid perovskite material is not eco-friendly and degrades rapidly because it is composed of Pb and organic materials.1214 In contrast, TMDCs are flexible and transparent, as well as inorganic materials. Therefore, photovoltaic devices with TMDCs are expected to be more eco-friendly and longer-lasting than organicinorganic hybrid perovskite solar cells. Despite their unique advantages, most TMDC heterostructures exhibit a low fill factor (FF, 0.220.5) and low power-conversion efficiency (PCE, 0.0050.9%) in photovoltaic devices.1519 Owing to their low efficiency, numerous studies have been conducted to improve their efficiency (19%) by using a heterostructure comprising TMDCs and bulk materials.2022 In these cases, however, the process sequence is complicated, and it is difficult to make the device flexible and transparent. Consequently, it is essential to achieve a high-efficiency device comprising a TMDC heterostructure.

Recently, it was reported that a ReS2 field-effect transistor has very high photoresponsivity (Rph = 88600 A W1) and external quantum efficiency (EQE = 3168%).2325 Also, it has a direct bandgap in not only monolayer form but also bulk form, unlike other TMDCs.24,25 According to these results, ReS2 exhibits potential for electronics and optoelectronics.

In this study, we examined the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure. Regarding the electrical properties, the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure shows different characteristics, such as a pn junction and an nn junction depending on the gate-bias regime. We explained three carrier-transfer mechanisms in the pn junction, depending on the drain bias. Additionally, we observed the optoelectrical properties of the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure under a negative gate bias (Vgs = 50 V). A high FF ( 0.56), PCE ( 1.5%) and EQE ( 15.3%) were obtained.

Results and discussion

Fig. 1(a) shows a schematic diagram of the ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure. In the experiment, we used exfoliated ReS2 and WSe2 flakes. The ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure comprises a ReS2 bottom layer and a WSe2 top layer. As shown in the side view and the top view, the ReS2 has a 1T structure, and the WSe2 has a 2H structure.2429 The band gap of ReS2 and WSe2 is around 1.5 eV and 1.2 eV, respectively.2426,28,29 Electrodes were made on each layer side. On the ReS2 side, Cr/Au was used for the n-type electrode, as Cr has a work function of 4.5 eV.29 We used Au electrodes on the WSe2 side. The Au work function is 5.1 eV (see Methods).29

To measure the topography and thickness of ReS2 and WSe2, we performed optical microscopy (OM) and atomic force microscopy (AFM) measurements. Fig. 1(b) shows an OM image and an AFM image of the ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure. In the OM image, the yellow dotted area and red dotted area correspond to the ReS2 and WSe2 flakes, respectively. Through the OM image, we confirmed that the ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure is well fabricated. Additionally, we measured the thickness of the blue dotted area in the OM image via AFM. The thicknesses of ReS2 and WSe2 were 7 and 5 nm, respectively. As a result, we confirmed the clean and smooth surface of the entire area shown in Fig. 1(b).

Fig. 1(c) shows the Raman spectrum of few-layer ReS2. On the ReS2 side, Raman scattering peaks were observed at 150, 200, and 308 cm1, corresponding to E2g, Ag-like, and Eg-like modes, respectively.24,26 ReS2 has the characteristic that there is no shift of the Raman scattering peaks with thickness dependence.24 The Raman spectrum of few-layer WSe2 shows E2g at 248 cm1, 2LA(M) at 256 cm1, and B2g at 307 cm1 in Fig. 1(d).27,30 Interestingly, the B2g mode of the WSe2 Raman scattering is observed in the bilayer or few-layer structures, but not in the monolayer.27 Additionally, we observed the Raman scattering of the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure region (Fig. 1(e)). None of the Raman scattering peaks for few-layer ReS2 and WSe2 in this study shift at all. Therefore, we assume that strain or carrier coupling (charge transfer) effects at the interface of the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure are negligible.31,32 By the way, in previous reports, the interlayer interaction (strain or carrier coupling effect) occurs between stacking layers according to the relative angle.3336 Although we did not observe this phenomenon, if the stacking angle is changed, it may affect the interlayer interaction.

To observe the electrical properties of few-layer ReS2 and WSe2, we fabricated few-layer ReS2 and WSe2 transistors (Fig. 2(a) and (b), respectively). Few-layer ReS2 and WSe2 were directly exfoliated onto a SiO2/Si wafer. The thicknesses of the few-layer ReS2 and WSe2 were 3.3 and 3.5 nm, respectively (see ESI, Fig. S1). The inset in Fig. 2(a) shows an OM image of the few-layer ReS2 transistor. Cr/Au (5/50 nm) was used for the electrodes of the few-layer ReS2 transistor. Fig. 2(a) shows the drainsource current (Ids) and gatesource bias (Vgs) dependence of the few-layer ReS2 transistor under a drainsource bias (Vds) of 3 V. As indicated by the results, we observed an n-type characteristic and a high current on/off ratio (>7 107). Fig. 2(b) shows the gate dependence of the few-layer WSe2 transistor at Vds = 3 V, and the inset presents an OM image of the few-layer WSe2 transistor. Here, Au (50 nm) was used for the electrodes. The few-layer WSe2 transistor exhibits an ambipolar characteristic with an off-state around Vgs = 30 V.

Fig. 2(c) shows the gate dependence of the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure at Vds = 3 V. We observed three sections along the current flow. (i) In the range of 60 V < Vgs < 40 V, the current increases ( 109 A) until Vgs 50 V and then decreases ( 1011 A). (ii) Around Vgs = 35 V, the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure does not permit current flow. (iii) Finally, the current increases again in the range of 35 V < Vgs, and the current is saturated in the range of 0 V < Vgs ( 106 A). To analyze the current flow in detail, we performed current mapping depending on Vgs and Vds, as shown in Fig. 2(d). Ids was expressed in logarithmic form, in the range of 1017 to 104 A. As shown in Fig. 2(d), the few-layer ReS2 channel has an off-state around Vgs = 60 V. Thus, the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure has a low current under both forward and reverse bias. This structure exhibited pn diode characteristics in the range of 60 V < Vgs < 30 V. It has a high current under the forward bias and a low current under the reverse bias, because few-layer ReS2 has an n-type characteristic and few-layer WSe2 has a p-type characteristic in this region. Fig. S2 shows the rectification behavior with respect to the gate bias in detail. Around Vgs = 30 V, the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure does not permit the current flow, because the few-layer WSe2 is in the off-state. Therefore, the current increase and decrease in regions (i) and (ii) of Fig. 2(c) can be explained by the off-state. Above Vgs = 30 V, the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure shows the nn junction characteristic, because few-layer ReS2 and WSe2 have n-type characteristics. Therefore, it has a high current under a forward and a reverse bias (region (iii) in Fig. 2(c) and (d)).

We further analyzed the p-n regime in the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure. The IdsVds curve was measured at Vgs = 50 V (Fig. 3(a)). The few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure shows outstanding rectification behavior because few-layer ReS2 has the n-type characteristic and few-layer WSe2 has the p-type characteristic under the negative gate bias (Vgs = 50 V). The forward/reverse current ratio is 7 102 as shown in Fig. 3(a). Our few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure has a comparable rectification ratio to other TMDC diode structures such as MoS2/WSe2, ReS2/ReSe2, MoTe2/SnSe2, and MoS2/MoTe2.18,19,37,38

To estimate the detailed carrier-transfer mechanisms of the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure, Fig. 3(b) is fitted by a logarithmic function of Ids and Vds. The blue line indicates the reverse bias current depending on the applied bias. The few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure has a low current under the reverse bias because the depletion region and band barrier are increased depending on the applied reverse bias. Therefore, the carrier transfer is limited by the high barrier at the interface. The generated carriers related to the low current are the minority carriers of the few-layer ReS2 and WSe2.32 The related band diagrams are shown in Fig. 3(d). The red line (forward bias) in Fig. 3(b) shows the three sections depending on the applied bias due to the inversion of the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure. This phenomenon appears or disappears depending on the gate bias (see ESI, Fig. S3). As shown in Fig. 2(a)(c), the few-layer ReS2 and WSe2 have different charge characteristics because of their different carrier densities. Therefore, we assume that the difference in the carrier density induces the inversion.32,39 The carrier density (electron(n), hole(p)) is estimated as follows.

Here, q, Cox, and Vth are the electron charge, the capacitance of the 300 nm-thick SiO2 layer (1.23 108 F cm2), and the threshold voltage, respectively. Because the few-layer WSe2 has a lower carrier density than the few-layer ReS2, the band of the few-layer WSe2 is more bent after contact, as shown in Fig. 3(d). When the forward bias is applied, (i) the current slowly increases and is linearly proportional to Vds under the low forward bias. In this region, the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure is operated by the direct tunneling induced by thermionic emission.32,40 (ii) The second region shows a significant current increase depending on the applied bias, as the forward bias affects the reduction of the barrier height and width. Conclusions

The device composed of few-layer ReS2 and WSe2 has different characteristics, such as (i) a pn region, (ii) an off-state, and (iii) an nn region depending on the gate bias. In the pn region, the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure shows a current increase and decrease, because the few-layer WSe2 enters the off-state around Vgs = 30 V. Through the current-mapping image, the pn region of the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure is verified. According to the results, the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure exhibits outstanding rectification behavior at Vgs = 50 V (rectification ratio 7 102). Regarding the diode characteristics, we explained the carrier-transfer mechanisms. The few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure exhibits (i) direct tunneling, (ii) FN tunneling, and (iii) a space charge region depending on Vds. According to the fitting of the FN tunneling equation, a negative slope and two inflection points were obtained. These results confirmed that FN tunneling occurred in the few-layer ReS2 and WSe2 interface owing to the inversion. Finally, we observed the optoelectrical properties of the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure under a negative gate bias (Vgs = 50 V). A high FF ( 0.56), PCE ( 1.5%) and EQE ( 15.3%) were obtained.

Methods

Fabrication of the few-layer ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure

Few-layer ReS2 was directly exfoliated onto the Si wafer with a 300 nm silicon dioxide layer to form the bottom layer. Additionally, few-layer WSe2 was exfoliated onto another SiO2 substrate coated with polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) and polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) for the top layer. Since the PVA polymer is dissolved in water and the PMMA is removed with acetone, the ReS2/WSe2 heterostructure was fabricated via a typical dry-transfer process. Electrodes were patterned by electron-beam lithography. Cr/Au (5/50 nm) was used for the few-layer ReS2 electrode (n-type) and Au (50 nm) was used for the few-layer WSe2 electrode (ambipolar). The SiO2 substrate was used for the gate material.

Characterization

The thickness and topology of the samples were confirmed via AFM. All of the Raman spectra were observed under a 532 nm laser (Nanobase and NTMDT). The Raman mapping images were obtained with an exposure time of 400 ms. For the electrical measurements, the system consisted of four probes (Keithley 4200 series) and a vacuum chamber ( 106 Torr). The same system and a 405 nm laser were used for obtaining optoelectronic data.
