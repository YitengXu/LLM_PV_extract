Abstract

Ultrathin two-dimensional (2D) semiconductor nanocrystals have attracted much attention of late due to their attractive functional properties. Concurrently, oriented-attachment (OA) growth has been considered as an important growth theory for the construction of various nanocrystals, especially ultrathin 2D nanocrystals. However, all the OA growth procedures reported in the preparation of nanocrystals have been driven by organic ligand molecules so far. In this paper, we report for the first time that a metal ion controlled OA growth to synthesize semiconductor nanocrystals: 2D Î²-In2S3 nanobelts and nanoflakes with â¼2 nm thickness can be achieved via OA controlled by Ca2+ and Al3+, respectively. Note that Ca2+ and Al3+ are not incorporated into the crystal lattice structure of Î²-In2S3. Î²-In2S3 nanobelts and nanoflakes show step-like absorptions in UV-visible spectra. Furthermore, Î²-In2S3 nanoflakes exhibit the most outstanding photoelectrochemical activity when compared with Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles and nanobelts.

Introduction

Along with the discovery of graphene,1,2 ultrathin two-dimensional (2D) semiconductor nanostructuresâincluding nanosheets, nanodisks, nanoplates, nanobelts and nanoflakesâhave attracted much attention due to their promising properties. For example, CdE (E = S, Se, Te), MoS2, WS2, and ZnSe 2D nanocrystals with a few-atomic layer thickness have been actively studied for a variety of applications, such as light-emitting diodes, optoelectronic devices, solar energy conservation and photocatalysis.3â11

To achieve these advances, many physical and chemical methods have been devoted to the synthesis of various 2D semiconductor ultrathin nanomaterials. Some of these methods are the exfoliation of layered materials, the lamellar soft template strategy, oriented attachment (OA) of building blocks and organic-ligand guidance.7,12â19 Among them, OA growth has now become a major research focus in the preparation of ultrathin 2D nanocrystals.20,21 In classic theories of nanocrystal growth, the shape of a nanocrystal is controlled by the relative surface energies of different crystalline facets, such that higher-energy facets grow faster. Different to the classic theories, the OA growth demonstrated by Penn and Banfield is initially based on the separation of nucleation and the subsequent growth process, and suggests a new crystal growth mechanism involving the attachment of two or more nanocrystals, followed by the sharing of a common crystallographic orientation and the joining of planar interfaces.22 In such a process, organic ligand molecules generally play a crucial role in triggering the OA by capping nanoparticle surfaces selectively.15,20,21,23â29 For example, Weller's group synthesized PbS nanosheets through OA growth driven by oleic acid and chlorine-containing co-solvents (linear chloroalkanes).15 Very recently, Wan's group reported solvent-induced OA growth of FeS2 nanocrystals.25 We also reported octylamine driven OA growth to synthesize GdF3 ultrathin nanowires.26,27

Metal ions have also been shown to be efficient directors of nanocrystal morphology, especially where metal nanoparticles are concerned. A particularly illustrative example is Pt-based nanocubes. Pt nanocubes have been produced by tungsten, iron, cobalt, and chromium carbonyls, as well as iron chloride, silver nitrate and silver acetylacetonate.30â36 The Cabot group reported an example of foreign metal ions that influence the morphology of chalcogenide semiconductor CuxSe to change from nanoparticles to nanocubes.37 However, all of these examples are based on classic growth theories of nanocrystals: metal ions manipulating the nucleation and growth rate of nanocrystals in different crystallographic directions. Until now, there have been no studies available on metal ions influencing the OA growth to control the nanocrystal morphology.

As one of the most important IIIâVI semiconductor nanomaterials, indium sulfide (In2S3) has been widely studied as it is a lucrative material for catalysis, photovoltaics, and solar cells along with other optoelectronic device based applications.38,39 In2S3 has a large exciton Bohr diameter of 33.8 nm which makes it a potential material for the fundamental study of size confinement effects in different shapes and sizes.40 In2S3 is known to exist in three different crystalline forms as a function of temperature: Î±-In2S3 (defect cubic), Î²-In2S3 (defect spinel), and Î³-In2S3 (layered structure). Of these, Î²-In2S3 is an n-type semiconductor with a band gap of 2.0â2.3 eV and is a potential candidate for photocatalytic applications because of its suitable band gap for solar energy conversion.41 Until now, various Î²-In2S3 nanocrystals including 2D nanosheets and nanobelts have been prepared successfully.39,42â46 However, there are few reports on the synthesis of Î²-In2S3 controlled by metal ions, or the effect of the 2D nanostructure on the photoelectrochemical (PEC) activity of Î²-In2S3.

In this article, we report an example of using metal ions to control OA growth to prepare ultrathin 2D nanocrystals for the first time. Nanobelts and nanoflakes of Î²-In2S3 with a thickness of â¼2 nm were controllably prepared via OA growth, where Ca2+ and Al3+ played crucial roles along with n-octylamine, respectively. However, the metal ions were not incorporated into the crystal lattice of Î²-In2S3. In contrast, without using metal ions, Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles were obtained. Experimental section

Chemicals

Indium(III) nitrate tetrahydrate (reagent grade, 99%), n-octylamine (99% mass fraction), thioacetamide (TAA, 99%), and sulfur (powder, 99.5%) were purchased from J&K Scientific Ltd. Calcium chloride (98%, anhydrous) and calcium nitrate (99%, anhydrous) were obtained from Beijing chemical factory. Aluminum nitrate nonahydrate (99%, AR) was provided by Tianjin institute of fine chemicals. Aluminum chloride (GR, 99.9%) was purchased from Xiya Chemical Industry Co. Ltd., Shandong. Chloroform and cyclohexane were of analytical grade and obtained from Tianjin Guangfu Co. Ltd. All chemicals were used as received without further purification.

Synthesis of Î²-In2S3 nanobelts

In a typical procedure to prepare Î²-In2S3 nanobelts, indium nitrate tetrahydrate (0.0764 g, 0.2 mmol) and 5.0 mL octylamine were added to 10.0 mL cyclohexane in a Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave with a capacity of 20 mL at room temperature, and stirred for 10 minutes. Moreover, thioacetamide (0.0225 g, 0.3 mmol) and sulfur powder (0.0070 g, 0.2 mmol) were added to the above solution. Then, calcium chloride (0.011 g, 0.1 mmol) was added to the precursor solution. The obtained precursor solution was stirred for 5 minutes, again. After the autoclave was sealed and maintained at 150 Â°C for 4 h in a preheated oven, it was taken out and cooled to room temperature. Yellow products were collected after being repeatedly washed with absolute ethanol and chloroform, and then dried in a vacuum oven. Then the obtained sample was re-dispersed in cyclohexane (â¼5 mL) and stored until its further use.

Synthesis of Î²-In2S3 nanoflakes

Ultrathin 2D Î²-In2S3 nanoflakes were prepared via a similar method except for adding Al3+ (aluminum nitrate nonahydrate, 0.1 mmol) instead of Ca2+ to the precursor solution.

Synthesis of Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles

Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles were prepared via a similar method without adding Ca2+ (calcium chloride) or Al3+ (aluminum nitrate nonahydrate) into the precursor solution.

Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) was used to characterize the phase and crystallization of the obtained products. No characteristic peaks were observed for impurities such as In2O3, InS and In(OH)3. The lower intensity and broader width of the diffraction peaks in the XRD patterns should be related to the smaller size effect of the obtained Î²-In2S3 nanobelts and nanoflakes. Fig. 1b depicts the precise scan patterns of (440) peaks between 45 and 51Â° of the three as-prepared samples. To further investigate the growth process of the nanobelts and nanoflakes, TEM images were taken for the samples obtained at different reaction stages, as shown in Fig. 5. In the first 5 min of the reaction, small particles with sizes of ca. 2 nm were produced during the reaction process of the nanobelts and nanoflakes, as shown in Fig. 5a and d, respectively. When the reactions lasted for 30 min, the original nanoparticles can be recognized along with belt-like and flake-like aggregates, as shown in Fig. 5b and e. When the reaction time was increased to 90 min, we observed nanobelts coexisting with some nanoparticles in the sample, as shown in Fig. 5c. Fig. 5f shows that nanoflakes were formed in the sample when the reaction lasted for 90 min. The evolution we witnessed illustrated that the OA growth process accounted for the formation of Î²-In2S3 nanobelts and nanoflakes. This result was also verified by the traces of OA in the HRTEM images of the primary Î²-In2S3 particles, nanobelts and nanoflakes (Fig. S4, see ESIâ ). In contrast, preparation without adding Ca2+ or Al3+ into the precursor solution led to the formation of spherical (or torispherical) Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles (see Fig. S2 in the ESIâ ). So, OA could not occur without the presence of Ca2+ or Al3+ ions, illustrating that Ca2+ and Al3+ ions play a crucial role in OA growth in this system.

Based on the above results, it was speculated that Ca2+ and Al3+ ions play a role in controlling the OA evolution of Î²-In2S3 2D nanostructures. Octylamine molecules were often reported to prefer to adsorb selectively onto special nanocrystal planes.26,28,53 Here, inferred from the HRTEM images (Fig. 2c and 3c), octylamine molecules should selectively adsorb onto the {110} planes of Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles at the early reaction stage. This enabled the OA growth of Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles along the ã001ã direction. At the same time, metal ions (Ca2+ or Al3+) in the solution could cause some of the octylamine molecules to desorb from partial {110} planes because of the combination between the metal ions (Ca2+ or Al3+) and octylamine. The bare {110} planes produced could promote the OA growth of Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles along the ã110ã direction. Therefore, 2D Î²-In2S3 nanocrystals were achieved due to the 2D OA growth along ã001ã and ã110ã directions. This process could be illustrated as a scheme in Fig. 5g. However, because of the much lower atomic potential (Z/r) of Ca2+ than Al3+, Ca2+ metal ions desorbed fewer octylamine molecules from the planes of Î²-In2S3. This induced the OA growth along the ã110ã of Î²-In2S3 to be much weaker in the Ca2+ solution than that in Al3+ solution. So, the 1D (quasi-2D) growth along ã001ã was dominant in the Ca2+ solution; 2D growth along ã001ã and ã110ã was dominant in the Al3+ solution. This may be the reason why the Ca2+-controlled reaction produced nanobelts but the Al3+-controlled reaction produced nanoflakes.

The band gap in the visible part of the spectrum makes Î²-In2S3 a promising material that may have valuable applications in photocatalytic and photoelectric conversion fields.44 In this work, the three prepared Î²-In2S3 samples in cyclohexane were further characterized by UV-visible absorption spectroscopy, as shown in Fig. 6. Curve a represents the absorption spectrum of the Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles. It shows a wide band between 320 and 600 nm. Curves b and c are the absorption spectra of the obtained 2D nanobelts and nanoflakes. Comparing these curves to that of Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles, curve a, the obtained 2D nanobelts and nanoflakes displayed an obvious step-like absorption between 280 and 380 nm. They are very consistent with the characteristic UV-vis absorption spectrum of the reported Î²-In2S3 with 2D nanostructures, which was identified as an indicator of conduction and valence band transitions in Î²-In2S3.39,54 This step-like absorption may be related to their 2D nanostructure, in which electrons confined along a specific direction have the staircase density of states (DOS).55,56 In addition, the shoulder peaks at â¼290 nm for both nanobelts and nanoflakes were enhanced in the absorption spectra. Moreover, the shoulder peak in nanobelts blue-shifted slightly compared with that of the nanoflakes, as shown in Fig. 6. This may be caused by the fact that the thickness of the obtained Î²-In2S3 nanobelts is a bit less than that of the nanoflakes.

As a proof-of-concept application of the as-prepared ultrathin 2D nanocrystals, the obtained Î²-In2S3 nanobelts and nanoflakes were used in PEC water splitting. For comparison, Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles were also examined in the PEC test. Under irradiation of a 350 W Xe lamp, electronâhole pairs were generated in Î²-In2S3. In general, the VB electrons (eâ) of Î²-In2S3 would be excited to the CB and create holes (h+) after absorbing photon energy. The large 2D structure and ultrathin level of thickness would provide more active sites, and a short and convenient pathway for photogenerated electrons and holes to migrate to the reaction sites of the surface.8,57 The applied potential causes the photogenerated electrons to drift toward the Pt mesh electrode, where the reduction of H2O accompanied by H2 evolution occurs. On the other hand, the holes left on the Î²-In2S3 surface oxidize H2O to produce O2. The PEC performance of the three samples was investigated in a 0.5 M NaOH electrolyte solution using a conventional three-electrode system. All of the samples exhibited an almost negligible dark current in a potential range of â0.2 to 0.7 V vs. Ag/AgCl, suggesting that the observed current stems from light absorption. As shown in Fig. 7a, the photocurrent density of Î²-In2S3 nanoflakes/FTO PEC at 0.7 V is 370 Î¼A cmâ2, which is much higher than that of the pure FTO. Meanwhile, Fig. 7a also shows that the photocurrent densities of Î²-In2S3 nanobelts/FTO and Î²-In2S3 nanoparticles/FTO PEC at 0.7 V are 120 Î¼A cmâ2 and 20 Î¼A cmâ2, respectively. This demonstrates that, of the three samples, the Î²-In2S3 nanoflakes had the best PEC activity for water splitting. The chemical stability against the anodic oxidation reaction is important for photoanode materials. Fig. 7b displays the photocurrent density vs. time (Jât) curves of Î²-In2S3 at a constant potential of 0.22 V vs. Ag/AgCl under illumination for 160 min. The results show that the three obtained Î²-In2S3 samples exhibited high stability for PEC water splitting. As displayed by the amperometric Jât cycles in Fig. 7c, tested with light irradiation (at a power density of 80 mW cmâ2) on and off, the current of the devices displays two distinct states: (1) a âlowâ current state under dark conditions and (2) a âhighâ current state under light conditions. The switching between these two states was reversible and fast, allowing the device to act as a high-quality photosensitive switch. To further identify the origin of the photocurrent, power dependent photocurrent responses were examined. The photocurrent density of the Î²-In2S3 nanoflakes increased with the amplification of light power because more photons were absorbed (Fig. 7d). The relationship between the photocurrent and incident optical power densities (Fig. 7e) demonstrates a power dependence of â¼0.30 (that is, I â P0.30), which indicates the excellent efficiency of the as-grown Î²-In2S3 nanoflakes in capturing photons.58 These results show that the Î²-In2S3 nanoflakes may have great potential for use in solar water splitting and photoswitching, as well as in photoelectronic devices.

Conclusions

In summary, we demonstrated controlled-synthesis of 2D Î²-In2S3 nanobelts and nanoflakes via OA growth. We discovered for the first time that metal ions, Ca2+ or Al3+, could play crucial roles in this OA growth process of 2D nanocrystals. The thickness of both as-synthesized Î²-In2S3 nanobelts and nanoflakes was approximately 2 nm. The XRD and XPS results indicated that Ca2+ and Al3+ ions had not been incorporated into the crystal lattice of Î²-In2S3. Room-temperature UV-vis spectra revealed that Î²-In2S3 nanobelts and nanoflakes demonstrated a step-like absorption band and exhibited a strong quantum confinement effect. Furthermore, our results revealed that the synthesized ultrathin 2D Î²-In2S3 nanoflakes showed superior PEC water-splitting activity compared to nanobelts and nanoparticles. The techniques and concepts we explored in this research may be of help for the development of OA growth theories as well as the potential use of ultrathin 2D Î²-In2S3 nanocrystals.
