Abstract

Utilization of â€œdarkâ€ triplet states is a key task for design of novel emissive organic compounds. Thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF) and room temperature phosphorescence (RTP) are two promising pathways to employ triplet excitons and reach high internal quantum efficiencies. In this paper we present a series of carbazoleâ€“pyrimidine cored compounds, showing RTP and TADF. A specific energy level scheme was shown to promote intersystem crossing and RTP in carbazoleâ€“pyrimidine derivatives with phosphorescence quantum yield values of up to 0.07 in rigid Zeonex films. The modification of the acceptor core with 2-methylthio and dimethylamine fragments allowed us to reduce the singletâ€“triplet energy gap, enhance the rISC (reverse intersystem crossing) rate and obtain high intensity TADF with a fluorescence quantum yield of up to 0.32. Time resolved fluorescence analysis revealed the presence of conformational disorder of the donorâ€“acceptor core governing TADF properties in solid films. A method to control the conformational disorder by enhancing the host rigidity was demonstrated. Sky-blue electroluminescence was shown for the dimethylamino-modified compound with the most intense TADF. Our results show that carbazoleâ€“pyrimidine cored materials are versatile donorâ€“acceptor systems for achieving RTP and TADF via simple structural modifications.

Introduction

Every three out of four excitons created in 1st generation OLED devices are of triplet nature with negligible radiative recombination rate. In this way, only singlet excitons can be used for light emission, remarkably limiting their performance. Phosphorescent compounds, containing heavy rare-earth atoms, allow triplet excitons to be employed and 100% internal quantum efficiencies to be obtained,1,2 however the stability of blue emitters still is insufficient. During the last several years, two new concepts were suggested to employ triplet excitons for light emission without the involvement of heavy atoms: TADF3,4 and RTP.5â€“9 In both cases triplet management was achieved via rather simple modification of the molecular core. Since the discovery of this effect, the main requirement for efficient TADF was thought to be a negligible energy difference between the lowest singlet and triplet states (Î”EST), which is obtained in the compounds possessing donorâ€“acceptor fragments with strong intermolecular charge transfer (ICT).10,11 However, later findings revealed that the mechanism behind TADF is more complex and efficient rISC requires a specific energy level scheme. According to the scheme, the lowest charge transfer singlet state (1CT) should be in the close proximity to localized and CT triplets (3LE and 3CT, respectively), which should be vibronically intercoupled.12,13 Despite the ongoing debates regarding the TADF mechanism, highly efficient electroluminescence has been demonstrated for TADF OLEDs with external quantum efficiencies (EQE) of about 20%11,14â€“17 and more (30%) by enhancing light extraction.18 The main requirement for efficient RTP is similar as for TADF: there should be at least two triplet states, separated by a low energy gap, situated below the S1 state and coupled by a spin-vibronic mechanism.19 Such an energy scheme leads to the emergence of highly efficient intersystem crossing (ISC) and rISC, which surely is important only for TADF. Certainly, other important requirements should be fulfilled: materials should contain heteroatoms, should be dispersed in a rigid matrix with high triplet energy and glass transition temperature and should be composed of rigid fragments.7,19â€“21 However, the RTP efficiency is usually low and the RTP lifetime22 is much longer than that of TADF.23 This results in unwanted EQE roll-off24 and the application of RTP in OLEDs is still scarce.5 Nevertheless, long-lived RTP is promising for other applications, like photodynamic therapy, bioimaging, and solar cells.6,7,22,25

Since the main requirements for both RTP and TADF are the same, the possibility of easy tuning of the nature of delayed emission via simple structural modification looks promising. For example, Ward et.al.21 have shown that TADF can be converted to intense RTP by impeding the rotation of the phenothiazine fragment along the Nâ€“C bond.12 The selection of particular donor and acceptor fragments may also allow one to change the nature of delayed emission.

In this work we present a series of carbazoleâ€“pyrimidine cored compounds showing RTP and TADF. Pyrimidineâ€“containing compounds were demonstrated to be promising for TADF applications,26â€“30 unlike for RTP, where their applications are still scarce.31 The conversion of RTP to TADF was achieved by altering the pyrimidine acceptor with various fragments: methyl (1a), ethyl-phenyl (1b), 2-methylthio (1c) and dimethylaminophenyl (1d). RTP and TADF properties were assessed by comprehensive analysis of time-resolved fluorescence, which revealed the presence of conformational disorder of the flexible donorâ€“acceptor molecular core. Sky-blue electroluminescence via TADF was demonstrated for the compound with the lowest singletâ€“triplet energy gap.

Experimental

Absorption and emission spectra of pyrimidine derivatives were measured in 1 wt% Zeonex (polyolefin) and PMMA (poly(methyl methacrylate)) films, prepared by drop-casting the mixture of polymer and compound dissolved in chlorobenzene on a quartz substrate at room temperature. Absorption spectra were recorded by UV-vis-NIR spectrophotometer Lambda 950 (Perkin Elmer). Time-integrated fluorescence spectra were measured employing a back-thinned CCD spectrometer PMA-11 (Hamamatsu) coupled with a CW xenon lamp and a monochromator (FWHM <10 nm) as an excitation source. The fluorescence quantum yield (Î¦F) was estimated by using the integrating sphere method.32 Time-resolved fluorescence, fluorescence transient and phosphorescence measurements were performed using nanosecond YAG:Nd3+ laser NT 242 with an optical parametric oscillator (Ekspla, excitation wavelength 350 nm, pulse duration 7 ns, repetition rate 0.01â€“1 kHz) and time-gated intensified CCD camera iStar DH340T (Andor) with spectrograph SR-303i (Shamrock). The measurements were performed at 1 Ã— 10âˆ’4 Torr pressure by exponentially increasing the delay and integration time as described in ref. 33. This allowed us to record up to 10 orders of magnitude in time and intensity of the photoluminescence decay. Temperature dependent measurements were performed in a closed cycle He cryostat (Cryo Industries 204N). OLED devices were fabricated on pre-cleaned indium-tin oxide (ITO)-coated glass substrates. The small-molecule and cathode layers were thermally evaporated using a vacuum evaporation apparatus (Vacuum Systems and Technologies Ltd) at 7.5 Ã— 10âˆ’8 Torr pressure and a deposition rate of about 1 Ã… sâˆ’1. OLED devices were encapsulated with a clear glass cover to prevent the interaction with O2 and H2O. Device currentâ€“voltage (Iâ€“V) characteristics and electroluminescence properties were measured using an ETO TEC 100 characterization system (Orb Optronics) and a GS-1290 spectrometer (RadOMA), powered by a 2601A power supply unit (Keithley).

DFT calculations

Quantum chemical calculations of ground-state geometries, electronic excitation energies, oscillator strengths of the singlet transitions, and spatial distributions of electron density of frontier orbitals for carbazoleâ€“pyrimidine cored derivatives were performed by using density functional theory (DFT) as implemented in the Gaussian 09 software package at the B3LYP/6-31G(d) level.35 The Polarizable Continuum Model (PCM) was used to estimate the solvation behaviour of toluene surroundings. Detailed analysis of the optimized geometry and HOMOâ€“LUMO gaps of derivatives 1aâ€“d were reported previously.34 Briefly, DFT analysis revealed that the phenyl-pyrimidine moiety is almost planar, whereas the phenyls at the 4th and 6th positions are twisted by about 16Â°â€“23Â° angles. The dihedral angle between phenyl and carbazole fragments was much larger (54Â°â€“55Â°). The electron density distribution was found to be rather similar for compounds 1aâ€“c, i.e. the electron density was localized mostly on carbazole fragments in the HOMO and the 2,4-phenylpyridine fragment in the LUMO, which is typical for compounds with ICT. A different situation was found for 1d. Here, the electron density in the HOMO was localized over the 4-dimethylaminophenyl moiety with some extension to the pyrimidine ring, while the LUMO was very similar to that of 1aâ€“c. In this case, the dimethylamino fragment acted as the electron donor instead of carbazole, while 2,4-phenylpyrimidine remained as the electron accepting moiety. Twisted carbazole fragments provided steric hindrance and suppression of concentration quenching in solid films.36

Room temperature phosphorescence vs. thermally activated delayed fluorescence

Room temperature phosphorescence. Singlet oxygen is a well-known quencher of triplet excitons,42 thus minor impact of triplet states on the emission properties should be observed in an oxygen-saturated environment. Conversely, intense RTP or TADF is expected to emerge in oxygen-deficient conditions. Indeed, the comparison of time-integrated emission spectra in both conditions evidences the impact of triplet states through phosphorescence and TADF.

Time-integrated room temperature emission spectra of Zeonex and PMMA films of carbazoleâ€“pyrimidine cored derivatives 1aâ€“d in oxygen-saturated and oxygen-deficient environments are shown in Fig. 4 and detailed parameters are given in Table 2. Emission spectra obtained in oxygen-saturated environments match the fluorescence spectra shown in Fig. 3. On the contrary, in oxygen-deficient environments additional peaks emerged in the range of about 450â€“550 nm for compounds 1aâ€“b, coinciding with the phosphorescence spectra shown in Fig. 3 (see Fig. S1 in the ESIâ€  for a detailed comparison).

Time resolved emission analysis (see Fig. 5) revealed that the fluorescence decays during the first âˆ¼100 ns, then after about 5 Î¼s the long-lived low intensity (the initial intensity is about 106 times lower than that of fluorescence) phosphorescence become observable. The emission quantum yield for compounds 1aâ€“b increased from about 0.51 to 0.58 in Zeonex and from about 0.74 to 0.81 in PMMA in oxygen-deficient environments. Phosphorescence accounted for up to 14% of the total emission of compounds 1aâ€“b in Zeonex films and about 6% in PMMA films, thus the phosphorescence quantum yield (Î¦RTP was calculated from the product of Î¦VacF in Zeonex and the ratio of the integrated phosphorescence spectrum to the total integrated emission spectrum6,7) can be estimated to be of about 0.07 for Zeonex and 0.06 for PMMA films. The estimated values of RTP quantum efficiency are comparable to the values of similar molecular compounds in solid films.7,20,22 Clearly, the Zeonex polymer favoured more efficient RTP. Î¦RTP was larger in Zeonex due to the different rate of non-radiative recombination caused by the different rigidity and polarity of the polymer.7â€“9,20,40 As it was shown by DFT calculations, both compounds 1a and b have two triplet levels below S1. Molecular systems having a T2 level situated below S1 tend to show an enhanced ISC rate.19,43 The tuning of the energy of singlet and triplet levels by ambient polarity may alter the ISC rate and Î¦RTP. Additionally, Zeonex has a higher glass transition temperature and is more rigid (see Fig. S2 in ESIâ€ ), thus can more efficiently suppress the non-radiative decay through molecular vibrations, which quenches RTP.20,22 Surprisingly, the fluorescence intensity of compounds 1aâ€“b in Zeonex films (see the range at about 350â€“450 nm) was higher in oxygen-rich environments than in oxygen-deficient surroundings. The relatively high population of long-lived triplets under CW excitation leads to the emergence of fluorescence quenching by singletâ€“triplet annihilation (STA). The fluorescence intensity decreased by about 4â€“5% for both 1a and 1b (STA quenching was minor for PMMA films with lower triplet concentration). Similar fluorescence quenching through STA was also shown for NPB:PMMA blends.40

Thermally activated delayed fluorescence

In order to clearly identify the mechanism of fluorescence enhancement of compounds 1câ€“d in 1 wt% Zeonex and PMMA films, fluorescence decay transients of films in oxygen-saturated and oxygen-free environments were measured (see Fig. 6). Both prompt and delayed emission was observed.

Initially, the prompt fluorescence was observed during the first hundred nanoseconds, which later turned into the delayed emission, observed up to 0.1 s with multiexponential decay. The ratio of delayed and prompt emission integrals (DF/PF ratio) was as high as 2.5 for 1c and 10.8 for 1d in PMMA films, while for 1d the DF/PF ratio was lower in non-polar Zeonex films. The delayed emission portion amounted to 72% (for 1c) and 92% (for 1d) of the total emission in PMMA films and corresponded to a delayed emission quantum yield (Î¦DF) of 0.43 and 0.27 for 1c and 1d, respectively. The TADF intensity was enhanced by embedding 1d into polar small-molecule host DPEPO (bis[2-(diphenylphosphino)phenyl]ether oxide, see Fig. S3 and S4 in the ESIâ€ ). The peak position of 1CT emission of 1d:DPEPO film was redshifted by 80 meV as compared to the peak in PMMA due to the slightly higher dipole moment of DPEPO. The more polar DPEPO surroundings favoured lower Î”EST and more efficient TADF with a DF/PF ratio of 11.41.

Temperature activation of the delayed emission for 1d in PMMA is shown in Fig. S5 in the ESI.â€  Only phosphorescence decay is observed up to about 200 K (the decay profile is the same as that of compounds 1aâ€“b), which later turns to TADF with exponentially increasing intensity10,11 up to room temperature. The intensity of delayed emission increased linearly with the increasing laser pulse energy (see Fig. S5b in the ESIâ€ ), typical for monomolecular recombination, such as TADF. Contrary to tripletâ€“triplet annihilation, which also could result in delayed emission, its intensity of delayed emission should increase quadratically.44,45 Additional proof for the delayed emission to originate from TADF is provided in Fig. 6 and Fig. S4 in the ESI.â€  Oxygen quenching of the delayed fluorescence proved that it has triplet-like nature. However, TADF in oxygen-saturated environments was not quenched completely, rather intense delayed emission was observed. TADF decayed single-exponentially in oxygen-saturated conditions with a decay time of about 70 Î¼s for 1c and 50 Î¼s for 1d (in PMMA). DF/PF ratios in air were remarkably lower: 0.11 for 1c and 0.43 for 1d and corresponded to delayed emission fractions of 10% (for 1c) and 30% (for 1d) of the total emission. This shows that the polymer host surroundings provide fair protection from oxygen impact. Our results show, that the estimation of Î¦F enhancement in oxygen-saturated/deficient environments should be carried out carefully, since TADF still may be present in air (Î¦F with excluded TADF contribution were used for DF/PF calculations in Table 2).

Conformational disorder of compound 1d dispersed in various solid surroundings was investigated by estimating time resolved emission spectra. Rather complex behaviour was observed (see Fig. 7a). This prompt 1CT emission redshifts up to about 2.82 eV and stabilizes after about 100 ns. Then the DF is observed up to about 1 ms. In the case of oxygen-deficient conditions (see Fig. 7b), the firsts steps are the same as in oxygen-rich environments, however TADF is still observed after more than âˆ¼1 ms delay. This double shift of time resolved fluorescence of TADF compounds in solid films was observed previously.46,47 Dias et.al.46 performed fluorescence transient measurements of 5 wt% DPTZ-DBTO2:Zeonex film and observed multiexponential decay of both PF and DF. Fluorescence spectra showed an initial redshift due to the interplay of 1CT and 1LE fluorescence decay. With increased delay time, 1CT TADF decay dominated up to about 400 ns, where the blueshift of the emission peak was observed up to hundreds of Î¼s due to the emergence of 3LE phosphorescence. Mehes et. al47 also observed a redshift of 1CT emission up to about 15 Î¼s and later blueshift at a ms time range in 2 wt% ACRF:DPEPO films. A time-dependent Î”EST model was suggested, where the singletâ€“triplet energy gap was tuned by time-dependent host polarity. According to this model, the initial stabilization of the 1CT state by the increase of host polarity is the nature of the blueshift of prompt fluorescence, while the opposite mechanism causes the later reverse temporal shift of DF. Also, the redshift of prompt fluorescence in solid films occurring in the first âˆ¼100 ns was observed by Santos et. al.48 and was explained by stabilization of the 1CT state, which is slower than in solutions.

Although the fluorescence dynamics of compound 1d is rather similar to that observed by Mehes et.al.,47 the presented mechanism is unlikely. We have observed the same spectral shifts in both polymer and small-molecule hosts with different rigidity and ability for reorganization, so a different explanation probably is needed. The recent report by Northey et.al.,49 based on molecular dynamics, ruled out the time-dependent Î”EST model.47 The authors proposed a new possible mechanism, where spectral shifts are explained by the dispersion of Î”EST values. Molecules having more planar conformations would have higher S1 energy and Î”EST, and thus will emit earlier, inducing the redshift of prompt fluorescence. Concomitantly with radiative S1 deactivation, ISC efficiently populates triplet states. The reverse trend is observed for the TADF. Molecules with more twisted conformations having the lowest Î”EST would emit first because of exponential krISC dependence on Î”EST causing the blueshift of fluorescence. This proposed mechanism nicely explains the fluorescence dynamics of compound 1d. In this case, the weakest steric interactions should be between pyrimidine and 4-dimethylaminophenyl fragments thus the energy barrier for twisting should be low. Initially, prompt 1CT emission is observed from molecules with the lowest pyrimidineâ€“4-dimethylaminophenyl twist angle having the highest oscillator strength and 1CT energy. Since the non-radiative decay rate (through ISC) is high, the triplet population rapidly rises and after several hundred nanoseconds TADF emerges firstly from the molecules with more twisted geometry, the lowest 1CT energy and Î”EST. The later slower TADF from more planar conformations with larger Î”EST is observed only in oxygen-free conditions, however DF from the most planar conformations with the largest singletâ€“triplet gaps cannot be observed due to quenching by structural defects or remaining oxygen. Interestingly, DF blueshift in more polar DPEPO was stronger than in PMMA (see Fig. S7 in the ESIâ€ ) due to more rapid rISC.

Selection of a more rigid polymer host allows us to reduce conformational disorder (see Fig. S9 in the ESIâ€ ). Dispersion of compound 1d in the more rigid Zeonex polymer resulted in smaller temporal shifts of prompt and delayed fluorescence spectra due to the more restricted molecular core conformation, and thus the lower Î”EST dispersion. TADF spectra at the largest delay times resembled those of prompt fluorescence observed just after the photoexcitation, originating from molecules with the same conformation. This shows that rigid hosts or less flexible molecular geometry50,51 should be used in order to reduce unwanted temporal shifts of fluorescence spectra.

In the final stage, compound 1d showing pronounced TADF properties, was used as a dopant for sky blue OLEDs.

Conclusions

In summary, we have presented a series of carbazoleâ€“pyrimidine cored compounds showing room temperature phosphorescence and thermally activated delayed emission. RTP was shown in compounds with a large energy gap between the lowest singlet state and two triplet states, while RTP was transformed to TADF in compounds with a reduced singletâ€“triplet energy gap. The alteration of the delayed emission type was achieved only by modifying the pyrimidine acceptor fragment and enhancing the charge transfer character. The RTP quantum yield reached about 7% in rigid nonpolar Zeonex due to efficient intersystem crossing. On the contrary, TADF was more intense in more polar surroundings due to the lowered singletâ€“triplet energy gap. The PMMA polymer matrix and DPEPO small-molecule host were shown to suppress TADF quenching by oxygen in air and allowed us to observe rather intense DF. Analysis of time resolved fluorescence revealed the existence of an initial redshift and later blueshift of delayed fluorescence. Such unusual behaviour in solid films was explained by the existence of various conformations of the donorâ€“acceptor core with different 1CT energies and Î”EST values acting on different time-scales. Selection of a more rigid polymer host allowed us to reduce conformational disorder and fluorescence temporal shifts. Finally, TADF enhanced sky-blue electroluminescence was demonstrated in an OLED from the compound with the most intense TADF.
