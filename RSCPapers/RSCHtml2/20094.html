Interfacial friction-induced electronic excitation mechanism for tribo-tunneling current generation

Jun Liu *ab, Keren Jiang b, Lan Nguyen b, Zhi Li b and Thomas Thundat *ab
aDepartment of Chemical and Biological Engineering, University at Buffalo, The State University of New York, New York 14260-4200, USA. E-mail: jliu238@buffalo.edu; tgthunda@buffalo.edu
bDepartment of Chemical and Materials Engineering, University of Alberta, Edmonton, Alberta T6G2 V4, Canada

Received 5th October 2018 , Accepted 4th February 2019

First published on 4th February 2019

Abstract

Direct-current (d.c.) electricity generation using a moving Schottky contact is emerging as a new strategy for mechanical energy conversion. Here, we demonstrate the generation of d.c. tunneling current with a density of 35 A m2 at a metalinsulatorsemiconductor (MIS) sliding system using micro-tips. The measured current densities were found to be two to three orders of magnitude higher than that observed with the conventional polymer-based triboelectric nanogenerators (TENGs). The electromotive force Vs* for the tribo-tunneling transport comes from the dynamic electronic excitation at the frictional interface rather than from the electrostatically trapped surface charges as in the case of conventional TENGs. The strong electronic excitation can give rise to a non-equilibrium interfacial charge variation m. Depending on the energy distribution of the excited electrons/holes, m subsequently dissipates non-adiabatically into tunneling current and trapped surface charges, or adiabatically into heat. These fundamental results not only enhance our understanding of the triboelectric phenomenon, but also open up new paths for the development of next-generation mechanical energy harvesting and sensing techniques.


Conceptual insights

Over the last decade, triboelectricity, the appearance of electrical charges on contact, has been pursued as a basis for developing energy harvesting devices based on mechanical vibration/friction. Although the observed triboelectric potential based on polymer materials is very large, the generated current is extremely small because of the limitations imposed by displacement current. From the materials point of view, the outer electrons of the atoms are tightly bound and not free to move through the insulating material. We have overcome this electric current limitation by using tribo-tunneling of electrons based on metalsemiconductor frictional contacts. Compared to conventional triboelectric devices, the tunneling current density is two to three orders of magnitude higher and it is continuous and direct. Here we present experimental and theoretical investigations of the physics underlying the current generation and its dependence on material characteristics. This fundamental understanding of the tribo-tunneling will be important in exploring novel semiconductor materials as well as heterojunction structures for scaling and optimizing the concept for practical applications. In addition, this technique also shows promise for applications in sensing.

Understanding the carrier dynamics at a frictional interface is essential for developing novel mechanical energy conversion technologies.13 To date, the d.c. generation phenomenon has been observed in various metalsemiconductor moving systems (metal-MoS2 layers,4 metal-Si,5 graphene-Si,6 metal-conducting polymer (PPy),7 SnO2-PPy,8etc.), and different mechanisms have been proposed to explain the generation of high density currents. Unlike a conventional triboelectric nanogenerator (TENG) that generates alternating current by dielectric displacement (a.c., J 0.010.1 A m2), sliding metalsemiconductor frictional contact induces a strong localized electric field and produces high current density (d.c., J of the order of 10 A m2) through quantum mechanical tunneling.4,5 In particular, it has been found that the electromotive force (emf, measured in volts) in a tribo-tunneling system is related to an electronic excitation process under non-equilibrium conditions, which cannot be explained by the electrostatics-based TENG theory.4,5 Though the triboelectric charge transfer phenomenon via contact electrification has been investigated for years, little is known about the electronic excitation due to frictional contact.1,9

Generally, the open-circuit voltage (Voc) in a conventional TENG is attributed to the separation of two dielectric materials (or a metal and a dielectric material) carrying opposite electrostatic charges.1012 As illustrated in Fig. 1, when the two bodies in contact are separated horizontally, the induced image charges on the electrodes, positive on one electrode and negative on another, provide the emf for the current flow in the external circuit.4,5Fig. 1c and d show the corresponding situations as marked in Fig. 1a for the dielectric/dielectric and metal/dielectric system, respectively. In contrast, the increasing interfacial potential Vs* in the metalinsulatorsemiconductor (MIS) sliding contact (Fig. 1b) shows very different features: (i) Vs* is excited at an intimately contacted MS interface in the absence of changing contact area or separation distance (capacitance C remains constant), and (ii) the sustained d.c. output arises instantaneously irrespective of the sliding direction (Fig. 1e).4,5 However, the Voc is negligible when the two contact bodies are intimately contacted in conventional TENGs (Step 1 in the Fig. 1a): the positive/negative electrostatic charges are confined in a two-dimensional interface cancelling each other, and therefore the electrodes are in isopotential.3

As shown in Fig. 2b, the contrast of contact potential difference, VCPD, can be observed on the rubbed area, which is associated with the increased local surface charge density (). Considering the electrical routing in the experiment (from sample to tip), a larger sample and hence a smaller Vs is predicted on the rubbed area, indicating positive charge (i.e. hole) accumulation at the native silicon oxide surface. As the contact force F increases, it can be seen that VCPD of the rubbed area increases from 40 mV to 96 mV, which can be considered as due to the increased microscopic contact area between the tip and the substrate, and the enhanced charge transfer under compression between the tip and the substrate surface atoms.20,21 In dielectric material based TENGs, those trapped surface charges induce image charges on the bottom electrode, generating voltage when the contact materials are separated vertically in distance or horizontally in area (Fig. 1c and d).

We also investigated the characteristics of Vs*, which accounts for the tribo-tunneling current. Fig. 2d and f show the C-AFM tunneling current signal under Peakforce tapping mode (Fig. 2c) and contact mode (Fig. 2e), respectively. Under the Peakforce tapping mode, the AFM probe and samples are intermittently brought into contact while the tip is scanned across the sample off resonance at 1 kHz (the resonance frequency is 97 kHz), and with the feedback loop controlling the maximum force on the tip (Peak force) for each individual cycle (see Fig. S4 in the ESI). It can be seen that C-AFM current is generated only under continuous sliding of the contact (Fig. 2f), indicating that a strong lateral interfacial atomic interaction is required for triggering the electronic excitation. It should be noted that the negative current direction here refers to the current flowing from the tip to the sample in the external circuit (i.e. the electron tunneling from the tip into the sample at the contact), which was ambiguous previously.5

We determined the Vs* of the tribo-tunneling current using a null method. In a d.c. generator system like a solar cell, the value of Voc can be determined by applying external bias to nullify the current output. Fig. 2gj show the C-AFM current distribution with varying external bias Vbias (F = 500 nN). The Voc is estimated to be 300400 mV when the current signal diminishes to 0. Notably, the value of Vs* is 34 times higher than that of the VCPD under the same force (96 mV). The physical difference between Vs* and VCPD is more evident from the microscopic experimental data (Fig. 3a), where a gold-coated spring connector with tip diameter 59 m is used to control the contact force in the measurement. It can be seen that the sliding movement induces Voc of magnitude around 300400 mV that immediately decays to zero under static conditions (Fig. 3b).

The current density (J) and power density as a function of electric load R are collected under F = 0.5 N and shown in Fig. 3c. The short-circuit current Isc is measured to be 100 nA, which corresponds to Jsc 35 A m2 considering a circular contact interface with a diameter of 50 m (Fig. 3a). Here, the parameter fill factor (FF), defined as the ratio of the maximum power to the product of Voc and Isc in a solar cell, is adopted to characterize the quality of power generation in the tribo-tunneling system:22

FF = Pmax/(IscVoc),(3)
Accordingly, the calculated FF is 0.23, and the lower value can be attributed to the large contact resistance of the point-plane interface. Previously, we have reported the Jsc of 2.5 A m2 and the power density of 0.3 W m2 in the MIS system by a multimeter probe with a tip diameter 1 mm, and FF of 0.34.5 Therefore, it is suggested that the reducing tip radius could enhance the d.c. density, which is in good agreement with our AFM results of the tribo-tunneling systems.4,5 However, the internal energy conversion efficiency may be influenced by the increased contact resistance in the sharp point contact.

We shall now discuss the mechanism of the Vs* generation in sliding MIS contacts. Neglecting the bulk defects within the thin oxide layer, from charge neutrality we can express the interfacial charge as:19,23

m + ss + sc = 0,(4)
where m, ss, and sc denote the charge density at a metal surface, SiSiOx interface and Si surface depletion layer (SCR), respectively (Fig. 3e). Empirically, the sign of m depends on the relative potential values of the contact materials in the triboelectric series, which is described by different contact electrification theories such as electron transfer, ion transfer, and mass transfer.9 It is known from the KPFM results that m < 0, which corresponds to electron transfer from the oxide surface to the metal, leaving excess electrons on the metal side and holes on the oxide surface. This is understandable when the metal Fermi-level EF is lower than the Eo of the oxide surface before contact.

Under frictional motion, atomic distance between the two contact materials may be squeezed periodically, considering the existence of microscopic nanogaps from surface roughness. At atomic level, periodically changing atomic distance is also expected by the stick-slip PrandtlTomlinson model.1,24 Consequently, electronic excitation of energetic electrons/holes to higher/lower energy distribution spectra may be induced, when the symmetry of interfacial electrostatic equilibrium is interrupted on and off.25 Here we assume a non-equilibrium charge density increase that can dissipate through (Fig. 3f):

m = m,tunnel + m,acc + m,phonon,(5)
where m,tunnel, m,acc, and m,phonon denote the portion of m dissipating into tunneling current, surface charge accumulation, and heat, respectively. Among the three terms, and m,tunnel and m,acc, are the results of non-adiabatic energy conversion into energetic carriers deviated from the Oppenheimer approximation.25 For the electrons with sufficient energy to overcome the Schottky barrier (marked as red in the energy distribution spectrum in Fig. 3f), they can pass through the thin oxide layer via quantum mechanical tunneling into the semiconductor side (forward tunneling), which corresponds to m,tunnel. The ones with insufficient energy for tunneling, but enough energy for contact electrification (it also has an energy barrier as predicted by density function theory (DFT) calculations20) will be trapped at the surface states (m,acc), or back tunneling into the metal side.9,26 The tribo-tunneling generator uses m,tunnel as the d.c. source, of which the tunneling current I can be calculated by:25,27,28
(6)
where d is the oxide thickness, is the Schottky energy barrier height, kB is the Boltzmann constant, and T is the temperature. and are parameters which are associated with surface state density and effective contact area.

In eqn (6), the first and second exponential components correspond to the exponential decayed probability of quantum tunneling as a function of forbidden region distance (one-dimensional)5 and the thermionic emission of hot carriers through the Schottky barrier, respectively. A similar situation can be found in catalytic reaction of molecules on the Schottky junctions.25,29,30 The chemically induced electronic excitation is manifested as exoelectron emission, surface chemiluminescence, and hot electron tunneling current25in analogy with tribo-exoelectron,31,32 triboluminescence,3335 and the tribo-tunneling current of the MIS junction in our case.

As the T increases, one would expect an enhanced thermionic emission from eqn (6) as well as a decreased semiconductor resistance, which is supposed to enhance the tribo-tunneling power output. On the other hand, we shall see that T may influence the thermodynamic equilibrium process: when the T is sufficiently high, the thermodynamic via energy level alignment between the two materials may be achieved much faster. If the charge transfer is associated with the extent of asymmetry of surface potential under dynamic motion (which has been proposed in many contact electrification studies1,9), one may expect a weaker electronic excitation.

As summarized in Fig. 3g, the J of tunneling d.c. is about 23 orders higher than dielectric a.c. density in conventional TENGs.11,3641 Moreover, the current output exhibits a sustained feature under continuous or a relatively fast reciprocating sliding fashion, while the dielectric a.c. manifests itself as transient output as a function of time.3,5 In conventional TENGs, the maximum electrostatic m achieved by external charge injection is 1 mC m2, which approaches the dielectric air break down limitation.3638 The quantification of m in the tribo-tunneling transport may need further scrutiny into the space where the voltage is built up. If we assume the voltage is built up capacitively across the 1.6 nm surface oxide, the dynamic m is estimated to be as high as 6.5 mC m2, using Voc = 300 mV, d = 1.6 nm, = 3.9, and 0 = 8.85 1012 F m1. A more plausible scenario is that the Voc is exerted on the Si depletion layer with the width on the order of 100 nm. Accordingly, m would be on the order of 0.11 mC m2.

The dependence of Voc signals on the Si doping type and resistivity are summarized in Fig. 4a. It can be seen that the tip-surface electronic interaction exhibits only a weak dependency on Si doping type or concentration, which may be related to the Fermi-level pinning at the Si surface with 12 nm native oxide.23,4244 The Fermi-level pinning effect can be inferred from the KPFM measurement of the surface potential of Si surfaces where the surface energy level E0 remains relatively constant in all doping type/concentrations (Fig. 4b). Specifically, the thin native oxide on the Si surface plays an important role in generating the interfacial electronic excitation, which is reflected in the increasing Voc output as a function of increasing thickness of oxide deposited with atomic layer deposition (ALD).5 For comparison we removed the native oxide on a 0.11 cm p-type Si by HF etch and measured the Voc signal (Fig. 4c). The measured Voc direction reverses right after the etching (5 min) and restores after 20 h of exposure to ambient air (oxide growth). This indicates that the electronic excitation induces opposite interfacial potential difference with and without surface native oxide (Fig. 4d). It is assumed that the removal of surface oxide may weaken the Fermi-level pinning effect, which in turn reverses the surface potential difference between the metal and the p-Si surface resulting in the opposite m (>0, electron transfer from the metal to sample surface) (Fig. 4e and f). As a result, m may be interpreted as increasing hot holes as illustrated in Fig. 3f. This is in good agreement with the observed Voc direction reversal in the combination of contact materials for the tribo-tunneling systems with different work functions.5,6

Under Peakforce tapping modes, localized mechanical properties such as elastic modulus, deformation, and adhesion can be extracted from the force curve collected on each cycle (Fig. S4 in the ESI). As shown in Fig. 5ac, the reduced modulus and adhesion, and the increased deformation of the rubbed area indicates a possible SiO surface bond rupture during the friction, increasing the surface state density and thus contributing to the m. As proposed by Grunthaner et al.,45 the excited Si surface with native oxide may induce a large number of SiO bond cleavages with stationary Si3+ defects (holes) and mobile O defects (electrons). These charge carriers may quickly recombine releasing heat or take part in electron tunneling, which is in line with our above discussion from an energy band diagram point of view.

The Voc outputs for three different sliding speeds v (v1 > v2 > v3) are shown in Fig. 5d. It can be seen that the Voc under the slowest sliding shows a smaller value compared to the other two cases. When the sliding speed/frequency is fast enough (the case with v1), the Voc output becomes continuous, which is in line with the reported result under a circular sliding mode.5 Interestingly, the contact force dependent Voc measurements (Fig. 5e) show that the increasing potential difference is not affected by the contact force in a wide force range. Since the surface oxide is more rigid than organic materials, it may be less prone to a significant contact area change, which has been considered as a main reason why the apparent charge density of polymer material-based contacts is always enhanced with increasing force.9 It is also noted that the Voc signal when the two materials are separated is fluctuated (Fig. 5e). This resembles the Voc output of a conventional vertical mode TENG, where the voltage is now a result of work function difference as well as trapped triboelectric surface charges.11

It should be noted that multiple influences may be exerted by the tip feature: the large curvature of the contact points results in high local pressure and electric field, which can increase the interfacial atomic interaction and thus enhance the electronic interaction and current density output. However, we demonstrated that the planeplane structure can also be used in designing the tribo-tunneling generator. As shown in Fig. 5f, a prototype was developed using ITO (indium tin oxide, Sigma-Aldrich, surface, resistivity 812 sq1)-coated glass as the moving metal part (Fig. 5f). Fig. 5g shows the Voc output of the generator, where the Si substrate was driven in frictional contact with the ITO glass, by a motor with the speed v of 3 cm s1. It can be seen that the prototype can generate a sustained signal of 0.20.3 V for more than 1000 cycles. Therefore, various configurations such as tip arrays, planeplane structures, and flexible materials may be used for designing the tribo-tunneling-based energy harvesting devices in the future applications.

Conclusions

In summary, it has been revealed that the tribo-tunneling transport in the metalinsulatorsemiconductor sliding system is associated with the friction-induced electronic excitation at the interface. The driving force of the tribo-tunneling transport and dielectric displacement current transport in conventional TENGs is very different: conventional TENGs use electrostatically trapped surface charges as the voltage source, whereas the tribo-tunneling stems from the increasing interfacial charge m under non-equilibrium conditions, which is subsequently dissipated into tunneling current, surface trapped charges, or heat, depending on the energy of the excited electrons/holes. It has been demonstrated that the strong electronic excitation in the MIS sliding system can generate high d.c. current density (J 35 A m2), which is 23 orders higher than the dielectric a.c. output in conventional TENGs. These basic understandings provided a new direction for optimizing and developing next-generation mechanical energy conversion technologies based on MIS sliding systems.
