Abstract

Two-dimensional metallic halide perovskites with interlayer alternating cations (ACI) have shown great potential in photovoltaics. Compared to their Ruddlesdenâ€“Popper (RP) and Dionâ€“Jacobson (DJ) counterparts, ACI perovskites have a narrower band gap (Eg) and enhanced optical absorption. However, the relatively low power conversion efficiency (PCE) hampers the development of ACI-based devices due to their crystal orientation and quality. In this paper, a method of passivation of hybrid GAMAnPbnI3n+1 (n = 5) perovskites by additive engineering was reported. Inverted perovskite solar cells (PSCs) were prepared by adding polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) and povidoneâ€“iodine (PVP-I), and the synergistic passivation effect of PVP and I was investigated. The optimal PVP-I concentration reduces the trap density, and enhances the charge mobility and transport because of the improved crystallinity and energy cascade, which eventually generates a PCE of 15.73%. Additionally, the optimized devices without encapsulation retain 80% of their initial efficiency for more than 700 h after storage under ambient conditions.

Introduction

Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) using 3D organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskite light absorbers have made great progress in the past decade, with the most advanced power conversion efficiency (PCE) exceeding 25%.1 However, the environmental instability of 3D perovskite materials is still a key obstacle to the practical application of PSCs.2,3 Two-dimensional (2D) perovskites prepared by inserting larger organic cations into 3D structures show excellent long-term stability, which is due to the van der Waals interaction between their hydrophobic bulk organic cations and the capping organic molecules. The lower layer number (n value) of inorganic layers sandwiched between two adjacent organic spacers can make PSCs have better stability. The general formulas of RP and DJ phases in the 2D perovskites are L2Anâˆ’1BnX3n+14,5 and LAnâˆ’1BnX3n+1,6,7 respectively, where L represents monovalent or divalent organic spacer cations, A represents monovalent cations, B represents divalent metal ions, and X represents halogen ions. However, RP and DJ perovskites suffer from poor charge dissociation and transport in polycrystalline films, which significantly limit the PCE of PSCs.

Hybrid halide ACI perovskites are novel perovskite materials derived from the oxide perovskite family with marvelous structures and promising applications in solar cells.8 Generally, a 2D ACI perovskite can be described by the general formula of GAMAnPbnI3n+1.9 Compared to typical RP phase perovskites, ACI perovskites exhibit narrower optical band gaps,10,11 lower exciton binding energies, and longer carrier lifetimes due to the small volume of GA and MA.12,13 Despite these advantages, it remains a challenge to control the crystallization and film formation and defect passivation of ACI perovskites. Various effective post-processing methods have been reported to improve the quality of perovskite films, such as mixed crystallization,14 additive engineering11,13 and solvent ligand engineering.15,16 As additives, polymers are selected to be effective candidates for additive engineering, such as mono-polymer polystyrene (PS),17 polyethylenimine (PEI)18 and polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA).19 It has been reported that the interaction between a polymer and the perovskite mesophase increases the perovskite grain size, thereby increasing the open-circuit voltage (Voc) and fill factor (FF), indicating that the polymer may be an effective candidate for additive engineering.

In this work, the photovoltaic performance and stability of PSCs with ACI quasi-2D perovskites were improved by the PVP-I additive contained in the perovskite precursor solution. By comparing the effects of PVP and PVP-I on perovskites, the synergistic passivation effect of PVP-I on perovskites is revealed. The dissolution of PVP and I in the precursor will lead to the interaction with the perovskite crystal to improve the performance. The synergistic effect of PVP-I is more pronounced compared to PVP alone. The optimal concentration of PVP-I relative to GAMA5Pb5I16 was determined and the effect of PVP-I and PVP on device efficiency was compared. It was found that PVP-I can significantly improve the photovoltaic performance. Morphological and optoelectronic studies were performed. ACI-type perovskites were fabricated in a nâ€“iâ€“p device structure with higher efficiency, while the pâ€“iâ€“n device structure is more stable during the preparation of the devices. Considering the large-scale application of perovskites, the improvement in the PCEs of inverted pâ€“iâ€“n devices was investigated. In addition, the stability before and after PVP-I treatment was compared, and it was observed that the stability of perovskite devices treated with PVP-I was significantly improved.

Experimental section

Materials

PbI2 (99.99%), guanidine hydroiodide (GAI), poly[bis(4-phenyl)(2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)amine] (PTAA, Mn:<6000) and [6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PC61BM) were purchased from Xiâ€™an Polymer Light Technology Corp. Methylammonium iodide (MAI) and methylammonium chloride (MACl) were purchased from Dyesol. Bathocuproine (BCP) was purchased from Lumtec. Isopropanol (IPA, 99.5%) was purchased from J&K. DMF (N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF, 99.8%) and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO, â‰¥99.9%) were purchased from Sigma Aldrich. All reagents and solvents were used as received.

Solution preparation

To prepare the perovskite solutions, 1.3 M based PbI2 mixed with GAI, MAI and PbI2 at molar ratios of 1:5:5 was solubilized in the solvent mixture of DMF/DMSO (v/v = 8/1), MACl was added as an additive to the perovskite precursor solution at a concentration of 10 mg mLâˆ’1 (10 mg was added to 1 mL precursor solution), and then stirred overnight in a N2-purged glovebox. The PTAA solution was prepared by dissolving 5 mg of PTAA in 1 mL of toluene. The PC61BM solution was prepared by dissolving 20 mg of PC61BM in 1 mL of toluene. The BCP solution was prepared by dissolving 0.5 mg of BCP in 1 mL of IPA.

Device fabrication

Commercially available ITO glass (15 Î© sqâˆ’1) was sequentially cleaned with deionized water, ethanol, and IPA for 15 min, respectively, and then dried under a N2 flow. PTAA was spin coated onto UV-Ozone treated ITO at 4000 rpm for 40 s, and annealed at 120 Â°C for 20 min in N2. The GAMAnPbnI3n+1 (n = 5) perovskite film was prepared by spin-coating at a low speed of 1000 rpm for 3 s and a high speed of 4000 rpm for 20 s, where 90 Î¼L of IPA was dropped onto the substrate at 10 s before the end of the spin-coating. These films were then annealed at 150 Â°C for 20 min. Next, 40 Î¼L of PC61BM solution was then spin-coated on the top of the perovskite layer at 2000 rpm for 30 s and BCP was dropped onto PC61BM at a speed of 3000 rpm for 30 s to form an electron extracting layer. Finally, a 100 nm silver counter electrode was thermally deposited onto a BCP layer under a pressure of 2 Ã— 10âˆ’4 Pa by using a shadow mask.

Characterization

Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR) spectra were recorded using a Nicolet 6700 at room temperature, and the powder samples for FTIR measurement were prepared by grinding PVP, PVP-I, PVP/PbI2 or the PVP-I/PbI2 mixture with KBr. The crystal structure of perovskite films was determined using a Shimadzu X-ray diffractometer with a Cu KÎ± radiation source (Î» = 0.1541 nm). The surface morphology of the thin films was analyzed using atomic force microscopy (AFM, Bruker Multimode 8). The optical absorption spectra of perovskite films were recorded using a Shimadzu UV-3600 UV-vis spectrometer. The steady-state PL spectrum and PL decay curve were measured using an Edinburgh FLS980 fluorescence spectrometer. UPS and XPS measurements were performed using a Thermo Fischer, ESCALAB 250Xi, system. The Jâ€“V characteristics of solar cells were recorded using a Keithley 2400 Keithley 2400 type light source under 100 m W cmâˆ’2 (Newport 94023 A, AM 1.5G) calibrated using a standard silicon solar cell (certified by NREL) as the reference. For Jâ€“V measurements, the scan voltage was set from âˆ’0.1 V to 1.2 V with a scan rate of 50 mV sâˆ’1. Before the Jâ€“V test, a 0.0625 cm2 nonreflective mask was used to define the accurate active cell area. The Nyquist plot was obtained by conducting EIS analysis at a voltage of 1.0 V under dark conditions using a CHI660E electrochemical workstation. The IPCE spectra were recorded using a TLS-300XU system, calibrated using a Si reference solar cell.

To study the effects of different additives on the morphology of ACI perovskite GAMA5Pb5I16 films, the top-view scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of perovskite films with and without additive treatment were obtained. As shown in Fig. 2a, with the addition of PVP-I, the perovskite film surface becomes denser and smoother. The grain size of the pristine film is small and not uniform. After adding PVP, large grains appear, and small grains are distributed around large grains. After adding PVP-I, the grains become larger and are evenly distributed. The average grain size increased from 254 nm to 354 nm and 389 nm after treatment with PVP and PVP-I. Blurring of the grain boundaries indicates that the grains are tightly bound, indicating that the film homogeneity is improved. The film with PVP-I has the largest grain size, which is likely due to the change in the crystal growth dynamics under the action of PVP and I. The improvement of the film quality may be due to several different types of functional groups in PVP-I, which can not only make the grains more tightly bonded, but also passivate anionic defects (such as lead-I antisite defects and methylammonium (MA+) vacancies) and cationic defects (such as insufficiently coordinated lead).20 Atomic force microscopy analysis further shows that the surface became flat and more uniform after the addition of additives in Fig. 2b. The root mean square roughness of the irregularity height determined by the Rayleigh-Reiss theory decreases from âˆ¼13.0 nm to âˆ¼11.1 nm and âˆ¼11.7 nm.

X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analysis was performed to further study the change of the binding energy of perovskite core-level peaks in the presence and absence of additives. In Fig. 3e and f, the Pb4f and I3d peaks of the perovskite films after the additive treatment are shifted to lower binding energy directions due to the effective passivation of the non-coordinated lead and iodine species. Finally, the stability of PSCs was studied in a dark environment with a temperature of 20 Â± 5 Â°C and a relative humidity (RH) of 20 Â± 5%. Fig. 7 shows that the PSC with PVP maintains âˆ¼79% of the initial PCE, the PSC with PVP-I maintains âˆ¼81% of its initial PCE, and the PCE of the pristine device decreases rapidly, only âˆ¼59% of the initial PCE. It is reported that the stability of PSCs is strongly dependent on point defects, because the degradation of perovskites in air is caused by defects.44 Therefore, defect engineering can improve both the photovoltaic performance and stability. Additive engineering is an effective method to control the defects and improve the stability of perovskite films. In our work, PVP and PVP-I were used as additives, which led to an improvement in the carrier lifetime, as shown in the TRPL data in Fig. 3c. This extended lifetime is due to the defect passivation of the PVP. In addition, the detailed Pb XPS analysis in Fig. 3f confirms that I in PVP-I can passivate iodide vacancies. Therefore, passivation of defects with PVP-I is more helpful to improve the stability.45

Conclusions

In summary, we have developed a defect-passivation and crystallization-control strategy based on PVP-I treatment. First, PVP improves the crystallinity of perovskite crystals and increases the crystal size. Second, PVP in the bulk phase of perovskite films significantly deactivates the trap state of the film. Moreover, the interaction of the carbonyl group in PVP with the disassortative Pb2+ effectively deactivates the cation defects and increases the grain size. Iodine can also passivate the iodine vacancies and improve the film quality. The obtained PCE of the champion device after PVP-I treatment was 15.73%. In addition, the stability of the PSCs is significantly improved. The PCE of the device with PVP-I maintained âˆ¼80% of its initial value after 30 days in the air and âˆ¼99% of its original efficiency under 200 s of AM 1.5G illumination. This PVP-I based processing provides a synergistic strategy to improve the performance and stability of ACI inverted PSCs.
