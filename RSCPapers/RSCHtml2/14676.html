Improved efficiency and thermal stability of ternary all-small-molecule organic solar cells by NCBA as a third component material

Abstract

In this work, organic solar cells (OSCs) were fabricated with a blend of PC71BM and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2 employed as a binary photoactive layer and with a dihydronaphthyl-based C60 bisadduct (NCBA) small-molecule acceptor used as a third component material. We demonstrate that the short-circuit current density (JSC), open-circuit voltage (VOC), fill factor (FF), power conversion efficiency (PCE), and thermal stability can all be enhanced simultaneously. In addition, the crystallinity can be finely optimized and the photon harvesting ability was enhanced for short-wavelength light by adjusting the NCBA doping ratio, leading to efficient exciton dissociation and charge-carrier transport. At the same time, the ternary photoactive layer, with a small amount of NCBA as a third component material, reduced monomolecular recombination and bimolecular recombination under open-circuit and short-circuit conditions, respectively. Such a ternary structure with NCBA as a third component material helped enhance the crystallinity and fix the surface morphology of the photoactive layer, thus reducing the decay ratio while increasing the thermal annealing treatment time. Consequently, the PCE reaches 9.1% for ternary OSCs with a 12 wt% NCBA doping ratio in a blended acceptor, with 87.2% of the initial PCE value maintained after 100 h of thermal annealing treatment at 90 C, which is much higher than that obtained for the PCE of binary OSCs.

1. Introduction

The photoactive layer of common binary organic solar cells (OSCs) consists of a blend of donor and acceptor materials.14 To improve power conversion efficiency (PCE), donor materials should have advantages of deep highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energy levels, a low bandgap, a broad visible-light absorption spectrum, and higher hole mobility,57 and the acceptor materials should have advantages of shallow lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy levels, high electron mobility, and strong electron affinity to accept electrons.810 As we know, the PCE of OSCs is proportional to the product of the open-circuit voltage (VOC), short-circuit density (JSC), and fill factor (FF). The VOC is governed by the difference between the LUMO energy level of the acceptor and the HOMO energy level of the donor, while the JSC is mainly determined by the visible-light absorption and charge carrier transport and collection.11 However, the common electron acceptor (PC71BM) shows a narrow visible-light absorption window with a deep LUMO energy level, which has limited photon harvesting and energy level differences.

Recently, ternary structure OSCs have attracted more attention due to their broad visible-light absorption, enhanced LUMO energy level for the acceptor and simple processing conditions used in single-junction OSCs.1,12 The ternary photoactive layer generally contains three components: the dominating donor:acceptor (D:A) system and the third component [the electron donor (D1:D2:A) and the electron acceptor (D:A1:A2) can be used as third component materials].1,13,14 The main fundamental working mechanisms of third component materials can be summarized in the following way: exciton dissociation, energy transfer, charge transfer and visible-light absorption and so on are all different work mechanisms of the photoactive layer compared with those of a common binary OSC. Thus, the selection of third component materials plays a vital role in photovoltaic performance.15,16 The Chunru group has synthesized fullerene derivatives of dihydronaphthyl-based C60 bisadduct (NCBA), with the chemical structure as shown in Fig. S1b.17,18 The structure has the advantage of improved absorption in the 300500 nm region, a shallower LUMO energy level and a higher thermally driven crystallization temperature compared with PC71BM.19

In this work, we report an improvement in ternary organic solar cell (OSC) performance based on the use of blended thin films of PC71BM and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2 as a binary photoactive layer and dihydronaphthyl-based C60 bisadduct (NCBA) as a third component material. The charge-carrier dynamics in the ternary OSCs were investigated by photoluminescence (PL) and time-resolved PL (TRPL) spectroscopy. The photovoltaic performance and thermal stability of the ternary OSCs were also investigated systematically.

3. Results and discussion

The OSC has the structure of the ITO/ZnO/p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:blend acceptor/MoO3/Ag, with the blend acceptor consisting of NCBA and PC71BM (the weight concentration of NCBA in the blend acceptor varies from 0 to 18 wt%, and neat NCBA). Fig. 1(a) presents the currentdensityvoltage (JV) curves with 0, 12, and 18 wt% NCBA as a third component material and neat NCBA as an electron acceptor under simulated 100 mW cm2 AM 1.5-G illumination; all OSC parameters are shown in Table 1. Both the VOC and the other photovoltaic parameters (JSC, FF, and PCE) show different change trends for increasing NCBA concentration. The binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM OSC exhibits lower photovoltaic performance (VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE values of 0.78 V, 14.9 mA cm2, 63.8%, and 7.41%, respectively). The photovoltaic performance is notably enhanced with increasing NCBA concentration, with the best values achieved with 12 wt% NCBA as the third component material (VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE values of 0.79 V, 17.1 mA cm2, 67.9%, and 9.17%, respectively). However, after continued increases in the NCBA concentration, different change trends were observed for the VOC and the other photovoltaic parameters (JSC, FF, and PCE). The VOC value was continuously enhanced from 0.79 V (12 wt% NCBA concentration) to 0.8 V (18 wt% NCBA concentration), reaching the highest VOC value with neat NCBA as an acceptor material (VOC = 0.81 V). The JSC, FF, and PCE values exhibited a strong decrease (JSC, FF, and PCE values of 15.4 mA cm2, 65.2%, and 8.03%, respectively, with 18 wt% NCBA). The worst photovoltaic performance was observed with neat NCBA as the acceptor material, even if the VOC value was significantly enhanced (JSC, FF, and PCE values of 12.2 mA cm2, 54.4%, and 5.37%, respectively). Fig. 1(b) shows the external quantum efficiency (EQE) curves for the binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM-based OSC and the optimized photovoltaic performance of the ternary OSC. The JSC values calculated from the EQE spectrum are 14.3 and 16.4 mA cm2, corresponding to NCBA concentrations of 0 and 12 wt%, respectively (the JSC values, measured under simulated 100 mW cm2 AM 1.5 G illumination, were 14.9 and 17.1 mA cm2, respectively), with the average error of both the measured and calculated JSC values lower than 5%.20 This finding clearly illustrates the agreement with the higher JSC of the OSC mentioned above. We focus next on investigating the reason for the different change trends for the NCBA concentration dependent photovoltaic parameters. At the same time, the reduction in JSC is mainly due to electrical losses rather than optical losses (as shown in the IQE curve in Fig. 1b). From the absorption spectrum of binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM thin films and ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM thin films, we found that the charge carrier recombination decreases when NCBA partially replaces PC71BM, as revealed in the IQE curve and the absorption spectrum. These results suggest that the IQE was enhanced because the photogenerated charge carriers weakened the charge carrier recombination within the photoactive layer before extraction from the photoactive layer to the electrode.

Fig. S2(a) shows the visible absorption coefficients for NCBA, PC71BM and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2 at the same thickness (approximately 30 nm). Among the three materials, p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2 presented much higher absorption coefficients than the NCBA and PC71BM acceptor, indicating that p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2 had the predominant absorption contribution in these photoactive layers. The weak absorption spectrum in the range 500690 nm is displayed compared with that in the binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM thin films. The visible-light absorption ratio for the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76) thin films is reduced to approximately 4% compared with that of the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM (3:2) thin films.

PL and TRPL spectroscopy techniques were employed to investigate the working mechanism of the ternary photoactive layer, with the results shown in Fig. 2(a) and (b). The photoactive thin films were excited at a wavelength of 676 nm, with the neat p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2 thin films exhibiting a strong PL emission intensity and an emission peak at approximately 735 nm [as shown in Fig. 2(a)]. However, after doping electron acceptor materials into the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2 thin films, the significant fluorescence quenching among the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA binary thin films and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM ternary thin films gives quenching ratios of 89.4%, 85.8%, and 93.5% of the initial p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2 PL intensity, respectively. This result illustrates the more efficient exciton dissociation process at the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2/blend-acceptor-material interface compared with those of the binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2/PC71BM and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2/NCBA interface.29,30 After doping PC71BM and NCBA into p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2, the strongly reduced fluorescence lifetime () values are shown in Fig. 2(b), along with the further reduced values for the NCBA:PC71BM blend as an electron-acceptor material. The values for the neat p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2, binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2: PC71BM, and ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76) are 7.5, 3.9, 2.6, and 2.2 ns, respectively. The short value for the ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76) thin films is attributed to the higher efficiency of exciton dissociation and to the fast speed of charge-carrier transport at the three interfaces of p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2/NCBA, p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2/PC71BM, and NCBA/PC71BM, which avoids electronhole-pair recombination within the photoactive layer. This result indicates a potential route for improving NCBA exciton utilization efficiency, in addition to improving exciton dissociation at the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2/NCBA and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2/PC71BM interfaces.3133

The space-charge-limited current (SCLC) method was employed to measure the charge-carrier mobility of ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM devices as a function of NCBA concentration. The SCLC curves are shown in Fig. 3, with all the hole-mobility (h) and electron-mobility (e) values summarized in Table 2. The h value exhibited a slight decrease with a lower NCBA concentration and a significant decrease with neat NCBA as an electron acceptor; h values of 2.74 103, 2.62 103, 2.42 103, and 0.95 103 cm2 V1 s1 correspond to the NCBA concentrations of 0, 12, 18, and 100 wt%, respectively, while the e values are 2.25 103, 2.54 103, 2.15 103, and 1.05 103 cm2 V1 s1, respectively. The enhancement of e could be ascribed to the fact that a small amount of NCBA can boost the exciton dissociation and enhance the charge-carrier transfer at the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2/blend-acceptor interface.34 The h value decreased linearly with increasing NCBA concentration, which is attributed to h weakening associated with exciton dissociation and the fact that hole transport and collection are governed by the donor/anode modified layer interface.15,35 While the excess NCBA replaces PC71BM (the NCBA concentration is higher than 12 wt%), it leads to a significant decrease in the e value, which possesses the disadvantages of charge-carrier extraction from the photoactive layer to the electrode.5 Simultaneously, the charge-carrier mobilities are determined by the crystallinity and the surface morphology of the photoactive layer.3638 Thus, different change trends for e and h are observed depending on the NCBA concentration. The balance of electron and hole mobility plays vital roles in photovoltaic performance.39,40 The e/h value of 0.97 for 12 wt%-NCBA-based OSCs indicates more balanced charge-carrier transport within the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM photoactive layer, resulting in a higher FF and better photovoltaic performance. However, the excess NCBA concentration will cause lower charge-carrier mobility and imbalanced charge-carrier transport, which causes the electrons to accumulate at the cathode interface, thus decreasing photovoltaic performance.41 We next investigate the effect of NCBA concentration on the crystallinity and the surface morphology of the photoactive layer.

Three-dimensional (3D) and phase images from atomic-force-microscopy (AFM) reveal the surface morphology of p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM ternary thin films dependent on the NCBA concentration [as shown in Fig. S4(a)S4(d)]. For the binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM and ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM thin films, it was clear that the statistical average roughness of the ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM thin films had slightly increased from 3.8 nm (0 wt%) to 4.5 nm after adding only 12 wt% NCBA, and further increased to 5.2 nm with 18 wt% NCBA replacing PC71BM. However, the higher statistical average roughness value of 12.75 nm was obtained when neat NCBA was used as the electron acceptor. At the same time, the higher statistical average roughness will damage the contact between p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM thin films and the charge-carrier extraction layer, leading to worse charge carrier transport and collection.49,50 As shown in the AFM phase images (Fig. S4b, d, f and h), while the weight ratio of NCBA in the thin films is above 12%, aggregations in the ternary photoactive layer were formed due to the high crystallinity. However, when using the neat NCBA as an electron acceptor, the aggregations in the photoactive layer were damaged despite the higher crystallinity. The aggregations can degrade the surface morphology of the films and the device performance.

We further investigated the thermal-stability as a function of thermal annealing time for the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM, ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76), and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.36:1.64) OSCs that have undergone continuous thermal annealing at 90 C for 100 h. Fig. 7 shows the dependence of the normalized photovoltaic parameters (VOC, FF, JSC and PCE) on thermal annealing time for the OSCs. After 100 h of thermal annealing treatment, the three OSCs each exhibit different decrease ratios. In the initial stage (during 0 to 20 hours), all the devices show significant attenuation, and the decrease ratio for the binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM OSC, ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA: PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76)-based OSC and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2: NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.36:1.64)-based OSC is 0.72, 0.88 and 0.89, respectively. For the increased thermal annealing time, the binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM OSC exhibits a significant decrease, with only 52% of the initial PCE maintained at the end of the experiment (the decrease ratio of VOC, FF and JSC are 15.2%, 19.5% and 21% respectively). However, for the ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76)-based OSC and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.36:1.64)-based OSC, the data show a gentle attenuation even for a long-term thermal annealing treatment, with a final PCE value of over 80% realized under 100 hours thermal annealing treatment (the decrease ratios for VOC, FF and JSC are 4%, 7.5% and 6.8%, respectively, for the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76)-based OSC, while the decrease ratios for VOC, FF and JSC are 4%, 7.3% and 6.6%, respectively, for the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.36:1.64)-based OSC). Compared with the binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM OSC, the two ternary OSCs exhibit better thermal-stability and a similar decay ratio. This result implies better thermal annealing for the ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM thin films.

We further investigated the effect of thermal annealing treatment on the crystallinity and surface morphology of the photoactive layer. Fig. S6 and S7 show the XRD curves and AFM images [the 3D and phase images measured using AFM for binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM for (a and b), ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76) for (c and d), and ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.36:1.64) thin films for (e and f)], respectively, for binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM, ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76), and ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.36:1.64) thin films after 100 h of thermal annealing treatment at 90 C. After the thermal annealing treatment, the diffraction peak intensities were found to significantly decrease, with many complex peaks introduced for the binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM thin film. Furthermore, the statistical average roughness for the binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM thin film was strongly enhanced (a value of approximately 180 nm), which is attributed to the disrupted molecular arrangement and weakened crystallinities.44,80 However, both the ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76) and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.36:1.64) thin films exhibited a slight decrease in the diffraction peak intensities and a negligible increase in statistical average roughness, i.e., 6.8 and 6.5 nm, respectively. From the AFM phase images, the strong aggregations in the binary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM thin film are shown under 100 hours of thermal annealing treatment. However, for the ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76) and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.36:1.64) thin films under 100 hours of thermal annealing treatment, the data show a stable surface morphology for the photoactive layer.

The higher crystallinity and the smooth surface morphology of the photoactive layer favour the interface contact between the ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM and charge-carrier extraction layer, leading to better photovoltaic performance as a function of thermal annealing time. We concluded that the small amount of NCBA replacing the PC71BM molecule enhances the crystallinities and fixes the surface morphology of the ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM thin film, resulting in improved photovoltaic performance and thermal stability.

4. Conclusions

In conclusion, in this paper, we report highly efficient OSCs with p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2 as donor materials and a blend of NCBA and PC71BM as an acceptor material. The best PCE, 9.1%, was achieved with p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76) as a photoactive layer, which is much larger than the 7.65% and 5.37% PCE values obtained for the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:PC71BM binary OSC and p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA binary OSCs, respectively. The improvement in PCE is mainly attributed to an increase in JSC and FF due to enhanced photon harvesting, improved exciton dissociation, increased charge-carrier mobility, and reduced monomolecular recombination and bimolecular recombination under open-circuit and short-circuit conditions, respectively. At the same time, replacing the PC71BM molecule with a small number of NCBA molecules led to an enhancement of the crystallinity and the thermal stability of the ternary p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2:NCBA:PC71BM thin films after a long thermal annealing treatment time. Thus, the p-DTS-(FBTTh2)2: NCBA:PC71BM (3:0.24:1.76) ternary OSC exhibited remarkable photovoltaic performance and thermal stability.
