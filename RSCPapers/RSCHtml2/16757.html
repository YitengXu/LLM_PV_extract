Abstract

We report on the synthesis and characterization of a donorâ€“acceptor ruthenium polypyridyl complex as a photosensitizer for p-type dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs). The electrochemical, photophysical, and photovoltaic performance of two ruthenium-based photosensitizers were tested in NiO-based DSSCs; bis-(2,2â€²-bipyridine-4,4â€²-dicarboxylic acid)2N-(1,10-phenanthroline)-4-nitronaphthalene-1,8-dicarboximide ruthenium(II), ([Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)](PF6)2) and tris-(2,2â€²-bipyridine-4,4â€²-dicarboxylic acid)3 ruthenium(II), [(Ru(dcb)3)Cl2]. The presence of an electron-accepting group, 4-nitronaphthalene-1,8-dicarboximide (NMI), attached to the phenanthroline of [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ resulted in long-lived charge separation between reduced [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ and NiO valence band holes; 10â€“50 Î¼s. In the reduced state for [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+, the electron localized on the distal NMI group. In tests with I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’ and Co(4,4â€²-di-tert-butyl-bipyridine)32+/3+ electrolytes, [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ outperformed [Ru(dcb)3]2+ in solar cell efficiency in devices. A record APCE (25%) was achieved for a ruthenium photosensitizer in a p-type DSSC. Insights on photosensitizer regeneration kinetics are included.

Introduction

For the last two decades, dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) have been an increasingly large sector of research focussed on the capture and conversion of solar energy into electrical and chemical potential.1â€“3 The long-studied topic made a massive leap in both efficiency and research interest with the discovery by O'Regan and GrÃ¤tzel4 in 1991. They succeeded in converting light to electrical power with a good efficiency using a nanocrystalline n-type semiconductor coupled with a photosensitizing ruthenium dye. The mechanism consists of electron injection from the ruthenium dye to the semiconductor conduction band. Since GrÃ¤tzel's report, many studies have followed to characterize and improve n-type DSSCs.5,6

More recently, a handful of groups have turned their attention to the complementary process on p-type semiconductors where hole injection into the valence band of the semiconductor results from light absorption by the photosensitizer, in contrast to electron injection into n-type semiconductors.7â€“10 Together, these processes can be combined into one system to produce a tandem cell with the capabilities surpassing the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit.11,12 For tandem cells to become a reality, the p-type and n-type half-cell processes should be comparable in photocurrent. Currently, n-type DSSCs are several times more efficient than their p-type counterparts, putting the impetus on increasing the efficiency of the p-type cells.

The most efficient p-type DSSCs have been fabricated using mesoporous NiO material. Although improvements have been measured,13 these cells are plagued with low efficiency values, partly due to rapid (ps) recombination of the semiconductor hole with the reduced dye.13 To overcome this fundamental obstacle in the p-type system, work has been done to increase the lifetime of the reduced dye and semiconductor hole. Previous work has shown an increase in lifetime with chromophores that localize the excess electron farther away from the semiconductor surface.8,11,14 Significantly longer charge separation can be achieved in dyes with charge transfer (CT) character, the longest being with weakly coupled, separate acceptor units. The latter have been long-lived enough to allow the use of redox couples other than I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’,15,16 most notably cobalt polypyridine complexes that have recently given breakthrough results in TiO2 DSSCs and could then allow extension to tandem cells with CoIII/II.17,18

Ruthenium polypyridine complexes are interesting in this regard, as they have intrinsic CT character. They also have rather broad absorption bands and offer tunability of the ligands for further optimization. Ruthenium dyes as photosensitizers have been reported for p-type DSSCs, but with modest results.19 This study presents a p-type DSSC utilizing a Ru-based photosensitizer with an appended acceptor group, 4-nitro-naphthalene-1,8-dicarboximide (NMI). Similar to the study using organic dyes, increased DSSC efficiencies were observed with this photosensitizer-acceptor dyad and a CoIII/II electrolyte compared with cells fabricated using a model ruthenium polypyridyl complex. Complementary kinetic studies were performed to identify processes that improve or compromise efficiency in the cells. A combination of faster regeneration rates and decreased recombination rates resulted in the increased efficiency for the sensitizer containing an appended electron acceptor group. We present evidence that suggests recombination with Ru-based sensitizers on NiO is intrinsically slower than with simple organic dyes.20â€“22

Electrochemistry

All electrochemical measurements were conducted in acetonitrile (Sigma-Aldrich, spectroscopic grade). Cyclic voltammetry was carried out using an Autolab potentiostat with a GPES electrochemical interface (Eco Chemie). The working electrode was a glassy carbon disc (diameter 3 mm, Alumina polished prior to use) with a non-aqueous Ag+/Ag reference electrode (CH Instruments, 0.01 M in AgNO3 in acetonitrile) and the ferrocenium/ferrocene (Fc+/Fc) couple in acetonitrile as an external standard. All solutions were prepared from dry acetonitrile in 0.1 M TBAPF6 (Fluka, electrochemical grade).

Photoelectrochemical characterization

Incident photon-to-current efficiency (IPCE) and current/voltage (I/V) measurements were performed as previously reported.8

Solar cells preparation

Preparation and assembly of the sandwich solar cells followed methods previously described.23 1 g NiCl2 anhydride and 1 g F108 polymer were dissolved in a mixture of 3 g of deionized water and 6 g of 99.5% of ethanol. The resulting green viscous solution was sonicated overnight. The mixture was centrifuged for 3 hours to sediment out undissolved NiCl2 and amorphous NiO. After centrifugation, the supernatant was collected as three separate batches for use as doctor-blading paste. Mesoporous NiO electrodes were prepared by spreading the doctor-blading paste onto conducting F-doped SnO2 (FTO) glass masked with adhesive tape and sintered in a furnace at 440 Â°C for 35 minutes.

After dyeing the mesoporous NiO electrodes in ethanolic solutions of each photosensitizer, solar cells were constructed by heat sealing the thermoplastic Surlyn between the NiO electrode and a pre-drilled platinised counter-electrode. The electrolyte solution was introduced into the cell through holes in the counter-electrode. To complete the solar cell, the holes in the counter-electrode were heat sealed with Surlyn and a glass microscope cover slip.

Transient absorption

The differences in photovoltaic performance for the two photosensitizers can be explained through examination of two competing reactions involving the reduced [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ on NiO. It has been shown for a range of different types of dyes that hole injection from the excited photosensitizer into the NiO valence band occurs predominantly on the sub-ps time scale.8,13,14,31â€“34 This means the reduced dye forms well before the nanosecond time resolution of our present experiment. From this reduced species chemisorbed to the NiO surface, recombination and regeneration are the two mechanisms for ground state recovery. First, recombination will short circuit the cell by allowing the NiO hole and reduced sensitizer to geminately react. This is detrimental to the APCE and overall efficiency of the cell. Second, regeneration by electron transfer from the reduced photosensitizer to the CoIII complex promotes the flow of charge through the cell and increases the APCE and device efficiency.

Recombination times have been measured using nanosecond transient absorption spectroscopy on assembled cells dyed with the respective photosensitizers and filled with an inert electrolyte (no redox mediator). In this configuration, the intrinsic dynamics associated with each sensitizer are modulated only by attachment to the NiO surface. Under these circumstances, the reduction of the sensitizer through hole injection and recombination between the reduced photosensitizer and valence band hole in NiO were observed.

In the experiments on NiO, we see no evidence for the excited-state of [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ (spectra shown in ESI Fig. S5â€¡) on any time scale within our temporal resolution (ca. 10 ns), which suggests that hole injection/sensitizer reduction occurs on a timescale of less than 10 ns.

The transient spectral measurements showed a peak at 490 nm, a shoulder around 560 nm, and an increasing absorption from 700 nm towards the red detection limit (Fig. 4) matching the spectroelectrochemical data of the NMI-phen ligand for the reduced species (Fig. 2). These corresponding features were observed in the transient absorption data for the [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ complex when attached to NiO.

By comparing the transient spectra of the [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ complex on NiO and the spectroelectrochemistry of NMI-phen in solution with literature values for similar reduced NMI molecules, it is apparent that for both [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ and NMI-phen, reduction is localized entirely on the NMI group.25,28 This tells us that the transient species observed within the first few hundred nanoseconds after photoexcitation is due to the reduced NMI group in the [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ complex. NiO holes absorb throughout the visible region, more towards the blue35 and should give broad, indistinct contributions to the transients. All these signals disappear on the time scale of tens of microseconds, which is attributed to recombination between the NiO hole and the reduced NMI group on the dye.

The control [Ru(dcb)3]2+ complex showed smaller signals over this wavelength range. The peaks at 510 nm and just below 400 nm, together with a ground state bleach around 470 nm (on top of the NiO(+) absorption), are attributable to the reduced complex [Ru(dcb)3]+. The smaller signals may be due to more rapid recombination components for [Ru(dcb)3]2+ than for [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+, which is attributed to the reduced NMI in [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ being situated further from the NiO interface than the reduced dcb in [Ru(dcb)3]2+.

From electrochemistry and comparison of the electrochemically reduced species to the photoexcited species, the excess electron can be said to be localized on the NMI unit, and the [Ru(dcb)3]2+ species, respectively. These observations represent the first spectroscopic identification of the reduced Ru-based photosensitizer on NiO. With this signal, a time constant for recombination can be positively established.

As can be seen from Table 3, the average recombination time, ã€ˆÏ„ã€‰, for [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ is an order of magnitude slower than for [Ru(dcb)3]2+. This can be attributed to the longer distance of the excess electron from the NiO surface when localized on the NMI-phen. A larger distance from the NiO decreases the electronic overlap necessary for rapid electron recombination, effectively slowing down the process and extending the lifetimes of the reduced photosensitizer and the hole in the substrate. Lengthening of the recombination timescale can, by itself, increase efficiency by allowing for a higher yield of hole transport through the substrate and regeneration of the sensitizer by the electrolyte.

Table 4 shows the fitting parameters for cells made with the [Co(dtb)3]3+/2+ redox couple. Both recombination and regeneration processes are occurring in this configuration. Once again, average times are given for each complex and the trend is opposite to that seen for recombination alone. Whereas the [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ recovery time decreases by an order of magnitude upon addition of Co3+/2+, the [Ru(dcb)3]2+ shows no significant change in recovery time.

Comparison of the given average times ã€ˆÏ„ã€‰ from Tables 3 and 4 show that for [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ on NiO, the rate in presence of Co3+/2+ is an order of magnitude faster than without Co3+/2+. This implies the average rate of regeneration is much faster than that of recombination, which will have a marked positive effect on the overall cell performance. Additionally, the lifetimes of the reduced species for the [Ru(dcb)3]2+ complex are similar with and without the [Co(dtb)3]3+/2+ redox mediator. This indicates that very little regeneration can occur with this cell configuration. These differences between the complexes are in qualitative agreement with the differences in APCE values using the [Co(dtb)3]3+/2+ redox mediator. Whereas the maximum APCE is only ca. 1% for [Ru(dcb)3]2+ it is ca. 25% for [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ (Fig. 3).

From the ten-fold shorter average lifetime of the reduced [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ in the presence of [Co(dtb)3]3+/2+ one may at first expect an APCE value around 90%. There are several possibilities why this is not the case. First, there may still be some losses at longer time scales due to holeâ€“electrolyte recombination, although these reactions have been shown to be inefficient for many sensitized NiO systems.16 Second, the kinetics measured here are strongly heterogeneous and the dyes reacting fastest in recombination may not be the ones reacting fastest with the electrolyte; therefore it is difficult to predict an exact yield. Third, there may be dyeâ€“NiO recombination reactions on a time-scale faster than resolved in the present study. Previous results have shown recombination reactions for a variety of dyes on NiO-electrodes on the sub-nanosecond timescale, and it is possible that this occurs to some extent also in these complexes. Nevertheless, they are at least greatly mitigated compared to the cases with perylene diimide and coumarin sensitizers,13 as we could clearly observe the reduced [Ru(dcb)3]2+ on the ns time scale. Moreover by the addition of an electron-accepting group to the complex, removing electron density from the interface between the photoanode and the photosensitizer, we could further slow down recombination and increase regeneration by the [Co(dtb)3]3+/2+ electrolyte.

Finally, we note that regeneration with Co3+ is about one order of magnitude faster than for previous organic dyads on NiO that had a naphtalenediimide (NDI) acceptor on a peryleneimide dye, although E0 for the NDI couples are almost identical (due to the nitro group in the present case). Moreover, the Ru2+ unit is charged and would repel the Co3+ electrolyte. It is possible that the faster regeneration instead is because the present NMI unit is less sterically hindered than the previous NDI that has an octyl substituent. We also speculate that the [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ geometry on NiO may be more orthogonal to the surface and thus more favorable for Co3+ access. With four carboxylic groups [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+ can bind in different ways, with NMI closer to the surface or more orthogonal to it. It is conceivable that the former case results in much faster recombination and that the recombination and regeneration processes observed here on the microsecond timescale relate mainly to the complexes in the orthogonal geometry. This would also explain why the APCE is much lower than the ca. 90% that the regeneration vs. recombination rate suggests. Further work with ultrafast spectroscopy may be able to elucidate both the injection and possible sub-ns components of recombination dynamics.

Conclusions

The work presented in this paper has shown the potential improvements that can be made to the efficiencies of p-type NiO DSSCs by understanding competing kinetic processes and using synthetic design to favor the desired process. It has been shown that the addition of an electron accepting ligand on a ruthenium(II)-based photosensitizer increases the efficiency of the cell by two processes: first, the recombination time is increased, providing more time for the flow of the hole to the back contact of the photoanode to occur. This was determined from the observation of a reduced transient species for Ru-based sensitizers, which was done for the first time in p-type cells. Second, the timescale for regeneration of the reduced sensitizer occurs faster for the appended complex. Processes occurring on the sub nanosecond timescale have yet to be fully characterized as a function of sensitizer. This work is planned and will provide increased insight into the kinetic â€œleaksâ€ affecting the efficiencies of these NiO/Ru-based sensitizer cells.

A significant improvement in overall efficiency as well as IPCE have been observed from the replacement of [Ru(dcb)3]2+ with [Ru(dcb)2(NMI-phen)]2+. It is clear that the extra electron accepting ligand plays a dramatic role in increasing the efficiency of these cells. Electronic separation of the electron from the NiO surface along with the energetic matching of the [Co(dtb)3]3+/2+ redox couple show a dramatic improvement in both cell efficiency and kinetic competition, similar to what has been reported for organic dyes on NiO.8,31,32

Increased improvement of these efficiencies and the ratio of rates through synthetic design hold the promise of closing the gap between n-type and p-type DSSCs that is necessary to the success of tandem cells.
