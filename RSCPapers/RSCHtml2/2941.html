Triplet photosensitizers: from molecular design to applications

Jianzhang Zhao *, Wanhua Wu , Jifu Sun and Song Guo
State Key Laboratory of Fine Chemicals, School of Chemical Engineering, Dalian University of Technology, E 208 Western Compus, 2 Ling-Gong Road, Dalian 116012, P.R. China. E-mail: zhaojzh@dlut.edu.cn

First published on 28th February 2013

Abstract

Triplet photosensitizers (PSs) are compounds that can be efficiently excited to the triplet excited state which subsequently act as catalysts in photochemical reactions. The name is originally derived from compounds that were used to transfer the triplet energy to other compounds that have only a small intrinsic triplet state yield. Triplet PSs are not only used for triplet energy transfer, but also for photocatalytic organic reactions, photodynamic therapy (PDT), photoinduced hydrogen production from water and triplet–triplet annihilation (TTA) upconversion. A good PS should exhibit strong absorption of the excitation light, a high yield of intersystem crossing (ISC) for efficient production of the triplet state, and a long triplet lifetime to allow for the reaction with a reactant molecule. Most transition metal complexes show efficient ISC, but small molar absorption coefficients in the visible spectral region and short-lived triplet excited states, which make them unsuitable as triplet PSs. One obstacle to the development of new triplet PSs is the difficulty in predicting the ISC of chromophores, especially of organic compounds without any heavy atoms. This review article summarizes some molecular design rationales for triplet PSs, based on the molecular structural factors that facilitate ISC. The design of transition metal complexes with large molar absorption coefficients in the visible spectral region and long-lived triplet excited states is presented. A new method of using a spin converter to construct heavy atom-free organic triplet PSs is discussed, with which ISC becomes predictable, C60 being an example. To enhance the performance of triplet PSs, energy funneling based triplet PSs are proposed, which show broadband absorption in the visible region. Applications of triplet PSs in photocatalytic organic reactions, hydrogen production, triplet–triplet annihilation upconversion and luminescent oxygen sensing are briefly introduced.

1. Introduction

1.1. General background

Upon photoexcitation, an excited singlet state of an organic chromophore will be initially populated. Usually the lowest excited singlet state S1 is involved in the subsequent photophysical processes (Kasha's rule). For example, radiative S1 → S0 decay produces the fluorescence.1–3 The triplet state of the chromophore can be populated via S1 → Tn intersystem crossing (ISC, n ≥ 1), thus efficient ISC is essential for triplet photosensitizers (PSs). Triplet PSs should show strong absorption (large molar absorption coefficient, ε) of visible or UV light and efficient ISC to produce the triplet excited state.

In comparison to the studies of fluorophores,4–9 the investigation of triplet excited state-related chromophores or the photophysical process is less developed (Fig. 1).10–12 Chromophores with the triplet excited state that has been populated upon excitation (not necessarily limited to photoexcitation) can be used in many areas, such as electroluminescence,13–20 phosphorescent bioimaging or molecular sensing,21–23 photodynamic therapy (PDT, via sensitization of singlet oxygen, 1O2),24–27 photoinitiated polymerization and more recently photocatalytic organic reactions.28–31 Different from the fluorophores, which are mainly used for luminescence purposes, triplet PSs are mainly used as triplet energy donors to initiate photochemical and photophysical processes, such as PDT,24–27 photocatalytic organic reactions,28–31 and triplet–triplet annihilation (TTA) upconversion (UC).32–34 An ideal triplet PS should show strong absorption of visible light, high triplet state quantum yields (effective ISC) and a long-lived triplet excited state.35,36

It is still a challenge to design a triplet PS, i.e. a chromophore with predetermined ISC property, especially the chromophores without any heavy atoms. This difficulty is mainly due to the lack of established relationships between the ISC and molecular structures.1

Chemical modification of a known chromophore, the method widely used for preparation of new fluorophores,4–7 is often not applicable to triplet PSs. ISC can disappear with subtle derivatization of a known triplet PS.1,3 For example, one may postulate that anthracene and 9,10-diphenylanthracene (DPA) will show similar fluorescence properties because of their similar π-conjugation frameworks. However, anthracene and DPA show drastically different photophysical properties. Anthracene shows a fluorescence quantum yield (ΦF) of 27% (in ethanol), due to the efficient S1 → T2 ISC.1 But for DPA, ΦF is up to 95%. The reason for these differences is the fact that phenyl substitution shifts the T2 state above the S1 state, thus changing the ISC quantum yields (ΦISC) of anthracene from 73% to less than 5% for DPA.1,37

The principal photophysical processes involved in fluorescence, i.e. S0 → S1 → S0 (simplified consideration, Fig. 2), are usually quantum mechanically allowed. The triplet excited state related processes (S0 → S1 → T1 or S0 → S1 → Tn → T1), however, are basically quantum mechanically forbidden.

This review article will give a brief introduction to ISC of organic chromophores, and will focus on molecular design methods and the principal photophysics of triplet PSs.1 Typically, newly developed triplet PSs will also be discussed. Moreover, the methods for the design of triplet PSs that show strong absorptivity of visible light and long-lived triplet excited states will be summarized. We will also present some new ideas for the design of heavy atom-free organic chromophores that show predetermined ISC property, and triplet PSs that show broadband absorption in the visible spectral region. The limitations of these methods will also be discussed.

1.2. To produce the triplet excited states of organic chromophores: ISC

Population of the T1 state of an organic chromophore requires a non-radiative transition S1 → Tn or S1 → T1, i.e., ISC. This transition involves two different spin states. Energy and the total angular momentum (orbital and spin) have to be conserved during ISC. The most important interaction that couples two spin states and that provides a means of conserving the total angular momentum is the coupling of the electron spin with the orbital angular momentum, i.e., the spin–orbit coupling (SOC).1

In the following sections we will briefly introduce the triplet PSs and discuss various factors that enhance ISC.1

1.2.1. Triplet PSs based on the heavy atom effect. Atoms with large atomic number can induce strong SOC, and as a result, enhance ISC. An electron moving in the vicinity of a nucleus with positive charge Z will be accelerated to relativistic velocity. As a result the coupling of the spin (μS) and the orbital (μL) magnetic momentum is strong. This heavy atom effect is actually a nuclear-charge effect,1 and scales with Z4 (Z is the nuclear charge). This mechanism is used for the design of the heavy atom-containing triplet PSs (see later sections). Typical heavy atoms include Ir, Pt, Ru, Os, Re, Rh, I and Br, etc.1

Therefore, one straightforward approach to prepare triplet PSs is to attach heavy atoms to a chromophore. Various transition metal complexes have been prepared following this line, but mainly for electro-luminescence.38,39 These complexes can be used as triplet PSs if the absorption in the visible region is strong. Unfortunately most of the complexes show small molar absorption coefficients (ε) in the visible spectral range (e.g. ε < 10000 M−1 cm−1). Triplet PSs with iodo- or bromo-substituents and the transition metal complexes belong to this category.24,40,41

1.2.2. Chromophores with low-lying n–π* transitions. Similar to the radiative transitions between different electronic states, selection rules apply to ISC.1 For example, the energy and angular momentum must be conserved in ISC.1 Perturbation theory requires that the energy difference between the two states involved in the transition should be small and close to the perturbation energy. If the S1 and T1 states originate from the same electronic configuration, the energy difference between these states is twice the electron exchange integral J for the pair of singly occupied orbitals.1J will decrease exponentially as the separation of the orbitals increases.

For n–π* transition, the exchange integral and consequently the S1/T1 states energy gap is small. ISC is much easier for these compounds, such as benzophenone.1 The exchange integral for π–π* transitions is usually much larger. As a result the energy difference between the S1 and T1 states is large for the compounds with low-lying π–π* transitions. Therefore ISC is weak in normal organic aromatic chromophores with large π-conjugation frameworks. According to El-Sayed's selection rule for ISC,1 S1 (n, π*) → T1(π,π*) is an allowed transition because the angular momentum is conserved and the S1/T1 state energy gap is small. The El-Sayed rule states that S1(π–π*)–T1(π–π*) is a forbidden transition. Thus most aromatic chromophores are fluorescent and the S1 → T1 ISC is ineffective. As a result, these compounds cannot be used as triplet PSs.

1.2.3. Chromophores with exciton coupling. When two identical chromophores were incorporated into one molecule in close vicinity, but without π-conjugation, then the exciton coupling, i.e. the interaction of the transition dipole moments of the chromophores, leads to two delocalized excited states for every local excitation of the single chromophore. If the lower of the two singlet exciton states is closer in energy to the triplet state, ISC can be enhanced.1,43,44 Exceptions do exist. For example, it was found that ISC of a Bodipy dimer is not dependent on the exciton coupling effect.26,45
1.2.4. Triplet PSs based on an intramolecular spin converter. Without the heavy atom effect, it is difficult to predict the ISC property of a chromophore from the point of view of organic chemists, i.e. the molecular structures. Therefore, development of a general molecular motif for triplet PSs is highly desired. With the general molecular motifs, ISC should become predictable and the chemical modification of the molecular structure should be feasible, and it should not eliminate the ISC property.

Concerning this aspect, a dyad as a triplet PS can be constructed, in which an intramolecular energy acceptor with intrinsic ISC property is used as a spin converter together with a light-harvesting energy donor. The most promising aspect of this strategy is that the ISC of these heavy atom-free chromophores is predictable. At present C60 is used as such a spin converter.46–53 Recently we attached antennae to C60 and the resulting dyads/triads were successfully used as triplet PSs for 1O2 photosensitization and TTA UC.49–53

Furthermore, most of the available triplet PSs are based on a single chromophore molecular structural profile, which cannot effectively harvest the excitation energy of a panchromatic light source, for example, solar light. In this review we will also describe a method for enhancing the efficiency of triplet PSs, i.e., an energy funneling strategy based on multi-intramolecular energy transfer (EnT) from the antenna to the energy acceptor. These triplet PSs show broadband absorption in the visible range. However, it should be pointed out that intramolecular electron transfer is a competitive channel to the EnT to dissipate the excited state energy of the energy donor.

1.2.5. Spectroscopy for the study of triplet excited states. Different from the singlet excited state, the triplet excited state of a chromophore is not definitely populated upon photoexcitation of a chromophore. Some triplet PSs are phosphorescent,13,20,35,54,55 but some are non-phosphorescent, thus appropriate spectroscopy is crucial for studying the non-luminescent triplet excited states, as well as the lifetime of the T1 state. Nanosecond time-resolved transient absorption (TA) spectroscopy is a typical method for such a purpose (Fig. 2).1,56–58

TA spectroscopy is based on the pulse–probe (or pump–probe) method. Two light sources were used for acquiring the TA spectrum. One beam from a xenon lamp is for monitoring the absorption change, another pulsed laser is for pump purposes. TA spectroscopy can be used for studying the localization of the triplet excited states of molecular dyads.49,51,59,60 Note that transient species other than those at the triplet excited state can also be detected by TA spectra, such as charge separated species, the cation/anion radicals.

A complementary method for studying the triplet excited state to TA spectroscopy is the time-resolved photoacoustic spectroscopy, or laser induced optoacoustic spectroscopy, which are based on the photothermal methods. These methods have been used for studying the lifetime as well as the quantum yield of triplet excited states of some chromophores.61

1.2.6. Applications of the triplet PSs. The applications of fluorophores are normally based on their luminescent ability. However, different from the fluorophores, the applications of triplet PSs are usually based on the triplet–triplet-energy-transfer (TTET) from the PS to the acceptor, or photoinduced electron transfer (ET) between the triplet PSs and the reactants. At present, the applications are still limited by using the triplet PSs that show weak absorption of visible light, such as in the photocatalytic organic reaction, Ru(bpy)3Cl2 is still the most popular photocatalyst, although it gives weak absorption in the visible region.13–28 Thus development of new triplet PSs that show strong absorption of visible light and long-lived triplet excited states is highly desired. Conventionally transition metal complexes, which show weak absorption in the visible region and short-lived triplet excited states but high phosphorescence quantum yield, were usually used for electroluminescence.14,38 Visible light-harvesting Ru(II) polyimine complexes were widely used as photosensitizers for dye-sensitized solar cells (DSCs).62 It should be pointed out that organic dyes, without producing triplet excited states upon photoexcitation, were also used for DSCs.62g,h These applications will not be discussed in this review article.

One of the major applications of triplet PSs is PDT.24–27 In this case the energy is transferred from the triplet excited state of the PSs to the O2 molecules that are in the ground state (which is the triplet state), and the product singlet oxygen (1O2) is highly reactive for oxidation. 1O2 quantum yield (ΦΔ) is an important parameter for characterization of the efficiency of a triplet PS to produce 1O2 upon photoexcitation. The visible light-harvesting ability, both wavelength and molar absorption coefficients (ε) and the triplet state quantum yields (ΦISC) are also crucial for triplet PSs.

Triplet PSs were also used for triplet–triplet annihilation (TTA) upconversion and the detailed mechanism of TTA upconversion has been discussed in recent literatures.32–34 The visible light-harvesting ability, lifetime of the triplet excited state and the triplet state quantum yield of triplet PSs are crucial for TTA upconversion. Therefore, as we have proved with Ru(II), Pt(II) and Ir(III) complexes, triplet PSs with strong absorption of visible light and long-lived triplet states are desired for TTA upconversion.35a

Another newly developed application of the triplet PSs is the photocatalytic preparative organic reactions.28–31 Currently most of the reported triplet PSs are those un-derivatized on-the-shelf compounds. We believe that designing of tailored organic triplet PSs for photocatalytic organic reactions will be flourishing. More detailed discussion of the application of triplet PSs will be disclosed in the later section.

2. Transition metal complexes as triplet PSs

Due to the heavy atom effect of transition metal atoms such as Pt(II), Ir(III), Ru(II), etc., efficient ISCs were usually observed for these complexes.12–15,63–65 Conventionally these complexes were used for electroluminescence.14,38,39,66,67 The molar absorption coefficients (ε) in the visible spectral region are small, which is a significant drawback for these transition metal complexes to be used as triplet PSs for the new applications such as photocatalysis,36,68–70 luminescent molecular probes,21–23,71–74 and TTA upconversion.32–35 Therefore, most of these complexes are actually not suitable to be used as triplet PSs, although these complexes are still being used in photocatalysis.36,68–70

The weak absorptivity of the transition metal complexes in the visible spectral region is due to at least two reasons, first, the lack of coordination ligands that show strong absorption of visible light. For example, the typical diimine ligand, 2,2′-bipyridine (bpy), shows absorption only in the UV region; second, the typical S0 → 1MLCT transition is featured by weak absorption.13,15,63–65 Furthermore, these complexes usually show short T1 state lifetime (less than 10 μs).

The traditional application, i.e. electroluminescence, does not require strong absorption of visible light and a long-lived T1 state (a long-lived T1 state is actually detrimental to electroluminescence).13,14,75 However, new applications of transition metal complexes have been developed, such as photocatalysis,36,68–70 phosphorescent molecular probes,21,22 phosphorescent bioimaging,21–23,71–73,76 and recently TTA UC.32–34,77 Concerning all these new applications, strong absorption in the visible range and/or a long-lived triplet excited state is crucial.35

A straightforward approach to prepare a transition metal complex that shows strong absorption in the visible region is to attach a visible light-harvesting chromophore, a typical fluorophore, such as boron dipyrromethene (Bodipy),4,5,9 to the coordination centre.79–81 Fluorophores usually exhibit strong absorption of visible light.2–7 However, very often this method does not lead to the transition metal complexes that show the effective strong absorption of visible light, i.e. the excitation energy harvested by the chromophore cannot be funnelled efficiently to the coordination centre or the triplet excited state manifold.79 As a result, complexes may show strong absorption of visible light, but the production of triplet excited states may not be enhanced.79–82

Based on the reported results, including those from our own group,35,63–65,83 two methods are available for preparation of transition metal complexes that show strong absorption of visible light and long-lived triplet excited states. (1) To dangle, or to tether, a visible light-harvesting antenna to the coordination centre, i.e. there is no π-conjugation between the coordination centre and the light harvesting ligand. In this case, the energy level of the ligand-localized singlet state (1IL state) have to be higher than the 1MLCT state of the coordination centre, otherwise the EnT from the ligand to the coordination centre will be frustrated. Note that the direct ISC (1IL → 3IL) is not efficient because the electrons in the frontier orbitals of the antenna are far away from the heavy atom. As a result, there is a limitation on the absorption wavelength for this method, i.e. the absorption wavelength of the ligand cannot be longer than the absorption wavelength of the coordination centre; and (2) to attach a visible light-harvesting ligand to the coordination centre by a π-conjugation bond, such as a CC bond,60,84–87 or direct metalation of a fluorophore (the antenna as the carbon donor of the C–metal bond).80,88–90 In this case the limitation of the first method can be eliminated, and the effective absorption wavelength can be easily extended.57,60b,80,89,90 However, limitations do exist for this approach either, that is, with increasing bulkiness of the visible light-harvesting antenna, the ISC will become less efficient and the indication for this effect is the significant residual fluorescence of the ligand.86,91

Besides the strong absorption of visible light, the long-lived triplet state is also important for triplet PSs, for example, in TTA UC,77,92 and luminescent O2 sensing,60,85,93 for which the TTET process is the crucial step. The methods for prolongation of the T1 state lifetime include either establishment of triplet states equilibrium between the 3MLCT excited state and the ligand-localized excited state (3IL state),35,60,94,95 or switching of the T1 state from 3MLCT to the 3IL.35,60,64,95 Readers are suggested to refer to some recent reviews on this subject.35,63,64,95

2.1. Ru(II) complexes as triplet PSs

Typical Ru(II) polyimine complexes show moderate absorption at ca. 450 nm, e.g. Ru(bpy)3[PF6]2.65 Coumarin-containing Ru(II) complex 1 (Fig. 3) shows strong absorption at 341 nm.96 The molar absorption coefficients (ε = 78700 M−1 cm−1) increased by 5-fold compared to the model complex 2 (ε = 14000 M−1 cm−1).
Fig. 3 (a) Ru(II) complex 1 with a coumarin light-harvesting ligand and the model complex 2.96 (b) Energy level diagram describing the photophysical processes of complex 1.

For 1, the energy level of the coumarin 1IL state is higher than the 1MLCT state, thus efficient EnT, or internal conversion (IC), from coumarin to the coordination centre occurs.96 The energy level of the 3IL state of coumarin (2.70 eV) is substantially higher than the 3MLCT state, thus the phosphorescence lifetime (τP) and the quantum yield (ΦP) were not perturbed by the ligand.97 On the other hand, the photophysical properties of the coordination centre will be profoundly altered given the 3IL state energy level is close to or much lower than the energy level of the 3MLCT state.63,94,97

A Ru(II) complex with pyrene units tethered to the coordination ligand (Phen) was reported (3, Fig. 4).98 The absorption in the 300–350 nm region is enhanced due to the pyrene units. The 3IL state energy level is close to the 3MLCT state, as a result a triplet state equilibrium is established, the lifetime of the triplet excited state (τP = 148 ± 8 μs) is greatly extended vs. complex Ru(phenyl-phen)3[PF6]2 (τP = 1.15 μs). Similar enhanced absorption of visible light and a long-lived triplet state (τT = 115 μs) were observed for the Ru(II) polyimine complex with other chromophores, such as 4-piperidinyl-1,8-naphthalimide (4, Fig. 4).64,94,95


Fig. 4 Ru(II) complexes 3 and 4 with the 3IL state energy level close to the 3MLCT state.94,98

The 3MLCT state of the coordination centre will be quenched given the 3IL state energy level is much lower than the 3MLCT state, which can be demonstrated by complex 5 (Fig. 5).79 Bodipy is a well-known fluorophore for its strong absorption of visible light and high fluorescence quantum yield (ΦF), as well as versatile derivatization chemistry.4–6,99 The 3IL state energy level of Bodipy (ca. 1.72 eV) is lower than the 3MLCT state of the Ru(II) coordination centre (1.94 eV), therefore the phosphorescence from the 3MLCT state is completely quenched by 3MLCT → 3IL.79 The TA spectrum indicated that the triplet state is localized on the Bodipy unit.79 The upward 1IL → 1MLCT EnT is unlikely because the 1IL state energy level is lower than the 1MLCT state. In other words, the strong visible light absorption of 5 (ε = 66000 M−1 cm−1 at 523 nm) is not much helpful to improve the production of the triplet excited state. No application of this complex in the photophysical process was reported.

2.2. Cyclometalated Ir(III) complexes as triplet PSs

Another method for preparation of transition metal complexes that show strong absorption in the visible region is by establishment of π-conjugation between the coordination centre and the light-harvesting antenna,56,60,86,87 or by direct metalation of the light-harvesting ligand (complexes 6–8, Fig. 6).80,88–90 The energy level diagram in Fig. 5 indicated that if the absorption of the ligand is more red-shifted than the coordination center, the 1IL → 1MLCT EnT will be frustrated. Therefore, another intramolecular EnT channel, i.e. the direct 1IL → 3IL ISC, has to be efficient to ensure efficient production of the triplet excited state. Concerning this aspect, the most efficient method is to maximize the heavy atom effect by direct metalation of the ligand (the antenna, Fig. 6).

Similar to the visible light-harvesting Ru(II) complexes,100 we used coumarin ligands for preparation of Ir(III) complexes (complexes 18–19, Fig. 8).101 The triplet state lifetimes of 18 and 19 (75.5 μs and 73.6 μs, respectively) are much longer than that of the corresponding Ru(II) complexes 16 and 17.100 With TA spectroscopy, emission spectra at 77 K and spin density analysis, the T1 states of 18 and 19 are identified as the coumarin–imidizole localized 3IL state. Bodipy-containing Ir(III) complexes which show strong absorption of visible light (ε = 83600 M−1 cm−1 at 501 nm) and a long-lived Bodipy-localized 3IL state (τT = 25 μs) were reported (complex 20, Fig. 9).81 However, the π-core of the Bodipy unit is not directly linked to the coordination centre, thus the heavy atom effect is not efficient; moreover, the Bodipy-localized 1IL state energy level (2.43 eV) is lower than the 1MLCT state energy level, thus 1IL → 1MLCT EnT (or IC) is frustrated. Experiments show that (1) the fluorescence of the Bodipy ligand is not completely quenched and (2) RT phosphorescence of Bodipy is absent, which is to some extent an indication of weak ISC.81

Connecting the π-core of Bodipy to the Ir(III) coordination centre via CC bonds may improve the ISC. Following this line, we prepared two Ir(III) complexes (Fig. 9).82 The Bodipy unit is tethered to the ligand in 22 (no π-conjugation exists between the coordination centre and the Bodipy unit). In 23, however, the π-core of the Bodipy unit is directly π-conjugated to the coordination center via a CC triple bond (Fig. 9).82 Strong absorption of visible light was observed for both 22 (ε = 71400 M−1 cm−1 at 499 nm) and 23 (ε = 83000 M−1 cm−1 at 527 nm). Fluorescence of the Bodipy ligand is more significantly quenched in 23 than in 22. RT phosphorescence of the Bodipy unit was observed at 742 nm for 23, but not for 22. TA spectra and spin density analysis indicated Bodipy-localized 3IL states for both 22 and 23, but the triplet state lifetimes are different (23.7 μs and 87.2 μs, respectively). 22 and 23 were used as triplet PSs for TTA upconversion and the ΦUC value of 23 is 2-fold that of 22, which indicated that the ISC in 23 is more efficient. ΦΔ values indicated the efficiency of the ISC. For 23, ΦΔ = 97%, which is ca. 2-fold that of 22 (ΦΔ = 52%). These results indicate that attaching a fluorophore to the coordination centre via a π-conjugation linker is a useful method for design of Ir(III) complexes that show effective strong absorption of visible light.

2.3. N⁁N Pt(II) bis(acetylide) complexes as triplet PSs

The fascinating aspect of these complexes is that the photophysical properties can be readily changed by using different acetylide ligands and the preparation of the complexes is facile.13,56,66,104,105 Recently these complexes were used for photocatalytic H2 production.106 However, usually N⁁N Pt(II) complexes show very weak absorptivity in the visible region and the T1 state lifetime is short,13,56,66 thus much room is left to develop new visible light-harvesting complexes.

A way to enhance the absorptivity and to prolong the T1 state lifetime is to attach fluorophores to a metal centre. Following this line, acetylide fluorophores were attached to the Pt(II) centre of the N⁁N Pt(II) bisacetylide complex to enhance the absorption in the visible range (Fig. 10).57,60b,86,107,108 The preparation of 24 follows the method of direct metalation of the chromophore.57 Strong absorption in the visible range was observed (ε = 68000 M−1 cm−1 at 570 nm). However, the T1 state lifetime is short (246 ns) and no RT phosphorescence was observed.

4. Heavy atom-free triplet PSs

Without the heavy atom, it is very difficult to predict the ISC of organic chromophores.1 In this section we will introduce some new methods for designing of heavy-atom free organic triplet PSs that show predictable ISC.

An intramolecular spin converter can be used to address the aforementioned challenge. A spin converter is a chromophore that undergoes efficient ISC, but does not necessarily have to show strong absorption of visible light, rather, a low S1 state energy level is crucial for the spin converter. The principle of this method is to attach a visible light-harvesting unit (intramolecular energy donor) to a spin converter (intramolecular energy acceptor). Upon photoexcitation of the light-harvesting unit, the singlet EnT to the acceptor (i.e. the spin converter) will occur, as a result the S1 state of the spin converter will be populated, and in turn the triplet excited state will be produced via the ISC of the spin converter (Fig. 23, exemplified with C60 as the spin converter), thus the spin converter–chromophore dyads can be used as triplet PSs.


Fig. 23 Simplified Jablonski diagram depicts the photophysics of a heavy atom-free triplet photosensitizer based on a spin converter, herein exemplified with a C60-antenna dyad (C60 as the spin converter). The different T1 state energy level of the antenna will change the feature of the triplet excited state of the dyad. The charge separated state of the C60 dyad is omitted.

With this method, more flexibility in molecular design of triplet PSs will be gained, for example, the absorption wavelength of the triplet PSs can be readily changed by using different visible-light antennae, and at the same time, ISC of the dyad becomes predictable. C60 can be used as such a spin converter. Thus heavy atom-free triplet PSs can be easily designed. However, the spin converter is not limited to C60, and any other chromophores that show a similar photophysical property are suitable to be used as spin converters.

4.1. C60–chromophore hybrids as heavy atom-free triplet PSs

Fullerene C60 is an ideal candidate as a spin converter. First, C60 shows efficient inherent ISC (ΦISC close to 100%).149 Second, C60 shows a low S1 state energy level (1.72 eV), thus the absorption wavelength of the energy donor (antenna) can be tuned in a wide range from UV up to ca. 700 nm. Despite the efficient ISC, C60 itself shows very weak absorption in the visible range (e.g. the absorption at 536 nm is 710 M−1 cm−1).149 Therefore, C60 alone is not a good triplet PS.

One method to address this problem is to attach a visible-light-harvesting antenna onto C60.59,150–153 The energy level of the S1 state of the antenna should be higher than the S1 state of C60 (ca. 720 nm).59a Otherwise the S1(antenna) → S1(C60) EnT (or internal conversion) will be frustrated (Fig. 23). The symmetry prohibited S0(C60) → S1(C60) transition of C60 does not necessarily mean a weak S1(antenna) → S1(C60) transition. In other words, the S1(antenna) → S1(C60) process may be highly efficient. Note that this EnT is not simply a Föster process because the spectral overlap between the fluorescence emission of the antenna and the UV/Vis absorption of C60 is not significant, due to the very weak absorption of C60 in the visible region.

The energy level of triplet excited states of the antenna and C60 will determine the localization of the triplet excited state of the dyads (Fig. 23). The T1 state will localize on C60 if the energy level of the T1 state of the antenna is higher than that of C60. Conversely the T1 state will be localized on the antenna, via the backward triplet EnT from C60 to the antenna. The localization of the T1 state can be studied by TA spectroscopy or by using DFT calculations on the spin density surfaces of the C60–organic chromophore hybrids.49,50,80 Localization of the T1 state on the antenna or the C60 part may have a substantial effect on the lifetime of the triplet excited state, which in turn may affect the TTET efficiency significantly. For example, we prepared styryl-Bodipy–C60 and C60–PBI hybrids and the triplet excited state is localized on styryl-Bodipy or PBI, not C60 (indicated by the nanosecond time-resolved transient absorption spectrum).49,50 This is a rare case in that the energy level of the S1 state of the antenna is higher than C60, but the T1 state energy level of the antenna is lower than the T1 state of C60.49,59b

4.2. Small organic molecules as heavy atom-free triplet PSs

It is more challenging to design small organic molecules as heavy atom-free triplet PSs. The mechanism of the ISC of these compounds vary from one to another (Fig. 37), thus it is difficult to predict the ISC of the organic chromophores.
Fig. 37 Heavy atom-free organic triplet PSs with absorption bands in UV or blue range.1,3,194

Some aromatic hydrocarbons show effective ISC (compounds 106–109, Fig. 37).1,3 However, chemical modification of the structures often induce unpredictable photophysical properties. Besides the aforementioned anthracene/DPA, another example is pyrene. Pyrene was extensively used in fluorescent molecular probes.183–188 The luminescence of pyrene is exceptionally long-lived (τF = 450 ns, in cyclohexane),3 which is due to the forbidden nature of the S1 → S0 transition.1 Note that P-type delayed fluorescence can be observed for pyrene at high concentrations. Attaching π-conjugation moiety to pyrene will produce a new fluorophore.188 In these cases the fluorescence of the pyrene becomes normal, for example, the prompt fluorescence was observed and the τF is a few ns, hence the emission is no longer sensitive to O2.186,188,189

Porphyrins have been known for a long time to show the triplet excited state upon photooxidation, thus these compounds are weakly fluorescent at RT (compounds 113–117, Fig. 38).27 For the free base porphrin, the ΦF value is only about 5%. The ΦISC value is up to 90%.190 However, no RT phosphorescence was observed for porphyrin.


Fig. 38 Porphyrin derivatives 113–117 as triplet PSs.190

The ISC mechanism of porphyrin is controversial. Recent studies suggested that vibration of the molecule, including excited state intramolecular proton transfer, may facilitate the ISC.190 Porphyrin derivatives were used for PDT.27,191 However, porphyrin-based organic triplet PSs that give large ε values at the lower energy side of the absorption spectrum are highly desired.35,112

Some aromatic ketones show efficient ISC, due to the low-lying n–π* transition, for which the SOC is strong. S1(n–π*) → Tn(π–π*) transition obeys El Sayed's rule.1 For example, ΦISC of benzonphenone (110 in Fig. 37) is ca. 100%.1,192 Benzophenone can be used as a photo-initiator in UV-curing applications. Butadione shows a similar property,180,193 as well as some ketocoumarins (Fig. 37 and 39).194 Ketocoumarins show strong absorption in the UV range (e.g. 350–392 nm, ε is up to 43000 M−1 cm−1).194 The ISC efficiency of these compounds is as high as 0.96 (Fig. 39). The triplet state energy levels of these PSs are in the range of 2.43–2.52 eV.195 However, some aromatic carbonyl compounds, such as phenothiazine derivatives, give strong fluorescence,196 indicating the complexity of the photophysical properties of these compounds.

5. Applications of the triplet PSs

5.1. Photodynamic therapy reagents

Photodynamic therapy (PDT) reagents are unique in that the PDT reagents alone are inactive, it is the 1O2 produced by these triplet PSs upon photoexcitation that cure the disease. The important properties of the PDT reagents are the UV/Vis absorption and ISC efficiency, thus their 1O2 production ability. However, the dark toxicity, ability to penetrate tissues and pharmacokinetics of the PDT reagents have to be considered.122,191,201 Usually absorption in the long wavelength region (near IR) is desired, however, this is not always the case and triplet PSs with a short excitation wavelength can also be used as PDT reagents for treatment of skin cancer or lesions. Some of the triplet PSs discussed in this review article have been tested for PDT application, such as the iodo-Bodipy or the iodo-aza-Bodipy derivatives.24–26,40,129,202

Conventionally the PDT reagents are the porphyrin derivatives, due to the near IR absorption and the efficient ISC.27,191 However, the preparation (especially the purification) of these reagents is difficult, and the molecular structures cannot be readily modified. Recently, Bodipy based compounds have attracted much attention for the PDT purposes.24–27,41 The application of the Bodipy chromophore for this purpose is due to its good photostability, readily derivatizable molecular structures, as well as its tunable photophysical properties, such as the absorption wavelength.4,5,9 Absorption ranging from green (500 nm) to near IR can be easily accessed by chemical modification.4,5,9,99,160 Furthermore, the aza-Bodipy shows more red-shifted absorption than the normal Bodipy.5a,24a,41,129,165 However, most of the Bodipy-based triplet PSs are dependent on the heavy atom effect, thus iodine or bromine atoms have to be attached to the π-core of Bodipy.25,41

Visible-light harvesting organic chromophore-C60 hybrids are known for the population of the triplet excited state, but this property was rarely studied for the PDT purpose.45,59,151 Recently we prepared new organic chromophore–C60 hybrids and the compounds were used for TTA upconversion and photosensitization of singlet oxygen (1O2). However, the PDT effect of these compounds was never investigated. It is still a substantial challenge to ‘design’ a heavy atom-free triplet PS for the PDT purpose. Removal of the heavy atom effect may reduce the dark toxicity of the compounds, which is desired for PDT purposes.27,191

5.2. Photoinduced catalytic H2 production

Photoinduced catalytic H2 production from water has attracted much attention.69,70,119,203,204 For the typical mechanism (Fig. 44), firstly the PS was photoexcited, then ET occurs, for example, from a SR to the PS. The electron will transfer to the WRC to produce H2 by reduction of H+. The property of PS, such as visible light-harvesting ability and the triplet excited state lifetime, are crucial for H2 production. However, most of the currently used transition metal complex triplet PSs show weak absorptivity of visible light and short-lived triplet excited states. Therefore, PSs with large molar absorption coefficients (to harvest the excitation energy more efficiently) and long-lived excited states (to allow efficient electron-transfer to occur) are highly desired.36 It should be pointed out that the singlet excited states may be also possible for the photocatalysis.
Fig. 44 Mechanism for the photoinduced H2 production. PS stands for photosensitizer, SR stands for the sacrificial reductant, WRC stands for the water-reduction catalyst.70 Reproduced with permission from ref. 70.

Cyclometalated Ir(III) diimine complexes as PSs for photoinduced H2 production were studied (Fig. 45).36,120 The sacrificial reductant is triethanolamine and the electron relay is [Co(bpy)3]Cl2. A blue LED (465 nm) was used for photoexcitation. The complexes show weak absorption at the excitation wavelength, with ε smaller than 1800 M−1 cm−1. Furthermore, the triplet excited state lifetimes of these complexes are generally shorter than 2 μs.


Fig. 45 140–145 as PSs for photoinduced H2 production.36

Ir(phenylazole)2(bpy)]PF6 was used as the PS for H2 production (Fig. 46).70 NEt3 was used as the SR and HFe3(CO)11 was used as the WRC. The absorption of PSs in the visible range is improved. For example, the absorption of 150 is centred at 438 nm (4900 M−1 cm−1). The lifetime of the phosphorescence is slightly extended, but is still less than 10 μs. Turn over numbers (TONs) are 837–1614.70

The photoinduced H2 production of triplet PSs is closely related to the visible light-harvesting (Fig. 47).106152–154 were used as triplet PSs together with the catalyst Co(dmgH)2PyCl and the sacrificial electron donor triethanolamine (TEOA). Complexes 152–154 show different visible light absorption, for example, the ε values at 442 nm are 4330, 5830 and 9370 M−1 cm−1, respectively. Accordingly the H2 production of the complexes within 1 h of irradiation is 18.4 μmol, 23.9 μmol and 32.9 μmol, respectively.


Fig. 47 N⁁N⁁N Pt(II) alkynyl complexes as triplet PSs for H2 production.106

Intramolecular ET will be more efficient than the intermolecular ET. A unique molecular motif was proposed by L. Sun et al. for photocatalytic H2 production (Fig. 48).205,206 The use of [Ru(terpy)2]2+ is due to its geometry which is favourable for the construction of the molecular array. Attachment of the ethynyl moiety to tertpy prolong the excited state lifetime. A long-lived triplet excited state is beneficial for the ET from the Ru(II) center to the active site. The UV/Vis absorptivity of the complex in the visible region is moderate, the ε is ca. 40000 M−1 cm−1 at 510 nm. The luminescence lifetime of the complex was determined as 6.5 ns, which is much longer than the unsubstituted [Ru(terpy)2][PF6]2 (less than 1 ns).206

Furthermore, the τT of the trident Pt(II) center is only 519 ns. It should be pointed out that the redox potential of the triplet PSs has to be considered for the H2 production.

5.3. TTA Upconversion

TTA upconversion shows advantages over the other upconversion methods, such as those using rare earth materials208–211 and two-photon absorption dyes.212 TTA UC requires a triplet PS to harvest the excitation energy, then via TTET, the T1 state of the triplet acceptor is produced; in turn the S1 state will be populated via TTA.32,33,37,77,169,171,181,213 The photophysical processes involved in TTA upconversion can be summarized in Fig. 50. It is clear that triplet PSs with strong absorption of excitation light and a long-lived triplet excited state are desired.
Fig. 50 Jablonski Diagram illustrating the mechanism of TTA upconversion. The effects of the light-harvesting and the excited state lifetime of the triplet PSs on the efficiency of the TTA upconversion are shown. E is energy. GS is the ground state (S0). 3MLCT* is the metal-to-ligand-charge-transfer triplet excited state. TTET is triplet–triplet EnT. 3A* is the triplet excited state of the triplet acceptor. 1A* is the singlet excited state of the annihilator. The emission bands observed for the upconversion are the delayed fluorescence from the acceptor.

Normally the triplet PSs for TTA UC are Ru(dmb)3[PF6]2 and the Pt(II)–Pd(II) porphyrin complexes.32,77 In order to increase the diversity of the triplet PSs for TTA upconversion, we developed a series of complexes that show strong absorptivity in the visible spectral region and long-lived triplet excited states,77 such as Ru(II) complexes,92,100,176 Pt(II) complexes,60b,86,89,90,107–110,214,215 Ir(III) complexes101,177–179 and Re(I) complexes.118 We also prepared iodo-Bodipy triplet PSs and bromo NDI derivatives for TTA upconversion,55,130,131 with which the excitation wavelength can be readily tuned in the range of 450 nm–650 nm.

Recently we used C60–Bodipy dyads as heavy atom-free triplet PSs for TTA upconversion (Fig. 51. The molecular structure of 97 is presented in Fig. 34).51 C60 was used as the intramolecular energy acceptor and the spin converter. The photoexcitation energy harvested by the Bodipy part is transferred to C60. The singlet excited state and in turn the triplet excited state of C60 will be produced, via the intrinsic ISC of C60. We demonstrated that triplet PSs with strong absorption of visible light and a long-lived triplet excited state are beneficial for TTA upconversion. However, the electron-donating antenna in the C60 dyad may facilitate the ET to C60 (a well-known electron acceptor), and the TTA upconversion can be diminished.52,53 This finding is helpful for future design of heavy atom-free triplet PSs that are based on C60 dyads.


Fig. 51 C60 dyad 97 as a heavy atom-free triplet PS.51 (a) TTA upconversion spectra with 97 and perylene was used as the triplet acceptor; (b) photographs of the upconversion. The blue emission is due to the upconverted emission from perylene. Reproduced with permission from ref. 51.

TTA upconversion has been successfully used to enhance solar cells,216 photocatalytic H2 production,217 luminescent O2 sensing,218 and luminescent bioimaging.174 We believe that this new upconversion method and its application will flourish in future, for which the design of efficient triplet PSs is one of the crucial aspects.

5.4. Photoredox organic reactions

Recently triplet PSs were used in catalytic photoredox organic reactions,28,219 such as aerobic oxidative hydroxylation of arylboronic catalyzed by Ru(II) complexes,220 intermolecular [3+2] cycloaddition of cyclopropylamines with olefins catalyzed by Ru(II) complexes,29 C–C bond formation by photocatalytic aerobic oxidation catalyzed by Ru(II) complexes,221 or an organic triplet PS catalyzed aerobic cross dehydrogenative coupling reactions.124 Most of the triplet PSs used in these studies are limited to the normal, unsubstituted model complexes. Generally the absorptivity of these transition metal complexes in the visible region is moderate or weak.28

For example, oxidative hydroxylation of arylboronic acids catalyzed by [Ru(bpy)3Cl2]·6H2O was reported (Fig. 52). The crucial step is the oxidation of amine by the Ru(II) complex at the T1 excited state, in turn the [O2]˙− is produced.220 We envisaged that the bimolecular reaction between the photoexcited catalyst (PS) and the amine is dependent on the T1 state lifetime, as well as the visible light absorbing ability of the triplet PS, therefore the photoreaction may be promoted with Ru(II) complexes that show strong absorption of visible light and long-lived triplet excited state.35,219,221,222

Ir(III) complexes were used as triplet PSs for photooxidation.168 The triplet PS is responsible for production of 1O2. However, similar to the normal Ir(III) complexes,15 these photocatalysts show weak absorption in the visible range. The molar extinction coefficients are smaller than 10000 M−1 cm−1 in the region beyond 400 nm.

We used Ir(III) complexes 18 and 19 (Fig. 8) that show strong absorption and a long-lived T1 state for photooxidation of DHN,103 and these new complexes are up to 5-fold more efficient than the model complexes reported in the literature (Fig. 53). It should be pointed out that fluorescent lamp was sometimes used as the excitation source for photoreactions. However, since the emission spectrum of the typical fluorescent lamp with rare earth phosphor is not continuous in the visible region (which is different from the incandescent lamp or xenon lamp), only part of the emission of the fluorescent lamp can be harvested by the photocatalysts, therefore this practice is not as ‘green’ as it looks.

5.5. Luminescent O2 sensing

Luminescent O2 sensing is important for chemical, biological and environmental science.12,223,224 Phosphorescence transition metal complexes can be used for O2 sensing because the phosphorescence can be quenched by O2.2,225–227 Conventionally the O2 sensing materials are Pt(II)–Pd(II) porphyrin complexes.228 Some of these complexes show strong absorption of visible light and the τP is usually up to a few dozens of μs or even ms. We proved that the O2 sensing can be improved by using transition metal complexes that show long-lived triplet excited states.60,84,85,93,107,206,229 Recently red-light excitable complexes were developed for O2 sensing. These complexes are mainly Pt(II)–Pd(II) complexes.230,231 The phosphorescence lifetimes are up to 286 μs.231

One of the trends in this area is to develop a ratiometric luminescent O2 sensor.91,198,232–237 For this kind of O2 sensors, the sensing material should show fluorescence and phosphorescence dual emission, with the fluorescence band inert to O2, whereas the phosphorescence can be quenched by O2. There are two methods to achieve ratiometric response. One method is to combine a fluorescent dye with a phosphorescent compound, either by mechanical mixing or by linking together the two chromophores by a non-π-conjugation covalent bond.232–234 Another method is to use a compound that shows fluorescence–phosphorescence dual emission.91,198,235

For the ratiometric O2 sensors that are based on two chromophores (fluorophores and phosphors), the following requirements are necessary for observation of dual emission: (1) the fluorescence and phosphorescence bands should exhibit good spectral separation, the singlet EnT from the fluorophore to the phosphor should not be efficient, otherwise the fluorescence will be quenched; (2) reverse triplet EnT from the phosphor to the fluorophore should not occur, otherwise the phosphorescence will be quenched; (3) no ET quenching should occur, otherwise both emission will be diminished; (4) a sufficient intracellular uptake for in vivo application.233

A coumarin-Ir(III) ratiometric O2 sensor was reported (165, Fig. 54).233 The sensor can be excited at 405 nm, where the coumarin 343 part shows much stronger absorptivity than the Ir(III) coordination center, however the singlet excited state of the Ir(III) center can be populated via the singlet EnT from the coumarin 343 to the Ir(III) coordination center. The τP of the BTP moiety is 5.9 μs. The fluorescence cannot be quenched by O2 but the phosphorescence band can be significantly quenched by O2 (Fig. 54). The sensor was used for O2 sensing in living cells.233


Fig. 54 (a) Ratiometric O2 sensor 165. (b) Emission spectra of 165 in CH3CN under aerated and Ar-purged conditions (λex = 405 nm) and (c) photo of the emission under different O2 partial pressure.233 The fluorescence is non-sensitive to the presence of O2, whereas the phosphorescence is dependent on the O2 concentration. Thus the emission color will change with varying O2 concentration. Reproduced with permission from ref. 233.

Similarly a fluorene – Pt(II) porphyrin dual emissive copolymer for ratiometric O2 sensing was reported.234 The fluorescence bands of the fluorene parts overlap with the absorption of Pt(II) porphyrin part, therefore, intramolecular EnT is possible and indeed the authors observed that the fluorescence bands become weaker with increasing contents of the Pt(II) porphyrin part (energy acceptor). The ratiometric O2 sensing was carried out with the emission intensity profile.

Ratiometric O2 sensing can also be achieved by mechanical mixing of a fluorescent dye and a phosphorescent material, such as a Pt(II) porphyrin complex with quantum dots.232,238 or two phosphorescent compounds with different τP.239

It is challenging to design a mono-chromophore dual emissive compound as a ratiometric O2 sensor. In this case the ISC has to be delicately balanced otherwise only fluorescence or phosphorescence can be observed.86,91,235 It is more likely to observe the fluorescence–phosphorescence dual emission given the bulkiness of the ligands is increased (Fig. 55). The fluorescence and the phosphorescence emission of 171 and 172 are at 502/683 nm and 521/692 nm, respectively; furthermore, the heavy atom effect of Ir(III) is more significant than the Pt(II) atoms.91 These findings are useful for design of monochromophore fluorescence–phosphorescence dual emissive compounds.


Fig. 55 Fluorescence–phosphorescence dual emissive complexes 166–177.91,235

Inspired by recently reported dual emissive Pt(II) and Ir(III) complexes (Fig. 55),91 we used cyclometalated Pt(II) complexes that give fluorescence–phosphorescence dual emission for ratiometric O2 sensing.235

Besides the above applications, phosphorescent complexes were also used for photoinduced charge separation, for example, with N⁁N Pt(II) bisacetylide complexes58,240 and the Ru(II) polyimine coordination center.241–243 Ir(III) complexes were used for photoinduced polymerization.244 Moreover, compounds with triplet states that become populated upon photoexcitation are also useful for optical power limiting and non-linear optics.245–248

6. Conclusion

We summarized the molecular design rationales for triplet photosensitizers to efficiently access the triplet excited states of chromophores, as well as some typical structures of triplet photosensitizers, including transition metal complexes, iodo- or bromo-substituted organic chromophores, and some recently developed heavy atom-free organic triplet photosensitizers. Applications of the triplet photosensitizers, such as in photosensitization of 1O2, photoredox reactions, luminescent O2 sensing, triplet–triplet annihilation upconversion, and photocatalytic H2 production, were briefly discussed.

It is still a substantial challenge to ‘design’ an efficient triplet photosensitizer, especially those without any heavy atoms because it is difficult to predict the intersystem crossing (ISC) of a chromophore, in particular those without any heavy atoms. The structure–property relationship inferred from the fluorescence is not applicable to the triplet state related paradigm. Furthermore, normally the transition metal complexes show weak absorptivity of visible light and short-lived triplet excited states, which are detrimental for the complexes to be used as triplet PSs.

In order to address these challenges, two methods for preparation of transition metal complexes that show strong absorptivity of visible light are proposed. One is to directly metalate an organic chromophore (organic chromophore as the C- donor for the C–M bond), or establish π-conjugation between the chromophore and the coordination centre; the second is to tether an organic chromophore on the coordination center (without π-conjugation between the two parts, but the two parts should have matching excited state energy levels). The lifetime of the triplet excited state is usually prolonged given the ligands have an appropriate T1 state energy level. The limitations of these methods were also discussed.

The crucial molecular structural design strategy for iodo- or bromo-organic chromophores is to attach the heavy atoms (I or Br) at the π-core of the chromophore to maximize the heavy atom effect, thus to ensure efficient ISC. The limitation is that when the bulkiness of the chromophore increases, the ISC will become less efficient.

Intramolecular spin converters can be used to construct heavy atom-free triplet photosensitizers that show predictable ISC. For example, the C60–organic chromophore dyads can be used as general molecular motifs for heavy atom–free organic triplet photosensitizers with C60 as the spin converter. The absorption of these dyads can be easily tuned and the ISC is predictable. Furthermore, energy funneling can be used for preparation of triplet photosensitizers that show panchromatic absorption. Most of the currently available triplet PSs are based on a mono-chromophore profile, which results in only one major absorption band in the visible spectral region.

This review article took a preliminary step toward summarizing the design rationales for triplet photosensitizers and we hope that more chemists will join the exploration of this fascinating area. The study of triplet photosensitizers is crucial for the development of photocatalytic organic reactions, photodynamic therapy (PDT), triplet–triplet annihilation upconversion, as well as for the study of photochemistry and photophysics.
