Abstract

This perspective article reports the most significant advances in the field of water oxidationâ€”from molecular water oxidation catalysts (WOCs) to photoelectrochemical cells. Different series of catalysts that can be applied in visible light-driven water oxidation catalysis are discussed in details and several key aspects of their catalytic mechanisms are introduced. In order to construct a water oxidation electrode from molecular catalysts, proper immobilization methods have to be employed. Herein, we present one section about how to attach catalysts onto an electrode/material surface. Finally, the state of the art photoelectrochemical cells that achieve visible light-driven water splitting are described.


Broader context

Visible light-driven water oxidation is still the bottleneck of the whole artificial water splitting processes. In the past decades, remarkable research advances have been made in the development of efficient water oxidation catalysts based on transition metals (Ru, Ir, Mn, Co and Fe). The understanding of water oxidation mechanisms in turn guides chemists to design more efficient catalysts. However, none of the reported water oxidation catalysts have fulfilled the following criteria: (i) fast reaction rate; (ii) low overpotential; (iii) high stability against oxidative and hydrolytic decomposition; and (iv) affordable cost for large-scale application. Therefore, the development of water oxidation catalysts, particularly earth abundant transition metal-based catalysts capable of working in an artificial photosystem, is urgently required.

1. Introduction

Through billion years of evolution, green plants and some other organisms have developed a remarkable function, the photosynthesis, to store solar energy in the form of chemical bonds by converting carbon dioxide into organic compounds, especially glucose.1,2 It is the original life on earth that made the choice of utilizing sunlight as an inexhaustible energy source and water as a nearly unlimited electron donor for its surviving and thriving. Since then, the photosynthetic reaction has supported nearly all kinds of life forms having ever appeared on earth. The photosystem carrying out the amazing photosynthetic reaction consists of two families of protein complexes: Photosystem II (PS II) where sunlight is used to extract protons and electrons from water (eqn (1)), and Photosystem I (PS I) where electrons and protons from PS II are used to generate carbohydrates (eqn (2)).

Light-driven water splitting (H2O + hv â†’ H2 + O2) that replicates the function of natural photosynthesis has been under extensive research for many years because the direct photonic-to-chemical energy conversion is probably the most promising approach to power our planet in the future.5â€“9 The water splitting process consists of two half reactions: (i) proton reduction (2H+ + 4eâˆ’ â†’ H2) and (ii) water oxidation (2H2O â†’ O2 + 4H+ + 4eâˆ’). The latter half reaction is usually considered as the bottleneck of the whole process. For instance, effective and noble metal-free proton reduction catalysts have been reported to achieve high TOFs up to 1850 sâˆ’1 while WOCs no more than 5 sâˆ’1.10,11 The difficulties in developing effective WOCs derive from the complexity of the water oxidation reaction itself that involves transfer of four protons and four electrons and the formation of Oâ€“O bond. Thereby water oxidation is extremely energy-demanding and needs thermodynamically 0.82 V and 1.23 V vs. NHE in pH 7 and 0, respectively. Additionally, there is always an overpotential leading to higher potentials in experiments than the thermodynamic ones. Compared with direct chemically- and electrochemically-driven water oxidation/splitting, light-driven water oxidation is even more challenging due to the requirement of accumulatively photo-induced electron transfer from WOCs to photosensitizers. Although many transition metal complexes, including Ru, Ir, Co, Fe and Mn complexes, were reported capable of catalyzing water oxidation using CeIV as chemical oxidant, only few of them could promote visible light-driven water oxidation in a homogeneous, three-component system composed of photosensitizer, sacrificial electron acceptor and WOC.12â€“20

In practice, artificial water splitting could be realized by using a photoelectrochemical cell (PEC) which carries out water oxidation and proton reduction at separate electrodes (Fig. 1, photoelectrochemical cell).21â€“26 These two half-reactions are connected through an external circuit of electron flow. The potential required to drive these reactions is provided by a light harvesting component (photosensitizer) which can be integrated with either the cathode or the anode of the cell. The generated hydrogen and oxygen are separated from each other by a membrane. Lack of efficient and robust WOC with a low overpotential is now considered as one of the major challenges to build PECs.

A few heterogeneous WOCs, including Mn-, Ru-, Ir- and Co-based metal oxidizes, have been reviewed recently.27â€“30 Usually these heterogeneous WOCs bear good catalytic durability but moderate reactivity. Meanwhile, their catalytic mechanisms are poorly understood and it is difficult to systematically conduct structural modifications on these heterogeneous WOCs to improve their catalytic reactivity. In comparison, the advantages of molecular WOCs could be seen in several aspects: (i) their simple structures could be easily tailored on the purpose of tuning their catalytic properties; (ii) common spectrum techniques can be applied in homogeneous water oxidation medium, and thus more insight could be obtained toward the reaction mechanisms. Importantly, effective development of WOCs will require detailed mechanistic knowledge. In this review, we focus on (i) molecular WOCs and their reaction mechanisms, especially those catalysts capable of promoting light-driven water oxidation; (ii) the immobilization of molecular WOCs and (iii) the PECs.

2. The structure of OEC in PS II

Several crystal structures of PS II have been resolved during the last decades, providing an invaluable overall map of PS II proteins as well as structural models of the OEC and its ligand environment.31â€“33 In 2004, Ferreira et al. reported the X-ray crystal structure of the cyanobacterial PS II complex at 3.5 Ã… resolutions and concluded a cuboidal geometry of the OEC which consists of three manganese and one calcium atoms, besides a fourth manganese atom positioned outside of the cubane.32 Later on, Loll et al. presented the PS II complex with a higher resolution of 3.0 Ã….31 Five carboxylates are suggested to bind bidentately with manganese and calcium atoms. The presence of bridging oxygen in Mn4OxCa was identified by X-ray emission spectroscopy.34 Very recently, Umena, Kawakami and their co-workers reported a PS II structure at a resolution of 1.9 Ã….35 From their electron density map, all the metal atoms of the Mn4CaO5 cluster together with all of their ligands were located unambiguously. Fig. 2 depicts the structure of the Mn4CaO5 cluster and its ligand environment. Three manganese, one calcium and four oxygen atoms form a cubane-like structure and the forth manganese atom dangles outside the cubane and is linked to two manganese atoms by two oxygen bridges. Meanwhile, four water molecules were found to coordinate to the Mn4CaO5 cluster: two to the dangling Mn and the other two to the calcium atom. In addition, all of the amino-acid residues coordinated to the Mn4CaO5 cluster were identified.

4 WOCs and their reaction mechanisms

WOCs have to manage to generate high valence metal-oxo species via proton-coupled electron transfer (PCET) steps, which is essential for water oxidation. Two widely accepted Oâ€“O bond formation mechanisms are (i) water nucleophilic attack (WNA) and (ii) interaction of two Mâ€“O units (I2M) (Fig. 4).47 In the WNA pathway, an oxygen of solvent water molecule nucleophilically attacks the metal-oxo group, leading to a 2eâˆ’ reduction of the metal center and Oâ€“O bond formation. In the I2M pathway, the Oâ€“O bond formation is achieved through the interaction of two mono-radical Mâ€“O units, affording a peroxo intermediate.

4.2 Ir-based WOCs: active and robust

In 2008, Bernhard and coworkers showed the first family of bis(cyclometalated) iridium(III) WOCs (15, Fig. 19).74 The surrounding ligand of this species could be easily tailored with various electron-donating and -withdrawing substituents, thereby tuning the redox properties of respective Ir catalysts. Most interestingly, these catalysts are exceptionally robust under harsh working conditions (strong oxidizing CeIV plus pH < 1), probably due to the strong Câ€“Ir bonding. Several catalysts have been shown to work over one week and overall TONs are up to 2760. However, their catalytic rates turn out to be relatively slow. Later on, Crabtree and coworkers found that another family of Ir complexes based on Cp* (Cp* = C5Me5) which was supposed to have strong electron donating ability were capable of catalyzing the oxidation of water.75,76 These Cp*â€“Ir complexes (such as 16 and 17, Fig. 19) showed better catalytic activity but worse robustness compared with Bernhard and colleagues' catalysts. The analogues of 16, formally [Cp*Ir(NâC)X], [Cp*Ir(NâN)X]+ and [CpIr(NâN)X]+ (Cp = C5H5), were concluded as precatalysts. Oxygen evolution under CeIV-pH 1 conditions was found to be first order in Ir catalysts. Most likely, the WNA pathway as discussed for [Ru(tpy)(bpy)(OH2)]2+ is involved. A plausible catalytic mechanism of Oâ€“O bond formation was proposed as shown in Fig. 20. The coordinating Xâˆ’ ligand is replaced by a H2O molecule, generating an Ir aqua complex. This aqua complex is oxidized by CeIV to the Ir = O state which is then attacked by a water molecule forming the Oâ€“O bond. Oxidation of the resulting peroxo intermediate produces O2.

Further development of Ir WOCs is achieved by replacing the phenylpyridine ligand in complex 16 by an abnormal pyridinium-carbene ligand. Complex 18 was obtained and showed a remarkably high TON of ca. 10000 within 5 days.77 The authors suggested that the excellent activity of 18 originates from the high electronic flexibility of the mesoionic ligand: the neutral carbine-type resonance form stabilizes low metal oxidation states while the charge separate form, that is a cationic iminium plus a metal-bound carbon anion, stabilizes high metal oxidation states. 4.3 First row transition metal-based WOCs

Water splitting catalyzed by earth abundant, environmentally friendly and inexpensive elements, such as Mn, Co and Fe, is ideal for the sustainable development of our energy system. Although nature chooses the manganese complex as its oxygen evolving center, few synthetic manganese complexes have so far achieved water oxidation.39 Only two Mn complexes [(tpy)2Mn2O2(H2O)2](NO3)3 (19; Fig. 21),78 and [Lb6Mn4O4]+ cubanes (20; Lb = (p-R-C6H4)2PO2âˆ’, R = H, Me, OMe; Fig. 23),79 were reported to catalyze water oxidation without using an oxygen-transferring oxidant.

5. Immobilization of molecular WOCs

When people consider applying the molecular catalysts discussed above into any electrocatalytic or photoelectrocatalytic device, towards achievement of sustained water oxidation in practice, a key problem that has to be confronted is how to immobilize the catalyst on the semi-conductive/conductive surface of the electrode. This issue, however, is usually overlooked, since most attention has been drawn on the development of highly efficient WOCs. To find an ideal method of grafting molecular WOCs on the electrode surface is definitely challenging if the following requirements for a qualified â€˜connectionâ€™ between a molecular WOC and an electrode are taken into account: (i) stability in harsh oxidative conditions. The practical electrical potential required to drive water oxidation is typically 1.29â€“1.8 V,36,85 and the existence of dioxygen is certainly inevitable in the reaction medium, so the WOC has to be immobilized in a way strong enough to survive in the water electrolysis conditions. (ii) Resistance against acidic aqueous conditions. The protons generated from O2 evolution could accumulate close to the electrode surface and dramatically decrease the pH value of the microenvironment around the catalysts. (iii) An efficient transfer of electrons from catalyst to electrode, which is of course essential for the efficiency of electrochemical catalysis.

From the aspect of molecular interaction force, the modes of molecular absorption on a host surface can be summarized in the following categories: â€œ(a) covalent attachment brought about by directly linking groups of interest or via linking agents, (b) electrostatic interactions, brought about via ion exchange, ion-pairing or donorâ€“acceptor interactions, (c) hydrogen bonding, especially important in biological systems, (d) hydrophobic interactions leading to a self-assembly of long chain fatty acid derivatives, (e) van der Waals forces involved in the physisorption of molecules on solid surfaces, and (f) physical entrapment inside the pores or cavities of hosts such as cyclodextrins, micelles, etc.â€.86 Several approaches have been developed during the past couple of years, aiming to immobilizing well-defined molecular WOCs on the electrode surface, which will be briefly reviewed below.

5.1. Covalent attachment

The covalent bond, in terms of bond energy, can certainly provide a stronger interlinkage between molecules and the host surface rather than other binding modes. This binding strategy has been successfully adopted in the construction of DSCs (dye-sensitized solar cells), at the photoanodes of which sensitizers bearing anchoring groups, carboxylic acid group in most situations, react with the surface hydroxyl groups of the semiconductor oxide to form a covalent bond.87 It has been verified that electrons can efficiently transfer from the sensitizers to the semiconductor surface through the carboxylic binding bridge. It is also found that a phosphonic acid group can bind onto a semiconductor oxide surface in a stronger manner than that of a carboxylic acid group.88 Meanwhile, the phosphonate binding linkage is more resistant against desorption in the presence of water compared with carboxylate linkage.

There are, however, two major difficulties in applying the same method in the immobilization of molecular WOCs on the oxide surface. One is the complication of preparing WOCs with a carboxylic or phosphonic anchoring group. The other is hydrolysis of carboxylate- or phosphonate-oxide linkages in acidic aqueous environment of water oxidation.

In 2007, Meyer's group reported the synthesis of a phosphonate-modified water oxidation complex, [(tpy-PO3H2)(H2O)2RuIII]2O4+ (23; tpy-PO3H2 = 4â€²-phosphonat-2,2â€²:6â€²,2â€²â€²-terpyridine), and its immobilization on a range of metal oxide electrode surfaces including ITO (tin-doped indium oxide), TiO2, ZrO2 and SnO2 (Fig. 26a).89 Electrocatalytic water oxidation could be initiated by stepping the applied potential to either 1.52 or 1.35 V vs. NHE at pH = 6.0, depending on different oxidized intermediates. Due to the limited catalytic activity of the tpy-Ru complex,90 the calculated turnover numbers based on the O2 evolution were not higher than 3. A dramatic drop of catalytic currents was also observed during the electrocatalysis. Nevertheless, this work provided the possibility of covalent molecular WOCs attachment on the electrode surface. As subsequent achievement of their exploration, Meyer and coworkers reported another two remarkable examples of molecular WOC-electrode assemblies via phosphonate linkages very recently. The first case is immobilization of [Ru(Mebimpy)(4,4â€²-(CH2PO3H2)2bpy)(OH2)]2+ (24; Mebimpy = 2,6-bis(1-methylbenzimidazol-2-yl)-pyridine) on FTO (fluorine-doped tin oxide) substrates (Fig. 26b), which could undergo electrocatalytic water oxidation at 1.85 V vs. NHE in a pH 5 buffer with a current density of âˆ¼14.8 Î¼A cmâˆ’2, corresponding to a turnover frequency of âˆ¼21.6 minâˆ’1.91 There was no obvious decay in the catalytic current for a period of more than 8 h electrolysis, corresponding to âˆ¼11000 turnovers. In the second case, a phosphonate-modified ruthenium trisdiimine moiety was introduced as an electron transfer mediator between the catalytic center [(bpm)RuII(Mebimpy)(OH2)]2+ (bpm is 2,2â€²-bipyrimidine) and the electrode surface ([((4,4â€²-CH2PO3H2)2bpy)2RuII(bpm)RuII(Mebimpy)(OH2)]4+ (25; Fig. 26c).92 The electrolysis was performed at 1.8 V vs. NHE in 1.0 M HClO4 resulting in a sustained catalytic current for more than 13 h without any sign of decrease in catalytic activity. A TOF of 36 minâˆ’1 and a TON of 28000 were achieved according to the catalytic current density. It should be noted that in both situations, the surface-bound complexes retain their chemical and physical properties, as well as the defined water oxidation mechanism of the corresponding single-site WOCs in homogeneous catalysis.93

Comparing with adsorption of complexes by a polymer support, a more straightforward strategy for catalyst immobilization is electropolymerization of molecular WOCs, as monomers, directly on the electrode surface. 5.4. Direct deposit

Physical adsorption of molecules on solid surfaces is a simple method for immobilization of water insoluble WOCs. 6. PEC device

The light-driven water oxidation discussed in section 4 implies that essential building blocks (WOCs and photosensitizers) are already in hand for the construction of PECs. However, efficient water oxidation/proton reduction catalysts and matched photosensitizers are necessary but not sufficient for developing fulfilled PECs. The complexity of such a device is that there is an electron transfer chain for each cycle of water molecule splitting, which includes a sequential ET process from water to catalyst, catalyst to photosensitizer, photosensitizer to anode, and anode to cathode. So the challenge now is how to arrange all these components in such a way that fast electron transfer occurs at every step of the process while charge recombination can be efficiently suppressed. For instance, fabrication of photosensitizer and catalyst that keeps the electron flow in the right direction is still a problem. To date, feasible PECs are rarely reported. One creative example of PECs was reported by Mallouk and co-workers, in which TiO2 semiconductor particles, [Ru(bpy)3]2+ photosensitizer, and heterogeneous IrO2 were assembled via phosphonate and carboxylate anchoring groups (Fig. 31).25 The purpose of this organization is to promote long-lived charge separation between the WOC and the electron acceptor section. Irradiation of the working electrode with visible light (> 410nm) under a bias potential of 325 mV vs. Ag/AgCl produced a pronounced photo-current (about 30 Î¼A). Very recently, Hou, Wang and co-workers reported a heteronanostructured photoanode fabricated by atomic layer deposition of WO3 and coated with a Mn-based oxygen-evolving catalyst.106 The coating was prepared by thermal decomposition of [(tpy)2Mn2O2(H2O)2](NO3)3 (19). The thermal decomposition product is not identified yet although it is clear that the Mn-containing material is not MnO2 according to elemental analysis, EPR, FTIR and atomic absorption spectroscopy. Since this review focuses on characterized molecular WOCs, here we will discuss only two examples that involved application of molecular WOCs as anodic building blocks reported respectively by the Spiccia and Sun groups.24,26 These two PECs have a similar architecture concept as illustrated in Fig. 32 but with different WOCs. The photo-anode consists of WOC on the Nafion membrane attached on a dye sensitized TiO2 film.

Dye-sensitized nanocrystalline TiO2, which has been successfully applied in dye-sensitized solar cells, is an attractive solution for generating long-lived photo-induced charge separation states. Due to the properties of semiconductive TiO2 nanoparticles, an electron injection from the excited dye into the conductive band of TiO2 is much faster than the reverse electron recombination process resulting in a long-lived oxidized state of photosensitizer.87 The long-lived electron-deficient photosensitizer is a premise for efficient electron extract from WOCs. Although TiO2 itself was reported to split water into H2 and O2, it only works at UV region due to its large band gap (3â€“3.2 eV).87,107 Therefore dyes that could absorb visible light are needed to assist a visible light-driven reaction. Thus, nanocrystalline TiO2 sensitized with [Ru(bpy)3]2+ derivatives was chosen as a visible light absorption system in the above mentioned PEC devices. A layer of the Nafion membrane, coated onto dye sensitized TiO2, was employed to immobilize WOCs through electrostatic attraction (vide supra). In an aqueous solution, Nafion is penetrable for both electrons and protons (Fig. 32b).

The working principle of the PEC is similar to that in DSSC: the photosensitizer is excited by photons and injects an electron into the conduction band of TiO2; the oxidized photosensitizer is then regenerated by transferring the hole to the WOC which reaches its active state after multi-hole transfer processes and subsequently oxidizes water to dioxygen. The current is generated by an electron flow through the external circuit from anode to Pt cathode, where protons are reduced.

In Spiccia's system,26 the WOC is a mono-cationic Mn-oxo cluster, [L6Mn4O4]+ (20) and the photosensitizer is [Ru(bpy)2(4,4â€²-(COOH)2bpy)]2+ (P7). The dye/catalyst ratio of 20-Nafion/P7-TiO2 is 4.5. 7. Concluding remarks

After decades of endeavors by the chemists, the field of water oxidation catalysis has been promoted to a new level. WOCs based on different transition metals, including Ru, Ir, Mn, Co and Fe, are being rapidly developed. Some catalysts exhibit promising catalytic activities, such as Ru complex 10 (TOF > 2500 minâˆ’1), Co4 complex 21 (TOF = 300 minâˆ’1) and Fe complex 22d (TOF = 78 minâˆ’1); some show impressive robustness, for instance Ir complex 18 (TON â‰ˆ 10000, TOF â‰ˆ 5 min) and Ru dimer 5 (TON = 10400, TOF = 72 minâˆ’1). Importantly, fundamental mechanistic studies on water oxidation have helped the chemists to understand the overall process, from well-studied molecular WOCs, such as the blue dimer, the Ru-Hbpp complex and the one-site Ru-aqua complexes.

Light-driven water oxidation plays a crucial role in the final goal of artificial water splitting and is currently more striking. Despite all the above advances, the development of efficient and durable catalysts for visible light water splitting is still a challenging problem. Although many WOCs are capable of catalyzing water oxidation using CeIV as oxidant or driven directly by electrical power, only a few of them have low overpotentials. Consequently, visible light-driven water oxidation using sensitizers with mild oxidation potentials is rarely reported. In the three-component light-driven water oxidation system, the intermolecular electron transfer from catalysts to sensitizers is not efficient enough to compete with the ET from sensitizer to the sacrificial acceptor so that photosensitizers tend to decompose in an oxidative way. To overcome these problems and improve the light-driven water oxidation system, an enhanced catalytic rate and electron transfer efficiency have to be considered.

Yet, there is still no clear recognition of the structureâ€“correlation of WOCs, which is necessary for designing effective catalysts. In the light of well documented literature sources, some empirical principles could be considered to minimize the overpotential and to increase the reactivity of WOCs: (i) lowering the oxidation potentials by stabilizing the high energy intermediates via introducing strong electron donating ligands, such as negatively charged ligands, while keeping the redox potentials of the designed metal oxoes in proper positions (>0.82 V); (ii) optimizing the surrounding ligands of the metal center to minimize the reorganization energy of the transition states; (iii) introducing an internal basic site to facilitate concerted proton-coupled electron transfer and/or to pre-orientate water molecules for attacking of metal oxo from the right direction (Fig. 4); (iv) in addition, using all-inorganic, carbon-free templates (such as polyoxometalate) instead of organic ligands for complexation with transition metals is an alternative to producing robust WOCs. More detailed knowledge on the reaction mechanism is always desired for further development of WOCs.

Immobilization of homogeneous WOCs on the electrode surface allows to carry out water oxidation under milder conditions (high pH solution, for instance, which favors water oxidation), protects the ligand from oxidative decomposition via collision between ligands and strong oxidants (CeIV and high valent Ru = O species), and ultimately increases the durability of WOCs.91,103 For instance, as mentioned in section 5.3, the polymer hybrid material based on complex [RuII2(Î¼-bpp)(H2O)2(pp-tpy)2]2+ is more durable than the monomer itself under CeIV-pH 1 working conditions. We have summarized the most commonly used methods to attach molecular WOCs to electrode and semiconductor surfaces (section 5). These include covalent attachment, electropolymerization, electrostatic interaction and direct deposit. The former two methods obviously have stronger bonding between the catalyst and material surface. However their relatively complex synthetic requirement is of disadvantage. There is no doubt that advances in material science will motivate novel methodologies for immobilization of molecular WOCs.

Construction of PECs which is the way of applying the artificial photosynthesis concept into practice is developing in parallel with the process of material and supramolecular chemistry. An ideal design of a PEC should facilitate the ET from the catalyst to the surface and avoid the backward charge recombination at the same time, which seems to be a paradox. The successful development of DSSCs, especially the application of dye sensitized crystalline TiO2, have been a great inspiration in the design of PECs. Under the current stage, the oxidative decomposition of photosensitizers due to slow ET from WOCs to sensitizers is still a serious problem. With a view to developing water splitting PECs with high efficiency and long term stability, the building blocks of PECs, including water oxidation/reduction catalysts, light harvesting unit and electrode materials, have to be significantly improved. Overcoming these large challenges will promise us a bright future of artificial photosynthesis.
