Abstract

The recent high interest and the fast development of heterostructures of two-dimensional (2D) layered materials are driven by their unique properties and many potential applications. One of the advantages of 2D layered nanostructures is their attractive physical, chemical and mechanical properties that could be designed and tuned by manipulating their composition and thickness at the atomic or molecular level during the synthesis process. This review firstly introduces several versatile chemical approaches for the bottom-up fabrication of 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials. A few types of nanolayered organicâ€“inorganic materials and their tunable properties are then presented and discussed from the view of having high-performance characteristics. The chemical attributes that would enhance the properties of nanolayered organicâ€“inorganic materials are also assessed. This article then focuses on the applications of nanolayered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials in clean energy research such as batteries, supercapacitors, water splitting, perovskite solar cells and thermoelectric devices. The final section discusses current challenges and future directions of nanolayered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials.

1. Introduction

The rapid progress in nanoscience and technology provides opportunities for manufacturing nanostructures via efficient processing routes. Nanostructures with a large surface area especially a large interface structure could enhance the effectiveness of these materials. Hence, hybrid nanolayered architectures based on two-dimensional (2D) structures are increasingly significant for the design and fabrication of next-generation structural nanomaterials that can provide outstanding physical, chemical and mechanical behaviors. 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid architectures are attracting attention in applied research for their potential applications in fields such as energy storage,1â€“3 energy conversion,4,5 catalysis,6,7 sensors,8 and electrochemistry.9 Research in 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid nanostructures is relatively new and publication records are quite limited.10 However, this field is developing rapidly because many emerging high-efficiency clean energy devices are incorporated with such novel nanostructures. Generally, the inorganic components of layered organicâ€“inorganic materials provide favorable physical, chemical and mechanical properties,11 while the organic components provide flexibility and low density to the material.11 Therefore, heterostructures based on 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic architectures offer opportunities to develop new desirable materials with tunable properties that are intermediate between those of components.

There are several bottom-up film deposition techniques for the synthesis of 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid nanomaterials including solâ€“gel,12 solution-processing,13â€“15 Langmuirâ€“Blodgett (LB),16 layer-by-layer (LbL) assembly,17,18 and molecular layer deposition (MLD)/atomic layer deposition (ALD)19â€“22 techniques. Gas-phase techniques such as ALD/MLD are able to control the film thickness and composition at the atomic and molecular level; thereby having a capability to uniformly coat high aspect ratio structures.11 Among the above-mentioned techniques; solution-processing, ALD and MLD are three approaches that have been widely used for many applications.10,11,23 Solution-processing is an alternative possibility to traditional vacuum-based deposition techniques that is employed to deposit transparent thin films. This technique is favored because of its simplicity, scalability and low cost.24 The inorganic ALD structure demonstrates a conformal structure at the atomic level control (âˆ¼1 Ã…) on high aspect ratio structures (Table 1).25â€“29 These thin films are continuous and pinhole-free.28,29 The ALD technique is typically used for the synthesis of metal oxides and metal nitride binary materials.11

In the late 2000s, ALD and MLD approaches were combined to generate organicâ€“inorganic hybrid composites.20 The feasibility to mix and match organic and inorganic precursors leads to the formation of a broad spectrum of hybrid multilayer films with various properties.11 The layered organicâ€“inorganic nanostructures may not only possess properties from film constituents, but also have completely new properties. It is also possible to tune the mechanical, chemical and other physical properties by varying the film components and thickness. For example, the film properties of metal alkoxides may vary from soft, low dense (representative of the organic constituent) to hard, dense metal oxides that are based on the inorganic constituent.

The tunable properties of engineered hybrid organicâ€“inorganic multilayers show high promise in fabricating advanced multilayer nanostructures with desirable properties. Hence, this review paper examines 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid nanostructures and their important roles and applications in the research field of clean energy. 2. Bottom-up chemical approaches for the fabrication of 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic heterostructures

Bottom-up chemical approaches play a substantial role in developing nanostructured materials at the atomic or molecular level. Therefore, these methods deliver superior chemical homogeneity in comparison to top-down physical techniques.50 Furthermore, chemical processing design and synthesis of new materials may be refined into the final end products.50 However, toxic reagents and solvents are regularly employed for the synthesis of nanostructured materials.51 There are several bottom-up chemical methods for fabricating 2D layered hybrid organicâ€“inorganic nanostructures; of which several will be described briefly in the following section.

2.1. Solution-processing

Solution-processing is a versatile technique for depositing transparent thin films over substrate surfaces. It permits researchers to tune the composition and, therefore, the properties of the final coatings. Fig. 2a presents a flowchart for a typical solution-processing method. Solution deposition uses a precursor whose chemical structure includes the cation of interest and reacts to form the desired structure. The precursor selection depends on the chemical formulation of the thin film, solvent and processing route. The solution-processing route for transparent conducting oxide thin films usually engages acetates, chlorides or nitrates since these precursors are inexpensive and commercially available.24 There are some other precursor candidates that have been reviewed by Pasquarelli et al.24 After the selection of precursors, the formulation of coating solution is the second step which involves the selection of a solvent and an additive. There are two main types of formulations used for the coating solution: (i) metalâ€“organic/ionic solutions and (ii) colloidal nanoparticle suspensions. Each type of formulation has intrinsic advantages and disadvantages.24

This film deposition that can be performed at atmospheric pressure with minimal equipment investment is the next step in a solution-processing technique. There are several scalable deposition approaches,24 as named in Fig. 2a, which are viable for uniform and large-area coverage.

Thermal processing is the last step of the solution-processing approach which is performed for (i) decomposition of the precursors and removal of any organics, (ii) crystallization or phase transformation, and (iii) controlling electron carrier concentration. Thermal processing can be performed on a hotplate or in an adjusted environment with a tube furnace. The curing and sintering temperatures during thermal treatment of nanoparticle thin films are different.52 Particles drop their organic capping shell at the curing temperature and begin displaying conductivity since they are in direct physical contact. However, sintering occurs at a higher temperature and leads to neck formation between particles. Therefore, sintering results in a dense film rather than nanoparticulate networks.

Chen et al.53 reported a facile solution-processing method to produce a monolayer or few layers of an organicâ€“inorganic perovskite (Fig. 2b and c). They dissolved PbBr2 and C4H9NH3+Br2âˆ’ in dimethylformamide (DMF) via a two-step solution process to form the perovskite precursor. The precursor directly crystallizes into a 2D cross star and layered cubic (C4H9NH3+)2PbBr42âˆ’ perovskites upon evaporation (Fig. 2b and c). The fabricated cross star nanostructure has large dimensional 2D perovskites that are ultrathin and exhibit high stability and significant photoelectric conversion efficiency. This work demonstrated the successful synthesis of new 2D perovskite structures and confirmed improvements of optoelectronic devices primarily based on ultrathin 2D perovskites.

2.2. Atomic layer deposition (ALD)

ALD or atomic layer epitaxy (ALE) is a chemical deposition technique for fabricating high-quality, large surface area thin films with atomic size precision.54Fig. 3a demonstrates an ALD method where thin layers cyclically grow to form a binary compound from gaseous precursors. ALD can be described as a surface controlled LbL thin film deposition approach. It has the greatest advantages for developing thin films where film thickness and composition could be controlled at the atomic level. ALD can create a wide variety of film components with high density, low impurity and precise control of thickness. Deposition at low temperatures can relieve adverse influences on sensitive substrates. ALD has been employed for the deposition of selective metals or metal oxides on 2D nanomaterials such as the deposition of platinum (Pt) at graphene line defects55 or Al2O3 on 2D transition metal chalcogenides (TMDs).56

The slow deposition rate is the main drawback of the ALD method with only a portion of coverage usually deposited in each cycle. Nevertheless, a very thin layer is normally required for next-generation electronic devices and, hence, the slow growth rate of the ALD technique may not be a crucial issue.

2.3. Molecular layer deposition (MLD)

MLD is another chemical deposition technique. Similar to ALD, MLD is also a gaseous-phase process and has emerged for the deposition of organic constituents in nanoscale films. In fact, MLD has opened a new avenue for synthesizing novel nanostructured materials. This method can also grow high-quality thin films with controllable thickness and composition at the molecular-scale. Fig. 4a illustrates a MLD process using two homobifunctional precursors. The molecules of one precursor react with the surface by mechanisms of interlocking and chaining chemistry to form a molecular layer of the precursor on the surface with new reactive sites.10 This process continues with the molecules of another precursor until the thin film layers cyclically grow to establish a binary compound from gaseous precursors. The MLD method has been combined with the ALD technique to fabricate organicâ€“inorganic hybrid nanostructures.57 Jaggernauth et al.58 used the MLD method to functionalize the surface of graphene oxide with an organicâ€“inorganic hybrid film using TMA (as a metal precursor) and ethylene glycol (EG) reagents (Fig. 4b). The analysis showed that thicknesses of 5, 8.5 and 12.5 nm were obtained after 20, 50 and 80 deposition cycles, respectively. Fig. 4c shows the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image of the deposited hybrid layer after 80 MLD cycles. The functionalized graphene oxide exhibits superior electrical, mechanical and thermal properties59â€“61 and, therefore, has great potential for applications such as energy-related materials, sensors or biomedical applications.

3. Tunable properties of 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic heterostructures

The MLD/ALD layered nanostructures are prepared from at least two materials; for instance, hybrid organicâ€“inorganic materials and their parent metal oxides. The sublayer and bilayer of the nanostructures, grown by using the metalcone MLD system and their parent metal oxide ALD system, can be adjusted by varying the relative cycle numbers of MLD and ALD. The organic sublayers fabricated by MLD exhibit a lower density that reflects the intrinsic properties of organic polymers, while the inorganic sublayers fabricated by ALD exhibit a much higher density. Thus, the density of layered nanostructures can be tuned from the low density of the pure MLD film to the high density of the inorganic ALD film.11 In addition to density, other properties such as conductivity, dielectric constant, mechanical and optical properties can be adjusted, as well. The following sections discuss the tunable properties of several noteworthy MLD/ALD layered nanostructures including Al2O3, zirconium dioxide (ZrO2), zinc oxide (ZnO) and lithium terephthalate (Li2C8H4O4, LiTP).

4.3. Water splitting

Water splitting using semiconductor photocatalysts is one of the green and sustainable technologies for the generation of hydrogen as clean energy.140 Implementation of 2D layered components in the field of photocatalysis has driven certain interest and has quickly grown to be one of several most desirable research topics.141,142 The fabrication of 2D layered composite photocatalysts can create numerous distinctive properties that usually may not be found in other types of photocatalysts, which are valuable and effective for photocatalytic efficiency advancement.143 For instance, graphene (or reduced graphene oxide) exhibits massive charge transportation properties, which is frequently incorporated with other inorganic materials such as transition metal oxides for photocatalytic and photo conversion application in renewable energy.144,145 Therefore, 2D layered composite nanostructures have various extraordinary benefits that favor photocatalytic applications such as (i) a large surface-to-volume ratio which is able to provide a massive number of active sites on the surface to substantially boost the photocatalytic activity.146,147 (ii) Some 2D layered materials, for instance, graphene, possess excellent conductivity and outstanding electron mobility because of the Ï€-conjugated framework. This can supply an ultrafast electron transfer platform to facilitate the splitting up of photogenerated electrons and holes within the photocatalyst.148,149 (iii) 2D layered materials with nano-size structures possess great catalyst activities, which are effective for catalyst dispersion. Additionally, the coupling of diverse 2D layered materials or nanoparticles/2D nanosheets will significantly improve the photocatalytic efficiency of the 2D combined materials because of an enhanced contact surface and effective interfacial charge transfer.140,150 The interfacial bonding strength of the coupled nanostructures could also be improved, resulting in more stable structures.151,152

Polymeric graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4) has been identified as the most stable allotrope of C3N4 with great photo-reactivity under ambient conditions.153 g-C3N4 has a stacking 2D layered structure like graphite with a band gap of 2.7 eV which makes it active in the visible light region.140 (Note: the visible light coverage range is between âˆ¼1.8 and 3.1 eV, or âˆ¼390 and 700 nm.) It shows high thermal and chemical stabilities against high temperature and acidic solvents, and has been extensively investigated in the field of photocatalysis.154,155 Wang et al.156 firstly investigated g-C3N4 in the field of photocatalysis for water splitting under visible light irradiation. Subsequently, Niu et al.157 fabricated 2D g-C3N4 nanosheets with a thickness of approximately 2 nm using a thermal oxidation etching technique for photocatalytic hydrogen production. 2D g-C3N4 nanosheets displayed much better photocatalytic activity than their bulk structure under visible light irradiation because of (i) a higher surface area that enhanced the electron transport capability along the in-plane direction, and (ii) a larger band gap that improved the oxidation and reduction of charge carriers.140 Recently, Cai et al.158 fabricated 2D g-C3N4/N-doped La2Ti2O7 (g-C3N4/NLTO) hybrid layered nanostructures using a facile two-step hydrothermal method followed by a modified thermal oxidation etching technique (Fig. 13a).

4.5. Thermoelectric devices

Thermoelectric systems are devices that generate electrical energy from heat. They are made of a type of semiconducting thermoelectric material and their energy conversion efficiency can be enhanced by improving their thermoelectric figure of merit (ZT).178ZT = (S2ÏƒT)Îºâˆ’1, where S, Ïƒ, T and Îº are the Seebeck coefficient, electrical conductivity, absolute temperature and thermal conductivity, respectively. The thermal conductivity Îº is frequently demonstrated as Îº = Îºp + Îºe, where Îºp and Îºe illustrate the phonon and electronic contributions, respectively. S2Ïƒ also reflects the power factor.82 The main barrier to feasible thermoelectric energy conversion efficiency is the failure to individually manipulate the Ïƒ and Îº properties of the regular semiconductor materials because charge carrier density and mobility of the common inorganic thermoelectrics are generally interlocked with thermal transport and thermopower.5 Thus, increasing Ïƒ is usually associated with increasing Îº. This phenomenon is an issue for increasing ZT and hence, thermoelectric energy conversion efficiency. To overcome this issue, organic materials such as conducting polymers have also been considered for thermoelectric applications because of their reasonably low thermal conductivity.179 However, the S value of organic materials is relatively lower than that of inorganic materials,5 which is a drawback for ZT. Hence, a great promising category of materials that has been emerging for thermoelectric applications is that of 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials.5,180,181 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials could improve the performance of thermoelectric materials because their thermal conductivity may well be suppressed without limiting the electrical transport properties.182,183 Ultrathin layered materials consisting of organicâ€“inorganic superlattices have been used as flexible thermoelectric materials for high-efficiency power generation.178,182,183 The 2D layered structures could block phonon transport (Îºp) by interfacial coupling and scattering effects without negatively affecting the electrical properties.182,183

Intercalation is a chemical technique to reversibly incorporate guest species; ranging from ions, atoms or molecules, inside a crystal gap.180 2D layered materials usually possess strong in-plane covalent bonding but weak out-of-plane van der Waals interactions through the interlayer gap (Fig. 15a).184 Therefore, intercalation could be a possible way to insert organic molecules as guests in between the 2D layers to perturb phonon propagation and reduce Îºp.

5. Challenges of 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic heterostructures

2D layered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid nanostructures that can be employed as versatile platforms for the exploration of different phenomena at the nanoscale are still at the starting stage. 2D nanostructures can provide unusual physical, chemical and mechanical properties to the system. Many opportunities exist in this research area, especially with growing demands for high-performance clean energy devices. In a 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid nanostructure that was synthesized by combined ALD/MLD processes, organic molecules are covalently bonded to the inorganic metal atoms to form a periodic thin-film structure.20 The fabricated 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid nanostructures may possess properties combined from those of the two parent materials. The hybrid nanostructures may also have some other brand-new properties. By combining different inorganic, organic and hybrid layers into one thin film and superstructure, it is possible to further tune the material properties. For instance, accurate control of the refractive index in optical applications,185 control of the electrical properties and non-volatile memories for storage capacitors and for transparent thin-film transistors,186,187 and tunability of the surface roughness in gas sensors,188 as well as tuning the bandgap of the 2D hybrid perovskites in the range of visible light to achieve high photovoltaic efficiency have been achieved.189

The practical applications of organicâ€“inorganic hybrid nanocomposites for clean energy require solutions that are related to the material processing, such as the restacking problem of 2D materials. For instance, wet-chemical methods are usually used to synthesize 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskites by directly spreading the diluted precursor solution onto smooth substrates, along with crystallization upon solvent evaporation.160 However, it should be noted that inorganic nanosheets have a tendency to become either (i) re-stacked and agglomerated in solvents, or (ii) roll up and form nanotubes under certain treatment,190 which decreases the effective surface area. This drawback can be overcome by using strategies such as surfactants to modify the surface of nanosheets for homogeneous dispersion. It is also feasible to create porous structures by removing the organic parts from the organicâ€“inorganic hybrid films using traditional annealing or wet-etching procedures.191,192 The porous structures are suitable for use in optics, electronics and catalysis. The fabrication of an efficient energy storage or conversion device is carried out by depositing or transferring the 2D organicâ€“inorganic hybrid nanostructure onto an electronically conducting substrate.

Another challenge in electronic devices is how to strongly attach or bind layered nanosheets onto electrode/conducting frameworks to create efficient interfaces for facile charge transport. Based on the nature of the electrode and hybrid nanosheets, a variety of electrode treatment methods such as self-assembled monolayer modification and hydrogen/nitrogen/plasma treatments are typically implemented for better attaching nanosheets onto electrodes.193 The result is that charge carriers migrate quite easily through interfaces toward electrodes. On the other hand, because of the low conductivity of organic materials in the 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic nanostructure, fewer charge carriers reach the electrodes. This could possibly be resolved by using an appropriate dopant especially surfactant and metal nanostructures such as graphene, gold, platinum, and silver that may facilitate charge movement towards electrodes. Furthermore, toxic elements, for example lead in 2D BA2PbBr4 (BA = C4H9NH3+, butylammonium ion) hybrid perovskite semiconductors, have detrimental effects for the environmental as well as health concerns.161 Thus, further studies are required; even on fundamental studies concerning the precise growth mechanisms of new types of thin films. Fundamental studies provide insights for the selection of complementary precursor combinations.

Recently, it has been indicated that periodically repeating organic layers inserted within thicker inorganic layers can block heat conduction.178 In another study, it has been shown that lead-free 2D BA2SnI4 and BA2GeI4 perovskites exhibited bandgaps of 1.45 and 1.74 eV, respectively.161 These results are encouraging for developing high-efficiency clean energy devices such as thermoelectrics and PSCs. 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic nanostructures could also enhance the gas barrier coatings on biopolymer materials.20 Furthermore, they have potential for application in environmental monitoring; however, selectivity remains a significant concern. Thus, advancement of optimized target-analyte-specific hybrid nanostructures is required. Research and modifications designed for the interface between inorganic host materials and guest organic nanosheets are essential for the performance of hybrid nanostructures. Thus, the science of interfaces requires more clarity and knowledge.

6. Conclusions and prospects

This review presents recent research activities in the field of 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic heteronanostructures. Details of the synthesis procedure, structural design, tunable properties, challenges and their applications in the state-of-the-art devices have been summarized. The 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic nanostructures are an emerging research topic. Therefore, research has been directed towards the design and synthesis of new ultrathin 2D hybrid structures with tunable components and properties. Generally, the inorganic component can provide favorable mechanical, chemical, electrical and optical properties, while the organic component can, in addition, provide flexibility, reduced density and lower cost. Hybrid organicâ€“inorganic thin films were then demonstrated to show great promise in advanced clean energy fields such as energy storage and conversion devices, flexible transparent conducting displays,70,71 solar cell applications,194,195 flexible electrical and optical thin film devices,196,197 catalysis,198,199 gas sensors200,201 and gas diffusion barriers.202,203

Progress has been made in the area of 2D layered hybrid organicâ€“inorganic nanostructures by fabricating metal alkoxide films, as illustrated by â€œmetalconesâ€, using MLD/ALD techniques. For instance, it has been demonstrated that hybrid organicâ€“inorganic multilayers can efficiently block heat conduction and create ultralow thermal conductivity that is significant in the field of thermoelectrics.74 It is anticipated that MLD/ALD techniques provide a fashionable and versatile method to fabricate new materials and devices in a controlled and industrially feasible way.178 Hence, the current and future 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic nanostructures will require continued investigation and identification for specific applications. With many efforts on the fabrication of 2D layered nanostructures on solid substrates, researchers have explored the synthesis of free-standing 2D organicâ€“inorganic nanostructures. However, the application of free-standing 2D nanostructures may have been limited because of the less control over their geometric and surface properties. There is no doubt that the advancement of 2D layered organicâ€“inorganic nanomaterials still encounters a range of challenges, such as (i) better understanding of the surface, structural disorder and structureâ€“property relationships to anticipate the functionality of the resulting 2D hybrid organicâ€“inorganic nanostructures and (ii) exploration of a simple and inexpensive process for large-scale industrial manufacturing. Overcoming these obstacles will help deliver 2D layered nanostructures with great potential for applications in advanced clean energy, nanoelectronics, sensing, catalysis and other related areas.
