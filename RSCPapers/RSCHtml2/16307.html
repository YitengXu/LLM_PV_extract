Abstract

A new series of organic sensitizers LJB-H, LJB-Fo and LJB-Fm with triphenylamine, 3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene plus various functionalized phenylenes, and cyanoacrylic acid as the donor, Ï€-spacer, and anchoring group, respectively. These organic sensitizers exhibit much higher molar extinction coefficients than the parent LJ1 dye as well as reveal a remarkable fluorine substituent effect. LJB-Fm with fluoro substitution at the meta position relative to cyanoacrylic acid possesses a lower S0â€“S1 gap and weaker coupling with the TiO2 electrode (cf.LJB-H and LJB-Fo). Conversely, due to the resonance effect with respect to cyanoacrylic acid, the ortho-F-substituted LJB-Fo has a larger energy gap but stronger TiO2 affinity. As a result, LJB-Fo shows better DSSC performance with Jsc = 15.58 mA cmâˆ’2, Voc = 787 mV, FF = 0.67 and Î· = 8.22%.

Introduction

Exhaustion of fossil fuel reserves and increased global warming are recognized as the most critical problems encountered in this century. In this regard, research targeted at solar energy harvesting is currently under extensive investigation. One useful technology should be ascribed to the dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSC), which is expected to dominate the low-cost niche market of consumer photovoltaics in the future.1 Typical DSSCs consist of a dyed semiconductor TiO2 photoelectrode, a Pt counter electrode and redox electrolyte.1c For the technical aspects, it has been recognized that the nature of sensitizers, no matter whether they are ruthenium based2 or pure organic,3 their redox potential, structure and photophysical properties, etc., play pivotal roles in determining the overall cell efficiencies. As such, there is continuous interest in exploring better sensitizers, electrolytes and device structure.4 Due to the relatively high cost of ruthenium element, numerous research groups have expended endeavors to develop metal free organic dyes as the alternative sensitizers. As a result, impressive photovoltaic performances have been reported on various tailor-made organic dyes, which include coumarin,5 indoline,6 oligoene,7 merocyanine,8 hemicyanine,9 perylene,10 spirobifluorene,11 and even chlorophyll derivatives,12 showing promising Î· values in the range of 5â€“9.8%. Recently, we reported a simple organic DSSC dye LJ1 (see Scheme 1).13 The core design of LJ1 lies in the introduction of triphenylamine14 (TPA) as donor and these two moieties are bridged by an electron-rich 3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene15 (EDOT) unit. Accordingly, the LJ1-sensitized solar cells prepared with a 12 + 4 Î¼m thickness of TiO2 film reached global AM 1.5 power conversion efficiencies up to 7.3%. Subsequently, Wang et al.15a reported using EDOT plus thienothiophene as Ï€-conjugated spacer connected to TPA, exhibiting an almost panchromatic response to solar irradiation. Very recently, we proposed that an ortho-fluorine substituent (with respect to the cyanoacrylic acid anchor) might play a pivotal role to enhance DSSC performance.16 Herein, we report on a comprehensive study in optimization of the LJ1 relevant dyes through increasing the optical absorption cross section and expanding light absorption capability to improve the photocurrent density. To achieve this goal, a facile approach was introduced by successfully incorporating a phenylene unit and fluorine-substituted phenylene into the LJ1 framework (see Scheme 1), coded LJB-H, LJB-Fo and LJB-Fm. We also aim to probe in-depth the fluorine substitution effect for LJB-FoversusLJB-Fm as well as their comparison to LJB-H, that lacks a fluorine substituent, on the DSSC performance. As a result, this new series of organic sensitizers exhibit much higher molar extinction coefficients than the parent LJ1 and show DSSC performance that reveals a significant fluorine substituent effect.

Fabrication of DSSC and photovoltaic measurements

The Pt counter electrode was prepared by spin-coating a 50mM H2PtCl6 in isopropyl alcohol on FTO glass, followed by sintering at 385 Â°C for 15â€“30 min. Transparent TiO2 paste was prepared using published procedures.17 The transparent TiO2 thin film with a thickness of 9 Î¼m was first deposited on a transparent conducting oxide (F-doped SnO2, FTO). This film was dried at 150 Â°C for 15 min and then a 4 Î¼m thick layer of 400 nm TiO2 particles (Ti-Nanoxide 300 paste from Solaronix) was deposited again by the doctor-blade method to form a square with dimensions of 0.4 Ã— 0.4 cm2. Afterwards, the double-layered films were sintered at 500 Â°C for 30 min. After sintering, the TiO2 films were treated with 40 mM of TiCl4 solution, rinsed with water and ethanol, and sintered at 500 Â°C for 30 min. After cooling to 80 Â°C, the TiO2 electrode was immersed into a solution containing 0.3 mM of LJB dyes, 1 mM of deoxycholic acid (DCA) in mixed CH3CN and tert-butanol solution (volume ratio: 1:1) overnight. After being rinsed with EtOH, the dye-coated TiO2 electrode was incorporated into a sandwich cell structure with a Pt-coated FTO as counter electrode, and a film (Surlyn 1702, 25 Î¼m) as a spacer between the electrodes. The electrolyte solution was then injected into the cell through a drilled hole in the back of the counter electrode. Lastly, the hole was sealed using a hot-melt ionomer film and a cover glass. Incident light-to-electricity conversion efficiency values were measured using a modified light source, 450 W Xe lamp (Oriel, 6266), an Oriel 81088 Air Mass 1.5 Global Filter and a digital source meter purchased from Keithley Instruments Inc. The incident light intensity was calibrated using a standard solar cell composed of a crystalline silicon solar cell and an IR cutoff filter (Schott, KG-5), giving the photoresponse range of an amorphous silicon solar cell.

All solid-state DSSCs experimental section

The solid-state DSSCs are prepared using a modification of a literature report.18 Briefly, an 80 nm-thick compact layer of TiO2 was deposited on the F-doped SnO2 (FTO, Tech 8 grade) by spray pyrolysis at 450 Â°C. After that, TiO2 (âˆ¼20 nm in size) paste was spin coated on the compact-layered TiO2 and sintered at 500 Â°C for 30 min, forming the mesoporous TiO2 film, the thickness of which varied from 1.0 Î¼m to 3.0 Î¼m in an aim to maximize the device performance. Subsequently, the sensitizers were applied to the as-prepared TiO2 film by a dipping method in 0.3 mM dye solution for 10 h, followed by spin coating with hole-transporting materials (a mixture composed of spiro-OMeTAD, tert-butylpyridine, and Li(CF3SO2)2N in chlorobenzene). Finally, the counter electrodes, Ag, were thermally evaporated onto the top of the spiro-OMeTAD blend. Note that no antireflective coating was applied in this application.

Results and discussion

The syntheses leading to the isolation of LJB series of organic dyes involve the multi-steps protocols which are depicted in Scheme 2. The first step is the coupling of 4-bromo- or 4-chloro-benzaldehyde with either 3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene (EDOT) substituted dioxaborolane or tributylstannane reagent using Suzuki or Stille cross coupling, respectively. We have also tested the reaction of EDOT substituted dioxaborolane with 4-chloro-3-fluorobenzaldehyde, but the product yield was not as good as the reaction employing dioxaborolane. The remainder of the preparation follows the known chemistry protocol of organic pushâ€“pull dyes. The resulting aldehydes (1a, 1 and 1b) was then reacted with N-bromosuccinimide (NBS) to afford the bromide substituted EDOT derivatives (2a, 2 and 2b), which then reacted with triphenylamine substituted dioxaborolane under Suzuki coupling to yield the amine functionalized aldehydes (3a, 3 and 3b). The condensation between 3a, 3 and 3b with cyanoacrylic acid in the presence of ammonium acetate afforded the final organic dyes LJB-Fo, LJB-H, and LJB-Fm, respectively.

Relative to LJ1, the new LJB series dyes achieved the original proposal of gaining a higher extinction coefficient by incorporating extended Ï€-conjugation into the frameworks of the parent structure. Prior to the fabrication of DSSCs, cyclic voltammetry was performed to ensure that their LUMOs are suited for injecting electrons into the conduction band of TiO2 and whether their HOMO matches the energy level of the redox electrolyte. In a practical manner, Eox of the dyes adsorbed on a 6 Î¼m thick TiO2 nanoporous thin film on FTO glass was measured (see Table 1). Their LUMO levels are also sufficiently more negative than the conduction band edge of the TiO2 electrode (ca. âˆ’0.5 V vs. NHE at pH 7).21 Especially for LBJ-Fm, the electron withdrawing fluorine substituent is located at the meta-position with respect to the cyanoacrylic acid group, leading to much stabilization of the Ï€* orbital and hence a smaller Ï€â€“Ï€* energy gap, consistent with the UV-vis absorption spectrum measurement.

For rationalization of the fluorine substitution effect, on the one hand, due to the higher TiO2 surface coverage, the electron recombination occurring in the proximity to photoanodes is reduced (cf.LJB-H), resulting in the reduction of dark current and hence an increase of Voc.25 However, LJB-Fm shows a slightly higher dye uptake than that of LJB-H, but gives a lower Voc value instead, casting doubt on this interpretation. Alternatively, the result may point to the dipole-induced changes of the conduction band energy level, Ecb, of TiO2 with respect to the redox potential of the electrolyte (Eredox). It has been reported that the electronic properties such as band bending, electron affinity, and work function, of semiconducting materials could be fine-tuned by adsorption of organic molecules with different dipole moments.26 Recently, this phenomenon has been successfully introduced to interpret electrochemical behavior in a DSSC system.27 More quantitatively, the Carâ€“Parrinello method has been applied to describe the characteristic appearance of the intrinsic dipole moment of dyes adsorbed onto TiO2 surfaces such as (TiO2)38 clusters by means of geometry optimization.28 Unfortunately, the computation methodology is currently unavailable in our laboratories. As for a qualitative approach, due to the resonance effect between ortho-fluorine and cyanoacrylic acid, the dipole moment (negative site) of the anchored LJB-Fo seems to point toward the TiO2 surface and hence shifts Ecb in a direction that increases the energy difference between Ecb and Eredox. On the other hand, the inductive effect via the meta-fluorine substitution in LJB-Fm may lead to a dipole moment pointing away (cf.LJB-Fo) from the TiO2 surface, and hence stabilizes Ecb upon anchoring onto the TiO2 surface.

Conclusion

In conclusion, we reported herein a new series of organic dyes, namely LJB-Fo, LJB-H and LJB-Fm, that contain TPA and cyanoacrylic acid as electron donor and acceptor, respectively, together with one phenylene (or fluorophenylene) unit as the spacer. Relative to the parent LJ1 dye, the LJB series of dyes achieve the goal of gaining greater absorptivity via incorporating an extra phenylene into the Ï€-framework. This, together with the extension of the absorption onset toward the longer wavelength region, leads to the enhancement of light-harvesting capabilities that allows us to reduce the thickness of the TiO2 layer as well as to suppress dark current and hence increase Voc. Upon optimization, LJB-Fo has attained 8.22% efficiency reproducibly along with an acetonitrile based volatile electrolyte. Based on the solid-state DSSC architecture, the LJB-Fo sensitizer gives Jsc = 7.75 mA cmâˆ’2, Voc = 950 mV, and FF = 0.62, corresponding to an overall Î· = 4.53%. The results also manifest the importance of fluorine substitution on overall device performances, which can be classified into inductive versus resonance effects that influence the Dâ€“A strength as well as electronic coupling with nanostructured TiO2 to different extents. Future extension to multiple fluoro substitution is feasible due to its ubiquitous applications in OLEDs and artificial amino acid syntheses. We thus believe that fluorine chemistry may pave a new avenue en route to photovoltaic applications.
