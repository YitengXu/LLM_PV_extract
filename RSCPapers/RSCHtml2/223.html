Abstract

With the rapid development of electric vehicles and mobile technologies, there is a high demand for electrochemical energy storage devices and electrochemical energy conversion devices. Devices meeting these needs include metal-ion batteries (MIBs), supercapacitors (SCs), electrochromic devices (ECDs), and multifunctional devices such as electrochromic batteries and supercapatteries. Currently, the goal has been the enhancement of operational parameters and physical properties that results in a higher performance of these devices. In the case of batteries, SCs, and supercapatteries, scientists seek to improve the equilibrium voltage, energy density, power, capacitance, and charge rate. In the case of ECDs, the focus is on improvement of the optical modulation and coloration efficiency. However, synthesis and characterization of new materials, or of materials with optimized properties, is time consuming and highly expensive. Computational simulation of materials can expedite the experimental endeavor by modelling novel atomic structures and predicting device performance. This is possible using ab initio theories and applying physical principles that allow us to understand the underlying mechanisms governing the behavior of materials in these devices. Taking as a point of departure density functional theory (DFT), in this review, we discuss the first principles methods used for the computation of physical properties and performance parameters of electrochemical energy storage devices. A wide coverage of DFT is given, dealing with the strengths and weaknesses of the most popular functionals used in the field of electrochemical energy storage. With these tools, ab initio methods for the computation of basic properties such as effective mass, mobility, optical band gap, transmissivity, conductivity (ionic and electronic), and criteria for structure stability (cohesive energy, formation energy, adsorption energy, and phonon frequency) are addressed. We also highlight the first principles techniques for the calculation of performance parameters in MIBs, SCs, and ECDs.

1 Introduction

The ever-growing demand for alternative sources of clean and carbon-free energy compels us to rethink how we address technological issues. New advanced and environmentally friendly materials are required to tackle the energy consumption that the world is facing today and the future of energy calls for the development of more sophisticated energy storage technologies. Electrochemical energy storage devices (EESDs) and electrochemical energy conversion devices (EECDs) have been developed as the most viable solution to the energy crisis; these include metal-ion batteries (MIBs), microbatteries (MBs), supercapacitors (SCs), electrochromic devices (ECDs), and others.1–10

The progress in these technologies depends not only on improving the physical properties and operational parameters of current materials, but also on designing novel materials with superior properties. The experimental discovery of new materials is laborious, expensive, and demanding in terms of infrastructure and laboratory resources. From this perspective, simulation and computational tools are highly attractive because computational modelling can boost the discovery process and help to unveil physical mechanisms that are unreachable with present technology.

Ab initio-based methods have gained considerable attention from material scientists in the last two decades. Scientists acknowledge the importance of applying fundamental theories to the understanding of phenomena taking place in modern electronic technology. In many cases, experimental information is available but an insight into the underlying mechanism of, for instance, ionic diffusivity in electrodes for batteries, is lacking. This is due to experimental limitations since it is quite difficult to track ion trajectories in materials with current technology. Moreover, when materials have not been synthesized, simulation and models from first principles represent an excellent tool to predict new structures and assess physical properties that may catalyze the experimental search for these materials; resulting in great progress in the field. Both experiment and computational modelling are supplementary to the scientific endeavor.

Many physical properties of materials, EESDs, and EECDs can be now routinely and precisely predicted using ab initio-based techniques; including ground state energies, formation energies, cohesive energies, activation and adsorption energies, equilibrium voltages, optical properties, electron mobility, ionic diffusion, specific capacity, and energy densities.11–14 On the other hand, an ever-increasing number of scientists apply artificial intelligence and machine learning techniques for the prediction of new crystal structures as potential candidates for electrodes and electrolytes in EESDs.15 Moreover, high-throughput computational studies require a body of methodologies and physical criteria that can help to discriminate among the myriad of possible candidate materials from large databases.16,17

It is worth mentioning that, in the last two decades, material scientists have been working on the development of multifunctional devices (MFDs) that combine two or more technologies in one single unit, with the aim of optimizing the use of materials, reducing cost, and production time. Among the MFDs that relate ECDs, MIBs, and SCs, we can count: electrochromic batteries, photoelectrochromic devices, electrochromic supercapacitors, and supercapatteries.7,18–21 All these technologies are currently under intense research and undergoing a great expansion. Hence, devices capable of manipulating and storing energy in one single unit are highly attractive. Under this scenario, it is worth devoting a review dealing, in a rigorous and concise form, with ab initio methodologies for the calculation of the most essential physical properties and performance parameters of contemporary EESDs and EECDs.

This review highlights some of the most fascinating advances made in the computation of physical properties and performance parameters in the field of electrochemical energy storage and conversion. As a first step, we provide an overview of density functional theory (DFT) which is the basis of ab initio methodologies. We then move on to review the theory for the calculation of the physical properties of crystalline materials that form the ground for the prediction of operational parameters in systems with similar features; including lithium-ion batteries (LIBs), ECDs, and SCs. In this way, the methods will be provided for those interested in either gaining insight into the electronic/ionic dynamics of existing materials and for those working on the design of new ones. Due to their close similarity in construction, components, and performance, we restrict ourselves to discuss the physical foundations within the context of LIBs, SCs, and ECDs which has been the basis for MFDs.

To achieve our goal, this review is organized as follows. In Section 2 we review the foundations of the DFT. Basics of the theory are given and a discussion of the most common exchange–correlation functionals is presented. The physical properties such as the group velocity, effective mass, mobility and optical band gap are treated in Section 3. Section 4 is devoted to discuss criteria for structural stability both at zero temperature and arbitrary temperatures. These criteria entail the determination of the cohesive energy, the energy of formation, the adsorption energy for pure and impure systems with different types of point defects (interstitial, substitutional, and charged supercells), as well as phonon frequencies. The basic theory for the calculation of the ionic diffusivity in electrodes and the ionic conductivity is addressed in Section 5. The theory not only applies to electrodes but also to solid state electrolytes. The principles for the calculation of the theoretical voltage, capacity, and energy density in LIBs is given in Section 6. Thermal corrections due to lattice vibrations are also taken into consideration. Sections 7 and 8 deal with the working principles of ECDs and SCs, respectively; and the computational techniques for the determination of performance parameters such as optical modulation, coloration efficiency, capacitance, capacity, energy density, and peak power are developed here. In Section 9, the importance of machine learning in the determination of the physical properties of materials is highlighted. Finally, a summary and pertinent remarks are given in Section 10.

2 Density functional theory

2.1 Overview

The calculation of physical properties from first principles of condensed matter systems has been quite challenging due to the large number of particles involved in the Hamiltonian. The first ab initio quantum method for evaluating the physical properties of metals, came from Arnold Sommerfeld around 192822 when he assumed an electron moving freely in a periodic crystal. Although a simple model, Sommerfeld paved the way for more advanced approaches. Quantum mechanics was immediately applied to the study of many electron systems and soon physicists realized that the problem turned out to be intractable under traditional methods. One of the first successful methods ever developed to deal with the many-body problem for the computation of the ground state energy of solids was the Hartree–Fock approach (still widely used today in chemistry).23,24 As the field evolved, several alternatives and more exact theories were put forward; among these, today we can count the DFT,25,26 the GW method,27 Monte Carlo methods,28,29 and time dependent DFT (TDDFT).30 Due to its relative simplicity, power prediction, and low computational cost, DFT has gained large popularity among chemists, condensed matter physicists, material engineers, and material scientists. In the following we shall deal with the elements of this theory.

2.2 The Kohn–Sham method

Kohn–Sham DFT is a powerful tool for the prediction of the ground-state properties of condensed matter systems. Yet, as any other theory, DFT contains assumptions that delimit its scope and one should be aware of its strengths and limitations. For instance, the electron density [eqn (4)] and the total energy [eqn (5)] correspond to the ground state which implies that no excited states are taken into account in the calculation. Also the temperature of the system is assumed to be 0 K and no external stresses are assumed to be applied. Under this warning, care should be exercised in applying the theory to interpreting spectral results, especially those related to radiation spectra where excitations dominate the electromagnetic phenomena. To treat electronic excited states that result from radiation-matter interactions, more sophisticated approaches have been developed. The TDDFT30–33 and the many-body perturbation theory, through the Bethe–Salpeter equation (BSE),34–37 deal with the time-dependent nature of electromagnetic radiation and are more suitable to treat light phenomena such as nonlinear optics, photoluminescence, and absorption and emission of light (in different intensity regimes). Nowadays, these methods are routinely applied and are available in several noncommercial codes.38–42 The catch is that they are computationally expensive and, therefore, large computational resources are required.

To end this subsection, it is worth mentioning that there are plenty of computational packages that solve the KSE and calculate several physical magnitudes, the differences among such packages mainly reside on the basis set used in each case to solve the KSE and on the type of electronic system under study. For instance, in the case of periodic systems, the codes ABINIT,43 VASP,44 QUANTUM EXPRESSO,45 CASTEP,46,47 and SIESTA,48 use pseudopotentials along with plane waves or atomic orbitals to solve the KSE. The pseudopotential approach approximates the core electron potential and explicitly deals with valence electrons. On the other hand, WIEN2K49 and RSPT50 are full potential codes that take into account both core and valence electrons without any approximation. Linearized augmented plane waves plus localized orbitals (LAPW + lo) and linearized muffin-tin orbitals (LMTO) are used as basis sets, respectively. Other codes, such as Gaussian,51 use Gaussian orbitals as basis set and are more suitable to treat atoms, molecules, clusters, and nanoparticles, which are usually non-periodic systems. A study conducted in 2016 on the performance of 15 DFT codes using 40 different basis sets, showed that most codes give similar results for the Perdew–Burke–Enrzerhof equation of state applied to 71 elemental crystals; only older methods showed less precision.52

2.3 Exchange–correlation functionals

In principle, all quantities in the KSE can be known save for Exc, which is undetermined. Thus, the KSE can only be solved provided that an explicit form for Exc is given. The first approximation to the exchange–correlation energy, the local density approximation (LDA), and its spin-dependent version, the local spin density approximation (LSDA),53,54 regards the electron density as nearly a constant all over the material. In the early days of DFT, the LDA ansatz represented a step forward towards the understanding of the physical properties in metals. However, as it is evident, the LDA is only applicable to systems with slowly-varying densities and, therefore, it is of no utility for most physical systems such as atoms, molecules and non-metallic materials. To cover these systems, soon, new families of exchange–correlation functionals were worked out at the end of the 20th century and early in the 21st century. Another family of functionals, dubbed the generalized gradient approximation (GGA), improved on the LDA by considering both the charge density and its gradient. The most popular GGA functional used today for the calculation of physical properties in periodic materials is the Perdew–Burke–Enrzerhof (PBE) functional,55 although revised versions of this, including the RevPBE, RPBE, PW91, and PBESol, have also been widely applied.56–58 Other improvements in DFT have led to meta-generalized-gradient approximations (MGGA), with the Tao-Perdew–Staroverov–Scuseria (TPSS) functional as one of the most representative exchange–correlation functionals in this family.59 The selection of functional from the myriad of options strongly relies on the type of system and the property under study. Physical properties of molecules and surface energies of solids are well described with the TPSS functional,60 while equilibrium properties of solids in bulk give better results with the functionals from the GGA family.61

The PBE functional and its revised versions have been quite successful in computing many equilibrium properties such as lattice parameters, formation energies, redox energies, binding energies, phonon frequencies, bulk moduli, ferroelectricity, and magnetism. Yet, despite their success, these functionals fail to predict excitonic states, the correct band gaps in most semiconductors, and the right electron localization in strongly correlated materials that possess partially filled d or f orbitals.60,62 Transition metal oxides, rear earth compounds, molecular systems, and many insulators and semiconductors fall within this category and are regularly used in electrodes and electrolytes for EESDs and MFDs.

In part, the failure to predict the right band gaps in these systems, comes from the discontinuity occurring in eqn (3) derived by the use of orbital-independent potentials.63,64 This scenario motivated the search for more sophisticated functionals resulting in the generation of more families. A comprehensive list of functionals can reach several hundreds and it is impossible to review them all here.65 Some functionals have been designed to meet certain requirements in some systems. For example, in atoms and molecules, the electronic density is highly inhomogeneous and varies drastically over space, so functionals must meet this type of charge distribution. On the other hand, in EESDs, EECDs, and MFDs, the main components are periodic solids. This scenario naturally narrows the number of functionals that can be used to deal with this kind of systems. From this limited set of functionals, some have been designed to improve band gaps, and others were developed to describe the localized character of d and f electrons in some transition metal oxides and rare earth compounds, where strong electronic correlations play a leading role. We shall, therefore, discuss the most popular and successful functionals applied to the computation of physical properties in the field of electrochemical energy storage. These functionals include hybrid functionals (hfs), with emphasis on the HSE06 hf; the LDA+U (collectively called DFT+U); and the modified Becke–Johnson (mBJ) potential. Other common functionals used in battery design are: the SCAN from the meta-GGA family, the PW6B95, B87-1, B3LYP-D3, and DSD-PBEP86 for the computation of atomization energies and the mPW2-PLYP-D, DSD-PBE86, PW6B95, and PBEQIDH for the computation of relative energies in solids. When using functionals, scientists should be aware of their merits and shortcomings. Therefore, the study of the theory of each functional beforehand is crucial for its utilization. Due to space limitations and their wide applicability in EESDs and EECDs, we shall briefly reviewed the most significant features of the hybrid functionals with their variants, the DFT+U methodology, and the mBJ potential; the rest are left for the interested reader.13

2.4.1 Optimized hybrid functionals. The HSE06 functional represents a substantial progress in computing the electronic properties and band gaps of many semiconducting systems, however, this functional underestimates band gaps in insulators. Even for some medium gap systems (1.2 eV to 4.9 eV) with defect levels, a series of significant discrepancies have been reported.71,72 For instance, in the case of GaAs and GaN, the HSE06 predicts a band gap of 1.2 eV and 3.2 eV, against the experimental values of 1.6 eV and 3.6 eV, respectively.73,74 This state of affairs calls for improvements in the level of accuracy of the hfs.

More recently, a new family of optimized hfs (ohfs), dubbed HSE(a,ω) functionals, has emerged.75,76 In ohfs, both the mixing and the screening parameters are varied. In the HSE06 functional, these parameters are fixed to 0.25 and 0.20, respectively. These values were tuned to better fit the experimental data but the values chosen had no physical justification. However, it was later shown that the mixing parameter is strongly related to the dielectric function of the material which implies that its value is material-dependent.77–79 The physical foundation lies in the condition known as the generalized Koopman' theorem which, for the addition of an electron to the system, can be mathematically stated as:

 
εKS − εc = ΔKS = ΔSCF = εN+1 − εN − εc(8)

The parameters chosen in the HSE(a,ω) functional must reproduce the experimental band gap and obey the generalized koopman' theorem which implies that ΔKS − ΔSCF ≈ 0. 2.5 DFT+U method

2.5.1 Method description. Although hfs and ohfs make accurate prediction of band gaps, calculations are computationally expensive and time consuming. Moreover, hfs do not deal with strong electron correlations, usually found in many transition metal oxides, which are standard materials in electrodes of EESDs. Dynamical mean field theory (DMFT) is the ideal theory to deal with this type of interactions,80,81 however, it is as expensive as hfs, so, other less time consuming approaches are desirable. One of these is the celebrated LDA+U method, generally known as DFT+U, which was put forward by Anisimov et al. in the early 1990s.82–85 The method was developed to account for transition metals oxides that tend to behave like Mott insulators, i.e., LDA/GGA calculations underestimate band gaps of these materials and in most cases predict them to have metallic character. The problem is strongly linked with the Hartree integral that includes an unphysical repulsive interaction between the electron and itself. Along with the inaccuracy of the exchange–correlation functional, a systematic error in the total energy emerges. This error is carried along with the calculations and for many systems, such as some transition metal oxides, the error becomes significant.

In an attempt to alleviate this problem, Anisimov et al. added a Hubbard term U (borrowed from the Hubbard model) that accounts for correlation effects and corrects for electron self-interaction. This is done by introducing orbital-dependent potentials that are applied to localized electrons in partially filled d or f shells in transition metal and rare earth compounds, respectively. The effect of this potential is to separate the localized orbitals relative to each other creating two Hubbard bands:86 the lower and the upper bands, with U determining the size of the splitting (see Fig. 2). 2.5.2 Computing U. So far there are two ways of computing U. The first one is a fitting procedure that relies on other results. These results can emerge from two sources: (a) experimental, such as measurements of lattice parameters, band gaps, and energy of formation; or (b) theoretical, such as calculations of these same properties–parameters from other ab initio calculations (e.g. GW, Monte Carlo, DMFT, etc.). Regardless of whether the results have an experimental or theoretical origin, the value of U is varied until the calculations match the results from the other observations.89–92 However, when neither experimental nor theoretical data are available, especially during the design of new materials with no experimental data at hand, e.g., in the design of novel materials applied to LIBs, SCs, ECDs, and other MFDs,93,94 the fitting procedure is no longer useful and one must resort to ab initio resources for the self-consistent computation of U. This leads us to the second way.

The problem of computing U from first principles dates back to the development of the DFT+U approach. So far, several approaches of ab initio methods has been put forward for the determination of U. Among these, we can enlist the constrained DFT (cDFT) approach,82,85,94,95 HF-based methods,96–98 the constrained random phase approximation (cRPA),99–101 and the density functional perturbation theory (DFPT).102,103 The methods differ on theoretical considerations, basis sets, and computational code used. Although, in principle, the methods should not depend on the basis set, it is a fact that computational codes solving the KSE rest on a particular basis set and, therefore, the methodology must be adapted to that specific code. In the literature of EESDs and EECDs the most common methods used for the computation of operational parameters are the cDFT and the DFPT. 2.7 Energy dispersion corrections

For systems where van der Waals interactions are significant, DFT alone is not designed to deal with weak interactions. These systems include adsorption of molecules/atoms on surfaces, layered compounds, heterostructures, interfaces, and two-dimensional systems (graphene, silicene, borophene, phosphorene, etc.); most of these are usually found in electrodes for SCs, MIBs, MBs, ECDs, and MFDs.4,5,113,114 In such systems, London dispersion forces can play an important role and neglecting these interactions could be detrimental for the accuracy in the prediction of a physical property. 3.4 Band gap

The concept of band gap in solid state physics is ubiquitous and routinely used. Yet, for our discussion, it is important to make a clear distinction among the band gaps found in this field because their quantitative similarity causes a lot of confusion. One has to distinguish three band gaps, namely: the electronic band gap (EBG), also named intrinsic or fundamental band gap, the optical band gap (OBG), and the Kohn–Sham band gap (KSBG); denoted with the symbols Eg, Egop, and EgKS, respectively. The first gap is defined as Eg = * − *, where * is the first ionization potential and * the first electron affinity.64 This gap can be experimentally determined, for instance, from X-ray absorption and emission spectra (XAS and XES) or from angular resolved photoemission and inverse photoemission spectra. The OBG is related to the optical absorption edge and is defined as Egop = Elow − E0, with Elow denoting the energy of the lowest excited many-particle state and E0 the ground-state energy for the optimized lattice. This gap is commonly determined from optical measurements of the absorption spectrum. Therefore, the OBG is the result of an excitonic state. In many semiconductors with low impurity concentrations Eg ≈ Egop, this similarity may lead to confusion between these gaps. In general, however, these magnitudes might differ considerably and one must be aware of it. On the other hand, the KSBG is defined as EgKS = εc − εv, where εc and εv are the energies of the CBM and the VBM, respectively. From ab initio calculations, this gap has been identified with the fundamental gap, although it is clear that the size of the KSBG depends on the choice of Exc. Usually, LDA, GGA, and MGGA functionals underestimate (sometimes considerably) the size of the EBG, leading to wrong physical interpretations of the electronic structure of a crystal. The formal connection between the KSBG and the EBG is established in the fact that * and * of an N-electron system can be associated with the Nth and (N + 1)th Kohn–Sham eigenvalues (εN and εN+1), i.e., * ≈ εv and * ≈ εc. These considerations lead to the expression:
 
Eg = *− * = εN+1 − εN + Δxc,(38)
where Δxc is the magnitude of the derivative discontinuity introduced by the use of Exc (assuming a system with integer particle number). From here, one realizes that, in general, Eg ≠ EgKS because the Δxc is not incorporated, per se, in DFT calculations.

We should keep in mind that the KSBG is derived from DFT which deals with eigenvalues for the ground state, whereas the OBG results from excited states. For this reason, one should resort to more advanced methodologies implemented in perturbative methods, such as the Bether–Salpeter equation, or through TDDFT.

4 Structural stability

The structural stability of a crystalline material can be assessed from the calculation of cohesive energy, enthalpy of formation, energy of formation, and phonon band structure. Fortunately, under some physical considerations, these properties can be reckoned from ab initio calculations. In this section we review the methodologies used for their computation.

4.2 Energy of formation: general aspects

5 Ionic diffusion

Ionic mobility, in both the electrolyte and the electrodes, determines, in the case of batteries and SCs, the charge/discharge rate whereas in ECDs, the transition time (tt) from the colored state to the bleached state. The time required for a full charge in batteries and SCs is crucial for the applicability of the device in portable electronic gadgets, energy storage systems, and electric vehicles. On the other hand, the discharge time defines the quantity of power that can be delivered to the load. Similarly, a shortest tt is desirable for the usability of ECDs. Thus, the accurate knowledge of the ionic dynamics is beneficial in these devices.

The ionic mobility in electrodes is limited by either the electronic conductivity (σe) or the ionic conductivity (σi). Electronic conductivity depends strongly on electron–electron scattering and electron–phonon scattering. It also depends on temperature and other factors: defects, impurity species, and doping levels. Ionic conductivity can be a function of temperature, crystal structure, and defects (vacancies, interstitials, direct exchange).159 In EESDs, the ionic mobility determines the rate capability of these devices. Both conductivities can be calculated from first principles theories. In Section 3.3 we outlined the description for the computation of the electronic conductivity from DFT,133 here, we focus on the calculations of the ionic conductivity and ionic diffusion from ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulations.160,161 Although there are other methods for the computation of ionic diffusion in solids, e.g., the nudge elastic band (NEB) method162 or the eigenvector-following method,163 AIMD can reveal new conduction mechanisms compared to NEB and its range of applicability extends to the nondilute regime, where the NEB method cannot be applied. Due to the complexity of the theory and the limited space available in this review, we only highlight its most relevant aspects. More details can be found in the corresponding literature.

AIMD uses the atomic forces from DFT to track the trajectories of ions in the system according to classical mechanics. In Section 2, we discussed the basis for the computation of the electronic structure calculation under DFT from which the atomic forces can be obtained after the optimization of the unit cell. 6 Ab initio calculations of performance parameters for Li-ion batteries

Batteries can be classified according to the number of charge cycles before disposal. Batteries that tolerate only one charge cycle are termed primary batteries, otherwise are called secondary or rechargeable batteries. The latter are of the most important for mobile technology including electric vehicles, laptops, smartphones, and tablets, just to mention a few. Over the last two decades LIBs have dominated the market due to its high charge capacity, high volumetric energy density, and mass energy density. It is thus worth discussing the working principles of these kind of EESDs.

6.1 Working principles of LIBs

A LIB is, in essence, an electrochemical Galvanic cell mainly composed of three elements: a cathode and an anode, both in contact with an electrolyte usually in liquid solution; although recently great efforts have been put in developing solid state electrolytes (see Fig. 6). The anode is commonly made up of carbon polymorphs such as graphite or graphene and the most advanced LIBs used Si–C anodes. The cathode is made up of layered materials of TMOs with lithium additions such as LiMxO2 (M = Co, Ni, Mn) and LiFe1−xMxPO4. The electrolyte contains Li salts dissolved in organic solvents (e.g. ethylene carbonate or propylene carbonate) and works as an ionic medium.174 During discharge, electrons flow through the external circuit from the anode to the cathode, while Li+ ions flow through the electrolyte in the same direction as the electrons. During this process the anode is reduced and the cathode is oxidized. A separator between the electrodes is placed to block the passage of electrons through the electrolyte. During charge, this process is reversed.

7 Ab initio calculations of performance parameters for electrochromic devices

Electrochromism is the effect of changing the optical properties of a material by intercalation/deintercalation of ions driven by an electric field. Electrochromic materials used in ECDs can be constituted of viologens, conducting polymers, metal coordination complexes, or TMOs.6,180,181 Among these, the latter group enjoys more popularity due to its low cost and high electrochromic performance. Understanding how absorption occurs in TMOs during intercalation/deintercalation could be the key to explain the electrochromic effect. Within the context of band theory, it is believed that electrons injected into the crystalline lattice of electrochromic electrodes fill new levels at the bottom of the conduction band and optical absorption can be seen as electron transitions between the occupied states in the valence band and the unoccupied states in the conduction band.182 The number of electrons injected(extracted) is modulated by the number of ions inserted(extracted) into(from) the lattice. Absorption in turn is related to transmissivity which is the variable of interest in these devices. In the following subsection we discuss how to compute the transmissivity from ab initio methods.

9 Machine learning applied to materials science

In the previous sections, we have presented the theoretical foundations for the ab initio computation of the most relevant physical properties and operational parameters related to EESDs, EECDs, and MFDs. The theory not only sets the foundations for a deep understanding of the physico-chemical process taking place in these systems but also defines criteria for the selection of materials with special properties suitable for technological applications. During the design of new materials, large databases are scrutinized and the computation of the physical properties turns out to be time consuming, demanding high computational resources. These issues can be lessened to some degree with the help of new computational tools such as machine learning.

Machine learning (ML) is a rapidly expanding subfield of artificial intelligence that aims at developing computational algorithms capable of learning without human intervention. ML has been applied to chemistry, materials engineering, condensed matter physics, and materials science for the prediction of crystal structures and physical properties of materials.15 Due to the complexity of this topic, it is impossible to treat it here, however, we feel it is worth reviewing a few examples to highlight its importance in these fields.

In 2016, De Jong et al. and Calfa et al. applied ML to provide a better insight into the mechanical properties of polycrystalline materials, in particular, in the study of the elastic properties (the bulk modulus B0 and the shear modulus G) of large data sets of polycrystalline inorganic compounds.209,210 Similarly, ML has been used in the prediction of the band gap and Fermi energies in metal oxides.210 Redox potentials, and electronic properties in molecular electrodes for Li-ion batteries have also been computed using DFT ML algorithms.211 In addition, researchers have applied ML to predict the crystal structure and properties of binary compounds,212 to reproduce the HOMO and LUMO for organic solar cells,213 and to have a better insight into the thermodynamic parameters such as adsorption energy and activation energy in heterogeneous catalytic reactions.214,215

Beyond these applications, scientists have employed ML at a different level; particularly, in order to improve ab initio computational methods. ML has been used to improve the DFT methodology, to optimize the exchange–correlation functionals in DFT calculations,216,217 to bypass the KSE,218 to derive new force fields in dynamic molecular simulations,166,167 and to create adaptive basis sets for large scale DFT simulations.219 Nowadays the use of ML is ubiquitous in materials science and its contribution in the search for materials with novel properties is invaluable.

10 Conclusions

In this review, we have presented, from first principles, the theoretical basis for the determination of physical properties and performance parameters of EESDs and EECDs. The application of this knowledge can be further extended to the study of MFDs which combine two or more technologies. Due to its prominent place in condensed matter physics, chemistry, and materials science, we outlined the foundations of DFT and of the most popular exchange correlation functionals. Among these, we reviewed the hybrid functionals, the DFT+U method, the mBJ potential, and the energy dispersion corrections for weak interacting systems. Advantages and drawbacks in each case were also discussed with the aim of guiding scientists in the selection of the best functional for each type of system. On the other hand, the most relevant physical properties of materials were also addressed. We highlighted the methodologies for the calculation of the effective mass, electron mobility, electronic conductivity, and relaxation time; characteristics crucial for the understanding of electron dynamics in materials. The ambiguities in the meaning of the different band gaps (i.e., EGB, OBG, and KSBG) were appropriately dispelled and mathematical expressions, along with thermal corrections, were offered to obtain the OBG of semiconductors from DFT calculations.

The criteria for the structural stability of periodic solids, quite useful for the design of new atomic structures and applicable in high-throughput computations, were widely covered. Chemical and thermodynamical arguments were offered for the calculation of cohesion energy, formation energy, and phonon frequencies. Because of its relevance, the energy of formation of transition metal oxides, with several point defects, was reviewed in detail. We discussed the basis for ionic diffusivity and ionic conductivity starting from AIMD simulations. With all these tools, the ab initio methodologies for the calculations of performance parameters of LIBs, ECDs, and SCs were established. We also outlined the methodology for the calculation of the transmissivity from first principles, which is a key factor for the prediction of operational parameters in ECDs and MFDs based on the electrochromic effect.

In summary, we have exposed the impact and the importance of ab initio methods in structure design, property prediction, and computation of performance parameters of EESDs, EECDs, and MFDs. Ab initio methodologies have become essential to the discovery of novel materials and to the optimization of current ones. The ever-growing interplay between theory and experiment is pivotal to meeting the current and future needs of electrochemical energy storage and conversion.
