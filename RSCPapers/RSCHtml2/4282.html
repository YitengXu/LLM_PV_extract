Abstract

Alkyne-containing organic semiconductors are once again becoming the subject of intense research focus, and recent advances have significantly enhanced their performance in optoelectronics. This Perspective focuses on the results achieved in organic solar cells and offers a critical insight into the architecture-properties-device response of these materials; emphasis is also given to studies investigating the effect of substituting single and/or olefinic Câ€“C bonds for alkyne linkages on photovoltaic properties. In addition, this Perspective discusses the challenges and opportunities in advancing the synthetic strategies to develop Ï€-conjugated alkynes.

1. Introduction

Investigations centered around the use of alkyne bonds to create Ï€-conjugated semiconductors for organic (opto)electronics have been carried out in the past two decades. Recent years, however, have seen a renewed interest in such architectures, either polymers or small molecules, most likely spurred by the advances in the field of graphyne structures.1â€“4

When introduced in a semiconductor core, the sp-hybrid carbonâ€“carbon triple bond may impart peculiar properties to the resulting material.5â€“7 For example, alkyne bonds do not present the Z/E isomerism issue, which may end in multiple isomers that may be difficult to separate. Moreover, alkyne bonds can be employed in the synthesis of shape-persistent architectures, due to their rigid and linear geometry characteristics. Also note that the rigidity provided to the (macro)molecular core by introducing the triple bond reduces the amplitude of thermal motions which are supposed to lower the mobility in organic semiconductors.8

The incorporation of alkyne linkages offers a viable approach to minimize steric repulsion between adjacent aromatic units, inducing planarity, enhancing Ï€-conjugation along the backbone and, ultimately, charge carrier mobility.9,10 Next, it has been demonstrated that, when used appropriately, the introduction of an alkyne linkage is an important tool to fine-tune the energetics of organic semiconductors. For example, the lowest occupied molecular orbital (LUMO) levels may be lowered by the presence of electron-withdrawing triple bond spacers.11,12 Theoretical calculations by Schmidt et al.13 support that side groups containing triple bonds may lead to an enhanced ability to accommodate the negative charge, and, therefore, to promising electron-accepting systems.

In addition, the oxidation potential might be increased by the presence of electron-withdrawing triple bond spacers, which stabilizes the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO).14 Notably, substituting acenes with triple bonds containing functional groups enables their protection from oxidative degradation.15

In this Perspective we give an overview of the most prominent triple-bond containing semiconductors, and their performance in organic bulk heterojunction (BHJ) solar cells. A schematic picture of typical BHJ device structures and their related working principles is depicted in Fig. 1. Detailed discussions on materials, device functioning and processing can be found in several dedicated review articles.16â€“19

The conventional device (A) typically consists of a transparent anode (e.g. indium tin oxide (ITO)-coated glass), an active layer, and a top metal cathode. In many cases, a thin layer of hole (HTL) and/or electron (ETL) transporting materials is deposited between the active layer and the anode (and/or the cathode), to assist hole and/or electron extraction, respectively. In the inverted configuration (B), the bottom transparent electrode serves as the cathode while the top electrode serves as the anode. In a BHJ device the light photons with appropriate energy are absorbed by the donor semiconductor in the active layer to generate excitons. The excitons subsequently dissociate into free charges at the donorâ€“acceptor interface. Next, holes and electrons flow in the donor and acceptor domains, respectively, and are ultimately collected at the device electrodes. The figures-of-merit of BHJ solar cells include the power conversion efficiency (PCE), the open circuit voltage (VOC), the short circuit current (JSC), and the fill factor (FF), which are, respectively, the ratio between the output device electrical energy and the incident solar energy (Pin), the cell voltage when the current flow is zero, the current flowing through the device when there is no reverse bias, and the ratio between the maximum power output and the theoretical counterpart (i.e. VOC Ã— JSC). PCE is calculated by the following equation: PCE = FF Ã— VOC Ã— JSC/Pin.

We focus on small molecule aryleneâ€“ethynylenes (also referred to as aryl acetylenes) and poly(aryl acetylene)s, i.e. systems featuring carbonâ€“carbon triple bonds in the main backbone. These architectures have drawn much attention during the past few decades as interesting and instructive semiconductors for the fabrication of diverse electronic devices, including BHJ solar cells. We also give insight into the challenges and opportunities for their design and synthetic strategies.

Interesting studies have been reported on the implementation of metallopolyynes, specifically Pt-based polyynes, in BHJ devices.20â€“23 A survey of these systems is beyond the scope of this Perspective. However, it is noteworthy to point out here that the ever-growing risks of decreased platinum availability hamper the use of polyplatinynes for large-scale photovoltaics.24

Finally, very recently, a polymer semiconductor based on the 1,3-diyne building block has been successfully implemented for the first time in BHJ solar cells and in organic field-effect transistors (OFETs) by Facchetti, Marks and co-workers.25 The diacetylene linker, indeed, holds the promise of a rich post-functionalization chemistry, enabling the realization of platforms for two-dimensional (2D) materials. Moreover, diynes have been identified as an important functionality in e.g. chemosensors, due to their peculiar reactivity.26â€“28 Therefore, further efforts towards this direction are worth pursuing.

2. Synthetic strategies

Sonogashira cross-coupling reaction is a widely applicable tool for the construction of aryl acetylenic systems from terminal alkynes and aryl halides. It is typically promoted by the palladium catalyst and copper co-catalyst, and it is highly efficient in terms of technical viability and tolerance to numerous functional groups.5,29â€“31

Alkyne metathesis is another useful approach to the synthesis of aryl acetylenes.5,32,33 It refers to a bond exchange reaction that can redistribute alkylidyne units between a pair of acetylene derivatives through reversible cleavage and reformation of carbonâ€“carbon triple bonds. The reversible nature of alkyne metathesis enables self-correction behaviour and can provide thermodynamically stable architectures in high yield at equilibrium.

Finally, Hayâ€“Glaser coupling is one of the earliest oxidative homocoupling reactions, and it is typically used for the construction of diynes.5,34

To date, the main challenge in the synthesis of triple-bond containing semiconductors is to enable optimal device performance, through e.g. precise control over the molecular weight and polydispersity index (PDI) in the case of polymers.

No emphasis is given yet to the synthetic methodologies employed, in terms of impact on humans, animals and the environment associated with them. In this regard, the application of the principles of green chemistry for the development of cleaner synthetic approaches is essential, specifically pertaining to alternative solvents, heterogeneous catalysis, and benign reaction conditions.35

Sonogashira reactions are typically carried out in aromatic hydrocarbons or polar aprotic solvents (e.g. dimethyl formamide, dimethyl acetamide, N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone), which enable the solubilization of a wide range of substrates.

Conventional alkyne metathesis reaction solvents are toluene or chlorinated solvents. Similarly, Hayâ€“Glaser coupling is generally carried out in solvents such as dipolar aprotics (dimethyl formamide, acetonitrile), toluene, tetrahydrofuran, and pyridine.

According to relevant legislation on chemical substances, however, all these solvents pose serious risks to human health and safety and the environment.36â€“38 In addition, solvents are a major input in a synthetic process, and account for âˆ¼90% of the waste associated to the process itself.39,40

In very recent years the use of greener solvents for the synthesis of triple bond containing semiconductors via Sonogashira coupling has been explored by Vaccaro et al.41 The authors employed a biomass-derived solvent medium (i.e. 2-methyl tetrahydrofuran (2-Me-THF) and cyclopentyl methyl ether (CPME)) to perform cleaner Sonogashira reactions. This was combined with the use of recoverable heterogeneous palladium catalysts, which facilitated the purification of the semiconductor and prevented its contamination by the palladium metal.

Shortly thereafter, the same authors prioritized renewable resources and proposed g-valerolactone (GVL) to replace dimethyl formamide or N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone in a model Sonogashira coupling.42 Other solvents with green credentials have been used in model Sonogashira reactions, including dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO),43 alcohols and aqueous media,44 ionic liquids,45 and the bio-based options dimethyl-isosorbide46 and Cyrene.47

Though only a few, and mostly focusing on model reactions, these attempts demonstrate the potential of these solvents to replace their traditional counterparts in the synthesis of triple bond containing semiconductors. On the other hand, there are no examples of alkyne metathesis protocols employing â€œgreenâ€ solvents.

Greener protocols for Hayâ€“Glaser couplings include the use of solvents such as polyethyleneglycol (PEG), supercritical CO2, ionic liquids, water, or solvent-free conditions, though not targeting semiconductor synthesis.48 Further efforts should therefore be made in such a direction.

In addition, the use of heterogeneous catalysis in the synthesis of triple bond containing semiconductors should be further pursued. Sonogashira still relies essentially on palladium as a catalyst. However, in 2013, the European Union identified palladium as one of the 20 metals having a risk of market deficit, which led to high costs.49 Note also that palladium is toxic. Therefore, it is important to improve metal recycling, and avoid its loss into waste streams.

Heterogeneous systems may fulfil this requirement since they can be easily separated from the reaction mixture and reused. The easy separation also helps achieving simplified workup and/or purification steps and, therefore, reducing energy consumption and production time. Finally, anchoring palladium onto a solid material may also limit the metal contamination of the target semiconductor, which is known to negatively influence the performance of the corresponding device.50â€“52

Moving away from toxic metal catalysts by developing more environmentally benign options, through e.g. the design of new ligands, is also worth pursuing. In addition, efforts should be made to develop synthetic protocols that can tolerate aerobic conditions, and avoid, therefore, costly deoxygenation steps. Room temperature synthesis should also be preferred, to achieve energy efficient processes.

Regarding the use of the carbonâ€“carbon triple bond as a spacer in regular polymers, we report here a series of representative works where ethynylene units have been placed between different electron-rich and/or electron-poor building blocks in order to systematically investigate their effect on the resulting material properties.

A representative work was published by Cremer et al., in which they demonstrated an effective way to modify the energy levels of a polythiophene by incorporating electron-withdrawing ethynylene units into the backbone of regioregular poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT).14 The electrochemical data of the resulting polymer, poly(ethynylene-bithienylene) (P1, Scheme 1), confirmed that the triple bonds in P1 determined a lowering of the HOMO energy of 0.3 eV in comparison to P3HT. Analogously to P3HT, even if less marked, the optical absorption spectrum of P1 was significantly red-shifted passing from solution to thin film (Î”Î»max âˆ¼ 50 nm with the absorption onset in film at âˆ¼600 nm), thus highlighting its good aggregation tendency in the solid state. The resulting optical bandgap of P1, estimated from the absorption onset in film, was slightly higher (2.06 eV) compared to that of P3HT (1.90 eV).

The photovoltaic performance of P1 was tested in BHJ solar cells using [6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PC61BM) as an electron-acceptor counterpart. Optimized P1:PC61BM (1:4 wt/wt) based devices gave an open-circuit voltage (VOC), a short-circuit current density (JSC), a fill factor (FF) and a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 1.01 V, 3.1 mA cmâˆ’2, 36% and 1.13%, respectively. As expected from the lower HOMO energy level, the VOC of P1 based cells was greatly improved compared to P3HT (1.01 V vs. 0.62 V) even though the overall efficiency was lower (1.13% and 5.0% for P1 and P3HT based cells, respectively). The UV-Vis characterization of the P1:PC61BM blend revealed a blue-shifted absorption compared to the pristine P1 film, thus suggesting a poor self-organization and aggregation of the polymer when combined with PC61BM. This likely hindered the charge transport properties within the active blend which, combined with a partially limited light harvesting, might explain the lower JSC and FF values of the resulting P1 based devices.

Qin et al. carried out a comparative study on two donor polymers P2 and P3 (Scheme 1), both consisting of 5,7-dithien-2,3-di(4-dodecyloxy-phenyl)thieno[3-4-b]pyrazine (DTTP) and 2,5-bis-(n-dodecyloxy) benzene (PE) units directly linked (P3) or connected by introducing an ethynylene spacer (P2), to evaluate the effect of the ethynylene unit on the resulting material properties.53 The optical absorption spectrum of P2 was blue-shifted, in both solution and film, compared to P3, confirming the electron-withdrawing effect of the acetylenic spacer that mainly influenced the bandgap and the energy level of the HOMO. The HOMO energies of P2 and P3 were estimated to be âˆ’5.00 and âˆ’4.64 eV, respectively, while the optical bandgaps (estimated from the onset of the absorption in film) were found to be 1.55 eV for P2 and 1.36 eV for P3. An alternative and valid approach to exploit the potential of newly designed PAEâ€“PAV polymers was demonstrated by Susarova et al.66 In this work, polymers P37 and P38 (Scheme 1) were synthesized and systematically tested as donor materials in BHJ solar cells in combination with seventeen different fullerene derivatives as the acceptor counterpart. 4. Summary and outlook

Remarkable advances have been made in the area of alkyne-based organic semiconductors in terms of diversity of structures and device performance. However, compared with other semiconductors, these systems continue to show large differences in terms of structural variability, as well as in device performance.

From a synthetic point of view, efficient approaches, tolerable for different functional groups, have been developed for their synthesis, hence enabling the easy variation of their effective conjugation, and the fine-tuning of physical and electronic properties. However, the synthesis of Ï€-conjugated alkynes essentially relies on the use of harmful solvents as reaction medium (e.g. toluene or dipolar aprotics) and homogeneous catalysts (which are typically not recoverable/reusable), and an excess of reactants is often used.

Developing synthetic methods that can be scalable at reasonable low cost and that comply with the principles of green chemistry and chemical engineering to meet legislative and environmental guidelines is required to enable industrial production.105,106 Significant efforts must therefore be directed towards the mass, energy, space, and time efficient synthesis of triple-bond containing systems by using greener solvents, recyclable and/or non-toxic metal catalysts, low-temperature, and aerobic reaction conditions.

From the device point of view, incorporating the electron withdrawing ethynylene unit within the semiconductor backbone is particularly attractive since it enables high VOC. It has also been demonstrated that a low-lying LUMO may also lead to an enhanced ability to accommodate electrons, and, therefore, to promising electron-acceptors. In addition, the cylindrical electronic symmetry of the alkyne linkage makes it more accommodating than single or double bonds to steric and conformational constraints, and this has been found to preserve the conjugation between eventually adjacent aromatic nuclei (i.e. planarization of the conjugated backbone) which, ultimately, leads to enhanced charge mobility.

Ï€-Conjugated alkynes often suffer from limited solubility in the solvents typically used to process (opto)electronic devices, which results in poor morphology and microstructure. These systems may benefit from alternative deposition techniques, such as nanoparticle ink techniques, including precipitation and miniemulsion methodologies.107,108

Most importantly, these techniques would enable the assembly of the active layers using green solvents. Note that to date halogenated or aromatic hydrocarbon solvents are the most employed option in device processing. However, these solvents are associated to serious (and often irreversible) toxicity hazards and are banned from mass-production factories. Therefore, substituting them for safer options is an essential step towards the industrial viability of the organic electronic technology.109â€“111

Other possible approaches to power further advances may be exploiting post-treatment deposition procedures, such as solvent vapour annealing techniques, or the use of additives.112â€“115 It must be further stressed here that processing solvents with green credential are worth investigating extensively, even when an optimal device response is obtained.

Finally, as is common with other semiconductor classes, Ï€-conjugated alkyne performance has been found to be dependent also on the device configuration (binary/ternary, conventional/inverted).

Thus, the design and the sustainable synthesis/implementation of alkyne-based semiconductors is a significant topic in the field of organic solar cells and for other types of devices.
