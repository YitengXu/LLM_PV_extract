Abstract

In perovskite solar cells (PerSCs), the electron transport layer (ETL) extracts and transports electrons from the perovskite light absorption layer (PAL) to the cathode, which plays an important role in improving the performance and long-term stability of the devices. Compared with fullerene derivatives, non-fullerene n-type organic semiconductors have some unique advantages, such as molecular structure diversity, ease of modification, adjustable frontier molecular orbitals, and good stability. Recently, non-fullerene electron transport materials (ETMs) have attracted more and more attention as efficient ETLs in PerSCs. A variety of non-fullerene ETMs have been reported. Herein, we summarized the latest developments of non-fullerene organic ETMs in PerSCs, including small molecular organic ETMs and polymer ETMs. Especially, the common effects of substituents and design strategies of molecular configuration on the properties of non-fullerene ETMs were systematically summarized. At the same time, the remaining challenges and promising future trends of PerSCs based on non-fullerene organic ETMs are discussed.

1. Introduction

PerSCs have attracted worldwide attention due to their unique optoelectronic properties, such as a low band gap, high absorption coefficient, high charge carrier mobility, and long exciton diffusion length. In the past decade, the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of PerSCs has rapidly increased from 3.8% to 25.2%.13 PerSCs are usually composed of a PAL, an ETL, a hole transport layer (HTL) and two electrodes (anode and cathode). According to the way of stacking of the functional layers, PerSCs can be divided into two different device configurations: conventional nip type and inverted pin type.4 Letter i represents perovskite materials which have accomplished great progress with wide absorption, excellent charge carrier separation and transport, and very good stability properties.59 On the other side, the n or p type transport layer plays a role in extracting charge carriers efficiently, avoiding charge carrier accumulation and recombination loss in the photoactive layer, respectively. The development of ETLs and HTLs is becoming more and more important as they have great influence on the performance of PALs. Particularly the extraction of electrons by ETLs has a great effect on controlling the overall device performance.10 A good ETM should have excellent electron transport properties, the ability to form a dense uniform film, energy levels well matching with that of the PAL, good morphological stability and low cost. In addition, the ability to passivate defects in the PAL is preferred.11 Until now, most reported highly efficient PerSCs are based on the conventional nip device configuration using inorganic ETMs as ETLs, such as TiO2, ZnO and SnO2. However, high-temperature sintering (TiO2 > 450 C) is usually the essential procedure to achieve high electron mobility, and high-density defect states may exhibit owing to surface crystal defects, so extra passivation to surface crystal defects is needed. These problems of inorganic ETLs much limit the low-cost commercialization and flexible application of PerSCs. In recent years, low-temperature, solution processable and diverse organic ETLs have been largely investigated as potential alternatives. Especially, inverted pin PerSCs can be easily manufactured with a simple device structure and could be deposited at low temperature since both the HTL and ETL are organic semiconductors.

Fullerenes (C60, C70) and their soluble form phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) are the widely used organic ETMs in inverted pin PerSCs due to their excellent electron transport properties and PCBM is the most representative ETM,1217 whereas, there are some intrinsic shortcomings for fullerene ETMs. Owing to diverse Sn and Pb-based halide perovskite materials in PerSCs, their conduction band changes a lot from 3.36 eV (MASnCl3) to 4.74 eV (FAPbI3).18 However, the LUMO level of fullerene ETMs can only be tuned in a narrow range because of the poor communication between the fullerene cage and its substituents.19,20 This in turns limits potential PAL/ETL combinations. Fullerenes also exhibit relatively poor morphological stability due to their relatively weak intermolecular interactions in the solid state, which is detrimental to the stability of PerSCs. Besides, high-cost material synthesis and purification are an obstacle for the commercial application of PerSCs.2022

Recently, inspired by the rapid development of high-performance non-fullerene electron acceptors for organic solar cells (OSCs), researchers have explored non-fullerene ETMs for PerSCs.23,24 Various non-fullerene ETMs have been reported as efficient ETLs or interfacial modifiers in PerSCs. They not only retain the n-type semiconductor properties of fullerenes, but also overcome some disadvantages of fullerenes by exhibiting properties such as readily tunable electronic energy levels, and good thermal and chemical stability. Their electronic, optical, and morphological characteristics can be rationally designed and precisely adjusted.25,26 Non-fullerene ETMs are emerging as a hot topic in the field of PerSCs. As shown in Fig. 1, they can be divided into four categories according to their chemical structures, namely rylene diimides (naphthalene diimide derivatives (NDIs) and perylene diimide derivatives (PDIs)), azaacene derivatives, indacenodithiophene (IDT) derivatives and n-type conjugated polymers.17 It was found that both the substituents and design strategies of molecular configuration play important roles in the properties of non-fullerene ETMs.27

In this article, we reviewed the latest developments of non-fullerene organic ETMs used as ETLs in PerSCs according to the four categories. The common effects of substituents and design strategies of molecular configuration on the properties of non-fullerene ETMs were systematically summarized. The remaining challenges and promising future directions for PerSCs based on non-fullerene organic ETMs are also discussed.

2. Rylene diimide small molecules

Rylene diimides have two diimide groups at the end positions of rylene. They usually possess high chemical and thermal stabilities owing to their conjugated planar structure.25 Their synthetic raw materials are usually very cheap. Moreover, rylene diimides have many functionalized reaction sites, which makes them easy to be modified. PDIs and NDIs are two types of most studied n-type rylene diimides. Both electronic (LUMO, HOMO) and optical properties (absorption, fluorescence) of PDIs and NDIs can be tailored over wide ranges.17,28

2.1 PDIs as ETLs in PerSCs

PDIs are widely investigated as promising non-fullerene ETMs due to the above-mentioned advantages of ease of modification, low-lying energy levels, and good stability. However, compared with PCBM, the performance of devices based on PDI ETLs is still lower.22,2932 The main problem for PDI derivatives is their strong aggregation due to their strong stacking tendency, which affects the film morphology and transport properties. Therefore, it's necessary to introduce functional groups or design novel geometries of PDI derivatives to disrupt the planarity of PDIs. In addition, the control of energy levels and the passivation of the PAL should also be taken into account.

Tetrachloro-substituted perylene diimide (TCl-PDI) has been used as an ETM in nip planar PerSCs to replace TiO2. It was reported that PerSCs based on TCl-PDI showed higher PCE and stability than that based on TiO2, which was attributed to the well-matched energy level of TCl-PDI/PAL and the superior crystalline characteristics of the PAL formed on the hydrophobic TCl-PDI.36

Constructing PDI dimers is the most used approach due to synthetic accessibility. Priyadharsini et al. designed benzo[ghi]perylenetriimide (BPTI) and its dimer (t-BPTI) as novel ETL materials. It was found that t-BPTI with a twisted structure could effectively inhibit the self-aggregation of PDI molecules. A smooth and continuous morphology of t-BPTI on the PAL was obtained. The optimal t-BPTI-based device showed higher PCE than the BPTI based device.37 The morphology of PDI dimers could be further adjusted by solvent additives. Ye et al. provided a simple way to tune the morphology and transport properties of PDI dimers by solvent additive engineering. A series of solvent additives (1,8-diiodooctane, 1-chloronaphthalene, 1-phenylnaphthalene, and 1-methylnaphthalene) was investigated for their role in the morphology and transport properties of the PDI dimer (Bis-PDI-T-EG) film as well as on the photovoltaic performance of PerSCs. It was found that 1-methylnaphthalene was the best additive that possessed good adjusting ability to control both morphological and electrical properties of the Bis-PDI-T-EG film. The fabricated PerSCs with an optimized Bis-PDI-T-EG + 0.05 v/v% MN electron transport layer (ETL) displayed the best PCE of 14.96%.38 In addition, it was reported that the electron mobility of di-PDI could be further increased by adding an n-type dopant, 1,3-dimethyl-2-phenyl-2,3-dihydro-1H-benzimidazole (DMBI), and an increased PCE could be obtained.39

Besides the twisted PDI dimers, a kind of more stereo molecule with a three-dimensional (3D) core is designed and termed as 3D PDI. This design rule makes inhibition of aggregation more effective and the charge transport isotropic. Jiang et al. reported a 3D molecule TPE-PDI4 with a tetraphenylethylene core, which showed good electron mobility, excellent solution processability, favorable film morphology, and compatible energy levels. It was used as an ETM in inverted pin PerSCs and a PCE of 16.29% was finally obtained, which was slightly higher than that of the PCBM-based PerSC. Furthermore, a higher PCE of 18.78% was achieved when TPE-PDI4/C60 was used as a double ETL.40 Cheng et al. synthesized a 3D molecule SFX-PDI4 based on the Spiro[fluorene-9,9-xanthene] (SFX) core. The 3D structure of SFX-PDI4 exhibited low-lying HOMO and LUMO energy levels, high electron mobility and good film-formation properties. When applied in inverted pin PerSCs, a high PCE of 15.3% was achieved without any significant hysteresis, which was just slightly lower than that of the device based on PCBM.41 Wang et al. designed two PDI trimers bridged by the truxene core, Tr-PDI3 and Tr-PDI3-C. In the Tr-PDI3 molecule, the three PDI units were connected to the truxene by CC bond, while Tr-PDI3-C was connected by a fused ring. Therefore, the Tr-PDI3 molecule showed a more twisted 3D structure than the Tr-PDI3-C molecule with a planar structure. The Tr-PDI3 film coated on the PAL exhibited smoother film morphology, and higher electron transport and extraction ability than the Tr-PDI3-C film. The PerSC using Tr-PDI3 as the ETL demonstrated a higher PCE of 17.45% than the device based on the Tr-PDI3-C ETL (16.90%). In addition, a PCE of 19.83% was further achieved by using a double ETL Tr-PDI3/C60 because the LUMO energy level of Tr-PDI3 was more compatible with the PAL.42 These results indicated that finely tuning the geometry of 3D PDI-based materials was closely related to the optical, electronic, morphological and photovoltaic properties.

As is well known, PDIN is a typical PDI with an amino terminus and it has been proved as an effective ETL in OSCs. Compared with C-PDI, the amino group of PDIN could not only reduce the work function of the cathode and promote electronic transport, but also passivate traps on the surface of the PAL, and enhance the surface wettability to the perovskite. Zhang et al. firstly tried to apply PDIN as an ETL in nip PerSCs and achieved a PCE of 17.66%.47 Miao et al. reported the first exploration of PDIN in inverted pin PerSCs. Trifluoroethanol was used to replace the conventional alcohol solvents such as methanol and ethanol. A high quality PDIN thin film was formed and the erosion to the PAL was reduced. This structure could increase the charge mobility of ETLs and affect the grain size of the perovskite. Cheng et al. reported two PDI dimers (FA-PDI2 and (FA2+-PDI2)Br2) modified by amino or ammonium groups at bay positions. The charged ammonium moiety in (FA2+-PDI2)Br2 showed a higher electronic conductivity and better hydrophobicity than FA-PDI2. The PerSCs based on the (FA2+-PDI2)Br2 ETL exhibited a promising PCE of 17.1%, in comparison to devices based on the reference ETL FA-PDI2 (13.5%) and PCBM (17.5%).53

Sulfur atom-modified PDIs can also tune the energy levels, improve the conductivity of PDIs and passivate the PAL. Li et al. introduced a varying number of annulated sulfur atoms on the basis of PDI dimers and obtained a series of ETMs, 2PDI-0S, 2PDI-2S, 2PDI-3S, and 2PDI-4S.54 The number of fused sulfurs tunes the LUMO of the PDIs with varying values (3.7 eV, 3.9 eV and 4.0 eV for 2PDI-2S, 2PDI-3S, and 2PDI-4S, respectively) and a deep HOMO (5.9 eV). The electron mobility of 2PDI-4S was comparable to that of PCBM suggesting that it could act as an effective ETL for PerSCs. Due to the chemical interaction between sulfur atoms and the PAL, the 2PDI-4S-based devices exhibited significantly higher stability than PCBM ones, and there was no significant attenuation after working at the maximum power for 400 hours.55

2.2 NDIs as ETLs in PerSCs

NDIs are analogs of PDIs that replace the perylene core by the naphthalene core and photoelectric properties are very similar. So they also have received increasing attention as ETLs in PerSCs. Compared with PDIs, NDIs can achieve a larger optical band gap, which is beneficial to the nip device configuration.56 At the same time, NDIs are also easy to aggregate by strong interactions, which will greatly affect the solubility, stability and charge-carrier transport. However, as the smallest part of the rylene family, the packing of NDIs can be adjusted easier without complicated synthesis. NDI ETMs can be simply synthesized in a few steps, which reduces the cost a lot. N- or side-substitution on mono-NDIs is the usual way to control the intermolecular interaction. Introducing NDI dimers is also an efficient method. Heteroatoms (e.g. O, S, N atoms etc.) on NDIs to passivate interfacial defects are introduced simultaneously.57,58
2.2.1 Effects of N-substituents on NDIs. The introduction of N-substituted alkyl chains into NDI derivatives can enhance solubility, but it leads to various mesophase transitions due to high conformational freedom and flexibility of alkyl groups, reducing the stability of PerSCs.57,5961 On the other hand, the introduction of N-substituted aromatic groups can enhance the long-term temporal stability, but it will reduce the solubility of NDIs in organic solvents, resulting in a decreased processability.62,63 Therefore, selecting suitable substituents on the nitrogen atom in NDIs is very important. The Im group introduced N-substituted phenylmethyl into the NDI core (NDI-PM) and first applied it as an ETL in pin PerSCs. NDI-PM exhibited electron mobility, charge extraction and solubility comparable to PCBM. As a result, NDI-PM-based PerSCs showed comparable performance to the PCBM-based devices. What's more, the thermal stability of NDI-based PerSCs was improved due to the much stronger hydrogen bonding in NDI-PM than PCBM.58 After that, Kwon et al. studied a series of similar N-substituted groups, such as 1-indanyl (NDI-ID(RR)), (R)-1-phenylethyl (NDI-PhE), 1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-1-naphthyl (NDI-TN(RR)) and their corresponding heterochiral side groups (NDI-ID(RS) and NDI-TN(RS)). With indane containing both alicyclic and aromatic characteristics, NDI-ID achieved both low-temperature solution processability and high temporal stability. NDI-ID-based devices showed simultaneously higher PCE up to 20.2% and longer temporal stability than PCBM-based ones.64 NDI-PhE with higher solubility and better film-forming ability was obtained by adding a methyl group into NDI-PM. This is because the two asymmetric-shaped chiral (R)-1-phenylethyl (PhE) groups reduce molecular symmetry and increase free volume. As a consequence, the best PCE of NDI-PhE based pin PerSCs reached 20.5% which was higher than that of PCBM.65 Then, they systematically studied the effect of substituents with chiral stereoisomerism on the physical and electronic properties of NDIs. It was found that the enantiomeric NDIs with a homochiral pendant group exhibited non-centrosymmetric molecular packing and had higher solubility and thermal stability than the homochiral NDIs. This was advantageous to the formation of a highly uniform ETL, and finally the NDI-ID (RR) and NDI-TN(RR) based PerSCs showed PCEs of 20.4% and 17.9% respectively, which were higher than those of the NDI-ID (RS) and NDI-TN(RS) based devices (20% and 16.5%). In addition, NDI-ID (RR)-based PerSCs demonstrated a degradation rate of less than 10% at 100 C in air for 1000 h.66 It could be seen that the design of asymmetrical structures was a potential approach to achieve high solution processability and significant temporal stability in NDIs. Furthermore, in 2018, Shao et al. designed and synthesized a series of asymmetrical small molecules AI1, AI2, and AI3 based on acenaphthylene-imide (AI). All of them showed high electron mobility (>104 cm2 V1 s1) and solution processability. When used as an intermediate layer between TiO2 and the PAL, the short-circuit current and the fill factor of the device were significantly improved compared to that of TiO2 based devices and they were similar to those of TiO2/PCBM based devices. This could be attributed to their good electron transport properties.67

In addition, preparation of NDI films by a new solvent method was explored. Peng et al. developed a new alcohol-soluble NDI with N-substituted pyridinium salt (PN6), which could reduce the work function of Ag. When it was used as an intermediate layer between PCBM and metal electrodes, a PCE of 17.27% was obtained and the performance of PerSCs was insensitive to the thickness of PN6.68 The potential of naphthalene-1,8:4,5-tetracarboxylic diimide (NDI-H, the simplest NDIs) as an ETL in PerSCs was examined by a thermal conversion method. By introducing thermally cleavable solubilizing groups tert-butoxycarbonyl (Boc) as the N-substituents, a soluble NDI-H precursor was synthesized. NDI-H could be obtained by heating the spin-coated film of the NDI-Boc precursor compound, and a NDI-H film oriented face to face with the substrate was successfully prepared.69

In 2018, Yang's group designed three IDTs with different conjugation lengths of the central multi-fused ring. Consequently, IDTCN, IDT6CN and ITCPTC with five, six and seven fused-rings were obtained. They were applied as ETLs in inverted pin PerSCs. The ITCPTC molecule showed the highest PCE of 17.42% due to its high electron mobility and the dominant face-on molecular orientation in neat films. When used as an interface modifier between PAL/C60, ITCPTC could further deliver a higher PCE of 19.51%. Coupled with the suitable energy levels, smooth film morphology, decent electron mobility, and electron extraction ability ITCP-M was made a promising ETM for inverted PerSCs. An excellent PCE of 19.15% was reported.90

It has been proved that the design of asymmetrical molecules was an effective way to adjust the dipoledipole interaction of IDT-based molecules and the intermolecular stacking. Yang et al. developed two novel asymmetric molecules, the methyl-substituted IDT6CN-TM and the fluorinated IDT6CN-4F. The larger dipole moment enabled a stronger stacking of IDT6CN-TM compared to IDT6CN-4F, and thus a higher electron mobility was observed in IDT6CN-TM neat films. The high electron mobility, efficient charge collection and decent interfacial properties (good surface coverage on the perovskite film) allowed IDT6CN-TM and IDT6CN-4F to work as efficient ETMs: IDT6CN-TM- and IDT6CN-4F-based inverted PerSCs achieve PCEs of 18.36% (FF of 80%) and 17.75%, which were significantly higher than that of the PCBM-based one.98 It should be noted that the stronger stacking might lead to more severe aggregation, poorer solubility and morphology in the case of rylene diimides. However, the good stacking resulted in high electron mobility here in the case of IDTs. This was because IDTs were usually well soluble in organic solvents and there was a balance between the good morphology and the stacking.

6. Polymer electron transport materials

A great number of organic polymers as ETMs or acceptors in OSCs have been reported and excellent results have been achieved.108110 Given the abundant acceptor polymer materials, their easily tunable properties, good morphological stability, strong solvent resistance (DMF, DMSO and GBL etc.) and film-forming properties, it is worth making ETMs and exploring their use for PerSCs.111,112 Herein we will introduce the application of typical polymers used as ETLs in PerSCs respectively, such as NDI polymers, PDI polymers and polyfluorene derivatives.

6.1 NDI polymer

NDI polymers exhibit high electron mobility, good solution processability, high stability, and feasible adjustment of chemical structure which are beneficial for being used as ETMs.113 Until now, NDI based polymer ETMs reported are mainly modified by the units of thiophene or fluorene. The conjugation degree of copolymerized units can effectively improve the conjugation and order of NDI polymers, which are beneficial for improving the electron mobility. As electron donating groups, modifying thiophene and fluorene with substituents or adjusting the amount of thiophene and fluorene are effective strategies to adjust the energy levels of NDI based polymers as well.114,115

The thiophene units are usually used to adjust the conjugation degree and then control the electron mobility of NDI polymers. With higher electron mobility, the performance of the device based on PNDI2OD-T2 was comparable to that of a PCBM-based device. The modification to thiophene can also be used to adjust the energy levels of ETMs. In 2018, Zhang's team reported a series of NDI polymers based on thiophenepiazthiolethiophene, pBTT, pBTTz, and pSNT. It was found that the LUMO level decreased from 3.78 eV to 3.86 eV by introducing sp2-nitrogen to the thiophene of pBTT to form pBTTz with a slightly declined electron mobility. The PCE of PerSCs based on pBTTz was improved from 12.8% (pBTT) to 14.4% owing to the better matching LUMO energy level. Adding sp2-nitrogen to the piazthiole of pBTT to get pSNT resulted in a lowest LUMO level of 3.88 eV and a higher electron mobility, but its PCE decreased to 12%. That was because that the HOMO level of pSNT (5.45 eV) was close to the valence band of the PAL (5.4 eV) and resulted in poor hole blocking.122

Besides the thiophene unit, its analogue of selenophene has also been investigated. The number of selenophene increased in the order of NDI-Se, NDI-BiSe and NDI-TriSe. Similar to thiophene, NDI-BiSe and NDI-TriSe showed increased conjugate planarity, which improved electron delocalization and crystallinity. Electron mobility increased in the order of 4.3 105 cm2 V1 s1 (NDI-Se), 6.0 105 cm2 V1 s1 (NDI-BiSe) and 3.0 104 cm2 V1 s1 (NDI-TriSe). The PCEs of NDI-Se, NDI-BiSe and NDI-TriSe-based PerSCs were 9.51%, 7.66% and 14.0%, respectively. The NDI-TriSe-based PerSC gave a PCE close to that of C60-based PerSC.123

According to the above discussion, we can conclude that increasing the conjugation degree of copolymerized units is an effective strategy to improve the electron mobility of NDI polymers, but the HOMO level also increases which weakens the blocking effect on holes. Modification of copolymerized units is essential to find a balance between high electron mobility and acceptable HOMOs.

Polyfluorene can be modified to serve as an ETL in PerSCs. F8BT is one kind that can be synthesized by the copolymerization of benzothiazole and fluorene. It exhibited high electron mobility and was used as an ETL for PerSCs in 2014, and a PCE of 7.05% was achieved.135 The LUMO level of F8BT was determined to be 3.75 eV, which is the same as the conduction band (3.75 eV) of CH3NH3PbI3xClx. This facilitated electron transfer. When combined with C60 as a double ETL, the energy level alignment between the PAL and the ETL was improved. At the same time, the F8BT's deep HOMO level (5.91 eV) can effectively prevent holes from transporting into the ETL. PCE was increased from 7.9% to 13.9%. When F8BT was combined with PCBM, a higher PCE of 15.9% was obtained.136 The amino-modified PFN and PFN-OX were also widely used as ETLs in PerSCs. PFN had good film-forming performance. When PFN was used to modify TiO2 in PerSCs, the PerSCs showed a PCE up to 14.31% because of the reduced recombination as well as the optimized PAL.137 However, PFN exhibited a certain solubility in DMF and DMSO which were the solvents of perovskite materials. This is disadvantageous when used in nip PerSCs. Thus, a cross-linkable conjugated polymer with oxetanyl (PFN-OX, Fig. 17), which shows better solvent resistance, was preferred to form an ETL in nip PerSCs. The PCE increased from 10.2% (PFN) to 11.1% (PFN-OX). If hybrid composite films consisting of PFN-OX and ZnO nanoparticles were utilized as ETLs, a remarkable power conversion efficiency over 16% was achieved. The hybrid composite of PFN-OX:ZnO could not only achieve the appropriate energy level alignment between the perovskite material and ITO electrode, but also formed optimized ohmic contact. Moreover, the charge separation and collection processes were improved for the devices based on PFN-OX:ZnO.138 PFN-OX can also be used as an n-dopant for PCBM. When PFN-OX-doped PCBM was used as an ETM, the PCE of PerSCs increased significantly from 11% to 16.2%.139

He's team recently designed two novel n-type polymers based on bithienimide and thienothiazole imide, PBTI and PDTzTI. Replacing thiophenes with thiazoles could greatly reduce HOMO and LUMO levels. Owing to the mismatched energy levels between PBTI and the PAL, its device performance was far worse than PDTzTI. PDTzTI had high electron mobility, good energy level matching, and passivation of interface traps, and its optimal PCE was 20.86%, which was even better than that of PCBM. What's more, PDTzTI had hydrophobicity and ion blocking ability, as well as good film formation ability. As a result, PDTzTI-based devices demonstrated excellent long-term and operational stability.140

In addition, Jen's team designed and synthesized an n-type conjugated cross-linked polymer, cross-linked hexaazatrinaphthylene (c-HATNA), to serve as an efficient and robust ETL for inverted pin PerSCs. By employing the thiolene reaction, c-HATNA could be efficiently obtained at a relatively low temperature of 110 C. The obtained c-HATNA film possessed excellent hydrophobicity and thermal stability, enabling the realization of stable PerSCs without encapsulation. By doping with Et3N, a high PCE of 18.21% was obtained. More importantly, the device with the c-HATNA ETL showed impressive thermal stability and a slight loss (<10%) of PCE after being heated at 70 C for 1000 h.141

7. Conclusion

We have summarized common non-fullerene organic ETMs, including NDIs, PDIs, azaacene-based molecules, IDTs and n-type conjugated polymers. Rylene diimides including NDIs and PDIs have a conjugated planar structure and reaction sites that are easy to functionalize. At the same time, they have the advantages of good thermal stability and low cost.25 But they all face the problem of strong aggregation, leading to poor film morphology and electronic transmission.34,69 Therefore, it is an effective strategy to introduce substituents or change the three-dimensional structure of molecules to regulate the interaction among molecules.37,73,74 Azaacene derivatives have higher electron mobility, but higher LUMO and poor solution processability hinder their use in PerSCs. Therefore, groups such as sulfide, sulfoxide and sulfone are introduced to promote energy level matching and improve solubility.75,76 IDT derivatives also have advantages such as modifiable structure, higher mobility, good solution processability, and ability to passivate the PAL, so they show better performance in PerSCs, but IDTs also faced the problem of high cost as well as fullerenes owing to their complicated synthetic routes and purification.25 The research on IDTs focuses on the modification of their terminal groups, side chains and fused ring core. Compared with small molecule ETMs, polymer ETMs can provide better film-forming properties, a wider range of rheological properties, and better mechanical flexibility of films and devices. However, the electron mobility and energy levels of polymer ETMs usually need to be adjusted to match with the PAL. In addition to the modification of the backbone in the polymer, cross-linking and doping strategies are also good to improve the electrical properties.141 According to the effects of substituents and design strategies of molecular configuration on the properties of non-fullerene ETMs, Fig. 18 summarizes the following strategies: (1) introducing halogen atoms to improve stability and hydrophobicity (e.g. F atoms in rylene diimide small molecules and polymers), and to increase electron mobility and improve film formation (e.g. Br atom in PDIs). (2) Introduction of Lewis base functional groups, such as amino groups, thiophene and pyridine to improve energy level matching and passivate the PAL. (3) Increasing steric hindrance to inhibit the self-aggregation of molecules to obtain smooth, uniform, pinhole-free ETLs and increase electron mobility (such as PDIs and NDIs). (4) Enhancing the conjugate effect to increase electron mobility (e.g. PDIs, IDTs and polymers) as well as to adjust the film morphology, and there is a balance in adjusting the aggregation while preserving high electron mobility. (5) Forming cross-linked ETLs to improve air stability, thermal stability and hydrophobicity.

Although some non-fullerene ETMs have achieved closed or even better performance in PerSCs than that of PCBM based ones, most PCEs of PerSCs based on non-fullerene ETLs are still lower. High device efficiency was commonly obtained based on non-fullerene/fullerene bilayer ETLs or mixing with other ETMs. In this case, those non-fullerene organic ETMs can be developed as ideal interfacial modifiers or defect passivators. To serve as independent ETLs in PerSCs, much research on non-fullerene ETMs is needed.
