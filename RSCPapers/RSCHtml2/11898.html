Abstract

Optoelectronic devices are ubiquitously built on substrates. To increase the efficiencies of light coupling into and out of optoelectronic devices, such as thin film solar cells and flexible lighting, a substrate with high transmittance and high haze is desired. Unfortunately, optical transmittance and optical haze are usually contrasting to each other in common substrates: plastic is highly transparent but with a low optical haze, whereas paper has a high optical haze but a low total transmittance. Herein, we combine these two materials through a simple templated infiltration approach to achieve a new type of substrate, plasticâ€“paper, which has a high optical transmittance (>85%) and high transmittance haze (>90%) in a broadband wavelength. The plasticâ€“paper has an ultra-flat surface, is mechanically flexible, durable in different solvents and compatible with standard processing in semiconductors, which are shown by organic light emitting diodes (OLED) fabricated directly onto the plasticâ€“paper substrate. Plasticâ€“paper leads to improved light coupling into and out of optoelectronic devices and demonstrates an improvement in efficiency for both OLED and typical GaAs solar cells. The fabrication method is also fully scalable with roll-to-roll production. The newly developed low-cost, high-performance transparent and hazy substrate is attractive for a range of optoelectronic devices.


Broader context

Plastic is widely used for printing and information media due to its excellent optical transmittance, clarity, flexibility and mechanical strength, whereas paper substrates with micro-sized fibers have been used for 2000 years. In this study, we integrated plastic into mesoporous paper to achieve a new type of substrate: plasticâ€“paper. This hybrid substrate combines the advantages of both substrates and leads to unique optical properties: high optical transmittance and simultaneously high optical haze. This novel and unique combination of optical properties results in dramatic light coupling in a broad wavelength range and broad angle for improved power conversion efficiencies of optoelectronic devices. This novel transparent plasticâ€“paper can be fabricated via the roll-to-roll fabrication method and can be applied to a range of optoelectronic applications.

Introduction

Flexible electronics are becoming increasingly popular as compared to silicon-based technologies because of their flexibility, lightweight and scalable roll-to-roll manufacturing.1â€“6 Substrates play a key role in enabling the success of flexible electronics, and they act as a mechanical support and sometimes functional components for devices built on them.7â€“13 For flexible optoelectronics, such as organic-light-emitting-diodes (OLED) and solar cells, the optical properties of the substrates are critical as light needs to be coupled into or out of the device through transparent substrates.9,10,14â€“16 Plastics are the most used flexible substrates and they have a high transmittance of up to 90%.9,17â€“19 However, plastic substrates only transmit light and have limited capability in light management, resulting in only about 20% of light coming out of the OLED after multiple reflections and scatterings at interfaces, even though the internal quantum efficiency approaches 100%.9 Similarly, for solar cells, more than 30% of incident light is lost to airâ€“semiconductor interfaces.12 Therefore, the greatest potential for the improvement of power conversion efficiency lies in the enhancement of light coupling through the substrate.9,12,16,18

Different light outcoupling designs and additional light management layers have been studied for OLED on plastic or glass substrates without the light coupling capability such as glass with a high refractive index to eliminate light scattering at emitter/glass interfaces,9,20 an additional half sphere lens to minimize light scattering at glass/air interfaces,9 and an additional outcoupling polymer layer17 with dispersed SiO2 microparticles.18 Similar antireflection coatings have been developed for solar cell applications by thin film interference and metal/dielectric plasmonic nanostructures.12,21â€“24 However, all these processes introduce additional fabrication steps (film deposition and subwavelength structures) and increase costs, and the enhancement is generally wavelength and angle dependent.

Alternatively, researchers have found that transparent substrates with built-in high transmission haze can effectively enable light coupling into or out of the device through the substrate and show broadband and angle insensitive responses.4,12,14,25 For example, Ag nanowires embedded in plastics can act as a transparent electrode with certain degree of haze.26â€“31 Paper has intrinsically high haze due to randomly mixed cellulose fibers that can largely scatter light, however paper scatters light heavily due to its porous structure and light scattering at numerous fiber/air interfaces. Finely tailoring the size32â€“34 and shape4,35â€“37 of cellulose fibers by chemical and mechanical treatments can result in different optical properties with high transparency and tunable haze,25,32â€“36,38â€“41 which allow broad angle absorption and broad angle scattering with more light absorbed or emitted, especially at high angles. However, the fabrication processes for tailoring cellulose fibers and removing solvents in papermaking are time and energy intensive, and produce large amounts of wastewater containing toxic chemicals, while the haze properties in these nano-papers are limited.14,36,39,42 Besides, nano-papers based on pure cellulose fibers are prone to water and vapor permeation as well as solvent disintegration, which make them unsuitable for direct device fabrication as well as long time operation. To avoid the fabrication inefficiency and limited haze caused by nanofibers in nano-paper, a transparent substrate based on regular paper with high transmission haze can be a solution. Opaque paper can be directly turned into a transparent substrate via polymer impregnation.32,43â€“46 However, studies on plasticâ€“fiber composites have focused on mechanical improvement in fiber reinforced plastic composites and on achieving higher transparency in plasticâ€“nanofiber composites. The unique high transmission haze caused by randomly distributed fibers in the nonwoven paper backbone is unknown.

Therefore, for flexible optoelectronic devices, the ideal substrate should have (1) high transparency and high haze for improved efficiency through light transmission and coupling in the substrate, and (2) a flat surface with good mechanical and chemical stability that are compatible with various devices fabrication processes, and (3) production in an inexpensive, scalable manner. Herein, we report a novel plasticâ€“paper structure, wherein polymers are well infiltrated in mesoporous paper made of micro-sized cellulose fibers and form a super flat surface layer by template transferring. This hybrid substrate combines the advantages of both substrates and leads to unique optical properties: a high optical haze (up to 90%) comparable to a paper substrate and high total transmittance (up to 85%) comparable to plastic. The newly developed substrate has bendability like paper, but unlike paper, is highly resistance to water and many solvents. Its unique optical haze and transmittance lead to an extraordinary performance when used in organic light emitting diodes and thin film solar cells: a 35â€“40% increase in current efficiency for OLED and 15% energy conversion efficiency increase for thin film solar cells compared with that of glass substrates. The plasticâ€“paper is fabricated from the backbone of regular paper via a simple, scalable polymer infiltration. Its excellent performance and low-cost, scalable manufacturing make the plasticâ€“paper extremely attractive for a range of flexible optoelectronics beyond solar cells and OLED lighting.

Results and discussion

Fig. 1a shows the schematic structure change from paper to plasticâ€“paper by templated polymer infiltration. As shown in the schematic, paper is composed of cellulose microfibers with micro-sized pores, which lead to a rough surface (micrometer roughness) and strong light scattering at numerous fiberâ€“air interfaces, and this results in the high opacity of paper (transmittance <40%) and high haze (>90%). On the other hand, plastic has excellent optical transmittance (âˆ¼90%) but low haze (<5%) due to its homogeneous structure. Herein, we combine the two materials through a simple templated infiltration approach to achieve a new type of substrate, plasticâ€“paper, which has a high optical transmittance (>85%) and a high transmittance haze (>90%). By templated polymer infiltration, the micro-sized air pores inside the paper were filled with a polymer having a similar refractive index and super flat surface layers were formed by transferring from the templates, which led to a minimized surface and interface scattering with high optical transparency for the plasticâ€“paper. Moreover, the plasticâ€“paper still has numerous interfaces between polymer and cellulose microfibers, which lead to small angle scattering inside the plasticâ€“paper, resulting in a high transmission haze.

In our study, regular roll paper was used as the paper substrate. The mesoporous, fibrous structure of the paper substrate allows fast polymer infiltration to form the plasticâ€“paper hybrid substrate. Transparent thermal setting epoxy resin was used as the infiltration polymer because it has a refractive index of 1.522, which is similar to that of cellulose, 1.544,47 thus ensuring less scattering at the interfaces. Moreover, epoxy resin has low viscosity and superior mobility before curing and can infiltrate micro-sized cavities easily, while it shows good mechanical properties and excellent solvent stability after curing, which can protection cellulose paper from easily fracturing or disintegrated by solvents during device fabrication.6,9,13,18 Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) was used as a flat template for templated infiltration to ensure super flat surface transferring. Note that PDMS neither reacts with nor sticks to epoxy resin and is well studied for surface feature transfer.48Fig. 1b shows images of the plasticâ€“paper made from a roll paper by the templated infiltration of epoxy resin. The clear letters at the lower part (plasticâ€“paper attached to the background) indicate high transparency while the fuzzy letters at the upper part (plasticâ€“paper away from the background) indicate high haze.

Apart from unique optical properties, the plasticâ€“paper hybrid substrate benefits from excellent mechanical and permeation ability from the templated infiltration, and has a super flat surface and shows improved mechanical strength, excellent folding tolerance and solvent stability compared to the original paper, and also benefits from the excellent encapsulation of epoxy resin. The materials and manufacturing infrastructures for plasticâ€“paper are readily available in the industry, which makes the newly developed plasticâ€“paper attractive as a high performance and low-cost substrate for optoelectronics.

The templated infiltration was carried out by the infiltration of epoxy resin (20 wt% paper) on both sides of roll paper and sandwiched between two flat PDMS films for curing under pressure. The PDMS film (transferred from silicon wafer/corning glass) allows super flat surface transferring and easy release from the epoxy resin after it is fully cured. Fig. 2a and b show the top view of scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of roll paper and plasticâ€“paper. Roll paper shows micro-sized cellulose fibers squeezed into a flat shape and intertwined together to form paper with micrometer surface roughness. After the templated infiltration of epoxy resin, the plasticâ€“paper shows a super smooth surface without clear features, as shown in Fig. 2b. The porous and rough surface was replaced a dense and flat surface resin layer. Fig. 2c and d show the cross-section SEM images of roll paper and plasticâ€“paper. The cross-section of roll paper shows a loosely packed structure with micro-sized fibers and pores, whereas the plasticâ€“paper shows a densely packed structure with the epoxy resin well infiltrated into the paper. Moreover, a thin surface (<10 Î¼m) was formed on both sides by templated curing, which ensures the flatness of plasticâ€“paper for device fabrication. To further characterize the surface smoothness, atomic force microscopy (AFM) was used to examine an area of the plasticâ€“paper of 1.6 Î¼m Ã— 1.6 Î¼m, which showed a local surface roughness of approximately 3 nm (Fig. 2e). The super flat surface benefits from the PDMS template during the curing of epoxy resin.

The mesoporous structure with a rough surface leads to strong light scattering at fiber/air interfaces and results in the high opacity of paper. Furthermore, porous paper has poor mechanical properties and poor solvent stability due to its loosely packed structure. Its high opacity, porous structure, and rough surface make paper unsuitable as a flexible substrate for devices to be built on. After templated infiltration, the small surface roughness of the hybrid substrate is critical to minimize light scattering at the surface for high transparency and also to ensure successful micro-devices fabrication on the surface. High stability in many solvents and the ultra low water and vapor permeation rate of epoxy resin47,49 was also maintained in the plasticâ€“paper substrate.

The optical properties of plastic, paper, and transparent plasticâ€“paper were comparably studied and they showed drastically different optical performances that can be used for different purposes. The total transmittance of polyethylene terephthalate (PET), roll paper and plasticâ€“paper were measured using an integrated sphere. As shown in Fig. 3a, PET has the highest optical transmittance of âˆ¼90%, and plasticâ€“paper is comparable with a total transmittance of âˆ¼85â€“90% from 400 nm to 1100 nm (broadband), whereas the paper substrate has the lowest optical transmittance of less than 40%. The broadband, high transmittance of plasticâ€“paper is the direct result of its flat surface and increased packing density after polymer infiltration into the porous paper (density between 7 mg cmâˆ’2 and 9.5 mg cmâˆ’2).

Optical haze is used to describe light scattering ability and was measured for these substrates, as shown in Fig. 3b. PET shows an ultra low transmission haze below 5%, whereas both paper and plasticâ€“paper show high haze above âˆ¼90% from 400 nm to 1100 nm (broadband), namely, most of the light was reflected and scattered when it passed through paper and plasticâ€“paper. The light scattering effect, or haze effect, is visually displayed in the inset of Fig. 3b. When a green laser went through, the plasticâ€“paper showed a high intensity and highly scattered light pattern on the wall, which indicate both good light transmittance, as well as high haze. The high haze of plasticâ€“paper comes from the significant scattering at the epoxy resinâ€“cellulose interface and mesoporous structure in the primary cellulose fibers. The angle dependence of haze for plasticâ€“paper was also characterized by the angular distribution of laser transmittance through different substrates, as shown in Fig. 3c. Plastic PET has a very narrow-angle distribution and the transmitted light for plastic was focused within an angle less than 2Â° (estimated as 5% of peak intensity), whereas paper and plasticâ€“paper had similarly broad angle distributions (60Â°â€“80Â°), and the transmitted light intensity for plasticâ€“paper is one order higher than that of paper in a broad angle range.

However, such optical behavior is impossible for traditional substrates such as paper, plastic and glass. A traditional approach for light management is to start with transparent and clear substrates and add another layer of coating or create other features to add optical haze.12 Often, light management through traditional approaches needs additional fabrication steps and the enhancement is not broadband. The plasticâ€“paper developed in this study shows the combined advantages of plastic and paper, as shown in Fig. 3d, to achieve built-in high haze and high transmittance in a broad wavelength range and broad angle for the improved efficiency of optoelectronics devices such as solar cells and LED lighting.

In addition to its outstanding optical properties, the plasticâ€“paper also shows improved mechanical properties and excellent solvent stability, which are critical for direct device fabrication. Fig. 4a shows stressâ€“strain curves for the substrates of paper, plasticâ€“paper, and epoxy resin film. For paper, epoxy resin and plasticâ€“paper, the ultimate strength and storage modulus of plasticâ€“paper is highest due to the composite effect wherein the epoxy resin can hold the porous fiber together, while the fiber structure can reinforce the polymer matrix. Fig. 4b shows the folding endurance of roll paper and plasticâ€“paper. The sample strip was pulled with a constant force (14.7 N) and folded to 135Â° repeatedly until it was broken. Compared with roll paper, the plasticâ€“paper shows 10 times increased folding endurance capacity both in the longitudinal direction (length direction of roll paper) and transverse direction (width direction of roll paper), which can be explained by its densely packed structure with enhanced mechanical properties due to the addition of epoxy resin. The increased mechanical properties, especially tear folding capacity, enable the usage of plasticâ€“paper as an enduring transparent substrate under constant bending or flexible applications.

Solvents stability is an important concern during the device fabrication process. Unlike paper substrates, which are not stable in solvents (especially water) and change shapes after experiencing wetâ€“dry cycles, plasticâ€“paper is highly stable in solvents widely used in semiconductor processing. After a three-week-long water stability test, regular roll paper had completely disintegrated into cellulose microfibers, while the plasticâ€“paper maintained its shape without any noticeable changes (Fig. 4c). The epoxy resin itself is highly water resistant and it crosslinks the cellulose microfibers together to prevent disintegration.49 When soaked in solvents (including water) and taken out to dry, regular paper tends to swell and then dry with many wrinkles, which is a major challenge for micro-device fabrication. The plasticâ€“paper does not experience this problem and has good shape retention. We also confirmed the stability of plasticâ€“paper in chemicals and solvents required for standard lithography and device fabrication such as photoresist (1813), developer (IMBK:IPA = 3:1), and acetone for at least 1 day (Fig. 4d). The plasticâ€“paper showed great shape retention and excellent stability in these chemicals and solvents and therefore can be directly used as a flexible substrate for many devices requiring lithography and patterning processes containing different solvents. The water and vapor permeation rate (WVPR) is critical for devices containing sensitive components such as conductive and semi-conductive polymers. Epoxy resin is well-known for its ultra-low WVPR, which is often used for device encapsulation.49 By templated infiltration, the air permeation rate of roll paper dropped from 2380 mL minâˆ’1 to below the detection limit after epoxy infiltration. Although the dense epoxy surface layer is thin (âˆ¼10 Î¼m), the plasticâ€“paper substrate shows an average WVPR of 5.8 g mâˆ’2 dayâˆ’1, which is at the same level of typical plastics (PET 5â€“20 g mâˆ’2 dayâˆ’1 and PEN 2â€“5 g mâˆ’2 dayâˆ’1).50â€“52 All the synergistic improvements in surface structure, mechanical properties, solvents stabilities and air permeation rate after epoxy infiltration make plasticâ€“paper a qualified substrate for device fabrication.

The application of transparent and haze paper can be extended to other optoelectronic devices for improved efficiency, such as solar cells and photo-electrochemical water splitting when light coupling is critical.6,12,54,55 By simply laminating a plasticâ€“paper on top of GaAs solar cells, the power conversion efficiency is improved by up to 15%. The dark current density increases from 12.7 mA cmâˆ’2 to 14.4 mA cmâˆ’2 upon the application of a plasticâ€“paper (Fig. 5d) due to high haze effect of plasticâ€“paper, as well as index matching. As shown in Fig. 5e, the refractive index of transparent substrates is between air and GaAs or silicon semiconductor solar cells and therefore the reflection at the air/semiconductor interface can be reduced due to index matching (âˆ¼5% improvement).12 Compared with glass or plastic substrates, which have high transparency but ultra-low haze, plasticâ€“paper will largely improve the light absorption by broad angle absorption and also increase the coupling length in solar cells due to broad angle scattering. The broad angle absorption and scattering come from the high haze effect of plasticâ€“paper which is angle insensitive for light transmission. The improved light absorption and coupling lead to increase solar cell efficiency when light is incident from a large angle and remove the need for solar tracker. Similarly, for OLED lighting devices with reverse light pathways, more light can be coupled out through plasticâ€“paper due to the high haze effect with broad angle transmission ability.

The transparent plasticâ€“paper has excellent optical properties and is fully compatible with optoelectronic device fabrication with enhanced performance due to improved light management. Using the simple templated infiltration method, we have developed a meter long transparent plasticâ€“paper (Fig. 6a). The newly-developed substrate can also be fabricated via a scalable fabrication process in the well-developed paper industry. In the existing roll-to-roll paper fabrication line, polymer infiltration and drying can be easily incorporated, as shown in Fig. 6b. The roll-to-roll processable transparent plasticâ€“paper with unique optical properties can be integrated into a range of low-cost optoelectronics with a much-improved performance.

Conclusion

For the first time, we utilize an extremely simple method to dramatically improve the optical transmittance of regular paper while keeping its high optical haze. The resulting transparent plasticâ€“paper has the advantages of plastic and paper: high transmittance, high haze, smooth surface, overall enhancement of mechanical performance, and solvent stability. In addition, the plasticâ€“paper is fully compatible with lithography based optoelectronic device fabrication. Bendable OLED devices with an enhanced lighting efficiency compared with standard substrate were demonstrated, due to the improved light coupling. We have also demonstrated scalable transparent plastic that is potentially roll-to-roll compatible. The newly develop transparent plasticâ€“paper is extremely attractive for a range of energy efficient optoelectronics applications, such as OLED and solar cells, wherein traditional paper or plastic alone cannot meet the requirement.

Experimental section

Plasticâ€“paper fabrication

Regular roll paper (Boardwalk B1540900 Butcher Paper, 15â€²â€² Ã— 900 ft, White Roll) was coated by epoxy resin (AeroMarine 300/21 epoxy resin). In the infiltration process, base (#300) and curing (#21) polymers were mixed together with a volume ratio of 2:1 and degassed under vacuum for 5 minutes. Then, the epoxy resin (20 wt% roll paper) was uniformly applied to each side of the roll paper. A PDMS (Sylgard 184) template was used as a flat template for epoxy curing. The PDMS was poured onto a high-quality glass (Corning) and cured at 150 Â°C for 20 min with superior surface flatness. The roll paper with epoxy resin (20 wt%) on each side was sandwiched between two PDMS templates and flattened by a glass rod. Then, the paper in between PDMS was naturally dried in air for 1 day under pressure.

Characterization

SEM measurement was carried out on a Hitachi SU-70 SEM with a voltage of 10 kV. AFM measurements were performed on a Digital Instruments Multimode AFM in tapping mode. Optical transmittance and haze were measured on a LAMBDA 1050 UV/Vis/NIR Spectrophotometer with an integrated sphere. Angular light scattering was measured on a lab setup with a green laser as the source and a motor for angle rotation. Strainâ€“stress curves were obtained on a Tinius Olsen H25KT while the foldability test was conducted on an MIT/U21B with a folding angle of 135 degrees at 175 folds per minute. The air permeation rate was measured on an L&W 166 with a measurement size of 50 cm2. Solar cell efficiency was measured on a Newport Model 91159 full spectrum solar simulator.

Fabrication of OLED on plasticâ€“paper

(1) A 200 Ã— 200 mm2 plasticâ€“paper substrate with a thickness of 120 Î¼m was attached to the same size glass carrier (conventional alkali-free glass, 0.7 mm thick) as a mechanical support during the device fabrication process. (2) 100 nm ITO film was deposited on plasticâ€“paper substrates by radio frequency magnetron sputtering with a power density of 6.2 W cmâˆ’2. The measured sheet resistance of the ITO films was approximately 40 Î© sqâˆ’1. (3) The ITO film was patterned using the conventional photolithography process. (4) The configuration of the Green OLEDs is shown in Fig. 5a. All layers were thermally deposited without breaking the vacuum at a base pressure of 2 Ã— 10âˆ’7 Torr. The deposition rates of both host and guest were controlled by their corresponding independent quartz crystal oscillators. (5) Devices were encapsulated immediately after preparation under a nitrogen atmosphere using SiNx (300 nm) thin films, which were fabricated using PECVD. The fabrication parameters for OLEDs on glass and plastic substrates are similar to those of OLED on plasticâ€“paper.

Characterization of OLED on plasticâ€“paper

The electroluminescence (EL) spectra of the devices were obtained on a Konica Minolta CS2000 spectra system. The emission area of the devices was 3 Ã— 3 mm2 as defined by the overlapping area of the anode and cathode. Luminanceâ€“current density (J) and voltage (V) were recorded simultaneously using a computer controlled source meter (Keithley 2400) and a multimeter (Keithley 2000) with a calibrated silicon photodiode.
