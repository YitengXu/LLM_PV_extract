Abstract

Cadmium sulfide has long been used as the buffer layer in thin-film solar cells. Despite its strengths, the problems associated with CdS â€“ a low bandgap, absorption loss, and toxicity of Cd â€“ have resulted in a search for alternate materials. Incorporating Zn results in a higher bandgap but obtaining complete coverage with a flake-free, compact morphology of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films using chemical bath deposition remains an unsolved problem. In this paper, we address the coverage issue and engineer the morphology of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films (x = 0.2, 0.5, and 0.8), focusing on controlling the formation mechanism by dynamically maintaining the pH of the precursor solution throughout the deposition. Heterogeneously grown Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films show high uniformity and improved structural properties. With a successive increment of the Zn amount, the Cd1âˆ’xZnxS optical bandgap enhances considerably from 2.55 eV to 3.77 eV. By depositing at the right pH, we report â€“ for both low and high Zn â€“ films that are compact and pin-hole free with 100% coverage which is rarely found in the literature. The significant improvement in average visible transmittance (AVT) from 51% (pristine CdS) to 75% (for Cd0.8Zn0.2S) and then to 85% (for Cd0.2Zn0.8S) indicates the potential to improve the efficiency of thin-film solar cells including for some novel applications such as semi-transparent photovoltaics.

1 Introduction

Human-driven climate change â€“ originating mostly from the burning of fossil fuels â€“ has triggered worldwide research in the past few decades on clean and renewable energy technologies such as solar photovoltaics (PVs). A massive deployment of solar panels will be required in the coming decades to increase the share of renewable, clean energy in power grids. Among the PV technologies, thin-film PVs comprise only a few micrometer thick layers of semiconductor (SC) materials, making them advantageous over other generations of PVs.1,2 The performance of solar cells does not only depend on the improved quality of the absorbers but also on the formation of good heterojunctions. For high-performing PVs, the separation of charge carriers by the built-in electric field in the space-charge region is as important as the photogeneration of carriers. Proper band alignment, favorable lattice mismatch between the absorber and buffer layer and reduced defect density at the interface can greatly minimize recombination and facilitate charge separation. CdS â€“ a IIâ€“VI group, n-type material â€“ is well-established for forming pâ€“n junctions in CIGS, CZTS, CdTe solar cells.3â€“7 However, it has some drawbacks that restrict the performance of thin-film PVs. CdS has been reported to limit the open-circuit voltage (VOC) of CZTS and CdTe cells owing to interface recombination arising from undesirable band alignment.8,9 It forms a cliff-like conduction band offset (CBO) with the absorber. Cliff-like CBO impedes electron flow from the buffer to the absorber and helps accumulate the electrons. It also allows high hole accumulation at the interface. Most importantly, CdS has been repeatedly found to be responsible for quantum efficiency drop in a short wavelength range (<550 nm) owing to the absorption of the blue portion of the solar spectrum.10,11 Its optical bandgap (Eg) (âˆ¼2.4 eV) is insufficient to allow short-wavelength light into the underlying absorber layer, which eventually deteriorates the short-circuit current (JSC). Also, such absorption loss reduces the average visible transmittance (AVT) for semi-transparent photovoltaics â€“ an emerging variant of thin-film PVs.12 On the other hand, the toxicity of Cd and the vast amount of chemical waste it generates have become a growing concern regarding its use on an industrial scale.

To resolve the environmental issue, Cd-free wide-bandgap materials such as ZnS,13 (Zn,Mg)O,14 (Zn,Sn)O,15 and In2S316 have been extensively explored but their cell performance remains inferior compared to that of the Cd containing cells. A longer fabrication time owing to the slow deposition rate, the use of additional complexing agents (hydrazine hydrate, tri-sodium citrate and so forth), poor lattice matching with chalcogenide absorbers, and the formation of oxide and hydroxide impurities are commonly observed issues during Cd-free buffer formation.17 Also, the post-deposition treatment becomes necessary for these buffers to mitigate defects and interface recombination, which is a complex and time-consuming process for industrial applications.

As an alternative to CdS, Cd1âˆ’xZnxS has attracted great attention of the scientific community in the recent past.11 It has a similar crystal structure and comparable lattice constant to chalcopyrites and kesterites, leading to improved lattice matching. Furthermore, Zn's high solubility into CdS allows tuning of the bandgap to a desirable range by altering the Zn composition. Cd1âˆ’xZnxS has also been reported to reduce interface recombination by forming a spike-like CBO with the absorber, which hinders charge carrier accumulation at the interface.8

Cd1âˆ’xZnxS thin-films have been synthesized using various techniques such as vacuum evaporation,18 sputtering,19 electrodeposition,20 spin coating,21 dip coating,22 spray pyrolysis,23 successive ionic layer adsorption and reaction,8 close space sublimation24 and chemical bath deposition (CBD).11,25 Among these techniques, CBD â€“ an industry standard for thin film solar cells â€“ is the most favorable method owing to its simplicity, low process temperature, easy deposition over a large area and low cost for large-scale production.26 In the CBD method, the formation and quality of the film significantly depend on the process parameters â€“ the concentration of reactant materials, temperature of the chemical bath, deposition time, pH of the solution and stirring rate. Several articles have been published where the effect of Zn concentration is reported.27,28 Some have reported the impact of the Cd concentration and the use of alternative sulfur sources instead of thiourea.25,28,29 It is commonly seen in various reports that the deposited films show different preferred orientations and morphology despite similar process parameters being used.27,30 Although a numerical study states that high Zn-containing buffer films in CIGS solar cells have the potential to achieve high photoconversion efficiency (PCE), research on high Zn-containing films is rarely found in the literature.31

Most notably, poor coverage is a common problem while fabricating Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films using the CBD technique, even with a low Zn content which becomes progressively more severe at a higher Zn content.29,32â€“36 Using the CBD method, Chavhan et al. found nano-sheet (x = 0.05), flake (x = 0.1) and curly nano-sheet (x = 0.15) morphologies in low Zn containing films.33 Also, a fully flower-like structure was found for a Cd0.8Zn0.2S film deposited using a bath with varying pH from 8.5 to 11 by Lilhare et al.36 Erturk et al. noticed pin-holes and cracks on the Cd1âˆ’xZnxS (x = 0.05) film surface20 while fabricating the films by electrodeposition.

Complete coverage of the substrate with a Cd1âˆ’xZnxS (x = 0.17) film (âˆ¼240 nm thick) has been achieved using the RF magnetron sputtering method for deposition.37 Similarly, a uniform, homogeneous âˆ¼600 nm thick Cd1âˆ’xZnxS film has been reported where multiple layers of films (20 alternate layers of CdS (50 nm) and Zn (10 nm)) were deposited by evaporation followed by annealing, which is hardly suitable for industrial applications.18

In summary, the fabrication route of depositing pin-hole-free and flake-free, compact, fully covering Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films (0 < x < 1) is still unclear. Changing reactant concentration, solution temperature, and stirring rates may influence the film properties to some extent, but fabricating compact, fully covering films with desired structural, morphological and optical properties remains unpredictable. To get rid of undesired morphologies, orientation and poor coverage issues, controlling the formation mechanism of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films for both low and high Zn contents is vital, where the pH of the solution can play a significant role. However, little attention has been paid to controlling the formation mechanism of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films and pH of the chemical bath to date. In the CBD method, ammonia acts as a complexing agent and determines the solution's pH. In most of the studies, the solution pH is set arbitrarily (âˆ¼10â€“11) at the initial stage of the CBD process and not measured throughout the deposition despite the volatile nature of ammonia. Since the pH of the solution changes over the deposition time, any conclusive idea regarding its effects on the formation process and properties of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films is unknown.

In this study, we fabricated uniform, compact, fully covering Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films (x = 0.2, 0.5 and 0.8) using the CBD technique using low amounts of reactants. We controlled the formation mechanism by varying the pH of the solution. To get a more accurate effect of pH on film formation and properties, we dynamically maintained the pH of the bath at fixed levels throughout the deposition period. Also, we investigated how different pH values of the chemical bath and varying Zn content simultaneously influence the formation mechanism. In addition, the correlation among pH, Zn content and the observed structural, morphological and optical properties is established with regard to the formation mechanism. We report Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films with superior optical properties â€“ tunable bandgaps from 2.46 to 3.77 eV with AVT increasing from 51% to 85% (for x = 0 to 0.8).

We have discussed deposition conditions, characterization techniques and major chemical reactions governing the formation mechanisms in the experimental section. In the result and discussion part, microstructural characterization is discussed in connection with the formation mechanism at low, medium and high pH of the chemical bath, followed by EDS analysis. Next, the structure of the deposited films is analyzed. Then, the optical characterization section discusses the filmsâ€™ transmittance and optical bandgap. Finally, we have summarized our findings in the conclusion section.

2.3 Reaction and growth mechanism

In CBD, chemical reactions greatly influence the formation of thin films. There are two formation mechanisms:38 (i) homogeneous formation (by homogeneous reaction or a cluster-by-cluster method) and (ii) heterogeneous formation (by heterogeneous reaction or an ion exchange method) (Fig. 1). Both reaction mechanisms can potentially occur during film deposition, but depending on the process conditions, one mechanism dominates over the other. The film properties strongly depend on the dominating mechanism. Dense and well-adhered films generally form from heterogeneous reaction, whereas homogeneous reaction leads to the formation of porous and less-adherent films. The key reactions that dictate the progress of both kinds of mechanisms are described as follows:

3.1.1 Deposition mechanism at low pH. The observed microstructures are greatly related to the formation mechanism. SEM images of Cd0.8Zn0.2S, Cd0.5Zn0.5S and Cd0.2Zn0.8S films deposited at pH â€“ 9.5 are depicted in Fig. 2(a)â€“(c). The Cd0.8Zn0.2S film is not dense and has a high degree of porosity. Particles are interconnected and form clusters with numerous voids. The morphology indicates the rapid accumulation of (Cd,Zn)S clusters formed in the solution on the substrate surface. The uncontrolled precipitation of (Cd,Zn)S on the substrate takes place due to the fast release of metal ions from their corresponding complexes, which can also be corroborated by the large thickness of the film (âˆ¼870 nm) as seen in the cross-sectional view (inset). When the amount of ammonia is low in the bath (low pH), metalâ€“amino complexes become less stable; hence, more metal ions are released immediately, according to eqn (4) and (5). On the other hand, as soon as thiourea is supplied to the reaction system (eqn (7)), the concentration of free metals and sulfur ions exceeds the ksp of metal sulfides (as discussed earlier), and homogeneous formation occurs. Thus, the homogeneous reaction is the dominating mechanism for the formation of low Znâ€“Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films under low pH conditions.

A relatively continuous and non-porous film is obtained for Cd0.5Zn0.5S compared to Cd0.8Zn0.2S. However, a flake structure is noticed for moderate Zn content films, which is also commonly reported in the literature.46â€“48 Liu et al.47 observed a flake structure in CdS while fabricating it in a low ammonia (0.1â€“0.4 M) solution; Patil et al.46 reported the same structure when using different complexing agents-ammonia and triethylamine whereas Kamble et al.48 attributed the high Sâ€“Cd ratio to the formation of this structure. Hence, the exact reason for the flake formation is yet to be reported. However, it is reported that flakes form at the initial stage of the deposition process when the reaction rate is high.49 With time, the reaction rate decreases due to the consumption of precursor ions during flake formation, which later leads to the formation of quasi-spherical particles on flakes and the substrate surface.49 Due to the higher amount of Zn compared to that in the Cd0.8Zn0.2S system, most of the supplied ammonia is used to form the Znâ€“amino complex. So, the amount of Cdâ€“amino complex/free Cd ions is reduced in the Cd0.5Zn0.5S system, eventually limiting the random precipitation of (Cd,Zn)S observed in the Cd0.8Zn0.2S film. After adding thiourea in the bath, the free Cd ions released from the amino complexes (according to eqn (4)) are used up for the flake formation, which reduces the homogeneous reaction rate. Therefore, low pH in the presence of a moderate amount of Zn is a favorable condition for the onset of heterogeneous reaction in the Cd0.5Zn0.5S film.

Intriguingly, Cd0.2Zn0.8S films at pH 9.5 have a smooth, continuous, and dense surface morphology as desired for a buffer layer. The film fully covers the glass substrate and has no pinholes. Pinhole-free, compact film formation is also evident from the cross-sectional view. The presence of a higher amount of Zn compared to Cd implies the formation of an increased amount of Znâ€“amino complexes compared to the Cdâ€“amino complex in the bath, which slows down the release of free metal ions compared to lower Zn films. As a result, the amount of free metal and sulfur ions is inadequate to surpass the ksp of CdS and ZnS. This considerably retards the immediate precipitation of (Cd,Zn)S and facilitates the heterogeneous reaction. Metalâ€“amino complexes adsorb on the substrate surface and form reaction sites as per eqn (8) and (9). Then, non-hydrolyzed thiourea forms intermediate metastable complexes (eqn (10) and (11)) and eventually form compact films according to eqn (12) and (13). Therefore, low pH is found to be suitable for the heterogeneous reaction and a viable route for the formation of high Zn containing Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films, free from the coverage area problem widely reported in the literature.10,25,36

A similar flake structure is noticed in the Cd0.5Zn0.5S film deposited at pH â€“ 9.5. In the Cd0.5Zn0.5S film, more ammonia is used up for Znâ€“amino complex formation owing to the presence of a higher amount of Zn compared to that in the Cd0.8Zn0.2S system. It reduces the formation of Cdâ€“amino complexes/free Cd ions and triggers the heterogeneous reaction after flake formation, as described earlier. Thus, the presence of a high amount of Zn in Cd0.5Zn0.5S pH â€“ 9.5 bath regulates the amount of free Cd ions, whereas the high amount of ammonia and relatively stable Cdâ€“amino complexes regulate the amount of free Cd ions in Cd0.8Zn0.2S, pH â€“ 10 bath. Therefore, similar structures are observed at two different compositions and pH values.

For Cd0.5Zn0.5S, pH â€“ 10 yields highly dense, compact and uniform film morphology (Fig. 2e). The film is continuous and covers the substrate surface uniformly. As the amount of Zn precursor is higher than Cd0.8Zn0.2S, the amount of free Cd ion is also lower in Cd0.5Zn0.5S film and hence no flake is formed in Cd0.5Zn0.5S film after adding thiourea to the reaction bath.

Full areal coverage is not achieved for Cd0.2Zn0.8S films deposited at pH â€“ 10 (Fig. 2f), even though the other process parameters are the same as x = 0.5. We see that particles deposit on the nucleation sites, but the nucleation does not take place uniformly on the substrate surface. Thus, it can be inferred that more ammonia is used up in forming Znâ€“amino complexes, and hence the amount of Cdâ€“amino complex is reduced in Cd0.2Zn0.8S films, which may result in reduced nucleation sites on the substrate surface. As the Cdâ€“amino complex is more stable at pH â€“ 10 compared to pH â€“ 9.5 due to the higher amount of ammonia in the bath, the amount of adsorbed Cd hydroxide and dihydroxo-diaminoâ€“Cd complexes is also reduced compared to the Cd0.2Zn0.8S, pH â€“ 9.5 system. High pH has been found to control the rapid and/or vast release of free metal ions from their complexes, and hence a uniform, compact film without any porous or flake structure is obtained in the Cd0.8Zn0.2S system. Hence, the heterogeneous reaction, and not the cluster by cluster method, dominates at high pH. It can be further substantiated from the deposition of Cd0.8Zn0.2S film in a low pH (9.5) bath where random aggregation of (Cd, Zn)S clusters plays the governing role over heterogeneous formation.

4 Conclusion

In summary, we have uncovered the effect of basicity on the formation mechanism of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films grown using chemical bath deposition and reported compact, pin-hole free, uniform films with low to high Zn contents, 100% coverage ratio, and good reproducibility. The key to forming a uniform, fully covering film is that the pH of the solution is maintained at constant values throughout the deposition process. Low pH has been found to be suitable for a high Zn containing bath, whereas high pH is favorable for a low Zn content. As the film formation mechanism changes from homogeneous to heterogeneous (when the right pH is set), a clear improvement in film crystallinity and morphology occurs (from porous to compact). This is further substantiated by an improved texture coefficient, stacking fault and dislocation density. The optical properties of the optimum films show a blue shift of the absorption edge and a higher bandgap with a higher Zn content. The bandgap increases from 2.46 eV (for CdS) to 3.77 eV (for Cd0.2Zn0.8S). We propose an empirical relationship between the Zn content and the resulting bandgap of CBD-grown compact Cd1âˆ’xZnxS samples, which is yet to be reported in the literature. At the same time, the average visible transmittance improves from 51% to 85% (from CdS to Cd0.2Zn0.8S). The improved properties are expected to improve the energy conversion efficiency of thin-film PV applications where a low-cost, industrially favorable solution method like CBD is desirable. Finding the optimal pH of the solution to manipulate the formation techniques for any Zn content and understanding the combined effects of pH and Zn contents on the structure, morphology and optical properties will provide a detailed guideline for depositing high-quality Cd1âˆ’xZnxS films using a low-cost CBD method.
