Abstract

In recent years, AgBiS2 has received great attention in optoelectronics thanks to its fascinating merits, including a suitable bandgap, large absorption coefficient from ultraviolet to near-infrared and remarkable material stability. However, the poor carrier transport properties limit its further development and applications. To address this issue, we propose a composite strategy to facilitate the carrier transport of AgBiS2 QD films. The AgBiS2â€“organic hybrid films demonstrated enhanced photoconductivity. Then, we fabricated phototransistors based on these blend films, and achieved a high photosensitivity (Ilight/Idark) of 1.5 Ã— 103, a decent responsivity of up to 20 A Wâˆ’1, an excellent specific detectivity of 4 Ã— 1013 Jones under low UV illumination and remarkable device stability. These results again demonstrate the great potential of AgBiS2 for next generation solution-processed photodetectors.

Introduction

In the past few years, ternary silver bismuth sulfide (AgBiS2) has attracted great attention because of its outstanding optoelectronic properties, including a high absorption coefficient (âˆ¼105 cmâˆ’1) from ultraviolet (UV) to near-infrared (NIR), an appropriate bandgap and excellent stability.1â€“12 In addition, AgBiS2 is composed of environmentally friendly and earth-abundant elements, and is favorable for next-generation optoelectronic devices, e.g., solar cells and photodetectors. Particularly, AgBiS2 quantum dots (QDs) have been successfully introduced in thin-film photovoltaics.1,2,5,13â€“16 In 2016, Bernechea and co-workers achieved a high power conversion efficiency of 6.3% with a relatively thin active film of âˆ¼35 nm AgBiS2 QDs.1 However, when the authors increased the thickness of photoactive layers, the device performance deteriorated severely due to the poor carrier transport properties of AgBiS2 QDs, which has also been proved by other groups.2,17 Recently, Diedenhofen et al. investigated the charge carrier dynamic behavior of AgBiS2 QD films via photoinduced time-resolved microwave conductivity (TRMC), and found that the diffusion lengths of electrons and holes are only 60 nm and 150 nm respectively at room temperature.17 These results suggest that a thicker AgBiS2 QD film is not suitable for optoelectronic applications due to its limited charge carrier diffusion lengths. Hence, exploring other strategies to enhance its charge carrier transport properties is urgently required.

Blending AgBiS2 QDs with other transport materials and forming hybrid optoelectronic materials may offer an alternative approach to facilitate the charge transport.18â€“24 In 2018, Mak et al. fabricated graphene/AgBiS2 hybrid phototransistors, with graphene as the transport medium to improve the charge transport properties of AgBiS2 QDs.25 However, the dark current (âˆ¼1 mA) of these devices was extremely high due to the high conductivity of graphene. On the other hand, considering the relatively narrow bandgap and high conductivity of AgBiS2, the noise and dark current of pristine AgBiS2 based photodetectors are relatively high. To address these issues, embedding AgBiS2 QDs in a wide bandgap organic semiconductor matrix to form an organicâ€“inorganic hybrid system may offer great potential. Through this strategy, organic semiconductors with high mobility can serve as an efficient charge transport channel and AgBiS2 QDs can effectively absorb incident photons.

In this work, we introduce 2,7-dioctyl[1]benzothieno[3,2-b][1]benzothiophene (C8-BTBT) as a p-type organic charge transport layer, which has demonstrated high charge carrier mobility in organic field effect transistors (FETs).26â€“30 We first probe the dark and photoconductivity of the C8-BTBT:AgBiS2 hybrid films with various blend ratios. Then, we systematically investigate and optimize the device performance of the C8-BTBT:AgBiS2 based phototransistors, and evaluate their potential for sensitive light detection.

Experimental methods

Synthesis of AgBiS2 quantum dots

The synthesis of AgBiS2 quantum dots was similar to that of a previous report. Firstly, 386 mg Bi(OAc)3 (Alfa, 99%) and 133.6 mg Ag(OAc) (Adamas, 99%) were added into 5.3 mL oleic acid (OA, Heowns, 90%), and then reacted at 100 Â°C for at least 12 hours in an atmosphere without oxygen and moisture. Then, 1 mmol hexamethyldisilathiane (Heowns, AR) dissolved in 5 mL 1-octadecene (Macklin, 95%) was quickly injected into the mixed solution (the hot plate was turned off at the same time). AgBiS2 QDs were precipitated by adding acetone, and they were obtained by centrifugation. Then, AgBiS2 QDs were purified by successive dispersion in toluene (Sinopharm, AR) and precipitation with acetone (Sinopharm, AR). Finally, the purified AgBiS2 QDs were dispersed in toluene.

Deposition of C8-BTBT:AgBiS2 hybrid films

10 mg C8-BTBT (Rhawn, 99%) and 2 mg polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA, Sinopharm) were dissolved in 1 mL chlorobenzene (Aladdin, 99%) with vigorous stirring until clear to form a C8-BTBT solution. 20 mg AgBiS2 QDs was dispersed in 1 mL toluene to form a AgBiS2 colloidal solution. Then, C8-BTBT and AgBiS2 QDs were mixed at volume ratios of 1:1, 2:1, 4:1 and 8:1 to form C8-BTBT:AgBiS2 mixed solutions. Then, the mixed solutions were spin-coated at 5000 rpm for 30 s to form C8-BTBT:AgBiS2 hybrid films. Finally, a ligand exchange process was used to reduce the surface defects. Briefly, 1 mg mLâˆ’1 tetramethylammonium iodide (TMAI, Heowns, AR) dissolved in methanol was dropped on top of the blend films and kept for 20 s. Then, the substrates were spin-coated at 5000 rpm for 30 s. The TMAI-treated films were washed with methanol (Sinopharm, AR). Finally, the blend films were annealed at 100 Â°C for 10 min, resulting in a film thickness of âˆ¼20 nm.

Deposition of AgBiS2 QD films

AgBiS2 QD colloidal solution was deposited on the substrates by spin-coating at 2000 rpm for 20 s. Then, a ligand exchange process was carried out in the same way as that for C8-BTBT:AgBiS2 blend films. All the above processes were cycled three times. Finally, the as-prepared films were annealed at 100 Â°C for 10 min. All the above film deposition processes were carried out in air.

Device fabrication

All devices were deposited on cleaned glass substrates or indium doped tin oxide (ITO) substrates (Meijingyuan Glass). Glass or ITO substrates were firstly washed with detergent solution, and then cleaned with deionized water (DI) water, isopropanol (Sinopharm, AR) and ethanol (Sinopharm, AR) with ultrasonification, respectively. Then the substrates were dried by compressed N2, and treated with O3 plasma for 10 min to remove the residual organics. Photoconductor architecture: the blend films or AgBiS2 QD films were directly deposited on glass substrates, and 60 nm Ag electrodes were evaporated through mask to form photoconductors with a channel length of 100 Î¼m. Photodiode architecture: 100 mg mLâˆ’1 Zn(OAc)2 (Aladdin, 99.9%) dissolved in 2-methoxyethanol (Sinopharm, AR) was deposited on the ITO substrates by spin-coating at 3000 rpm for 30 s, followed by a thermal annealing process at 200 Â°C for 30 min to form ZnO electron transport layers (ETLs). Then, AgBiS2 QDs were deposited on the ZnO layers. 5 mg mLâˆ’1 PTB7 (Luminescence Technology) dissolved in chlorobenzene was spin-coated on AgBiS2 QD layers and annealed at 100 Â°C for 10 min in a glovebox, forming the hole transport layers (HTLs). Finally, 5 nm MoO3 and 120 nm Ag were evaporated as an interfacial modification layer and electrode, respectively. The device area was 0.1 cm2. Phototransistor architecture: 100 nm Cu and 280 nm Ta2O5 were deposited on the glass substrates as gate electrodes and dielectric layers successively via magnetron sputtering. Then, 60 mg mLâˆ’1 PAN (Macklin) dissolved in DMF (Sigma-Aldrich, 99.9%) was deposited on top of Ta2O5 by spin-coating at 2000 rpm for 40 s as the second dielectric layer. The blend films were deposited on top of samples to form active layers. Finally, 5 nm MoO3 and 60 nm Ag were evaporated on the blend films as interfacial modification layers and source-drain electrodes, respectively. The channel width and length were kept at 2 mm and 100 Î¼m, respectively.

Characterization

The X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were recorded with a D8 Advance X-ray diffractometer. A Keithley 2400 source meter was used to test the Jâ€“V curves for photodiodes under the illumination of an AAA solar simulator (San-Ei, XES-50S2). The optical properties of films were recorded with an UV-vis-NIR spectrophotometer (PerkinElmer, Lamda1050). The electrical performances for photoconductors and phototransistors were collected using a Keithley semiconductor analyzer (B1500A).

Results and discussion

Prior to the device fabrication, we focus on the preparation and characterization of AgBiS2â€“organic hybrid films. Fig. 1a schematically illustrates the deposition of the blend films and the ligand exchange process. Fig. 1b displays the XRD pattern of the obtained AgBiS2 QD films. Characteristic peaks of AgBiS2 crystals can be observed clearly, which is consistent with the reference (PDF#21-1178) and previous reports, indicating excellent crystallinity. The absorption spectra of AgBiS2, C8-BTBT and their hybrid films deposited on glass substrates are shown in Fig. 1c. Interestingly, AgBiS2 QD films possess a wide absorption range and high absorbance, covering the whole range from UV to NIR. C8-BTBT films present the absorption edge at âˆ¼370 nm and the main absorption peak located at âˆ¼360 nm, which is in line with the wide bandgap nature of this organic semiconductor. For the blend films, the absorption spectrum exhibited both features, suggesting excellent compatibility. Furthermore, we also tested the photoluminescence (PL) spectra of the pristine C8-BTBT and hybrid films as shown in Fig. S1 (ESIâ€ ). We observed a remarkable reduction of the PL intensity, compared with pristine C8-BTBT films, indicating efficient PL quenching, i.e., improved charge extraction, which could effectively result in an improved photocurrent, in terms of device performance. To investigate the distribution of the AgBiS2 QDs in the hybrid films, we performed surface morphology analysis with scanning electron microscopy (SEM). We can clearly observe bright grains from the SEM image as shown in Fig. S2a (ESIâ€ ), which is correlated with the higher reflected electrons due to the larger atomic number of AgBiS2. In addition, we also recorded the energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) profiles of these SEM samples, and found a relatively uniform distribution of Ag, Bi and S in the hybrid films as shown in Fig. S2bâ€“e (ESIâ€ ).

Having successfully obtained AgBiS2 QD and hybrid films, we then turn to the electrical characterization of these thin films as shown in Fig. 2. We first evaluated the conductance (G) of the pure AgBiS2 QD thin films in the dark and under illumination in a wide temperature range from âˆ’40 Â°C to 100 Â°C, as shown in Fig. 2a. It can be observed that the pristine AgBiS2 QDs possess relatively high dark conductivity when the channel length was 100 Î¼m, and it increased almost two orders of magnitude with the increase of temperature from âˆ’40 Â°C to 100 Â°C. This can be attributed to the relatively high trap density and increased carrier concentration by thermal excitation. However, the AgBiS2 QDs do not have noticeable photoconductivity under a white LED with an intensity of 15 mW cmâˆ’2, which is not common for intrinsic semiconductors. The low or negligible photocurrent could be caused by either low photo-generation or poor charge transport caused by the high trap density. Then, we fabricated vertical layered photodiodes based on AgBiS2 QDs, ZnO and poly[4,8-bis(5-(2-ethylhexyl)thiophen-2-yl)benzo[1,2-b;4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene-2,6-diyl-alt-((4-(2-ethylhexyl)-3-fluorothieno[3,4-b]thiophene-)-2-carboxylate-2-6-diyl)] (PTB7) as electron and hole transport layers, respectively. The Jâ€“V curves of the fabricated photodiodes are depicted in Fig. 2b. The inset shows a typical nâ€“iâ€“p structure of the AgBiS2 QD based photodiodes. Again, we tested them both in the dark and under white light illumination, and found that these photodiodes presented decent photocurrent, which increased with the increase of incident light intensity linearly, as shown in Fig. 2c. This result suggests that the photo-generation within the AgBiS2 QDs should not be a problem. It is also worth noting that the active layer of the AgBiS2 QDs is around 30 nm, which is extremely thin and should not limit the charge transport. However, for the lateral photoconductors, the charge transport length is 100 Î¼m, which is a few orders of magnitude longer than the diffusion lengths of the electrons and holes reported by Diedenhofen et al. to be 60 nm and 150 nm respectively at room temperature.

Based on the above analysis, it is crucial to reduce the dark conductivity and increase the charge transport of the AgBiS2 thin films. In this work, we introduce C8-BTBT with relatively high mobility as the p-type organic matrix to facilitate the charge transport,26,31,32 and AgBiS2 QDs can effectively absorb photons. Such hybrid C8-BTBT:AgBiS2 thin films could offer synergistic effects and possess both merits of these two materials. We first optimized the blending ratio of the composite films. Fig. 2d presents the thin film conductance both in the dark and under illumination of the hybrid films with various blending ratios at room temperature, and the ratio of Glight and Gdark is shown in Fig. 2e. Intriguingly, the dark conductivity decreases significantly with the increase of C8-BTBT content, due to the wide bandgap nature and poor conductivity of this organic small molecule. However, the photoconductivity increased remarkably when the blending ratio was 2:1. The highest Glight/Gdark reached up to âˆ¼4 Ã— 103 when the light intensity was 3 mW cmâˆ’2 under a 365 nm LED. This could be attributed to the excellent charge transport properties of the C8-BTBT matrix. With the further increase of the organic contents, the photoconductivity decreased continually. This is mainly caused by the decreased photo-generation due to the decrease of absorption, as the concentration of AgBiS2 QDs in the blend films is highly diluted. Furthermore, we also tested the conductance of the optimized 2:1 blend films at the temperature range of âˆ’40 Â°Câ€“100 Â°C. The photocurrent was measured under a green LED (535 nm) with an intensity of 5 mW cmâˆ’2, and we observed decent photon induced conductivity as shown in Fig. 2f. Compared with the pristine AgBiS2 QDs, the conductivity of the blend films exhibited less temperature-dependency, which is promising for photodetection in real applications.

To further increase the responsivity of the AgBiS2 QD based photoconductors and achieve a controllable gain, we introduce the C8-BTBT:AgBiS2 hybrid films to fabricate phototransistors. The device architecture is depicted in Fig. 3a. From the bottom to the top of the device, we used Cu as the gate electrode. A Ta2O5 and polyacrylonitrile (PAN) double layer is adopted as the dielectric layer, and the blend films work as both the photosensitive layer and the charge transport layer within the phototransistors. MoO3 is an interfacial modification layer to improve the hole injection and Ag is the source and drain electrode. It is worth noting that we did not use the conventional Si substrates, and replaced the SiO2 dielectric layers with Ta2O5/PAN layers, to rule out the influence of the photoconductivity from the Si substrates. To demonstrate the photoresponse of the Si substrates, we fabricated zinc doped tin oxide (ZTO) based TFT devices, and tested them under white light illumination as shown in Fig. S3a (ESIâ€ ). To eliminate the photosensitivity of ZTO, we filtered the white light with a 450 nm long-pass filter. In principle, wide bandgap ZTO should not have any absorption in the visible region as shown in Fig. S3b (ESIâ€ ). Then, the photoconductivity of TFT should be accounted to the Si substrates. For comparison, we also fabricated AgBiS2â€“organic hybrid phototransistors based on SiO2/Si substrates. The transfer curves are depicted as shown in Fig. S3c (ESIâ€ ). It can be observed that the SiO2/Si based devices did not present satisfactory photoresponsivity. Hence, we used glass as a substrate to eliminate the influence of the photoconductivity of Si substrates, and replace the dielectric layer with Ta2O5/PAN, which also possesses a high dielectric constant and break-down voltage.

Conclusions

In summary, a high mobility C8-BTBT organic semiconductor was introduced to facilitate the charge transport of AgBiS2 QD based thin films. The photoconductivity of the C8-BTBT:AgBiS2 hybrid films was systematically investigated in a wide range of temperatures from âˆ’40 Â°C to 100 Â°C. Comparing with the results of AgBiS2 QD based photodiodes, we carefully excluded the effect of photo-generation, and pointed out that the device performance was mainly limited by the poor charge transport. Based on the optimized blend films, we further fabricated photodetectors with a transistor architecture. After optimization, the phototransistors presented a wide response range from UV to NIR, excellent device stability, a high photosensitivity of 1.5 Ã— 103 and a decent responsivity of up to 20 A Wâˆ’1. Benefiting from the extremely low dark current and noise, the devices reached an ultrahigh specific detectivity of 4 Ã— 1013 Jones under UV illumination, which is almost four orders of magnitude higher than that in a previous report, indicating great potential for next generation solution-processed photodetectors.
