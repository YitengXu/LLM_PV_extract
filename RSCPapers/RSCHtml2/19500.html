Abstract

In this paper, a sequence of Zn-modified Co@Nâ€“C composites are designed and developed via the direct annealing of Znâ€“Co bimetallic zeolitic imidazolate framework materials with different Co/Zn ratios and at different pyrolysis temperatures. As proved by the various measurements made, the size of the Co particles in the composites could be successfully adjusted from 18 nm to 6 nm. In addition, with a decrease in particle size the Co particles become covered by carbon layers, and the specific surface area and number of Coâ€“Nâ€“C bonds are successfully increased. As a result, the current density and CO faradaic efficiency (FE(CO)%) in the electrochemical CO2 reduction reaction gradually improved (from 1.8 to 4.2 mA cmâˆ’2 for current density and from 25% to 60% for FE(CO)%) with a decrease in the Co particle size. Meanwhile, the ratio of H2/CO in the obtained syngas could be regulated in the range 5:1 to 1:1.5. The Znâ€“Co@Nâ€“C-25 composite displayed optimal activity, and there was no obvious decrease in catalytic performance, even after 40 h, implying excellent stability for the Znâ€“Co@Nâ€“C catalyst.

1. Introduction

Syngas, made up of CO and H2, is a fundamental building block for the production of synfuel and industrial chemicals via well-established industrial technologies.1â€“3 For example, optimal H2/CO ratios of 1.5â€“2.2 are commonly desired for the synthesis of methanol and Fischerâ€“Tropsch reactions; syngas with a H2/CO ratio of 3 is typically used in the methanation reaction; and a H2/CO ratio of 1 is generally used for fine chemical synthesis and hydroformylation.4â€“6 At present, syngas with a tunable H2/CO ratio is mainly obtained from coal gasification and natural gas reforming, which uses nonrenewable fuels.7â€“10 Electroreduction of CO2 (CO2RR) to syngas, which uses CO2 and H2O as ideal carbon and hydrogen sources, not only serves as a clean and efficient syngas production technology, but also can be easily coupled to other intermittent energy sources, such as wind, solar and tidal power.11â€“18

Currently, different catalysts have been used to obtain syngas via CO2RR. Sheng et al. synthesized a carbon-supported palladium catalyst for CO2RR to syngas, and the H2/CO ratio was found to be between 2:1 to 1:1 under âˆ’0.5 to âˆ’0.6 V vs. RHE.19 A Co3O4â€“CDâ€“C3N4 (CD, carbon dot) three-component electrocatalyst was also designed and used for CO2 reduction to syngas, and the H2/CO ratio of the syngas produced was tunable from 0.07 to 4:1 by controlling the potential from âˆ’0.3 to âˆ’0.8 V vs. RHE.20 Wu and co-workers employed porous palladium nanomesh catalysts for electrochemical conversion of CO2 into syngas, and tunable syngas H2/CO ratios from 2:1 to 1:1 could be achieved from âˆ’0.5 to âˆ’0.9 V vs. RHE.21 In these cases, adjustable H2/CO ratios of syngas were obtained through change in the applied potential. However, the ability to adjust the ratios of syngas through regulation of the catalyst is of great importance and should be investigated.22â€“24

Metalâ€“nitrogen (Mâ€“N) active sites anchored within a carbon material (Mâ€“Nâ€“C), which could provide the active sites for CO2 and could easily transfer electrons, have been selected as a new type of efficient CO2RR catalyst in recent years.25â€“32 For synthesis of the Mâ€“Nâ€“C composites an annealing process is typically needed. In this process, the metal species aggregate easily into large particles, and the size of these particles is uncontrolled. More importantly, the particle sizes have a significant influence on the Mâ€“N bonds and on the catalytic performance in CO2RR.33 Therefore, how to control particle size is an important consideration for these Mâ€“Nâ€“C composites.

Herein, we introduce the design of a Zn-to-Co-based zeolitic imidazolate framework (ZIF-67) to obtain Znâ€“Co bimetallic ZIF materials and to use the Zn species to slow down aggregation of the Co species during the annealing process. The influence of Co/Zn ratios and pyrolysis temperature on the size of the Co particles is investigated. Coâ€“Zn@Nâ€“C composites with different Co particle sizes are also employed as catalysts for CO2RR. The relationship between Co particle size, structure, chemical bonds, and catalytic performance is discussed in detail. The Znâ€“Co@Nâ€“C-25 composite displayed optimal activity for CO2RR, and there was no decrease in catalytic performance after 40 hours, implying excellent stability for this Znâ€“Co@Nâ€“C catalyst.

4. Conclusions

In this study we have developed a series of Znâ€“Co@Nâ€“C composites, and the Co particle size in these materials was successfully adjusted via controlling the Co/Zn ratios and pyrolysis temperatures. In addition, the existing states of the different components in the materials were investigated. For the catalytic performance in the CO2RR, the current density and FE(CO)% values of Znâ€“Co@Nâ€“C gradually improved with a decrease in the Co particle size (from 25% to 60% for FE(CO)% and from 1.8 to 4.2 mA cmâˆ’2 for current density).
