Abstract

Bipolar membranes are one of the attractive candidates for water electrolysis applications, because they enable hydrogen and oxygen evolution reactions to take place under their optimal pH conditions, thereby minimizing the overall cell voltage for water splitting. This study demonstrates the preparation of bipolar membranes using dual-sulfonated poly(arylene ether ketone) as a cation exchange layer and butyl-N3-substituted imidazolium-functionalized poly(arylene ether ketone) as an anion exchange layer. The synthesized bipolar membrane (BPM) was employed in an acidâ€“alkaline dual-electrolyte water electrolysis system as a separator to restrain bulk neutralization. Results indicate that this BPM promoted water spitting reactions between the membranes at an applied voltage of 2.31 V and a current density of 160 mA cmâˆ’2 in 1 M H2SO4 and 2 M KOH without any deposition of water dissociation catalysts at room temperature. The BPM synthesized in this work also exhibited excellent chemical stability by experiencing a weight loss less than 3.4% in both strong acids and bases after 28 days. These results indicate that this BPM is feasible to be applied in the novel type acidâ€“alkaline water electrolysis system to produce hydrogen energy.

1. Introduction

Water electrolysis has become a promising technology for hydrogen production owing to its several distinct features including simple processing, high abundance of water resources and zero emission of greenhouse gases.1â€“3 Generally, there are two types of water electrolysis: (1) proton exchange membrane (PEM) water electrolysis that utilizes a highly acidic PEM as a solid polymer electrolyte to conduct H+ ions from the anode to cathode, (2) alkaline water electrolysis that uses a concentrated aqueous alkaline solution to conduct OHâˆ’ ions. In this case, electrodes are separated by a porous diaphragm.4â€“6 Anion exchange polymer membranes (AEM) are also currently developed for water electrolysis applications, but they are still at the research and development (R&D) stage.6 The theoretical thermodynamic cell voltage to decompose 1 mole of water molecule is 1.23 V at 25 Â°C and 1 atm. However, an additional amount of voltage is required in the actual water electrolyzer to overcome the kinetics and ohmic resistance in the system, in particular, the high overpotential of hydrogen evolution reactions (HERs) at the cathode and oxygen evolution reactions (OERs) at the anode.2,6â€“8 HER and HER electrocatalysts are known to be kinetically and thermodynamically favorable in an acidic medium, while OER and OER electrocatalysts perform the best in an alkaline solution.9â€“11 Because of this common fact, a PEM water electrolyzer usually suffers from the slow acidic OER and high incorporation cost of platinum group metal (PGM) catalysts due to the instability of non-noble catalysts in a highly corrosive environment. In contrast, alkaline water electrolysis that allows the use of earth-abundant catalysts has the disadvantage of low energy efficiency at high current density associated with the sluggish kinetics of HERs in an alkaline medium.1,12

The employment of bipolar membranes has been proposed in dual-electrolyte water electrolysis systems, as it offers distinct advantages that could overcome the drawbacks in the conventional single-electrolyte water electrolyzer. A bipolar membrane comprises a cation selective layer with fixed negative charges permeable to only cations and an anion exchange layer with positive charges that allows the migration of only anions. The bipolar membrane has been traditionally used for electrodialysis to recover acids and bases from aqueous salt solutions.13â€“16 Recently, many studies have been intensively carried out on bipolar membranes for new applications such as self-humidification fuel cells,17,18 CO2 electrolysis,19,20 electrochemical water splitting10,21 and photoelectrochemical water splitting,22â€“24 and reassuring results were obtained. When a bipolar membrane is incorporated into a water electrolysis system, it combines the advantages of both PEM and AEM electrolyzers by enabling HERs to preferentially take place in the acidic cathode and OERs in the basic electrode, respectively, thereby lowering the overall overpotential of the cell.9,22 It also mitigates the stringent catalyst selection for HERs and OERs, which is limited by the single pH environment in a conventional water electrolysis system.16,21,25,26

The operating principles of the bipolar membrane in different devices are schematically illustrated in Fig. 1. The bipolar membrane can operate under two conditions: either under forward or reverse bias condition. Water electrolysis is performed by a bipolar membrane under the reverse bias condition, where the cation exchange layer is positioned next to the cathodic electrode for HERs and the anion exchange layer faces the anodic electrode for OERs.22 Under the reverse bias condition, huge electric field is created in the interfacial layer to dissociate water molecules into H+ and OHâˆ’. H+ ions migrate through the proton exchange membrane towards the cathode and OHâˆ’ ions permeate the anion exchange membrane and move towards the anode. When OHâˆ’ ions reach the surface of the anode, the hydroxide ions will be oxidized into O2 by releasing electrons. At the cathode side, H+ ions accept electrons from the anode and are reduced into H2 gas. The H+ and OHâˆ’ ions consumed for HERs and OERs are continuously replaced by the H+ and OHâˆ’ ions produced in the bipolar membrane interface. An ideal bipolar membrane should have equal consumption and generation of H+ and OHâˆ’ at the electrodes and the interface in order to maintain the pH gradient arising from the two electrolytes of different pH values.16

Water dissociation reactions at the bipolar membrane interface occur via a proton-deprotonation process, which is catalyzed by weak acids/bases in the ion exchange membranes.22,27â€“29 It is suggested that the water splitting reaction takes place preferentially in anion exchange membranes because it contains primary, secondary, and tertiary amines that show catalytic activity towards the water dissociation reaction.30,31 When the PEM and AEM are sandwiched together to form a bipolar membrane, a thin intermediate region in between the two membranes is necessary to minimize charge-carrier depletion as well as to promote rapid water dissociation into hydrogen and hydroxyl ions. The structure of the junction and fixed charge densities in the respective ion exchange membranes are imperative in determining the performance of the bipolar membrane.22,23

In the present work, we developed a new type of hydrocarbon-based bipolar membrane composed of a pendant dual-sulfonated poly(arylene ether ketone) (PAEK-SDPA) proton exchange membrane (PEM) and a N3-butyl-substituted imidazolium-based PAEK (PAEK-APBI) anion exchange membrane (AEM) for water electrolysis applications. Poly(arylene ether ketone) (PAEK) was employed as the main polymer backbone material to synthesize ion exchange membranes for its cost-effectiveness, and outstanding thermal, mechanical, and chemical stability, owing to the presence of the rigid aromatic ring in the chemical structure.32â€“35 PAEK-SDPA was chosen as the PEM because it exhibits thermal stability, mechanical robustness and high proton conductivity.36 However, the selection of PAEK-APBI as the AEM was ascribed to its comparably good anion conductivity as well as the enhanced alkaline stability of the imidazolium cations by the butyl substituent at the N3 position of the imidazolium ring.34 Additionally, PAEK-APBI contains an imidazole group that is known for its catalytic activity in water dissociation reactions. The bipolar membrane was prepared by a simple hot-pressing method. The properties of the bipolar membrane including water uptake, swelling ratio, chemical and thermal stability, and mechanical durability were investigated in detail. Additionally, water electrolysis of the cell that involves both acids and bases was conducted on the bipolar membrane to study its feasibility to be used as an alternative to the conventional monopolar membrane water electrolysis system.

2.2 Preparation of membranes

2.2.1 Preparation of PAEK-SDPA. Sulfonated 3-3-diphenylpropylamine (SDPA) PAEK backbone with carboxylic acid at the pendant site (PAEK-COOH) and that of pendant SDPA (PAEK-SDPA) were prepared according to the procedure published previously in the literature.36 PAEK-COOH was first synthesized from the step-condensation polymerization process between 4,4â€²-difluorobenzophenone, 4,4-bis(4-hydroxyphenyl)-valeric acid and bisphenol A. Bisphenol A was added to control the hydrophobic segment of PAEK. The molar ratio of bisphenol A (n) and 4,4-bis(4-hydroxyphenyl)-valeric acid (m) was fixed at n:m = 0.2:0.8. SDPA was synthesized via the sulfonation process of 3-3-diphenylpropylamine (DPA) with concentrated sulfuric acid. SDPA was then grafted to the carboxylic group of the PAEK backbone under the reaction catalyzed by TBTU and DIPEA. Then, 1.6 g of PAEK was completely dissolved in 16 mL of DMAc in a 80 mL vial. Following this, 1.3 g of TBTU in 7 mL of DMSO was added into the PAEK solution under vigorous stirring at room temperature. After 4 h of stirring, 0.86 g of SDPA in 7 mL of DMSO was added into the reaction mixture and stirred for 30 min. To prevent gelation, 1.3 mL of DIPEA was slowly dropped into the reaction mixture. After 48 h of reaction, the polymer solution was fed into the dialysis tubing and stirred in a beaker filled with 3 L of DI water for 3 days to eliminate unwanted impurities. The pure polymer was obtained after freeze-drying. The synthetic route of PAEK-SDPA is shown in Fig. S1.â€ 
2.2.2 Preparation of PAEK-APBI. A butyl-N3-substituted PAEK-imidazolium-functionalized membrane (PAEK-APBI) was prepared according to the synthesis method outlined in our previously reported work.34 PAEK-COOH was functionalized with the NHS group to form PAEK-NHS, involving the coupling reagent of DCC. PAEK-APBI was synthesized through the amidation reaction of PAEK-NHS and 1-(3-aminopropyl)-imidazole (API). To prepare the polymer solution, 0.3 g of PAEK-NHS was dissolved in 5 mL of DMAc solvent until a homogeneous solution was formed. The solution was then mixed with 125 mol% of API and continuously stirred at room temperature for 6 h to obtain API-grafted PAEK. Finally, the imidazole group of the polymer was quaternized by adding bromobutane (2 equiv. of API) into the solution, followed by stirring at 70 Â°C for 48 h. The synthetic scheme of PAEK-APBI is depicted in Fig. S2.â€ 
2.2.3 Preparation of the bipolar membrane (BPM). The bipolar membrane that consists of PAEK-SDPA as the cation exchange layer and PAEK-APBI as the anion exchange layer was prepared by a hot-pressing method. First, 0.3 g of PAEK-SDPA was dissolved in 6 mL of DMSO and cast on a Petri dish. The solution was dried in an oven at 80 Â°C for 24 h. The membrane formed was peeled off from the Petri dish and pre-treated with 1 M HCl for ion-exchange to H+. The PAEK-APBI solution prepared earlier was cast onto a Petri dish and dried in an oven at 80 Â°C for 24 h. The membrane was immersed in 1 M NaOH for 24 h to convert Brâˆ’ into OHâˆ’. The two membranes were rinsed several times with deionized (DI) water to remove the excess H+ and OHâˆ’ ions and completely dried in an oven before they adhered together. After that, the two pre-formed membranes were placed together and hot-pressed at 100 Â°C under 10 MPa for 5 min. The thickness of the bipolar membrane was around 170â€“190 Î¼m. The membrane was stored in DI water prior to electrochemical testing. This fabrication process did not involve any water dissociation catalyst as the interfacial layer.

3.3 Water uptake and swelling ratio

Water uptake and swelling ratio are other two essential properties that evaluate the bipolar membrane performance in water electrolysis. Water uptake of the ion exchange membrane reflects the membrane hydrophilicity, and it plays a very important role to conduct ions in the membrane. An appropriate amount of water in the membrane is needed for the facile ion migration via the membrane to prevent ohmic loss.38 As depicted in Fig. 5(a) and (b), the BPM was able to retain higher water uptake than the PEM and AEM without too much swelling. Under hydrated conditions, water molecules tend to diffuse through the bipolar membrane and interact with the charged interface due to the highly polar nature of both water molecules and the bipolar membrane. Furthermore, the water swelling behavior of the BPM could be suppressed by the low swelling degree of the AEM, the thickness of which was higher than that of the PEM. This explained why BPM has the highest water content among the three membranes but retained its swelling below 22% even at high temperatures.
Fig. 5 (a) Water uptake and (b) swelling percentage of the AEM, PEM and BPM at different temperatures.

3.4 Ionic conductivity

Ionic conductivity of the membranes is a crucial parameter to determine the bipolar membrane's performance in water electrolysis because it directly affects the efficiency of H+ and OHâˆ’ migration from the bipolar membrane interface to the cation and anion selective layers when electric field is applied. A high ion transport rate in the membrane is very critical for fast HERs and OERs, as well as low voltage drop in the membrane. As shown in Fig. 6, both the proton and hydroxide ion conductivity of the PEM and AEM gradually increased with the increase in temperature. High temperatures enlarge the free volume, and thus, increase the water absorbance and ionic conductivity of the membrane.34,39 The proton conductivity of PEM was 0.0581 S cmâˆ’1 and 0.1146 S cmâˆ’1 at 30 Â°C and 80 Â°C, respectively, while the anion conductivity of AEM was 0.0049 S cmâˆ’1 and 0.0256 S cmâˆ’1 at the same temperatures, respectively. The anion conductivity of the AEM was lower than that of the PEM because the hydroxide ion mobility is generally slower than that of protons.40

3.6 Chemical stability

Chemical stability is one of the crucial criteria in determining the long-term operation of the bipolar membrane because it is exposed to both highly acidic and basic solutions in the water electrolysis system. The proton exchange membrane should be able to withstand extremely low pH solutions, while the anion exchange membrane has to possess superior stability in high pH solutions.42 The IEC loss rate was used to evaluate the chemical stability of the PEM and AEM in strong acids and bases. As displayed in Fig. 8(a), the PEM and AEM were capable of retaining 96% and 90% of their initial IEC value under harsh pH conditions.
Fig. 8 (a) IEC variation of the PEM and AEM and (b) weight loss of the prepared BPM after immersion in 4 M H2SO4 and 4 M KOH for 28 days.

Apart from that, the chemical stability of the BPM was examined from its weight loss percentage in concentrated acidic and alkaline solutions at room temperature against time, as illustrated in Fig. 8. Chemical stability comparisons of the BPM from this study with the bipolar membranes in other studied reported in the literature were also done. EBPM-2 reported by Pan et al.43 has lost 1.85% and 1.33% of weight after aging in 1 M HCl and 1 M NaOH for 7 days. BPM-GO reported by Manohar et al.15 had a weight loss of 7.8% and 9.9% after immersion in 5 M HCl and 5 M NaOH for only 1 day. In this study, BPM experienced total weight loss of only 2.20% and 3.33% in 4 M H2SO4 and 4 M KOH respectively after 28 days of experiment. It was assured that our BPM exhibits excellent chemical stability in strong acids and bases.

3.7 Water electrolysis performance

3.7.1 Comparison of water electrolysis performance of the AEM, PEM and BPM. To study and compare the water electrolysis performance of monopolar and bipolar membranes under different electrolyte combinations, electrochemical testing was conducted on the AEM, PEM and BPM at different temperatures from 25 Â°C to 80 Â°C. The amount of catalyst loading at the anode and cathode for all cases was kept constant at 0.4 mg cmâˆ’2 of Pt/C. Water electrolysis experiments of the 3 above-mentioned membranes were performed with the following electrolyte pairs and concentrations: a single alkaline electrolyte separated by the AEM (2 M KOHâ€–AEMâ€–2 M KOH); a single acidic electrolyte separated by the PEM (1 M H2SO4â€–PEMâ€–1 M H2SO4); an acidic catholyte and an alkaline anolyte separated by the BPM (1 M H2SO4â€–BPMâ€–2 M KOH).

According to the water electrolysis performance illustrated in Fig. 9, the membranes can be arranged in the order of increasing cell voltage as BPM < PEM < AEM at all temperature to meet the same current density. Under similar catalyst conditions but different pH environments, the AEM in an alkaline medium showed lower performance than the PEM in an acidic medium due to several reasons: (1) excessive energy is required to break the Hâ€“Oâ€“H bond in the water dissociation step in alkaline HERs9,12 and (2) low efficiencies of the acidic medium-based catalyst (Pt/C) in a basic solution that requires high overpotentials to activate the catalyst.12,44 The water electrolysis voltage for the 3 above-mentioned membranes gradually decreased with the increase in temperature due to the decreasing overpotential of the cell at higher temperatures.

It is worth noting that the BPM with the use of dual-pH electrolyte configuration exhibited the lowest external voltage for water splitting in comparison with the monopolar PEM and AEM in a single pH electrolyte solution of either acid or alkaline nature. This can be attributed to the particular arrangement that allows facile proton reduction and hydroxide ion oxidation reactions in an acidic and a basic environment, respectively, which significantly minimize the total potential loss in the system.9 When the OER takes place in an alkaline environment, a lot of OHâˆ’ are readily available in the KOH solution for O2 generation. OHâˆ’ will be oxidized into oxygen and water molecules by giving out electrons. However, the elementary step of acidic OERs is different from alkaline OERs. H2O that acts as the reactant will attach to the catalyst active sites, and additional energy is required to dissociate H2O to form OH* before yielding O* intermediates. Then, H2O will attack O* to produce HOO* intermediates, which are desorbed from the electrode surface to produce O2.45

HER occurs more easily in an acidic medium because H2SO4 can provide abundance of H+ for the reduction process. In acids, H+ is reduced to a H* intermediate when it is adsorbed onto the active site of the electrode surface. H2 molecules will evolve either via (1) proton and electron transfer process of H*, or (2) recombination of the two adsorbed H* on the active sites.46 On the contrary, in alkaline HERs, H+ is absent and only H2O is available at the cathode to be reduced into H2. H2O binding onto the electrode surface in alkalis is known to be poorer than H+ adsorption in acids. Moreover, the strong covalent Oâ€“Hâ€“O bond has to be broken prior to the H* adsorption process. Due to these demerits, the water dissociation kinetics of the alkaline HER is two orders of magnitude lower than that of the acidic HER, leading to a higher overpotential in the alkaline HER.12,44 The fundamental mechanisms of oxygen and hydrogen evolution reactions in different pH environments are summarized in Table 2 for a better understanding of the water electrolysis performance in different medias.

3.7.2 Effect of anode electrocatalysts. To further investigate the influence of non-noble OER electrocatalysts on the water electrolysis performance, a commercial nickel foam was introduced into the system to replace Pt/C as the anodic catalyst in an alkaline solution. The nickel (Ni) foam has been widely used as a current collector as well as a catalyst in alkaline water electrolysis owing to its several advantages such as low cost, high porosity, good conductivity, and excellent chemical stability in the alkaline medium.47,48 Most importantly, the incorporation of the Ni foam into the water electrolysis system could eliminate the use of expensive noble metal catalysts, which can directly reduce the overall hydrogen production cost. Due to the instability of the Ni foam in acidic solutions, water electrolysis performance with the Ni foam was only accessed on the AEM, BPM and commercial Fumasep FBM. Meanwhile, the other conditions like the amount of cathodic catalyst loading and the electrolyte combination remained unchanged. It is worth mentioning that the AEM water electrolysis performance became fivefold higher than the previous performance with the Pt (C)//Pt (A) catalyst pair. The low external voltage maybe associated with the 3D conductive frameworks and macroporous structure of the Ni foam, which can provide a larger catalytic active surface area to facilitate OERs in the alkaline medium.48 As displayed in Fig. 10(a), the voltage to achieve current densities of 20, 60 and 100 mA cmâˆ’2 at 25 Â°C for the BPM was greatly reduced from previous 2.80, 5.31, and 6.55 V to 1.59, 1.89, and 2.07 V respectively. Furthermore, in comparison with the BPM, Fumasep FBM displayed a higher external voltage of 1.71, 2.06, and 2.27 V to afford current densities of 20, 60 and 100 mA cmâˆ’2. The BPM in this study showed better performance than the AEM and commercial Fumasep FBM. This could be ascribed to the minimized interfacial resistance and also excellent ionic conductivity of both the individual ion exchange layers in the BPM that allowed ions to be transported at fast rates, although the water dissociation catalyst was not introduced into the bipolar membrane. A low applied voltage in water electrolysis is highly desirable because this will reduce the energy consumption in the hydrogen generation process.

3.7.3 Stability of the BPM-based cell. To evaluate the BPM-based cell stability, a chronopotentiometry test was performed at a fixed current density of 20 mA cmâˆ’2 at room temperature. As shown in Fig. 11(a), the water electrolysis voltage of BPM only slightly increased from 1.59 V to 1.65 V after 12 h of testing time. The slight voltage increment may be associated with a minor crossover of the K+ and SO42âˆ’ co-ions in the electrolytes.16 The overall performance remained stable at 1.62 Â± 0.03 V without huge polarization being observed. This result indicated that the BPM in the dual-electrolyte water electrolyzer was able to maintain the pH gradient formed between the cathode and anode chambers. The membrane before and after water electrolysis is also displayed in Fig. 11(b). The membrane surface was smooth and clear before water electrolysis. After the electrochemical test for 12 h, the membrane became slightly dented out due to the force exerted by the gasket and electrode, when the electrolyte passed through the cell, but the blistering effect was absent in the bipolar membrane. Blisters can be formed from the gas trapped in between the membrane layers when they are not mechanically stable enough.14 In addition, the photos of the bipolar membrane after immersion in deionized water for 14 days were taken, which are shown in Fig. 12. From Fig. 12, it is confirmed that delamination and shrinking did not occur at the interface of the bipolar membrane even though it was made of two different types of ion exchange membranes with different water absorption and swelling degrees. This further indicates the excellent compatibility of the two membranes, owing to the similar polymeric backbone structures as well as the strong electrostatic interaction generated in the interface from the two membranes with opposite charges.

4. Conclusion

In this study, a new bipolar membrane composed of a PAEK-based dual-sulfonic acid-functionalized cation exchange layer and a N3-butyl-substituted imidazolium-functionalized anion exchange layer has been successfully fabricated by a hot-pressing method. The bipolar membrane prepared was explored in the novel asymmetric acidâ€“alkaline water electrolysis system, which was favorable for acidic HERs and alkaline OERs. Benefiting from the pH-differential operating condition, our BPM exhibited the lowest cell voltage to afford the same current density in comparison with the PEM and AEM in single electrolyte solutions. In addition, when water electrolysis was performed with a Pt/C (C)â€–nickel foam (A) catalyst pair, the BPM outperformed the commercial Fumasep FBM by delivering only 1.59 V of cell voltage at a current density of 20 mA cmâˆ’2. After 12 h of chronopotentiometry test, no noticeable degradation in the bipolar membrane was observed. The contact area of the two layers of BPM remained intact without delamination from each other. The outstanding chemical stability in strong acids and bases as well as good water electrolysis performance of the BPM proven in this work shows that the BPM is very promising to be applied practically in water electrolysis to produce hydrogen energy in the future.
