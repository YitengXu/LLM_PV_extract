Abstract

The availability of diverse building blocks leading to highly tunable functionality has enabled metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) to have great success in myriad applications ranging from molecular recognition to catalysis. Moreover, combining the use of their porous nature with tailored reactivity at the metal struts and organic linkers provides valuable tools for energy-related applications. An exciting new research area that is expected to be highly beneficial for energy-related MOF use is the control of MOF morphology to develop nanosheets, nanorods and nanocrystals. Nanoscale MOFs offer larger external surface areas, easier access to functional groups, and close contact with other materials in hybrid structures that result in faster electron and ion transfer, in addition to ease of fabrication into thin films. This review provides an overview of the recent development of nanoscale MOFs as pristine materials or as a component in hybrid structures for solar energy conversion, supercapacitors, batteries and fuel cells.

1. Introduction

Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) are three-dimensional crystalline porous coordination polymers derived from an inorganic building unit, often termed a node, and an organic building unit often called a linker.1 As was proposed in the original reports by Robson in 1989 (ref. 2) and 1990,3 the high tunability of the architectural components of MOFs have led to an explosion of applications involving gas storage,4â€“6 catalysis,7â€“11 molecular separation,12â€“14 drug delivery,15â€“19 sensing,20â€“25 and energy devices.26â€“31 While most of these applications will work in the laboratory regardless of MOF crystal size, the implementation of MOFs for select real-world applications require both uniform crystal size distributions, as well as small sizes themselves. Synthetically, achieving nanometer-sized MOF crystals (nanoMOFs) with uniform particle size distributions is an unsolved problem. The state-of-the-art of nanoMOF synthesis was recently reviewed by Mirkin and coworkers, in which efforts in rapid nucleation, burst nucleation, and nano-confinement were discussed.32 While the synthesis of nanoMOFs is not the focus of this review, it will be mentioned herein when appropriate during the discussion of individual examples. The well-defined uniform size and shape of the MOF nanopores has made using MOFs to obtain nanoparticles or nanostructured materials very attractive. As a result, in many articles nanoMOF terminology is used to indicate not the actual MOF size but rather the MOF's pore size. Here, we will include only nanoscale MOF applications, excluding any studies involving sacrificial nanoMOF templates.

NanoMOFs have seen the greatest success in the field of drug delivery, most notably in antitumor applications; however, widespread implementation in the larger nanotechnology fields such as nanoelectronics, nanomechanics, and nanophotonics has remained elusive. Herein we will highlight the use of MOFs in nanodevice-related applications notably for energy generation and storage. Specific examples will focus on the benefits of deploying nanometer-sized MOFs for these applications. Due to the difficulty in synthesizing true nanoscale MOFs and inconsistent reporting in the literature, it is worth noting that not all of examples described in this review will be explicitly nanoMOF materials. Every effort will be made by the authors of this review to accurately report the particle sizes of the MOFs discussed, when that information is available.

Especially, we would like to thank those whose studies are highlighted here in the nanometer-scale context. At the same time, we have to apologize for the many excellent publications we could not consider herein, simply due to the limitations of space.

2. nanoMOFs in energy devices

The world's continuing need for energy and increased pollution due to fossil fuels necessitates developing strategies for tapping into abundant and cleaner energy sources. There is high demand and an increasing collective effort to improve current methods for the harvest, storage, and conversion of energy. MOFs with their varying compositions, structures, and functions show great potential for use in energy related applications such as photovoltaic devices, supercapacitors, batteries and fuel cells, which we will subsequently review in this text.

As MOF synthesis and modification methods improve, MOF researchers uncover new methods of controlling MOF size, shape and dimensions, leading to exciting new applications involving MOF nanoparticles, nanorods and nanosheets. In many cases, nanoMOF materials offer higher external surface areas, better access to electrolytes, and active surfaces leading to an enhancement in device performance.

3. nanoMOFs for solar energy conversion

Solar energy can be utilized in photovoltaic systems either in solar cells, where the harvested light is used to generate energy, or in photocatalytic systems where it is used to generate chemical energy. In both processes, multiple components need to work synergistically to achieve efficient conversion. MOF's adaptability and ordered porous structure offer a suitable platform to study and design multi-component systems that can work together efficiently.

In the area of photovoltaics, MOFs have been extensively studied as materials for dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs). Although in comparison to other solar cell materials, power conversion efficiencies of MOF-based solar cells have been comparatively low, the lure of MOF versatility is sufficient to keep researchers aiming for improved designs. One promising development of advances in MOF design and fabrication is the relatively recent research involving their incorporation into perovskite solar cells (PSCs) and organic solar cells (OSCs), mostly to improve morphology or stability. Although photocatalysis is a vast topic, only nanoMOFs used for water splitting to produce H2 from water will be included here.

Researchers utilize nanoscale MOFs in various ways; sometimes to solve a common problem such as solution processability or to fulfil a unique requirement such as shorter distances for better diffusion. Each benefit will be mentioned in the corresponding section.

3.1. nanoMOFs in sensitized solar cells

Dye sensitized solar cells have three main components: photoanode, counter electrode and the electrolyte. Usually, pristine MOFs or MOF composites have been incorporated into dye sensitized solar cells as a part of the photoanode as light harvesting sensitizers. Most MOFs are used as sacrificial templates to obtain carbon or metal oxide-based counter electrodes as an alternative to precious metal electrodes, but a few examples of MOF-based counter electrodes have also been reported. Less frequently, MOFs have also been used in the electrolyte medium in quasi-solid DSSC, as part of the effort to develop more stable electrolytes.

Depending on the function of the component, working with nanoscale materials offers different benefits as described in the following sections.

3.1.1 nanoMOFs at the photoanode. In the photoanode, MOFs are used as sensitizers that absorb the incident light. In addition to absorbing a broad range of light from UV to near IR, a good sensitizer should have LUMO levels suitable for injecting the photoexcited electrons into the conduction band of the semiconductor (often metal oxide-based), and HOMO levels suitable for accepting electrons from the electrolyte for regeneration and completing the circuit. With their porous structure and ordered framework, MOFs offer unique opportunities in light harvesting and energy transfer. Incorporation of known light absorbers, electron acceptors or donors into MOFs either as part of the MOF structure or as guest molecules in pores makes MOFs suitable for light harvesting. In addition, the long range ordered MOF framework provides spatial organization of the sensitizing groups and offers a scaffold to support short and long range energy transfer along with better stability and lower losses in comparison to small molecule systems.26,28,31

Since 2011, MOFs have been investigated as possible materials to enhance the metal oxide semiconductor in the photoanode using a variety of fabrication methods that yielded layers, hybrid composites, anchored assemblies, and particles that are embedded or grown on the material surface.33â€“38 In most of these studies, using another component (graphene, ferrocene, carbon nanotubes) to improve upon the MOF properties such as conductivity, orientation, and charge separation enhanced cell performance.39â€“44 Since some of these studies are summarized in other reviews,30,31,45 we will focus here on recent developments regarding nanoMOFs and their application in energy-related contexts.

The highly cross-linked network structure of MOFs renders them insoluble and makes it harder to fabricate and target them into devices. At the nanometer scale MOFs can be incorporated into solutions and colloidal mixtures and be easily processed using conventional methods. Although the success of growing MOF nanocrystals on surfaces are highly case dependent, fabricating films is generally more optimized. Several methods have been developed to obtain high quality MOF-films that can be attached to different surfaces.46 As sensitizers, MOFs fabricated as nanoscale thin films are more efficient than microscale films. For efficient sensitization, the harvested light has to be transferred to the MOF/semiconductor interface. Even if there is large degree of coupling between the metal struts, the energy migration distance remains in the low nanometer scale. Thus, only the energy harvested close enough to the MOF interface can be transferred and used while the remaining energy will be thermally lost.47â€“49


Light harvesting and energy transfer. Light harvesting metal polypyridyl complexes have been incorporated in MOFs in an effort to use uniform MOF structure and rigidity to generate and direct long-range energy transfer.49â€“51 Likewise, MOFs containing metalated-porphyrins have been studied to understand and manipulate MOF structures in a way to increase energy transfer efficiency.52,53 It has been shown that structural anisotropy in MOFs can result in one- or two-dimensional energy transfer.52,54,55 Non-uniform doping or asymmetric units can enhance energy transfer in one direction and limit it in another.56,57 In addition to oriented or surface-attached fabrications, this topic is especially relevant for thin films and 2D-MOF structures where growth in one direction is suppressed. In a comparative study, Cao et al. investigated the efficiency of resonance energy transfer and exciton quenching in a MOF designed to have either 3D-network or 2D-layer structures (âˆ¼2 nm thickness), using the same functionalized benzene tris-ethyne ligand (BTE) linker but different hafnium-based struts, as shown in Fig. 1.58 Modeling of both structures revealed that due to lower chromophore connectivity and slow exciton movement (diffusion length is 1.68 times less), the overall energy transfer is less efficient in the 2D-MOF. In contrast, when the MOFs were exposed to an external quencher in solution, independent of the size of the quencher, the energy transfer was more efficient in the 2D-MOF, leading to 5 times faster quenching. Regardless of the porous structure that could accommodate small molecules, the preferred mode of adsorption was on the surface, which limited access to inner excitons in the 3D-MOF. In the 2D-MOF however, the excitons on the layers were more accessible leading to more efficient quenching, despite the slower exciton migration noted earlier. It should be pointed out here that smaller morphology such as nanocrystals would increase the outside surface area in 3D MOFs and help provide more access to outside moieties.

Most MOFs are classified as insulators. Unfortunately, improving MOF conductivity is not straightforward as it usually requires either changing the MOF structure in a way to improve electronic coupling between linkers and struts, which in-turn limits the variety of building blocks that can be used; or introducing conductive moieties into the pores, which in-turn may cause the MOF to lose its porosity and limits access to the redox mediator in the solar cell.73,74 A solution to coming up with suitable building blocks may be found in the development of advanced computational methods that can not only account for factors involving the choice of metal and linkers but also the effects of having solvent or other guest molecules in the pores, in addition to MOF morphology and chromophore alignment.73

3.1.2 nanoMOFs as cathode materials. The counter electrode in solar cells contains often a catalyst layered on a conducting oxide surface. The catalyst uses the charge carriers transferred from the external circuit to regenerate the redox mediator (e.g. Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’ in the electrolyte). Since the commonly used precious metal Pt based counter electrode is expensive, less expensive polymer-based and carbon-based counter electrodes have been investigated. MOFs have been successfully used as sacrificial templates to obtain porous carbon-based counter electrodes with a variety of dopants such as nitrogen, transition metals, metal oxides and sulfides.30,31,78 The incredible variety of MOFs provides a rich set of tools to adjust the catalytic activity and stability of the counter electrode. Less frequently, MOFs are utilized as counter electrode materials themselves. A big hurdle in more extensive MOF use is the inherent low conductivity of most MOFs. 3.2. nanoMOFs in perovskite solar cells and organic solar cells

Following the rapid rise in power conversion efficiencies, metal organic perovskite solar cell research has seen an explosion of interest in recent years. Among the most challenging issues of perovskite solar cells is stability and the accompanying concern of toxic lead exposure. Several groups have turned to MOFs in an effort to improve stability.81,82 Confining perovskite QDs in MOF nano-pores not only improved stability but also gave rise to new optoelectronic properties, leading to various new applications.83â€“85 Vinogradov et al. was the first to use MOFs as a component in a perovskite solar cell.86 Instead of a pristine nanoporous TiO2 layer, a nanocomposite of TiO2 and Ti based MIL-125 was fabricated in between the perovskite and the ITO layers as an additional photoactive scaffold. Later, Zr based MOF-25 and ZIF-8 nanocrystals were used to modify the TiO2 surface as an interlayer in order to provide an additional scaffold for improved coverage and better crystallinity without compromising perovskite access to the electron conducting TiO2 layer underneath, leading to higher power conversion efficiencies in each case.37,87,88 In a more drastic approach, Ryu et al. completely replaced the TiO2 layer with a nanocrystalline (âˆ¼6 nm diameter) Ti-MIL-125 layer (âˆ¼20 nm thickness) to serve as the electron transport layer in a nâ€“iâ€“p heterojunction solar cell.89 As shown in Fig. 7, PCBM needed to be incorporated into the Ti-MOF layer to increase electron conductivity and to efficiently block the penetration of the perovskite layer into the indium tin oxide (ITO) surface. Although the flexible device fabricated on ITO/polyethylene naphthalate (PEN) substrate performed slightly lower (17.43% PCE) than the rigid device (18.94% PCE), it was stable over 700 bending cycles with a modest performance drop to 15.43%.

3.3. nanoMOFs for evolution of H2 and O2 from water

Herein, we specifically focus on light-driven water-splitting processes, mentioning electrocatalysis only to provide a more complete discussion.

With their long-range order and diverse structures, MOFs are attractive candidates to assemble multiple synergistic components into a cooperative system that can use solar energy to derive the two half reactions of water splitting: reduction to evolve hydrogen and oxidation to evolve oxygen. Although sometimes studied together, hydrogen evolution reactions (HER) are easier to derive, thus more commonly explored. Photocatalytic hydrogen evolution processes usually utilize a photosensitizer to capture the light and form a photo-excited state, then an electron relay agent to transfer that photo-excited electron to the catalyst, a catalyst to reduce the proton source (acidic water) into H2, and finally a sacrificial reductant to regenerate the initial photosensitizer. 4. Supercapacitors

Supercapacitors are produced from the formation of an electrochemical or electrostatical double-layer capacitor at the interface of a conductive electrode and an electrolyte, or in the case of pseudo-capacitors through fast redox reactions at or near the surface. Whether it is carbon-based electrodes for the electrostatical double-layer supercapacitor or transition metal oxide electrodes for the pseudocapacitors, high conductivity, large surface areas and easy electrolyte access to active sites are important parameters for obtaining excellent energy storage capabilities and high power densities. Although extensive research has been devoted to using MOFs as templates to prepare nanostructured materials such as nanoporous carbon or metal oxides, nanoMOFs of various shapes (i.e. particles, rods, sheets) have also been investigated for use as electrodes for supercapacitors. The controllable porous structure of nanoMOFs provides much desired advantages such as low density and exceptionally high external surface area when fabricating electrodes. Additionally, the presence of redox active metal centers has the potential to increase pseudocapacitance as opposed to nearly inert commercial porous carbon electrodes. However, issues like low conductivity and structural stability in conventional electrolytes need to be overcome to extend their use. Combining nanoMOFs with highly conductive materials such as conducting polymers like polyaniline and carbon-based materials like graphene increase the overall conductivity and provide the benefits of nanoMOF porosity while various binders improve electrode stability.126,127 Generally, MOFs are found to be more stable in organic electrolytes than conventional aqueous electrolytes such as NaOH and H2SO4. This means that gel or large organic electrolytes such as tetraethylammonium tetrafluoroborate often are utilized for nanoMOFs with large enough pores or interlayer distances to accommodate them.

5. Batteries

Effective energy storage devices are necessary components of reliable use of renewable energy sources, especially for powering portable electronics and vehicles. Thus, there is a high demand for storage devices that can provide high power and energy densities with better rate performances and cycle lives than what is currently available. Improving rechargeable batteries, supercapacitors and fuel cells are all parts of this effort.

A typical rechargeable battery has four main components; cathode, anode, separator and electrolyte. Battery types are usually named according to the active components at the anode and the cathode. For instance, in a Liâ€“O2 battery, Li ion is oxidized at the anode and O2 is reduced at the cathode. The most common batteries are Li ion batteries with LiCoO2 and LiFePO4 as cathode materials and graphene as the anode material. Liâ€“S and Liâ€“O2 (or Liâ€“air) batteries are less common but they are attractive contenders with their high theoretical capacities, 1675 mA h gâˆ’1 and 3840 mA h gâˆ’1. Other battery types are Na-ion batteries, Liâ€“Na dual-ion batteries, redox flow batteries and Znâ€“air batteries. The separator and the electrolyte play key roles in a battery as well. The porous separator prevents contact between the anode and the cathode while allowing the passage of ions between the two sides. Typical separators are polymer based such as polyethylene and, polypropylene. The electrolyte is the medium that improves the movement of ions between the two terminals. For lithium batteries, electrolytes are commonly liquids containing a lithium salt in an organic solvent such as LiPF6 in ethylene carbonate.

Highly desirable properties in a battery are safety, lightness, fast charge/discharge performance, long cycle life, and high power and energy densities. There is room for a lot of improvement in all of these areas. Every battery type has its set of leading problems that need better solutions. For instance, Liâ€“S batteries produce soluble intermediates that need to be captured to prevent electrode loss limitations, and Liâ€“O2 batteries need efficient methods to keep the air intake clean for best performance. Researchers have utilized MOFs in all four basic components of batteries but especially as anode materials and separators. MOF porosity and adjustable structure allows for their utilization in various functions. Herein, we will focus on recent work with a special attention to nanoscale MOF usage.

5.1. Li and Na ion batteries

Li ion rechargeable batteries are widely used for portable electronics. Na-ion batteries and Liâ€“Na dual ion batteries have similar working principles and are attractive due to lower cost and abundance of sodium. In all three, during charging, an intercalated Li/Na compound at the cathode provides the Li/Na ion to be transferred via the electrolyte, through the separator to the anode to be stored via insertion, alloying and conversion reactions. Then, during discharging the stored ions make the return journey back to the cathode, while the anode captures and transfers the electrons to the external circuit to be used. The efficiency of these processes depends mostly on the reversibility of removing and transporting all the ions from the lattice of the electrode materials. A major safety concern that needs attention is the flammable electrolyte that can explode if the battery is damaged. This is especially important if Li/Na ion batteries are to be used to power electric or hybrid vehicles.
5.1.1 nanoMOFs at anodes. Graphite is the anode material of most commercial batteries. Li/Na ions are inserted into graphite layers during the charging process. However, the extraction of the ions during the discharging process is not efficient leading to much lower capacities than the theoretical capacity of 372 mA gâˆ’1. In addition, structural issues due to large volumetric changes during cycling leads to poor rate performance. The porous nature and diverse building blocks of MOFs are attractive properties for developing better anode electrode materials. The high MOF surface area decreases local current density and reduces dendrite formation while availability of various deposition sites lowers nucleation overpotential leading to more uniform Li deposition. Nanoscale MOF fabrications are desirable due to the close contact they provide with the electrolyte for faster ion transfer. On the other hand, conductive MOFs or known conducting additives are needed to enhance electron injection to the external circuit.

5.1.2 nanoMOFs at cathodes. MOF-based cathode materials for alkali ion batteries are less studied than anode materials, mostly because they have low gravimetric capacities and energy densities than commercially available intercalated Li/Na compounds. Increasing the number of ions inserted per unit through redox active linkers in addition to or in place of metal sites is an effective strategy, specifically if redox activity at metal nodes results in structure stability issues.190,191 N/N+ redox active linkers is especially useful in obtaining voltage windows (âˆ¼4 V) that are high enough for practical use.191,192 Finding solutions to increase electronic and ionic conductivity is also an important part of building a high performing MOF cathode.193 For improvement of ion conductivity, one possible method is electrolyte design,194 another is to couple alkali ion insertion with anion chelation in a dual ion insertion/extraction mechanism which is especially effective for slow moving large Na+ diffusion in Na-ion batteries.192,195,196 Permanent MOF pores and layered structures can accommodate large anions without significant volume increase that usually jeopardizes other cathode materials. A specific advantage of nanoscale MOF morphologies for cathode fabrications is their great potential for achieving theoretical capacities in practice by improving charge transfer rate and increasing the contact surface areas.

5.2. Liâ€“S batteries

Contrary to rechargeable Li ion batteries that intercalate Li+, in a Liâ€“S battery, Li is stored at the anode and S is stored at the cathode. During discharge, Li+ ions move to the cathode via the electrolyte, through the separator and get incorporated into LiS2via a series of lithium polysulfide reductions. Thus, for every sulfur, two Li+ ions can be stored, leading to a higher theoretical capacity of 1675 mA h gâˆ’1, compared to most Li ion batteries that store less than one Li per host atom. Despite this high capacity, abundance and low price of sulfur, Liâ€“S batteries have not seen wide-spread use due to three major challenges. One issue has to do with the solubility of the intermediate lithium polysulfides in the electrolyte that continuously deplete the cathode and corrode the anode in a process called â€˜the shuttle effectâ€™. Another issue, is the large volume change (âˆ¼80%) at the cathode upon LiS2 formation that causes mechanical stress and degradation, limiting cycling life. MOFs have great potential as cathode materials because through pore size and aperture design, and structure functionalization, they can prevent polysulfide leakage. Additionally, rigid MOF structures and suitable morphologies can help contain volume changes. MOFs can be utilized to adjust the electrolyte or the separator, as well. However, the third major challenge of Liâ€“S batteries is also a continuing MOF challenge. Sulfur has such a low conductivity (5 Ã— 10âˆ’30 S cmâˆ’1 at RT) that it is almost an insulator. Conductive additives are usually used, but they contribute to cathode volume and weight. MOF-derived materials with their higher conductivities and inherited MOF pore structure are attractive contenders as Liâ€“S cathode materials. Here, we will focus on recent nanoscale pristine or composite MOF use in Liâ€“S batteries.
5.2.1 nanoMOFs as sulfur hosts at the cathode. Similar to other Liâ€“S cathode hosts, nanoscale MOFs are designed in a way to improve polysulfide retention. Entrapment in MOF pores through aperture and pore size control,211,212 incorporation of sulphilic sites at the linkers213 or uncoordinated metal nodes,214â€“217 or a combination of these methods218â€“220 have been investigated. Increasing the number and function of defect sites and hybrid materials has also been utilized to improve sulfur deposition and usage while maintaining control over polysulfide leakage.

5.2.2 nanoMOFs as polysulfide sieves at the separator. Similar to their function in Li ion batteries, separators keep the anode and the cathode half-cells physically apart, but allow electrolyte movement and ion diffusion. With their uniform pores, MOF-separators also support uniform Li+ transport to the anode, which is important for suppressing Li dendrite formation, especially at high current densities. A function specific to Liâ€“S batteries however, is the possibility of preventing the migration of dissolved polysulfides to the anode to improve cycling life and rate performance. Thus, separators can be utilized as ionic sieves specifically at high sulfur loadings when the polysulfide capture measures at the cathode are not enough. MOFs are structurally very suitable for functionalization of separators since their pores can be adjusted to be small enough to retain polysulfides, yet big enough to let Li ions through. They are thermally stable and electrically insulating which is fine for the anode side of the separator. However, good conductivity is important for the cathode side, therefore most researchers use a conductive material along with the MOF when the modification side is going to face the cathode half of the battery. In addition, binders or flexible materials are needed to mediate MOF brittleness.

5.3. Liâ€“O2 batteries

Liâ€“O2 rechargeable batteries oxidize Li metal in the anode and reduce oxygen at the cathode to generate current. In contrast to Li ion and Liâ€“S batteries, only anode active material is stored in the battery while the porous cathode uses ambient oxygen, leading to a high energy density. When the anode is Li, high theoretical specific capacity of 3842 mA h gâˆ’1 and a specific energy (40.1 MJ kgâˆ’1) close to that of gasoline (46.8 MJ kgâˆ’1) are obtained. Even not at full capacity, a stable Liâ€“O2 battery can provide enough power for use in vehicles or solar energy storage-based grid backup systems. The two major drawbacks that are holding the commercialization of Liâ€“O2 batteries are related to improvements needed at the cathode and the electrolyte. The most obvious source of oxygen at the cathode is air, that is why these batteries are also called Liâ€“air batteries. However, water vapor, air contaminants and other air gasses can cause side reactions, causing electrolyte degradation and cathode pore blockage. Thus, using an atmosphere of purified oxygen for battery testing is common in a laboratory setting. The search for electrolyte compositions that will be stable in the presence of reactive intermediates and the discharge product over multiple cycles is continuing. Aqueous, non-aqueous, a mix of both and solid electrolytes are main electrolyte types that are being improved by researchers. nanoMOFs with their ordered pores and metal active sites can provide uniform and concentrated O2 adsorption as well as efficient electrolyte access and O2 diffusion in addition to catalyzing oxygen reduction and oxygen evolution reactions. Although very limited, MOFs have also been utilized in separators in less explored battery designs.

Cathode porosity, and the distribution and efficiency of the catalytic sites are important factors for battery performance.

6. nanoMOF fuel cells

In contrast to most batteries that store chemical energy within the cell, fuel cells use a constant flow of chemical energy in the form of a fuel from outside the cell to generate electricity. The most common fuel is hydrogen, but fuel cells with methanol and ethanol, and at high temperatures CO and dilute methane are also used.246 The three main parts of a fuel cell include an anode, an electrolyte and a cathode. At the anode, the fuel is oxidized using a catalyst to form ions and electrons both of which are then transferred to the cathode; electrons via an external circuit and the ions via the electrolyte. At the cathode, another catalyst is used to combine the ions and electrons to reduce oxygen which is usually supplied from air. Fuel cells are mainly classified by the type of electrolyte used. Befitting the most common fuel, proton exchange membrane fuel cells are the most common type. Fuel cells are fabricated as stacks of cells to increase either the voltage or the current output. MOFs and especially their derivatives have been explored as cheaper replacements for precious metal based catalysts, and as electrolyte holding membranes. Since there are reviews focusing on MOF-derived catalysts and membranes for fuel cells,247â€“250 herein, we will only focus on recent publications on the use of nanoscale MOFs in these areas.

6.1. nanoMOF proton exchange membranes

Proton exchange membranes can be designed to hold an aqueous or a non-aqueous electrolyte. Non-aqueous electrolytes are designed mainly for fuel cells operating at temperatures higher than 100 Â°C. Functionalization of MOF linkers with acidic groups such as â€“SO3H, â€“COOH and â€“PO3H2 or loading the pores with protonic guest molecules such as imidazole and histamine are well-established strategies for enhancing MOF proton conductivity.251,252 In addition, the crystalline MOF network is very useful in studying proton transfer mechanisms.253 Many membrane fabrications are composites of nanoscale MOFs with other materials to improve flexibility, processability and uniform distribution. nanoMOFs are also used as additives for common membranes such as NafionÂ® and SPEEK to improve their performance, especially at higher temperatures.250
6.1.1 Applications for low temperature and aqueous conditions. Proton conductivity increases and transfer kinetics improve as the temperature is raised, but a delicate balance of water and heat control is necessary to prevent excessive electrolyte loss due to water shortage, or catalyst poisoning due to water overload. MOFs are usually used as part of a composite or as an additive for polymer-based membranes in an effort to improve proton conductivity. Filling MOF pores with proton conductive molecules not only enhances proton transfer and provides opportunities for ternary hybrids but also limits fuel and gas diffusion by blocking the pores. An important issue for fabricating hybrid membranes is obtaining uniform distribution of the components. Nanoscale MOFs are easier to distribute across polymer matrixes. 6.2. nanoMOFs for oxygen reduction reactions at the fuel cell cathode

Two types of targeted oxygen reduction happen at the cathode. In acidic media with a proton exchange membrane, oxygen is reduced by four electrons to form water, using the protons that were transferred from the anode. In basic media with an anion exchange membrane, oxygen is reduced by two electrons to form hydroxide which is then transferred to the anode for hydrogen oxidation, generating water and electrons. Both systems use expensive Pt-based catalysts at the cathode that the industry is looking to replace or reduce. The four-electron reduction reaction in the acidic system is more sluggish, and it is hard to find materials that are stable in acidic media. Although the two-electron reduction reaction in the basic system is faster, hydrogen peroxide forming side reactions limit efficiency. Proton exchange materials are more studied then hydroxide exchange materials, as well. Regardless of the cell type, MOFs can provide uniformly distributed reduction sites and effective O2 access and product removal due to their ordered pore structure. However, their insulating nature greatly diminishes their use as pristine materials. A limited number of conductive-enough MOFs have been synthesized.265,266 A more general approach is the use of MOFs as precursors to derive carbon-based catalysts that inherit some of their nanostructure and active site distribution.78,247,249,267,268 Another strategy to alleviate insulation issues is combining MOFs with conductive components such as graphene-based materials and carbon nanotubes.269â€“274 Such hybrid materials have the potential to utilize MOF functionality for designing efficient catalysts. Within this context, nanoscale MOFs offer better incorporation into hybrid structures. In addition, MOF dimension reduction and oriented growth strategies that provide better coupling with conductive materials can improve catalytic performance.275 Unfortunately, limited practical studies into the use of newly developed ORR catalysts as cathode materials in fuel cell devices restricts our understanding of their stability, susceptibility to side reactions and general performance.

Apart from use as a catalyst, MOF pores can be utilized to improve access to O2. For instance, Azzaroni and coworkers demonstrated the use of a ZIF-8 top layer as an O2 adsorbent to increase oxygen concentration on the conducting polymer catalytic surface. The ZIF-8 layer facilitated O2 uptake from solution, leading to enhancement of ORR in neutral media.276,277

6.3. Alcohol oxidation at the anode

Hydrogen gas storage issues raises the need for energy dense liquid fuels, especially for portable devices and vehicles. On top of cost concerns, methanol and ethanol oxidation intermediates such as carbon monoxide are adsorbed on the porous Pt anode, leading to incomplete fuel consumption and catalyst deactivation. Cu-MOFs were first studied for ethanol oxidation.278,279 An indirect proton transfer pathway facilitated by an OH group at the linker was reported. Later, HKUST-1, was grown on a surface modified gold electrode for methanol oxidation. The role of linkers was minimal in this case. Although alcohol adsorption and proton conduction are important, the real challenges are reliable catalytic oxidation without compromise of structure stability and efficient electron transfer to the external circuit. Apart from MOF-derived catalysts, MOF hybrids with a conductive component are promising candidates. Recently, Iqbal and coworkers investigated the use of a Co-MOF/graphene oxide hybrid as a catalyst for methanol oxidation where graphene oxide was instrumental in catalyst stability and electrical activity.280

In another report, Samadi-Maybodi and coworkers fabricated a modified carbon paste electrode using Ni-doped ZIF-8 nanoparticles (60â€“90 nm) and graphite powder for direct methanol fuel cells.281 Here, ZIF-8 provided an encapsulation platform that is stable in alkaline solution for the Ni nanoparticle catalyst.

7. Conclusions and outlook

It is a fairly recent scientific revelation that optimized functional structures, whether in biology or in technology, are nanoscale materials. Upon the establishment of MOF as a novel type of material, the emergence of nanoMOFs will be a logical next step. This review intends to summarize the many benefits (high order, molecular and elemental variability, chemical tunability, etc.) why MOFs at the nanoscale are particularly functional and thus interesting to explore for future applications. This review highlights nanoscale MOF use in solar energy conversion including studies of light harvesting and energy transfer in dye sensitized solar cells and performance enhancement in perovskite, organic solar cells, and photocatalytic fuel production from water in addition to their diverse utilization in energy storage systems such as supercapacitors, batteries and fuel cells. It focuses on the benefits gained by using nanosheets, nanorods or nanocrystals such as better compatibility with other materials, easier processability and improved performance due to larger external surface areas, more exposure to the active sites and faster electron and ion transfer. This work intended to show the challenges that MOFs at the nanoscale face, among them limited conductivity, stability and homogeneity.

Clearly, we are currently witnessing a nascent stage of nanoMOF materials science and will see a rapid rise of this type of nanomaterials in energy and other fields. It is the authors' hope that this review may contribute to an increasing enthusiasm for the next stage of materials science and technology, and that possibly readers may become inspired to spearhead efforts to develop these materials of the future.
