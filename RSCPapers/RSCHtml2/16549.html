High performance of electrochemical lithium storage batteries: ZnO-based nanomaterials for lithium-ion and lithium–sulfur batteries

Abstract

As one of the most promising electrode materials, zinc oxide-based nanomaterials have attracted great attention in recent decades for remarkable features such as relatively low cost, relatively high reversible capacity and good physical and chemical stability. In this article, we aim to present a general review of synthetic methods of zinc oxide-based nanomaterials and related morphologies. In addition, recent advances in lithium storage batteries are summarized and discussed (lithium-ion and lithium–sulfur batteries). Tentative conclusions and assessments aim to promote the next generation of electrochemical lithium storage devices.

1. Introduction

Enormous growth in fossil fuel consumption in past decades has led to many serious environmental problems.1 To relieve environmental stress, secondary batteries are regarded as important electrochemical energy storage devices (EESDs) due to high efficiency and environmental friendliness.2–5 Significant progress has been achieved in rechargeable battery applications, such as lithium-ion battery (LIB) powered mobile devices and commercial vehicles (Tesla). In addition, owing to greater specific capacity, the lithium–sulfur battery (Li–S) is currently considered the most promising energy storage device for electrical vehicles. Furthermore, other devices, such as metal–air batteries and super-capacitors, have also been extensively investigated for various reasons.6,7

Because conventional electrode materials, briefly discussed in the following, do not meet diverse requirements for EESDs,8 researchers are focused on transition metal oxides (MOs) (e.g., zinc oxide, titanium oxide, tin oxide) and corresponding composites.9–17 Among various MOs, zinc oxide and its composites have proved valuable in addressing problems of intrinsic resistance and rapid disintegration.18,19 In addition, due to the advantages of high special capacity, relative stability, environmental friendliness and low cost, great attention has been focused on various zinc oxide-based nanomaterials in recent years.20 Thus far, study results show that Li-ion battery, Li–S and Zn–air batteries have desirable features of high theoretical special capacity, low cost and portability. For example, Xiao et al. synthesized in situ ZnO via hybridization with conductive cobalt (Co) and N-doped carbon (C–N), demonstrating its promise to displace commercial graphite in LIBs.21 Hierarchical porous carbon was prepared by Strubel et al. via a versatile approach based on ZnO nanoparticles.22 Such high-performance nanomaterials are expected to perform far better than certain existing Li–S materials.23 Gan et al. directly synthesized polypyrrole on the surface of zinc oxide particles by in situ polymerization, circumventing direct contact with an electrolyte and reducing volume variation.24

In this review, we mainly introduce the recent progress of ZnO-based nanomaterials vis-à-vis Li-ion and Li–S batteries. First, we provide a brief assessment of synthetic strategies for zinc oxide-based nanomaterials, and their functional applications in lithium storage batteries. In particular, several substantial improvements are presented in sequence. Meanwhile, advantages and disadvantages of these nanomaterials are discussed. In addition, other applications, such as the zinc–air battery, are introduced as well. Finally, insights into ongoing research are outlined in the last section.

2. Lithium-ion battery

The LIB rechargeable battery has been a practical and easily accessible solution for a long time, especially in electronic products such as mobile phones, laptops and smart watches. In LIBs, lithium ions migrate in and out of two electrode interiors.25 While discharging, the reaction mechanism at the anode is described in the following equation: xLi+ + 6C + xe− = LixC6.26 Although the principal mechanism is clear, inevitable and troublesome side effects occur in the electrode and electrolyte. Such side effects cause problems in the following areas: chemical and physical stability, oxidation–reduction quality, capacity fade, coulombic efficiency, volume expansion–shrinkage and costs. The classic rocking chair battery, in which lithium ions move continuously, is presented vividly in Fig. 1(a) and (b).27

Various surface morphologies and nanostructures contribute to distinctive features that have been investigated. Regarding cathodes, lithium storage materials (e.g., LiMn2O4, LiCoO2 and LiFePO4) mixed with zinc oxide have not been explored systemically in this field.28–31 In contrast, for anodes it is acknowledged that the common lithium anode has gradually developed based on commercial carbonaceous materials, owing to a dendrite-free recharge process.32,33 Nonetheless, zinc oxide-based composites are considered promising as anode materials. Based on previous references, the electrochemical mechanism for ZnO anode electrodes is described in the following equations, which is essentially different from graphite:

 
Conversion reaction: ZnO + 2Li+ + 2e− ⇌ Zn + Li2O(1)
 
Alloying–dealloying reaction: Zn + Li+ + e− ⇌ LiZn.(2)

Note that in reaction (1), the zinc oxide material occupies more lithium ions, which are quite valuable during alloying–dealloying process. Due to the essential nature of zinc oxide, low columbic efficiency, severe capacity fading and poor electrochemical kinetics have restricted its widespread application. Therefore, it is necessary to explore diverse methods of surmounting these problems. For instance, coating the zinc oxide material could alleviate volume change efficiency, and combining it with other elements or carbon materials may increase initial capacity and reduce degradation during cycling. Moreover, the short transport path of lithium ions, relative high electrochemical conductivity and longevity are advantages of ZnO-based nanomaterials. In general, zinc oxide material (theoretical capacity 987 mA h g−1) and tin oxide material (more than 600 mA h g−1) have similar mechanisms, but the former has higher theoretical capacity.34 Cobalt oxide and copper oxide anode material also possess lower theoretical capacity at 890 mA h g−1 and 674 mA h g−1, respectively.35 Therefore, as an environment-friendly and low-cost anode material, ZnO-based composite will prove to be quite promising for next-generation batteries.

ZnO-based composite possesses better electrochemical properties of theoretical specific capacities up to 600 mA h g−1.36 Nanostructures of these materials include zero-dimensional (0D), one-dimensional (1D), two-dimensional (2D) and three-dimensional (3D) structures (e.g., microspheres and nanoflowers).37–43 Herein, we compare various characteristics in the three dimensions to identify the key role ZnO-based nanomaterials play. A series of improvements are presented in subsequent sections.

2.1 Zero-dimensional nanostructure

Bresser et al. investigated properties of diverse elements and found that transition-metal-doped, zinc oxide nanoparticles materials have intriguing performance characteristics.44 They replaced Zn with transition metals (Co and Fe) in the wurtzite lattice to enhance electrochemical properties of zinc oxide. The n-type transition-metal (TM)-doped zinc oxide nanoparticles, which had a general formula of Zn0.9TM0.1O (TM = Fe or Co), were prepared via an environment-friendly and sucrose-assisted wet chemical synthesis method. However, this anode material tends to aggregate due to nanoparticles’ relative high surface energy. Therefore, this research group further modified the previous structure to carbon-coated Zn0.9Fe0.1O nanoparticles.45–48 The carbon coating protected the electrode from seriously reacting with the electrolyte. In addition, the inside material could also change volume under limits rather than seriously disintegrate. Their reversible capacity was slightly reduced to ∼840 mA h g−1 at the current density of 48 mA g−1 after a single cycle. Specific capacities of ∼720, 670, 580, 475, and 360 mA h g−1 were measured at current densities of 0.095, 0.19, 0.48, 0.95 and 1.9 A g−1, respectively (Fig. 2).

Upon cycling, unfortunately, traditional 0D zinc oxide material at the nanoscale inherently undergoes negligible volume variety due to high surface energy. The lithiation and delithiation process, severe cracking, fracture and pulverization will take place gradually in anode (demonstrated in Fig. 1(c)). In addition, across the electrode the repeated expansion and shrinkage of particles cause dramatic capacity decay and delaminated active material, shown in Fig. 1(d).49 Thus, the protective coating method has attracted great attention.

In this way, zinc oxide material was used as a coating to suppress gassing behavior by Han et al. As a relatively safe and stable anode material, the poor rate capacity of Li4Ti5O12 (LTO), which is caused by moderate Li+ diffusion coefficient and low intrinsic electronic conductivity, limits the further application of this material.50 In addition, common anode materials based on LTO show severe gassing behavior while charging and discharging. Han et al. took the two factors into consideration.51 They assumed that reactions on the surface of LTO could be the major reason for such behavior and then took measurements on modified LTO spinel particles with a ZnO coating (Fig. 3). Experimental data demonstrated that the ZnO coating could stabilize the interface of electrode and electrolyte and prevent side reactions between LTO and electrolyte by forming an interface (SEI) film (shown in Fig. 3(a)). It is believed that this coating not only decreased reaction kinetics of side reactions but also improved both lithium ion diffusion and electronic conductivity in the meantime. In the LTO battery, the aforementioned method may be an effective way of overcoming the barriers of gassing behavior.

2.2 1D nanostructure

Generally speaking, 0D nanomaterials suffer severe capacity fading and poor kinetics upon cycling due to the aggregation from intrinsic high surface energy.52,53 After long-term cycling, the electrode may partly disintegrate. Nonetheless, in contrast to ZnO nanoparticles, 1D nanomaterials such as nanorods surpass the 0D in some aspects.54 ZnO combined with carbon materials have shown much better performance, since carbon materials, such as specific carbon nanofibers, are capable to enhance electronic conductivity, mechanical stability/flexibility and specific capacity.55–57

In order to investigate the special feature of ZnO based 1D nanomaterials, Shen and co-workers attached Zn(CH3COO)2 and Cu(CH3COO)2 composite onto polyacrylonitrile (PAN) fibers via electrospinning.58 After heat treatment, ZnO and Cu particles were decorated on the carbonized nanofibers (CNFs). Study results demonstrated that reversible capacities of 618 and 493 mA h g−1 at large current densities of 500 and 1000 mA g−1 even after 50 cycles. As shown in Fig. 4, the capacity still remained at 321 mA h g−1 under 5000 mA g−1 current density. In the ZnO/Cu/CNFs composites, the synergistic effect not only enhanced electrical conductivity and structural stability, but also shortened the Li-ion diffusion length. Later, Shilpa et al. successfully encapsulated ZnO in the hollow composite (C–rGO) nanofibers by electrospinning.59 This ZnO–C–rGO composite exhibited a high capacity of 618.98 mA h g−1 and maintained at 445.81 mA h g−1 after 100 cycles at the current density of 50 mA g−1. In addition, this composite also showed an excellent rate performance which retained at 472 mA h g−1 under a large current rate of 500 mA g−1 and 815 mA h g−1 under a small current rate of 50 mA g−1, respectively.

Ever since, many researchers have focused on synthesizing new electrode materials. The addition of multiwalled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) is proved to be an effective way in delaying the anodes’ capacity fading after long-term cycle.60 With the use of a sol–gel spin coating technique, free-standing ZnO and MWCNT composites were synthesized in a 3D bucky papers network by Köse et al.61 They found that the sol–gel-coated ZnO/MWCNT anode by chelating with glycerin, showed high discharge capacity of 460 mA h g−1 at the current rate of 0.2C after 100 cycles. It is well known that there is a charge accumulation in the carbon nanotube that limits lithium ion movement (known as high irreversible capacity). Although this composite cannot entirely avoid this shortcoming, study results indicated that it can increase energy storage capability and provide high capacity retention.

Meanwhile, novel materials are emerging all the time. Feng's group reported CoO–ZnO nanotubes and ZnCo2O4 hierarchical nanorods.62 Based on special features of zinc oxide, they attached these oxides on copper substrate and found that different concentrations of Co(NO3)2 solution could form diverse morphologies. Consequently, they achieved hexagonal hierarchical rod structures through increasing concentration of Co(NO3)2 to 0.3 M. Some advantages of CoO–ZnO nanorod and nanotube arrays can be summarized. (1) ZnO nanorods serve as reaction precursors to promote activity. (2) High electronic conductivity is caused by the copper substrate. (3) While charging and discharging, volume change could be reduced effectively. (4) This material was cost-effective with less pollution.

In addition, Zhu and colleagues applied a one-step strategy to make SnO2/ZnO11 (molar ratio of Sn:Zn = 1:1) heterogeneous nanofibers (HNFs) by electrospinning.63 After an intensive search for the molar ratios between Sn:Zn, they found that the optimum ratio is 1:1. The SnO2/ZnO11 HNFs exhibited good reversible capacities of 625.4, 567.4, 556.7, 525.7, 529.1 and 521.1 mA h g−1 after 20, 40, 60, 80, 100 and 120 cycles. Furthermore, due to their special heterogeneous and mesoporous structures, the materials showed a reversible capacity of 237.2 mA h g−1 at a high current density of 4000 mA g−1. As for comparison, they found that pure SnO2 NFs and pure ZnO NFs had serious degradation resulting from drastic volumetric change during the insertion and extraction of Li+. Regarding SnO2/ZnO HNFs, the evenly distributed SnO2 nanorods with large spaces can bear more pressure during cycling. In addition, SnO2 nanorods are less likely to peel off from ZnO nanofibers because of their heterogeneous structure. In addition, such morphology has a relative large length to diameter ratio, contributing to faster electron transfer and larger specific surface area. In conclusion, the SnO2/ZnO HNFs with optimum molar ratio showed a high specific surface area and outstanding electrochemical performance.

In 2015, Xu et al. synthesized ZnO/C hierarchical porous nanorods through one-pot, wet-chemical reaction followed by thermal calcination.64 Then, an extra PEDOT-PSS (poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene)-poly(styrenesulfonate)) layer was coated to the surface of ZnO/C nanorods. This coating structure resulted in long cycle stability and high rate capacity (e.g., 623.94 mA h g−1 reversible capacity after 1500 cycles at 1C). Some specific details are exhibited in the Fig. 4. The coating layer could alleviate volume change and prevented pulverization effectively. Next, long cycle stability and high rate capacity are attributed to high electronic conductivity of PEDOT-PSS coating layer and the material's hierarchical structures.

2.3 2D nanostructure

2D nanomaterials have drawn our attention in recently years due to their relative high electrochemical conductivity and excellent 2D tunnels for lithium ions.65 Although not comparable, 2D ZnO-based nanomaterials shows superior properties than conventional ZnO powders to some extent, because 2D nanostructures own stratified structures in favor of work system. Indeed, 2D nanostructure is not ideal as the classic stratified structure of graphite, it also provides relatively abundant 2D tunnels for lithium ions, and keeps relatively stable while cycling because lithium ions insertion and extraction are easier. Meanwhile, during charging–discharging, scattered ZnO-based nanoparticles tend to aggregate seriously due to their high surface energy, resulting in the irretrievable capacity fading.

Huang et al. reported the fabrication of porous ZnO nanosheets on Cu foil substrate through a chemical bath deposition method and compared the morphology and the corresponding electrochemical performance with those from commercial ZnO powders, as shown in Fig. 5.66 It is obvious that porous ZnO nanosheets shows the first discharge–charge capacity of 1120 mA h g−1 and 750 mA h g−1, respectively, which is much higher than that of ZnO powder electrode. As a result, the advantages of 2D advanced materials can be concluded into three points. First of all, porous nanosheets create short diffusion paths and large contact interfaces. Second, particles could interact with each other easily due to their net-like structures and eventually lead to a better electrical conductivity. Finally, the porous nanosheets optimized the cyclability and helped to alleviate disintegration.

However, 2D ZnO-based materials also have some drawbacks including volume expansion, aggregation and disintegration to some extent, which are mentioned before.67 Thus, further investigation is needed to optimize the property of ZnO nanosheet materials. Due to the stable structure of ZnO, various 2D morphologies of zinc oxide composites are emerging. Li et al. took vertically aligned graphene sheets (VAGN) anchored on Cu foil into account.68 ZnO nanoparticles (ZnO-NPs) were gradually grown on both sides of the VAGN during the hydrothermal synthesis process to form ZnO–VAGN composite, which exhibited excellent electrochemical performance. Interestingly, the sheet structures offered short pathways of Li-ion transport and largely enhanced ion conductivity. The electrode, made of ZnO–VAGN composite, surprisingly retained at high reversible capacity of 810 m Ah g−1 at current density of 80 mA g−1 as well as low capacity loss (about 6.0%) after 105 cycles. Wu et al. adopted a similar approach to address several inherent problems.69 They fabricated ZnO/reduced graphene oxide (rGO) composites and deposited the composites on Ni foil current collector through the electrostatic spray deposition technique (ESD). During the alloying–dealloying process, many side reactions may occur that lead to device fade.70 One noteworthy consequence is removal of the active substance from the substrate (current collector); thus the electrode cannot load lithium ions as mentioned in the previous section. Wu et al. found that the addition of rGO prevented ZnO-based nanomaterials from aggregating into large particles to some extent.71 Moreover, this composite also showed a specific capacity of 1724 m Ah g−1 during the first discharge and possessed strong defensive stability at high current density. Huang et al. further utilized pyrrole as the carbon source to prepare sheet-like ZnO as shown in Fig. 9.72 The experimental data showed that carbon coating improved electronic conductivity of active materials and stabilized their structure during the cycling process.73 In addition, carbon coating also prevented active materials from dissolving to electrolyte. After 200 cycles, 400 mA h g−1 capacity could be reflected at charge–discharge rate of 1C. Furthermore, it still retained high capacity of 380 mA h g−1 even at a high current rate of 3C after 100 cycles, which showed a much more stable cycle performance than bare ZnO materials (Fig. 7).

2.4 3D micro/nanostructure

3D ZnO-based nanomaterials are considered as a complicated system. Their morphologies are diverse and cannot be described by a general expression. Thus, we classify them as microsphere, nanoflower and other complex structures.74–78 And other complex structures involves metal oxide and carbon composite materials.79–82 Since the existence of various models of 3D architectures and constituents, these anode materials exhibit different properties (e.g., specific surface area, electrochemical conductivity, and reversible capacity).83 According to practical data, excellent 3D structures and moderate proportion of components exhibit higher specific surface area, relative great conductivity and outstanding capacity.84 Moreover, a large number of interspaces can alleviate volume change to some extent and provide efficient and even rapid pathways for lithium ions. Nevertheless, large numbers of voids are possible that cause cracking and pulverization of materials, which means irretrievable capacity damage.85–87 Hence, such integrated materials continue to be investigated to identify better architectures and more suitable proportions (Table 1).

2.4.1 ZnO-based microsphere. Based on many experiments and resulting data, some high reversible capacity can be easily realized (e.g., one microsphere material achieved 257 mA h g−1 after 150 cycles at current density of 50 mA g−1 and another one obtained 850 mA h g−1 after 200 cycles at current density of 100 mA g−1).88,89 Xie et al. successfully synthesized ZnO–ZnCo2O4 hybrid, hollow microspheres via the annealing process shows outstanding properties as the anode material for LIBs.90 Its reversible capacity could reach 1199 mA h g−1 after 200 cycles at current density of 200 mA g−1, where the hollow structures played a pivotal role in improving charge–discharge capacity and stabilizing work system (Fig. 8).

Xie et al. was the first to made yolk–shell ZnO–C microspheres.91 The data showed that the structure was beyond hollow microsphere and solid microsphere. Differences can be indicated not only in geometry, but also in electrochemical properties such as discharge capacities, coulombic efficiencies and rate performance. The yolk–shell structure was unique and the material's electrochemical performance was far better than the other two microspheres. For instance, after 150 cycles, the material still retained 99.3% of coulombic efficiency and reached relative capacity up to 520 mA h g−1 at a current density of 100 mA g−1. The wrapper carbon isolated every zinc oxide nanoparticle to prevent aggregation and support the whole system. They suggested that the pulverization, which resulted in capacity fade, may be assuaged effectively. Xie et al. proposed an idea for metallic element doping and prepared a hierarchical ZnO–Ag–C composite microspheres (radius of 1.5 μm) with porosity by a similar method, as shown in Fig. 11.92 Study results revealed that capacity remained at 729 mA h g−1 even after 200 cycles at 100 mA g−1. 2.4.2 ZnO-based nanoflower. Nanoflower is another classic 3D morphology. Mashkoor et al. synthesized hierarchical ZnO nanoflower modified by Au nanoparticles (AuNPs).95 The hybrid nanomaterial possessed abundant void spaces, which were beneficial to the diffusion of lithium ion, and exhibited some benign properties comparing to previously reported common zinc oxide anodes. The Au–ZnO phase contributed to the tremendous increase of capacity and longevity in the Li-ion battery. Morphology and electrochemical performance are shown in Fig. 11. It can be seen that every flower-like structure's diameter is about 6–10 μm and every branch length varies by 2–3 μm. In addition, the material had a 1280 mA h g−1 initial discharge capacity and even achieved over 392 mA h g−1 reversible capacity after 50 cycles. In contrast, at a current density of 120 mA h g−1, the Au–ZnO cell degraded much slower than conventional ones. For instance, after 10 cycles, the Au–ZnO cell decreased to 402 mA h g−1 capacity while the capacity of the ZnO film electrode was reduced to 252 mA h g−1. Afterward, bi-component ZnO/ZnCo2O4 composite materials were prepared by Xu et al. in a scalable and facile way, by controlling the ratio of Zn to Co in the process.96 The porous nanoflower-like material possessed abundant open space and electroactive reaction sites, which showed great cycling stability of capacity of 847 mA h g−1 at density of 500 mA g−1 after 200 cycles. Meanwhile, incredible rate capability (∼538 mA h g−1 at 3200 mA g−1) was exhibited. This kind of material surpasses other ZnO-based or ZnCo2O4-based material. As a promising electrode material, it showed high reversible capacity, stability and rate capacity (Fig. 12).

2.4.3 Other complex composites. As previously mentioned, this section addresses metal oxides and carbon composite materials. Metal oxides are typical materials for LIBs depending on battery performance and cost issues. LIB based on lithium cobalt oxide (LiCoO2) has high energy capacity, but it cannot stay stable upon many cycles. In addition, this kind of battery is likely to cause safety risks if broken during transportation. Hence, with effort, Dai et al. proposed that ZnO coating has the ability to improve rate capability and cycling performance from 3.0 to 4.5 V, by modifying LiCoO2 electrodes with ZnO.97 The thin film of zinc oxide theoretically played an important part among electrode materials such as diffusing through the whole material. They applied radio frequency (RF) magnetron sputtering technology to coat zinc oxide on as-prepared LiCoO2 composite and discovered the composite, whose coating thickness was 17 nm (named LiCoO2/ZnO17), exhibited discharge capacity of 191 mA h g−1 at 0.2C and maintained 81% capacity after 200 cycles. Furthermore, at 10C, reversible capacity of 106 mA h g−1 was delivered. According to study results, better lithium ion diffusion and stable nanostructure resulted from this special morphology.

Gao et al. reported the fabrication of sandwich-like TiO2@ZnO arrays that could be used directly as electrodes without conductivity additive and polymer binder for flexible LIB.98 Among the open porous 3D interconnected arrays, the intrinsic flaws of ZnO nanoparticles (aggregation and volume change) can be alleviated to some extent due to the strong support of TiO2 nanosheets. The general nanostructure and electrochemical performance are demonstrated in Fig. 13(a)–(c). After 100 cycles, the flexible electrode made from TiO2@ZnO arrays achieved a reversible capacity of 340.2 mA h g−1 at a current density of 200 mA g−1.

Another important material is carbon composite. As carbon sources, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are drawing great attention due to functional advantages and convenient synthesis. Zhou et al. developed an in situ calcination strategy and successfully applied this method to fabricate porous ZnO/N-doped carbon (ZnO/NC) polyhedrons.99 They used a metal–organic framework ZIF-8 as the precursor and achieved excellent results. On the one hand, the porous N-doped MOFs alleviate volume variations and offer abundant hosting sides for lithium ions during charging and discharging. On the other hand, the precursor improves electronic conductivity. In addition, at the initial low rate of 0.5C (1C = 978 mA g−1), ZnO/NC polyhedrons showed high reversible discharge capacity of 834.3 mA h g−1. Moreover, at 5C or 10C, superior rate performance of 399.2 mA h g−1 and 253.5 mA h g−1 were demonstrated, respectively. After 100 cycles, they exhibited excellent cycling stability (399.2 mA h g−1 at 5C). Fig. 17(a) and (b) provide information on morphology and performance. This material clearly has the potential to open up opportunities for the next generation of LIBs.100

Meanwhile, some other works also proceeded to overcome limitations such as poor electronic conductivity.101,102 Yue et al. doped cobalt into carbon-coated ZnO (ZnO@C) and obtained cobalt (Co)-doped MOF-5s (Co-MOF-5s) by a secondary growth method followed by a heat treatment.103 The morphology can be seen in Fig. 14(c) and (d). The doping of Co played an important role on increasing the degree of graphitization. The high-graphitized carbon framework is beneficial from the high electronic conductivity. This as-synthesized CZO@C composite maintained a reversible capacity of 725 mA h g−1 at the current density of 100 mA g−1, after 50 cycles which was much higher than ZnO@C composite (335 mA h g−1) because of the contribution from high-graphitized carbon framework. Similar to MOFs, porous carbon or graphene materials are also good alternatives to serve as carbon sources. Shen et al. prepared ZnO-loaded/porous carbon (PC) composites via a facile solvothermal method.104 During the process, ZnO crystals were gradually well-distributed in PC host medium. They controlled the amounts of loaded ZnO contents and achieved the near-perfect weight percentage (54 wt%). They found that this flexible structure buffered stress, preventing aggregation and pulverization effectively. Besides, 653.7 mA h g−1 reversible capacity was observed after 100 cycles. Cycled at the current density of 1000 mA g−1, it showed high a high reversible capacity of 496.8 mA h g−1. In addition, Hsieh et al. made full use of graphene and combined it with zinc oxide.105 They attached zinc oxide (ZnO) nanocrystals to graphene nanosheets (GNs) via a series of methods from microwave heating, modified Hummers’ method to homogenizing dispersion. Both sides of GNs were dispersed over by ZnO nanocrystals uniformly and ZnO@GN composite formed. At 0.1C, the as-prepared ZnO@GN material obtained the capacity of 850 mA h g−1 and retained 82.1% coulombic efficiency at 1st cycle. It maintained a capacity of ∼60% at 5C and decayed ∼8% capacity after 50 cycles. All these were far better than fresh GN anode according to statistics. The 3D integration system enhanced the performance by providing abundant redox sites and short ionic pathway.

So far, plenty of advanced electrode materials, which possess various morphologies and metallic metal, have been well investigated.106–111 In our opinions, common ZnO electrode materials’ property cannot meet people's need because they have tendency to aggregate and serious volume change during charging and discharging process. These problems may lead to the irretrievable capacity fading and the risk of safety, which means the device failure. Therefore, zinc oxide needs to be modified and protected by an extra layer. This effective coating carbon has the ability to get lithium ions through and endure stress from the volume change. Besides, zinc oxide composites are evenly distributed into stable supporting system such as MOFs and conductive polymers. Such a feasible measure assists in supressing pulverization and improving conductivity of the system to some extent. Meanwhile, appropriate metallic elements, added moderately, play functional parts such as improving electrochemical conductivity and specific capacity in the system of ZnO-based nanomaterials. Nonetheless, what we mainly focus on is the part that zinc oxide material plays. According to the above literatures, although this material has severe volume change while discharging–charging, the excellent property of higher specific capacity, relative prominent ionic and electrochemical conductivity, prolonged cycling stability and so on have attracted great attention in applied research. Besides, the synergistic effect in the zinc oxide-based composite endow LIBs many unexpected properties.

3. Lithium–sulfur battery

Among rechargeable batteries, the lithium–sulfur (Li–S) battery is well known for high energy density (theoretical capacity of 1672 mA h g−1) and relative light weight.112 Compared with conventional lithium-ion batteries, lithium–sulfur batteries are lower cost due to the use of sulfur.113–115 The Li–S battery has recently attracted attention due to its high theoretical specific energy of 2600 Wh kg−1, which is about five times higher than that of common LIB.116,117 The reaction process between electrode and electrolyte contains the dissolution of Li+ moving between the cathode surface and anode during discharging and charging as shown in Fig. 18. As is well known, the single host ion only combines 0.5–0.7 lithium ions in LIB, while each sulfur atom unites with two lithium ions. Nevertheless, compared to LIB, Li–S has volume expansion–shrinkage as well, which could cause mechanical tension on the electrode and lead to reduced electrochemical connection and hindered flow of Li+. Moreover, charge carriers can only diffuse on material surfaces because of poor electrical insulating of sulfur (5 × 10−30 S cm−1 at 25 °C).118 In addition, the dissolved polysulfides deposits on the anode, reduces to Li2S and Li2S2, which always lead to low coulombic efficiency, poor cycling stability and irretrievable capacity fading.119 At present researchers are trying to address these challenges (e.g., electrochemical conductivity and the dissolution of Li2Sx (2 ≤ x ≤ 8)) through various solutions.120 As a metal oxide, zinc oxide and its composite make a significant contribution to Li–S batteries.121–123 Therefore, recent typical applications of zinc oxide-based nanomaterials in the Li–S battery are reviewed in the following paragraphs (Fig. 15).124,125

Finding a solution to improve the poor electronic conductivity is a big challenge for Li–S battery. In order to address this issue, sulfur is usually dispersed in a conductive matrix such as conductive polymers and carbon nanomaterials. Liang et al.126 used a classic two-step method consisting of hydrothermal procedure and heating treatment to make ZnO porous nanostructures. The synthesized S/ZnO composite material was heat treated at the temperature of 155 °C. They found that the size of composite material was lighter than pure ZnO material because the former only absorbed sulfur on the surface. Moreover, compared to pure S, the S/ZnO composite showed good cycling stability and high discharge capacity of 1414 mA h g−1. After 100 cycles, 662 mA h g−1 capacity was maintained at current density of 335 mA g−1, which is much better than pure S (470 mA h g−1 for the initial cycle and 169 mA h g−1 after 100 cycles). Surprisingly, the obtained columbic efficiency was up to 97%. The combination with zinc oxide increases conductivity as the insulating property of sulfur and its reduced products (Li2S/Li2S2). in addition, the dissolution of polysulfides (Li2Sn, 4 ≤ n ≤ 8) can be reduced effectively (Table 3).

Regarding carbon sources, zeolitic imidazolate frameworks (ZIFs) made a tremendous contribution (high thermal stability) to the Li–S battery.127–129 Importantly, zinc oxide still plays a key role in these nanomaterials. Meanwhile, other improvements had been made. Yu et al. proposed dual protection in electrodes by successfully utilizing tight self-assembled rGO skin to prepare reduced graphene oxide (rGO)-sulfur aerogel material and modifying them via atomic layer deposition (ALD).132 Polysulfides were protected incredibly around cathode due to the co-existence of ALD-oxide coating and rGO skin. Both of the two protective layers have the synergistic effect on improving the performance of Li–S better. They tried two metal oxides with various weight percentages of sulfur and found that the zinc oxide composite material demonstrated better properties than magnesium oxide composite in general. After that, they discovered 55 wt% sulfur loading G-S electrode, which was modified by ZnO, achieved discharge capacity of 998 mA h g−1 at current density of 0.2C. After 100 cycles, capacity of 846 mA h g−1 was still retained with the coulombic efficiency up to 92%. Based on the experiment, rGO along with ALD of zinc oxide acted as the valid barrier to constrain the polysulfides around cathode, showing to superior rate capacity, high coulombic efficiency and prominent cycling stability. The ZnO coating layer made the undeniable contributions for the large number of hydroxyl groups which trap polysulfide anions effectively. Gu et al.133 further developed a facile ball-milling method to synthesize sulfur/carbon nanotubes, which were wrapped by zinc oxide or nickel hydroxide (ZnO@S/CNT and Ni(OH)2@S/CNT). Their method was simple and controllable. By adding zinc oxide additives, performance was far better than the control sample because of strong interactions between ZnO and Sx2−, leading to higher adsorption energy for Li2S8. Furthermore, the ZnO@S/CNT composite displayed high reversible capacity and electrochemical conductivity over bare S/CNT composite due to the presence of zinc oxide. Meanwhile, after 70 charging–discharging cycles, the ZnO@S/CNT composite still maintained capacity of about 942 mA h g−1.

In brief, to face these challenges researchers did not give up and attempt to seek novel solutions of various nanomaterials including ZnO-based material. Our own viewpoints and suggestions follow. (1) Increase the void fraction of zinc oxide nanomaterial to further improve sulfur loading such as the porous zinc oxide material mentioned above. (2) Combining with suitable carbon material (e.g., rGO, CNT, etc.) to form composites could increase the use of sulfur based on the intimate connection between sulfur and carbon. (3) Add other appropriate materials such as metal compounds to improve electrochemical conductivity, reversible capacity and effective morphology. According to recent experiments, the synergistic effect in zinc oxide composite is so obvious that it surpasses commercial zinc oxide material.

4. Others

With the evolution of battery technology, a myriad of prototypes of metal–air battery have been introduced recently. Except for the two advanced lithium storage batteries mentioned above, the ZnO-based nanomaterials are also widely applied in many other fields. For example, the metal–air battery, which can be described as a breathing battery, has attracted great interest. In contrast to other advanced metal–air batteries, the zinc–air battery (Zn–air) is one of the strongest candidates to compete with LIBs and Li–S in the lithium storage battery. Besides, not only continuous even inexhaustible oxygen supply but also abundance and relative low-cost are the outstanding characteristics of Zn–air.134

When the cathode is fully exposed to oxygen in air, upon discharging, they will yield hydroxyl ions and then form zincate (Zn(OH)42−), releasing electrons. Due to limitation of the electrode material, the development of Zn–air is far from satisfactory at present. In spite of the fact that Zn–air has high available energy density (1086 Wh kg−1 including oxygen), low equivalent weight and other excellent features, the corrosion rate of the zinc anode electrode remains an unsolved issue. For example, reaction (7) shows a severe parasitic process, resulting in a gradual self-corrosion of metal zinc that degrades electrode performance and produces unnecessary hydrogen gas, especially for the nano-zinc anode. After recharging, zincate gradually comes back to the electrode where electrode geometry changes and grows branches, causing poor circulation.137,138 This may result from concentration gradients or irregular current distribution.139–141 Moreover, CO2 from air possibly enter electrolyte and react with OH− to a great extent. A great deal of pores can be blocked by precipitation of carbonates. These challenges mentioned above have been perplexing researchers in recent years but this review mainly focuses on the key role that zinc oxide material plays actually, and the modifications of zinc oxide material.

Commercial zinc electrode underperforms due to poor electrochemical conductivity between nanoparticles during discharging. In essence, zincate ions dehydrate to bulky zinc oxide on the surface of particles, leading to ZnO deposition within the void space, loose interconnectivity between nanoparticles, and even active materials peeling off from the electrode.142 It is well-known that zinc oxide material could act as a significant additive on passive film. Recently, zinc oxide–based composite was used in zinc–air batteries as well. Comparable to the conventional zinc anode, after charging the zinc oxide material is reduced to zinc material, which serves as anode in the zinc–air battery. This novel approach exhibits outstanding advantages, especially in carbon-coated zinc oxide material. The charging process drives the yolk–shell-like architecture because the zinc core has smaller volume than zinc oxide in the shell, which confines the formation and extension of the dendrites. Meanwhile, such special architecture also provides sufficient cushion buffer between core and shell in order to decrease the stress that coating layer suffers from initially during cycling.

As mentioned previously, morphology change and dendrite formation continue intractable and unsolved, which seriously restricts development of the zinc–air battery.143 Miyazaki et al. and Zhu et al. found that polymer coating was beneficial to electrode capacity and longevity.144,145 Inspired by this idea, Gan et al. adopted alkaline polymer film to cover ZnO particles, based on a polyvinyl alcohol (PVA (as traditional binder)) and poly (diallyldimethylammonium chloride) (PDDA) mixture in a rechargeable zinc–air battery.146 The general procedure is shown in Fig. 19(a). At 50 mA cm−2, cycle stability and electrochemical conductivity of ZnO/PVA/PDDA composite were superior to the pure ZnO electrode (utilizing PVA as binder), which are exhibited in Fig. 19(b)–(d), owing to confinement of dendrite growth and retention of zinc active nanomaterial. This PVA/PDDA polymer film offered abundant void space for volume change and ionic transfer during cycling. Moreover, in accordance to Fig. 19(b), the cyclic voltammogram of ZnO/PVA/PDDA composite electrode showed an anodic peak at 0.183 V, lower than that of the bare ZnO electrode, because PVA/PDDA film limited diffusion of the discharge product to some extent. Meanwhile, compared with bare ZnO material, the composite had a lower potential interval, which determined the faster electrochemical kinetics of the redox process. After charging, this system formed the yolk–shell-like structure to relieve the stress that the coating layer suffered from, thereby avoiding dendritic growth, severe volume variety and pulverization of electrode to some extent.

Meanwhile, there is another marked improvement of zinc oxide material, in alkaline medium, such as subjecting metal surface to an initial or mechanical treatment. Lee et al. conducted three contrasting investigations on pure zinc powder, zinc oxide powder (ZnO) and the modified zinc oxide material (ZnOPO) containing organic corrosion inhibitor (polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE)) and lead oxide, in order to achieve the best scheme to optimize this ZnO-based composite.147 They synthesized different electrodes to compare in detail via the pelletizing method and took the mechanism into consideration; results indicated that pure zinc electrode tended to generate severe hydrogen evolution.148,149 In contrast, the additive of lead oxide as a surfactant effectively postponed corrosion, and shifted the hydrogen evolution reaction to more negative side. Lead may be susceptible to oxidation, where the anodic part is enhanced and the cathodic part is suppressed. At the same time, PTFE prevented the corrosion rate and effectively protected the zinc oxide core, which is similar to the PVA/PDDA film.

In brief, the two abovementioned methods to modify zinc oxide and the ZnO-based nanomaterials have been gradually breaking through the numerous limitations in zinc–air battery development such as morphology change, volume variety and dendrite formation of anode.150 The ZnO-based nanomaterial has not been extensively investigated thus far.

5. Conclusions

In the field of LIB, zero- to three-dimensional electrode nanomaterials, which are also widely applied as electrochemical supercapacitors (ECs), have been discussed along with corresponding characteristics.151–155 As shown in this review, electrochemical performance has been promoted step by step with the optimization of novel morphologies and tuneable composition of ZnO-based composites.156–159 In our perspective, the proper degree of modification is the key point in LIBs. In addition, specific surface area, lithium ion transportation, volume change and subsequent pulverization are all significant challenges for commercial applications.160

By introducing the sulfur element, the cost of high-power batteries is cut in half. This approach has been extended to various applications.161 However, many serious problems remain unsolved. The insulating nature and volume change of sulfur and its polysulfides cause problems. Polysulfide dissolution results in irretrievable capacity fading and even safety risks. Several ideas for improvement follow. (1) Various morphologies and proportions should be taken into account to improve specific capacity and cycling performance (e.g., use of pre-prepared porous S/ZnO composite materials obtained leads to superior properties).162–164 (2) Research results indicate that coating structure and conductive matrix have the special ability to mitigate volume change and prohibit sulfur dissolution to some extent.165 (3) Use of smaller sulfur allotropes S2–4 or ultrafine sulfur effectively diminishes polysulfide dissolution because cyclo-S8 molecules determine this intrinsic problem to a great extent.166–168

Fabrication technology for nanomaterials is equally significant. (1) The hydrothermal procedure is a moderate and prevailing method in the preparation of ZnO-based nanomaterial. Various reactions take place at different temperatures, determining the morphology and its corresponding property. (2) The next most promising is the simple template-free method. Researchers have fully used adsorption between protonated MIM ligand (HMIM) and zinc oxide and the strong interactions between HMIM and carbon surface. This method does not require a template but achieves the effect of one. (3) The ball-milling synthetic method, a mechanochemical route, is utilized to grind various materials. It is simple and practical to make large amounts of metal oxide (e.g., zinc oxide) coating homogenous composite cathode material. Due to its economy and reproducibility, ball milling technology has been widely adopted for achieving high packing density material.

The recently developed Zn–air battery is expected to be a prominent EESD. Compared with the other two batteries, Zn–air batteries possess the characteristics of light weight, low toxicity, and environmental friendliness because the heavy cathode material is substituted by an O2-breathing electrode that is light weight and environment friendly.169 Nonetheless, several disadvantages constrain its rapid development in the future. First, parasitic reactions, non-uniform current densities and volume variety are serious issues that must be addressed. In addition, electrode corrosion, blockage and dendrite growth on the anode would occur constantly during cycles, causing capacity fade and even safety issues. Thus, more effective measurements should be taken such as using corrosion inhibitors and chemical additives. At the same time, despite ZnO-based nanomaterial in zinc–air batteries, this type of experiment has not been more extensive than in other commercial batteries. Its outstanding features have captured our attention recently because this special material easily turns itself into something like yolk–shell structure through charging, which plays a crucial part in later cycling. Moreover, the addition of other appropriate elements can decrease the corrosion reaction and hydrogen evolution to some extent. Therefore, we recommend further investigation of the coating layer and suitable additions of elements in ZnO-based nanomaterial. In future, researchers will seek to prevent the corrosion rate, dendritic growth and shape change by modifying structure and adding electrode additives.

The focus should be on comprehensive benefits, such as cost, safety, availability, and environmental impact rather than only high efficiency.170 This means that as abundant and environment-friendly materials, ZnO-based nanomaterials have a promising future for applications in EESDs such as LIB, Li–S, and Zn–air.
