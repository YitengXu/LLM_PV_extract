Correlation of polymeric compatibilizer structure to its impact on the morphology and function of P3HT:PCBM bulk heterojunctions

Abstract

The impact of various polymeric compatibilizers, including end-functionalized P3HTs and diblock copolymers containing P3HT, on the structure and function of poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT):[6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) bulk heterojunctions is presented. Careful analyses of small angle neutron scattering curves provide a measure of the miscibility of PCBM in P3HT, the average PCBM domain size, and the interfacial area between PCBM and the P3HT-rich phase in the uncompatibilized and compatibilized systems. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) also provides information regarding the changes in the crystallinity of P3HT due to the presence of the compatibilizer. Results show that most compatibilizers cause the domain sizes to decrease and the P3HT crystallinity to increase; however, some cause an increase in domain size, suggesting that they are not effective interfacial modifiers. The correlation of morphology with photovoltaic activity shows that the decreased domain size, increased crystallinity and increased interfacial area do not always result in improved power conversion efficiency (PCE). It appears that the introduction of an insulating molecule at the PCBM:P3HT interface as a compatibilizer results in a decrease in PCE. Thus, the presence of the compatibilizer at this interface dominates the photovoltaic activity, rather than the morphological control.

Introduction

Organic photovoltaic (OPV) devices based on the bulk heterojunction (BHJ) concept have attracted significant interest for sustainable solar energy conversion due to their low cost, flexibility, ease of processing, and straightforward thin film formation.18 There are three consecutive steps in the conversion of solar energy to electricity in BHJ solar cells: the generation of excitons by photon absorption, the dissociation of excitons at the donoracceptor interface into free charge carriers, and the transport of electrons and holes to the electrodes. Excitons can only travel a short distance (10 nm) before they recombine;911 thus, to facilitate exciton dissociation and charge transport, an ideal morphology of BHJ active layers that contain phase separated nanodomains that are 10 nm has been suggested. Based on this model, significant effort has been made to control and optimize the morphology of BHJ active layers by adjusting the donoracceptor ratio,12,13 by the application of thermal and vapor annealing,1416 and by processing with additives.1723

Another approach to control the morphology of BHJ active layers is to use diblock copolymers as biphasic compatibilizers. P3HT and PCBM phase separate at compositions that are used in OPV active layers; however, compatibilizers have the ability to moderate the domain sizes in a phase separated mixture and adjust the rate of phase segregation.2430 For instance, Frechet et al. reported that incorporating donoracceptor (P3HT-b-PCBM) block copolymers as compatibilizers in P3HT:PCBM mixtures24 modifies the phase separation behavior between donor and acceptor phases, resulting in an improvement of the device structure and stability. Wudl et al. synthesized a diblock copolymer comprised of P3HT as a donor and C60 as an acceptor, and found that the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of the resultant BHJ increased 35% with addition of 5 wt% of this diblock copolymer as a compatibilizer.27 Jo and coauthors used similar P3HT-b-C60 copolymers as compatibilizers and demonstrated a decrease of domain size in P3HT:PCBM mixtures with the addition of a small amount of P3HT-b-C60.28 The presence of the compatibilizer also decreases the rate of domain size coarsening and thus may improve the long-term stability of devices. Similarly, a P3HT-based triblock copolymer was introduced into a P3HT:PCBM active layer by Tsai et al., and devices exhibited a PCE of 4.4% with the addition of 1.5 wt% of this triblock copolymer.29 Chen et al. used P3HT-b-PEO to compatibilize donoracceptor interfaces, showing that at low loadings of P3HT-b-PEO, the domain size decreases due to the polar interaction between the methyl ester of PCBM and the EO repeat units.30

However, little work has been done to accurately determine the morphology of the active layer of BHJ solar cells with compatibilizers. Moreover, there is no report on the impact of the compatibilizers on the specific interfacial area (S/V) in conjugated polymer:fullerene blends. This is surprising, as the amount of donoracceptor interfacial area is a critical parameter because an increase of S/V will increase the probability of exciton dissociation and thus the amount of charge carriers produced. This shortage of detailed morphological characterization may be attributed to the limited contrast between the carbon-based conjugated polymer (donor) and carbon nanoparticle fullerene derivatives (acceptor) when optical, X-ray- or electron-based probes are used, which may lead to ambiguity in the identification of the resultant structure. Fortunately, neutron scattering provides significant contrast between the two components in this system, providing a technique that provides access to detailed structural and morphological information.

In the present work, several additive modifiers, including end-functionalized P3HT and diblock copolymers, have been synthesized, and the impact of these different compatibilizers on the thermal properties, morphology, and structure of P3HT:PCBM mixtures has been examined using differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) and small angle neutron scattering (SANS). These experiments provide quantifiable measures of the specific interfacial area and miscibility of amorphous PCBM in amorphous P3HT with and without compatibilizers, allowing these structural parameters to be correlated with photovoltaic properties of the resultant active layers.

Materials

[6,6]-Phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) was purchased from Nano-C and regioregular poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) was purchased from Rieke Metals (Sepiolid P200, Mw = 25000 g mol1, PDI = 1.6, regioregularity, 98%). Both were used without further purification. Compatibilizers used in this study were synthesized at Oak Ridge National Laboratory, and include poly(3-hexylthiophene) end-capped with 3-pyridine (P3HT-3Py, Mn = 6600 g mol1, PDI = 1.2, regioregularity, >95%) or with 2-pyridine (P3HT-2Py, Mn = 8000 g mol1, PDI = 1.2, regioregularity, >95%),31 and a diblock copolymer of P3HT and poly(ethylene oxide) (P3HT-b-PEO).30 A diblock copolymer of P3HT and poly(methyl methacrylate) (P3HT-b-PMMA) was purchased from Polymer Source. The chemical structures of the compatibilizers are presented in Scheme 1. Mixtures of P3HT:PCBM (1:0.8; wt/wt), which are denoted as P3HT:PCBM when uncompatibilized, were produced by co-dissolving P3HT, PCBM, and the compatibilizer in o-dichlorobenzene (ODCB). Solutions were then deposited dropwise on a gently heated (ca. 110 C) 11 mm diameter quartz disk window to accelerate drying. This procedure yields solid disks around 1 mm thick on top of quartz windows. All the samples were dried under vacuum at room temperature overnight to affect the removal of residual ODCB. Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC)

DSC studies were carried out using a TA Instruments temperature modulated DSC (TA Q1000). Indium was used as a standard for temperature and heat flow calibration. The heat capacity was evaluated with respect to a sapphire standard. Measurements were performed at a heating and cooling rate of 10 C min1 with dry nitrogen gas purged into the DSC cell at a flow rate of 50 mL min1. In our scans, endotherms are presented with downward deflection.

Small-angle neutron scattering

The SANS experiments were completed on the General Purpose SANS instrument at the High Flux Isotope Reactor at Oak Ridge National Laboratory. The raw data were corrected for scattering from the empty cell, detector dark current, and detector sensitivity. The corrected data were then normalized to an absolute scale using a Porasil-A standard. By evaluating the slope of IQ4versus Q4 at high Q (Q = 4/sin , where is the neutron wavelength and is the scattering angle), the contributions from incoherent scattering and thermal density fluctuation are eliminated, yielding the absolute coherent scattering.

Photovoltaic device fabrication and characterization

Solutions were prepared by dissolving P3HT:PCBM (1:0.8) in ODCB at a total solid concentration of 20 mg mL1. To fabricate the compatibilized active layers, the compatibilizer was added to the P3HT:PCBM solution at a level of 1%. Indium tin oxide (ITO) glass substrates were first washed with detergent and then cleaned in an ultrasonic bath using DI water, acetone and isopropyl alcohol. The cleaned ITO substrates were subsequently treated with UV-ozone for 20 minutes. Filtered PEDOT:PSS (Baytron P 4083) was then spin cast on the UV-treated ITO glass at 4000 rpm for 40 s. After baking the PEDOT:PSS films in air at 140 C for 20 minutes, the photovoltaic layer P3HT:PCBM/compatibilizer was spin cast at 900 rpm for 60 s, followed by annealing at 140 C for 20 minutes under vacuum. The active layer films created by these protocols have thicknesses of 80 nm. Finally, to create the OPV device, 15 nm Ca and 80 nm Al was thermally deposited through a shadow mask. The currentvoltage (IV) characteristics of the photovoltaic cells were measured using a computer-controlled Newport system under AM1.5G illumination at 100 mW cm2.

Our recent neutron scattering results clearly indicate that P3HT:PCBM (1:0.8) thin films do not exist as a two-phase structure, but as three phases comprising P3HT crystals, a pure amorphous PCBM phase, and a miscible phase of amorphous P3HT and amorphous PCBM.3638 The data reported here are analyzed similarly, but to quantitatively analyze the SANS curves, the scattering length density (SLD) of each phase is required, which requires knowledge of the chemical composition and density of the phase. The density of crystalline P3HT is reported to be 1.12 g cm3,39 but the density of amorphous P3HT is not reported. The density of amorphous P3HT is approximated as 90% of the crystalline density40 or 1.01 g cm3. The scattering length density, b, of crystalline P3HT and amorphous P3HT are then calculated to be bP3HT,c = 0.69 106 2 and bP3HT,a = 0.62 106 2, respectively.41 The density of crystalline PCBM formed by casting from ODCB is reported to be 1.67 g cm3.42 Following the same assumption that amorphous = 0.9cryst, the SLD of amorphous PCBM is calculated to be 4.4 106 2, which agrees with the published values43 and our neutron reflectivity results.36 From these values, the SLD of the miscible P3HT:PCBM phase, bM, can be determined:

bM = bP3HT,a(1 FB) + bPCBM,aFB(2)
where FB is the volume fraction of PCBM in the P3HT:PCBM miscible phase. FB has been reported to be 20%,38 which results in a scattering length density of the miscible phase, bM = 1.38 106 2.

In the neutron scattering experiments, the measured scattering intensity is a sum of the scattering between the P3HT crystal and the PCBM pure phase, between the P3HT crystal and the miscible amorphous phase, and between the PCBM pure phase and the miscible phase. The contribution of each phase pair to the total scattering is proportional to the square of the difference of the scattering length density of the two phases, (bi bj).2 Examination of the scattering length density contrast (SLDC) of these three pairs clearly shows that the contrast between the P3HT crystals (0.69 106 2) and the P3HT:PCBM miscible phase (1.38 106 2) is very small relative to the contrast between pure PCBM (4.4 106 2) and either P3HT-containing phase (or a mixture of the two). Moreover, the SLDC between PCBM and P3HT crystals can be approximated as equal to the SLDC between PCBM and the miscible PCBM/P3HT phase. Based on this analysis, the scattering is dominated by the structure of the pure PCBM phase. Hence, the size, interfacial areas, and scattering length density contrast values that are reported refer to those of the PCBM-pure phase dispersed in a P3HT-rich phase. It is important to emphasize that this P3HT-rich phase is actually a mixture of P3HT crystals distributed among a miscible mixture of amorphous P3HT and PCBM, but this mixture can be represented as a single phase because the SLDs of the crystalline P3HT and the P3HT:PCBM miscible phase are similar.

In order to complete the analysis, the SLD of the P3HT-rich phase (bp) must be estimated and then used in fitting the absolute scattering intensities to models to extract the size and composition of the phases. Discussion

The morphology of the active layer in an OPV device dramatically impacts its photovoltaic performance. The excitons that are formed must have a pathway to a donoracceptor interface to dissociate efficiently and connected conduits for charge transport to the electrodes. The qualitative and quantitative analysis of the neutron scattering of P3HT:PCBM mixtures with different compatibilizers offers unprecedented insight into the thermodynamics and structure of these additive-modified BHJ thin films. The results and analyses show that the morphology of the P3HT:PCBM mixtures changes with the addition of compatibilizers, as represented through changes in the specific interfacial area, S/V, and correlation length, . It is crucial to understand the precise changes that occur upon compatibilizer addition, as these parameters are intimately linked to the generation and dissociation of excitons, and the transport of the free charges to the electrodes.

The increase of the amount of interfacial area between the PCBM and the P3HT-rich phase provides more opportunity for exciton dissociation.5154 The generated exciton must dissociate into free charges before it recombines, where this dissociation occurs most readily at the donoracceptor interface.5154 The free charges can then transport to the electrodes if sufficient transport channels exist. Therefore, the presence of interfacial area between the P3HT and PCBM is crucial to allow efficient exciton dissociation and a reduction in exciton recombination. In general, the addition of a compatibilizer shows an increase in the interfacial area between the PCBM and the P3HT-rich phase, which is conducive to more effective exciton dissociation. In particular, the sample that is compatibilized with 1% P3HT-b-PEO increases the interfacial area by more than a factor of two; both diblock copolymers show an increase in interfacial area, commensurate with smaller domain size. Samples compatibilized with 3Py end-functionalized P3HT show a significant decrease in the interfacial area between PCBM and P3HT, which should inhibit exciton dissociation. It is worth noting that the S/V determined by this analysis follows the same trend as 1/ because 1 and 2 do not change much with the addition of compatibilizers.

The change in structure manifest in S/V, however, can be beneficial or detrimental to photovoltaic performance. A decrease in domain size may increase the propensity of the exciton to find an interface to allow exciton dissociation, however the presence of the compatibilizer at the P3HT:PCBM interface may limit the exciton dissociation at the interface, particularly if the compatibilizer has poor transport properties. Also, a smaller domain size may result in loss of connectivity of donor or acceptor domains throughout the sample, resulting in trapping of charges that prevents their transport to the electrodes.

Moreover, previous studies ascribe the increased efficiency of P3HT:PCBM active layers with thermal annealing to an increase in P3HT crystallinity,55 which results in improved hole transport. It is reasoned that the increase in charge carrier mobility is due to an increase of the in-plane stacking of P3HT, allowing charges to move easily from one grain to another.56,57 The crystallinity of the samples that are compatibilized with block copolymers (P3HT-b-PMMA-1 and P3HT-b-PEO-1) are 24% and 28% (assuming Hf = 37 J g1) respectively, which are levels of crystallinity greater than that of uncompatibilized P3HT:PCBM (21%). Hence, the increase of crystallinity of P3HT in the samples that are compatibilized with block copolymers should facilitate hole transport.

Similarly, the samples that are compatibilized with block copolymers (P3HT-b-PMMA-1 and P3HT-b-PEO-1) exhibit a significant decrease in domain size, which corresponds to an increase in S/V, which should increase the propensity of the exciton to find an interface for dissociation, provide more pathways connecting the streams to the rivers,38 and should facilitate charge transport. However, the systems that are compatibilized with block copolymers actually show a lower PCE than the uncompatibilized sample. As mentioned in the Introduction section, there are three consecutive steps in the conversion of solar energy to electricity in BHJ solar cells: the generation of excitons by photon absorption, the travel of excitons to the donoracceptor interface and dissociation into free charge carriers, and the transport of electrons and holes to the electrodes. The structural studies show that the morphology improvements should result in a higher probability that excitons reach an interface and that the increased crystallinity should improve hole transport. One explanation for the observed decrease in PCE is that the dissociation of excitons at the donoracceptor interface is altered by the compatibilizers. The presence of a non-conductive block in the diblock copolymers at the donoracceptor interface appears to inhibit exciton dissociation at the interface and increases the series resistance, resulting in a decrease of PCE.

The addition of the 2-Py end-functionalized P3HT as a compatibilizer shows a different relationship between morphology alteration and OPV activity. This sample shows a decrease in domain size with addition of the compatibilizer, a commensurate improvement in S/V, and an increase of crystallinity of P3HT from 21% to 24% (assuming Hf = 37 J g1), both of which suggest improved probability of diffusion of excitons to a donoracceptor interface and facilitated charge transport to the electrodes. For this sample, the improved morphological changes do translate to improved photovoltaic activity, with an increase of JSC and PCE. One of the advantages of using end-functionalized P3HT as a compatibilizer is that there is no insulating block, such as the PMMA or PEO blocks present in the block copolymers studied. Therefore, the improvement in the morphology combined with a design (absence of an insulating block) that does not limit the exciton transport and/or dissociation at the donoracceptor interface results in an improvement of the PCE is an important hallmark of the pyridyl-terminated P3HTs. It is interesting to note that the system that is compatibilized with the 3-Py end functionalized P3HT shows little change in the domain size, S/V, and crystallinity, but its addition results in an increase in PCE. One interpretation of this result is that the presence of 3-Py end functionalized P3HT at the donoracceptor interface facilitates the exciton dissociation, resulting in an increase of JSC and PCE.

Conclusion

The small angle neutron scattering curves of mixtures of P3HT and PCBM that are compatibilized by end-functionalized P3HT or diblock copolymers are carefully analyzed to provide detailed structural information of the BHJ thin films, and the resultant structural changes are correlated with their photovoltaic activity.

The SANS analysis demonstrates that the presence of the compatibilizers alters the average domain size and the specific interfacial area between the pure PCBM and P3HT-rich phase in these P3HT:PCBM mixtures. Aspects of the morphology are significantly improved with addition of the block copolymer or 2-Py end functionalized P3HT, exhibiting an increase in crystallinity and specific interfacial area and decreasing the domain size. The domain size, interfacial area or crystallinity of the 3-Py end-functionalized P3HT samples however exhibits very little change.

Correlation of these results with the photovoltaic performance of these bulk heterojunctions as OPV active layers indicates that these morphological changes do not necessarily lead to the creation of bulk-heterojunction structures with improved photovoltaic activity. For instance, samples with smaller domain size, more crystallinity, and increased interfacial area between phases exhibit a decrease in power conversion efficiency if the compatibilizer contains an insulating block (i.e. diblock copolymers), while samples that are modified with molecules that have good charge transport (i.e. end-functionalized P3HT) exhibit improved power conversion efficiencies regardless of the morphological changes.

Thus, there appear to be clear trade-offs when the impact of the compatibilizer at the donoracceptor interface is considered: the ability to reduce domain sizes is generally beneficial, but the impact on the change in the photophysics of the active layer with the interfacial modifier must also be considered. The results presented here suggest that an insulating block in the compatibilizer is detrimental to performance, ostensibly because it hinders exciton transport to the interface and/or its dissociation by increasing series resistance. This effect seems to be more important in determining the power conversion efficiency of the resultant BHJ than morphological alterations in crystallinity, interfacial area, or domain size.
