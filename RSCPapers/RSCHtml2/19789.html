Abstract

This feature article highlights the recent development of coreâ€“shell SnO2 nanostructures for their potential applications, i.e. optical, electrical, photo-electrochemical, photo-catalytic and gas sensing. Current studies reveal that the coreâ€“shell SnO2 nanocrystals could pave the way for developing new challenging devices because coreâ€“shell structures exhibit significant improvement of different physical properties, e.g. electrical, optical, electrochemical, etc. Here, we mainly highlight the impacts of coreâ€“shell structures of SnO2 nanocrystals on optical, electrical, photo-electrochemical, photo-catalytic and gas sensing properties. To begin with, we illustrate general synthesis methodologies for preparing coreâ€“shell SnO2 nanostructures and describe the structural changes due to the formation of coreâ€“shell nanocrystals. Successively, we emphasize the fundamental understanding of free charge carrier generation, charge transfer, charge separation, and interfacial charge transfer of the coreâ€“shell structures. The chemical and electronic mechanisms for SnO2 sensor performance have been discussed. We discuss a general analysis of the band structure of the heterojunction formed between the SnO2 core and shell materials. Recent findings reveal that the photoelectrochemical, photocatalytic and gas sensing properties are enhanced manifold in coreâ€“shell SnO2 nanocrystals. Finally an outlook on the prospects of this research field is given.

1. Introduction

Tin oxide (SnO2) is an important metal-oxide, and an n-type wide band gap (3.6 eV at 300 K) semiconductor.1â€“3 On account of its outstanding electrical, optical, and electrochemical properties, SnO2 offers a wide range of applications in solar cells,4,5 catalytic support materials,6 transparent electrodes,7,8 and solid-state chemical sensors.9â€“13 These unique properties have enthused us to design new synthesis methodologies for well-controlled SnO2 nanostructures. There are numerous synthesis methods available in the literature such as discharge,14 laser ablation,15 solution,16 vaporâ€“liquidâ€“solid (VLS),17 solâ€“gel,18 hydro(solvo)thermal,19â€“22 microwave1etc. to prepare various morphologies of SnO2 such as nanoparticles,23 nanorods,24 nanodisks,25 nanosheets,26 nanobelts,27 nanowires,28etc. It is evident that SnO2 is one of the most important and extensively used metal oxide semiconductor materials for gas sensors due to its high sensitivity and low cost. Over the past decades, significant attention has been paid to enhancing the sensing performance of the SnO2-based gas sensor.29â€“32 Again, SnO2 also finds application as photocatalytic materials.33 However, it is found that pure SnO2 has some limitations owing to its low surface area and low stability.3 To combat these problems, fabrication of coreâ€“shell structures of SnO2 is found to be promising.

Coreâ€“shell structures of SnO2 are found to be promising materials to improve the performance of various SnO2-based devices. The photocatalytic activities of SnO2 are found to be increased when coreâ€“shell structures are formed due to efficient charge separation. There are a number of studies associated with the improvement of the photocatalytic activity of coreâ€“shell structured SnO2 such as SnO2â€“TiO2,34 SnO2â€“ZnO,35 SnO2â€“CdS,36etc. It is found that the appropriate shell materials would serve as additional electron sources that can significantly improve the electron conduction in SnO2 which improves the gas-sensing response.31 Again, coupling SnO2 (large bandgap 3.6 eV) with another smaller bandgap semiconductor also facilitates the charge separation by accumulating electrons and holes in separate particles which will enhance the photo electrochemical properties of SnO2.37â€“41

A few review articles are in the literature on the gas-sensing applications of SnO2.42,43 GÃ¶pel et al.42 have mainly focused on the development of SnO2 nanostructures for gas sensing. Ma et al.43 wrote a mini-review on various aspects on the performances of SnO2-based gas sensors. Batzill et al.44 have highlighted the gas sensing properties of SnO2 and their controlling factors. There are a few review articles on the photoelectrochemistry of transparent SnO2,45 synthesis and applications of one-dimensional SnO2 nanostructures,46 application of SnO2:F films in solar cells47 and synthesis and applications of hierarchical SnO2 nanostructures.48 Interestingly, there is no review article on the impact of coreâ€“shell structures of SnO2 on its potential applications.

The main focus of this feature article is to address the important questions: how the coreâ€“shell structures of SnO2 nanocrystals influence optical and electrical, photo-electrochemical, photo-catalytic and gas sensing properties. 2. General synthesis methods for preparing coreâ€“shell structures of SnO2 nanocrystals

The synthesis of coreâ€“shell nanocrystals is typically classified into two major categories: â€œtop-downâ€ and â€œbottom-upâ€.49 The bottom-up approach has proven to be more appropriate for the synthesis of coreâ€“shell nanocrystals. The most vital step during the synthesis of coreâ€“shell nanoparticles is to maintain a uniform coating and to control the shell thickness. Coreâ€“shell nanoparticles are typically synthesized using a two-step process, the synthesis of the core is the first step and the synthesis of the shell is the second step. The synthesis techniques of coreâ€“shell nanoparticles can be classified into two types.

(i) The core particles are synthesized first and then the shell is formed by surface modification.50â€“52

(ii) The core particles are synthesized in situ which is followed by surface coating.53,54 In this method, the core particles are synthesized first using appropriate reactants in the presence of a surface modifier; then more reactants are added to form the shell in situ.55 The shell material is selectively deposited on the modified core surface and coreâ€“shell particles are formed. The main disadvantage of this process is that impurities may be trapped between the core and the shell layers.

Temperature is the most important reaction parameter which is being used to control the reaction kinetics for coreâ€“shell nanoparticle formation. The working temperature range depends mostly on the reaction mechanism of core or shell formation. In general, a low temperature is favorable for coreâ€“shell nanoparticle synthesis.49 The proper band alignment and the small lattice mismatch between the shell and the core are the important parameters for choosing core and shell materials.49 Due to the lattice mismatch, a lattice strain will develop which will produce surface defects in the interface. Cozzoli et al.56 have reported the influence of interfacial energy on coreâ€“shell structure formation. It is recommended to choose shell materials with less lattice mismatch with the core.

In the view of SnO2-based coreâ€“shell nanoparticles, significant efforts have been devoted in the last decade to synthesize high quality SnO2-based coreâ€“shell nanoparticles. The chemical and the physical properties of coreâ€“shell SnO2 nanostructures depend on the nature of the shell. There are few techniques that can be taken care for the formation of a uniform shell coating over SnO2 core nanoparticles. The techniques are (i) sequential addition of the reactants, (ii) the proper choice of the shell forming agent, (iii) control over the rate of addition of the shell forming agent, (iv) control over the reaction temperature and (v) the appropriate selection of organic ligands adhered to the surface of the core. There are different chemical synthesis methods available in the literature such as hydrothermal, sonochemical, colloidal, chemical vapor deposition, etc. to prepare coreâ€“shell SnO2 nanocrystals.36,57â€“60 In this section, we briefly discuss some of the current methods for preparing various coreâ€“shell structures of SnO2 nanocrystals.

2.1. Hydrothermal method

This is an outstanding method for generating coreâ€“shell nanocrystals with controlled crystal sizes and good dispersibility in solutions. It refers to a chemical synthesis procedure within a sealed autoclave environment under high pressure and temperature (often above the critical point of the solvent). The hydrothermal method is being used for the synthesis of SnO2-based coreâ€“shell nanoparticles.36,57 Patra et al.36 have used this method for the synthesis of coreâ€“shell SnO2â€“CdS nanorods by using a single-source precursor cadmium thiobenzoate complex for the CdS shell. They have modified the surface of SnO2 nanorods by cetyl trimethyl ammonium bromide (CTAB) using ultrasonic irradiation. The detail synthesis procedure is represented in Fig. 1a. The TEM image of the as-prepared sample is also given in the inset of Fig. 1a. The main advantage of this synthesis method is the use of a single source precursor for the CdS shell. The surface capping agent CTAB is being used for creating a uniform shell thickness. Fan et al.57 have used a hydrothermal process for the fabrication of ZnO nanostructures hierarchically assembled on SnO2 nanowire backbones. Firstly, the SnO2 nanowires were coated with ZnO nanoparticles as seeds by a dip-coating technique. Then, the SnO2 nanowire covered substrates were immersed into an aqueous solution of zinc nitrate (Zn(NO3)2Â·6H2O) and hexamethylenetetramine (C6H12N4). The main advantage of this synthesis technique is that the morphology of the ZnO shell can be tuned by adjusting the salt concentration, reaction time, and additives. Zhang et al.58 have reported an innovative hydrothermal shell-by-shell templating strategy appropriate for the preparation of SnO2 rattle-type nanospheres. First, they have used an organic capping agent (PVP) to anchor the SiO2 nanoparticles and prevent the aggregation of nanoparticles in solution. Then, the SnO2 nanospheres were coated with silica and used as templates to prepare SnO2 rattle-type nanospheres. The fabrication process is a three-step procedure, and each step is well controlled (Fig. 1b). Peng et al.34 have used a hydrothermal process for the synthesis of TiO2 coated SnO2 nanotubes with different SnO2 contents. It is clear from the above discussion that the hydrothermal method is an effective approach for the synthesis of SnO2-based coreâ€“shell nanoparticles.

2.2. Sonochemical method

An alternative approach for the formation of SnO2-based coreâ€“shell nanoparticles is the sonochemical synthesis by employing acoustic waves. In this method, the required amount of SnO2 core materials is taken in an appropriate solvent and sonicated. After sonication, the required amount of the shell forming agent was added to form coreâ€“shell nanostructures. The ultrasound helps to initiate reaction leading to growth of shell and disperse the nanoparticles in solution. Gao et al.59 have used a simple sonochemical approach for the preparation of SnO2â€“CdS coreâ€“shell heterostructures. They have prepared SnO2 core particles by thermal evaporation of metallic Sn powders. Then, cadmium chloride and thiourea were added to water in a sonication cell and the reaction mixture was irradiated with a high-intensity ultrasonication (100 W, 40 kHz). Argon gas was bubbled through the reaction mixture prior to the sonication to expel dissolved oxygen. This method is an effective approach for the synthesis of SnO2-based coreâ€“shell nanoparticles.

2.3. Colloidal method

Another approach for the synthesis of SnO2-based coreâ€“shell nanoparticles is the colloidal synthesis. This method requires three components: core and shell precursors, organic surfactants and solvents.61 Upon heating the reaction mixtures to a sufficiently high temperature, the precursors chemically transform into active atomic or molecular species followed by the formation of coreâ€“shell nanocrystals whose subsequent growth is greatly affected by the presence of surfactant molecules. The advantages of this method are that it can be used for large-scale production, it is safe, convenient, energy efficient, etc. Kamat et al.60 have synthesized CdS-capped SnO2 colloids in aqueous medium and this method involves the adsorption of Cd2+ ions onto the interior core of SnO2 followed by the exposure of the solution to H2S. The as-prepared CdS-capped SnO2 colloids were found to be stable for several days. Again, it is possible to vary the thickness of the CdS shell by controlling the concentration of Cd2+. A simple two-step colloidal method has been used by Li et al.62 for the preparation of ZnS coated SnO2 nanowires. Initially, SnO2 core particles are immersed in Na2S solution for a certain time. Then, the samples are immersed in Zn(NO3)2 solution for the same time. The thickness of the ZnS shell is also tuned by controlling the amount of the shell precursor and deposition time.

2.4. Chemical vapor deposition

Chemical vapor deposition (CVD) is being used to produce high-purity, high-performance coreâ€“shell materials. In typical CVD, the wafer (substrate) is exposed to one or more volatile precursors, which react and/or decompose on the substrate surface to produce the desired deposit. The advantages of this technique are (i) uniform distribution over large areas, (ii) absence of compositional gradients across the substrate, and (iii) more selective area deposition because of higher activation energy for reaction with foreign substances. Mathur et al.63 have used two-step chemical vapor deposition (CVD) in combination with the metal-catalyzed vaporâ€“liquidâ€“solid (VLS) growth mechanism as a new approach to synthesize TiO2 coated SnO2 nanowires. They have utilized the Sn(OtBu)4 and Ti(OiPr)4 complexes in the CVD process. The thickness of the TiO2 shell was adjusted by controlling the precursor flux and deposition time. Kim et al.32 have fabricated coreâ€“shell SnO2â€“ZnO nanofibers by means of a novel two-step process. First, SnO2 core nanofibers were synthesized by electrospinning. Sequentially, ZnO shell layers were grown by CVD in a controlled manner on the core nanofibers. Hotchandani et al.64 have developed a photo-electrochemical cell coupled with an SnO2â€“CdSe nanocrystalline semiconductor electrode which has been prepared by sequential deposition of SnO2 and CdSe films onto an optically transparent electrode. The disadvantages of the CVD technique are (i) safety and contamination, (ii) poisonous nature of hydrides and carbonyls, and (iii) high cost of chemicals. 3.1. Structural information

3.2. Optical and electrical properties

SnO2 is an n-type semiconductor with a large band gap (Eg = 3.6 eV at 300 K)1,3 and oxygen vacancies are known to be the most common defects in SnO2 and usually act as radiative centers in luminescence processes. These oxygen vacancies typically act as deep defect donors in SnO2 and would cause the formation of new donor levels in the band gap.1 The photoluminescence (PL) emission of SnO2 is due to the transition of an electron from a level close to the conduction band edge to a deeply trapped hole in the bulk of the SnO2 nanocrystals.87 Enhancement of luminescence (by elimination of non-radiative pathways) of SnO2 can be achieved by surface coating with another band gap semiconductor material. The choice of the shell material is vital for the localization of the electronâ€“hole pair. A recent review on coreâ€“shell semiconducting nanocrystals discusses the fundamental properties of coreâ€“shell structures of IIâ€“VI, IVâ€“VI, and IIIâ€“V semiconductors.88 SnO2 nanocrystals can also be coated with an insulating inorganic shell to enhance the fluorescence. Depending on the shell materials, coreâ€“shell SnO2 nanostructures may be of the following three types.

1. Type I â€“ where the band gap of core SnO2 is smaller than that of the shell. Both the conduction and valence band edges of the core SnO2 lie within the band gap of the shell, which confines both electrons and holes in the SnO2 core.

2. Reverse Type I â€“ where the core SnO2 has a wider band gap than the shell, and the conduction and valence band edges of the shell lie within those of the core SnO2. The lowest available exciton energy separation occurs when the charge carriers are localized in the shell.

3. Type II â€“ where the valence and conduction band edges of core SnO2 are both lower and higher than the band edges of the shell. The lowest energy separation of the electron and the hole will occur when the hole is confined in the core SnO2 valence band and the electron is confined in the shell conduction band.

3.4. Photocatalytic properties

Photocatalytic activity depends on the ability of the catalyst to create electronâ€“hole pairs and formation of free radicals for secondary reactions. Most commonly used heterogeneous photocatalysts are semiconductors,117 SnO2 particularly has attracted great attention.33,118,119 During the photo-excitation, an electron is excited from the valance band to the conduction band, leaving behind a hole. The photogenerated electron and hole can recombine, or else the hole may react with an electron donor such as a reagent (e.g. a pollutant). However, the fast recombination rate of the photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs in pure SnO2 hampers,120 thus the coreâ€“shell structures of SnO2 are being used to enhance the photocatalytic efficiency by increasing the charge separation and extending the photo-responding range into the visible region.

Considering the band structures of a SnO2-based coreâ€“shell nanocatalyst,121,122 different work functions lead to the negatively charged carriers moving from SnO2 (the material with low work function) to other semiconductor shell materials (the one with a high work function) until their Fermi levels align; thus, an electrostatic field is created at the interface. At thermal equilibrium, the conduction band (CB) and valence band (VB) of SnO2 and other semiconductor shell materials bend, and a depletion layer forms around the interface. When the SnO2-based coreâ€“shell nanocatalyst is radiated with UV light with photon energy higher or equal to the band gaps of SnO2 and other semiconductors, electrons (eâˆ’) in the VB can be excited to the CB with simultaneous generation of the same amount of holes (h+) in the VB. The photogenerated electrons and holes are separated under the influence of the electrostatic field induced by different work functions. Thus, electrons move to the SnO2 side and holes to the shell side. Then, the electronic acceptors like adsorbed O2 can easily trap the photoelectrons to produce a superoxide anion radical (Ë™O2âˆ’).123 The formed Ë™O2âˆ’ might either attack organic molecules or provide hydroxyl radical species (Ë™OH) by reacting with photogenerated electrons.124 The photoinduced holes can be easily trapped by OHâˆ’ to further produce a Ë™OH species, which is an extremely strong oxidant for the partial or complete mineralization of organic chemicals.

3.5. Gas sensing properties

In principle, gas sensing by semiconducting metal oxide is based on the oxidationâ€“reduction reaction of the detected gases flowing on the semiconductor surface, which causes a significant resistance change in proportion to the gas concentration.130,131 For a given type of material, the sensor performance depends on various factors such as the working temperature, exposed crystal planes, grain size, doping or surface modification, and so on.

Among the different semiconducting metal oxides, an SnO2 gas sensor was initially proposed132 and patented in 1962.133 SnO2 is one of the most significant material for use as a gas sensor for domestic, commercial and industrial purposes due to its low operating temperatures, high sensitivities, mechanical simplicity and low manufacturing costs.134,135 Extensive research has been conducted on understanding the chemical and electronic mechanisms that preside over SnO2 sensor performance and on extending SnO2 sensors to detection of other gases, including trace amounts of toxic gases.136,137 Unfortunately, lot of difficulties exists with SnO2 sensors and this is typically due to the low surface area and low stability. SnO2 sensors often provide an undesired signal when an inert gas is present in the environment. SnO2 sensors also tend to lack long-term stability and require a moderately high operating temperature. SnO2-based sensors also involve long response and recovery times. One fashionable way to overcome these problems is to construct various SnO2-based coreâ€“shell heterostructures.

For SnO2, the sensing mechanism is typically explained by the space-charge model.138 In this model, the sensitivity of SnO2 is strongly dependent on the change of the space-charge length under different gas atmospheres, resulting in higher sensitivity of the sensing materials with a smaller size.139 However, the electron transport mechanism of the coreâ€“shell heterostructures should be significantly different from that of the pure SnO2 and other shell materials due to the formation of heterojunctions at their interfaces.
