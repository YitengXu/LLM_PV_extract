Reversible switching transitions of stimuli-responsive shape changing polymers

Abstract

The past decade has witnessed significant advancements in stimuli-responsive shape changing polymers. The reversible switching transition is the most important feature of stimuli-responsive polymers, which determines the shape changing effect. This article presents a comprehensive review of the various reversible switching transitions and recent progress in stimuli-responsive shape changing polymers. The advantages and limitations of the various stimuli-responsive polymers are discussed. Future research directions are suggested.

1 Introduction

The last decade has witnessed remarkable advancements in smart/stimuli-responsive materials and products, which will play a significant role in all areas of human life. The smart materials and products mimic biological systems such as venus flytrap leaves which can snap shut on doomed insect prey. Popular in plants, these stimuli-responsive functions are more common in animals, such as sea cucumbers which change their stiffness upon stimulation. Inspired by nature, many synthetic stimuli-responsive shape changing materials have been developed.1–25 Synthetic stimuli-responsive shape changing polymers26 significantly change their shapes in addition to other mechanical/physical properties such as stiffness, phase separation, surface morphology, permeability, optical properties, and electrical properties, upon small variation of environmental conditions.

Stimuli-responsive shape changing polymers broadly fall into three categories: one-way shape memory polymers (OWSMPs), two-way shape changing polymers (TWSCPs), and stimuli-responsive hydrogels with significant volume change as shown in Fig. 1. OWSMPs can fix a temporary shape and recover their original shape upon stimulation. TWSCPs change their shape upon stimulation without the requirement of pre-deformation or programming. They may recover their original shape upon removal of the stimulation, or changing the amplitude or direction of the stimulation. Stimuli-responsive hydrogels change their volume upon environmental stimulation. Although the basic shape changing effect of stimuli-responsive shape changing polymers is simple elongation, bending, and swelling, through proper material structure and stimuli design, complicated shape changing effects such as walking, rotation, and vibration can be achieved. The general mechanism of the stimuli-responsive shape changing effect is attributed to the reversible switching structure of stimuli-responsive polymers. This reversible switching structure may be responsive to stimuli such as heat, electricity, magnetic field, water/chemicals, light, enzymes, ions, ultrasound, and pH.


Fig. 1 Stimuli-responsive shape changing polymers change their shapes in different ways as the response to different stimulations.

The detailed mechanisms of the stimuli-responsive shape changing effect of synthetic polymers can be ascribed to 6 reasons depending on the molecular and material structures: phase change causes entropy elasticity recovery (e.g. OWSMPs), movement of mesogens causes orientation change (e.g. liquid crystal elastomers), trans–cis isomerization causes conformation change (e.g. azobenzene based polymers), reversible crosslinking causes internal stress redistribution (e.g. anthracene and coumarin based polymers), hydrophilic/hydrophobic changes cause swelling/deswelling (stimuli-responsive hydrogels), and mismatched mechanical/thermal properties cause bending (laminated composites). Electroactive polymers (EAPs) (also called artificial muscles) are another type of important stimuli-responsive materials which have been well studied. The activation mechanism of EAPs is either because of coulomb forces (dielectric EAPs) or transport of ions (ionic EAPs).27,28 The mechanisms of some other stimuli-responsive shape changing polymers have not been fully understood, but may be related to photo-thermal effects, such as a CNTs/polydimethylsiloxane elastomer29,30 and crosslinked polyarylamide based nanofibers.31

Reversible switching transition is the most important feature of stimuli-responsive materials,32–53 which determines the shape changing effect and affects other properties of stimuli-responsive materials. This review is intended to give a comprehensive up-to-date survey on the various reversible switching transitions followed by a brief introduction of the recent progress of the various stimuli-responsive polymers. In addition to introducing new mechanisms of stimuli-responsive polymers, future research directions and challenges of this promising area are briefly discussed.

2 One-way shape memory polymers (OWSMPs)

OWSMPs can “memorize” their original (or permanent) shape and rapidly change their shape from a temporary shape to their original (or permanent) shape by stimulation such as heat, light, electricity, water, cooling, ions, and pH.54–56 Numerous OWSMPs have been fabricated in labs and several thermoresponsive OWSMPs are commercially available, such as DiAPLEX (SMP Technologies Inc., originally from Mitsubishi Heavy Industries), Veriflex®, Verilyte™ and Veritex™ (Cornerstone Research Group, Inc.), Tecoflex® (Lubrizol Advanced Materials), and TEMBO® (Composite Technology Development, Inc.). OWSMPs can be widely used in many areas such as medicine,45,57–71 textiles,67,72–77 aerospace,78–81 sensors and actuators,82,83 household products,84 data storage media,85 self-healing materials,86–91 and MEMS.92

As shown in Fig. 2, a relatively stable polymer network and reversible switching transition of the polymer are the two prerequisites for the one-way shape memory effect (OWSME). The crosslinking structure of OWSMPs determines the original shape, which can be acted by molecular entanglement, crystalline phases, chemical crosslinking, or interpenetrated networks.51,93–96 The lock which can be opened later in the network is the reversible switching transition responsible for fixing and unfixing the temporary shape. If the OWSMPs are deformed, the internal stress produced during deformation can be stored in the polymer network if the reversible switches are locked because of the low mobility of the macromolecules. Upon stimulation such as heating, photoirradiation, or ions, which can open the reversible interaction, the mobility of the macromolecules is increased. Therefore, the internal stress is released, leading to the shape recovery of the polymer. Many reversible switching transitions have been used in the case of OWSMPs, such as crystallization/melting transition,51,94,97–99 vitrification/glass transition,42,51,97–135 liquid crystal anisotropic/isotropic transition,136–142 reversible molecule crosslinking reactions,143–148 and supramolecular association/dissociation.149–152 The typical reversible molecule crosslinking reactions as the switching transitions include dimerization reaction,53,147,148,153–155 Diels–Alder reaction,156–164 and oxidation/redox reaction of the mercapto group.165 The typical reversible supramolecular associations/dissociations acting as switching transitions are hydrogen bonding,149,151,152,166–171 self-assembly metal–ligand coordination,172,173 and self-assembly of β-cyclodextrin.174–179

2.1 OWSMPs based on reversible glass/vitrification or melting/crystallization transition

The originally reported OWSMPs were mostly based on conventional glass transition or melting transition as switches.97–105,111–130,180–186 These OWSMPs are processed or thermally set to have an “original/permanent” shape. They are thermomechanically programmed to obtain a temporary shape; the programmed temporary shape can be fixed at a temperature below the glass transition or melting transition. Upon being heated to a temperature above the glass transition/melting transition temperature, they recover their original shape as a result of releasing the internal stress. The glass transition of OWSMPs as a switching transition can be tuned smoothly in a wide range; the melting switching transition cannot be tuned in a wide range because the melting transition temperature is mostly determined by its crystalline structure.51,94–99

The stable polymer network of glass transition (Tg)/melting transition (Tm) type OWSMPs can be formed by physical or chemical crosslinking.106–110,187 Physically crosslinked OWSMPs can have better processability. Chemically crosslinked OWSMPs can have better mechanical properties and chemical resistance.42,124–128,130,134,188–192 Typical physically crosslinked OWSMPs include shape memory polyurethanes, polynorbornene, ε-caprolactone and L-lactide block copolymers,109 and polyethylene terephthalate-polyethylene oxide block copolymers.106,107 Typical chemically crosslinked OWSMPs include shape memory epoxy,134 shape memory polystyrene, crosslinking polycyclooctene,132,133,193,194 crosslinking acrylate, unsaturated polyesters, crosslinking polyurethane, crosslinking poly(ε-caprolactone), and polyethylene.131

The shape recovery of OWSMPs can be triggered by direct heating or indirect methods which can increase the mobility of the macromolecules. The indirect methods include electrical resistive heating, thermomagnetic or electromagnetic Joule heating,62,63,113,195–215 infrared-light heating,216–226 high intensity focused ultrasound heating,227 and moisture/water/solvent permeation.38,41,169,228–242 Because indirect methods are more often preferred practically, great efforts have been made to improve the indirect methods triggered shape memory performance of OWSMPs.243–267

2.2 OWSMPs based on reversible photodimerization

The intensity, polarization, and phase of light can be easily manipulated. The complex patterns of light can be spatially modulated.268 Therefore, light as a wireless triggering method has more advantages. Photochemically reactive moieties such as cinnamic acid, cinnamylidene acetic acid, coumarin, anthracene and their derivatives are able to form photoreversible covalent crosslinks in polymers. The cinnamamide moieties as shown in N,N-bis(2-hydroxyethyl) cinnamamide (BHECA) in Fig. 3 can undergo reversible photodimerization. They have been incorporated into macromolecules to act as photoactive shape memory switches.143–148 These OWSMPs based on photodimerization are first deformed and the temporary shape is fixed by the photodimerization of the reactive moieties using an appropriate wavelength. Upon light irradiation using another appropriate wavelength, the reversible crosslinking cleaves, leading to the shape recovery of the polymers.53,153,154,269 Wu et al.155 prepared a biodegradable OWSMP for potential biomedical applications composing of a soft diol (polycaprolactone diol), biodegradable hard diol (poly-(L-lactic acid) diol), and N,N-bis(2-hydroxyethyl) cinnamamide (BHECA) as shown in Fig. 3. The cinnamamide moieties were incorporated through BHECA.

2.3 OWSMPs based on reversible Diels–Alder reaction

Molecule pairs such as cyclopentadiene–pyridinyldithioformate, and furan–maleimide can undergo thermally reversible Diels–Alder reaction between furan and maleimide as shown in Fig. 4.156,157 Taking advantage of the thermally reversible Diels–Alder reaction, many researchers have developed smart polymers with the functions of reversible hard–soft conversion,156–158,160,161 and thermo-responsive mending capacity.159 Toncelli et al.163 first prepared furan functionalized polyketones crosslinked with (methylene-di-p-phenylene)bis-maleimide and found that low crosslink density polymers show OWSME. Later, Defize et al.160 systematically studied the OWSME of a semi-crystalline polymer network based on the thermally reversible Diels–Alder reaction. Diels–Alder adducts were used to crosslink 4-arm star-shaped poly(ε-caprolactones) which were first end-functionalized by furan and maleimide moieties using two successive esterification reactions. The OWSMP showed outstanding OWSME. Recently, Defize et al.162 studied star-shaped poly(ε-caprolactone)s functionalized by various dienes (furan and anthracene) and a dienophile (maleimide) and found that both the polymers showed excellent OWSME.
Fig. 4 The reversible Diels–Alder reaction between furan and maleimide.

2.4 OWSMPs based on reversible oxidation/redox reaction

SH-containing mercapto groups can undergo reversible oxidation/redox crosslinking reaction as shown in Fig. 5. Aoki et al.165 studied the OWSME of cellulose derivatives which have a crosslinkable mercapto group capable of reversible oxidation/redox crosslinking reaction. The film made of the cellulose derivatives was deformed into a spring shape and oxidized in solution. After that, the film was reduced in another solution to recover it. As shown in Fig. 5, the film regained its flat shape. The disadvantage of this OWSMP is its long shape recovery time up to several days. In addition, the OWSME of the polymer also decays significantly after repeated recovery cycles.
Fig. 5 The oxidation/redox reaction induced OWSME (reprinted with permission, copyright © 2007 American Chemical Society, DOI: 10.1021/bm7006828).

2.5 OWSMPs based on reversible hydrogen bonding

Hydrogen bonding is highly sensitive to environmental temperature, i.e., high temperature decreases hydrogen bonding density, while low temperature increases it. Several researchers have fabricated OWSMPs utilizing the thermal reversibility of hydrogen bonding.149,150 For example, Li et al.151 prepared lightly crosslinked OWSMPs using hydrogen bonding as the reversible switch. Stimuli-responsive shape changing polymers change their shapes in different ways as the response to different stimulations. Recently, photo-crosslinkable benzophenone side groups were incorporated into the above macromers so that the covalent crosslinking reaction of the OWSMP can be achieved using UV light.152 Because the photo-crosslinking can happen after melt processing, they enable melt-processing of the polymer. Hu et al.167,171 incorporated a large amount of hydrogen bonding into shape memory polyurethane using N,N-bis(2-hydroxylethyl) isonicotinamine. Because hydrogen bonding can be attacked by water, the thermoresponsive OWSME can also be triggered by water.

2.6 OWSMPs based on reversible metal–ligand coordination

Utilizing reversible metal–ligand coordination, Kumpfer and Rowan172 and McKenzie et al.270 prepared a new type of OWSMPs. As shown in Fig. 6, addition of metal salts into low molecular weight poly(butadiene) end-capped with 4-oxy-2,6-bis(N-methylbenzimidazolyl)pyridine (–OMebip) ligands resulted in the formation of high molecular weight metallo-supramolecular polymers. The metal–ligand coordination bonds were also used to fix and release the temporary shape with light as the stimulus. The use of different metal salts can tailor the fixing behavior of this polymer. The mechanical properties of the OWSMPs are not as good as those of traditional OWSMPs based on glass/melting transitions. The advantage of this polymer is that it is not only thermo-active but also photo-, and chemo-active because these stimuli can result in softening of the polymer and increase decomplexation or rate of exchange of the metal–ligand complexes.

2.7 OWSMPs by cooling

Traditional OWSMPs are heated to a temperature above the switching transition temperature to trigger the shape recovery. Recently, cooling induced OWSME was reported by Sun et al.47 They prepared a composite with silicone (matrix) as the elastic component and tin powder as the filler. The mechanism for the cooling-induced shape recovery is that cooling to a low temperature of 13.2 °C induces phase transition of tin from b-tin to a-tin. The phase transition causes tin particles to split into finer particles. Softening of tin leads to the recovery of the elastic matrix. This shape recovery process is slow because the phase transition of tin takes a very long time. In addition, the shape recovery only happens at a very low temperature. In the research, the composite was cooled to −40 °C for a week followed by immersion into liquid nitrogen (−196 °C) for 12 hours.

Wang et al.271 fabricated another type of cooling induced shape recovery materials with a much faster recovery time. The material was made of a piece of sponge filled with a thermoresponsive hydrogel. The hydrogel macromolecule is composed of a hydrophobic block of poly(propylene oxide) and two hydrophilic blocks of poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO–PPO–PEO). PEO–PPO–PEO is a thermoresponsive hydrogel, which can absorb more water at low temperature. If the PEO–PPO–PEO filled sponge in water is cooled, it is softened and fully recovers its original shape in less than one minute.

2.8 Summary remarks

Research on OWSMPs has been conducted for several decades. To improve the versatility and performance of OWSMPs, many OWSMPs with different switching structures have been studied. Of the various OWSMPs, those based on reversible glass/vitrification and melting/crystallization transitions have been most intensively studied.272 Through molecular structure design, material macrostructure design, and stimulation design, various OWSMEs have been achieved. Examples of the various OWSMEs include: (1) stepwise OWSME with well-separated thermal transitions, (2) stepwise OWSME with a single broad glass/melting switching transition, (3) spatial and well-controlled OWSME by selective heating of pre-deformed OWSMPs, and (4) spatial and well-controlled OWSME by homogeneous heating of pre-deformed structurally inhomogeneous OWSMPs.261 These complex OWSMEs have provided broad and advanced application potentials for the OWSMPs.

At the same time, researchers have extensively explored the possibility of employing other reversible switching structures as the shape memory switches of OWSMPs, such as reversible photodimerization, reversible Diels–Alder reaction, reversible oxidation/redox reaction, reversible hydrogen bonding, reversible metal–ligand coordination, and cooling induced phase change of metals. Although OWSMEs based on the above switches have been demonstrated, research on most of these novel OWSMPs is still superficial. No in-depth and detailed research has been conducted after the report of these OWSMPs.

The OWSMPs based on these novel switching structures still have one weakness or another. For example, the shape fixity of OWSMPs based on reversible photodimerization is low. The molecular design of OWSMPs based on reversible Diels–Alder reaction is complicated and requires high precision; otherwise, the OWSMPs will just soften during the Diels–Alder reaction without showing the OWSME. OWSMPs based on reversible oxidation/redox reaction can only act in solution. Their OWSME decays obviously after a certain period of time in addition to their low recovery speed. OWSMPs based on reversible hydrogen bonding have been studied by several research groups.149,242,273,274 The hydrogen bonding association/dissociation temperature may have a certain relationship with the glass transition temperature of the OWSMP. Another concern of the OWSMPs based on hydrogen bonding association/dissociation is that during the cyclic thermomechanical process, the hydrogen bonding couples slip way from each other and form new hydrogen bonding away from their original location. Therefore, the polymer cannot recover its original shape. OWSMPs based on reversible metal–ligand coordination bonds can only function in solution because they are based on metal–ligand coordination bonds as the switching transition. In addition, the mechanical properties of this type of OWSMPs are inferior to those of traditional OWSMPs based on glass/melting transitions. Cooling induced OWSMPs are a new type of SMPs which either employ the cooling induced phase change of tin powder, or cooling induced hydrophilicity increase of hydrogels. Further studies are needed to improve the shape memory properties of the above novel OWSMPs, such as the fixity ratio, recovery ratios, mechanical properties, and recovery speed so that these novel OWSMPs can be used for broader applications.

3 Two-way shape changing polymers (TWSCPs)

For OWSMPs, external thermomechanical programming has to be employed to deform the polymer to achieve a temporary shape. Two-way shape changing polymers (TWSCPs) do not require the pre-deformation; they change their shape upon stimulation. TWSCPs can be used in those applications which require the two-way shape changing effect (TWSCE), such as artificial muscles and robots.

3.1 TWSCPs based on liquid crystal polymers

Heating a pre-deformed liquid crystal polymer to its anisotropic–isotropic phase transition temperature can trigger the shape recovery of the polymers. Several researchers136,138,139,141,142,275 have demonstrated the OWSME of a deformed liquid crystal polymer.

The alignment and rearrangement of liquid crystal moieties in liquid crystal elastomers have inspired researchers to develop polymers with two-way SCE. Upon being heated to the anisotropic–isotropic phase-transition temperature, the mesogens become disordered, and the liquid crystal elastomer generally contracts. When the liquid crystal elastomer is cooled below the phase-transition temperature, it expands. Main-chain liquid crystal elastomers with mesogens can have higher shape changing strain than side-chain liquid crystal elastomers. Currently, a maximum spontaneous elongation up to 500% has been achieved on a main-chain liquid crystal elastomer in a fiber form by Ahir et al.276

At the beginning, liquid crystal monodomains were mostly studied as TWSCPs because it was thought that to achieve the TWSCE on a macroscopic scale the mesogens have to be aligned uniformly over the whole material. Preparation of liquid crystal monodomain requires two processes: the synthesis of liquid crystal elastomers and the formation of the liquid crystal monodomain by loading. To obtain highly oriented nematic structures and outstanding TWSCE, Krause et al.277 prepared shape changing nanofibers of main-chain liquid crystal elastomers by electrospinning. Photo-induced crosslinks were performed during the electro-spinning process. Exceptional mechanical properties and TWSCE were obtained as a result of the high molecular orientation in the nanofibers.

Later research found that glass-forming polydomain nematic networks could also show outstanding two-way SCE. For example, Burke,278 Qin and Mather279 prepared a glass-forming polydomain nematic network as shown in Fig. 7. 2-tert-Butyl-1,4-bis-4-(4-pentenyloxy)benzoylhydroquinone (5tB) was first prepared by etherification reaction between 5-pentenyloxybenzoic acid and tert-butylhydroquinone. An unsaturated liquid crystal polyester (P5tB) was synthesized from 5tB using acyclic diene metathesis polymerization. The unsaturated P5tB was then crosslinked with dicumyl peroxide. Polydomain liquid crystal elastomers do not require the elastomer alignment procedure during processing; therefore, it simplifies the fabrication of liquid crystal TWSCPs.


Fig. 7 Synthesis of the glass-forming nematic network (reprinted with permission, copyright © 2008 American Chemical Society, DOI: 10.1021/ma8022926).

In comparison with side-chain liquid crystal elastomers, normally main-chain liquid crystal elastomers show higher shape changing amplitude. Combining mesogen side chains and main chains in one elastomer can increase the shape changing amplitude. In comparison with nematic liquid crystal elastomers, monodomain chiral smectic C elastomers can exhibit biaxial SCE.280 One axial SCE is from the shrinkage and expansion because of the smectic–isotropic transformation. Another one is from the shear deformation which is associated with the molecular tilting in the smectic phase.

3.2 Semicrystalline TWSCPs under constant stress

Many semicrystalline OWSMPs have been demonstrated to be able to show TWSCE under a proper constant load. Chung et al.281 first revealed the TWSCE on a crosslinked semicrystalline poly(cyclooctene) film to which a constant weight was applied. During cooling under a constant stress, crystallites form in the loading direction, leading to elongation. When being heated to a temperature above the melting transition of the polymer, the OWSMP contracts as a result of shape recovery. Inspired by this study, many researchers have observed the TWSCE on semicrystalline OWSMPs under a constant tensile load, such as semicrystalline shape memory polyurethane,282 polyhedral oligomeric silsesquioxane/poly(ε-caprolactone) networks,283 crosslinked poly(ε-caprolactone) (PCL)-based polyesterurethane,284 linear, three- and four-arm star poly(ε-caprolactone) functionalized with methacrylate,285 and poly(ethylene-co-vinyl acetate).286 Westbrook et al.287 have developed a 1D constitutive model to describe the TWSCE of the semicrystalline OWSMPs.

The amplitude of the TWSCE based on semicrystalline OWSMPs is low because of the limitation of the crystallites formation in the stressed direction. The maximum strain increment achieved is 44% with a recovery ratio of 85% under a stress of 0.4 MPa, which was observed on chemically crosslinked polycaprolactone. The applied stress at the end of the sample is the key factor for the elongation effect of the semicrystalline phase. To induce internal stress on the semicrystalline OWSMP, Kang et al.288 coated a stretched semicrystalline SMP fiber with an elastomer which was cured using UV. The elastomer coating can apply a tensile stress on the OWSMP fiber. Similarly, Westbrook et al.289 embedded a pre-stretched semicrystalline OWSMP strip in an elastomer matrix. TWSME was observed on the both free-standing polymer composites.

3.3 Photoactive TWSCPs based on azobenzenes

In comparison with other photoactive polymers such as anthracene based polymers, and coumarin based polymers,290–294 azobenzene based TWSCPs can produce larger geometrical changes. The schematic representation of the photo-responsive TWSCE of azobenzene based liquid crystal elastomers is presented in Fig. 8. Azobenzenes and their derivatives undergo reversible trans–cis isomerization upon irradiation of light of suitable wavelength.295 The isomerization causes the angle change of the two isomers accompanied by a significant change in molecular length between 9.0 Å (trans) and 5.5 Å (cis).296,297 Due to the photoisomerization of azobenzene, the liquid crystal elastomers experience a reduction in the alignment order and even liquid crystal to isotropic transition. Since 2001, many researchers296,298–311 have demonstrated that liquid crystal elastomers coupled with azobenzenes can show significant photo-induced deformation.

3.6 Photoactive TWSCPs based on the photo-thermal effect

Ahir and Vaia29,30 filled near infrared irradiation absorbing CNTs29,340 into a polydimethylsiloxane elastomer and observed TWSCE on the composite. The expansion or contraction of the composite depends on the extent to which the composite is strained. If the material is slightly pulled, it expands when it is exposed to infrared light. Conversely, if the material is subjected to a strain greater than 10%, it contracts under identical exposure to infrared light. This process is completely reversible and persists after numerous cycles. The above materials are elastomer materials filled with near infrared radiation fillers. Recently, Ugur et al.31 observed the TWSCE on a pure polymer nanofiber which is made of crosslinked polyarylamide with the molecular structure shown in Fig. 12. The crosslinked multi-block polyarylamide contained “coil-like” polyethylene oxide and “rod-like” polyarylamide blocks. 3.8 Thermoactive compositionally anisotropic materials

Laminates, layered materials or any hybrid materials made of constituents with different thermomechanical properties may change their geometry upon environmental temperature changes. The driving force of the shape changing effect is unbalanced mechanical stress caused by heating. The unbalanced stress may arise from different aspects such as different coefficients of thermal expansion, different modulus changes, and OWSME (if shape memory materials are used). The layered materials can be both OWSMPs, or an OWSMP layered with a non-OWSMP, or both non-OWSMPs. Langer and Lendlein345 and Chen et al.346 first reported laminated TWSCP materials made of OWSMP/elastomer. Tobushi et al.347 fabricated a two-way shape changing laminate with shape memory alloys and OWSMPs.

Based on a similar mechanism, many double layer cantilevers made of non-OWSMPs have been developed. For example, Kalaitzidou et al.348,349 fabricated a layered shape changing tube by coating a polydimethylsiloxane film with nanometer-thick gold. Upon cooling, the polydimethylsiloxane film shrinks more than gold, which produces a compression at the polydimethylsiloxane side to fold the film. Conversely, upon heating, the layered composite unfolds. LeMieux et al.350 made a highly sensitive bimorph actuator with ultrathermal sensitivity by coating plasma-polymerized polystyrene on one side of an atomic force microscope silicon tip by a chemical vapor deposition process. 3.9 Electroactive polymers (EAPs)

Electroactive polymers (EAPs) (also called electroactive artificial muscles) are another type of important stimuli-responsive materials which can show TWSCE. The activation mechanism of EAPs is either because of coulomb forces (dielectric EAPs) or because of transport of ions (ionic EAPs).27,28,351,352 Accordingly, EAPs fall into two major categories based on the primary activation mechanisms: electronic EAPs and ionic EAPs.353–355 Generally, electronic EAPs have high mechanical energy densities and can operate in air but require high voltage input; ionic EAPs need small voltages but need to maintain their wetness although some can also operate in a dry state. EAPs can be used in various areas such as biomedical devices, artificial muscles, biomimetic robots, micro/nano-electrochemical devices, drug delivery, cell culture, robotics, electromechanical systems, biomimetics, miniature manipulators, nano-motors, active sound absorbing materials, flexible speakers, flexible touch screens and keyboards, power generators, stress and strain sensors, data storage, dust-wipers and various medical instruments and auxiliaries.356,357

A dielectric EAP consists of a dielectric elastomer film with compliant electrodes coated on both surfaces as shown Fig. 15. The charge on the electrodes gives rise to electrostatic forces to deform the elastomer. Dielectric electroactive actuators have been fabricated into actuators of different shapes and configurations, such as plane, tube, roller, stacks, diaphragms, circular, helical, folded, hinge, and spherical shapes.358–361 The performance of the dielectric electroactive elastomer actuators can be markedly improved by pre-straining. Active strains of several hundred percent and strain rates up to several thousands have been achieved on dielectric electroactive triblock copolymers swollen with aliphatic/alicyclic white mineral oil.361–364 All-organic EAPs are made of polymer network/conducting polymer composites.365,366 For example, Huang and Zhang367 filled copper phthalocyanine into polyaniline/polyurethane to prepare all-organic electroactive materials. The high-dielectric-constant copper phthalocyanine particulates enhanced the dielectric constant of the polyurethane matrix and this combined two-component dielectric matrix in turn served as the high-dielectric-constant host for the polyaniline.

Electrostrictive cellulose paper actuators are fabricated by depositing very thin electrodes on both sides of the cellulose papers. The working principle of electroactive cellulose paper is a combination of piezoelectric effects and ionic migration effects, which is associated with the dipole moment of the cellulose paper ingredients.368–370 Ion migration is mainly by Li+ ion in cellulose, which can produce large deformation of the cellulose actuator. Piezoelectricity mainly arises from the crystal structure of cellulose fibers. Fig. 16 shows an electroactive cellulose paper in the shape of dragonfly wings which flap upon electrical current stimulation. Cellulose papers as EAPs have the advantages of biodegradability, abundance, lightweight, low price, large displacement output, low actuation voltage and dryness.371–373 The drawbacks of electroactive cellulose papers are low output force and low actuating frequency band. The output force and displacement of electrostrictive cellulose paper actuators can be improved by coating conducting polymers such as polypyrrole and polyaniline on the papers.374,375 CNTs grafted to cellulose papers can increase not only the blocking force output but also the frequency bandwidth.376–378 Poly(ethylene oxide)–poly(ethylene glycol)379 and chitosan372,373 can improve the polymer chain flexibility and ion mobility, thus increasing the performance of cellulose papers. In addition, chitosan can reduce the humidity sensitivity of cellulose paper actuators.371,380 The electro-mechanical efficiency can also be simply increased by mechanical stretching.381

Ferroelectric EAPs are mostly poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF) and its derivatives such as poly(vinylidene fluoride-trifluoroethylene) (P(VDF-TrFE)), which show polymorphism.382,383 The electroactive effect of P(VDF-TrFE) is because of the phase transformation between its ferroelectric and paraelectric phases arising from molecular conformational change. Electroactive conducting polymers such as polypyrrole, polyaniline, and polythiophene in electrochemical doping electrolyte solution show a significant electroactive effect as a result of the reversible counter-ion insertion and expulsion between the polymer matrix and the surrounding electrolyte solution.386 For large deformation, additives such as the dopants ClO4−, Cl− and PF6− are normally added. Grafted chains forming crystalline domains generate dipole moments and respond to an applied electric field. The strain induced by the applied electrical field is dependent on the free volume fraction, crystalline graft unit mass percentage, and molecular orientation of the graft elastomer.365,401

Electroactive liquid crystal elastomers are made of monodomain nematic liquid crystal elastomers incorporated with conducting ingredients. Joule heating induced by an electric field can change the orientation of ordered liquid crystal groups to produce anisotropic deformations.402 Carbon particles, graphite, metallic particles and conducting polymers such as polypyrrole can be used as the conductive fillers. Electroactive liquid crystal elastomers may also be fabricated using swollen nematic elastomers with low molecular mass liquid crystals.403–407 The swollen nematic elastomers with global orientation, which were significantly softened by the low molecular mass liquid crystals, were placed between rigid electrodes without mechanical constraint. High deformation which dominantly resulted from the director-rotation toward the electric field has been observed.386

Electro-viscoelastic elastomers are made of a low modulus elastomer filled with a polar phase such as starch, cellulose, lithium polymethacrylate, and polyaniline, and inorganic solid particles, e.g. silica, zeolite and TiO2.408–411 An electric field is applied during curing of the elastomer to orient and fix the position of the polar phase in the matrix. Upon electrical stimulation the interaction between dipoles occurs, which increases the elastic modulus of the elastomer.

Electroactive ionic polymer–metal composite actuators are normally made from ion exchange polymer films which are surface coated with noble metal electrodes.412,413 As shown in Fig. 18, ion concentration between the two surfaces of the film changes because of the ion transport in ion–water clusters if an electric field is applied across the film. 3.10 Summary remarks

The past decades have seen tremendous research on TWSCPs because of their wide applications. TWSCPs based on liquid crystal elastomers provide not only high shape changing amplitudes but also high mechanical output. They have been intensively studied for the potential applications as artificial muscles, soft actuators and nonlinear optical devices.139,316,428–430 The future research direction is to investigate more detailed all-round properties of liquid crystal elastomers, such as chemical and mechanical fatigue and environmental stability, so that the liquid crystal elastomeric TWSCPs can be applied successfully and widely.

All semicrystalline OWSMPs may exhibit TWSCE more or less provided that they are subjected to proper tensile stress. The TWSCE of semicrystalline SMPs can only be elongation/contraction. The shape changing amplitude of semicrystalline SMPs is generally low because it is limited by the crystallites formation in the loaded direction. This feature may affect the applications of semicrystalline TWSCPs.

Azobenzene based photoactive TWSCPs have been studied for several decades. Now researchers are able to achieve complex shape changing effects such as oscillation, twisting, swimming,290–292 rotation,293 and inchworm walk.294 In addition to azobenzene based photoactive TWSCPs, several other types of photoactive TWSCPs have also been reported, such as anthracene based polymers, coumarin based polymers, polymers with addition–fragmentation chain transfer reaction, and photoactive polymers based on the photothermal effect. More research is needed on these photoactive TWSCPs.

Thermoactive compositionally anisotropic materials can be easily prepared by laminating or hybridizing different material couples. The thermoactive compositionally anisotropic material is promising in that it does not involve the complicated synthesis process of novel materials. Second, the TWSCE of thermoactive compositionally anisotropic materials can be tailored by selecting different coupling materials and changing the content of each component. Unlike most stimuli-active materials, thermoactive compositionally anisotropic materials do not show robust TWSCE unless one of the components of the materials is a stimuli-active material in itself. In addition, the TWSCE of thermoactive compositionally anisotropic materials is mostly bending effect, which is determined by their thermoactive mechanism.

EAPs undergo substantial shape change upon electrical stimulation. EAPs with various structures and mechanisms have been intensively studied. The shape changing strain and output stress have been significantly improved. EAPs have the characteristics of lightweight, easy processing, high strain, high fatigue resistance, reliability, and the capability to operate both in dry and wet environments; therefore, they have many advantages for applications. Researchers are trying to further improve the environmental stability including aging and thermal resistance studies of EAPs. Other research includes fabricating EAPs into new forms like fibers, yarns, and series to improve the shape changing performance.

4 Active hydrogels with significant volume change

Stimuli-responsive hydrogels are water-soluble three-dimensional macromolecular gels containing a high quantity of water within their structures. They change their degree of swelling in response to external stimuli. Researchers have synthesized numerous hydrogel systems, which can respond to different stimuli such as temperature,431–433 pH,174,434 light,435,436 electric field,437,438 magnetic field,439,440 ionic strength,441,442 specific-antibody interaction, enzyme,443 glucose,444 and multiple-stimuli.445–447 Several review papers have been published respectively focusing on molecule design,448,449 biomedical applications,450 and myriad synthesis methods.451,452

4.1 Thermoactive hydrogels

Thermoactive hydrogels undergo phase transition at a lower critical solution temperature (LCST) or upper critical solution temperature (UCST) with increasing or decreasing environmental temperature. The hydrophilic/hydrophobic change at the LCST or UCST leads to the volume change of the hydrogels.453 PNIPAM is one of the most studied thermoactive hydrogels.454–458 The LCST and their swelling/deswelling properties can be tailored by adding weak acidic or basic comonomers into the hydrogel molecules. Hydrogel molecules containing acrylamide and acrylic acid moieties can show an UCST.

Many studies have been conducted to improve the response speed of stimuli-active hydrogels.459–461 Imran et al.459 reviewed the research on improving the responsiveness of hydrogels such as by adjusting the hydrophilic/hydrophobic balance,462 grafting chains,463 formation of IPNs,464,465 using macroporous or mesoporous structures,466 gel formation by vacuum synthesis,467 freezing techniques,468 and using hydrophilic polymer-lined nanopores.461 Recently, Imran469–471 used a hydrophilic and hydrophobic polyrotaxane as a movable crosslinker and N-isopropylacrylamide (NIPA) as a monomer and prepared hydrogels with fast response and good mechanical properties. The crosslink architecture having vinyl modified cyclodextrin macrocycles can slide and rotate along the polymer chains in a free state even in the gel status. Fig. 19469 shows the schematic representation of the hydrophobic and hydrophilic polyrotaxane crosslinkers and the preparation of rotaxne–NIPA gels in dimethyl sulfoxide.

Most hydrogels do not have good mechanical properties. Various methods have been employed to improve the mechanical properties such as formation of IPNs in hydrogels,459 using topological gel structures,472 incorporating inorganic clay nanoparticles as crosslinkers,473 incorporating macromolecular organic microspheres as crosslinkers,464,474–478 and grafting hydrogels onto strong substrates such as ethylene vinyl acetate, polypropylene, cellulose, glass, polyethylene, polyvinylidene fluoride, and polyethylene terephthalate.479

Two-way bending can be achieved by laminating a thermoresponsive hydrogel with another material. Saha et al.480 fabricated a type of compositionally anisotropic microcylinders as shown in Fig. 20 using a thermoactive hydrogel. The microcylinders are fabricated based on electrohydrodynamic co-jetting followed by microsectioning.480–483 The cylinders are comprised of a thermoactive hydrogel compartment as well as an inert compartment. The asymmetric expansion of the two compartments in water creates surface stresses resulting in significant and controllable bending of the microcylinders. The star-like bilayer patterns were first produced using photolithography. 4-Acryloylbenzophenone was incorporated to provide sensitivity to UV light during photolithography. Thermoactive hydrogels can be used in tissue scaffolds,484 molecular recognition,92 bio-microelectromechanical system devices,485 gene delivery vectors,486 drug carriers and delivery media,487 artificial muscles,92 chemical valves,488 immobilization of enzymes489 and cells,490,491 bioseparation,492 microfluidic devices and biosensors,493,494 regenerative medicine,495 molecular machines,496 nanotemplates,497 catalysis,498 and photonic crystals.499 One of the most important applications of thermoactive hydrogels is drug delivery. 4.2 pH-active hydrogels

pH-active hydrogels are widely studied because of their physiological significance. pH-active hydrogels are usually prepared by including weak acid or alkaline moieties in the macromolecules, such as carboxyls or amino, respectively.174–177,179,503–505 pH-responsive hydrogels accept or release protons to change the swelling of the hydrogels as a response to pH change.450 Poly(methacrylic acid), chitosan506 and poly(vinyl alcohol)/poly(acrylic acid) hydrogels are the typical pH-active hydrogels.174,507 The preparation strategies, properties and applications of different pH-active hydrogels can be found in some outstanding references.508–510 The pH-sensitivities of pH-active hydrogels can be adjusted by adding salts into water. The mechanical strength of pH-responsive hydrogels can be improved by constructing a double network hydrogel system.511,512

pH-active hydrogels with good biocompatibility have been used in drug delivery, microactuators, microsensors, and microfluidic flow control.174,177,506,513–516 For drug delivery, pH-active hydrogels can protect enzymes, proteins, and unstable antibiotics from degradation in an acidic or alkaline environment by deswelling. They subsequently release the drug by swelling in the alkaline or acidic environment, respectively.517 pH-responsive hydrogels are also proposed to be used for adaptive liquid microlens invented by Dong et al.518 The adaptive liquid microlens can vary focal lengths according to ambient pH.

4.3 Photoactive hydrogels

Hydrogels modified with photoactive moieties such as azobenzene, spiropyrans, triphenylmethane leuco and their derivatives can show photoactive swelling and deswelling. Most photoactive azobenzene based hydrogels519–522 take advantage of the varying interaction of azobenzene with β-cyclodextrin as shown in Fig. 22(a). trans-Azobenzene units and β-cyclodextrin have high binding affinity, while the binding, if any, between cis-azobenzene units and β-cyclodextrin is low.521,522 The self-assembly of trans-azobenzene with the cavity of β-cyclodextrin offers a great molecular switch. For example, Zhao et al.523 prepared a photoactive supramolecular inclusion complex by a deoxycholic acid-modified β-cyclodextrin derivative (deoxycholate-β-cyclodextrin) (see Fig. 22(b)) and azobenzene-branched poly(acrylic acid) (see Fig. 22(c)) copolymer. The photoactive inclusion complexes of azobenzenes in cyclodextrins have been used in several photoactive structures such as light-controlled micelles and vesicles,524–527 light-gated ion channels,522,528 tunable wettability surfaces,529 and delivery vehicles.530,531

Another strategy to prepare photo-active hydrogels is by incorporating spiropyran or its derivatives into hydrogel networks.58,532,533 Spiropyrans upon irradiation by light break the covalent bond between the carbon and oxygen and give birth to ionic pairs.533–535 Sumaru et al.534 and Szilagyi et al.536 prepared photoactive hydrogels by radical copolymerization of a vinyl monomer having a spirobenzopyran residue and a crosslinker. 4.5 Electroactive polyelectrolyte hydrogels

Electroactive polyelectrolyte hydrogels can be regarded as a crosslinking polymer network to which electric charges are attached. They can be prepared by crosslinking copolymerization of weakly ionisable comonomers in a polymer network.548 Electric field induces the changes of the degree of ionization and the distribution of ions, which leads to the swelling and shrinkage of the gel. Electroactive polyelectrolyte polymeric gels can behave like a conventional solid actuator or biological muscle, or like a shapeless amoeba.549

A number of active gels with a strong electroactive effect have been reported, such as polyacrylic acid/polyvinyl alcohol hydrogels, poly(vinyl alcohol)/poly(sodium maleate-co-sodium acrylate), poly(acrylic acid)/poly(vinyl sulfonic acid) copolymer, sulfonated polystyrene, alginate/poly(methacrylic acid), hyaluronic acid/poly(vinyl alcohol), chitosan/polyaniline, and pristine soy protein hydrogels (glycinin and β-conglycinin).550–552 Electro-responsive hydrogels can be used in a variety of fields such as soft actuators, artificial muscles, robotics, biomimetic devices, sensors, drug delivery systems and flexible electronics.439,553,554

4.6 Magnetoactive hydrogels

Magnetoactive polymeric gels are also called ferrogels [5, 6]. They are colloidal dispersions of monodomain magnetic particles with a typical size of ∼10 nm.555 In magnetic field-active polymeric gels, the finely distributed ferromagnetic particles are attached to the flexible network by adhesive forces, which cause direct coupling between magnetic and mechanical properties of the magnetic field-active polymeric gels.555 Significant elongation, contraction, curvature, and even bending can be induced within less than one second in magnetoactive polymeric gels.556 Magnetoactive hydrogels can also be prepared by filling thermoactive hydrogels with magnetic particles because magnetic particles such as iron oxide particles can produce heat by an external alternating magnetic field.439,551–554 Magnetoactive hydrogels are especially useful for targeted and remotely controlled drug delivery systems557–560 and microfluidic valves.561,562 For drug delivery, the external magnetic field can not only produce heat to control the release of the drug, but also guide the nanocomposites to the disease site.

4.7 Self-oscillating hydrogels

Some hydrogels capable of Belousov–Zhabotinsky (BZ) reaction can undergo autonomous self-oscillation,563 which is similar to biological processes in living organisms such as cardiac rhythms.564–567 BZ reaction is the oxidation of an organic substrate by an oxidizing chemical such as the bromate ion in the presence of a strong acid and metallic catalyst. The organic substrate may be citric acid or malonic acid, and the metallic catalyst may be ruthenium tris(2,2′-bipyridine), cerium ion, or ferroin. During the reaction, the catalyst undergoes spontaneous redox oscillation. For example, utilizing the BZ reaction, Yoshida568 developed a self-oscillating hydrogel based on poly(N-isopropylacrylamide) in which the catalyst ruthenium tris(2,2′-bipyridine) was covalently immobilized. Fig. 26 shows the self-walking motion of the gel through the swelling–deswelling movement of the hydrogel. If the length of the hydrogel is much longer than the chemical wavelength of the reaction, a train of excited pulses of the oxidized state evolves and the gel may show peristaltic motion by the reaction–diffusion mechanism. The self-peristaltic motion can be tuned not only by the raw reactants, but also by local illumination because light illumination can affect the ruthenium tris(2,2′-bipyridine) catalyzed reaction.569 The periodic peristaltic motion of BZ hydrogels can be used for conveyers or transport soft materials for biomedical applications.570,571

4.8 Summary remarks

Stimuli-responsive hydrogels, as a type of important stimuli-responsive/smart materials, have raised much research interest. In addition to thermoresponsive hydrogels and pH-responsive hydrogels, more stimuli-responsive hydrogels have been developed, which are responsive to light, ions, electricity, pH, and magnetic field. Stimuli-responsive hydrogels can be widely used based on three functions: (1) sensing an external signal (sensor), (2) evaluation (processor), and (3) action (actuator).573,574

One of the research focuses in the past decade of stimuli-responsive hydrogels was to improve the response speed. The second research focus in the past decade was to increase the mechanical properties of stimuli-responsive hydrogels. To obtain successful commercial products, more time and effort should be given to overcome these problems.

The most widely proposed applications of stimuli-responsive hydrogels are biomaterial and biomimetic material applications. Stimuli-responsive hydrogels can be easily miniaturized for these miniaturization and microfluidic medical applications. Future research may broaden their applications to wider areas. Another future research direction may be to accurately control the volume and shapes of thermoresponsive hydrogels, which is preferred for many applications.

5 Conclusions and outlook

The past decade has witnessed significant advances in stimuli-responsive shape changing polymers. Owing to the unique capability of changing shape or producing mechanical power in addition to other smart properties, stimuli-responsive polymers have found broad applications in biomedicine, aerospace, energy, bionics engineering, electronic engineering, civil engineering, and household products. A few smart products especially for medical, aerospace, civil engineering, energy and bionics areas are commercially available. While the field is rapidly progressing and many exciting achievements have been made, research in this field is far from complete.

Our mother nature has shown us abundant stimuli-responsive/smart effects in different forms such as humans, plants, animals, insects, and bacteria. Biological processes in living organisms sense the variation of the surroundings and respond or adapt to the surroundings. From cells, through organs, to human body as the most intelligent system of nature, humans respond to the variation of temperature, light, pH, glucose, enzymes, etc.575 Inspired by these intelligent systems in nature, researchers can retro-engineer stimuli-responsive polymers to mimic the architectures of these intelligent systems. In this way, researchers can not only develop stimuli-responsive materials, but also understand in detail the activity of the intelligent nature systems. Some of the intelligent systems in nature are at the nanoscale or microscale level such as the conformation and surface property change, through which the delivery of ions, wettability, and adhesion of the biological systems are tailored.151 One of the challenges in this direction may be to tactfully transfer the response in single molecules or molecule clusters into bulk materials if the macroscopic shape changing effect is required.

Supramolecular association–dissociation and dynamic ionic association–dissociation like those functioning in biological systems can be promising reversible switches for stimuli-responsive polymers. For example, supramolecular hydrogen bonding has been employed as the switch for thermoresponsive OWSME.151 Various other supramolecular structures containing donors and acceptors may also be used as the stimuli-responsive switches. These dynamic ionic association–dissociation and supramolecular association–dissociation mostly work in water or liquid systems. They can more favorably be used in hydrogel systems. Some of them may be able to function in a dry state.

Materials or structures which can change their shapes in movies such as “Transformer” have attracted much interest of human beings. The past five years have seen significant progress in the complicated and spatially controlled stimuli-responsive effect. Because the stimuli-responsive effect is intrinsic to these stimuli-responsive shape changing materials, they do not require additional complex electrical feedback systems for their actions. Based on the simple bending, compression, and shrinkage, some complex movements such as worm walking, rotation or vibration have been achieved. Advanced applications of stimuli-responsive polymers such as robots should be studied, which require complicated movements with controlled steps, strains, accelerations, and stresses.

Blending, laminating, prepreging, in situ polymerization, grafting, segmenting, interpenetrating networks, and compounding can significantly improve the properties and increase the versatility of stimuli-responsive polymers. For example, lamination is a simple method to prepare stimuli-responsive materials with complicated, controlled, multi-step movements. The properties of the stimuli-responsive materials can be tailored by simply changing the layer composition, stacking sequence, and thickness. For advanced applications of these materials, high reliability and precise control of strain, acceleration, and stress are preferred. Localized stimulation methods provide a facile and efficient method to achieve a localized effect, which may be able to provide the possibility of complicated, controlled, multi-step movement of shape changing materials.

The studies on gas or solvent vapor-stimuli polymers are few. Water and other solvents can trigger the shape recovery of certain OWSMPs; however, water or solvent vapor has not been reported to be able to trigger OWSME. Super sensitive stimuli-responsive polymers may be developed, which are responsive to gas or solvent vapors attaching to the surface of the polymers. The gas or solvent vapor responsive polymers will have promising applications as gas sensors and actuators.

Most stimuli-responsive polymer studies have been focused on fabrication and applications of stimuli-responsive polymers. Kinetic analysis, mechanics modeling and molecular modeling are very important to understand the structures and properties of stimuli-responsive polymers. Now, the kinetic study and modeling research of stimuli-responsive polymers lag far behind. This is partially due to the complicated structures of various stimuli-responsive materials which are very challenging for theoretical studies. Another reason is that the shape changing effects of stimuli-responsive materials are affected by too many factors. Continued efforts have to be made on reliable molecular simulations and multi-scale mechanics modeling to optimize molecular structures and accurately predict the deformation, stress–strain relationships, shape changing processes and stress relaxation of stimuli-responsive polymers.576–603

A large number of papers have been published and a large number of patents have been opened regarding the wide applications of stimuli-responsive polymers. Most of the literature demonstrates only the application potentials.89,598,600,604–610 The commercialized products of stimuli-responsive polymers are still countable. Practical applications involve more specific and stringent requirements on the overall properties of stimuli-responsive polymers. More detailed work has to be done to improve the properties of present stimuli-responsive polymers to meet all the requirements for their applications.

Controlling the hydrophobicity and hydrophilicity of a surface has tremendous significance in many aspects of nature and has important applications. Stimuli-responsive effect can change the morphology of the surface and thus can tune the surface wettability.611–613 High surface roughness of a substrate can increase the superhydrophobicity and superhydrophilicity of the treated surfaces. Stimuli-responsive polymers with smart hydrophobicity and hydrophilicity transversion may have promising applciations in self-cleaning, lab-on-a-chip, microfluidic devices, and biochemical filters due to the stimuli-responsive surface wettability.
