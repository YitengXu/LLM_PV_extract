Abstract

Semiconductive polymers may offer a route to photovoltaic and electronic function when characteristics such as light weight, flexibility, and low-temperature processing are required, as in bendable displays, solar panels, and smart textiles.1,2 Key semiconductor material requirements for solar cells and transistors are:3 (i) high optical absorption at wavelengths >500 nm, where solar irradiance peaks;4,5 (ii) high field-effect mobility (Âµ) >0.1 cm2Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1,6,7 and (iii) low-cost processing.8 We report the performance of donorâ€“acceptor copolymers designed to achieve these properties.

A donorâ€“acceptor structure reduces optical bandgap, enables absorption down to the near infrared,9â€“12 and may raise power conversion efficiency (PCE) in solar cells through increased photocurrent.4,13 Benzothiadiazoles14â€“18 and thienopirazines19â€“26 are commonly used as acceptors. Of these, thienopyrazine (TP) is advantageous because it allows modification of the pyrazine ring without introducing steric effects directly into the polymer backbone. Thus, alkyl and aryl groups can be added at positions 2 and 3 to tune structural and electronic properties of the acceptor. In this work we introduce a rigid naphthalene moiety fused to the 2 and 3 TP positions. This gives a planar, electron rich Ï€-face of acenaphtho[1,2-b]thieno[3,4-e]pyrazine (ACTP), which may promote Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking between polymer chains and lead to improved charge carrier mobility.

The purified, dark-green powders were characterized by gel permeation chromatography (Table 1), differential scanning calorimetry, UV-vis absorption, and photoelectron spectroscopy, and have a low bandgap of ca. 1.6 eV, with an absorption onset of ca. 800 nm.

The steric effects of the fluorene side chains largely impact mobility as seen in the case of 5, with branched chains. This material has Mw just 19% higher than 1 but its mobility is twelve times smaller than that of 1. We attribute such low mobility to decreased film order, manifested in the smoothed AFM of Fig. 2d, and the featureless XRD in Fig. 2e.

We also evaluated 1, 4 and 5 in bulk heterojunction solar cells made using indium-tin oxide (ITO) coated glass as substrate. First, 50 nm of poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene)-poly(styrenesulfonate) were spin coated on the ITO, followed by spin-coating in N2 environment mixtures of polymer and [6,6]-phenyl C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PC[61]BM) dissolved in orthodichlorobenzene. Films were solvent-annealed in a closed Petri dish,39 and their typical thickness was âˆ¼110 nm. Aluminum electrodes were evaporated onto the polymer blend, and Iâ€“V characteristics were recorded in the dark and under simulated 1 sun AM 1.5 G radiation. Using a 1 : 4 weight ratio of polymer : PC[61]BM we obtained the highest PCE for polymer 5 of 0.64%. Further optimization of cells from 5 by depositing ca. 1 nm LiF layer before the Al cathode to improve charge injection, resulted in a PCE of 1.38%. Despite its higher field effect mobility, polymer 1 had a lower PCE than 5, possibly because the high mobility of 1 was achieved only after thermal annealing, yet when annealing the solar cells we did not observe a systematic trend of PCE improvement. Morphological changes including phase segregation of the blend and degradation of PEDOT interfaces might be occurring during annealing. Indeed, so far the best PCE results were obtained on solar cells that were not annealed. Furthermore, PCE is influenced by several factors and we are investigating details of charge transport, morphology and thermal annealing in our polymer : PCBM blends to gain a better understanding of these systems.

We suspect this is due to weak coupling of the donor and acceptor, which may be improved by tuning the substituents and their locations.
