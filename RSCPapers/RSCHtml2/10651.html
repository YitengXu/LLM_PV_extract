Photophysical properties of metal complexes

Nathan J. Patmore
Department of Chemistry, University of Sheffield, Sheffield, UK S3 7HF. E-mail: n.patmore@sheffield.ac.uk

First published on 7th May 2008

Abstract

This review summarises developments in the photophysical properties of metal complexes and their polynuclear assemblies reported in the literature for 2007.

Highlights

Highlights include the study of excited state intervalence transfer in a photo-chemically prepared diruthenium mixed-valence species stabilised in a polymer film1 and determination of the ultrafast relaxation dynamics of iron(II)-trisbipyridine upon excitation into the singlet MLCT charge transfer band.2 Luminescent metal complexes are continuing to be developed for use as phosphorescent emitters in light emitting diode devices, as biological probes for in vivo studies, and as sensitisers in dye-sensitised solar cells. Examples include the development of a highly phosphorescent perfect green emitting Ir(III) complex for application in organic light emitting diodes3 and the use of a Zn(II) phthalocyanine/organic dye mixture to achieve co-sensitisation for efficient panchromatic dye-sensitised solar cells.4

Review articles

A number of review articles on emissive complexes and materials with potential application in optoelectronic devices based on: pyridyl azolate Ru(II) and Os(II) complexes;5 polymers and complexes incorporating Ln(III) ions;6 gold-heterometal complexes;7 and organometallic poly(aryleneethynylene)s have appeared.8 Multicomponent systems that employ light to induce energy and electron transfer or molecular motions,9 excitation energy transfer processes in covalently linked cyclic metalloporphyrin arrays that show potential as artificial photosynthetic antenna,10 and electron transfer capabilities of donor–acceptor systems having conjugated bridges of various lengths have been described.11 Luminescent Pt(II) terpyridyl complexes that exhibit pH and metal cation sensing abilities,12 as well as metal-based sensors that act as fluorescent probes for detecting nitric oxide13 and chemical warfare agents,14 have been summarised. The properties and applications of ruthenium phthalocyanine and naphthalocyanine complexes15 and the use of metalloporphyrin arrays containing poly(pyridyl) ligands as artificial photosynthetic reaction centre models have been reviewed.16 The effect of substituent changes or aggregation on the photochemical and photophysical properties of main group metal phthalocyanines was discussed.17 A cross-section of the current literature on luminescent polynuclear arrays designed using metal ions has been presented,18 and recent work on transition-metal sensitised near-IR luminescence from lanthanides in d–f heteronuclear arrays summarised.19 A special issue of Coordination Chemistry Reviews (2007, 251, Issues 3–4) was devoted to “highlighting the many aspects of the electronic spectroscopy of inorganic compounds”.20

Main group compounds

The electron-to-photon efficiency of OLED devices incorporating an [Alq3] (q = quin-8-olate) donor core covalently attached to three [Pt(TPP)] acceptors using a variety of oligofluorene bridges varied from 0.001 to 0.208, indicating that careful alignment of the component triplet energy levels is required for good device efficiency.21 The hemicage metal chelates [M(L1)] (M = Al3+, Ga3+, In3+) display improved photoluminescence quantum efficiencies that are 1.48 (Al3+), 1.79 (Ga3+) and 1.26 (In3+) times greater than their uncaged [Mq3] analogues. This is likely to be due to the rigidity of the hemicage structure that decreases nonradiative decay of the excited states.22 Ligands based on 8-hydroxyquinoline and having extended conjugated fluorophores were optimised for use in turn-on and ratiometric fluorescence sensor arrays that can distinguish between a variety of cationic metal ions (including Al3+, Mg2+ and Ca2+).23

Water soluble In(III) phthalocyanines, containing quarternized 3-pyridyloxy substituents, displayed lower fluorescence quantum yields and shorter triplet state life times but higher triplet state quantum yields when compared to metal phthalocyanines in general. The complexes gave good singlet oxygen quantum yields in water (∼0.56) and show potential as Type II photosensitisers for photodynamic therapy of cancer.24 The photochemistry and photophysics of related phenoxy and tert-butylphenoxy In(III) phthalocyanines display similar trends, with the large triplet state and singlet oxygen quantum yields attributed to the heavy atom effect of the In central metal.25 The heavy atom effect is also used to account for the thousand-fold reduction in fluorescent quantum yield when Tl(III) ions are added to an aqueous solution of 5,10,15,20-tetrakis(4-sulfanatophenyl)porphyrin.26

Copper, silver and gold complexes

Detailed theoretical investigations employing time-dependent DFT revealed that emission from the cyclic trinuclear complex {[3,5-(CF3)2-pyrazolate]Cu}3 originates from the lowest triplet excited state that has 3MCCT (metal-centred charge transfer) and 3LMCT mixed character.27 The dinuclear Cu(I) complexes [Cu2(μ-X)2(μ-1,8-naphthyridine)(PPh3)2] (X = I, Br) exhibit red phosphorescence at room temperature from a naphthyridine based 3MLCT state.28 Unusually long-lived (up to 150 μs) and efficient (0.16 < Φ < 0.70) emission for monocopper systems was observed in amidophosphine complexes of copper of formula [(L2)Cu(L)2] (L = PPh3, PMe3; (L)2 = dppe).29 Structural rearrangements in the MLCT excited state of [Cu(NN)2]+ (NN = 2,9-dimethyl- or 2,9-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline) were investigated using transient absorption and fluorescence up-conversion spectroscopies, that revealed slow (10–15 ps) ISC from the 1MLCT state to the 3MLCT states and ultrafast (∼80 fs) structural rearrangements.30 Transient absorption spectroscopy revealed that the presence of an anthracene chromophore in [Cu(NN)2]+ [NN = 2,9-bis(dimethylene-anthracene)-1,10-phenanthroline)] results in equilibration between the close-lying 1MLCT, 3MLCT and 3(π–π*) excited states via ISC and triplet–triplet energy transfer.31 Addition of fluoride ions to the triarylboron centre in the Cu(I) complex [Cu(L3)(PPh3)2][PF6] results in the formation of a 1:1 complex that displays a distinct visual red-shift (∼100 nm) of the MLCT absorption.32 The related complex [Cu(L4)(PPh3)2][PF6] displayed highly efficient (Φ = 0.88) bright yellow–green phosphorescence in the solid state at ambient temperature that is quenched upon fluoride ion addition, this time due to ligand dissociation from the metal.33 The amino amidate Cu(II) complex, [Cu(L5)2], acts as a turn-on fluorescent indicator for citrate, based on a decomplexation-induced fluorescent enhancement process, with micromolar sensitivity.34

A series of luminescent cyclometalated alkynylgold(III) complexes, I, display an absorption band around 400 nm that is assigned to originate predominantly from an LC transition (π → π*) of the cyclometalated ligand with some mixing from a [π(CCR) → π*(C∧N^C)] LLCT. The emission in both the solid state and solution is from a 3LC state of the C^N^C ligand in almost every instance.35 The crystalline solids [Au(CO)Cl] and [Au(RNC)Cl] (R = alkyl group) both display similar emission spectra consisting of orange–red unstructured phosphorescence with extremely large Stokes shifts. Experimental and theoretical studies indicate that Au–Au covalent bonds form in the distorted Au-centred phosphorescent excited state.36 Photolysis of [Au(L)Cl] [L = CO, (p-tosyl)CH2NC, (alkyl)NC] results in the formation of Au(III) and Au(0) photoproducts; the better π-accepting ability of L the better the photoproduct quantum yield.37 The d10 complexes [M(PPh3)(L6)] (M = Cu, Ag, Au) all display an intense absorption at ∼600 nm and solution luminescence, which could potentially be suitable for applications in vivo.38 Pentanuclear Au(I)–Cu(I) and Au(I)–Ag(I) mixed metal alkynyl complexes [nBu4N][Au3M2(CC-C6H4-p-R)6] (M = Cu, Ag; R = alkoxy group) display emission from [(CC-C6H4-p-R) → Au3M2] or [(AuCC-C6H4-p-R) → Au3M2] charge-transfer triplet excited states.39 The emission energy of the highly luminescent cluster compounds [E(AuL)3M]2+ [M = Cu, Ag; E = O, S, Se; L = PPh2-py, PPh2(CH2)2-py] is influenced by the chalcogenide, and varies from blue for O to green for S to orange for Se.40

In the solid state, the dinuclear complex {[Tl(η6-toluene)][Au(C6Cl5)2]} displays intense blue phosphorescence (3MMCT) and rigidochromic behaviour.41 The macrocyclic octanuclear cluster cyclo-{[Pt(C6Cl5)2(μ-OH)(μ-Ag)]4} surprisingly displays bright long-lived green–yellow emission (τ = 5–6 μs, λem = 521 nm) from a 3(dσ*pσ) excited state related to the Pt–Ag bonding interactions.42

Zinc, cadmium and mercury complexes

A variety of zinc based complexes have been developed for the fluorescent sensing of biologically relevant compounds. Examples include, a bis(Zn2+-dipicolylamine) complex for the detection of flavin adenine dinucleotide;43 bis(Zn2+-dipyridylamine) units linked by a naphthalene based bridge for pyrophosphate detection;44 a dizinc complex with a polyamine macrocycle able to selectively bind and sense uridine and uridine containing ribonucleotides;45 a Cu(II) ion-selective fluorionophore based on a zinc porphyrin-dipyridylamino complex;46 and a tetranuclear pentacoordinated Zn(II) phenolate-based complex for the selective detection of phosphate anions.47 In addition, the ability of a Zn(II)-N,N′-phenylene-salicylideneimine complex to act as a sensor for the detection of chemical explosives by fluorescence quenching was evaluated in the presence of nitroaromatics and 2,3-dimethyl-2,3-dinitrobutane.48

Complexation of pyrene-oligo(phenylene vinylene)-2,2′-bipyridine molecular rods to Zn(II) ions results in a change in the fluorescence emission colour that is dependent on the number of phenylene vinylene units in the rod.49 Substitution effects on the quantum efficiency of a series of photo-luminescent alkynylated Zn Schiff base complexes have been studied.50 A series of Zn(II) diimine bis(thiolate) complexes with photochromic diarylethene-containing phenanthroline ligands exhibited strong 3LLCT phosphorescence at 510–620 nm in the solid state that could be switched via photochromic reactions.51

Strong emission, that is dependent on the solid-state coordination mode, was observed from three supramolecular Cd(II) dicarboxylate networks (λem = 453, 492 and 396 nm).52 The ligand L7 acts as fluorescent sensor for selective imaging of Cd2+ in living cells.53

A red-emitting seminaphthofluorescein chromophore having a thioether-rich metal-binding unit acts as a turn-on and single-excitation dual emission sensor for ratiometric Hg(II) detection in aqueous solutions.54 Another selective fluorescent probe for Hg2+ detection in aqueous solution, L8, exhibits a turn-off fluorescence sensing ability.55 Fluorescence studies on the interactions between Hg2+, Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions and a 1,3-bis(aminoethoxy)-calix[4]arene derivative reveals two distinct binding sites and the ability to differentiate Hg2+ ions.56

Miscellaneous transition metal complexes

Titanium hydrazido(2−) complexes [Ti(NNR′R″)(4,4′-R‴-bpy)L] [R′, R″, R‴ = alkyl or aryl group; L = N,N-di(pyrrolyl-α-methyl)-N-methylamine] display low energy LLCT transitions (hydrazido π → bpy π*), a somewhat unusual occurrence for Group 4 complexes.57 The spectroscopy and photophysics of trans-[Cr(cyclam)(NCS)2]+ in a range of solvents revealed a correlation between the ν(CN) wavenumber and energy of the LMCT absorption, the spin-forbidden (doublet-quartet) absorption and emission maxima, indicating the dominating role of the NCS− ligand on solvent effects.58 Studies on six bimetallic complexes [MM′(tren)(CA)]n+ [M, M′ = Cr, Ga; tren = tris(2-aminoethyl)amine; CA = chloranilate; n = 2, 3] that exhibit sharp and intense features in their absorption spectra revealed that the transitions are a consequence of spin-exchange interactions in both the ground and excited state manifolds of the compounds.59

The ultrafast relaxation of [Fe(bpy)3]2+ upon excitation into the 1MLCT band was investigated using femtosecond fluorescence up-conversion and transient absorption spectroscopies. The studies revealed ultrafast (<20 fs) ISC from the 1MLCT to 3MLCT, relaxation from the 3MLCT in 120 fs, and arrival at a high spin metal state in 960 fs that decays to the low-spin ground state in 665 ps.2 Computational studies on [Fe(NN)3]2+ (NN = bpy, phen, bpz) and [Fe(terpy)2]2+ predict LC and MLCT transitions at a higher energy than experimentally observed, and the presence of d–d transitions that are buried under these bands.60

Laser photoexcitation of the phenolate-to-Fe(III) charge-transfer band in [Fe(L9)(dpq)] (dpq = dipyridoquinoxaline) at visible wavelengths, including red-light (≥630 nm), leads to cleavage of supercoiled pUC19 DNA to its nicked circular form via a photoredox pathway.61 Ferrocene-based organic radicals, II, exhibit broad absorption bands in the near-IR region (λmax = 1000 or 1500 nm) that correspond to the excitation of a neutral donor–acceptor (DA) ground state to a short lived charge-separated D+A− state, which was monitored using picosecond transient absorption spectroscopy.62 Spectroscopic studies show that the photoinduced charge-separated state lifetimes and efficiencies in fulleropyrrolidine–oligothienylenevinylene dyads are increased upon addition of a ferrocene donor moiety at the end of the thienylene-vinylene chain.63 Ferrocene, ferrocenylboronic acid and hexacyanoferrate(II) have been employed as photosensitisers of TiO2 and show photocurrent switching upon changes of electrode potential and photon energy.64 A photoswitchable molecular wire with a dithienylethene (DTE) linker, [{(dppe)(Cp*)Fe(–CC–)}2(μ-DTE)], exhibits photochromic behaviour that switches electronic communication between the two metal centres on and off.65 The LMCT excited state of [Fe(edta)(H2O)]− or [Fe(edta)(OH)]2− (edta = ethylenediamine-tetraacetate) can undergo self-quenching to generate the [(H2O)(edta˙)FeII(μ-OHx)FeIII(edta)]x−4 millisecond intermediate, the fate of which depends on the availability of molecular oxygen.66 The photochemistry and two-photon photophysical properties of [Fe2(μ-SR)2(NO)4] (R = organic dye molecule) have been studied. Efficient energy transfer from the dye-molecule excited-state to the dinuclear metal centre occurs, and under continuous photolysis (436 nm) the molecule decomposes with a modest quantum yield and concomitant release of NO.67 Intermolecular PET was observed from the reduced species [Ru(bpy)3]+ to a bioinspired model of the iron-only hydrogenase active site, [(CO)6Fe2{(μ-SCH2)2X}] (X = CH2, NCH2C6H5), showing promise for use in light-driven proton reduction to molecular hydrogen.68

Organic dye σ-bonded metal complexes of Pd(0), III, surprisingly display strong fluorescence [Φ = 0.65 (L = PPh3) and 0.22 (L = dppe)] that is rationalised by weak electronic interactions between the metal centres and dye π-system.69 Energy transfer from ruthenium to palladium in dinuclear Ru⋯Pd complexes [(bpy)2Ru(μ-L)Pd(Me)(X)] (L = bpym, 5,5-Me2-bpym, 5,5-Br2-bpym; X = Cl or solvent) was most efficient when the electron-withdrawing 5,5-Br2-bpym bridging ligand was employed, as evidenced in the photophysical properties and the rate of photophysical dimerisation of α-methylstyrene.70

The luminescent properties of a Np(VI) aquo ion and [Na2(NpVIO2)2-(GeW9O34)2]14− have been studied in solution for the first time. Short-lived (<40 ns) near-IR luminescence can be sensitised through both ligand absorptions and LMCT bands of the ion itself.71 A series of Th(IV) ketimide complexes [(C5Me5)2Th{NC(CH3)(ArF)}2], containing fluorinated aromatic groups (ArF), display moderately intense absorptions in the mid-visible region resulting from non-bonding nitrogen p-orbital to antibonding CN π*-orbital transitions. Low temperature (77 K) luminescence is observed from both the singlet and triplet exited states, with triplet state lifetimes in the range 50–400 μs.72

Rhenium complexes

The strong luminescent properties of Re(I) tricarbonyl polypyridine complexes makes them good candidates for use as luminescent sensors. Functionalised polypyridine (NN) receptors in [Re(CO)3(NN)(L)]+ (L = pyridine-based ligand) complexes have been employed as: turn-on emission sensors for metal ions,73 anion recognition74 and indole-binding proteins;75 a turn-off emission sensor for biologically relevant oxometalates;76 an off–on–off emission sensor for pH changes.77 Photoinduced geometry changes associated with the MLCT excited state of [Re(L10)(CO)3Cl], probed in a combined DFT/Raman spectroscopy study, are consistent with simple MO theory descriptions of the charge-transfer process.78 Photocyclisation of the diarylethylene ligand in [Re(L11)(CO)3Cl] occurs in the 3MLCT state upon prolonged excitation (λ = 352 nm) which results in a large red-shift of the absorption maximum, an example of metal coordination-assisted near-IR photochromic behaviour.79 Electroluminescent devices comprised of [Re(NN)(CO)3Cl] complexes containing diimine ligands (NN) functionalised with electron-transporting 2,5-diphenyl-1,3,4-oxadiazole or hole-transporting carbazole moieties display biexponential phosphorescent emission, with triplet excited state lifetimes of 0.41 and 0.12 μs for the former carrier transporting group and 0.91 and 0.22 μs for the latter.80 On a fs to 10 ps timescale the transient spectra of fac-[(RCO2)Re(CO)3(NN)] [R = ferrocene, 4-(dimethylamino)benzyl; NN = bpy, phen] are transformed from the 1MLCT (Re → NN) into the spectra of two 3MLCT excited states (Re → RCO2 and Re → NN) that have lifetimes spanning 102 ns to several μs. The position of these 3MLCT states markedly affects photo-decarboxylation from this complex, initiated in 3LLCT (RCO2 → NN) states.81 The luminescence spectra of [Re(CO)4(phen)]+ and [Re(CO)3CN(phen)] are assigned to emission from 3MLCT states (τ = 1–3 μs), whereas for [Re(CO)4(N^C)] (N^C = benzo[h]quinoline) emission is from the 3LC state (τ = 35 μs). They, and related Ir(III) complexes, have also been investigated as good sensitisers of singlet oxygen, which was kinetically modelled.82 Alkoxy-bridged Re(I) rectangles, [{(CO)3Re(μ-OR)2Re(CO)3}2(μ-4,4′-bpy)2] (R = C4H9, C8H17, C12H25), exhibit weak emission in acetonitrile (λem = 666 nm, Φ = 0.00039, τ = 11 ns) that is significantly enhanced in 90:10 water–acetonitrile solutions (λem = 602 nm, Φ = 0.00654, τ = 212 ns) due to aggregation of the rectangles that deactivates nonradiative decay by restricted intramolecular vibrational and torsional motions.83

The complexes fac-[Re(L)(CO3)(11-CO2Et-dppz)]+ show emission from a 3LC (L = py) and 3MLCT (L = 4-Me2N-py) excited state. The formation of these excited states within a few ps of excitation was monitored with time-resolved IR spectroscopy.84 Transient visible and IR absorption spectroscopy were used to study the complex solvent dependent photophysics of fac-[Re(py)(CO3)(11,12-X2-dppz)]+ (X = H, F or Me) complexes. Upon excitation, mixtures of 3LC (ππ*) and 3MLCT states are formed in an equilibrium that can be perturbed by substituents or solvent, resulting in some dramatic changes in the photophysical properties.85 The heterodinuclear complex [{fac-(CO)3Re(dppz)}(μ-dpp[5]){Ru(tpm)(dppz)}]3+ [dpp[5] = 4,4′-dipyridylpentane; tpm = tris(1-pyrazoyl)methane] exhibits PEnT from the Re to Ru centres. It also acts as a ‘multifunctional light switch’ in which luminescence is enhanced upon DNA addition in aqueous solutions due to DNA binding, and photoirradiation leads to DNA cleavage.86

Ruthenium and osmium complexes

Mononuclear ruthenium complexes

Emission from the fourth 3MLCT state of [Ru(bpy)3]2+ was observed in the luminescence spectrum recorded as a KBr disc.87 The excited state absorption and circular dichroism spectra of [Ru(phen)3]2+ in the UV region showed new excited state transitions for the reduced ligand, and a strong reduction of the excitonic coupling due to a blue shift of the π–π* transition.88 Time-dependent DFT calculations on [Ru(NN)]2+ (NN = bpy, bpz) complexes show that emission could originate from two quasi-degenerate 3MLCT states, and an optimised geometry of the 3MC state is presented that is of similar energy to the 3MLCT states and has a four-coordinate Ru atom.89 The kinetics and mechanism for quenching of the MLCT excited-state of [Ru(bpy)2(bpz)]2+ by proton-coupled electron transfer from 1,4-hydroquinone was elucidated using detailed transient absorption, time-resolved emission, and time-resolved EPR measurements.90 Tetrazolate-based complexes [Ru(bpy)2(L12)]+ are poorly luminescent at room temperature, but methylation of the tetrazole ring at the 3-position results in a 10-fold increase in the excited-state lifetime for reasons not entirely understood at present.91 The excited state electronic properties of [Ru(bpy)2(L13)]+ were examined by emission, transient resonance Raman and transient absorption spectroscopies, which reveal that for the pyridine based complexes (X = CH) the lowest emissive state is exclusively bpy-based whereas for the pyrazine-based complexes (X = N) excited state localisation shows considerable solvent and pH dependency.92 Protonation of the imidazole-containing ligand of [Ru(bpy)2(L14)]2+ dramatically alters the photophysical properties and PET processes due to a change from a 3MLCT(bpy)-based to 3MLCT(L14)-based lowest excited state.93 The complex [Ru(NN)2(MeOH)2]2+ (NN = 2,7-diphenyl-phen) undergoes a cis-trans photoisomerisation process; the emission spectrum of the trans-isomer is red-shifted compared to the cis-isomer which is rationalised in terms of the excited state energies.94 A family of Ru(II) complexes containing substituted 1,10-phenanthroline ligands with extended conjugation, [Ru(bpy)2(L15)]2+, unusually display two simultaneously emissive 3MLCT excited states in solutions at room temperature, originating from bpy-based (τ = 0.9 to 2.2 μs) and longer lived phen(L15)-based (τ = 3.7 to 11.5 μs) excited states.95

Visible light decomposition of NH3 to N2 in a photocatalyst aqueous solution based on a photoelectron relay system of either sensitiser [Ru(bpy)3]2+/K2S2O8 or [Ru(bpy)3]2+/methyl viologen dichloride/O2 was investigated using absorption spectroscopy and gas chromatography analysis.96 Upon excitation of the tyrosine-containing complex [Ru(L16)2(NCS)2] at 532 nm, PET from the MLCT state to methyl viologen occurs followed by intramolecular electron transfer from the tyrosine moieties to Ru(III) with a rate constant of kf > 1 × 108 s−1.97 TiO2 electrodes modified with [Ru(L17)2(dcb)]2+ dye complexes exhibit extended absorption domains and higher absorbances than the prototypical [Ru(dcb)2(NCS)2] dye, with good incident photon-to-current efficiency values (near 80%) providing a cobalt-based mediator is used.98 The complex [Ru(bpy)2(L18)]2+ exhibits 3MLCT emission at room temperature (λem = 594 nm, τ = 650 ns); oxidative quenching experiments indicate that the complex may be considered an electron transfer sensitiser and/or catalyst in photochemical reductions.99

The photophysical properties of a series of complexes containing pyrimidine-substituted terpy ligands, [Ru(terpy)(L19)]2+ and [Ru(L19)2]2+, are significantly improved [τ = 8 to 200 ns, Φ = (1.3 to 12.0) × 10−4 at 298 K] by comparison to [Ru(terpy)2]2+ (τ = 0.25 ns, Φ = 0.05 × 10−4 at 298 K) due to enhanced separation between the MLCT and MC excited states.100 Similar improvements in photophysical properties were observed in [Ru(NNN)2]2+ [NNN = anthracene and bromo-anthracene derivatives of bis(pyridyl)triazine ligands] due to interaction between the anthrancene and MLCT excited states.101 Multichromophoric complexes containing ethynylpyrenyl or ethynyltolyl subunits, [Ru(terpy)(L20)]2+ and [Ru(L20)2]2+, exhibit relatively long-lived 3LC-based emission (R = pyrene) and shorter lifetime 3MLCT-based emission (R = tolyl), due to changes in the balance between the 3LC and 3MLCT excited state energies.102 At low concentration, the complex [Ru(terpy)(CO)2(O2CCF3)]+ exhibits no MLCT absorptions in the visible region and 3LC-based emission at 77 K, but at high concentration a weak MLCT absorption is observed. This is proposed to arise from the formation of a terpy π–π stacked dimeric species, and at high concentration a new emission band appears originating from a π–π stacked dimeric (or oligomeric) species.103 Going from a 5- to 6-membered chelate ring in the bis-tridentate complexes [Ru(NNN)2]2+ (NNN = polypyridyl-based tridentate ligands) substantially increases the excited state lifetime from 180 to 810 ns, due to increased ligand field splitting that results in retardation of decay through 3MC states. By contrast, the lifetime of the tris-bidentate complexes [Ru(NN)3]2+ (NN = polypyridyl-based bidentate ligands) is reduced from 430 ns to around 1 ns in going from a 5- to 6-membered ring.104

The modulation of the 3MLCT luminescence energy of [Ru(bpy)(CN)4]2− over a range of about 6000 cm−1 was achieved using a variety of metal cations (Li+, Na+, K+, Cs+, Ba2+). This effect was exploited to switch the direction of PEnT in a bis-bipyridyl bridged [Ru(bpy)3]2+/[Ru(bpy)(CN)4]2− dyad, in which the 3MLCT excited state for the [Ru(bpy)3]2+ fragment is higher (no metal ions) or lower (addition of Ba2+) in energy than that of the [Ru(bpy)(CN)4]2− fragment.105 The 3MLCT energies, luminescence lifetimes and quantum yields in a series of [Ru(NN)(CN)4]2− complexes (NN = substituted bpy- or phen-based ligand) were tuned by substituting a variety of electron donating and withdrawing groups at the 4,4′-positions of bpy and 4,7-positions of phen. The relative effect of the diimine modifications on luminescence properties is greater than that observed in the related [Ru(NN)2(CN)2] and [Ru(NN)3]2+ complexes due to greater mixing of the NN-π* and CN-π* orbitals.106 The mechanism of quenching by oxygen of the excited states of [Ru(NN)(CN)4]2− [NN = bpy, phen, terpy, dmb, 1-ethyl-2-(2-pyridyl)benzimidazole] complexes in aqueous media was investigated, and a kinetic model that quantitatively reproduces the quenching rates proposed.107 The photophysical properties of [Ru(bpy)(CN)4] are solvent dependent, with studies showing that as the solvent Gutmann acceptor number increases the solute-solvent interaction increases, inducing a larger Ru/bpy charge difference which results in an increase in the absorption and emission energies.108 Solvent effects on the absorption and emission spectra of [Ru(L21)(NCS)3]3− were similarly ascribed to changes in the complex dipole moments. The four lowest energy transitions in the absorption spectrum were attributed to a mixture of MLCT and LLCT (Ru/NCS → L21 π*) transitions with the aid of time-dependent DFT calculations.109 Ru(II)-based luminescence in hydrogen bonded assemblies of the complex cation [Ru(NN)(H2biim)]2+ (NN = bpy, 4,4′-tBu-bpy; H2biim = biimidazole) and [M(CN)6]3− (M = Fe or Co) complex anions is enhanced for M = Co, but quenched in solution for M = Fe due to PET.110 Coordination of the resorufin dye molecule results in [Ru(NO)(Me2bpb)(L)] [H2Me2bpb = 1,2-bis(pyridine-2-carboxamido)-4,5-dimethylbenzene; L = resorufin] that is photosensitised to release NO efficiently upon irradiation in the visible light region.111 The use of 2-(dibutylamino)ethanol as a co-reactant with [Ru(bpy)3]2+ results in substantial increases in electro-chemiluminescence intensities over systems incorporating tripropylamine.112

Time-dependent DFT on the excited states of the [Ru(dcb)2(NCS)2] dye absorbed onto a model TiO2 surface was used to investigate the role of sensitiser protonation. Two different electron injection mechanisms were proposed for DSSCs employing dyes carrying a different number of protons.113 DFT methods were used to show that the excited state energies and light-harvesting ability of the [Ru(dcb)2(NCS)2] dye are key causes for its outstanding efficiency in DSSCs by comparison to the related [Ru(dcb)2(L)2] (L = CN−, 2L = dcb) dyes.114 The high molar extinction coefficient charge transfer sensitiser [Ru(dcb)(L22)(NCS)2][NBu4] (λmax = 400 and 500 nm, ε = 34500 and 18900 M−1 cm−1 respectively) anchored to TiO2 films exhibits superior power conversion efficiency compared to [Ru(dcb)2(NCS)2][NBu4]2.115 All the cell parameters of DSSCs incorporating supramolecular substituents in dyes of formula [Ru(dcb)(NN)(NCS)2] (NN = bpy functionalised with C9H19, triphenylamine and triphenyldiamine at the 4,4′-positions) were improved, due to an increased spatial separation between photogenerated holes and electrons.116 DSSCs incorporating the thiocyanate Ru(II) sensitisers cis-[Ru(dppz-11-CO2H)2−n(phen)n(NCS)2] (n = 0 or 1) and [Ru(dcb)(L23)(NCS)] showed reduced efficiency compared to cells prepared with the [Ru(dcb)2(NCS)2] dye.117,118 Studies, including ultrafast transient IR spectroscopy measurements, on TiO2 based DSSCs having heteroleptic bis(terpyridine) ruthenium dyes containing terpy ligands functionalised at the 4′-position by a thienyl spacer and phosphate anchoring group show that the use of a thienyl spacer actually results in faster electron injection and improved photovoltaic photoconversion efficiencies.119 The photoelectrochemical properties of a series of bis(terpyridine) Ru(II) complexes having one terpyridine ligand functionalised in the 4′-position by phosphonic or carboxylic acid, and another also functionalised in the 4′-position by a potential electron donor (tyrosine or carotenic amide) have been studied. Upon attachment to TiO2 and irradiation with visible light, the anticipated long-lived charge-separated state could only be observed with certainty for the caretenoid donor.120 Improved DSSC efficiency was observed for similar bis(terpyridine) Ru(II) complexes containing a phosphonic acid functionalised terpy ligand and a second terthiophene donor functionalised terpy ligand.121 The cyclometalated complexes IV show promise for application as sensitisers in DSSCs as a result of their broad and red-shifted absorptions (λmax = 523 or 552 nm) in comparison to the analogous [Ru(terpy)2]2+ complexes (λmax = 487 nm).122

Electrochemical and photochemical reduction of a Ru(II) complex containing a nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+) model ligand, [Ru(bpy)2(L24)]2+, in the presence of proton sources results in the formation of [Ru(bpy)2(HHL24)]2+. This complex acts as a hydride donor in catalytic reactions, originating from the MLCT excited states, and mimicking the enzymatic NAD+/NADH system.123 An analogous complex containing a tridentate version of L24 having two redox active sites, [Ru(terpy)(L)]2+ [L = 2,6-bis(benzo[b]-1,5-naphthyridin-6-yl)-4-tbutylpyridine], undergoes four-electron electrochemical and photochemical reduction in aqueous solutions.124 Prolonged exposure of covalently linked [Ru(terpy)2]2+-cytochrome c bioconjugates to light at 480 nm results in changes in the absorption and emission spectra that suggest PET from the ruthenium chromophore to the heme protein.125

The dramatic effect on the luminescence properties of Ru(II) complexes containing dppz-based ligands upon binding to DNA has continued to be exploited. There is a marked difference in the photophysical properties of Ru(II) complexes containing a tridentate analogue of dppz, [Ru(NNN)2]2+ and [Ru(NNN)(terpy)]2+ [NNN = 3-(2′-pyridyl)-dppz], that have MLCT excited state lifetimes of 2.4 and 5 ns, respectively. Both complexes bind to DNA, but luminescence enhancement is only observed for the heteroleptic complex as one dppz-based ligand in the homoleptic complex is not chelated and remains exposed to the solvent.126 The achiral DNA metallo-intercalators [Ru(tpm)(L25)(L)]n+ [tpm = tris(1-pyrazolyl)methane; n = 1, L = Cl; n = 2, L = MeCN and pyridine] have lower emission quantum yields (Φ = ∼10−4) and lifetimes (τ = ∼50 ns) than the analogous dppz-based complexes, but similar binding affinities with CT-DNA.127 Charge separation in the ruthenium quencher conjugate complex [{Ru(phen)(dppz)(bpy′-his)}{Ru(NH3)5}]5+ has been investigated using steady-state and time-resolved luminescence measurements. When intercalated into guanine-containing DNA intramolecular PET is observed, and upon excitation the complex is capable of injecting charge into the duplex.128 The potential of using flow cytometry to explore the cellular uptake of metal complexes has been demonstrated using [Ru(NN)2(dppz)]2+ (NN = a series of 4,4′-substituted bpy and 4,7-substituted phen ligands) complexes.129 DFT studies on the excited states and spectral properties of [Ru(phen)2(6-OH-dppz)]2+ were used to explain the photoinduced oxidation reduction mechanism of DNA photocleavage by the complex.130 DFT calculations were also used to rationalise the photophysical properties of [Ru(NN)2(dppz)]2+ (NN = phen or 1,4,5,8-tetraazaphenanthrene) when used as molecular light switches by intercalation in DNA.131 Inorganic–organic hybrid luminescent binary probes consisting of a [Ru(4,7-diphenyl-phen)2(4,4′-bpy)]2+ energy donor and the organic fluorophore Cy5 as energy acceptor appended to target oligonucleotide sequences have been used for DNA detection based on spin-forbidden resonance energy transfer.132

Porous crystalline samples of [Ru(phen)3](tfpb)2 [tfpb = tetrakis(bis-3,5-trifluoromethylphenylborate] act as oxygen sensors by quenching of the emissive states.133 Ruthenium complexes containing a dipyrrolyl-functionalised bpy ligand that functions as an anion receptor, [Ru(bpy)2(L26)]n+ (n = 2 or 3), have been used for emission-based selective detection of dihydrogen phosphate.134 [Ru(bpy)3]2+-based complexes having pendant catechol or phenol functionalities act as sensors for fluoride ions at the ppm concentration level due to the appearance of new absorption bands in the visible region upon ion complexation.135 Photolysis of [Ru(NH3)5(pz)]2+ (pz = pyrazine) at 436 nm in pH 7.4 oxygenated phosphate buffer solution generates the Ru(III) analogue plus singlet oxygen and superoxide, with a rate constant for oxygen quenching of the 3MLCT excited state of ∼(3 ± 1) × 1010 M−1 s−1.136

Mononuclear osmium complexes

A series of [Os(NN)(CN)4]2− (NN = bpy, bpym, phen, 4,4′-tBu-bpy) complexes, that are weakly emissive, display three MLCT absorptions that are considerably blue-shifted in water compared to organic solvents. Addition of Lewis-acidic metal ions (Na+, Ba2+, Zn2+) leads to substantial increases in emission energy, intensity and lifetimes.137 The Os(II) complex [Os(L27)3]2+ exhibits an intense absorption band at 485 nm and a weak band at 659 nm assignable to MLCT transitions, and ligand-sensitised emission from the 3MLCT state at 736 nm. The photophysical properties are red-shifted in comparison to the Ru(II) analogue, as expected due to the higher energy of the Os dπ orbitals.138 High-efficiency blue phosphorescence by way of localised excitation was achieved by the strategic design of [Os(CO)3(O2CCF3)(L28)] and [Os(CO)3(O2CCF3)(L29)] that have emission efficiencies of 0.26 (λmax = 460 nm) and 0.27 (λmax = 450 nm) respectively.139 Rapid intramolecular energy transfer from a free base porphyrin covalently attached to a [Os(terpy)2]2+ complex by a phenylene linker most likely occurs by way of a Förster dipole–dipole mechanism. Equipping the distal terpy ligand with a ethynylene residue allows it compete with this process via interligand electron transfer.140

Complexes containing two or more Ru or Os chromophores

Stabilisation of the PF6− salt of the ligand bridged complex cis,cis-[(bpy)2ClRu(pz)RuCl(bpy)2]2+ (pz = pyrazine) in poly(methyl methylacrylate) allows a comparison between the photophysical properties of the ground state and photochemically prepared excited state, mixed valence form [(bpy)2ClRuIII(pz−˙)RuIICl(bpy)2]2+. Comparison of the intervalence absorption band properties in the near-IR with those of the ground-state cis,cis-[(bpy)2ClRuIII(pz)RuIICl(bpy)2]3+ mixed-valence complex allows a comparison to be made between pz and pz−˙ as bridging ligands for electron transfer.1 Theoretical studies on the Creutz–Taube ion, [{(NH3)5Ru}2(μ-pz)]5+ have been used to determine excited states and assign theoretical absorption bands with the experimental absorption spectra.141

Three of the four dinuclear Ru(II) complexes [{Ru(NN)2}2(L30)]2+ (NN = phen or 1,4,5,8-tetraazaphenanthrene) are luminescent and display complicated photo-physical behaviour that is not always predictable.142 The excited state dynamics for a series of mono- and di-nuclear d6 metal complexes containing the bpym ligand have been probed on the pico- and nano-second timescales using time-resolved IR spectroscopy. The lifetime of the lowest energy metal → bpym 3MLCT excited states was shorter for the dinuclear complexes, [{Ru(CN)4}2(μ-bpym)]4−, [{Ru(bpyam)2}2(μ-bpym)]4+ (bpyam = 4,4′-(CONEt)2-bpy) and [{Re(CO)3Cl}2(μ-bpym)] compared to the analogous mononuclear species.143 Rod-like binuclear complexes having a cyclometalated bridge or terminal ligand, [(tterpy)Ru(L31)Ru(NNN)]3+ [X = C−, NNN = 4′-tolyl-terpy (tterpy); X = N, NNN = 6-phenyl-4-tolyl-bpy], have virtually identical energy levels, but MLCT excited states of different (bridging or terminal) localisation. The energy transfer time constant for the bridge-cyclometalated complex (2.7 ps) is faster than the terminal-cyclometalated one (8.0 ps), explained in terms of different electronic factors for Dexter energy transfer.144 Functionalisation of the naphthyl core of naphthalenebisimide (NBI) with two [Ru(bpy)3]2+ units, as opposed to conventional linking on the NBI nitrogen, results in improved electronic coupling and a 1000-fold increase in the rate of Ru-to-NBI PET.145 The formation of the hydrogen bonded assembly [{Ru(4,4′-tBu-bpy)2(L32)}2{μ-Ru(CN)2(CNtBu)4}]4+ results in quenching of excited state emission, likely achieved by PEnT from the [Ru(4,4′-tBu-bpy)2(L32)]2+ fragment to the cyano bridge complex.146

The complex [{(bpy)2Ru(dpp)}2RhIIICl2]5+ [dpp = 2,3-bis(2-pyridyl)pyrazine] undergoes photoinitiated electron collection at the central Rh core, resulting in Rh(III) to Rh(I) conversion to produce [{(bpy)2Ru(dpp)}2RhI]5+. Addition of water to this system in an acetonitrile solution containing dimethylaniline results in the photocatalytic production of hydrogen with Φ ≈ 0.01.147 The performance of DSSCs prepared using the dinuclear Ru(II) dye [{Ru(dcb)2(NCS)}2(μ-tbpe)][NnBu4]4 [tbpe = trans-1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethylene] is improved by comparison to other closely related mono- and dinuclear Ru(II) dyes, attributed to efficient intramolecular charge and energy transfer from one of the linked dye molecules to the other through the tbpe bridge.148 Photoactivation of the dinuclear Ru(II) arene complexes [{(η6-arene)RuCl}2(μ-dpp)]2+ [arene = indan, benzene; dpp = 2,3-bis(2-pyridyl)pyrazine] in aqueous solutions containing DNA results in the loss of the arene (the free arene is fluorescent and acts as a marker to monitor the reaction) and formation of a reactive Ru(II) complex that can bind to DNA.149

Emission studies on cis-[{(phen)2Ru(PHEHAT)}2RuCl2]4+ (PHEHAT = 1,10-phenanthroline[5,6-b]-1,4,5,8,9,12-hexaazatriphenylene) indicate that PEnT occurs from the periphery Ru(II) ions to the central Ru atom, but for the complexes [{(phen)2Ru(PHEHAT)}2Ru(NN)]6+ (NN = phen, 1,4,5,8-tetrazaphenanthrene) energy transfer occurs in the other direction.150 The mono-, di- and tri-nuclear cyanoruthenates [Ru(CN)4(HAT)]2−, [{Ru(CN)4}2(μ2-HAT)]4− and [{Ru(CN)4}3(μ3-HAT)]6− (HAT = hexaazatriphenylene) display strong MLCT absorptions that are strongly solvatochromic; the trinuclear complex is a panchromatic absorber of visible light. The complexes show red 3MLCT-based luminescence as solids and glasses at 77 K. When coordinated to Yb(III) and Nd(III) ions, the MLCT emission is quenched by Ru → Ln PEnT, resulting in sensitised near-IR lanthanide emission.151 The dinuclear complexes V display MLCT absorptions in the visible region (400–600 nm), with weak 3Os → L charge-transfer emission observed for the RuOs and OsOs complexes (Φ = 10−3 to 10−4) but no appreciable emission observed for the RuRu complex. These phenomena are attributed to a nonemissive triplet state centred on the ethenylthiophene spacer that lies lower in energy than the 3Ru → L triplet level, but higher in energy than the 3Os → L triplet level.152

Rhodium and iridium complexes

The colour of emission from cyclometalated Ir(III) complexes can be tuned by incorporating an ancillary ligand. Photophysical studies have been performed for functionalised biscyclometalated complexes [Ir(C^N)2(X2)]0/+, where X2 is acetylacetonate,153–155 bipyridine-like (N^N),156–158 a strong-field ligand,159 or isocyanides.160,161 Cyclometalated Ir(III) complexes also show great promise for application in organic light emitting diodes and have been incorporated into electroluminescent devices displaying red,162–164 green3,165–167 or blue168–174 emission. The neutral Ir(I) pyridyl azolate complex [Ir(COD)(fptz)] [fptz = 3-trifluoromethyl-5-(2-pyridyl)triazolate] has been used as dopant to fabricate a red-emitting OLED.175 Cyclometalated Ir(III) complexes have also been developed as selective phosphorescent chemosensors for Ag+ and Hg2+ ions,176,177 as well as for the amino acid homocysteine178 and estrogen receptor α.179

Phosphorescent resonant energy transfer from the green emitting [Ir(4-tBu-ppy)3] complex to the red emitting [Ir(CN)2(acac)] (CN = 2-(2′-benzothienyl)pyridinato, acac = acetylacetonate) phosphor was studied using steady-state and time-resolved photoluminescence spectroscopy, which reveal that under conditions where molecular diffusion is inhibited (solid-state polymer matrix) triplet energy transfer only occurs via the Förster mechanism.180 Iridium to ruthenium energy transfer processes in a heterometallic [(ppy)2Ir(bpy)(phenyl)2(bpy)Ru(bpy)2]3+ complex and a related tetranuclear (Ir)2–Ir–Ru assembly are found to be too fast (ken > 108 s−1) to be accounted for by a through-space Förster mechanism, suggesting allowance by a through-bond-mediated Dexter mechanism.181 A luminescent multicomponent species comprised of a fullerene substituted [Ir(ppy)(bpy)]+ complex undergoes triplet–triplet energy transfer from an Ir-based MLCT state to a C60-based state at 77 K, resulting in phosphorescence (τ = 4.8 ms, λem = 826 nm) from the functionalised fullerene.182 The luminescent Ir(III) diimine bis(biotin) complexes [Ir(CN)(NN)]+ (CN = biotin-functionalised ppy ligands; NN = 4,7-diphenyl-phen or 3,4,7,8-tetramethyl-phen) show emission intensity enhancement and excited-state lifetime extension upon binding to the avidin protein.183 Visible light induced catalytic water reduction without an electron relay was achieved in a system comprising of a [Ir(ppy)2(bpy)](PF6) photosensitiser, colloidal platinum catalyst and sacrificial reductant with a hydrogen quantum yield of 0.26 and up to 63 turnovers.184

Analysis of the intervalence charge transfer bands in a series of mixed-valence heterobimetallic complexes of general structure [Rh(L2){η5-(X-ferrocenyl)-indenyl}]+ (L2 = COD, norbornadiene; L = CO; X = 1 or 2) revealed electronic interactions that range from moderate (Class II) to almost delocalised (Class II/III).185 Studies on light-harvesting systems having carboxy functionalised [Ru(terpy)2]2+ species assembled about dirhodium (II,II) tetracarboxylate cores show efficient energy transfer from the Ru-based 3MLCT state to the lowest-energy excited state of the Rh2 core, a process that is less efficient for triazine analogues of the terpy ligands.186 Quenching of Ru-based MLCT luminescence is observed in [(NN)2Ru(bpy)-(ph)n-(bpy)Rh(NN′)2]5+ (NN = 4,7-Me2-Phen; NN′ = 4,4′-Me2-bpy; ph = 1,4-phenylene; n = 1–3) dyads, indicating efficient intramolecular PET from the Ru moiety to Rh unit with rate constants determined by time-resolved emission and absorption spectroscopy in the nano- and pico-second time scale.187

Platinum complexes

The Pt(II) complexes [Pt(NNN)Cl]+ [NNN = 2,6-bis(N-methylbenzimidazol-2-yl), terpy] when incorporated into zirconium phosphate frameworks display intense emission at room temperature, characteristic of strong Pt⋯Pt interactions.188 Studies on the photophysical properties of [Pt(NNN)Cl]+ complexes have been performed, where NNN is a terpy-based ligand functionalised with alkyl- and aryl-acetylene groups,189 or an anthraquinone bridging ligand that was used to study the effect of Pt⋯Pt interactions on the excited state.190 Pt(II) terpyridyl acetylide complexes have been investigated with respect to their excited state absorption properties,191 as well as studies on inducing switching between LLCT, ILCT and MLCT lowest excited states by pH change and metal ions.192 The acetylide complexes [Pt(terpy)(L33)][BF4] display strong solvatochromism and form gels and liquid crystals with Pt⋯Pt and π–π* interactions, as evidenced by the appearance of an intense emission band in the near-IR at 830 nm.193 The luminescent phenolic ethynyl complexes [Pt(tBu3-terpy)(CC-4-OH-3-R-C6H4)]+ (R = H, Cl, CHO, CH2NH-pyridine) show fluorescence quenching in the presence of anions including fluoride, acetate and dihydrogenphosphate and selective sensing towards fluoride in some cases.194 The photophysical properties of a Pt(II) metallacycle complex bearing the bidentate diacetylide ligand tolan-2,2′-diacetylide (tda), [Pt(NN)(tda)] (NN = 4,4′-tBu-bpy), were compared with those of a reference compound [Pt(NN)(CCPh)2]. Both complexes display room temperature emission at ∼560 nm originating from a mixed-metal ligand-to-ligand charge transfer excited state, but the rigidity of the metalacycle complex reduces non-radiative decay, increasing quantum yields and excited state lifetimes.195 Sensitised near-IR lanthanide luminescence as a result of Pt → Ln energy transfer is observed in heterododecanuclear Pt6Ln6 (Ln = Nd, Yb) complexes of 4-ethynyl-2,2′-bipyridine (HCCbpy) incorporating an emissive [Pt(Me3SiCCbpy)(CCbpy)2] fragment.196 The fluorene-based ligands of [Pt(PnBu3)2(CCR)2] (R = benzothiazolylfluorene or diphenylaminofluorene) have strong effective two-photon absorption cross-sections, while the Pt(II) centre gives rise to efficient intersystem crossing to form the T1 state that is long-lived (τ > 100 μs) and likely to be localised on a single fluorenyl ligand.197 Short fluorescence lifetimes and low quantum yields were observed for [Pt(PnBu3)2(CCR)2] (R = thiophenealkynyl or phenylalkynyl) complexes, consistent with fast intersystem crossing to a triplet excited state that also has a relatively short phosphorescence lifetime.198 The luminescent acetylene- and (buta-1,3-diyne)-platinum(0) complexes, [(L)Pt{(1,2-η2)-R-(CC)n-R}] or [{(L)Pt}2{μ-(1,2-η2)-R-(CC)2-R}] [L = (PPh3)2 or 1,3-bis(diphenylphosphanyl)propane; n = 1 or 2; R = C6H5 or CH3], exhibit two emissions at 380–550 nm and 500–800 nm. The higher energy emission was attributed to the 3[P(dπ*) → Pt] transition and the lower energy, longer lived, emission was assigned to the 3[acetylene(π*) → Pt] state.199

The trinuclear cyclometalated Pt(II) complex [{(CNN)Pt}3(dpmp)] [CNN = 4,6-diphenyl-bpy; dpmp = bis(diphenylphosphinomethyl)phenylphosphine] exhibits a concentration dependent broad electronic absorption band from 400 to 600 nm and strong red emission in acetonitrile, ascribed to [dσ*π*] excited-states resulting from intramolecular Pt⋯Pt interactions that are more prevalent at higher concentrations.200 The luminescent cyclometalated complexes [Pt(L)Cl], [Pt2(L−)2] and [Pt(L)(PPh3)]+ [HL = 2-phenyl-6-(1H-pyrazol-3-yl)pyridine] display a moderate room temperature luminescent quantum yield for the former complex (0.14) and relatively high quantum yields (> 0.52) for the latter two complexes.201 Cyclometalation of ligated benzophenone imine Pt(II) compounds resulted in the formation of a family of complexes, VI, that are emissive at room temperature (λem ∼ 535 nm) and in the solid state (λem = 560–610 nm), with excited-state lifetimes of 300–600 ns.202 Due to metal perturbation on the anthracenyl ring in the dicyclometalated anthracenyl complexes syn- and anti-[Pt2(L)2(PAnP)]2+ (L = diphosphine, H2PAnP = 9,10-PPh2-anthracene), the 1B2u− ← 1Ag transition that is forbidden in anthracene is observed, and fluorescence is observed from the ligand-centered S1 excited state.203 The cyclometalated Pt(II) complexes [Pt(L34)(Cl)], [{Pt(L34)}2(μ-dppm)]2+, [{Pt(L34)}3(μ-dpmp)]3+ [dppm = bis(diphenylphosphino)methane; dpmp = bis(diphenylphosphinomethyl)phenylphosphine], are strongly emissive with quantum yields of up to 0.68 in solution at room temperature, and efficient yellow emitting LEDs were fabricated from the mononuclear complex.204

Short fluorescent lifetimes (in the ps range) and low quantum yields (ranging from 0.002 to 0.2) were determined for a series of stilbenoid NCN-pincer Pt(II) complexes, [PtCl(L35)], indicating that the Pt centre favours nonradiative decay when compared to the organic stilbene analogue.205 The electronic structures and spectroscopic properties of [Pt(CNMe)2(CN)2]n (n = 1−4), calculated using Møller–Plesset perturbation (MP2) and DFT methods, were used to explain that the experimentally observed red-shift of the emission bands that occurs upon aggregation is due to shortening of the Pt–Pt distance in the T1 state which results in promotion of an electron from the σ*[Pt(dz2)] to σ[Pt(pz)] orbital.206 Emission by [Pt(4-phenyl-terpy)(CN)]+ is from an excited state with largely 3MLCT character, but with some intraligand 3π–π* character (τ = 0.1 μs), but emission from [Pt(4-R-terpy)(CN)]+ complexes (R = o-CH3C6H4, o-ClC6H4 or o-CF3C6H4) appears exclusively intraligand 3π–π* in origin (τ ∼ 0.8 μs).207

Strong blue phosphorescence was observed at 77 K from thin films or CH2Cl2 matrices of the Pt(II) complexes [Pt(fppz)(μ-pz)]2 [fppzH = 3-CF3-5-(2-pyridyl)pyrazole; Hpz = pyrazole or 3,5-Me2-pyrazole], from a 3LC state that has MLCT character.208 A series of cyclometalated (C^N) complexes of general formula [Pt(C^N)(O^O)] and [Ir(C^N)2(O^O)] (O^O = acac-based ligand) are excellent photosensitisers for the production of singlet oxygen, with high quantum yields for the reaction observed for both the Pt(II) (Φ = 0.9–1.0) and Ir(III) (Φ = 0.5–0.9) complexes.209 The photo-hydrogen-evolving activity of three different Ru(II)Pt(II) dimers, [(bpy)2Ru(μ-bridge)PtCl2]2+ (bridge = tetraimine ligand), was evaluated and shown to be related to the extent of intramolecular quenching of the Ru-based 3MLCT excited state by the Pt(II) catalyst as well as steric effects at Pt.210 Changes in the emission maxima and intensity occur when solid samples of cis-[Pt(PN)2] (PN = diphenylphosphinoaminothiazoline) are exposed to alcohol vapours.211 In the presence of calf thymus DNA, luminescence enhancement occurs when [Pt(L25)(py)2]2+ binds externally to DNA at low [DNA]/[complex] ratios, whereas luminescence is quenched on intercalation at high [DNA]/[complex] ratios.212

The Pt(II) dithiolate complexes [Pt(mesBIAN)(SS)] (mesBIAN = bis(mesityl)-biazanaphthenequinone; SS = dithiolate) are non-luminescent when aromatic dithiolates are employed, but near-IR luminescence from a mixed metal-ligand to ligand charge transfer is observed when non-aromatic dithiolate ligands are employed.213 Attachment of the diimine dithiolate sensitisers [Pt(dcb)(SS)] (SS = cis-1,2-dicarbomethoxyethylene-1,2-dithiolate or 1,2-benzenedithiolate) to TiO2 provides an integrated system of chromophore-electron relay-catalyst for the visible-light driven generation of hydrogen from water.214 In the solid state, non-luminescent [Pt(R2-dto)2] (dto = dithiooxamide; R = butyl) absorbs gaseous HCl resulting in the formation of a luminescent ion pair species (λem ∼ 740 nm, τ = 28 ns); the process is reversed by exposure to ammonia vapours or upon heating.215 The p-dithiobenzoquinone linked [{(terpy)Pt}2{μ-Cp*Ir(η4-C6H4S2)}]4+ self aggregates in the solid state via Pt⋯Pt and π–π interactions that impart unusual UV-Vis and luminescence properties upon cooling, such as a colour change from yellow to magenta due to lattice contractions.216

Metalloporphyrin and metallophthalocyanine complexes

Molecular co-sensitisation for efficient panchromatic DSSCs was achieved by combining a zinc carboxyphthalocyanine sensitiser with another organic dye having complementary spectral properties, resulting in a DSSC that has a higher efficiency than could be achieved by using each dye separately.4 A near-IR absorbing unsymmetrical zinc phthalocyanine sensitiser, having three tBu groups and two carboxylic acid groups, yields a 3.05% power conversion efficiency under “one sun” when incorporated into a liquid electrolyte cell. This impressive performance was attributed to the directionality created in the excited state, achieved by the use of the unsymmetrically substituted donor and acceptor groups.217 Tetrachelate Zn(II) TPP chromophores having 3-carboxy, 4-carboxy, 3-(4-carboxyphenyl) and 3-ethynyl(4-carboxyphenyl) substituents on the TPP phenyl rings were used to evaluate the effect of spacer length and position on metal oxide semiconductor sensitisation. The meta-substituted systems, that allow the rings to lie flat and close to the semiconductor surface, display the largest charge injection efficiency.218 Slow electron injection into TiO2 from the T1 state of an axially substituted Ru(II) phthalocyanine dye demonstrates that ultrafast electron injection is not a pre-requisite for efficient photovoltaic device function.219

PET and PEnT processes in fullerene-metalloporphyrin systems continue to attract attention. For azobenzene-linked Zn(II) porphyrin-fullerene dyads, the rates of PET generating the charge-separated (CS) state are significantly faster than the rates of back electron transfer demonstrating that charge recombination is occurring in the Marcus inverted region.220 Self-assembled metalloporphyrin-C60 dyads employing a Hamilton receptor/cyanuric acid binding motif display PET to generate a CS state when a Zn(II) porphyrin is used, but PEnT is observed in the case of the analogous Sn(IV) complex due to the shift in the metal oxidation potential.221 Cytidine-guanosine hydrogen bonding interactions were used to assemble a Zn(II) phthalocyanine–C60 dyad, that undergoes PET to generate a CS state having a shorter lifetime (3.0 ns) than the analogous porphyrin dyad (2.02 μs).222 PET between a butadiyne linked Zn(II) porphyrin dimer and a connected C60 unit was controlled by selective excitation of either a perpendicular or a planar conformation of the dimer. Upon appending a ferrocene unit to the other end of the dimer and examining the long-range CS state that could be generated, the planar configuration had a charge recombination rate an order of magnitude greater than the perpendicular conformation.223 PET in a triad consisting of a ferrocene electron donor and fullerene electron acceptor attached to imidazole rings fused to opposite β,β′-pyrrolic positions of a Zn(II) porphyrin results in the formation of an unusually long-lived CS state, 630 μs. Studies showed that after porphyrin excitation, electron transfer to C60 occurs within 230 ps and is followed by hole transfer to ferrocene after 500 ps to generate the CS state.224 Electron transfer between ferrocene and C60 was also studied using butadiene-linked Zn(II) porphyrin oligomers (having one, two, three or four porphyrin units) as the bridge. In this instance, the rates of charge separation and long-range charge recombination were remarkably fast (kCR2 = 15–1.3 × 108 s−1) with little distance dependence.225 Changes in the absorption spectra were used to investigate on–off communication between fullerene substituents in nickel tetraazachlorin (TAC)–C60 conjugates, that is dependent on the nature of the TAC peripheral ligands.226 PET was observed in single-wall carbon nanotubes bearing covalently linked Zn(II) phthalocyanines using transient absorption spectroscopy, and the dynamics of charge-separation (2.0 × 1010 s−1) and charge recombination (1.5 × 106 s−1) reveal a notable stabilisation of the CS state in dimethylformamide.227

General trends in the photophysical and photochemical properties for a number of poly(oxyethylene)-substituted zinc and oxotitanium phthalocyanine complexes have been described. All the complexes show good singlet oxygen quantum yields (0.60–0.80) and hence have potential as Type II photosensitizers.228–230 Ultrafast electron transfer processes that occur upon Soret excitation of Zn(II) porphyrin complexes meso-linked directly to ferrocene were studied by femtosecond up-conversion and pump-probe techniques and used to estimate the charge separation rates from the S2 state (>1013 s−1) and S1 state (6.3 × 1012), and the CS state lifetime (∼20 ps).231 Efficient on/off fluorescence switching from thienyl- and phenyl-substituted Zn(II) porphyrin-ferrocene dyads was achieved by controlling excited state electron transfer quenching by oxidation/reduction of the ferrocene moiety.232 Singlet–singlet electron transfer rates have been determined for a series of cofacial dyads having a Zn(II) porphyrin acceptor and corrole or porphyrin free base donor with relatively flexible spacers. Faster electron transfer rates were observed when employing less sterically encumbered macrocycles, or more flexible spacers, due to stronger intermacrocycle interactions and a more favourable orientation of the chromophores.233 Donor–acceptor molecules of [ZnTPP] surrounded by four perylene-3,4:9,10-bis(dicarboximide) (PDI) chromophores aggregate in solution to form a columnar stack of five molecules. Laser excitation results in quantitative formation of [ZnTPP+˙–PDI−˙] ion pairs (τ = 4.8 ns) that recombine to produce the lowest triplet state of PDI.234 Intramolecular charge transfer in 5,15-bis(azulenylethynyl) substituted Zn(II) porphyrins induces significant enhancement of the near-IR two photon absorption.235 A series of chromophores combining ethyne bridged zinc porphyrin–[M(terpy)2]2+ (M = Ru, Os) and oligomeric, ethyne bridged zinc porphyrin architectures have excited state lifetimes that unusually can be modulated over 4 orders of magnitude (1 ns < τ < 45 μs). Transient absorption spectroscopy shows that the intensely absorbing excited state transition (T1 → Tn) can be extensively modulated (880 nm < λmax < 1126 nm), while concomitantly maintaining impressively large T1 → Tn absorption manifold spectral bandwidth.236 The S2- and S1-state properties of zinc porphyrins incorporated into polypeptides are dependent on the extent of polymerisation, and the singlet–singlet annihilation rate became faster with increasing degree of polymerisation because of the close distance between the zinc porphyrins.237 A study of singlet–singlet annihilation kinetics for a series of Zn(II) porphyrin appended polypropylenimine dendrimers shows that the energy transfer efficiency is significantly improved by extending the chain connecting the chromophore with the dendrimer by one additional carbon, creating more favourable chromophore distances and orientations.238

A zinc chlorophyll derivative that self-assembles into a cyclic tetramer exhibits ultrafast energy transfer between porphyrins that is ∼20 times faster than other self-assembled porphyrin tetramers, but is still ∼10 times slower than the fastest rates observed in photosynthetic proteins.239 Charge recombination and charge separation rates in a series of Zn(II) porphyrin donor, phenyl bridge, Au(I) or Fe(III) acceptor (D–B–A) systems were investigated to determine the effect of D–A distance, D–B energy gap and acceptor influence on the CS state lifetime.240 A strongly coupled zinc phthalocyanine-tin porphyrin dyad undergoes ultra-fast single step charge separation over a 34 Å distance upon light excitation.241 Long-range triplet excited state equilibration over 30 Å occurs in a complex consisting of [Pt(TPP)] antennae connected by a fluorene-thiophene-fluorene bridge to a Pt(II) quinolate chromophore, and is characterised by a double exponential emission decay (τ1 = 39 ± 4 ps, τ2 = 351 ± 15 ps) that suggests the participation of three separate excited states.242 Fluorescence from a [Zn(TPP)] chromophore is effectively quenched by intramolecular PET to appended Eu(III) bis(phthalocyaninato) complexes, with the charge separation (several hundred fs) and charge recombination (several ps) processes affected by the connecting position and number of porphyrin moieties.243

Oxidation of an Fe(III) porphyrin complex followed by irradiation with 355 nm laser light gave a highly reactive porphyrin-iron-oxo transient that reacts with organic reductants such as alkenes and arylalkanes.244 The ground and excited state reactivity of an Fe(II) porphyrinogen that has extremely short-lived excited states was studied.245 Ultrafast stimulated emission and structural dynamics in nickel porphyrins were investigated using ultrafast transient absorption spectroscopy that shows intersystem crossing in the S2 state is faster than the internal conversion of S2 → S1, and excited state dynamics that are dependent on the nature of the meso-substituents.246 Ultrafast transient absorption spectroscopy was used to study the effect of benzoannulation and α-octabutoxy substitution on the photophysical behaviour of Ni(II) phthalocyanines, demonstrating that peripheral substitution modifies the deactivation mechanism of the S1(π,π*) state by inducing substantial changes in the relative energies of the metal, ligand and charge-transfer excited states.247 A Pt(II)-tetraphenyltetrabenzoporphyrin has been employed as a dopant in an efficient and stable NIR OLED device, having an external quantum efficiency greater than 6% at λmax = 765 nm and a full width at half maximum of 500 cm−1 (31 nm).248 A 4,4′-bis(5-tetracenyl)-1,1′-biphenylene emitter/Pd(II) porphyrin sensitiser couple was used for noncoherently excited photon up-conversion from the near-IR (around 700 nm) into blue-green emission (480–580 nm) with an external quantum yield of 0.04.249

Lanthanide complexes

There continues to be significant interest in the use of organic chromophores to sensitise emission from lanthanide ions due to the wide range of applications these luminescent species have. There have been a number of studies on sensitised Ln(III) ion emission incorporating a variety of organic sensitisers based on: benzothiazole derivatives;250 carbazole,251 ferrocene252 and tartaric acid253 functionalised β-diketonates; pyridoxal and amino acid derived Schiff bases;254 tetradentate 1-hydroxypyridin-2-one derivatives;255 septadentate N,N-(2-hydroxy-5-methyl-1,3-xylylene)bis(N-(carboxymethyl)glycine) (5-Me-HXTA);256 8-hydroxy-5-nitroquinoline;257 macrocylic azacarboxylates (DO3A) linked to benzophenone via amide linkers;258 and carboxylate functionalised bipyridines.259,260 Thiophene-derivatised pyridine-bis(oxazoline) is a highly efficient sensitiser of lanthanide emission, as reflected in the high emission quantum yields of the Eu(III) (76.2 ± 6.6%) and Tb(III) (58.6 ± 4.1%) complexes.261 Chiral, octadentate, 2-hydroxyisophthalamide ligands are able to sensitise emission from four different luminescent lanthanide cations [Sm(III), Eu(III), Tb(III), Dy(III)], each of which have strong circularly polarised luminescence activity and lifetimes ranging from the millisecond to microsecond time scale.262 Strong ligand (π–π*) sensitised emission is observed from the fully fluorinated NIR-emitting complexes [Ln(L36)3] (Ln = Nd, Yb, Er), although unexpectedly the emission is partially quenched from the visible emitting complexes (Ln = Eu, Tb, Sm, Dy) due to a low-lying ligand π–σ* state. The NIR-emitting complexes display unprecedented luminescence lifetimes in deuterated acetonitrile [τ = 44 μs (Nd), 741 μs (Er) and 1111 μs (Yb)].263 Near-IR emission in lanthanide tris 2-carboxamide-8-hydroxyquinoline complexes is substantially enhanced by ligand disubstitution with bromine, with a 2.5–3.0 fold increase in the quantum yield observed.264 Pyridine dicarboxamide-based ligands sensitise emission from Eu(III) by two-photon absorption of the ligand, an illustration of the two-photon antenna effect phenomenon.265 Anthracene (An) sensitised emission from [Ln(L37)(hfac)3] (hfac = hexafluoroacetylacetonate) occurs via an unusual mechanism in which anthracene fluorescence is initially quenched by intramolecular PET to the benzimidazole unit to generate a short lived CS state that collapses by back electron transfer to give 3An*, which then sensitises the Ln(III) centre by energy transfer.266 Addition of [15]crown-5 to SmI2 in acetonitrile produces a sterically encumbered complex that decreases solvent induced luminescence quenching of the Sm(II) complex and increases the excited state lifetime.267

Self-assembled hexametallic rings of [Eu(6-CO2-terpy)2]+, incorporating a central hexacoordinated lanthanide cation, display luminescence quantum yields that are similar to that for the monomeric complex indicating the absence of intramolecular quenching effects.268 Complexation of either enantiomer of the chiral ligand L38 with Ln(III) ions results in the formation of a homochiral luminescent assembly, as evidenced by their circularly polarised luminescence spectra in solution.269 Dinuclear Yb/Yb and Yb/Al helicates based on bis(8-hydroxyquinoline) ligands display efficient near-IR emission, with slightly lower luminescence quantum yields and lifetimes observed for the homobimetallic helicates due to intramolecular self-quenching.270 Emission from a family of luminescent “molecular lanterns” [Ln2L4] [L = 3,5-bis(benzocarboxylate)-4-amino-1,2,4-triazole] can be tuned by the reversible incorporation of a [Ln(H2O)8]3+ cation within the cage.271,272 The lanthanide-organic frameworks [LnLn′(L)3(H2O)2](NN) (H2L = adipic acid; Ln = Tb; Ln′ = Tb, Eu; NN = 4,4′-bpy) exhibit Ln-based emission that is sensitised by the uncoordinated 4,4′-bpy guest molecule.273 NIR luminescence of the Er(III) ion in [Er1.4Yb0.6(L)6(phen)2] (L = benzoate) is enhanced compared to the homodinuclear Er2 complex due to a Yb → Er energy transfer process.274

Luminescent lanthanide complexes, typically having cyclen-based sensitisers, have been developed for use as emissive DNA275 and biological probes,276–278 that have also been shown to have good cellular uptake and low cytotoxicity.279–283 Excitation luminescence spectroscopy of Eu(III) complexes of 1,4,7-tris(carbomoylmethyl)-1,4,7,10-tetraazacyclododecane derivatives has been used to monitor the kinetics of their catalytic cleavage of a RNA analogue.284 Luminescence of a Eu(III)-tetracycline (1:1) complex is quenched upon binding to adenosine and guanosine triphosphates (ATP and GTP) and was used as a fluorescent probe to monitor conversions of ATP and GTP to their enzymatic products.285 Luminescent cyclen-based lanthanide complexes have also been developed for the detection of bacterial spores,286 as probes for monitoring the enzymatic reduction of appended reporter substrates,287 and as anion sensors.288

Bis phosphine Pt(II) alkynyl chromophores functionalised with a terpy ligand have been coordinated to {Ln(hfac)3} fragments to generate heterotrinuclear PtLn2 complexes that exhibit sensitised Ln(III) luminescence via efficient Pt → Ln energy transfer from Pt(II) alkynyl based 3MLCT and 3ILCT excited states.289 Similar Pt2Ln4 heteronuclear complexes also exhibit efficient energy transfer through an alkynyl-terpy bridge that has a Pt⋯Ln separation of ca. 14.2 Å and Pt → Ln energy transfer rates of 6.07 × 107 s−1 for Pt2Nd4 and 2.12 × 105 s−1 for Pt2Yb4 species.290 An Er(III) terpy complex coordinated to carboxylate functionalised [Pt(TPP)] porphyrins exhibits sensitised luminescence (Φ = 0.015%), with photophysical studies and theoretical calculations suggesting Pt → Er energy transfer occurs through the first excited state of the porphyrin via a Förster resonance mechanism.291 Heterodimetallic bisporphyrins complexes (YbZn, YbPd and YbPt) in which a porphyrinate moiety is linked to a transition-metal porphyrinate by a flexible three carbon chain exhibit sensitised NIR emission with extended lifetimes and intensities increasing in the order YbZn < YbPd < YbPt.292 Co-crystallisation of [Co(CN)6]3− or [Cr(CN)6]3− with Ln(III) (Nd, Yb) salts affords dinuclear (M = Co) and infinite chain (M = Cr) cyanide bridged d/f systems that exhibit near-IR sensitised luminescence due to fast (>108 s−1) energy transfer to the Ln(III) centre via the Dexter mechanism.293 Cyanide-bridged near-IR luminescent coordination networks containing [Ru(phen)(CN)4]2− anions and Ln(III) cations coordinated to oligopyridine ligands (phen, terpy and bpm) adopt a number of different structural types which have Ru → Ln energy transfer rates that depend on the energy of the Ln(III) f–f state, with Nd(III) (having a high density of low-lying f–f states) being the most effective energy acceptor and Yb(III) (with a single low-lying f–f state) the least effective.294 The free diimine binding site of the luminescent complexes [Re(CO)3Cl(bppz)] and [Pt(CC-C6H4CF3)2(bppz)] [bppz = 2,3-bis(2-pyridyl)pyrazine] were used to bind {Ln(diketonate)3} fragments and sensitise near-IR emission. The efficiency of the 3MLCT luminescence quenching by Re/Pt → Ln energy transfer is similarly dependent on the energy of the Ln(III) f–f states.295

A series of phosphine oxide (PO) Eu(III) complexes, [Eu(PO)2(tta)3] (tta = 2-thenoyltrifluoroacetonate), display excellent electroluminescence performance. Four-layer OLED devices based on these pure species have maximum brightness of more than 1000 cd m−2, turn-on voltages lower than 5 V, maximum external quantum yields of more than 3% and excellent spectral stability.296 Ce(III) complexes encapsulated by tripodal polybenzimidazole ligands display ligand sensitised bright blue emission in solution (Φ = 0.55) and in the solid state, with studies on the electroluminescent properties demonstrating the potential for using Ce(III) complexes in LED devices.297
