Abstract

We report a simple and novel method involving an ethyl methyl imidazolium triflate ionic liquid (EMI OTf) to prepare highly mechanically stable crystalline Î±-Mn2O3 and calcium-incorporated manganese oxide (CaMn-oxide) thin film water oxidation electrocatalysts (WOCs) on a fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) electrode (FTO/EMI Î±-Mn2O3 and FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide). Electrochemical measurements showed that the incorporation of calcium into manganese oxide promotes water oxidation at a very low energy cost, similar to that observed in natural photosystem II (PSII). The low energy cost is due to the involvement of the MnIVO species in the catalysis as compared to the MnVO species that has been previously detected in calcium-free Î±-Mn2O3. Moreover, the FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide electro-catalyst showed unprecedentedly high activity (TOF â‰ˆ 29.8 sâˆ’1 at Î· = 0.57 V) and robustness (tested for 40 h) for water oxidation in a neutral potassium phosphate (KPi) buffer solution (0.1 M, pH 7.0). The mechanism of water oxidation by FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide was discussed and applied for water oxidation by the Î¼-oxido-CaMn4O5 cluster of photosystem II (PS II).

The most efficient catalytic water oxidation, however, is achieved by the Î¼-oxido CaMn-oxide cluster (CaMn4O5) housed in an enzyme called photosystem II (PSII) in plants, algae, and cyanobacteria; this system achieves water oxidation with a turnover frequency (TOF) of 100 to 400 sâˆ’1.15â€“18 Consequently, particular attention has been paid to Mn-oxides as bio-mimic, earth-abundant, and eco-friendly WOCs.19â€“21 Indeed, various Mn-oxides, including Î±-Mn2O3,22,23 Mn3O4,21 Î»-MnO2,23,24 Î²-MnO2,25â€“27 R-MnO2,23 Î±-MnO2,28 and Î³-MnO2,29 have been reported as WOCs.

Although using CaMn-oxides as dispersed powder WOCs in the presence of chemical or photochemical oxidants is of great interest, their immobilization on conducting electrodes such as FTO and their use as anodes in electrochemical water splitting devices is important to their practical applications. However, few studies on the immobilization of these compounds have been reported. To the best of our knowledge, there are a few reports in the literature where CaMn-oxides were immobilized by dip-coating,48,49 screen-printing,48,49 ion-beam-sputtering,50 or NafionÂ®-assisted techniques.51 In the first report by RamÃ­rez et al.,48 a CaMn-oxide (Ca2Mn3O8) film was supported on FTO/quartz or quartz substrates by either dip-coating (â‰ˆ100 nm thickness) or screen-printing (6 Î¼m) and tested for the water oxidation reaction in 0.1 M KPi (pH 7.0) and 1.0 M tris(hydroxymethyl)amino-methane (pH 7.8). We previously optimized an electrochemical/sintering route for the preparation of a highly active and robust FTO/Î±-Mn2O3 WOC and studied its kinetics and mechanism for water oxidation.52,53 However, all our attempts and those of others to use electrochemical deposition routes for the preparation of Ca-incorporated Mn-oxides have been unsuccessful.49 Here, we report a simple novel route for the preparation of highly mechanically stable thin films of EMI OTf-supported Î±-Mn2O3 and CaMn-oxide WOCs on an FTO electrode surface (FTO/EMI Î±-Mn2O3 and FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide). Careful investigations of the two electro-catalysts showed enhancement of their water oxidation activities by incorporation of Ca into the Mn-oxide. Moreover, the involvement of Ca promotes water oxidation at the MnIV oxidation state. The MnIV species is easily accessible at a low energy cost compared with the MnV active species that is required for catalysis in the absence of Ca. This indicates the role of Ca in the Ca incorporated Mn-oxides. A mechanism for water oxidation by FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide was proposed and discussed along with that reported for water oxidation by the Î¼-oxido-CaMn4O5 cluster of photosystem II (PS II).

The FTO/EMI Î±-Mn2O3 and FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide electro-catalysts were prepared by spreading MnCl2Â·4H2O (1.0 g, 5.05 mmol) or a mixed solution of MnCl2Â·4H2O (1.0 g, 5.05 mmol) and CaCl2 (0.56 g, 5.05 mmol) in MeOH (4.0 mL) containing 20% v/v EMI OTf ionic liquid (1.0 mL) on the FTO surface using a doctor blade at 110 Â°C, respectively. The FTO/EMI Î±-Mn2O3 and FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide precursors were then sintered at 550 Â°C for 1 h. The resulting films were washed extensively under flowing water, resulting in highly mechanically strong films that were not removed by hard scratching or long sonication times; this indicates the good adhesion properties of the films due to strong binding between the particles as well as between the particles and the conducting FTO surface. Indeed, we also recently reported the utilization of EMI OTf ionic liquid for the preparation of a highly mechanically stable thin film of BiVO4 water oxidation photoanode on FTO electrode.54

High resolution X-ray crystallographic studies showed that three Mn and one Ca of the cluster form a distorted Î¼-oxido CaMn3O4 cubane motif, and a fourth dangler Mn binds to the cubane via two bridging Î¼-oxido ligands. Four water molecules are coordinated to the dangler MnIII and CaII ions.15,16,66,67 A schematic of the structure of the cluster is shown in the yellow centre of Scheme 1. Previous mechanistic and kinetic studies of water oxidation by FTO/Î±-Mn2O3 showed that the Mn active site of the catalyst undergoes two stepwise proton-coupled electron transfer (PCET) oxidations (MnIIâ€“OH2/MnIIIâ€“OH at 0.62 V and MnIIIâ€“OH/MnIVO at 1.17 V) followed by an electron transfer oxidation (MnIVO/MnVO at more than 1.4 V) to generate the MnVO active species. Finally, the MnVO active species undergoes a rate-limiting electrophilic attack on H2O/OH to release O2.52 A careful comparison of the electrochemical properties of the FTO/EMI Î±-Mn2O3 and FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide electro-catalysts allowed us to propose a mechanism of water oxidation by FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide, as shown in Scheme 1. Previous studies indicated that CaMn-oxides have similar structural motifs to the Î¼-oxido-CaMn4O5 clusters of PS II.41 The Mn adopts an MnO6 octahedral geometry with a surface-bound water molecule. The Ca at the surface has a six-coordinated structure with surface-bound water molecules.47 In the current proposed mechanism, species 1 represents MnIIâ€“OH2 and Caâ€“OH2 at the active site of the catalyst (see XPS results). The MnIIâ€“OH2 active site undergoes a PCET oxidation at Ep1 = 0.67 V to form MnIIIâ€“OH (species 2). The presence of Ca enables easier oxidation of the Mn site in FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide than that observed for FTO/EMI Î±-Mn2O3 (Ep1 = 0.76 V) (Fig. 4a). Indeed, Ca has been reported to stabilize and facilitate high oxidation states of Mn.21,73 Moreover, redox inactive metal ions with Lewis acid properties, such as Ca2+, are known to have major influences on redox-active transition metal complexes to promote a variety of reactions involving the transfer of electrons.74

Upon further oxidation, a catalytic current peak concomitant with O2 evolution started at Eapp. = 0.95 V. Studies on the pH dependence of the onset potential of the peak potential values of this catalytic water oxidation over the pH range of 5.0 to 10.0 reveal that its onset potentials shift reductively by approximately 59 mV per pH decade (Fig. S8â€ ), suggesting stepwise proton-coupled electron transfer (PCET) oxidations of the active site MnIIIâ€“OH to MnIVO. It is interesting that the redox potential, 0.90â€“0.95 V, for S1/S2 and S2/S3 of the OEC cluster that involves MnIII/IV oxidation is similar to the redox potential for the MnIII/IV oxidation of the current system. A very close structure model of the cluster {[CaMn4O4(tBuCO2)8 (tBuCO2H)2(py)] (tBu = tert-butyl; py = pyridine)} showed MnIII2 MnIV2/MnIII MnIV3 oxidation at a slightly lower potential of 0.8 V.75 The observation of the catalytic current peak over the MnIIIâ€“OH/MnIVO oxidation of FTO/EMI Î±-Mn2O3 (Ep2 = 1.14 V) clearly indicates that MnIVO is the active species involved in the catalysis process (species 3). Moreover, the observation of this catalytic current only for FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide and its absence in FTO/EMI Î±-Mn2O3 indicates the crucial role of Ca in this catalytic peak current. We propose that H2O bound to Ca undergoes electrophilic attack of the oxygen of the terminal MnIVO species to form the Oâ€“O bond of the evolved O2 (species 5 and 7). In natural photosynthesis, Ca has been proposed to play structural and functional roles in the Î¼-oxido-CaMn4O5 cluster of PS II.76,77 It is proposed to play a role as a weak Lewis acid, where H2O/OH bound to Ca forms the Oâ€“O bond of the evolved O2 by nucleophilic attack, however, to oxygen of a terminal MnVO or MnIVO species in the final state of the water oxidation reaction.68,78â€“82 Moreover, the presence of a peroxide bridge between Ca and Mn in the Î¼-oxido-CaMn4O5 cluster of PS II was recently reported.67 The current results, however, showed that the generation of MnVO species requires a high potential (>1.4 V) that is greater than the oxidizing power of P680+/P680 (1.1 V) of PS II. Indeed, reported density functional theory calculations on Ca birnessite demonstrated that MnIII/IV oxidation occurs before Oâ€“O bond formation and OHâˆ’ bound to Ca2+ is involved in the Oâ€“O bond formation through electrophilic attack, however, by the Î¼-oxo bridges between Mn centers.47

Applying a more positive potential to FTO/CaMn-oxide resulted in the formation of a MnVO active site (species 4) that undergoes nucleophilic attack by an outer sphere H2O (as observed with FTO/Î±-Mn2O3)52 or H2O bound to either Ca (species 6 and 7), an adjacent Mn site, or a combination of them. A direct attack of an outer sphere H2O on a terminal oxido ligand bound to Mn is also proposed in PS II.83 The different Tafel slopes observed for the current FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide and FTO/Î±-Mn2O3 (Fig. S7â€ ) is consistent with different water oxidation mechanisms by the two electrocatalysts.61

Our current results demonstrate that the MnIVO active species can be accessible with low oxidizing power, similar to that of PS II, and this species is active only for water oxidation in the presence of Ca. The MnVO active species requires a high oxidizing power, greater than that of PSII, and is only accessible in model compounds with high potentials obtained either electrochemically or by powerful oxidants. We are currently investigating the proposed mechanism in more detail.

Conclusions

In conclusion, we report a novel method involving EMI OTF ionic liquid for the preparation of highly mechanically stable thin films of Î±-Mn2O3 and CaMn-oxide water oxidation electro-catalysts on FTO electrode. Both electro-catalysts showed high water oxidation activity and stability in neutral KPi buffer solution (0.1 M, pH 7.0). The incorporation of Ca remarkably enhances the water oxidation activity of the FTO/EMI CaMn-oxide electro-catalyst. Moreover, the presence of Ca promotes water oxidation at the MnIVO active species, which is accessible at a low energy cost. A mechanism of water oxidation by the CaMn-oxide electro-catalyst was proposed and discussed in context with that of PS II.
