Abstract

Î±-Fe2O3 is an ideal photoanode for solar water oxidation owing to its visible light absorbing capability, suitable valence band position, low cost, high abundance, non-toxicity and eco-friendliness. However, the reported efficiencies are very low due to the poor kinetics of water oxidation by photogenerated holes on Î±-Fe2O3. In the present study, an Î±-Fe2O3 electrode is obtained by heating a film of Fe which is prepared by the electrochemical reduction of Fe2+ ions. Film thickness and calcination temperature are carefully optimized to get a maximum photoresponse in neutral phosphate solution. In order to improve the water oxidation kinetics and reduce the charge carrier recombination, an iridium-phosphate (Ir-Pi) catalyst is electrodeposited on the surface of Î±-Fe2O3. Ir-Pi is found to reduce the OER onset potential by 350 mV, and enhances the photocurrent density by 3 times at 1.23 V vs. RHE.

1. Introduction

Solar water splitting requires photons with a minimum energy of 1.23 eV. However, in practice, additional energy is needed to carry out this reaction at reasonable rates, mainly due to kinetic overpotentials.1 Assuming 0.4 V overpotential for the anodic reaction and 0.2 V for the cathodic reaction, a light absorbing material with a band gap energy of 1.83 eV would be needed to split water molecules.2 At the same time, very high bandgap materials such as TiO2, ZnO, etc. are not suitable for practical applications because they can absorb only the ultra-violet portion of solar light. The optimum band gap required is around 2.0 eV as this provides sufficient energy for water splitting by absorbing a major part of the visible region of the solar spectrum.3

One of the most interesting semiconductors is hematite (Î±-Fe2O3) which has the band gap energy close to 2.0 eV, and valence band position positive enough for water oxidation reaction. Furthermore, it is attractive owing to high abundance, low cost, non-toxicity, good stability, and environmental compatibility.4,5 On the other hand, its conduction band is not negative to water reduction potential and hence external bias is needed to drive hydrogen evolution reaction at the cathode. The required bias voltage can be provided by a solar cell connected in a tandem configuration. Despite having favorable light absorbing features and valence band position, the reported efficiencies of photoelectrochemical (PEC) water oxidation at Î±-Fe2O3 electrodes are extremely low.4â6 The poor efficiency has been attributed to a low rate constant of water oxidation reaction (WOR) by photogenerated holes, and short hole diffusion length (2â4 nm) which allows only holes close to the surface to participate in the reaction.7 Issues related with short hole diffusion lengths have been largely addressed by nanostructuring of the photoelectrodes.8 However, surface and bulk recombination losses still limit the overall performance of hematite based photoanodes. Recent studies suggest that the light driven oxygen evolution reaction (OER) at Î±-Fe2O3 is sluggish, and most of the photogenerated holes simply undergo recombination with electrons.9 The recombination shifts the photocurrent onset away from the flatband potential, thus, limiting the water splitting efficiency. It has been shown that this loss process can be reduced by interfacing hematite electrodes with OER cocatalysts.9,10

Recently, electrochemical water oxidation at low overpotentials was demonstrated by our group using an amorphous iridium-phosphate (Ir-Pi) catalyst electrodeposited onto carbon paper electrode.11 It is anticipated that the photogenerated holes in Î±-Fe2O3 are capable of driving water oxidation reaction by Ir-Pi electrocatalyst. Herein, we report the electrodeposition of Ir-Pi catalyst on the surface of Î±-Fe2O3, which in turn is prepared by heating an electrodeposited Fe film on FTO coated glass. The hybrid Ir-PiâFe2O3 is used for the photoelectrochemical water oxidation in neutral phosphate buffer solution. Ir-Pi reduces the onset potential of OER by 350 mV and enhances the photocurrent by three times at 1.23 V vs. RHE.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Reagents, materials and characterization studies

Analytical grade KH2PO4, K2HPO4, FeSO4Â·7H2O (Merck) and IrCl3 (Aldrich) were used as received. All solutions were prepared using double distilled water. Phosphate buffer solution was prepared by mixing calculated volumes of 0.1 M KH2PO4 and 0.1 M K2HPO4 solutions. The solution pH was maintained at 7.0, unless otherwise stated. The physical characterization studies of the materials were carried out by using scanning electron microscopy (SEM), energy dispersive X-ray analysis (EDXA), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), diffuse reflectance spectroscopy and Raman spectroscopy. For SEM images, Ultra 55 scanning electron microscope equipped with EDXA system at 20 kV was used. Surface compositions of the electrodeposits were analyzed by XPS using SPECS GmbH spectrometer (Phoibos 100 MCD Energy Analyzer) with Mg KÎ± radiation. Raman spectra were collected using LabRam HR (UV) system at an excitation wavelength of 532 nm. UV-Vis diffuse reflectance spectroscopic studies were carried out by Perkin Elmer Lambda 750 spectrophotometer with pure BaSO4 as the reference. The optical band gap was calculated from Tauc plot for which Î± value was extracted from reflectance using KubelkaâMunk model. Electrochemical deposition and photoelectrochemical studies were conducted using PARC EG&G potentiostat/galvanostat model Versastat II as the electrochemical measurement unit and Xe lamp as the light source. The light from the source was calibrated to 1 Sun intensity (100 mW cmâ2) using a photodiode power meter (Thorlab, S120B) and a standard solar cell. Fe2O3 or Ir-PiâFe2O3 (area = 1 cm2) as the working electrode, saturated calomel electrode (SCE) reference and a large area Pt counter electrode were used. Photoelectrochemical studies were carried out by linear sweep voltammetry at 50 mV sâ1 in 0.1 M neutral phosphate solution under dark, and illumination from both front and back sides of the coating. Applied bias photon-to-current efficiencies (ABPE) of the electrodes were calculated from voltammograms, assuming 100% faradaic efficiency. Incident photon-to-current efficiency (IPCE) was measured using Oriel IQE 200 equipped with a Xe lamp and Cornerstone monochromator. Electrochemical impedance spectra were recorded by CH Instruments potentiostat/galvanostat model CHI 608C, which was coupled to a frequency response analyzer. An ac perturbation of 10 mV was applied at a frequency of 10 kHz and impedance was measured as a function of applied potential. MottâSchottky plot generated from the impedance data was used for the determination of flat band potential. Current density values are reported on the basis of geometrical area. Potential values are converted to reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) scale using the equation, ERHE = ESCE + E0SCE + 0.059 Ã pH, where ESCE is the measured potential using SCE reference and E0SCE is the standard potential of SCE at 25 Â°C (0.242 V), unless otherwise stated.

2.2. Electrochemical deposition of Fe film

Iron film was electrodeposited onto fluorine doped tin oxide (FTO) coated conducting glass plate (TISXZ006, sheet resistance < 10 Î© sqâ1) as reported in the literature.12 Prior to the deposition, FTO glass plates were cleaned thoroughly by sonication for 15 min each in water, acetone and isopropanol. The FTO glass slides were dried in Ar flow and electrical contact was made through a Cu wire attached to the conducting side of FTO glass using Ag paste. The exposed area was 1 cm2 and remaining part of the electrode was covered with PTFE tape. Electrodeposition was carried out in a glass cell with FTO as the working electrode, a large Pt foil as counter electrode and a SCE as the reference. The electrolyte was freshly prepared 10 mM FeSO4Â·7H2O solution in water. Electrodeposition was carried out at â1.05 V vs. SCE for different time intervals in the range 1â15 min. After deposition, electrodes were gently rinsed with water, dried in Ar flow and stored in vacuum desiccators.

2.3. Preparation of Î±-Fe2O3

The electrodeposited Fe film was converted to Î±-Fe2O3 by thermal treatment. For this, Fe film coated FTO glass plates were separated from Cu contact wires. Silver paste used for the contact was completely removed using acetone to avoid any possible diffusion of Ag during high temperature heating. The FeâFTO plate was kept in an alumina boat and heated in air at different temperatures and durations. Temperature was in the range of 300â900 Â°C and heating time was 1â6 h. Formation of Î±-Fe2O3 was monitored by XRD and Raman spectroscopy.

2.4. Electrodeposition of Ir-Pi on Î±-Fe2O3

Amorphous iridium-phosphate (Ir-Pi) was deposited onto Î±-Fe2O3 coated FTO electrodes using electrochemical method as reported in the case of Ir-Pi deposition on carbon electrodes.11 The electrolyte was 0.5 mM IrCl3 in 0.1 M potassium phosphate solution (pH 7.0). Deposition was carried out 1.0 V vs. SCE for different time intervals, typically in the range of 0â40 min without stirring the solution. After deposition, electrodes were thoroughly rinsed with double distilled water, dried at 60 Â°C for 1 h and stored in a desiccator.

3.2. Morphology

The morphology of the electrodeposit was studied by SEM. As it is seen from Fig. 2a, the electrodeposited Fe film has cubic shaped particles. Raman spectra were recorded for all the samples. The as-deposited Fe film doesn't exhibit any Raman active bands (Fig. 3(i)). 3.4. Chemical composition

XRD and Raman studies confirm both electrodeposition of Fe film and its conversion to Î±-Fe2O3 upon heating at 600 Â°C in air. Hence further physicochemical studies are mainly performed for detecting Ir and P after the electrochemical deposition of Ir-Pi catalyst. Hence, EDXA and XPS measurements were carried out for Fe2O3 before and after Ir-Pi deposition. EDXA spectra are shown Fig. 4. In the case of Fe2O3 (Fig. 4a), major peaks are assigned to Fe, O, Si and Sn. X-ray photoelectron spectra were recorded for Fe2O3 after electrochemically depositing Ir-Pi and the results are shown in Fig. 5. In the Fe 2p region (Fig. 5a), a pair of peaks are present at 724.4 and 710.8 eV, which correspond to Fe 2p1/2 and 2p3/2, respectively.19 Fe 2p3/2 peak appears to be narrower and stronger than Fe 2p1/2. Also, the area of Fe 2p3/2 peak is greater than that of Fe 2p1/2 because Fe 2p3/2 has degeneracy of four states while Fe 2p1/2 has only two, in spinâorbit coupling.20 A satellite peak of Fe 2p3/2 peak is found at 718.9 eV which is 8.1 eV higher than the binding energy of Fe 2p3/2 and this peak does not overlap with main photoelectron peaks. There is another peak at higher energy side of Fe 2p1/2, which may be a satellite peak for Fe 2p1/2. In the Fe 2p spectrum, no characteristic peaks for metallic Fe or other oxides of Fe are found. The results indicate that the Fe is in Fe3+ state and the oxide formed after heat treatment to Fe film is predominantly Fe2O3. In the high resolution spectrum of Ir 4f (Fig. 5b), a doublet is present at 62.2 and 65.2 eV, which correspond to Ir 4f7/2 and 4f5/2, respectively.21 The 4f5/2 peak is more intense than the 4f7/2 signal and the spinâorbit splitting value is 3.0 eV. These results are comparable to the literature values of Ir 4f in the Ir-Pi catalysts on carbon paper.11 The peak at 133.1 eV in P 2p spectrum (Fig. 5c) is assigned to phosphate.22 This is similar to the binding energy of P 2p in the Ir-Pi catalyst on carbon electrode,11 but the binding energy is marginally greater (0.3 eV) than the P 2p peak observed in the case of Co-Pi deposited on carbon.23

3.5. Bandgap measurements

In order to study the optical properties, UV-VIS diffuse reflectance spectroscopy (DRS) measurements were carried out and the results are shown in Fig. 6a. The prepared Î±-Fe2O3 displays strong light absorption capability both in UV and visible light region (Fig. 6a(i)). The absorption edge is around 567 nm which is close to the values reported in the literature.4â6 However, after depositing Ir-Pi on the surface of Î±-Fe2O3, the absorption onset is blue shifted to 545 nm (Fig. 6a(ii)), indicating a modified band gap. Also, Ir-Pi deposited Fe2O3 shows higher reflectance than bare Fe2O3 between 450 and 750 nm. To accurately find the band gaps of the materials, Tauc equation was used,24
Â 
(Î±hÎ½) = A(hÎ½ â Eg)n (1)
where Î± is the absorption coefficient, hÎ½ is photon energy, A is a constant which does not depend on hÎ½ and Eg is the band gap. Here, n can take values of 3, 2, 3/2 and 1/2 corresponding to indirect (forbidden), indirect (allowed), direct (forbidden) and direct (allowed) transitions, respectively.25 Thus, the plots of (Î±hÎ½)1/n versus hÎ½ (Tauc plots) yield the value of the Eg when extrapolated to (Î±hÎ½)1/n = 0. In the present case, n = 2 is used to account for the indirect allowed optical transition in Fe2O3 and Î± values are extracted from the reflectance using KubelkaâMunk radiative transfer model.26 As it is seen from the inset of Fig. 6a, the Tauc plot provides the Eg of 2.18 eV for bare Fe2O3 whereas it is slightly modified to 2.28 eV after depositing Ir-Pi catalyst.

Flat band potential (EFB) is an approximate measure of the conduction band position in n-type semiconductors. Moreover, it provides the theoretical onset potential (Eonset) for the photoelectrochemical oxygen evolution reaction. In general, the flatband potential is determined by measuring the capacitance (C) at different electrode potential (E) using the following equation,

Â 
1/C2 = [2/ÎµrÎµ0eNd][E â EFB â (kBT/e)] (2)
where e is the electronic charge, Îµr is the dielectric constant of the semiconductor, Îµ0 is the permittivity of air, Nd is the donor density, E is the applied potential and EFB is the flatband potential. Thus, X-intercept of the linear region of the MottâSchottky plot (1/C2 vs. E) provides the EFB of the semiconductor electrode. Accordingly, we examined the behavior of the bulk capacitance of Fe2O3 and Ir-Pi deposited Fe2O3 electrodes using electrochemical impedance spectroscopy at 10 kHz in the potential range 0.60â1.1 V. The resulting MottâSchottky plot is shown in Fig. 6b. In the case of Î±-Fe2O3 electrode, EFB value is about 0.59 V (Fig. 6b(i)). This is in agreement with literature reports on EFB of Î±-Fe2O3.27 Similarly, Fig. 6b(ii) shows the MâS plot of Î±-Fe2O3 electrode after Ir-Pi deposition. Only a negligible change in EFB to 0.61 V is noticed in the presence of Ir-Pi. Since EFB is an approximate measure of conduction band position in n-type semiconductor, these studies rule out any drastic shift in the conduction band of Fe2O3 by Ir-Pi deposition.

In order to confirm the electrochemical deposition of Ir-Pi on Î±-Fe2O3 substrate, cyclic voltammetry experiments were carried out. 3.8. Effect of Ir-Pi deposition on PEC activity

To study the effect of Ir-Pi deposition on Î±-Fe2O3 for oxygen evolution reaction (OER) under photoelectrochemical condition, Ir-Pi was deposited onto Î±-Fe2O3 electrode from 0.5 mM IrCl3 + 0.1 M phosphate solution (pH 7.0) at 1.0 V vs. SCE for 25 min. Then, the linear sweep voltammograms were recorded for both Fe2O3 and Ir-PiâFe2O3 at 50 mV sâ1 in 0.1 M neutral phosphate electrolyte under front side illuminations. Results are shown in Fig. 9a. In the case of bare Fe2O3, only negligibly small current flows in dark till potential reaches 1.80 V, indicating the absence of any electrochemical reaction at this potential range. However, current increases sharply above 1.80 V due to electrochemical OER (Fig. 9a(i)). Upon illumination of Fe2O3 electrode, current starts flowing at potential close to 1.0 V and it reaches 62 Î¼A cmâ2 at 1.23 V (Fig. 9a(ii)). The photocurrent onset is around 400 mV higher than the experimentally determined flatband potential of Fe2O3, suggesting a high overpotential for PEC oxygen evolution on as-prepared Fe2O3 electrode. On the other hand, the onset potential is as low as 0.65 V in the case of Ir-PiâFe2O3 (Fig. 9a(iv)), which is 350 mV lower than that on bare Fe2O3 electrode under identical conditions. Similar to the present study, IrO2 nanoparticles loaded Fe2O3 was reported for photoelectrochemical water oxidation.28 Around 200 mV cathodic shift in the photocurrent onset was observed after IrO2 deposition. Similarly, photoelectrodeposited Co-Pi caused 170 mV improvement29 whereas FeOOH cocatalyst shifted the onset potential by 120 mV.30 It is also reported that photo or electrochemical deposition of Ni-oxides or Co-oxides on hematite could lower the required bias voltage by 100â300 mV.31 Thus, 350 mV cathodic shift in OER onset potential observed for Ir-Pi deposited Fe2O3 in the present case is superior to the literature reports on similar studies using Co-Pi, IrO2, FeOOH and Co/Ni-oxide catalysts. In addition, a high current density of 180 Î¼A cmâ2 is observed at 1.23 V for Ir-Pi loaded Fe2O3 which is 2.9 times higher than the current observed for Fe2O3 electrode alone. Under dark also, onset potential of electrochemical OER is reduced to 1.50 V on Ir-PiâFe2O3 from 1.80 V observed for Fe2O3 without Ir-Pi (Fig. 9a(i) and (iii)). This is anticipated owing to high electrochemical activity of Ir-Pi catalyst towards electrochemical OER. Also, applied bias photon-to-current efficiency (ABPE) calculated from LSV curve indicates a maximum value of 0.027% at 0.90 V for Ir-PiâFe2O3 whereas it is 10 times less (0.0027%) for bare Fe2O3 at 1.10 V (Fig. S3â ). The evolved gas was detected and confirmed as oxygen using Pt electrode sensor (Fig. S4â ). These results clearly confirm the electrochemical deposition of Ir-Pi and its positive role towards both electrochemical and photoelectrochemical OER in neutral electrolytes. 3.10. Proposed mechanism

Bandgap measurements using diffuse reflectance spectroscopy suggest only a slight shift in the Eg of Fe2O3 after Ir-Pi loading (Fig. 6a). Similarly, MottâSchottky plots indicate almost no variation in the conduction band position of Ir-PiâFe2O3 compared to Fe2O3 (Fig. 6b). These studies clearly exclude any modification in the optical properties of Fe2O3 by Ir-Pi as a reason for the enhancement in PEC performance. Hence, it is proposed that the Ir-Pi deposit acts as a cocatalyst, and the enhancement in photocurrent is due to fast kinetics of OER at Ir-PiâFe2O3 electrode. A similar behavior was also reported for Ir32 and Co33 based catalysts deposited hematite photoanodes. The proposed mechanism of water oxidation reaction on Ir-PiâFe2O3 hybrid photoelectrode is illustrated in Scheme 1. In general, illumination of Fe2O3 produces electronâhole pairs. Electrons in the conduction band are transported to external circuit under applied bias. Although, the photogenerated holes in the valence bad of Fe2O3 can directly oxidize water, bare Fe2O3 has poor catalytic activity towards OER. Hence, in the absence Ir-Pi, most of the holes recombine with electrons without causing any reaction. Instead, in the presence Ir-Pi, Ir(IV/V) centers in the catalyst layer rapidly accept the holes before recombining with electrons to form Ir(V/VI) unit and thus the charge carrier separation is improved. Thermodynamically, the oxidation potential of Ir(V/VI) is higher enough for the oxidation of water. Therefore, a high photocurrent due to an immediate evolution of oxygen gas is observed for Ir-PiâFe2O3. It is proposed in the OER mechanism on Co-Pi that the catalyst undergoes a reversible one electron, one proton equilibrium step prior to a rate limiting oxygen evolution step.34 In the present case, the equilibrium redox process corresponds to the oxidation of Ir(IV/V) mixed valence cluster to Ir(V/VI) unit along with liberation of a H+,

4. Conclusions

Photoelectrochemical water oxidation in neutral phosphate electrolyte was studied using Î±-Fe2O3 as a photoanode and Ir-Pi as a cocatalyst. Electrochemical deposition of Fe film by the reduction of Fe2+ ions and its subsequent calcination above 400 Â°C in air resulted in the formation Î±-Fe2O3 film. Under the optimized conditions, the as-prepared Î±-Fe2O3 provided 62 Î¼A cmâ2 for front side illumination and 24 Î¼A cmâ2 for back side illumination, at 1.23 V vs. RHE in 0.1 M neutral phosphate solution. After 25 min of Ir-Pi deposition at 1.0 V vs. SCE from 0.5 mM IrCl3 + 0.1 M phosphate solution (pH 7.0), there was an increase in the photocurrent density at 1.23 V vs. RHE by 2.9 and 4.8 times, respectively for front and back side illuminated electrodes. The results of these studies indicate the favorable role of Ir-Pi as a cocatalyst for solar assisted water oxidation process in neutral solutions.
