Abstract

Because of its unmatched resource potential, solar energy utilization currently is one of the hottest research areas. Much effort has been devoted to developing advanced materials for converting solar energy into electricity, solar fuels, active chemicals, or heat. Among them, TiO2 nanomaterials have attracted much attention due to their unique properties such as low cost, nontoxicity, good stability and excellent optical and electrical properties. Great progress has been made, but research opportunities are still present for creating new nanostructured TiO2 materials. Coreâ€“shell structured nanomaterials are of great interest as they provide a platform to integrate multiple components into a functional system, showing improved or new physical and chemical properties, which are unavailable from the isolated components. Consequently, significant effort is underway to design, fabricate and evaluate coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials for solar energy utilization to overcome the remaining challenges, for example, insufficient light absorption and low quantum efficiency. This review strives to provide a comprehensive overview of major advances in the synthesis of coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials for solar energy utilization. This review starts from the general protocols to construct coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials, and then discusses their applications in photocatalysis, water splitting, photocatalytic CO2 reduction, solar cells and photothermal conversion. Finally, we conclude with an outlook section to offer some insights on the future directions and prospects of coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials and solar energy conversion.

1. Introduction

Titanium dioxide (TiO2) is one of the most widely investigated metal oxides in the past few decades because of its outstanding features such as low cost, environmental benignity, plentiful polymorphs, good stability, and excellent electronic and optical properties.1â€“5 The discovery of photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting on a TiO2 electrode by Fujishima and Honda has sparked the exponential growth of scientific research on TiO2 materials,6 which greatly extends their applications from conventional areas (i.e., pigments, cosmetics, toothpaste, and paint) to recent promising fields including photocatalysis,7â€“17 water splitting,18â€“21 dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs),22â€“28 lithium-ion batteries (LIBs),29â€“33 catalyst supports,34â€“37 sensors,38 biomedicines39â€“41 and recently emerging perovskite solar cells.42,43 Beyond all doubt, these functional applications cover the major leading ways for overcoming the challenges of energy and environmental problems facing humanity today. However, their performances are greatly limited by the properties of the TiO2 materials themselves. One limitation is the wide band gap, making TiO2 only sensitive to ultraviolet (UV) light, which just occupies less than 5% of the solar spectrum. Another key issue is the rapid electronâ€“hole recombination and relatively poor charge transport property, which results in ultralow quantum efficiencies. To overcome these intrinsic drawbacks, various strategies have been developed to modify TiO2 materials. For example, doping with different elements,44â€“48 coupling with a visible-light sensitizer,49,50 surface hydrogenation51 and plasmonâ€“exciton integration52 have been extensively investigated and applied to broaden the active spectrum. The separation and transport of charges could be improved by methods such as engineering the electronic structures, reducing the defects and traps and forming heterojunctions.53,54

Besides, it is greatly desired to maximize the specific surface areas and control surface chemistry,55,56 which can promote the interactions between molecules/ions and TiO2 in nearly all the functional applications listed above.

Recently, coreâ€“shell structured nanomaterials have attracted a great deal of attention as they represent the integration of individual components into a functional system, showing improved physical and chemical properties (e.g., stability, non-toxicity, dispersibility, multi-functionality), which are unavailable from the isolated components.57â€“65 More importantly, the active interfaces between individual components within a coreâ€“shell nanoparticle might give rise to outstanding synergistic functions and new properties. This makes them attractive in a variety of applications, such as catalysis, adsorption, separation, energy storage and conversion, drug delivery and optics.66â€“71 For TiO2 nanomaterials, this coreâ€“shell structured design would provide a promising way to overcome their disadvantages, thus resulting in improved performances.72â€“74 Compared to sole TiO2 material, coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 composites show tunable optical and electrical properties, even new functions, which are originated from the unique coreâ€“shell structures. For example, the introduction of conductive cores would greatly facilitate the transport of electrons in the TiO2 shells. Integrating with visible-light sensitizers can increase the solar light absorption. Forming heterojunctions would tremendously enhance the separation of photogenerated charges. The incorporation of magnetite nanoparticles enables TiO2 with magnetic separation properties. This interesting concept has been demonstrated in some previous reviews, but lacking a systematic overview and understanding.

Recently, a number of excellent reviews have appeared covering the synthesis, structures, properties, and applications of various TiO2 nanomaterials, such as nanoparticles,75â€“77 one-dimensional (1D) nanowires,78,79 2D nanosheets,80,81 and mesoporous materials.56,82 This review aims to provide a comprehensive overview of major advances in coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials. Based on the geometry, coreâ€“shell structures typically comprise four categories: coreâ€“shell structure (core@shell), hollow structure (void@shell), yolkâ€“shell structure (core@void@shell), and Janus structure (eccentric core@shell). Here, we mainly focus on the core@TiO2 shell type structures, which are the basis of the other three, attempting to give a snapshot on the state-of-the-art research status of this particular type of TiO2 nanomaterial. First, we summarize the general pathways, corresponding mechanisms and key factors for controllable synthesis of coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials. Second, we comprehensively review the applications of coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials in chemistry and energy transition, including: photocatalysis, water splitting, photocatalytic CO2 reduction, solar cells and photothermal conversion. Overall, the structures, interfaces, properties and related performances are discussed in detail. Finally, we present a brief conclusion and some perspectives on the future developments of this area.

2.1. Solâ€“gel process strategies

The solâ€“gel process is a versatile and popular solution approach for preparation of advanced materials, including ceramics, oxides, films and organicâ€“inorganic hybrids.83â€“85 Through thoughtful choice of synthesis parameters (such as capping agents, precursors, solvents and catalysts), it is possible to fabricate well-defined coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials.
2.1.1. Capping agents. Liz-MarzÃ¡n and co-workers first reported the solâ€“gel synthesis of Ag@TiO2 coreâ€“shell nanoparticles by refluxing silver nitrate with titanium tetrabutoxide (TBOT) in a mixture of ethanol and dimethylformamide (DMF) under the assistance of acetylacetonate.86 DMF as a solvent can first reduce silver ions, yielding stable Ag nanoparticles and a small amount of H2O, which in situ hydrolyzes TBOT, giving rise to the formation of coreâ€“shell structures. The acetylacetonate works as a capping agent, which helps to slow down the hydrolysis reaction of TBOT, making a uniform coating possible.87 Later, Sakai et al. showed the synthesis of uniform Ag@TiO2 coreâ€“shell nanoparticles by separate processes.88 The Ag nanoparticles were synthesized by using cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) as a protective agent, which triggers the growth of an amorphous TiO2 layer with a thickness of 5â€“10 nm. CTAB facilitates preferential heterogeneous nucleation of TiO2, which is critical for the formation of uniform coreâ€“shell nanoparticles. By using pre-formed Au nanoparticles as a core, Liu et al. demonstrated the synthesis of Au@TiO2 coreâ€“shell nanoparticles,89 in which the surface of Au nanoparticles was modified with a thin layer of (3-mercaptopropyl)triethoxysilane (MPTS) that can promote the coating of TiO2 shells due to the affinity of Siâ€“OH groups toward amorphous TiO2 networks. These results indicate that controllable heterogeneous nucleation of TiO2 on core surface is very important for uniform coreâ€“shell structures.

Holgado et al. have directly revealed the uniform coating of TiO2 on colloidal SiO2 spheres using titanium ethoxide (TEOT) as a precursor.90 In this work, hydroxypropyl cellulose (HPC) was used as a capping agent because of its good repulsive steric force, which is beneficial for the uniform growth of TiO2 shells in aqueous solution without aggregation. Kim et al. monitored and studied a detailed TiO2 coating process,91 where monodispersed SiO2 nanoparticles with a mean size of 161 nm were used as cores. As the reaction time increased, the particle size gradually grew to 186, 218, 257 and 370 nm, respectively. Combined with Nielsen's chronomal analysis and Overbeek's theory, coating of the TiO2 shells on SiO2 is presumed to be a surface reaction, in which the rate determining step was a first-order polynuclear-layer growth. Furthermore, Yin and co-workers have extended this method to synthesize uniform sandwich-like Fe3O4@SiO2@TiO2 coreâ€“shell nanoparticles.92 The presence of a SiO2 interlayer and HPC greatly facilitates the uniform coating of TiO2 without aggregation. The thickness of TiO2 shells can be controlled from 12.6 to 14.7 nm by adjusting the total amount of TBOT. Interestingly, well-defined superparamagnetic 1D Fe3O4@SiO2@TiO2 peapod-like nanostructures can be obtained through magnetic stirring.93 The formation process involves chaining the Fe3O4@SiO2 cores first under a magnetic field and subsequent fixing the chain structure during the TiO2 coating. The number of Fe3O4@SiO2 cores arranged linearly in the chains can be precisely controlled by tuning the amount of titanium precursors or magnetic stirring rate.

As an alternative, Lutensol ON50, which is a type of nonionic surfactant (alkyl polyethylene glycol ether, RO(CH2CH2O)5H), has also been applied as a capping agent for TiO2 coating. The first work was presented by Imhof and co-workers.94 During the coating process, the hydrophilic part of Lutensol is likely to interact with the core surface while the hydrophobic part extends into the medium, thus providing additional steric stabilization for uniform growth of TiO2 shells.95,96 The resultant coreâ€“shell nanoparticles are monodispersed in size and have a very smooth surface (Fig. 2a). This method offers flexibility of tuning the thickness of TiO2 shells from 10 to 250 nm by changing the number of cores and amount of precursors. The authors tried to understand the TiO2 shell growth process by analyzing the competitive coating in a dispersion of polystyrene (PS) spheres with bimodal size distributions (340 and 670 nm, 1:1). Imhof et al. have also explored poly(vinylpyrrolidone) (PVP) as a capping agent to uniformly coat colloidal PS spheres with a smooth TiO2 layer.102 The positive charges on the surface of PS spheres can enable the negatively-charged titanium oligomers to rapidly deposit on them at neutral pH,103 however, the PS spheres are unstable during the charge reaction, which tend to result in significant aggregation. The addition of PVP can greatly improve the stability and dispersivity of PS spheres during the charge reversal process, thus making the uniform coating possible. The resultant TiO2 shell thicknesses can be varied from just a few nanometers to âˆ¼50 nm. However, if too much PVP is added, the effect of steric blocking is higher than that of the electrostatic attraction in some domains and no TiO2 nanoparticles are formed on the core surfaces, which would cause the formation of separated TiO2 aggregates in the solution.104

2.1.2. TiO2 precursors. As claimed above, it is exceptionally difficult to uniformly coat nanoparticles with TiO2 shells because in titanium alkoxides, titanium atoms are highly electrophilic and can easily react with nucleophiles like water. This leads to their high sensitivity to moisture, thus resulting in fast homogeneous nucleation, and readily causing the aggregation of cores due to the energy change in the system.

Recently, Liu et al. have tried to use titanium diisopropoxide bis (acetylacetonate) (TDA) as a precursor for coating TiO2 on the helical structures of amino acid-derived amphiphile fibers.105 It was found that TDA can slowly hydrolyze in the pH range of 3â€“8, thus the nucleation and growth of TiO2 shells are well controllable. Additionally, the interactions between lipid helical fibers and TiO2 precursors through the titaniumâ€“carboxylic acid (Ti-COOH) coordination promote the formation of lipid@TiO2 helical fibers. After calcination, the organic lipid can be subsequently removed, and the amorphous TiO2 be transformed to highly crystallized anatase, leading to the formation of chiral TiO2 fibers. Later, Su et al. used this strategy for preparing Fe3O4@TiO2 coreâ€“shell nanoparticles with controllable shell thicknesses.106

Lee et al. have employed titanium bis(ammonium lactato)dihydroxide (TALH) as a precursor for the synethsis of coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanoparticles.107 They found that the TALH is relatively stable in water, which eliminates the need for an alcohol-based solution and an inert atmosphere for the controllable hydrolysis and condensation. In addition, the hydrolyzed TALH can be directly converted into anatase upon heating around 90â€“100 Â°C.108,109 Strohm et al. have demonstrated that the deposition of TiO2 from hydrolyzed TALH is very sensitive to the nature of the core surface.110 With polyelectrolyte-modified PS spheres as an example, strong interactions with the coating solution lead to uniform growth of TiO2 shells at a slow rate without the formation of homogeneous TiO2 precipitates, whereas a poor interaction or a low nucleation density results in the preferred growth of patchy deposition with isolated nanoparticles.110

Yin and co-workers have disclosed that a titanium-glycolate chelated complex formed by reacting titanium alkoxide and ethylene glycol is an excellent precursor for TiO2 coating because of its superior stability to water.111 In aqueous solution, this precursor can undergo a more controlled reaction with acetone as a catalyst.112 As a result, a uniform titanium-glycolate layer on several materials can be obtained including gold and silver nanoparticles, gold nanorods, silver nanoplates, SiO2 and polymer microspheres. Moreover, the thickness of the titanium-glycolate shells can be facilely tuned from a few nanometers to âˆ¼40 nm through sequential coatings. Due to its simplicity and generality, this method is especially promising for the synthesis of metal@TiO2 coreâ€“shell structured nanomaterials with sizes less than 50 nm. All these works suggest that rational control of the hydrolysis and condensation of TiO2 precursors is another key issue for uniform coating, which can be realized by designing the molecular structures of the precursors.

Recently, Sen and co-workers have demonstrated a new and versatile synthetic approach to uniform metal@TiO2 coreâ€“shell nanomaterials based on the oxidative conversion of Ti3+ ions to hydrous TiO2 through an electrochemical process, which occurs uniquely on the nanostructured metallic surfaces (Fig. 3).113 It has been demonstrated that Pt, Au and Ni are active for catalyzing TiO2 growth due to the known lower overpotential for H+ reduction to hydrogen on their surfaces. By varying the morphology of core metal nanostructures as well as by controlling selective TiO2 growth, segmented metalâ€“TiO2 nanowires, metal@TiO2 coreâ€“shell nano/microparticles and nanotubes were fabricated. Additionally, TiO2 segment length or shell thickness can be well tuned by varying the heating, electricity, Ti3+ concentration and reaction time.114 Since the deposition occurs only on the surfaces of nanostructured Pt, Au and Ni surfaces, the homogeneous nucleation and growth of TiO2 in solution can be avoided, which is a general problem possibly associated with traditional solâ€“gel coating methods.

2.1.3. Solvents and catalysts. In solâ€“gel chemistry, the solvents have a key effect on solubility, stability and reaction rates. It was found that the hydrolysis and condensation reactions are much dependent on several competing factors that are not limited to the viscosity, degree of solvation, dielectric constants, boiling points, surface tension, and steric factors of the solvent media. Generally, a water and ethanol mixture is widely used for solâ€“gel reactions, however, this is not suitable for precise control of TiO2 coating. Recently, Koodali and co-workers have demonstrated that polar aprotic solvents such as DMF and CH3CN can react with M(OR)x groups to form solvate complexes, which limits the attack of the nucleophile reaction and results in longer gelation time. In addition, these solvents characteristically lack protons for H-bonding to the hydroxyl groups and this has an effect of reducing the electrophilicity on the Ti atoms, rendering the metal atoms less susceptible to nucleophilic attack, and thus slowing down hydrolysis as a result.115â€“121 Therefore, choosing the appropriate co-solvent is important for uniform coating. For example, well-defined SiO2@TiO2 coreâ€“shell microspheres can be obtained by using carboxyl-modified SiO2 spheres as a core and an ethanol/acetonitrile mixture as the solvent.122 In this case, the acetonitrile promotes the solubility and stability of TBOT and restricts its hydrolysis, resulting in better control for uniform deposition of TiO2 without the need of capping agents or special precursors. The shell thickness can be facilely tuned from 12 to 100 nm by changing the concentration of TBOT.123

Generally, the hydrolysis kinetics of TBOT is dominated by the amount of catalyst while maintaining other parameters. At a low concentration of ammonia, the reaction rate is very slow, resulting in ultralow concentration of titania oligomers (Cto). Upon slightly increasing the content, the accelerated hydrolysis leads to quickly increasing oligomers in the system, resulting in the heterogeneous nucleation (Che) of TiO2. Although the appearance of nuclei consumes oligomers, the continuing production by hydrolysis and condensation leaves Cto above the saturation concentration (Csat). Therefore, TiO2 nuclei can gradually grow into networks, forming a uniform shell. With further increase in the ammonia concentration, the reaction kinetics is strongly enhanced, leading to a thick shell. While the initial content of ammonia is too high, heterogeneous and homogeneous nucleations occur almost simultaneously, leading to the formation of large and aggregated nanoparticles. 2.3. Layer-by-layer assembly strategies

The layer-by-layer (LbL) assembly strategies have attracted much attention for coating.166â€“169 Caruso et al. first used the LbL technique to deposit uniform TiO2 layers on various colloidal nanoparticles by using TALH as a precursor and poly(dimethyldiallylammonium chloride) (PDADMAC) as the polyelectrolyte.170â€“172 The success for forming uniform coreâ€“shell nanoparticles relies on the use of water-stable TALH, which can be first assembled with PDADMAC via electrostatic interactions at ambient temperature, and then undergo confinement hydrolysis and condensation to produce TiO2 shells. In contrast, titanium alkoxides or chlorides would hydrolyze rapidly in the presence of water, which results in the precipitation of isolated TiO2 in solution and possible aggregation without assembly with the polyelectrolyte. Calcination of the TALH/PDADMAC-coated PS nanoparticles leads to the formation of hollow TiO2 spheres, and the thickness of the shells can be easily tailored from 5 to 50 nm by the number of TALH/PDADMAC layers deposited.170 As an extension, Yu et al. demonstrated the use of porous polycarbonate (PC) membrane supports for the preparation of titania tubes via the LbL deposition of TALH in alternation with oppositely charged poly(ethylenimine) (PEI).173 The composition and crystal phase of the resultant TiO2 tubes can be well controlled by annealing the PEI/TALH multilayer-coated PC membranes at suitable temperatures.

Xiao et al. have prepared rutile@anatase coreâ€“shell nanorod arrays by the LbL assembly of TALH and PDADMAC on rutile nanorods, which can be further used as a scaffold for CdS quantum dot modification, resulting in a ternary coreâ€“shell heterostructure. Owing to the intimate integration and appropriate band alignment between the nano-building blocks, the resultant ternary coreâ€“shell structures exhibit improved photogenerated electronâ€“hole separation and charge collection under simulated solar light irradiation.174

Lee et al. have reported the synthesis of highly photoreactive TiO2-coated polymer fibers through a LbL assembly method with negatively charged colloidal TiO2 nanoparticles as a precursor.175 In addition, instead of traditional linear cationic polyelectrolytes, such as PDADMAC and PEI, positively charged polyhedral oligosil-sesquioxane (POSS) molecules were introduced for the LbL TiO2 coating.175 The resultant TiO2-coated fibers display excellent photocatalytic properties in the degradation of allyl alcohol under UV illumination.176

Later on, Wang and Zeng have demonstrated the use of polyaniline (PAN) to mediate the LbL assembly for a uniform TiO2 layer with oleate-protected anatase nanoparticles as the precursor.177 In this case, the oil-soluble TiO2 nanoparticles play the vital role, which can first assemble on the surface of SiO2 spheres in toluene, and act as the starting sites for the polymerization of aniline, resulting in the formation of uniform SiO2@TiO2@PAN spheres. Then, an outer anatase shell can be deposited by direct hydrolysis of TiF4 in ethanol solution, which prefers to take place on both the outer and inner surfaces of the permeable PAN shells, leading to the formation of TiO2 shells. The authors showed that the present approach gives a high synthetic flexibility for tailor-making complex shelled nanostructures with different architectural designs.

Afterwards, a special case of LbL modification has also been shown to work for uniform deposition of TiO2 shells with the assistance of PVP molecules.178 For example, Au nanoparticles can be coated by a layer of hydrophobic ligands first, followed by modifying with a layer of PVP due to its affinity. Then, TiF4 slowly hydrolyzes in ethanol solution, enabling the successful encapsulation on Au@PVP. The strong interactions between the Auâ€“ligand, ligandâ€“PVP, and PVPâ€“TiO2 layers result in the apparent Auâ€“TiO2 interfacial energy, when considered as a whole, being greatly reduced. With these understandings, they developed a general method for coating oxides (e.g., TiO2, ZnO, Fe3O4, MnO, Co2O3, Eu2O3, and so on) on a variety of cores, including metals, oxides, polymer nanoparticles, and functionalized carbon materials.

Very recently, Dinh has explored uniform titanate nanodisks (TNDs) as a precursor for preparing coreâ€“shell SiO2@TND-PEI nanospheres through the LbL technique,179,180 which could then self-assemble into a 3D opal structure of Au/TiO2 hollow nanospheres, as illustrated in Fig. 9A. Due to the hydrophilic surface of the SiO2 spheres, PEI is attracted through electrostatic interaction and makes the surface charge positive. Then, TNDs with negative surface charge can be deposited by the LbL technique via electrostatic interaction with PEI, followed by loading Au precursors (HAuCl4) to produce TND-PEI-AuCl4âˆ’/SiO2 due to the strong interaction between AuCl4âˆ’ and the amine groups in PEI. Assembly of TNDâ€“PEIâ€“AuCl4âˆ’/SiO2 is performed by centrifugation at a low speed, followed by calcination to obtain the Au/TiO2/SiO2 3D opal structures. The LbL technique combined with the uniform size of TNDs allows the formation of a homogeneous and tunable shell thickness, which is crucial for the formation of 3D opal structures, showing remarkable activity in the decomposition of isopropanol compared to the same photocatalyst without 3D assembly.

2.4. Atomic layer deposition methods

Atomic layer deposition (ALD) is one of the most promising techniques for the deposition of conformal and homogenous thin films.181â€“183

The application of ALD to TiO2 coating has been well demonstrated on various substrates with different morphologies such as films,184,185 nanoparticles,186,187 porous membranes,188,189 nanowires,189,190 nanotubes191â€“193 and 3D nanostructures.194â€“202 For example, the ZnO@TiO2 coreâ€“shell structured nanorod arrays can be prepared by using ZnO nanorod arrays and TiCl4 as the core and precursor, respectively.203 The TiO2 shell thickness increases at a rate of 0.9 Ã… per ALD cycle. Interestingly, shells thinner than 4 nm are conformal, amorphous, and smooth, while thicker shells are polycrystalline anatase and fairly rough due to variations in grain size. Alibabaei et al. have prepared mesoscopic coreâ€“shell films consisting of thin nanometer TiO2 shells deposited on nano ITO (tin-doped indium oxide) by ALD for application in DSSC photoanodes.187 The TiO2 layers are homogeneous over a range of thicknesses from 0.6 to 3.6 nm obtained by varying the number of ALD cycles from 10 to 60. At thicknesses â‰¥3 nm, electron trapping in the TiO2 shells occurs, resulting in an increase of back electron transfer decreasing device efficiencies. ALD is also explored for coating TiO2 on various metal surfaces with varying thicknesses.204â€“206 For example, Ag nanoparticles can be well protected by a pinhole-free thin-layer of ALD-TiO2 from oxidation, which deliver plasmon-enhanced photocurrents in the DSSCs.204,205

However, these studies generally reported homogeneous amorphous TiO2 films at a low temperature and rough polycrystalline anatase layers at a high temperature, which relies on self-limiting surface reactions of alternating gas precursors with the substrate surface. Taking CNTs as an example, the chemically inert carbon walls restrict the nucleation of TiO2 coating, leading to poor coverage. It is generally accepted that nucleation and growth initiate preferably at defect sites of the CNTs, where oxygen functional groups can chemisorb.207 Therefore, most of the studies about ALD-TiO2 coated CNTs used functionalized CNTs.208 Recently, Utke and co-workers have developed a new temperature-programmed ALD strategy to obtain complete TiO2 coating on non-functionalized CNTs.209 Typically, starting the ALD deposition at low temperature enables the complete and continuous coverage of CNTs with an amorphous ultrathin film. Subsequently, increasing the temperature triggers a conformal anatase coating on the amorphous layer. In contrast, starting with the deposition at high temperature and then decreasing results in sporadic crystal grains covered by an amorphous TiO2 layer. More recently, they have realized highly conformal thin layer (âˆ¼10 nm) and monocrystalline anatase on non-functionalized CNTs.210 The strategy adopts the above deposition of amorphous TiO2 for uniform coreâ€“shell structures, then combining with a post-annealing at 450 Â°C under vacuum results in a complete phase transformation to crystalline anatase. It might be because the amorphous TiO2 possesses some short-range order, for example, through Tiâ€“O octahedrons, which transform to a long-range order by a sort of crystallization front moving along the film during annealing, hence leading to the monocrystallinity at a half-micron scale in relation to the thickness of âˆ¼10 nm only.

Hu and co-workers have developed a liquid-phase ALD process for conformal TiO2 coating.211,212 In a typical deposition cycle, the substrates were immersed in TIPO/toluene solution for the chemisorption reaction, followed by immediate sonication in isopropanol to remove unreacted species. Then, they were immersed in water/ethanol solution to hydrolyze. The above steps can be repeated to obtain the desired thickness, which circumvents the need for vacuum conditions critical in traditional gas-phase ALD.

Chen et al. demonstrated a novel molecular layer deposition (MLD) process for N-doped TiO2 coating.213 The process is based on a four-step reaction sequence. Firstly, TiCl4 reacts with hydroxy groups on the substrate surface to form â€“TiClx species. Next, the hydroxy end of the ethanolamine reacts preferentially with the â€“TiClx species resulting in a â€“OCH2CH2NH2 terminated surface, which subsequently anchors the malonyl chloride in the following step to form a new surface species terminated by acyl chloride. That facilitates the reaction with the amino end of another ethanolamine molecule to reform hydroxyl groups on the surface. Finally, N-doped TiO2 can be obtained after annealing in a H2/Ar atmosphere. The perfectly replicated helical morphology of the initial templates highlights the advantage of MLD for the coating of high-curvature surfaces. The resultant TiO2 shell thickness can be facilely tuned by the desired number of the four steps.

3. Solar energy utilization

3.1. Photocatalysis

TiO2 has been widely used as a photocatalyst. Fundamentally, upon absorption of photons with energy larger than the band gap of TiO2, electrons are excited from the valence band to conduction band, creating electronâ€“hole pairs. These charge carriers migrate to the surface, where they are then available to undergo redox reactions with adsorbed chemicals. This is followed by the formation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) at the solid/liquid interface.
3.1.1. Surface hydrogenation-driven photocatalysis. Indeed, bandgap engineering is a crucial step for optimizing light harvesting capability. Persistent efforts involving controlled metal or nonmetal impurities to generate donor or acceptor states in the band gap and self-doping that produces Ti3+ species have been well demonstrated.47,237,238

Recently, the hydrogenation into coreâ€“shell structured black TiO2 has been demonstrated to dramatically enhance light absorption in visible and infrared regions, thus resulting in high photocatalytic efficiency. For example, Mao and co-workers have showed that photodegradation of methylene blue (MB) is complete after 8 min for black TiO2 nanocrystals, whereas the white TiO2 nanocrystals under the same testing conditions took nearly 1 h.214 Similar improvement is also observed for photocatalytic decomposition of phenol. Huang and co-workers described that aluminothermic-reduced black TiO2 can absorb âˆ¼65% of the total solar energy by improving visible and infrared absorptions, superior to pristine TiO2 (âˆ¼5%).233 When used as a photocatalyst under UV light irradiation, the coreâ€“shell structured black TiO2 exhibits a complete decomposition of methyl orange (MO) (breaking the NN bonding and the benzene ring). As a reference, an incomplete decomposition of MO (breaking the NN bonding) is obtained based on pristine TiO2. Visible-light catalytic activity was further investigated, and it was found that the coreâ€“shell structured black TiO2 degrades 52% of MO after 6 h, whereas pristine TiO2 only 4%. By doping with nonmetal X (X = H, N, S, I) into the disordered shell of black TiO2 (TiO2@TiO2âˆ’x), the solar energy absorption could be further improved from 65% up to 85%.236 Surprisingly, the resultant TiO2@TiO2âˆ’xXx material displays higher photodegradation activity than TiO2@TiO2âˆ’x and TiO2 under both UV and visible-light irradiations due to the optimized effect of nonmetal X dopants on oxygen vacancies.

Recently, Moon et al. have fabricated a rGO@TiO2 hybrid photocatalyst for solar production of H2O2, which exhibits a higher activity than that of bare TiO2, Ag/TiO2, Au/TiO2, or Pt/TiO2. Despite the great progress, the synergistic effect between different phases still remains unclear. Liu and co-workers have systematically demonstrated that in nanostructured TiO2 electrodes, the internal movement of electrons is faster inside anatase building blocks, while the rutile counterparts exhibit more efficient intergrain charge transfer. As a result, surface rutilization of anatase nanoparticles into coreâ€“shell structures can not only improve the charge mobility by facilitating the intergrain charge transfer between the adjacent building blocks, but also minimize the internal energy consumption and protect electrons form recombination, although the conduction band edge of rutile TiO2 is slightly higher than that of the anatase polymorph.296,297 Cao et al. have clearly figured out the charge transfer direction across the phase junction through design and synthesis of two types of TiO2 heterojunctions: anatase films/rutile nanorods and rutile nanorods/anatase films. Rutile nanowires on anatase film demonstrated enhanced photocurrent density and onset potential, whereas strong negative performance was obtained from anatase film on rutile nanowire structures.298 That is because photogenerated electrons tend to transfer from the rutile nanorods to anatase films, and holes are eager to migrate from anatase to the rutile surface, which provides an important guide for future heterojunction-based photoelectrode design.299

In addition to phase junctions, the coreâ€“shell semiconductor@TiO2 nanostructures have also been studied and demonstrated as an effective way to improve the photocatalytic performance with both enhanced light absorption and charge separation efficiency.300 Various semiconductors (including: ZnO, Fe2O3, CdS, CuOx, SnO2, etc.) have been extensively explored to form a heterojunction with TiO2 for boosting the photocatalytic performance.301â€“308 For example, 1D CdS@TiO2 coreâ€“shell nanocomposites have been synthesized via a two-step solvothermal method. 3.2.1. Photocatalytic hydrogen evolution. In comparison to the photo-oxidation half-reaction, water splitting for hydrogen generation is a more challenging process, and continues to be a dream for clean and sustainable energy sources. However, implementations of TiO2 materials in water splitting still remain limited because of its wide band gap and low carrier densities. Much effort has been devoted to solve this problem.120,121,322â€“324 Zheng et al. have evaluated the effect of hydrogen treatment on the photocatalytic activity of anatase nanowires.222 They found that the hydrogen production rate of hydrogenated TiO2 nanowires (2.15 mmol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1) is 3.2 and 3.8 times higher than those of air-treated TiO2 and commercial P25, respectively. Huang and co-workers have showed that the TiO2@TiO2âˆ’xHx coreâ€“shell nanoparticles steadily produce hydrogen up to 8.2 mmol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1, which is much larger than that of pristine TiO2 (0.61 mmol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1).225 The enhancement in the visible-light absorption of coreâ€“shell structured black TiO2 may be attributed to two factors: surface disorder and the formation of oxygen vacancies.214,217,325 Interestingly, it was found that the dramatic improvement in visible-light absorbance of black TiO2 is not a main contributor to its enhanced photoactivity.214,217,222 As shown by Chen et al. when full solar radiation was filtered so that only visible and infrared light was allowed to reach coreâ€“shell structured black TiO2 with Pt, the rate of H2 production drops from 10 to 0.1 mmol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1.214 Furthermore, electrochemical impedance measurements reveals that black TiO2 increased their donor densities. However, when the concentration of oxygen vacancies is too high, the defects can also act as charge recombination centers and thus cause a negative effect on the photocatalytic performance.

Huang and co-workers have also showed a beneficial effect on the photocatalytic hydrogen evolution of coreâ€“shell structured black TiO2 co-doped with various nonmetal atoms.235,236 They believed that nonmetal atoms can reduce the trapped states from oxygen vacancies, thus improving the visible-light photocatalytic performances. 3.5. Solar cells

Another attractive application is use in solar cells, which directly convert solar energy into electricity. Improving the conversion efficiency is a critical step to enable commercialization of photovoltaic technologies.

3.5.2. Perovskite solar cells. The emergence of perovskite solar cells (PSCs) recently brings a new hope to the solar cell industry due to their incredible improvement of the PCE exceeding 20.0% within several years. The efficiency and stability of PSCs depend strongly on the morphology and type of materials selected as the electron transport layer (ETL) in the device.

Understanding and engineering the interfaces between the ETL and perovskite are crucial to improve the device performance. Various fabrication methods have been adopted to deposit compact TiO2 ETL on the TCO substrates including spin coating, ALD, and chemical bath deposition. Although a similar compact structure can be obtained, the resultant ETL appears in different morphologies with variation of surface roughness, film thickness and crystallinity which in turn varies the device performance. For example, Lu et al. identified that the optimum thickness of ALD-TiO2 as the ETL for highly efficient planar PSCs is âˆ¼10 nm due to high transmittance, low leakage current, and low charge transfer resistance and recombination rate.408 However, solution-based deposition techniques like spin-coating required much thicker compact TiO2 layers (>65 nm) as the ETL for achieving high performance devices.409,410 This is due to the fact that a thinner ETL increases the risk of pinhole formation which subsequently offers a direct contact between perovskite layer and TCO substrate.411 It is known that enhanced electronic coupling and chemical binding between the TiO2 ETL and perovskite interface play the key role in substantially reducing the trap states and recombination.412,413 For example, Cl-rich TiO2 nanocrystals have recently been developed and used as an ETL for planar PSCs by Tan et al. 3.6. Photothermal conversion

In addition to being an abundant and sustainable energy source in the above-mentioned processes, the photothermal effect provides an alternative approach for efficient conversion of solar energy into heat, which is widely used in industrial processes such as solar power generation, chemical separation/purification, as well as seawater desalination.421â€“423
3.6.1. Photothermal desalination. Recently, Huang and co-workers have prepared coreâ€“shell structured black TiO2 with a unique nanocage shape for solar-driven steam generation.424 A self-floating nanocages film delivers a superior light-to-heat conversion rate (âˆ¼1.9 times higher than that of water itself). The popular defect structures (Ti3+) can enhance the light absorption, leading to the above band gap non-radiative relaxation and phonon relaxation of below band gap states. The mesoporous structures resulted from the nanoparticle aggregation and the close contact interfaces facilitate the heat transfer and water vapor permeation.

3.6.2. Photothermal hydrogen production. Recently, Nikitenko et al. have reported a simple and easily scalable synthesis of Ti@TiO2 coreâ€“shell nanoparticles, which exhibit strong photothermal catalytic activities toward HER in the absence of any noble metals.430 The stable Ti@TiO2 nanoparticles with quasi-spherical Ti cores (20â€“80 nm) and 5â€“15 nm defect-free anatase/rutile shells were prepared by the ultrasound and hydrothermal treatment of air-passivated titanium metal nanoparticles in water. In contrast to pristine TiO2, the Ti@TiO2 nanoparticles extend the photo-response from the UV to NIR light region due to the light absorption by a non-plasmonic Ti core, which exhibit the highest H2 production under the joint effect of Vis/NIR light and heat. However, a simple heating of the reaction mixture to 60 Â°C under a dark condition or using air-passivated Ti as the catalyst is insufficient to produce hydrogen. On the other hand, a decrease in the system temperature from 60 to 35 Â°C causes a significant drop in the H2 evolution even under Vis/NIR irradiation. These results suggest that the driving force for H2 production consists of the Ti core inter-band transition excitation followed by the formation of energetic electronâ€“hole pairs through non-radiative Landau damping. Then, electrons and holes are accumulated in the Ti cores and TiO2 shells, respectively. Scavenging of holes by methanol molecules produces the intermediates that are transformed into HCOOH and into H+ ions, which then migrate to TiO2/TiO2 junctions and are reduced to H2 by electrons transferred from the Ti core. This intermediate migration process would be effectively activated by the photothermal energy.
3.6.3. Photothermal therapy. In general, the heat caused by the photothermal nanoparticles is concentrated in an ultra-small space, which creates a localized high-temperature microenvironment around the nanoparticles. That would be useful for various sensitive systems, such as light-triggered local hyperthermia, which is called photothermal therapy (PTT).74 For example, coreâ€“shell structured black TiO2 with dramatically near-infrared absorption was demonstrated as a photothermal agent for cancer therapy to circumvent the obstacle of ultraviolet light excitation.431 Under irradiation with an 808 nm NIR laser at 2 W cmâˆ’2 for 600 s, the temperature of black TiO2 aqueous solution is 66 Â°C with an increase of 44 Â°C, which is much higher than that of pure water. Later, black TiO2@mesoporous SiO2 nanocomposites have been designed for 808 nm NIR triggered thermal imaging and PTT integrated with chemotherapy for killing breast cancer,432 in which the black TiO2 core can not only act as the photothermal therapy agent, but also accelerate the doxorubicin-release in the pores of mesoporous SiO2 shells, thus showing a superior synergistic therapy effect than PTT or chemotherapy alone.433

4. Conclusions and perspectives

Undoubtedly, the development of coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 materials has been regarded as a new research area and will explosively increase in future. Compared with single TiO2 counterparts, the unique coreâ€“shell structures can provide new and improved physical and chemical properties, which produce breakthrough advances in a large number of applications. In this review, we comprehensively summarized recent progress in this research area. First, five general synthetic pathways for the core@TiO2 shell structured nanomaterials are reviewed, including solâ€“gel process strategies, hydro/solvothermal methods, layer-by-layer assembly strategies, atomic layer deposition method and thermal induced reduction strategies, basics about their corresponding principles and the key factors for controllable synthesis are highlighted. Then, the applications of these core@TiO2 shell structures for solar energy conversion have been overviewed in detail based on the structureâ€“performance relationship, including: photocatalysis, photocatalytic water splitting, PEC water splitting, photocatalytic CO2 reduction, solar cells and photothermal conversion.

Future directions in this area have been schematically summarized in Fig. 25. Regarding the synthesis of coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials, the central task is to realize the uniform coating of TiO2 on desired cores. The rational choice of suitable precursors, capping agents, and reaction system can greatly facilitate the coating process. Despite remarkable efforts having been achieved in wet-chemistry to control the hydrolysis and condensation of TiO2 for uniform coating, a simple, robust, and general synthetic method is much in demand toward various cores with different composition, geometry and surface properties. In addition, the real-time and real-space growth mechanisms of TiO2 shells during the solution coating processes are still lacking. A few guidelines have been reported in this review, but systematic studies of how the solâ€“gel chemistry and interfacial chemistry work are much needed for rational design, especially focusing on the basis of chemical science (thermodynamics, kinetics and diffusion principles). Compared to solution-based methods, ALD is an easy and mature process to control the TiO2 coating at the atomic level owing to its self-limiting mechanism, but it is not suitable for producing thick TiO2 shells (time-consuming) and complete coating of particular materials. New ALD processes and reactors should be paid more attention in the coming years. Since there is no single method to work for every situation, exploration of synergistic features and interconnection among different methods will continue to attract much more attention. Moreover, the synthetic method is still facing a challenge in establishing controllable fabrication of TiO2 shells with desired morphology, thickness, phase, crystallinity, specific crystal facet, surface chemistry and more, which are expected to provide the most opportunities to advance this field. It is very important and highly urgent to develop fast and simple methods for large scale synthesis of coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials for practical applications.

Creating ordered mesopores in the TiO2 shells is highly of special interest to further improve their performances in catalysis and energy conversion reactions, which is an important issue in this research area. Although mesoporous TiO2 shells have been obtained through cooperative assembly methods, the resultant pore structures stemming from nanoparticle aggregation are generally disordered because the great challenge remains in controlling the assembly of ordered micelles on nanosized and curved surfaces. A promising breakthrough is based on a single micelle assembly strategy, in which ordered mesoporous TiO2 shells with different structures, thicknesses and pore sizes can be constructed by super-assembly, using single micelle units as building blocks like superlattice structures do. In addition, more work is required to manipulate the interfacial growth of TiO2 shells for novel hierarchical nanostructures, for instance, Janus coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 materials or controllable satellite-like shell structures, which are extremely fascinating for synthetic chemistry and applications.

From the application point of view, it can be seen that coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials have been widely used in solar energy utilization, converting solar energy into active chemicals, H2, hydrocarbon fuels, heat and electricity. Although substantial progress has been obtained by combining TiO2 shells with functional cores or surface/interface engineering, there is still big room for improvement. To realize the final goal of industrializing solar hydrogen production, photocatalysts with an absorption edge range up to 600â€“700 nm together with an AQY of 40â€“60% are necessary to get a STH conversion efficiency of about 10%. Rational design of well-defined coreâ€“shell structures with a light-absorbing core, TiO2 shells and co-catalysts for HER and OER provides potential for achieving this target. Alternatively, surface plasmon resonance should be well developed to promote the solar energy conversion, which is a promising branch of research in coreâ€“shell structured TiO2. Apart from photodegradation and photosplitting, the utilization of coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 with magnetic, improved optical, and new electronic properties for the light-induced selective chemical transformations to produce fine chemicals is a new ground for future photo-derived catalysis. In perovskite solar cells, a high-quality coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 ETL plays a key role and novel nanostructures for enhanced electron extraction and transfer are much desired.

Most importantly, the comprehensive and in-depth insights into the structureâ€“propertyâ€“performance relationships of coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 nanomaterials are much needed in the near future, which hold the keys to developing advanced TiO2 nanomaterials. Strictly related to this point, better understanding and systematic studies of the interface chemistry between cores and TiO2 shells are required, for example to maximize forward charge transfer and utilization, while suppressing backward or side reactions. It is critical for fabricating highly efficient heterojunctions with more complex structures such as tandem junctions with perfect band alignment, full spectrum absorption, or multiple function coupling. It should be well considered and engineered toward system materials in the future. Moreover, controlling the molecular and nanoscale structures of TiO2 shells has been well demonstrated to be an effective way to improve the application performance. For example, doping the TiO2 shell with metal or nonmetal dopants to modify its band gap and band position and developing a porous single-crystal TiO2 shell for charge transfer are dreams of researchers. More efforts should be devoted to this area. Significantly, it is highly expected that coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 will see some breakthroughs in other applications such as gas sensing, catalysis, energy storage and biomedicine.

Overall, the development of coreâ€“shell structured TiO2 provides new opportunities for constructing advanced TiO2 materials with improved performances in a wide range of applications. We believe that, in the future, significant breakthroughs can be expected in this research area from both synthetic, fundamental, and practical viewpoints.
