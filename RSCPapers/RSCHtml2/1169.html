Abstract

Bismuth sulphide nanocrystalâ€“polymer hybrid layers are of interest for various optoelectronic, thermoelectric or sensing applications. In this work, we present a ligand-free in situ route for the formation of Bi2S3 nanorods directly within a polymer matrix. For this purpose, we introduce a novel bismuth xanthate (bismuth(III) O-3,3-dimethylbutan-2-yl dithiocarbonate), which is highly soluble in non-polar organic solvents. The analysis of the crystal structure revealed that the prepared bismuth xanthate crystallises in the monoclinic space group C2/c and forms dimers. The bismuth xanthate can be converted into nanocrystalline Bi2S3 with an orthorhombic crystal structure via a thermally induced solid state reaction at moderate temperatures below 200 Â°C. In combination with the high solubility in non-polar solvents this synthetic route for Bi2S3 is of particular interest for the preparation of Bi2S3â€“polymer nanocomposites as exemplarily investigated on Bi2S3â€“poly(methyl methacrylate) and Bi2S3â€“poly(3-hexylthiophene-2,5-diyl) (P3HT) nanocomposite layers. Atomic force and transmission electron microscopy revealed that Bi2S3 nanorods are dispersed in the polymer matrix. Photoluminescence experiments showed a quenching of the P3HT fluorescence with increasing Bi2S3 content in the hybrid layer.

1. Introduction

Organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials are a highly interesting and versatile class of materials that offer a plethora of applications due to their variety of physical and chemical properties. These can be tailored in a wide range by using different material combinations, morphologies and nanostructures. Of particular interest are nanocrystalâ€“polymer hybrid materials, often called nanocomposites, in which inorganic nanocrystals with specific properties e.g. concerning light emission or light absorption are integrated into a polymer matrix. This allows combining the advantages of easy processing and structural flexibility of polymers, with the optical properties and stability provided by the inorganic material.1 Semiconducting quantum dots offer the unique possibility of tuning their properties by controlling their size and shape.2 Organicâ€“inorganic nanocomposites find applications, for example, in photochromic devices,3 non linear optics (NLO),4 electroluminescent devices,5 hybrid optical sensors6 and also in polymerâ€“nanoparticle hybrid solar cells.7â€“9

Bismuth sulphide (Bi2S3) is a non-toxic semiconductor, consisting of abundant elements, with a direct band gap of 1.3 to 1.7 eV,10 and a high absorption coefficient in the order of 105 cmâˆ’1.11 This makes Bi2S3 also an interesting candidate for a variety of applications, e.g. for polymerâ€“nanoparticle hybrid solar cells. There are already some reports discussing Bi2S3 as an acceptor material in bulk-heterojunction hybrid solar cells;12,13 however, the efficiencies are still relatively low. Furthermore, Bi2S3 nanoparticles and nanocomposites can find numerous applications in thermoelectric devices,14 memory devices,10 photodetectors,15 as electrode materials in lithium-ion batteries,16 as well as in electronic, optoelectronic and gas sensor devices.17â€“20

Several synthetic methods to prepare Bi2S3 nanostructures exist starting from metal salts and a sulphur source21,22 or single source precursors.23â€“25 Thus, an obvious and common way to prepare bismuth sulphideâ€“polymer nanocomposites is to synthesise Bi2S3 nanostructures first and then mix them in a second step with a polymer to obtain the organicâ€“inorganic hybrid nanocomposite. Following this strategy several types of nanoparticle syntheses have been applied so far. A quite common approach is the synthesis of Bi2S3 nanoparticles via a colloidal synthesis using metal salts, a sulphur source and ligands stabilizing the nanoparticles. Wang et al.,5 for example, used Bi(NO3)3 with sodium octanoate and elemental sulphur to form Bi2S3 nanoparticles, which were subsequently mixed with a conjugated polymer.12 In further approaches, the polymer matrix was polymerised in the presence of Bi2S3 nanoparticles,26 or pre-formed nanostructured Bi2S3 layers were coated with a polymer.27

All these approaches are rather tedious and require several steps. In contrast, in this work we discuss a convenient and fast route to prepare metal sulphideâ€“polymer nanocomposites by synthesising Bi2S3 nanocrystals directly within a polymer matrix in the solid state without the necessity of a separate nanoparticle synthesis step. This method is based on metal xanthates, which are highly soluble in a variety of organic solvents depending on the alkyl moiety of the xanthate group. The good solubility makes this route very versatile in terms of selecting the matrix polymers, which are also inherently different in solubility and, even more crucial, it provides the prerequisite for a homogeneous distribution of both materials in the nanocomposite. A second beneficial property of metal xanthates in this regard is that they decompose at low temperatures (100â€“200 Â°C) and the side products of the decomposition are volatile, which leads to tidy layers without impurities.28 Thus, this method is well suited for the preparation of metal sulphide nanostructures in a polymer matrix at low temperatures, which also allows using thermally less stable conjugated polymers as matrix without harming their optoelectronic properties. Metal sulphideâ€“conjugated polymer nanocomposites, prepared via metal xanthates, can be applied as absorber layers in polymerâ€“nanoparticle hybrid solar cells. This has recently been demonstrated for several nanocomposite materials29 containing CdS,30,31 CuInS2 (ref. 28 and 32) or Sb2S3 (ref. 33) nanoparticles and conjugated polymers or small molecules.34

The synthesis of bismuth xanthates with methyl, ethyl, i-propyl, n-butyl, cyclohexyl and benzyl-moieties has been reported in the literature several decades ago.35â€“37 Quite recently, some of these compounds have been applied for the synthesis of Bi2S3 nanowires and nanoparticles.23,25 In this contribution, we introduce a new bismuth xanthate (bismuth(III) O-3,3-dimethylbutan-2-yl dithiocarbonate, BiHexXan), which is highly soluble in non-polar organic solvents due to its asymmetric, branched alkyl side chain. Therefore, this compound is well suited for the preparation of Bi2S3â€“polymer nanocomposite materials. The formation of Bi2S3 from this precursor is investigated in detail and first Bi2S3â€“polymer nanocomposites are prepared and studied.

The morphology of the prepared Bi2S3â€“polymer nanocomposite layers was characterised by atomic force microscopy (AFM). Fig. 5 shows surface images of a Bi2S3â€“PMMA layer as well as of a Bi2S3â€“P3HT layer. The images of the prepared layers reveal that the polymer forms a few 100 nm large domains, which can be clearly seen in the topographic image as well as in the phase contrast image. In the Bi2S3â€“PMMA layers structures with smaller sizes of about 50 nm are also visible, which can be ascribed to the Bi2S3 nanostructures present in the nanocomposite. These smaller structures are not observed in the AFM image of the Bi2S3â€“P3HT hybrid layer, which might be due to surface-energetically preferential diffusion of P3HT-rich phases towards the airâ€“film interface and thus formation of a P3HT-rich cover layer at the surface of the nanocomposite film. Bright field TEM images of the Bi2S3â€“P3HT sample at different magnifications are shown in Fig. 6, which exhibit that nanorods are formed in the conjugated polymer matrix. The Bi2S3 nanorods reveal regular shapes with a diameter of 7â€“22 nm and a length of 40â€“70 nm. The formation of nanorods in the matrix of a conjugated polymer via a ligand-free in situ process in the solid state is a highly interesting result, as it was not possible before to realise nanorod structures via ligand-free in situ processes.29,48 Until now, only spherical particles could be obtained. In particular, nanorods are very desirable, thinking of a possible application of the nanocomposites as absorber layers in hybrid solar cells, because they inherently facilitate charge transport through the layer, while retaining a high polymerâ€“nanoparticle interfacial area.49 Current record power conversion efficiencies of hybrid solar cells of 4.7% are also reached using nanorods of CdSe instead of spherical nanoparticles in combination with the low band gap polymer PCPDTBT.50


Fig. 6 TEM images of Bi2S3 nanorods in a P3HT matrix at different magnifications.

The reason for the formation of Bi2S3 nanorods in this solid state reaction is most presumably the predominant growth of the orthorhombic Bi2S3 nanocrystals along the [001] direction.10,51 Therefore, these elongated nanostructures are quite common in solution grown Bi2S3 nanocrystals and there exist several publications reporting on Bi2S3 nanorods or nanowires yielded by different synthetic approaches.11,12,23,24,52

A further interesting aspect is that no aggregation of the nanorods and thus phase separation between the organic and inorganic phase occurs. The Bi2S3 nanorods are homogeneously distributed over the whole layer. A possible explanation for that could be that the Bi2S3 nanorods are formed directly in the polymer matrix from a homogeneous mixture of bismuth xanthate and polymer. Nevertheless, the nanorods are in close proximity to each other; therefore, continuous pathways should be present in both phases.

To investigate the optical properties of the prepared P3HTâ€“Bi2S3 nanocomposites in more detail, we performed photoluminescence (PL) quenching measurements on P3HTâ€“Bi2S3 nanocomposites with different contents of the inorganic component. The PL of the P3HT is found to be strongly quenched when Bi2S3 is present in the composites. The detailed results of the PL-quenching study are summarised in Fig. 7, which shows the PL spectra of pure P3HT and P3HTâ€“Bi2S3 nanocomposite layers with different polymer:Bi2S3 weight ratios of 1:3, 1:6, 1:9 and 1:12. To obtain reliable results, we increased the thickness of the layers with increasing Bi2S3 content to ensure that the polymer content was equal in each sample. Optical excitation of the pristine P3HT film at 560 nm resulted in the appearance of an emission band at 670 nm. This emission is quenched after introduction of the Bi2S3 nanorods into the polymer film (see Fig. 7A). The PL emission was quenched to 22% of the initial emission intensity at a P3HT:Bi2S3 weight ratio of 1:3 and is further reduced to 6% (P3HT:Bi2S3 = 1:6), to 3% (P3HT:Bi2S3 = 1:9) and to 0.4% (P3HT:Bi2S3 = 1:12) of the original level (see Fig. 7B). It has to be considered that part of the reduction of the PL intensity may stem from absorption of the emitted light by the Bi2S3 nanorods. However, the qualitative quenching of the PL of P3HT by the Bi2S3 nanoparticles in the nanocomposite seems to be quite efficient and is comparable to or even higher than PL quenching efficiencies which were obtained by PL quenching studies on other polymerâ€“nanoparticle hybrid materials used in hybrid photovoltaics.28,53,54


Fig. 7 (A) Photoluminescence quenching in P3HTâ€“Bi2S3 nanocomposites; (B) normalised integrated PL vs. Bi2S3 volume ratio in the nanocomposite.

The photoluminescence quenching study suggests good suitability of the prepared Bi2S3â€“P3HT nanocomposite layers as an absorber material in polymerâ€“nanoparticle hybrid solar cells. Thus, we prepared polymerâ€“nanoparticle hybrid solar cells with Bi2S3â€“P3HT nanocomposite layers as an absorber material in the configuration glass/ITO/PEDOT:PSS/P3HT:Bi2S3/Al with different P3HT:Bi2S3 ratios. However, all prepared solar cells were short-circuited and no diode characteristics could be observed. To prevent short circuits in the solar cells we introduced additional interlayers between electrodes and the absorber layer. Also with this improved geometry, we could not realise working solar cells with the in situ prepared Bi2S3â€“P3HT hybrid layers so far, even though Bi2S3â€“P3HT hybrid solar cells with a power conversion efficiency of 1% were already reported with a similar device architecture using pre-synthesised Bi2S3 nanoparticles.13

4. Conclusions

The novel bismuth xanthate, bismuth(III) O-3,3-dimethylbutan-2-yl dithiocarbonate, is a valuable precursor for the preparation of Bi2S3 at temperatures below 200 Â°C. In combination with the high solubility in non-polar organic solvents caused by the branched alkyl side chains, this synthetic route for Bi2S3 is of particular interest for the preparation of Bi2S3â€“polymer nanocomposites. By mixing the xanthate with PMMA as well as with the conjugated polymer P3HT in a solvent, coating of precursor layers and heating them to 200 Â°C in an inert atmosphere, Bi2S3 nanorods are formed within the polymer matrix by a ligand-free solid state in situ reaction pathway. Such metal sulphide nanorods may be of special interest for application in hybrid solar cells as nanorods facilitate charge transport and also provide a high interfacial area towards the conjugated polymer. Studying Bi2S3â€“P3HT nanocomposites concerning their applicability in hybrid solar cells, it was not possible to realise working solar cells using the in situ prepared Bi2S3â€“P3HT hybrid layers, even though we observed that the fluorescence of the conjugated polymer is efficiently quenched by the Bi2S3 nanorods, which points to an efficient charge transfer at the hybrid interface.
