Abstract

Recent development of artificial photosynthetic systems using photofunctional nanomaterials composed of multiporphyrins and carbon-based Ï€-electron acceptors such as fullerenes, carbon nanotubes and a new type of nanocarbon is reviewed by focusing on their photoinduced electron-transfer properties. Introduction

Life as we know it today mainly exists thanks to photosynthesis, the process through which light energy is converted into a stable electrochemical potential, which is eventually stored as chemical energy through a series of â€˜darkâ€™ (light-independent) reactions by plants, algae, and photosynthetic bacteria.1 The primary processes of photosynthesis involve photon capture (i.e., light harvesting) and energy transfer by the â€˜antenna complexesâ€™, and subsequently energy conversion in the reaction centers (RCs).1 The basic functional entity, which effectively carries out these primary steps of photosynthesis in purple photosynthetic bacteria, is the core complex RCâ€“LH1, which consists of the light-harvesting antenna complex LH1 and the reaction center.2â€“4

The development of artificial photosynthetic systems using a large portion of the solar spectrum would be a major advance in energy production, and a critical breakthrough with respect to the rising concern of environmental pollution caused by the use of fossil fuels.5 The design of such systems can be guided by the key steps of natural photosynthesis such as the efficient capture of visible light photons and electron/hole separation by electron transfer to give energetic oxidizing and reducing equivalents with long lifetimes (âˆ¼1 s).1â€“4 However, even relatively simple bacterial photosynthetic systems are actually extremely complex, and their synthetic imitation has been a challenging task. Mimicking the natural photosynthetic process requires making synthetic models of all the important components and then linking them together into a working molecular assembly.6â€“14

Porphyrins, synthetic analogues of natural chlorophyll molecules used in photosynthesis, have been widely used as promising candidates for light-capturing chromophores.8â€“14 Porphyrins are characterized by high molar absorption coefficients, fast energy and/or electron transfer to acceptor components in the system, and a variety of redox potentials caused by meso- and/or Î²-substituents in the porphyrin framework and introduction of a metal.15,16 The structural complexity of light-harvesting antenna complexes (LH1 and LH2), in which various chlorophyll moieties are superbly embedded in a transmembrane protein matrix, has also prompted the design and preparation of light-harvesting multiporphyrin systems.17â€“29

Fullerenes, made up of alternating hexagons and pentagons of carbon atoms, hold great promise as three-dimensional electron acceptors because of the small reorganization energy associated with electron transfer reactions, which results from the Ï€-electron systems being delocalized over the three-dimensional curved surface together with the rigid and confined structure of the aromatic Ï€ sphere.11,30 One can expect that fullerene-based donorâ€“acceptor systems should lead to acceleration of the charge separation and deceleration of the charge recombination processes, as expected by the Marcus theory of electron transfer.11,30,31

Similar promises rest on quasi-one-dimensional carbon nanotubes, in particular single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWNTs).32,33 The structure of SWNTs basically arises from the folding of a planar graphene sheet of hexagonal carbon rings. SWNTs have a characteristic curved surface that facilitates binding of larger organic molecules such as porphyrins via noncovalent or hydrophobic interactions.34â€“36 Fullerenes employed as Ï€-electron acceptors can be then replaced by SWNTs to utilize their extensive conjugated Ï€-system. Therefore, a combination of light-harvesting multiporphyrins and fullerenes/SWNTs would seem to be ideal as photofunctional nanomaterials for the construction of artificial photosynthetic systems.

This review focuses on recent development of artificial photosynthetic systems using photofunctional nanomaterials composed of multiporphyrins and carbon-based Ï€-electron acceptors such as fullerenes, SWNTs and a new type of nanocarbon. First, electron donorâ€“acceptor ensembles composed of porphyrins and fullerenes are described, covering small electron donorâ€“acceptor dyads to gigantic multi-component systems. Such photofunctional nanomaterials can then be utilized to construct efficient light energy conversion systems such as photovoltaic devices.

Electron donorâ€“acceptor ensembles based on porphyrins with non-covalent bonding

The synthesis of multi-component artificial photosynthetic model compounds shown in Fig. 2 has been very difficult, and the final yield is very low.53â€“55 The use of non-covalent binding such as metalâ€“ligand coordination, electrostatic interaction, hydrogen bonds and rotaxane formation would provide a simpler but more elegant way to construct electron donorâ€“acceptor ensembles mimicking the efficient biological electron transfer systems.56â€“61 When a non-coordinating solvent is used, an efficient charge separation occurs in the supramolecular donorâ€“acceptor complexes but the CS state is extremely short-lived.58â€“61 In contrast, the CS state could be long-lived in a coordinating solvent, but the formation of the supramolecular complex between the donor and acceptor moieties is normally prohibited by the solvent coordination. Porphyrin nanochannels including guest electron donors

The Ï€â€“Ï€ interaction also plays an important role in the assembly of porphyrins for the development of photofunctional materials based on the self-assembly of porphyrin compounds. A variety of porphyrin nanostructures have so far been reported, including nanorods exhibiting photoconductivity,86 and surface fabrication of electrodes toward the development of photovoltaic cells.87 Shelnutt and coworkers developed a porphyrin nanotube that can form platinum nanoparticles on the surface for the catalysis of photochemical H2 evolution.88

The self-assembly of porphyrin compounds has also attracted much attention in relation to the development of functional materials composed of electron donors and acceptors. The construction of porphyrin-based porous materials provides large channels, which can contain guest electron-donor or -acceptor molecules.89 Molecular recognition in the course of guest inclusion has been reported by Suslick and coworkers.89 They have prepared zeolite-like porous materials by self-assembly of metalloporphyrins and metal clusters, demonstrating molecular recognition based on the shape and size of guest molecules.89

Construction of multi-component supramolecular assemblies provides novel structures and integrated functionality that cannot be achieved by each building block alone. We have developed novel porphyrin supramolecules by using dodecaphenylporphyrin (H2DPP),90 which exhibits a saddle-distorted structure that can be recognized as a curved surface.91 Supramolecules based on self-assembly of saddle-distorted DPP-derivatives due to intermolecular interactions can give isolated inner spaces for the protection of the guest molecules from external lethal attack. This structural motif can contribute to the stabilization of unstable and metastable species in the cavity, and the observation of unprecedented characteristics of molecules. Such a strategy makes it possible to access the novel functionality of porphyrin-based multi-component materials, at a molecular level as single crystals (vide infra).92

Concluding remarks

As demonstrated in this review, the combination of porphyrins and carbon-based Ï€-electron acceptors such as fullerenes provides excellent photofunctional molecules and materials, in terms of their electron-transfer properties, for the development of artificial photosynthetic systems. The small electron transfer reorganization energies of these Ï€-compounds are essential for the design of artificial photosynthetic systems composed of functional Ï€-compounds with fast charge separation but extremely slow charge recombination that can be successfully used to construct efficient light energy conversion systems such as photovoltaic devices. There are two essential factors in efficient photocurrent generation. One is fast charge separation between electron donors and electron acceptor, and the other is the resulting hole transport and electron transport in the thin film. Three-dimensional steric control in the supramolecular Ï€-complexes between porphyrins and fullerenes in particular contributes to both the efficient formation of charge separation and the hole and electron transport in the thin film. The further development of photofunctional materials composed of multiporphyrins and carbon-based Ï€-electron acceptors involving Ï€â€“Ï€ interactions will certainly provide a promising perspective for the development of efficient light energy conversion systems.
