Abstract

We fabricated highly efficient planar type CH3NH3PbI3âˆ’xClx (MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) mixed halide perovskite solar cells via spray coating with a controlled composition of the solvents. The cells had a power conversion efficiency of 17.8% (forward scan), 18.3% (reverse scan), and 16.08 Â± 1.28% (average) for unit cells under 1 Sun conditions. We controlled the ratio of DMF (dimethylformamide), a quickly evaporating solvent, and GBL (Î³-butyrolactone), a slowly evaporating solvent, to 10:0, 9:1, 8:2, and 7:3 (vol:vol). We obtained the largest MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite crystal grains in the 8:2 sample because the inward flux of the spray solution was balanced with the outward flux of the evaporating solvent. Consequently, the moistened underlying polycrystalline perovskite film with small crystal grains re-dissolved and merged into larger crystalline grains by re-crystallization. By controlling the re-dissolution and crystal grain growth of the MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite film via spray coating, we fabricated a sub-module (10 cm Ã— 10 cm, active area = 40 cm2) with 10.5 V open circuit voltage, 84.15 mA short circuit current, 70.16% fill factor, and 15.5% power conversion efficiency under 1 Sun conditions.

Recently, organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskite materials have received a great deal of attention as promising light harvesting materials for photovoltaic devices due to their high absorption coefficient via a direct bandgap, a long diffusion length of charge carriers through the formation of shallow traps, high open circuit voltage with a small exciton binding energy, low temperature solution processability, and a certified record efficiency of more than 22% under 1 Sun conditions.1 The perovskite materials have an AMX3 crystal structure (A: a monovalent metal cation or alkyl ammonium; M: a divalent metal such as Pb, Sn, or Ge; and X: a halogen such as I, Br, or I). Pure AMX3 (A: methyl ammonium or formamidinium, M: Pb, X: Br or I) perovskite and mixed halide AMX3âˆ’xYx (A: methyl ammonium or formamidinium, M: Pb, X: I, and Y: Br or Cl) perovskite materials have usually been used for solar cells.

In particular, CH3NH3PbI3âˆ’xClx (MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) mixed halide perovskite solar cells have advantages over pure CH3NH3PbI3 (MAPbI3) perovskite solar cells in terms of the fabrication process because MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite solar cells have a 10-fold longer charge carrier diffusion length than pure MAPbI3 perovskite solar cells. Therefore, the charge collection efficiency and reproducibility of MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite solar cells are better than those of MAPbI3 perovskite solar cells.2 However, directly obtaining a MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite thin film with a uniform composition is difficult using the conventional process because it is formed by a solidâ€“solid reaction of a 3:1 molar ratio of MAI:PbCl2 and the subsequent elimination/sublimation of unreacted MAI and MACl by-products.3

Song et al. deposited a MAPbI3âˆ’xClx film with a uniform morphology by dissolving MAPbI3âˆ’xClx powder collected in the conventional solidâ€“solid reaction, but the composition was not uniform.3a Heo et al. reported that a MAPbI3âˆ’xClx film with a uniform composition could be formed through a liquid phase reaction, and the corresponding devices exhibited reproducible efficiency due to their long charge carrier diffusion length (âˆ¼1100 nm).3b

To find a commercial application for perovskite hybrid solar cells, it is equally important to develop a large area coating process for the formation of uniform perovskite films because the key technology for achieving high efficiency in such cells greatly depends on the formation of uniform perovskite films. Therefore, a readily commercializable spray coating method4 could help to deposit perovskite films on a large area because it could selectively deposit onto a patterned area by masking, unlike spin-coating5 and slot-die coating6 methods. Therefore, we synthesized a MAPbI3âˆ’xClx powder with a uniform composition and spray coated the MAPbI3âˆ’xClx film with large grains by controlling the ratio of a solvent mixture of DMF (dimethylformamide) and GBL (Î³-butyrolactone). By controlling the spray coating conditions, we fabricated an efficient sub-module (100 cm2, active area = 40 cm2) with âˆ¼15.5% power conversion efficiency under 1 Sun conditions (100 mW cmâˆ’2).

Finally, we obtained the MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide black powder by drying it in a vacuum oven for 5 h at room temperature. We then prepared a 0.8 M MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite/mixture solvent (DMF:GBL = 10â€“x:x vol:vol, x = 0â€“3) solution for the spray coating. Based on our series of experiments, we propose the mechanism of MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite crystal grain growth shown in Fig. 3. Spraying the MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite solution onto the hot TiO2/FTO substrate generates an inward flux (Fin) of the MAPbI3âˆ’xClx solution and an outward flux (Fout) of the solvents, and the ratio of Fin to Fout is controllable via the experimental conditions. When the perovskite solution lands on the hot substrate, the solvents quickly evaporate, and the perovskite begins to precipitate/nucleate/crystallize. Therefore, the solvent concentration profile and crystallization rate profile from the top to the bottom of the perovskite film have a reverse correlation under continuous spraying conditions, as illustrated in Fig. 3a. In the early stage of spray coating, a perovskite film with small crystal grains forms from the quick evaporation of solvents and consequent quick crystallization. By continuously supplying perovskite solution (Fin), the early film is soaked by the freshly deposited perovskite layer, which has a higher solvent concentration. Accordingly, the small early deposited perovskite crystal grains can be grown into larger crystals by re-dissolution/grain merging/re-crystallization, a process repeated under continuous spraying of perovskite solution, as illustrated in Fig. 3b.


Fig. 3 Schematic illustration of (a) the proposed mechanism of the spray coating process, (b) crystalline grain growth, and (c) the morphology of the formed crystalline grains in the perovskite film with respect to the balance between Fin and Fout.

Fig. 3c is a schematic illustration of the crystalline grains formed in the perovskite film with respect to the balance between Fin and Fout. When Fin is much smaller than Fout (as in the case of the DMF:GBL = 10:0 sample), the sprayed perovskite solution is quickly crystallized before melting the perovskite film underneath. On the other hand, when Fin is much larger than Fout (as in the case of the DMF:GBL = 7:3 sample), the underlying perovskite film does not fully dry during the spray coating process, and the wet perovskite film is thus dewetted. Practically, the perovskite film formed using the DMF:GBL = 7:3 sample was light yellow in colour after finishing the spray coating, though the colour quickly changed to black during further heat-treatment. Therefore, that perovskite film seems to have smaller crystalline grains than the perovskite film formed by using the DMF:GBL = 8:2 sample and a rougher morphology due to dewetting. On the other hand, when Fin is similar to or slightly higher than Fout, the continuously sprayed perovskite solution can moisten the underlying polycrystalline perovskite layer and re-dissolve and merge the small crystalline grains, thereby growing larger crystalline grains by re-crystallization. The balance of Fin and Fout is controllable by experimental parameters such as solution concentration, solvent mixture, spray flow rate, substrate temperature, and so on.

Conclusions

We fabricated highly efficient planar type MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite solar cells via spray coating using a controlled composition of the solvents. By controlling the ratio of DMF to GBL to 10:0, 9:1, 8:2, and 7:3 (vol:vol), we were able to control the size of the MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite crystal grains via a re-dissolution and crystal grain growth mechanism. When Fin â‰« Fout (as in the case of our DMF:GBL = 10:0 sample), the sprayed perovskite solution crystallized before it melted the underlying perovskite film, thereby forming small crystal grains. In contrast, when Fin â‰ª Fout (as in the case of our DMF:GBL = 7:3 sample), the underlying perovskite film did not fully dry during the spray coating process, so the wet perovskite film was partially dewetted; a further drying process was required and the finally dried film had relatively small crystal grains. Under Fin â‰¥ Fout (as in the case of our DMF:GBL = 9:1 and 8:2 (optimal) samples), the underlying polycrystalline perovskite film could be moistened and re-dissolved, merging the small crystalline grains into larger crystalline grains by re-crystallization. The power conversion efficiencies (Î·) of the MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite solar cells with different solvent compositions were 14.7% (forward scan), 15.2% (reverse scan), and 12.62 Â± 1.24% (average) for the 10:0 sample; 16.7% (forward scan), 17.2% (reverse scan), and 14.60 Â± 1.40% (average) for the 9:1 sample; 17.8% (forward scan), 18.3% (reverse scan), and 16.08 Â± 1.28% (average) for the 8:2 sample; and 15.5% (forward scan), 15.9% (reverse scan), and 13.63 Â± 1.25% (average) for the 7:3 sample.
