Abstract

Green hydrogen is an ideal and zero-pollution fuel that can be obtained using solar and other renewable energies. The efficient photocatalytic production of hydrogen mainly relies on the development of high-performance photocatalysts. In this work, we report the synthesis and photocatalytic hydrogen evolution application of a 2D coordination nanosheet (CONASH) constructed from bis-terpyridine ligands (Tpy) and Zn ions, which revealed an optical bandgap of 1.70 eV and exhibited excellent photocatalytic hydrogen-production performances of 3.10 mmol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1 and 11.84 mmol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1 under N2 and CO2 atmospheres, respectively. Such high photocatalytic hydrogen evolution rates are among the top tier in noble metal-free coordination complexes, providing a new way for designing hydrogen evolution photocatalysts based on highly structure- and band-adjustable 2D CONASHs.

Introduction

The increasing concerns about CO2 emissions and the scarcity of fossil fuels make the development of renewable and clean energy one of the most urgent tasks for our society.1 As a zero-pollution energy with high mass energy density, hydrogen has received fast-growing attention.2 Today, there are many methods available for H2 production, including the thermal decomposition of fossil fuels,3 electrolysis of water,4 decomposition of biomass,5 and the photocatalytic splitting of water.6 Although a large proportion of H2 is currently produced from fossil fuels through relatively mature techniques, it is still not possible to avoid CO2 and other pollutant emissions under the current technology framework.7 Also, water electrolysis faces the problems of huge energy consumption, low efficiency, and high cost for H2 production.8 Therefore, the solar-driven photocatalytic splitting of water is undoubtedly one of ideal routes for green H2 production.9,10 In this regard, it is crucial to design and develop efficient semiconducting photocatalysts.

Many semiconducting photocatalysts have been widely studied for water splitting hydrogen evolution to date, including TiO2,11 ZnO,12 SnO2,13 WO3,14 ZnS,15 ZnIn2S4,16 g-C3N4,17 CdS,18 and BiVO4.19 However, most of these suffer from a low photogenerated carrier mobility and utilization, resulting in an overall low photocatalytic activity. Among the various photocatalysts, metalâ€“organic coordination materials are highly attractive with adjustable band gaps and tailored catalytic active sites due to the large number of available metal ions and a tremendous library of organic ligand blocks.20â€“22 Many coordination materials have been reported and shown excellent photocatalytic hydrogen-production properties. Since the first report of MOFs for photocatalytic hydrogen production,23 many MOFs have been formulated as photocatalysts with impressive performances, such as NH2-MIL-125(Ti),24 UiO-66,25 Co(II)@MIL-125-NH2,26 ZIF-8/g-C3N4,27 CdS/Ni-MOF,28 and 40-P-CdS/Ni-MOLs.29 In addition, many metalâ€“organic coordination polymers with excellent photocatalytic hydrogen-production properties have also been reported. Wei et al. synthesized Co(II)-CP using Co(II) and amino-modified ligands that exhibited a high photocatalytic hydrogen-production activity of 1778 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1, which was attributed to the faster charge transfer and the suppression of the electronâ€“hole recombination.30 Verma et al. integrated tetrathiafulvalene (TTF) and terpyridine (TPY) derivatives through amide linkages and obtained a TPYâ€“TTF gelling agent for Znâ€“TPYâ€“TTF synthesis, which had a high photocatalytic hydrogen-production activity of 530 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1.31 Through an in situ-generated triphenylamine-terpyridine (TPA-Tpy) and Zn(II)-assisted self-assembly, a donorâ€“acceptor coordination polymer (TPA-Zn) was designed, demonstrating a broad-spectrum absorption and high hydrogen generation performance under visible-light irradiation (27.1 mmol gâˆ’1 over 9 h) with a good hydrogen generation performance relying on the intermittent disruption of conjugation by Zn(II) metal nodes to provide a suitable amount of exciton-separation ability and charge-carrier mobility.32 Although exhibiting great potential in photocatalytic H2 evolution, these materials still suffer from limited efficiency due to the bulky sizes and buried catalytic sites. Therefore, the design of low-dimensional structures for these metalâ€“organic coordination materials should be a practical way to boost their photocatalytic activity, such as 2D CONASH.

2D CONASH is a new and intriguing type of 2D material constructed with alternating metal centres and organic ligands with a variety of special physical and chemical properties that have been explored for applications in electrochromism,33 electrocatalysis,34 photoelectric conversions,35 and photodetecting.36 In addition, 2D CONASH also exhibits excellent photocatalytic hydrogen-production performance due to its high-density and readily accessible catalytic sites, reduced reactant/product diffusion pathways, and fast electron transport. It was found that the Pt single-atom-decorated MOF nanosheets exhibited a H2-generation rate of 11320 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1, suggesting that the ultrathin 2D structure had advantages for the photocatalytic performances of metalâ€“organic coordination materials.37 In addition, the photocatalytic H2-production activity of 2D CONASH could be significantly improved by introducing photosensitizers. For example, after the introduction of ((MBA)Ir(ppy)2)Cl or ((MBA)Ru(bpy)2)Cl2 into Ce-based CONASHs through post-synthetic modifications, the obtained Ce6-BTB-Ir or Ce6-BTB-Ru CONASHs showed 8.7â€“9.3-fold enhancement in photocatalytic H2-production activity.38 To date, the research on CONASH is still in its infancy, especially its applications in the field of photocatalytic H2 production. In addition, many photocatalysts contain heavy metals that are potentially harmful to the environment,39,40 or need noble metals, such as Au and Pt, as the cocatalysts when applied for photocatalytic H2 production.41,42 Therefore, it is of great interest to develop environmentally friendly and noble metal-free CONASH catalysts with high photocatalytic performances.

In this work, we report the bottomâ€“up synthesis of a novel semiconducting Znâ€“Tpy CONASH from a two-armed nitrile-containing terpyridine ligand and Zn(II) via a liquidâ€“liquid interface reaction. We introduced cyano groups into the Tpy ligands to facilitate the generation of 2D CONASH structures through a hydrogen bond-driven self-assembly. The chemical structure was well characterized by X-ray diffraction (XRD), Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), and fitted by theoretical simulations. Compared with the original Tpy ligand, the Znâ€“Tpy CONASH reduced the optical band gap to 1.70 eV and exhibited excellent H2-evolution activities of 3.10 mmol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1 (under N2) and 11.84 mmol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1 (under CO2) without using any noble metal components. Through theoretical simulations, the mechanism for the much enhanced photocatalytic activity was well revealed. This work should inspire future attempts towards novel 2D CONASH designs and bandgap engineering for broad optical and photocatalysis applications.

Experimental

Materials

4-Bromophenylacetonitrile, terephthalaldehyde, 4-formylphenylboronic acid, acetyl pyridine, potassium carbonate (K2CO3), and tetrakis(triphenylphosphine) palladium (0) (Pd(PPh3)4) were purchased from Dongguan and Aladdin Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd, respectively, and used without further purification. 1,4-Dioxane, aqueous ammonia 28%, and common solvents of analytical grade were supplied by AR Chemical Co. Ltd. CCK-8 was acquired from Biotime. Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium (DMEM), fetal bovine serum (FBS), antibiotic and antimycotic solution, and Dulbecco's phosphate buffered saline (DPBS) were procured from Gibco.

The Tpy ligand was synthesized by a three-step reaction process as shown in Scheme S1.â€  Compound 1 and Compound 2 were synthesized according to previous reports.43,44 Then, compound 1 (2 mmol) and compound 2 (1 mmol) in 1,4-dioxane/H2O (14 mL/2 mL) were added in to a two-neck flask. After cooling to room temperature the precipitate was filtered followed by washing with chloroform, DMF, and water, to give a green solid product in a 65% yield.

Cell culture, imaging, and cytotoxicity assay

The 4T1 cells were cultured on 96-well plates in Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium (DMEM, GibcoTM) containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS, GibcoTM), and 1% antibiotic and antimycotic solution (GibcoTM) at 37 Â°C in a humidified atmosphere with 5% CO2. When the cells were completely attached, the Znâ€“Tpy suspension with different concentrations was added to the cell wells (10 Î¼L per well). After culturing in an incubator for another 30 min, the culture medium in every well was removed and washed with Dulbecco's phosphate buffered saline (DPBS) three times. Subsequently, the 4T1 cells were imaged by fluorescence microscopy. To evaluate the photostability of the Znâ€“Tpy NPs in cells, the cells were continuously irradiated with a high-power light (Î»Ex = 480 nm, P = 150 W) for a certain time interval and the change of the fluorescence intensity was analyzed. The Znâ€“Tpy suspension with different concentrations were added to the cells for 10 Î¼L per well, and the final concentrations of Znâ€“Tpy in the cells were 6.25, 12.5, 25, 50, and 100 Î¼g mLâˆ’1, respectively. The absorbance of each well was measured at 450 nm using a microplate reader (Thermo Scientific Multiskan FC).

Photocatalytic hydrogen-production experiments

The photocatalytic hydrogen-production experiments were carried out in a 10 mL glass cell. For the photocatalytic experiments, 2 mg catalyst was dispersed in 8 mL of water containing 2 mL of triethanolamine (TEOA) as a sacrificial reagent. The suspension was sonicated to prepare a homogeneous dispersion. The reaction mixture was then purged with N2 for 30 min to remove any traces of dissolved H2 gas. The reaction mixture was irradiated with a 300 W xenon lamp under magnetic stirring at 500 rpm minâˆ’1, and 250 Î¼L of headspace gas was sampled every 0.5 h using a Microliter Syringes gas-tight syringe and injected into the gas chromatograph for detection. In addition, no hydrogen evolution was observed for the mixture of water and TEOA under light irradiation in the absence of a photocatalyst. In addition, we also exhausted the system with CO2 instead of N2 for 30 min to make the system saturated by CO2, and then used the same irradiation condition to perform the photocatalytic experiment for hydrogen production. Tpy and Znâ€“Tpy coated on FTO conductive glass were used as the working electrodes (the effective coating area of the sample was 1.0 cm2), while Pt, SCE, and 0.5 M Na2SO4 were used as the counter electrode, reference electrode, and electrolyte, respectively. Then the Mottâ€“Schottky curve, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), and photocurrent response signal of Tpy and Znâ€“Tpy were tested by the electrochemical workstation.

Comprehensive spectroscopic characterizations were also performed to verify the chemical structure of Znâ€“Tpy CONASH, including Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). It could be found from the FT-IR spectra that the nitrile group peaks of Znâ€“Tpy were obviously shifted towards lower wavenumbers compared with the pristine Tpy ligand. This was because of the formation of hydrogen bonds between the N atoms of the â€“CN groups and the H atoms of the neighbouring ligands in Znâ€“Tpy CONASH, leading to the reduced stretching vibration frequency of CN bonds.49 In addition, there were two distinct SO42âˆ’ peaks (1114 cmâˆ’1 and 614 cmâˆ’1) formed in Znâ€“Tpy CONASH compared with Tpy, indicating the incorporation of SO42âˆ’ in the final CONASH structure. To be specific, the characteristic vibration peak at 1114 cmâˆ’1 could be attributed to the Î½3 mode of SO42âˆ’,50 while the additional peaks at 1195 and 1014 cmâˆ’1 were caused by the reduced symmetry of the SO42âˆ’ structure from Td to C2v (Fig. 2c and S7bâ€ ).51

XPS was also employed to further demonstrate the coordination structure of Znâ€“Tpy CONASH. The results confirmed the existence of C, N, O, S, and Zn atoms, as shown in Fig. 2d. The measured atomic ratio of Zn:N:S was 1:4.19:0.96, which was close to the theoretical stoichiometry of the SO42âˆ’ bridged coordination structure Zn2(Î¼-O2SO2)2(Tpy)2. The binding energies of N 1s were fitted with two peaks at 399.1 and 398.1 eV, which originated from the terpyridine and nitrile groups, respectively. In addition, while the S 2p peak of sulfate is usually at 169 eV,52 the S 2p core level spectrum of Znâ€“Tpy exhibited a low binding energy of 167.8 eV due to the formation of bridged structures.53 These results demonstrate the formation of Zn2(Î¼-O2SO2)2(Tpy)2 in the prepared Znâ€“Tpy CONASH.

This phenomenon can be explained by the ligand-based intermolecular charge-transfer effect.53

The photocatalytic H2-evolution performance of 2D CONASH was comprehensively explored. A 300 W xenon lamp was used as a light source and triethanolamine (TEOA) was added as the sacrificial reagent. Under saturation of N2, the evolution of H2 was 5.11 mmol gâˆ’1 in the initial 3 h under light irradiation (C1 = 1.70 mmol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1, Fig. 4a), which increased up to 3.10 mmol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1 in the 5th cycle (Fig. 4b). Such a H2-evolution activity was around 29 times higher than that of the pristine Tpy (0.107 mmol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1). The DLS analysis of the CONASH samples before and after cyclic photocatalysis (Fig. S6â€ ) revealed a much reduced average particle size of the photocatalysts from 1535 to 122 nm. The reduced sample size should provide a higher specific surface area and more catalytic sites, resulting in the observed gradual improvement of the photocatalytic activity of the Znâ€“Tpy in the cyclic test. Since the Znâ€“Tpy CONASH sample is a layered structure, the bulk layered structure may evolve gradually peeling off by the H2 bubbles during the process of photocatalysis, leading to the formation of thinner and smaller CONASH samples.

The band structure and optoelectrical properties of 2D CONASH were further investigated by electrochemical and photoelectrical characterizations. Mottâ€“Schottky (Mâ€“S) analyses were performed for Tpy and Znâ€“Tpy to evaluate the experimental feasibility of splitting water.60 It could be found from the Mâˆ’S diagram that both the Tpy and Znâ€“Tpy structures have positive slopes (Fig. 5a), suggesting that they were both n-type semiconductors. The corresponding flat band potentials (Efb) of Tpy and Znâ€“Tpy were âˆ’1.25 V and âˆ’0.94 V (vs. SCE), respectively. The elaborate band potentials of CBM and VBM for Tpy and Znâ€“Tpy are shown in Fig. 5b. Accordingly, the reduction potential of water was lower than the CBM of Tpy and Znâ€“Tpy CONASH, suggesting that the semiconducting Tpy and Znâ€“Tpy can be applied as photoreduction catalysts for H2 production from water.

Photocurrent measurements were performed both in the presence and absence of light irradiation (Fig. 5c). At the same potential (0.5 V vs. SCE), the photocurrent density of Tpy was 120 nA cmâˆ’1, while that of Znâ€“Tpy was 180 nA cmâˆ’1. This indicates that the Zn coordination promotes the separation and fast transportation of photogenerated carriers, thereby significantly enhancing the catalytic performance. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) is a very powerful tool for evaluating the carrier-transfer capability.62 As shown in Fig. 5d, the arc radius of Zn-coordinated Znâ€“Tpy was smaller compared with the Tpy, indicating that the charge-transfer resistance at the solidâ€“liquid interface was smaller, which is conducive for the rapid transfer of photogenerated carriers to the catalyst surfaces to trigger the photochemical reactions. To understand the differences in the observed photocatalytic performances, the electrostatic potential and deformation electron density of Znâ€“Tpy and Tpy ligand were simulated using Dmol3 module, Materials Studio.47,48 As shown in Fig. 5e, the negative and positive electrostatic potentials indicated electrophilic and nucleophilic regions, respectively. The calculated Mulliken atomic charges for Zn, O, and S atoms in the bridging Zn2(Î¼-O2SO2)2 structure were 0.793, âˆ’0.519, and 0.690 Ha eâˆ’1, respectively, suggesting the formation of a partially charged status and strong intramolecular electrostatic fields. This is critical for boosting the separation efficiency of the photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs and improving the photocatalytic activity and efficiency of the Znâ€“Tpy CONASH photocatalyst. As a comparison, the Mulliken atomic charges for the different atoms of Tpy ligand varied from âˆ’0.340 to 0.227 Ha eâˆ’1, with the difference much smaller than that of the Znâ€“Tpy. Besides, the deformation electron density maps of Tpy and Znâ€“Tpy also clearly showed a strong charge accumulation occurred in the Zn2(Î¼-O2SO2)2 region, as shown in Fig. S10.â€  These results explain the much enhanced photocatalytic activity of the coordinated Znâ€“Tpy well, which also indicates the importance of building intramolecular electrostatic fields through structural design in the development of 2D CONASH photocatalysts.

Conclusions

In summary, a novel 2D CONASH was designed and prepared through the coordination of Zn ions with bis-terpyridine ligand with the structure clarified by comprehensive microscopic, spectroscopic analyses and theoretical simulations. The Znâ€“Tpy CONASH exhibited n-type semiconductor properties with an optical band gap of 1.70 eV, stable orange-red photoluminescence emission at 638 nm, and VBM and CBM of 1.00 V and âˆ’0.70 V (vs. NHE), respectively. The experimental photocatalytic H2-evolution activity from water for Znâ€“Tpy CONASH was 3.10 mmol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1 under the N2 atmosphere, and was remarkably enhanced up to 11.84 mmol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1 after using CO2 for saturation instead, which are top ranking values among non-noble metal complexes and coordination compounds. The reduced band gap and strong built-in intramolecular electrostatic fields in the coordinated CONASH are responsible for the boosted photocatalytic H2 production. This work opens a new door for 2D CONASH structural and band designs, and their applications in photocatalytic H2 production.
