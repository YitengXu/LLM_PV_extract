Abstract

Flexible and transparent electrodes based on one-dimensional Ag nanowire@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 coreâ€“shell nanostructures with a tunable composition were fabricated by electrodeposition. The highly conductive Ag nanowire (NW) network was considered as a current collector, and the NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 nanostructures could grow on the surface of the Ag NWs, providing active materials with the synergistic effect of Ni and Co elements. The coreâ€“shell nanostructures integrate the advantages of metallic NWs for effective charge transport, the NiCo alloyed layer for a stable structure, and NiCo(OH)2 for high electrochemical performance. The composite electrode with a transmittance of 54.3% delivers a high areal capacitance of 22.2 mF cmâˆ’2 at a current density of 0.1 mA cmâˆ’2, and an excellent capacitance retention rate of 91.9% at 100 mV sâˆ’1 after 5000 cycles. Furthermore, an asymmetric supercapacitor composed of the Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 cathode and the Ag NW/graphene anode achieves a maximum areal capacitance of 9.6 mF cmâˆ’2 at a current density of 0.2 mA cmâˆ’2, high energy density of 3.0 W h kgâˆ’1 and power density of 3.5 W kgâˆ’1. The Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 coreâ€“shell nanostructured electrode shows great potential for flexible and transparent energy storage devices.

1. Introduction

In recent years, transparent conductive films (TCFs) based on indium tin oxide (ITO) are in great demand for touch screens, displays, thin film solar cells and wearable devices.1â€“4 However, their intrinsic brittleness, high costs of the fabrication process and raw materials limit further development of ITO-based TCFs in the next-generation photoelectric devices.5â€“7 Therefore, emerging substitutes for ITO have been developing, such as graphene,8â€“10 carbon nanotubes,11 conductive polymers,12â€“14 MXenes,15 metal meshes,16â€“21 and metal nanowire percolation networks.22â€“24 Furthermore, with the rapid development of transparent and flexible electronic devices, their energy conversion and storage systems not only require large power/energy density, but also put forward high transparency and flexibility demands. Transparent and flexible supercapacitors (TFSs) as one of the ideal energy storage devices are the most promising to be realized by guaranteeing the transparency and flexibility of the electrodes and electrolyte.25

Generally, there are two strategies to fabricate the electrodes for TFSs. One method is to use the above-discussed TCFs as both the current collector and active materials simultaneously, e.g., conductive polymers and carbon materials. Higgins and Coleman spray-deposited poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene)/poly(styrene-4-sulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS) on a flexible polyethylene glycol terephthalate (PET) substrate for TFS electrodes. The fabricated film with an area of 1 cm2 displayed an areal capacitance of 1 mF cmâˆ’2 and transmittance of 80%.26 Ajayan et al. prepared mechanically flexible and transparent carbon films using porous templates. The highly textured graphitic films acted as both the current collector and active materials, which were integrated with a solid polymer electrolyte to function as a TFS.27 Another method is to use the TCFs as a current collector, onto which the active materials are deposited to obtain electrochemical performance. Wang et al. prepared the electrode by depositing MnO2 island active materials on the surface of ITO films. The TFS based on the fabricated electrodes exhibited an areal capacitance of 4.73 mF cmâˆ’2 and transmittance of 44%.28 Lai et al. constructed TFSs based on Ag NW conducting networks and a PEDOT:PSS layer, which showed high transparency (>50%), good mechanical flexibility and high cycling stability with only slight areal capacitance decays after 100 times of bending (âˆ¼25%) and 5000 chargeâ€“discharge cycles (âˆ¼15%).29

The former method fabricates the electrodes by merging the current collector and active materials. However, the commonly used conductive polymers and carbon materials with limited photoelectric properties would restrict the electron transport efficiency of the electrodes, resulting in the decrease of TFS performance. Furthermore, in order to guarantee high transparency of the energy storage devices, the active material loading has to be reduced, and vice versa. In the latter method, the construction of a current collector and active materials is separated. Thus, an enhanced electrochemical performance and a large electrochemical window of the TFS can be achieved by properly selecting TCFs with excellent photoelectric properties and diversified active materials. As is well known, the TCFs based on metallic NW interconnected networks exhibit high conductivity and good transparency, which are considered as a potential current collector with high efficiency for electron transport.29 However, it is still a great challenge to resist the oxidation and corrosion of the metallic NWs during the processes of active material growth and TFS chargeâ€“discharge.30,31 Therefore, how to effectively attach active materials to the metallic NW surfaces without damaging their structures and consequently improve the electrochemical performance of the TFS electrodes is one of the urgent issues to be addressed.

It has been reported that the binary NiCo hydroxide exhibits much improved electrochemical performances by offering more redox reactions and higher stability, as compared to a single Ni or Co hydroxide.32 The electrodeposition of the NiCo hydroxide on the metallic NW surfaces can be implemented below the decomposition potential of Ag, which can not only avoid the damage of metallic NWs, but also form a protective layer of the NWs from air and electrolyte.33,34

Herein, we successfully fabricated a composite electrode consisting of Ag NWs and NiCo hydroxides with a unique coreâ€“shell nanostructure by electrodeposition. It is very interesting that a NiCo bi-metallic layer was formed between the Ag NW and NiCo hydroxide. The distinct one-dimensional Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 structure is supposed to offer multiple advantages. First, the NiCo layer not only assists to improve the electron transport efficiency at the interfaces, but also serves as an interlayer to decrease the lattice mismatch of the core and shell nanostructure. Second, the existence of the NiCo layer slows down the hydroxide expansion during the chargeâ€“discharge process, promoting the electrochemical cycling stability of the electrode. Third, the alloyed NiCo can further transform into active NiCo(OH)2 in an alkaline environment to contribute high electrochemical performances of the electrodes. Through properly tuning the Ni to Co ratio, this gives rise to a high areal capacitance of 22.2 mF cmâˆ’2 at a current density of 0.1 mA cmâˆ’2 and only a slight capacitance decay (<10%) after 5000 chargeâ€“discharge cycles, benefitting from the excellent stability of NiCo hydroxides. Additionally, the asymmetric TFS with a potential window of 1.2 V was assembled by utilizing the Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 electrode as a positive electrode and the Ag NW/graphene composite electrode as a negative electrode.35 The device exhibited excellent electrochemical performances compared with the others using metallic networks as the current collectors.

2. Experimental

2.1 Preparation of the Ag NWs

Ag NWs with a high aspect ratio were prepared by a hydrothermal method as previously reported.35â€“37 First, 3 mmol silver nitrate, 4 mmol glucose and 0.6 mmol ferric sulfate were, respectively, fully dissolved in 20, 40 and 40 mL deionized water. After that, the above silver nitrate solution was dripped into the glucose solution, followed by the introduction of the ferric sulfate solution into the mixture. Then 4.5 g poly(vinyl pyrrolidone) (PVP, K30) was completely dissolved in the above solution. Finally, 40 mL of the resultant solution was transferred to a 100 mL autoclave and heated at 180 Â°C for 6 h. After the hydrothermal reaction, grayâ€“green Ag NWs were collected by centrifugation.

2.2 Preparation of the Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 coreâ€“shell nanostructure

The fabrication process of the Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 coreâ€“shell nanostructure is illustrated in Fig. 1. First, a PVA thin film with a solution concentration of 0.005 g mLâˆ’1 was deposited onto a clean PET substrate via a spin-coating method. The Ag NW dispersion in ethanol was then dropped on the surface of the PVA film with a controlled thickness of âˆ¼10 Î¼m to construct Ag NW percolation networks by another spin-coating process. The optical transmittance spectra of the blank PET before and after depositing the PVA layer are displayed in Fig. S1,â€  revealing that the transparent PVA layer has no obvious impact on the film transmittance. Subsequently, the film was laminated using a mechanical pressure of 25 MPa for 3 min to enhance the connections of the NWs and consequently improve the network conductivity.

The Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 coreâ€“shell nanostructure was prepared by the electrodeposition method. The freshly prepared 0.1 M ammonium acetate, 0.1 M cobalt acetate (Co(CH3COO)2Â·4H2O) and nickel acetate (Ni(CH3COO)2Â·4H2O) solutions were used as the sources of Co and Ni. The concentration ratios of Ni to Co were controlled at 1:0, 0.75:0.25, 0.5:0.5, 0.25:0.75 and 0:1, of which the products were denoted as Sample 1, Sample 2, Sample 3, Sample 4 and Sample 5, respectively. The counter and reference electrodes for the electrodeposition were graphene rods and the saturated calomel electrode (SCE). Electrodeposition was conducted at a constant potential of âˆ’0.9 V (vs. SCE) for 5 min. The transmittance spectra of the electrode with different deposition times are presented in Fig. S2,â€  indicating that the transmittance gradually decreased with the extended deposition time due to the increase of the active material loading. Considering the transparency and electrochemical performance of the electrode, 5 min is consequently used as an optimized deposition time. The deposited electrodes were repeatedly washed with deionized water and ethanol, and ultimately dried at 50 Â°C.

2.3 Fabrication of a solid-state asymmetric supercapacitor (ASC) device

To assemble an ASC device, a flexible and transparent Ag NW/graphene composite film studied in our previous research was used as the anode,35 while the Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 film was applied as the cathode. Furthermore, the PVA/LiClO4/KOH gel was prepared as the polymer electrolyte according to the following procedure: 2 g PVA powder was dissolved and stirred in 20 mL deionized water at 85 Â°C for 1 h. Afterwards, 0.01 mol KOH and 0.01 mol LiClO4 were added to the PVA solution with stirring for 30 min. The electrode was cut into a rectangle with an area of 1 Ã— 2 cm2 and a copper conductive tape was stuck to one side of the electrode. The electrolyte was dropped onto the surface of the anode and cathode, and then two electrodes were gently assembled to obtain a sandwich-structured supercapacitor. The device was then treated at 50 Â°C for 4 h until the electrolyte gel layer became colorless to obtain a flexible and transparent ASC.

3. Results and discussion

The overall fabrication process for the Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 coreâ€“shell nanostructure is briefly illustrated in Fig. 1. It is worth noting that a PVA layer between the substrate and the Ag NW conductive network is beneficial for improving the network adhesion and electrode stability. Fig. S3â€  displays the cycling stability of the electrodes with and without the PVA layer. The electrode without the PVA layer is unable to run after 1200 cycles; however, it maintains a high capacitance retention of 97.6% after 5000 cycles with the PVA layer. The improved stability is contributed by the increased adhesion of the Ag NWs to the substrate and decreased exfoliation of the active materials during the chargeâ€“discharge process.37

Fig. 2a shows a typical SEM image of the Ag NW film, indicating that the diameter of the prepared Ag NWs ranges from 80 to 150 nm. The deformation of the NWs caused by the mechanical press can be clearly observed as marked using red dashed circles. This reveals close contacts of the NWs, which could decrease the resistance of electron transport among the NWs.37 To investigate the synergistic effects of Ni and Co elements, we applied different Ni to Co ratios during the electrodeposition process. The microstructures and the corresponding elemental distributions of five samples are presented in Fig. 2bâ€“f. The surfaces of the Ag NWs are uniformly covered by the active materials, and the average diameter of the NWs increased to approximately 500 nm. Moreover, the ratio of Ni to Co has a crucial influence on the surface morphology of the NWs. As the amount of Co increased, the one-dimensional structure became looser. The active materials were not only deposited onto the NW surfaces but also partially collapsed on the PET substrate for Sample 5 without the addition of Ni, as seen in Fig. 2f. Therefore, the stable surface structure can be achieved by regulating the ratio of Ni to Co.

The XPS measurement was conducted to probe the surface chemical compositions and valence states of the deposited materials. Fig. 3b shows the XPS spectra of Samples 1, 3 and 5, revealing the presence of Ag and Ni elements in Sample 1, Ag, Ni and Co elements in Sample 3, and Ag and Co elements in Sample 5, in addition to the C and O elements. The high-resolution XPS spectra of Co 2p, Ni 2p and O 1s for Sample 3 are displayed in Fig. 3câ€“e. The bands of Co 2p3/2 and Co 2p1/2 accompanied with satellite peaks in Fig. 3c can be de-convoluted into multiple peaks, which centered at 780.36, 781.96, 796.12, and 797.90 eV, respectively, resulting from the existence of Co2+.34,40 Two peaks of Ni 2p3/2 and Ni 2p1/2 at 855.2 and 872.8 eV, and two shake-up satellite peaks at 861.3 and 879.6 eV reveal the characteristic of Ni2+, as illustrated in Fig. 3d.41 In addition, the spectrum of O 1s can be de-convoluted into two peaks at 531.07 and 531.77 eV (Fig. 3e), confirming that the O element comes from metal hydroxyl and the PET substrate, respectively.40 The XPS analysis indicates the co-existence of NiCo hydroxides on the surface of the one-dimensional nanostructure.

It seemed confusing that XRD patterns suggested the generation of metallic Ni and Co, while XPS spectra revealed the formation of NiCo hydroxides. This could be attributed to the different detection depths of XRD and XPS, i.e., dozens of micrometers and several nanometers, respectively. Therefore, it could be assumed that the metallic Ni and Co grow on the Ag NW surface, and the outer layer of the composite nanowires consisted of NiCo hydroxides. These results disclosed that the one-dimensional nanostructure was Ag NW@NiCo modified by NiCo(OH)2, as illustrated in Fig. 3f.

The composition and microstructure of the coreâ€“shell nanostructure were further confirmed using TEM, taking Sample 3 as an example. As shown in Fig. 3g, the Co and Ni elements are homogeneously distributed throughout the surface of the Ag NW. The diameter of the core structure with a dark color is 119 nm, and the thickness of the shell structure with a light color is 204 nm. Fig. 3h displays a high-resolution TEM image of the area marked in Fig. 3g, and the selected-area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern inserted in Fig. 3h suggests the polycrystalline structure of the shell. Fig. 3i and j, respectively, correspond to the enlarged image of green and yellow dashed cubes. The lattice fringe spacing in the internal area is calculated as 0.20 nm, which can be indexed to the (111) reflection of metallic Ni (d = 0.2035 nm) and the (002) plane of metallic Co (d = 0.2023 nm), implying that the layers bonded to the Ag NWs are composed of crystalline Ni and Co.42 Correspondingly, the lattice fringe spacing in the external area is evaluated to be 0.23 nm, which is identified as the (011) reflection of Ni(OH)2 (d = 0.2339 nm) and (102) plane of Co(OH)2 (d = 0.2364 nm), revealing that the nanostructure in the outer area of the NWs is the NiCo hydroxide.42 Thus, it is further confirmed that the coreâ€“shell nanostructure is Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2.

To understand the diffusion effect on the electrochemical performances of the electrodes, the EIS spectra of the electrodes were recorded in a frequency range from 1 MHz to 0.01 Hz (Fig. 5d). All impedance spectra consist of two parts with a semicircle in the high frequency region and a straight slope in the low frequency region.45 An equivalent circuit of the electrochemical system in the inset was used to analyze the charge-transfer resistance.40,46 The internal resistance (Rs) values of the five samples are 8.0, 6.3, 6.7, 6.7, and 10.5 Î©, while the charge transfer resistance (Rct) values are 19.3, 15.9, 10.1, 12.2, and 28.6 Î©, respectively. The excellent conductivity of the electrodes is mainly attributed to the coreâ€“shell nanostructure with fast charge transfer, as well as the NiCo metallic layer with a low interface resistance. Additionally, the electrodes with Ni and Co co-doping exhibit higher electrical conductivity to boost the reactions with the electrolyte than those with Ni or Co single-doping due to the synergistic effect. Furthermore, the slopes of the EIS plots of Samples 2, 3 and 4 are close to perpendicular, which are greater than those of Samples 1 and 5, revealing an ideal capacitive behavior and good ionic conductivity by co-doping Ni and Co. Fig. 5e shows the cycling stabilities of the composite electrodes measured by repeating the CV process between âˆ’0.1 and 0.6 V at 100 mV sâˆ’1 for 5000 cycles. It is worth noting that Samples 2, 3 and 4 exhibit ultrahigh electrochemical durability with areal capacitance retentions of 88.9%, 91.9% and 97.6% after 5000 cycles, which are markedly larger than the 53.2% of Sample 1 and the 70.7% of Sample 5. The improved electrochemical stability of the electrodes not only lies on the synergistic effect of NiCo hydroxides, but also is attributed to the stable structure of the coreâ€“shell Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2.

In order to explore the relationship between structure variation and electrochemical stability of the electrodes during the chargeâ€“discharge process, the SEM images with a high resolution of five samples before and after 1000 chargeâ€“discharge cycles were taken as shown in Fig. 5f. After 1000 electrochemical cycles, the deposited materials formed compact layers on the Ag NW surfaces. Owing to the existence of the NiCo alloy layer, the expansion and collapse of NiCo hydroxides were not obviously observed after 1000 cycles. The Ag NWs could also be perfectly protected from the air and electrolyte to maintain high electron transport of the NW network. In addition, Samples 2, 3 and 4 maintained the original morphologies to a large extent, revealing more stable structures than Samples 1 and 5. The compact and stable coreâ€“shell structures during the chargeâ€“discharge process effectively guarantee the electrochemical stability of the electrodes. Moreover, the transmittance spectra were recorded while the electrode was charging and discharging. As shown in Fig. S5,â€  the transmittance of Sample 2 decreased from 42.92% to 41.83% during the charging process, while it increased to 42.91% after discharging. The result indicates that the transmittance remained stable during charging and discharging processes. 4. Conclusions

In summary, an Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 electrode was obtained by electrochemical deposition. The photoelectric and electrochemical performances of the electrode can be optimized by adjusting the ratio of Ni to Co. The best electrochemical performance of the electrode with a transmittance of 54.3% at 550 nm can be achieved when the ratio of Ni to Co is 0.5:0.5. Its areal capacitance reached a maximum of 22.2 mF cmâˆ’2 at a current density of 0.1 mA cmâˆ’2 in 1 M KOH. The corresponding areal capacitance retention rate is 91.9% at 100 mV sâˆ’1 after 5000 cycles. Furthermore, asymmetric supercapacitors were assembled with Ag NW/graphene as the anode electrode and Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 as the cathode electrode. A high areal capacitance of 9.6 mF cmâˆ’2 was achieved, and the potential window could be as large as 1.2 V. The energy density of the supercapacitor is 3.0 W h kgâˆ’1 with a power density of 2.6 W kgâˆ’1. The results gave sufficient evidence to confirm that the fabricated Ag NW@NiCo/NiCo(OH)2 electrode exhibited excellent electrochemical performances and it showed potential for application in flexible and transparent energy storage devices.
