The n- and p-type thermoelectric response of a semiconducting Co-based quaternary Heusler alloy: a density functional approach

Enamullah * and Pil-Ryung Cha *

Abstract

In the combined framework of density functional and Boltzmann transport theory, we have systematically studied the electronic structure, mechanical stability and thermoelectric properties of the semiconducting quaternary Heusler alloy, CoFeTiAl. Ab initio simulation predicts a most stable non-magnetic semiconducting phase with a direct band gap of 1.39 eV at the Fermi level using the hybrid (HSE06) exchangecorrelation functional. The formation energy (with respect to the elemental ground state) and lattice dynamics simulation suggests that the alloy is thermodynamically as well as mechanically stable. A detailed thermoelectric response (e.g., Seebeck coefficient, electrical and thermal conductivity and power factor) of the alloy has been investigated using Boltzmann transport theory for both electrons and phonons, as implemented in BoltzTraP and ShengBTE codes respectively. From the theoretical findings, we have obtained the upper limit of figure of merit and approaches to unity for both n- and p-type behavior at moderate temperatures. Hence a better thermoelectric response is expected from the present alloy for practical applications.

1 Introduction

Thermoelectric (TE) materials are in the center of worldwide research activities due to their ability to convert waste heat into electricity.1,2 Thermoelectricity is based upon two principles, due to Seebeck and Peltier, which in general, convert residual heat into electricity and vice versa.1 The maximum efficiency (max) of a TE device is calculated by the cold and hot side temperatures (Th and Tc) and the intrinsic figure of merit (ZT) through the following equation,3

For a given temperature difference, the conversion efficiency depends upon the value of ZT. The higher the value the higher the conversion efficiency. Following eqn (3) (see the next section), the ZT value depends upon transport coefficients such as the Seebeck coefficient (S) and electrical () and thermal (e, l) conductivities. Hence, in order to maximize ZT, one needs to minimize the denominator (i.e., e, l) and simultaneously maximize the numerator (i.e., ). However, maximizing ZT is a challenging task due the proportional relationship between and e through the WiedemannFranz equation; e = LT, where, L represents the Lorentz number.

In earlier days, metals were used for TE devices due to their high electrical conductivity but their reported ZT values were low due to their high thermal conductivity and low Seebeck coefficient. One of the advantages in considering semiconducting materials is that they have a much higher Seebeck coefficient than metals. Most commonly TE materials are based upon bismuth telluride and silicongermanium alloy since they give ZT values greater than unity at their optimal temperature.4,5 Other materials are lead telluride,68 skutterudite9 and chalcogenides.10,11

After the discovery of Heusler alloys (HAs), they have become one of the most researched materials among the material science community, since they pose various magnetic and non-magnetic properties applicable for spintronics, solar cells and thermoelectrics.12 Recently they have become the central focus in TE research due to their semiconducting behavior and tunable band gap properties. Among the various HAs, half-HAs drew more attention due to their decent value of thermopower and figure of merit in a moderate temperature range.1320 For example, among the half-Heusler family, XNiSn (X = Zr, Ti, Hf)-based compounds are the most reliable for TEs with maximum ZT values lying in the range of 0.71.5.13,14,1620 However, this value is achieved by various kinds of doping (e.g., mixing X with each other or doping Sb with Sn etc.) or nanostructuring21 approaches etc. The maximum ZT obtained for the pristine alloy of ZrNiSn (no doping) remains low (<0.2).18 Our recent work has theoretically predicted a maximum ZT of 0.46 for the pristine bulk crystal of Bi-based half-HA using first principle simulations.22 Recently, another high value of ZT (1.42) has been reported for Zr-based half-HA at 973 K with a conversion efficiency of 9%.23

On the other hand, full-HAs have received less attention due to their high thermal conductivity and low value of ZT.24 Indeed, in the past years, full-HAs were explored for TE research, in particular those which are Fe2-based2428 and also others.2931 For example, the power factors of bulk Fe2-based alloys24,25 are more (or comparable) than the conventional Bi2Te3 but the ZT value is very low, 0.1, due to the high lattice thermal conductivity (27 W m1 K1 at 300 K).24 However, a recent nanostructured approach improves the value of ZT up to 0.3 in the Fe2VAl compound.26 Also, the TE properties have been investigated under various conditions in full-HAs (e.g., doping of (Co,Ti) in Fe2VAl27 and intrinsic defects in Co2TiZ(Si,Ge,Sn)31) which resulted in changing the TE properties significantly.

However, the quaternary HAs (see description in the next section) have not been explored much (except very few by Bhat et al.,32,33) for TE research and hence need to be investigated further. As per our knowledge, no theoretical or experimental work has been done on exploring the thermoelectric properties for the present alloy.

This article is organized as follows. In Section 2, we describe the crystal structure of a quaternary CoFeTiAl alloy followed by computational details of the electronic structure and TE simulations. The analysis of stability, electronic structure and TE properties is contained in Section 3. Section 4 is devoted to the summary and conclusions of our work.

2 Crystal structure and computational details

The equiatomic quaternary Heusler alloy, XXYZ (where, X, X and Y are the transition metal and Z belongs to non-magnetic element) consists of four interpenetrating face centered cubic (FCC) sublattices crystallized in the Fmm (#216) space group.12 In general, the transition metals, X, X and Y, occupy 4a(0,0,0), 4b(1/2,1/2,1/2) and 4c(1/4,1/4,1/4) whereas, Z occupies the 4d(3/4,3/4,3/4) Wyckoff sites. The occupation of atoms on the lattice sites usually depends upon the electronegativity value.12

Our ab initio simulations are based on Density Functional Theory (DFT) as implemented in the Vienna ab initio Simulation Package (VASP)34 with the projector-augmented wave (PAW) basis.35 For the electronic exchange and correlation functional, we have used the generalized gradient approximations (GGAs) under the scheme of PerdewBurkeErnzerhof (PBE). For a more reliable estimation of the band gap a sophisticated hybrid functional, HeydScuseriaErnzerhof (HSE06)36 has been used. The electronic wave functions are expanded in a plane-wave basis set with a cutoff energy up to 500 eV. The structure is fully relaxed (cell volume, shape and atomic positions) using a conjugate gradient algorithm up to the energy (forces) converging to 106 eV per cell. We have performed the ionic relaxation as well as self-consistent-field calculations using both the PBE and the HSE06 functionals. A mixing parameter of 25% and a screening parameter (controls the short-rangeness of the interaction) of 0.2 1 are used for HSE06 simulation. Brillouin zone sampling was done on a -centered k-mesh. For the self-consistent-field calculations, 243k-points and 63k-points were used for the PBE and the HSE06 functionals, respectively. A relatively denser k-mesh of 463 was used for the thermoelectric study.

Mechanical stability of the alloy has been analyzed by performing lattice dynamics simulations. The simulation gives elastic constants by which one can check the mechanical stability of the alloy by satisfying the BornHuang (BH) criteria.37 Thermodynamic stability has also been investigated by calculating the formation energy (Ef). 3 Results and discussion

3.1 Stability analysis and electronic structure

The thermodynamic stability of the alloy has been investigated by considering different atomic configurations and various magnetic/non-magnetic phases. In particular, we have taken three non-degenerate atomic configurations namely type-1, type-2 and type-3 as shown in Table 1. Corresponding to each atomic configuration, we have further checked the phase stability by taking non-magnetic, ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic phases into account. The non-magnetic phase of type-1 emerged as the energetically most stable configuration. All the different phases (ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic) in type-1 eventually end up with the non-magnetic phase. Whereas, in type-2 and type-3 configurations, the ferromagnetic phase ends up with the antiferromagnetic in which Ti is antiferromagnetically aligned with the other transition elements.

In the type-1 configuration, Co occupies at 4a, Fe at 4b, Ti at 4c and Al at 4d Wyckoff sites (as shown in Fig. 1). Whereas, in type-2 (type-3), Co occupies at 4d (4b), Fe at 4b (4d), Ti at 4c and Al at 4a Wyckoff sites. The advantage of considering the Heusler alloy is that one can easily predict the magnetic and non-magnetic phases by just counting the total number of valence electrons in the primitive cell, by the so called SlaterPauling rule42,43viz. mt = (Zt 24) B. According to formula, the total number of valence electrons in the present system is 24 which leads mt to be zero, indicating towards non-magnetic semiconducting behavior. The formation energy (Ef) of the alloy in its most stable phase is 0.613 eV per atom. The stability checks have been performed using the PBE functional at a relaxed/equilibrium lattice constant (arlp = 5.80 ) which is also consistent with other literature44.

The total electronic density of states (TDOS) and band structure of the alloy have been analyzed using the HSE06 exchangecorrelation functional at a hybrid-relaxed lattice constant (arlp = 5.74 ) shown in Fig. 2. The band gap (Eg) is obtained by calculating the energy difference between the highest occupied valence band and the lowest unoccupied conduction band. In general, the band gap obtained from the conventional PBE is highly underestimated due to the existence of non-physical Coulomb repulsion.4548 This self Coulomb repulsion reduces after including the exact HartreeFock exchange.49 For comparison purposes, the electronic structure obtained by the PBE functional is given in the ESI.50 Hence, in order to calculate the most accurate band gap, a more sophisticated HSE06 functional36 is taken into account. A direct band gap of 1.39 eV is obtained at the point (solid red plot in the right panel) around the Fermi level (EF) and concludes the semiconducting behavior of the alloy (also supported by ref. 44). The total, as well as the atom- and the orbital-projected, density of states are shown in Fig. 2. The electronic states around EF are mostly contributed to by the transition metals. Specifically, the valence electronic states are mainly due to the Co and Fe atoms whereas, the conduction states are contributed to by all the transition elements. The states arising from the d-orbital electrons are mostly dominated over the s and p-orbital states. In the half-metallic quaternary HAs, the band gap in the minority spin channel is due the strong hybridization of d-orbitals of transition elements. Within the crystal field theory, d-orbitals of the transition metals (e.g., Co, Fe and Ti) split into t2g (dxy, dyx, dzx) and eg (dz2,dx2y2) orbitals. In order to discuss the origin of the band gap in the minority spin channel, a schematic representation of possible hybridization in different d-orbitals is shown in Fig. 3. Observing the upper panel of Fig. 3, it is seen that the interacting orbitals (i.e., dz2 and dx2y2) of Co and Fe atoms form a bonding state eg and anti-bonding state eu. Also, the dxy, dyx and dzx orbitals of Co and Fe form t2g bonding and t1u anti-bonding orbitals. The coefficient which arises before e and t denotes the degeneracy of the corresponding orbitals. Observing the lower panel of Fig. 3, it is seen that the eg orbitals of CoFe atoms hybridize with the 3d orbitals(e.g., dz2 and dx2y2) of the Ti atom to form lower lying lower energy orbitals, 2 eg, and upper lying (higher energy) anti-bonding orbitals, 2 eg, that are unoccupied and lie above the Fermi level. The other 3 t2g orbitals of CoFe, hybridize with the dxy, dyx and dzx orbitals of the Ti atom and create six new orbitals, three of which are occupied and bonding whereas the other three are unoccupied and anti-bonding. Finally, the orbitals (i.e., 2 eu and 3 t1u) of CoFe atoms cannot couple with any d-orbital of the Ti atom. The t1u states are occupied and lie below EF whereas, the eu states are just above EF, forming a minority gap state around the Fermi level. There is a total of 12 states below EF, an extra 3 states coming from the deep lying 3p and 1s orbitals. The d-orbitals (t2g and eg) projected density of states from the transition elements are also shown in Fig. 3 (bottom plot).

3.2 Mechanical stability and phonon dispersion

In order to check the mechanical stability of the alloy, elastic constants (Cij) are calculated. The values are, C11 = 365.20 GPa, C12 = 106.46 GPa and C44 = 120.65 GPa. These values clearly satisfy the BH criteria, (C11 C12)/2 > 0, (C11 + 2C12)/3 > 0 and C44 > 0, which indicate the alloy to be mechanically stable.

Phonon dispersion plots for the alloy are shown in Fig. 4. The primitive cell contains 4 atoms, hence there are 12 vibrational modes as shown in Fig. 4 (left panel). Fig. 4 depicts that three are acoustic, whereas the remaining nine modes are optical. We do not find any imaginary/anomaly frequencies along the symmetry points (e.g. W, L, and X) in the first Brillouin zone which indicates the alloy to be mechanically stable. Some of the optical/acoustic modes are degenerate at the point whereas some are well separated along L to W or X to W symmetry points. Due to the comparable masses of the transition elements (Co, Fe and Ti) and significant difference with Al atom, there is a well separated band gap existing among the phonon modes. In the phonon density of states (Ph.DOS) plot (right panel), the Ph.DOS (<8 THz) are mainly because of the heavier transition metals (almost equally contributed by Co, Fe and Ti atoms) whereas, the lighter atom (Al) vibrates with a higher frequency (>10 THz). The optical and acoustic modes are overlapped along L to W and X to W symmetry points because of the comparable masses of transition elements. The mixing of these two (acoustic and optical) modes gives a considerable phononphonon scattering, which results in low thermal conductivity of the compound.

3.3 Thermoelectric properties

The topology of the electronic band structure and the band gap (Eg) play a very significant role in predicting the thermoelectric response of semiconducting compounds. It is generally believed that the flatness of electronic bands (high rise in density of states) leads to a high effective band mass which eventually produces a high Seebeck coefficient.51 However, this also corresponds to the low mobility () since in the parabolic band assumption and in the case of elastic scattering (= e/mI*) is inversely proportional to the transport effective mass, mI*. Furthermore, the scattering time () decreases with the band effective mass when the charge carriers are mainly scattered by the phonons. Low mobility of carriers affects the electrical transport (or conductivity) significantly.

From the band structure plot (Fig. 2), it is seen that the energies of extrema/valley points arising at , X and W of the conduction band are very close, indicating a high degenerate energy level that acts to enhance the electronic transport significantly. Also, the valley points, arising at and X of the valence band are close in energy, causing the hole transport enhancement. Therefore, both the n- and p-type responses of the alloy are expected to be promising for thermoelectric applications.

From Fig. 2, it is clear that the topology of the electronic bands are almost similar in both cases (PBE electronic structure is shown in the ESI). Hence, in order to calculate the more accurate TE response of the alloy, we rigidly shift the Eg obtained from the PBE to match with the Eg obtained from the HSE06 functional. This kind of rigid shift is often used to calculate the TE properties of a material. The absolute value of the Seebeck coefficient i.e., |S| initially increases (for both n- and p-type) up to 400 K due to the extrinsic transport of the majority charge carriers while minority charge carriers start playing a role at higher temperatures. At higher temperatures, the electrons and holes coexist and therefore contribute to S significantly. Hence, the total Seebeck coefficient can be expressed as S = (Snn + Spp)/(n + p). Also, due to opposite sign of S for both electrons and holes, the total S is usually lower than either of the individual contributions, unless the direct band gap is large enough to minimize the minority carrier contribution. Hence, the total S either for n- or p-type decreases at higher temperatures (>400 K). Thus, it is necessary to adjust the carrier concentration to make the semiconducting alloy show predominant n-type or p-type transport properties. The electrical and electronic thermal conductivities (i.e., / vs. T and e/ vs. T) are increasing with T due to thermally excited charge carriers (Fig. 5 and 6). The estimation of from ab initio simulation is challenging due to the various scattering factors. Hence, in general, is estimated by comparing the theoretical / (since the BoltzTraP code gives only the scaled i.e., /) to the experimentally observed value of . Unfortunately, due to unavailability of the experimental , estimation of for the proposed alloy is not possible. However, considering the calculated value of from the other Heusler cases,53,54 we have taken two constant values of , i.e., 1.21 1013 s.53 and 2.45 1013 s.54 to predict the value of ZT. At 1.21 1013 s, the obtained value of ZT is 0.62(0.50) for n(p)-type behavior at 900 K. However, for 2.45 1013 s, we obtained the highest value of ZT as 0.75(0.66) for n(p)-type behavior at 900 K.

4 Summary and conclusions

Using ab initio density functional and the Boltzmann transport theory, we present a systematic study of the electronic structure, mechanical/thermodynamic stability and the transport properties of a CoFeTiAl Heusler alloy. We propose a semiconducting quaternary Heusler alloy, CoFeTiAl, as a promising candidate for thermoelectric applications at moderate temperatures. The band gap plays a very crucial role in predicting the thermoelectric properties, hence we have used a range-separated hybrid(HSE06) functional in predicting the accurate band gap of the alloy. We have predicted the upper limit of ZT for both n-type and p-type channels. The upper limit of ZT (i.e., ZTe) saturates to unity for both n- and p-type channels for a whole range of temperatures, probably because of the existence of multiple-valleys at various symmetry points in the conduction and the valence bands. It is also observed that not only the band topology, but also the band gap, affects the transport properties very significantly. Hence, the estimation should be very accurate to predict the reliable value of ZT. We have also predicted the corresponding carrier concentration (n). The optimal value for n is 4.32 1018 cm3 and 2.24 1018 cm3 for n- and p-type at 900 K, respectively. The lattice part of thermal conductivity is calculated using a more accurate ShengBTE code based upon Boltzmann transport equation for phonons. The minimum value of l is 3.79 (W m1 K1) obtained at 900 K. After considering the two different values of relaxation time () (1.21 and 2.45 1013 s), we have obtained the highest value of ZT as 0.75(0.66) at a later value of for the n(p)-type behavior at 900 K. Reduction in lattice thermal conductivity can further be made by considering various mechanisms e.g., intrinsic defects or external doping which could further enhance the value of ZT.

Hence, we expect our present analysis will pave a new direction to our experimenting colleagues, to carry out the experimental research of the proposed alloy.
