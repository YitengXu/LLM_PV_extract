Theoretical investigations of the small molecular acceptor materials based on oligothiophene – naphthalene diimide in organic solar cells†

College of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Southwest University, Chongqing 400715, China. E-mail: liming@swu.edu.cn

Abstract

A series of novel acceptor–donor–acceptor–donor–acceptor (A2–D–A1–D–A2) structured acceptor oligomers with naphthalene diimide (NDI) as centre and acceptor unit (A1), oligothiophenes (T = 1, 2, 3, 4) as π-bridges (D), and 2-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)rhodanine (DCRD) as terminal groups (A2) have been investigated using quantum chemistry and Marcus theory in this research. Compared with NDI-2T1Me—NDI-2T4Me, the NDI-2T1DCRD—NDI-2T4DCRD exhibit stronger and wider absorption peaks because of the increase of the electron withdrawing ability of the electron deficient units. In particular for the NDI-2T3DCRD, the electron mobility (μe = 5.71 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 S−1) was accurately predicted quantitatively using first principle simulation. The blend of poly(3-hexylthiophene) with NDI-2T3DCRD as an active layer exhibits obviously strong and wide light absorption, which form a perfectly complementary absorption. The results indicate that the incorporation of DCRD as acceptor units into D–A–D type oligomers has been proven to create promising candidates for high efficiency acceptor materials for organic solar cells (OSCs). Overall, this research may provide a theoretical guidance for designing small molecular acceptors for OSC applications.

1 Introduction

Solar energy has the advantages of being clean, unlimited, renewable, and readily available, and has already been extensively studied with various energy resources as an alternative to other exhausted energy resources.1,2 As the most important applications of solar energy, organic solar cells (OSCs) have advantages in solution processing, are potentially lightweight, have low cost fabrication, and have flexibility, all of which are promising cost-effective alternatives to inorganic solar cells.3,4 Over the past few years, significant progress has been made with OSCs materials based on a bulk heterojunction (BHJ) structure with a conjugated organic semiconductor as electron donor and fullerene derivative as electron acceptor, and these materials have achieved a dramatic improvement in power conversion efficiency (PCE) of over 10% for polymer-based OSC architectures. For example, Liu et al.5 developed the multiple polymer: fullerene combinations via the formation of a near ideal polymer/fullerene morphology with the PCE reaching up to 10.8% and fill factors (FF) up to 77%. Chen et al.6 achieved 10.1% PCE by implementing deterministic aperiodic nanostructure (DAN) patterning for broadband, self-enhanced light absorption with optimum charge extraction in the single junction polymer solar cells.

However, the fullerene derivative acceptor materials have many considerable drawbacks, which considerably limit the further development of PCEs in OSCs. Such drawbacks are: (1) negligible light absorption in the visible near infrared (NIR) region,7–10 and (2) a narrow range of accessible lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy levels limiting the accessible photovoltage. In comparison, non-fullerene acceptor materials (including polymers and small molecules) are superior to conventional fullerene materials and have easily adjustable energy levels, simple synthesis, and a low cost of production.11–13 More importantly, this type of material has a wider light absorption in the solar visible region.14,15 In addition, the small molecular oligomers have more advantages over polymer materials, such as the definite molecular weight, well defined molecular structure, and high purity without batch to batch variations.16–19 They have easily adjustable highest occupied molecular orbitals (HOMO), and LUMO levels, medium energy gaps (Eg), as well as promising carrier (hole or electron) transport properties.20,21 Recently, Zhao et al.22 reported a small molecular compound 3,9-bis(2-methylene-(3-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)-indanone))-5,5,11,11-tetrakis(4-hexylphenyl)-dithieno[2,3-d:2′,3′-d′]-s-indaceno[1,2-b:5,6-b′]dithiophene (ITIC)23,24 which was used to replace the fullerene-based acceptors, a state-of-the-art PCE of 11.21% for polymer solar cells (PSCs) was demonstrated. This indicates that small molecular oligomers have a promising potential for replacing fullerene derivatives and enhancing the performance of OSC devices.

Alternations of electron rich units (D) and electron deficient units (A) in designing a small molecular backbone are an effective strategy to improve the performance of OSCs.25 Kim et al.26 achieved the maximum photocurrent response (13%) in the D–A–A type ternary cells, and Jung and Jo27 have shown a PCE of 5.0% with small D–A–D–A–D type molecules composed of benzothiadiazole and diketopyrrolopyrrole as non-fullerene electron acceptors. This demonstrates that it is an effective strategy to improve the PCE when the small molecular acceptors are designed with suitable D-units and A-units. Lately, aromatic diimide derivatives [such as naphthalene diimide (NDI) and perylene diimide] are promising n-type materials for fabricating OSCs because of their excellent optoelectronic properties, good oxidative and thermal stabilities, large electron affinities, and high electron mobilities.28–30 Interestingly, it is found that the reported acceptors based on aromatic diimide also have the D–A structures, which have complementary electronic and optical properties with polymer donors, narrow band gaps and high electron carrier mobilities.31–33 Earmme et al.,33 and Ahmed et al.34 have made some achievements using the NDI-based oligomers as acceptors. However, the solar cells generally exhibit low efficiencies (1–6%) when used with aromatic diimide derivatives as acceptor materials.33,35 In order to enhance the performance of D–A oligomers, it is necessary to understand how to simultaneously tune their electronic, optical, and charge transport properties.

In this research, a series of new A2–D–A1–D–A2 small molecules were designed using a strong electron withdrawing group of 2-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)rhodanine (DCRD)36,37 as the A-units (A2) based on the π-conjugated oligomers34 with D–A configurations which are composed of a central electron withdrawing group of NDI (A1) and an electron donating group of oligothiophenes (D) (in Scheme 1) to further assess the criteria for new acceptor materials. The aim is to tune the HOMO/LUMO energy levels, to improve the light absorption properties so that there is a good match with the donor absorption bands, and to enhance the intermolecular carrier mobilities of the acceptor materials by increasing the electron withdrawing ability of the electron deficient unit of the D–A oligomers. The D–A architectures of the oligothiophene functionalized NDI molecules possessed all the advantages of the small molecules which were described previously,38–42 and they will be further applicable to tuning the material properties after modifications as will be described in this paper. According to Deng et al.43 the electron mobility of NDI-2T3DCRD (μe = 5.71 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 S−1) was accurately predicted quantitatively using a first principles simulation.

2 Computational methodologies

2.1 The frontier molecular orbital theory and spectrum simulation

In this work, the ground state geometry optimizations and spectra simulations of all the investigated oligomers were performed using density functional theory44,45 (DFT) and time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT), respectively. The spin-restricted DFT and the unrestricted DFT were used to optimize the ground state geometries and the ion state geometries,46 respectively. All of the alkyl branched chains of the polymers were replaced by methyl groups (Me) to simplify the calculation because the alkyl branched chains have little effect on the optoelectronic properties.47,48 Several hybrid functionals (B3LYP,49 M06,50 and PBE0 51) at the 6-31G** and 6-311G** basis set level were used to obtain the appropriate energy level, which is consistent with the experimental value as much as possible. The test results are summarized in the ESI (Table S1†). As shown in Table S1,† the calculated HOMO level of NDI-2T3H at the PBE0/6-311G** level (−5.64 eV) is in accordance with the experimental data (−5.6 eV). Furthermore, all the structures used for optimization have no imaginary frequencies, which suggest that all the investigated structures have the minima on the potential energy surfaces. For the optical properties, we chose four hybrid functionals (B3LYP, PBE0, BMK52 and CAM-B3LYP53) at the 6-311G** basis set level to simulate the absorption spectra of NDI-2T3H on the basis of the optimized ground state structure. It can be seen from Table S2 (ESI†) that the maximum absorbance wavelength of NDI-2T3H at the BMK/6-311G** level (599 nm) agrees well with the experimental result (607 nm), and the peak pattern is also consistent with that of the ref. 34. Based on the previous test, the PBE0/6-311G** level was selected to optimize the ground structures and the BMK/6-311G** level was chosen to simulate the absorption spectra of all the oligomers. The DFT and TD-DFT calculations were carried out using the Gaussian 09 program.54

The reorganization energy is defined as the energy difference between the charged and neutral systems at the two different geometries. 3.3 Charge transport related properties

When the promising candidates for OSCs materials are designed, understanding the relationship between the molecular structure and the charge transport properties of the materials is essential. It is generally thought that charge transport includes the hole transport and electron transport. After the excitons dissociate at the interface layer, the hole and electron will be transported together with the donor and acceptor to the respective electrodes. In the process of transportation, the mobilities of the hole and electron are very important for the development of the performance of OSCs. Reports have demonstrated that the hole mobility of the donor material plays a crucial role in the organophotovoltaic performance.106 In the same way, the high electron mobility of the acceptor material in the electron transport process enhances the improvement of the PCEs. 4 Conclusions

In summary, the properties of electronic and optical absorption of NDI-based oligomers acceptors (NDI-2T1DCRD—NDI-2T4DCRD) and their performances in OSC applications have been discussed with reference to quantum-chemical calculations and Marcus theory. As can be seen from the calculated results, increasing the electron withdrawing ability of electron deficient units not only extends the intra-molecular π conjugation and yields a stronger and broader light absorption in the visible and NIR, but also enhances the exciton separation efficiency at the donor/acceptor interface and the electron mobility. Particularly, the NDI-2T3DCRD has a promising charge transfer rate (5.71 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 S−1) and can effectively complement the light absorption of donor (P3HT) materials. This result indicates that adjusting the electron withdrawing ability of terminal groups in NDI-based type oligomers acceptors is a feasible approach to improve the electronic and optical properties of the NDI-based oligomers' performance. It is concluded that the NDI-2T3DCRD may be a good candidate for acceptor materials in OSC applications and gives a design guideline for designing efficient oligomer acceptors for OSC applications.
