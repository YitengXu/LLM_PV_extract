Electrospun Cu2ZnSnS4 microfibers with strong (112) preferred orientation: fabrication and characterization

Chunhong Mua, Yuanqiang Song*a, Aifang Liua, Xiaoning Wanga, Jiarui Hua, Hong Jia and Hongping Zhangb
aSchool of Energy Science and Engineering, University of Electronic Science and Technology of China, Chengdu 610054, People's Republic of China. E-mail: yuanqiangsong@uestc.edu.cn
bSchool of Materials Science and Engineering, Southwest University of Science and Technology, Mianyang 621010, People's Republic of China

Received 21st November 2014 , Accepted 19th January 2015

First published on 19th January 2015

Abstract

In this study, Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) microfibers were fabricated using a non-vacuum method of electrospinning following vulcanization process. CZTS fibers were obtained via the vulcanization of the electrospun precursor fibers at 500 C (CZTS-500), 550 C (CZTS-550), and 600 C (CZTS-600), in sulfur ambient conditions. Samples were characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) equipped with energy-dispersive spectroscopy (EDS), X-ray diffraction (XRD), XRD mapping scan, Raman spectrum and UV-vis absorption. Polycrystalline CZTS fibers with kesterite crystallization were formed in CZTS-500, whereas higher temperature vulcanization in CZTS-550 led to a strong preferential crystallization along CZTS [112] crystal direction around fiber surface, which is definitely confirmed by XRD mapping scan. More interestingly, the band gap value (Eg), which was 1.48 eV for CZTS-500, reduced to 1.43 eV for CZTS-550. Eg reduction may be related to lattice distortion induced by stress or strain around fiber surface, and can be beneficial for broadening optical absorption range thus increasing the efficiency of CZTS-based solar cells.

1. Introduction

Third generation solar cells based on new type of thin film semiconductors such as CuInxGa1xSe2 (CIGS),1 CuInS2 (CIS),2 Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS),3 are promising candidates for low cost commercialized solar cell products. CIGS is one of the most successful thin-film photovoltaic materials, based on which the fabricated cells have recently reached a power conversion efficiency of nearly 20%.4,5 However, the four rare elements that CIGS is composed of make CIGS-based cell production very expensive. On the other hand, the four elements comprising CZTS are naturally abundant and environmentally friendly, which makes CZST one of the most promising candidates for large-scale commercial applications.

As a p-type direct band gap semiconductor, CZTS with the crystal structure and optical properties similar to those of CIGS, is also an excellent absorbance material for solar spectra (its band gap is about 1.5 eV and absorbance coefficient about 104 cm1). However, the low photoelectric conversion efficiency68 of CZTS-based cells is one of the biggest obstacles, limiting its commercial applications. The highest efficiencies reported to date were obtained from the cells using a CZTS absorbed layer with large grain size and high film quality.6,7 The grain size grows nearly to 2 m in Cu2ZnSn(S, Se)4 (CZTSSe) solar cells with a high efficiency beyond 11%.9 The recently reported highest efficiency measured in CZTSSe solar cells can also be partially attributed to fine crystallization with large grain size.10 However, the efficiencies of CZTS or CZTS based solar cells are nowhere near those of CIGS.11

Previous studies have confirmed grain boundary (GB) as the reason for the high efficiency of CIGS solar cells.1216 With the aid of these GBs, in the presence of an electric field in the vicinity of the GBs, minority carriers move much easier to the n-type CdS and ZnO layers, leading to an enhanced minority carrier collection.13 GBs in CZTS, however, cause two contradictory effects on the efficiency. On one hand, higher positive surface potential has been found in the vicinity of the GBs in CZTS,17 which can benefit the minority carriers collection as well as the efficiency. On the other hand, the GBs in CZTS create more defect levels in the energy gap of CZTS compared to CIGS.18 These defects would be detrimental to the charge separation by promoting the recombination of the excited electron and hole carriers, and therefore reduce the solar cell's efficiency. All things considered, GBs would be one of the main factors, which limits the efficiency of CZTS solar cell, according to recently experimental report of the limited efficiency of CZTS solar cells compared with CIGS. Based on this speculation, either the effective passivation treatment of GBs or decreasing the GBs density would be needed to improve the efficiency.

Nanoarray pn junctions has been adopted in silicon nanowire-based solar cells,19 for the purpose of efficiency enhancement.20,21 The mechanism of the enhancement of photoelectric conversion efficiency in nano-structured solar cells is supposed to be (a) nano-structured cells' surface would trap much more light with a highly reduced reflectance, and (b) much higher specific surface area would be obtained in the nano-structured pn junctions, for the utilization of photo absorption as well as photoelectric generation, leading to higher photoelectric conversion efficiency. This efficiency-enhancement method is readily applied to CZTS solar cells, provided that the technology for production of nano-structured CZTS can be developed. According to previous report, CuSe and CuInSe2 nano-bundles have been successfully fabricated via a facile and promising solvothermal method.22 CZTS nanorods have also been successfully synthesized, using anodized aluminum as the growth mask or template.2325 For the first time, CZTS fibers were fabricated by Hsu et al. using electrospinning process.26 As a low-cost, non-vacuum and productive method widely used for the production of nano-structured fibers, electrospinning deserves more attention, focusing on the fabrication and growth mechanism of CZTS nanofibers. In this study, single-phased CZTS microfibers have been successfully synthesized via electrospinning following vulcanization approach. While well crystallized CZTS fibers in kesterite can be obtained by vulcanization at 500 C, higher vulcanization temperature at 550 C leads to strong [112] preferential crystallization in kesterite [112] direction around CZTS fiber surface. Moreover, the band gap (Eg) of CZTS fibers reduced from 1.48 eV to 1.43 eV coupled with [112] preferential orientation growth. The preferential growth as well as reduced Eg are beneficial for exploring high-efficiency CZTS-based solar cells.

2. Experimental details

Cu(CH3COO)2, Zn(CH3COO)2 and SnCl4 were weighed and mixed according to the stoichiometry of Cu2ZnSnS4 compound, and the mixture was dissolved along with a certain amount of polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) in ethylene glycol monomethyl ether constant with stirring to form a transparent solution. The solution is pumped into a plastic syringe, and then ejected out of a metal needle with a diameter of 0.6 mm. The precursor fibers are collected on Si (100) substrates connected with the ground. The distance between the needle and the collector plane is kept as 5 centimeter, and a high voltage of 10 kV is applied between them.

The vulcanization is carried out in a tube furnace. The precursor microfibers on silicon substrates are first dried at 80 C for 2 h. In order to eliminate any possible carbon residue in the final products, the dried precursor fibers are annealed at 300 C for 30 minutes in air before vulcanization. The vulcanization process is carried out at 500 C (CZTS-500), 550 C (CZTS-550), and 600 C (CZTS-600), for 1 h in sulfur ambient. During the vulcanization process Ar is used as the protective gas and carrier.

3. Characterization

The morphologies of the CZTS microfibers were characterized by SEM (JEOL Ltd.). The compositions as well as the elemental ratio of each element was obtained by EDS equipped on SEM. Crystallographic information on CZTS crystals was obtained via XRD carried out on Bede D1 diffractometer operating in the Bragg configuration, using a monochromatized Cu K radiation ( = 1.540596 ). Raman spectra measurements were performed using a Renishaw via a Raman spectroscope in air at room temperature. The 325 nm line was used as the excitation source with a spectral resolution of 4 cm1. The power density was kept under 5 mW, which is low enough to avoid heating effects on the samples. Optical absorption properties were characterized in the wavelength range of 4001200 nm via a UV-vis spectrometer (UV3600, SHIMADZU). In order to eliminate the scattering noise induced by non-uniform fibers structure, the reflection mode was adopted in optical measurement using an integrating sphere accessory.

4. Results and discussion

4.1 Micro morphologies of CZTS fibers

Fig. 1(a)(d) and (f) show the morphologies of the electrospun precursor fibers, CZTS-300, CZTS-500, CZTS-550, and CZTS-600, respectively. Evidently, all the samples except CZTS-600 are composed of micro-fibers with varied diameters. The diameter of the CZTS fibers first decreases (as shown in Fig. 1(b)) and then increases with increasing annealing temperature (as shown in Fig. 1(c) and (d)). The precursor fibers shown in Fig. 1(a) are straight without any branch, and the diameter distribution is almost uniform with an average diameter of submicron. The diameter is highly reduced by annealing at 300 C as shown in Fig. 1(b), but the fibers still remain straight. For sample CZTS-500 as shown in Fig. 1(c), the diameter increases with the combination and interconnection of adjacent fibers, leading to a porous netlike morphology, which is also the case for CZTS-550 with larger diameter. However, a higher annealing temperature would cause evident surface cracking due to excessive contraction, as shown in the case of CZTS-600. The much detailed morphology of the CZTS-550 surface is shown in Fig. 1(e), from which many micro gibbosities can be seen. The gibbosities may be crystal grains formed by vulcanization.

4.2 Crystalline characterization

Fig. 2 shows the X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of CZTS fibers annealed at varied temperatures. Evidently, as shown in Fig. 2(a), annealing at 300 C is enough to lead to partial crystallization with the emergence of CZTS (112) and (220) peaks. For CZTS-500 obtained at higher vulcanization temperature, its XRD profile matched well with the standard CZTS PDF card of kesterite crystal phase, confirming high crystallinity and phase formation of CZTS fibers. For CZTS-550, with the continued rising of the vulcanization temperature, however, peak (112) was amazingly enhanced and the other peaks distinctively diminished. In fact, it seems only one diffractive peak emerges at the position of 2 = 28.35, which is corresponding to the (112) peak of CZTS. The shape of the (112) peak is very narrow with a high intensity of more than 2000. The results definitely indicate a preferential growth along [112] crystal direction in CZTS-550. The FWHM of (112) peak obtained from the rocking curve is 0.087 degree, as shown in the inset of Fig. 2(b). Based on Scherrer-equation:

Fig. 2 XRD of (a) CZTS-300 and CZTS-500 as indicated in the figure, and (b) CZTS-550, in which the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of CZTS (112) is 0.087 as shown in the inset.

To date, the kesterite crystalline CZTS microfibers have confirmed that the higher vulcanization temperature can lead to obvious preferential growth in (112) direction. Z. Q. Li et al.27 have successfully grown CZTS nanowires with polycrystal crystallization using CuSe nanowire bundles as self-sacrificial templates. Koichiro Oishi et al.28 have recently reported an orientational growth of CZTS films in (200) direction with an increased growth temperature. In their experiments the CZTS films were grown on Si(100) by vacuum evaporation. However, the essential mechanism contributing to the good crystallization and strongly preferential growth of electrospun CZTS fibers may be different. The minimization of surface energy may contribute to (112) preferential growth in the CZTS fiber system. It can be deduced that the CZTS (112) crystal surface is the easiest growth surface with the lowest free energy for the growth of electrospun CZTS microfibers, thus leading to a (112) preferential crystallization in the radial direction around the fiber surface.

In order to verify this hypothesis, XRD mapping scan around one CZTS fiber has been done according to the configuration shown in Fig. 3, and the corresponding results are shown in Fig. 4.


Fig. 3 Configuration of XRD mapping scan around one CZTS fiber. During the process, for each stepping rotation () of the substrate, there is a 2 scan.

Fig. 4 XRD mapping scanned around one CZTS fiber according to the configuration shown in Fig. 3.

As can clearly be seen from Fig. 4, for each stepping rotation around one fiber, there emerges only one peak at 2 = 28.35, which corresponds to the CZTS (112) diffraction peak. In other words, there is only one preferential crystallization along CZTS (112) out of the surface around the CZTS fiber. The fact can be reasonably explained by energy minimization principle in fiber-composed CZTS films. The specific surface area as well as surface energy for a crystal fiber with diameter in micro or even nano scale is very high, so the minimum energy plane is the easiest crystallization and growth direction. In CZTS system with a kesterite structure, the thermodynamically preferred growth direction will be along (112) orientation, which is the close-packed plane in CZTS system. Herein, in the CZTS fibers in micro or nano scale, the energetic driving force would be much higher due to the high surface energy. In fact, several groups have reported the preferential crystallization effect along (112) plane in CZTS films.28,29

4.3 Raman and XPS analysis

Since it is hard to completely identify the CZTS phase based only on XRD results,30,31 Raman spectroscopy was recorded on the CZTS fibers for further identification as well as clarification of possible secondary phase. As can be seen from the Raman spectra shown in Fig. 5, a typical CZTS characteristic peak at about 337 cm1 was observed, demonstrating CZTS phase formation in all three samples of CZTS-500, CZTS-550, CZTS-600.32 Furthermore, a little uplift at around 286 and 363 cm1 in the shoulder of peak 337 cm1 in CZTS-550 and CZTS-600 further confirms the phase formation of CZTS compounds.32
Fig. 5 Raman spectra of CZTS fibers obtained via vulcanization with varied temperature.

The XPS characterization demonstrated very similar spectra, as shown in Fig. 6, for all the vulcanized CZTS samples. A linear background subtraction was performed on the raw data, and the peaks fitted using Gaussian method. As expected, all data from the spectra confirmed the existence of Cu1+, Zn2+, Sn4+, and S2, for the four elements in CZTS fibers, according to the previous reports33,34 and the XPS database. The existence of Cu1+ is reasonably attributed by the two narrow peaks of Cu 2p at about 932.9 eV and 952.5 eV as shown in Fig. 6(a), Zn2+ is confirmed by Zn 2p located at 1022.5 eV and 1045 eV in Fig. 6(b), Sn4+ by Sn 3d at 486 eV and 494.8 eV, and S2 in sulfide phase by S 2p located at 161.5 eV and 162.8 eV, respectively.35

The curves and linear fit are depicted in Fig. 7, in which the film thickness d is about 100 m obtained from its cross-section SEM image shown in the inset. As can be seen, Eg for all samples have a value of about 1.5 eV, which is agreement with previous reports. More precisely, there is a little difference of Eg for CZTS samples annealed at varied temperatures, i.e., while Eg for CZTS-500 is almost 1.48 eV,26,33,38 it reduces to 1.43 eV for CZTS-550. Eg variation can generally be attributed to either secondary phase formation,39 elemental substitution,40 or crystal distortion.41,42 The mechanism corresponding to Eg reduction observed in orientationally crystallized CZTS fibers may be related with strain or stress induced lattice distortion caused by crystal plane bending around the surface of CZTS micro or nano fibers, which needs further and more detailed investigations. Nevertheless, Eg reduction would broaden the optical absorption spectrum, thus increasing the photoelectric efficiency of CZTS-based solar cell.

5. Conclusion

In this study, Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) microfibers were fabricated using a non-vacuum method of electrospinning following vulcanization process. Low temperature vulcanization at 500 C produced polycrystallized CZTS fibers with kesterite crystallization, while higher temperature vulcanization at 550 C led to a strong preferential crystallization along CZTS (112) crystal direction around the fiber surface. Correspondingly, the band gap value Eg, which was 1.48 eV for CZTS-500, reduced to 1.43 eV for CZTS-550. Preferential crystallization as well as Eg reduction shall be beneficial for exploring high-qualified CZTS using for CZTS-based solar cells with broadening optical absorption range and increased efficiency.
