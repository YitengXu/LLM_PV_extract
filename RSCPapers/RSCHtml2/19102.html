Abstract

Electroluminescence (EL), the light emission under an applied electrical potential, is one of several key light emitting techniques applied over a vast range of applications. Alternative Current Electroluminescent (ACEL) is one of the major light emitting techniques discussed under EL. Even though it possesses some intrinsic advantages over Light Emitting Diodes (LEDs) and Light Emitting Cells (LEC), it has not been thoroughly discussed like LEDs and most of the time has been kept under the shadow of LEDs. On the other hand, flexible light emitting devices have become an emerging trend in the field as they are widely applied in consumer electronics and wearable devices, where an ACEL technique can make a significant impact for their development. This review discusses the ACEL technique in detail covering different device architectures, materials and fabrication methods highlighting the use of electrospinning techniques. In the second part, the review briefly summarizes research works in the flexible light emitting field, covering both optical and mechanical performances.

Introduction

Light has become a remarkable part of human life as people seek different yet efficient light emitting sources throughout civilization. With the invention of electricity, people have explored light emission from extremely heated metallic filaments though it was a byproduct with low efficiency. Later, research on different semiconducting materials under electrical potential has led the way towards identification of cold light emission and opened the field of electroluminescence. Electroluminescence (EL) principle is the method of light emission as a result of an applied electrical potential or a flow of electric current across an active material. Here, based on the process of light generation within the active material, the EL principle is further divided into different categories where ACEL and LED outshine all other mechanisms due to their high efficiencies and wider applicability.

This electroluminescence phenomenon has been previously identified as cold emission of light and has been on the discussion table since the early 1900s where it is believed that George Destriau first introduced the term electroluminescence in 1935.1 He reported the illuminating behavior of ZnS (zinc sulphide) powders soaked in castor oil upon the application of a high electric potential. However, throughout history, the term Electroluminescence Display (ELD) has been shared among four types of devices1 namely, powder phosphor devices, thin-film electroluminescence devices (TFEL), thick-dielectric electroluminescence device and LEDs. Later, in 1955, a review published by Piper and Williams2 reviewed available electroluminescence techniques and proposed three possible mechanisms of excitation: field ionization, ionization by impact and charge carrier injection which later was trimmed down to two basic operations: high-field EL and injection EL.3

Luminous behavior under a comparatively large electric field is defined as high-field EL principle and typically operated under the application of large alternative currents (AC). The earliest stage of EL, which used powder phosphor material, is currently known as powder EL and categorized under high-field EL. In addition to that, TFEL devices are also powered by an AC supply and listed under high-field EL. In contrast, injection EL behavior has been observed from forward biased pn junctions i.e., from LEDs. The properties of light output are controlled by the diode material and wide band semiconductor diodes produce light output within the visible spectrum.3

Even though there are number of review articles summarizing LED working principles and current trends,48 there are few recent discussions about the ACEL technique. Given that properties and advantages are shadowing some aspects in LED-based light emission and displays, the ACEL technique should be discussed in detail covering its materials and fabrication techniques. At the same time, with current trends on flexible electronics, it is important to understand the role of light emitting devices in that field along with their challenges and opportunities for development. Hence, this review will be a guide for a better and sound understanding of ACEL devices and will provide opportunities to identify and tackle challenges in flexible light emitting devices in general.

In this review, authors discuss the ACEL technique in detail covering its mechanism, materials and fabrication methods while briefing LED principles for comparison. Under the discussion of materials, authors briefly looked into the applicability of the electrospinning technique in each discussed subsection with the intention of highlighting the electrospinning technique as a possible candidate for ACEL fabrication. As the second part of this review, authors summarized different research works discussed under flexible light emitting devices. As a quick yet detailed and comprehensive analysis, different research findings have been tabulated highlighting key mechanical and optical outputs of each at the end of the respective sections.

Light emitting principles

Based on this understanding, the field of EL-based displays has evolved throughout the years developing efficient and attractive designs. Current trends in display and lighting technology are mainly governed by two major principles: high-field EL (ACEL) and LED based displays. The operating mechanisms of each of those two principles are quite different from each other and have their own advantages and disadvantages under different operating conditions. A brief introduction on their operation principles and comparative advantages is discussed in the following section.

Light emitting diodes

Light Emitting Diodes (LEDs) are simple semiconductor pn junctions, which were first reported by a Russian inventor Oleg Losev9 in 1920. Like in any other pn junction diodes, a LED allows the current flow towards only one direction which subsequently allows the junction material to emit light under a forward bias condition. The LED is often considered as a point illuminous source since the diode occupies less than 1 mm2 of space. For applications and commercialization, it is built up with electrodes and a lens for light scattering.

The luminous behavior of a LED is explained as injection electroluminescence where the luminescence is produced due to the injection of minority carriers in the pn junction.10 In a typical LED, a positively doped semiconductor layer lies in contact with a negatively doped semiconductor layer. Under thermal equilibrium (unbiased state), a depletion region is created between these two layers (pn junction) forming a diffusion potential (Vd). This potential acts as an insulator layer between them and prevents any further current flow. When the junction is forward biased, (positive voltage is applied to p-side and negative voltage is applied to n-side), holes from p-side are pushed to the opposite side while electrons from n-side are pushed in the opposite direction. Successively, when a hole and an electron combine, they emit optical radiation. Fig. 1 illustrates a simple energy diagram of a LED under an unbiased condition and forward biased conditions.

According to the basis of quantum theory, during this recombination process, electrons directly jump from the conduction band to the valence band. Since electrons are jumping from a higher energy band to a lower energy band, they emit the excess amount of energy as radiation. In this situation, the excess amount is equal to the bandgap energy (Eg) of the junction. This provides a huge advantage for applicability of LEDs in various application fields. As a result, by selecting suitable materials, the wavelength of the output radiation can be manipulated easily from the infra-red region to different colors within the visible region and even to the ultraviolet spectrum as well.

Since the device structure replicates the structure of a diode, they both possess the same IV characteristic curves. Under a forward bias state, when the bias voltage is sufficiently large enough to overtake the depletion region barrier, current flows through and emits light. Typically, the forward bias voltage of a pn junction is in the range of 12 V and hence a low voltage DC (direct current) can easily power up a LED to its full brightness.

However, LEDs possess several intrinsic disadvantages as well. The fabrication method is one such key limitation of LEDs. There are number of different methods available for pn junction fabrications like Metal Organic Chemical Vapour Deposition (MOCVD), Hydride Vapour Phase Epitaxy (HVPE), Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE) and others.10,11 A key similarity among these methods is the need of special environmental conditions. They need specially controlled environments or chambers to fabricate LEDs which ultimately results in complex manufacturing processes.3,6 On the other hand, the efficiency of a LED is improved by introducing several additional functional layers like a hole injection layer, hole transporting layer or hole blocking layer to the basic configuration.6,12,13 This has further enhanced the complexity of LED fabrication processes.

Moreover, since light emission occurs at the junction, it is very necessary to protect the junction and light structure from environmental variables like temperature, humidity, dust etc.10 This has raised the requirement of packaging against any potential environmental factors which result in another complex process step for fabrication processes. Additionally, it affects the ruggedness of the overall device as well.

These limitations have compromised the use of LEDs in large area light applications and a number of outdoor applications.

Alternative current electroluminescence (ACEL)

In contrast with LED technology, ACEL works based on a high-field EL principle. This phenomenon was first identified by Destriau in 1935.1 During his study, Destriau suspended his phosphor powders (Cu doped ZnS) in castor oil while insulating that from its electrodes by means of mica sheets.14 The device replicated a capacitor, where the phosphor material is separated from electrodes by an insulating layer. Upon the application of a high AC voltage, he observed light emission from his device which led the way for an understanding of the EL phenomenon under a high electric field, and the device's structure made it possible to develop thinner light emitting devices covering wider areas.

A typical device structure consists of an opaque conductor; first dielectric layer, a phosphor layer, second dielectric layer and a transparent electrode in sequence (Fig. 2(a)). Over the past years, several alternations have been developed for the device structure and will be discussed in another section. When an AC voltage is applied across its two terminals, the illuminance centers within the phosphor layer excite and emit light.3,1416 In order to understand the working principle, the whole process is divided into a few steps:16 (1) electron injection, (2) electron transport, (3) impact excitation and (4) radiative recombination and optical outcoupling, which is illustrated as in Fig. 2(b).17


Fig. 2 (a) An example of an electroluminescence device representing capacitive structure (phosphor material is sandwiched between two MgF2 insulating layers). (b) Operating principle of a typical ACEL device with steps. (Reproduced with permission,17 copyright 2004 Springer Nature).

Upon application of sufficient AC voltage, a high electric field is developed within the phosphor layer. When the electric field inside the phosphor is large enough, electrons trapped at the insulatorphosphor interface are injected into the phosphor layer.3 The injection is governed by different mechanisms and at higher electrical fields, tunneling emission has become predominant.3 These injected electrons are then transported to the opposite electrode across the phosphor layer. As those electrons accelerate across the phosphor layer, they impact and excite luminance centers (luminance impurities) of the phosphor layer to their excited stage (E) from ground state (G) (Fig. 2(b)). As those excited luminance centers return to their respective ground states (G), the excess energy is released in the form of photon emission.16 As the excited luminance particles reach the ground state, the accelerated electrons are trapped at the opposite phosphorinsulator interface.

Threshold voltage (Vth) is one key parameter defining the performances of an EL device. Threshold voltage is defined as the voltage at which the luminance output of 1 cd m2 is achieved.18 In general, the threshold voltage is considered as the point when the electron injection from an insulatorphosphor interface starts and is a function of dielectric properties of the insulator layer and the phosphor layer. After this point, the luminosity increases exponentially until it hits its saturation value. Another parameter which defines the performance is L40, which is defined as the luminosity of the ACEL device at 40 V above its threshold voltage value.18 This voltage value is an important parameter since most EL devices are operated at this voltage value. The peak output wavelength, or the color output of an ACEL device, is the next important factor to note. Since the light emission is dependent on the excitation and relaxation of luminescence particles within the phosphor layer, different phosphor materials (especially different luminescence impurities) result in different colors.19 Metal sulfides doped with transition metals serve as the phosphor layer for most EL devices and the transition metal works as the luminescence center since the sulfide provides the host matrix. The luminance intensity of an EL device is basically controlled by the applied voltage and frequency. For some phosphor particles, the peak emission wavelength shows a slight blue shift as the frequency of the applied signal increases.20,21

One of the very important characteristics of an ACEL device is its ability to be formed into a very thin structure. Submicron range active layer thickness allows a device to made into a thin structure as it enhances its flexibility. ACEL devices are surface light sources where the emission is uniform across the whole surface area. When compared with a point light source like LED, this is vital for wide area lighting and display applications. Moreover, the emissive nature of the display provides wider viewing angles as well.15 Above all these characteristics, the ruggedness of the EL lamp shines. Since electron injection at the insulatorphosphor interface doesnt depend on the environmental temperature, the operating temperature window of an EL display can be between 60 C to even up to 95 C.22 The working temperature of the control electronic circuit is the limiting factor. The next most important advantage of an ACEL device is its fabrication process. Unlike LEDs, an ACEL has a very simple fabrication process which even can be carried out in an ambient environment.19 This allows the development of large area display devices, which can be fabricated with simple processes like printing.2325

Despite being a thin surface light emitting display, ACEL devices have some inherent disadvantages as well. Limited color capabilities are significant. Typically, a single material system emits light in very narrow wavelength range and hence a color conversion layer is required to build up other colors. In addition, they have widely limited in uses in backlight applications due to low lumen output ratings. However, this has not been highlighted in many applications since they are not used in high luminance applications like in hallway lighting or with lamp-posts. Even though they are capable of working in a wider temperature window, humidity has been reported as one such environmental limitation.26 When exposed to humid conditions, brightness has been reported to degrade over time, raising the need of encapsulation.

Apart from materials and device architecture, the electrical characteristics of an ACEL device have also created some limitations for its operation. A main concern is its AC voltage requirement.27 Though not significant for household applications, wearable displays or any other portable lighting devices would need to have an inverter to provide larger AC voltages above 100 V. However, when compared with LEDs, EL devices require very low current densities and extremely low power requirements as well.

Alternative current electroluminescence (ACEL)

In the previous section, an overview on working principles of an ACEL device was presented with a quick glance at its applicability, advantages and limitation as compared with LEDs. In this section, a detailed overview of ACEL device architecture, materials and fabrication methods will be discussed together with different performance enhancing approaches.

ACEL devices were of interest in a number of research groups throughout the past few decades. Different material systems have been used to achieve different colors, different light output levels targeting different applications. Tiwari et al.17 prepared an ACEL device with manganese doped zinc sulphide (ZnS:Mn) as the phosphor material (Fig. 2(a)) to evaluate its electrical characteristics. An electron beam evaporation technique was used for the deposition of all layers except a tin oxide (SnO2) transparent electrode. The obtained luminosity of the ZnS:Mn phosphor was found to have an S-shaped relationship with applied voltage while the frequency and the luminosity had linear relationships. Park et al.21 prepared a flexible EL device with silver nanowire (AgNW) electrodes. This device has shown remarkable flexibility properties (Fig. 3(a)), as conventional ITO (Indium Tin Oxide) electrodes tend to crack after repetitive bending cycles. Wang et al.28 mixed EL phosphor particles with stretchable polymers to achieve a highly flexible and stretchable light emitting device which sustained 180 bending and over 100% stretching without significant luminance sacrifice (Fig. 3(b)). A wearable EL watch developed with a dispenser printing method is another interesting work.25 All functional layers of the EL watch were printed on a fabric substrate and achieved a maximum of 200 cd m2 light intensity when powered by a 400 V, 2000 Hz AC voltage input. Fig. 3(c) shows29 another interesting work on dispenser printed ACEL device with luminescence of 44 cd m2 at 400 V, 400 Hz.

Device architecture

Device architecture of an EL device can be explained as the arrangement of device functional layers; conductive layers, insulator layers and phosphor layer to maximize its performance. From the study of different device architectures, two major classes can be identified. The first and most common architecture is a layer by layer device resembling tandem device architecture. Here, the functional layers are deposited on a substrate material and the simplicity of the fabrication process has resulted in wide applicability over a range of products. However, most widespread application range includes flat displays and lights which extends over a large area. The second structural approach is based on the concept of lighting wires or yarns. In this approach, the total device is developed in the form of a long cylindrical cable which later can be weaved into a large area fabric light source.30

Tandem device architecture is a very traditional approach where electrode-dielectric-phosphor-dielectric-electrode layers are sequentially deposited on top of each other. There, one electrode and one insulator layer must be transparent to facilitate light emission (Fig. 4(a)15). The concept is based on the requirement of duplicating a capacitor to isolate phosphor material from applied current.31 This double insulator approach was particularly developed in the 1970s to overcome electrical breakdown and stability issues.15

Over time, different variations in this traditional device architecture have been introduced to the field to enhance their properties and increase applicability over a wide range of applications. During later stages of EL-based research and device commercialization, this two insulator layer approach has been dropped and switched to a single insulator layer approach which provides isolation from an opaque electrode.26 The single dielectric layer configuration has provided thinner and more flexible devices while retaining original luminance properties.21 In the device prepared by Schrage and Kaskel,32 they deployed the single insulating layer approach with a barium titanate (BaTiO3) dielectric layer to isolate their phosphor from a silver electrode (Fig. 4(b)). As an alternative approach to traditional device design, Silver et al.33 proposed a single emissive layer approach. There, the emissive layer was formed by suspending phosphor particles in a mixer of dielectric particles and an organic binder oil, which acted as a local dielectric field for phosphor particles (Fig. 4(c)). More recently, Stauffer and Tybrandt34 also devised a similar kind of device where EL particles and BaTiO3 particles are filtered into a single layer (Fig. 4(d)).

In this device architecture, another classification is available based on the arranged sequence of device functional layers. First, in a forward architecture, the substrate is transparent, and the functional materials are applied on top of that starting from a transparent electrode (Fig. 4(e)). Here, the light emission is collected through the substrate.35 However, this approach is limited only to transparent substrate materials. For other kinds of materials, the reverse architecture is used where the transparent electrode is formed as the final layer on top of the phosphor material (Fig. 4(f)).36 This approach enabled the use of almost any substrate material including fabric,25 paper,23 metal24 and even ceramics.37 In addition to these current layers, during color tuning of the EL device, another color filtration layer is applied over the transparent surface (Fig. 4(g)).38 Further details on this topic will be discussed in detail in another section. Further on, a floating electrode approach has also been applied for EL devices (Fig. 4(h)).39 There, the bottom electrode in a reversed architecture EL device is split into two driven electrodes where the top transparent electrode works as the floating electrode.26 This resembles an arrangement of two EL devices in series and requires a comparatively higher electrical field as well.

The second type of structural design approach is based on the development of light emitting yarns. Here, the light emitting device is fabricated around a conductive base wire.30 As shown in Fig. 5(a), the phosphor layer is isolated from the base conductor with an insulator layer and the second electrode is completed by a thin conductive wire wrapped around the phosphor layer. One of the key advantage of this yarn-like structure is its potential to be integrated into fabrics and other flexible displays. An EL wire is a very popular commercial application of yarn-like device structures. As shown in Fig. 5(b), its device architecture is quite the same as the one we discussed earlier.40 It has already been applied over a number of wearable designs and other lighting applications and has even been developed into a concept in hobby electronics.41

Fabrication methods

One of the key advantages of ACEL devices over LED lighting sources is its simple fabrication process. Typically, the fabrication process of a LED involves special environmental control measures like a vacuum chamber and material deposition is typically carried out by thermal deposition techniques.4244 This limits the use of LEDs in large area light emitting device fabrications, as it involves costly scaling up of all fabrication equipment. In contrast with LED fabrication, ACEL fabrication requires very simple and open-air fabrication steps where the scaling up is very simple.

Where device architecture is concerned, an ACEL device has four or five device layers deposited on top of different substrates. The fabrication of a device can be carried out by a layer-by-layer deposition technique starting from the substrate layer with pre-fabricated individual materials. Under this section, fabrication of a device will be discussed without going into details of fabricating functional materials in each of those layers. Basically, device fabrication can be divided into two categories; printing and coating. Screen-printing45,46 is the most used printing method under the printing category while spray coating and spin coating dominate the other category. Even though sputtering,47,48 thermal evaporation49,50 and ion beam deposition17,51 have been used during early stages of EL device fabrications, researchers have moved towards much simpler fabrication methods like printing and coating.

Screen printing is a mass printing technique, where the printing paste consisting of functional materials is deposited through a fine screen with a rubber squeegee.23 The screen can be patterned with desired path designs and allows extremely accurate ink deposition which may be crucial in device fabrications. Basically, functional materials like PEDOT:PSS, ZnS:Cu or other phosphor materials are mixed with an ink which serves as the transport medium and additionally modifies the viscosity of the ink to ease the printing process. Screen printing has been employed during fabrications of multilayer watch fabrication,25,52 organic EL device fabrication38 and even in self-powered displays.53 In addition, this process provides huge variations over substrate materials like ITO,54 textiles,23,55 PET35,36,56 and even metal surfaces.57 In addition to screen printing, de Vos et al.58 used a dispenser printing technique while Angelo59 used an inkjet printing technique during his ACEL device fabrication process. One common factor among these printing approaches is the orientation of substrate material. Screen printing, inkjet printing or dispenser printing has been applied for planar surfaces. As a rather unconventional approach, Lee et al.60 proposed a pad printing technique where the ink is transferred to curved surfaces with silicon transfer pads. It should also be noted that each printing step is followed by annealing at elevated temperatures (slightly above 100 C) before printing the subsequent layer.

Spin coating is the next highlighted device fabrication technique. A viscous coating ink is dropped on top of the substrate kept on a rotating disk and centrifugal force spreads out the ink over the surface of the substrate. After each of these spinning steps, the substrate is annealed to secure the thin film and to prepare it for the next coating step. Spin coating has been used in a number of applications including organic light devices,61,62 different color emitting EL devices,63 flexible and stretchable light emitting devices28,64,65 and many more.6670 In addition to this method, a spray coating technique has also been used during fabrication processes for a whole device as well as for one or two functional layers. Gupta et al.71 spray coated patterned ITO sheets with phosphor and boron nitride dielectric layers. Wroblewski et al.24 spray coated a curved metal surface with functional material to build up an interactive package lighting using the EL principle. They achieved different functional properties by varying spray deposition parameters like pressure and working distance. Schrage and Kaskel32 also spray coated an EL device onto a flexible PET substrate while Sloma et al.23 and Ely et al.35 used a spray coating technique for transparent electrodes. Moreover, Park et al.21 used a bar coating technique for their EL device as an alternative coating approach. Dias and Monaragala30 developed a yarn type EL device with an innovative yarn coating approach. They developed a custom coating device and UV curing apparatus foe this yarn coating system.

In addition to these printing and coating approaches, some unconventional approaches like injection molding72 and mold forming73 techniques have also been used. Where printing and coating approaches are concerned, both are simple fabrication methods which require less preparedness. They can be carried out as open-air fabrication processes and are easily scalable based on the requirements.

Electrospinning

Among different fabrication processes used in opto-electronic applications, electrospinning can be identified with its unique advantages.74 Electrospinning is a high-aspect ratio fiber formation technique where a polymer-based solution is pulled through by an electrostatic pull force. A typical electrospinning setup (Fig. 6(a)) consists of a polymer solution filled syringe with a metal needle, syringe driving pump, high voltage supply and a grounded collector. When a high voltage is applied across the needle and the collector, the solution droplet at the end of the needle tip is ionized and the charge distribution at the droplet results in Taylor cone formation and a subsequent fiber jet.75 As the jet of fibers accelerate through the air, the solvent used for polymer solution evaporates and the polymer becomes hardened as a mat of fibers and is collected at the collector. Basically, any polymer with sufficient molecular weight can be electrospun through this method and a number of variables like solution viscosity, polymer molecular weight, applied voltage, solution flow rate, tip-to-collector distance, ambient temperature and humidity control the nature and morphology of the fiber mat.7579 This simple process can be easily scalable over a vast area and can result in a highly flexible, stretchable fiber mat with oriented polymer morphology along the fiber axis. In addition, polymer solutions, polymers containing quantum dots,80 carbon nanotubes,81 and ceramic dielectrics82,83 can also be mixed and electrospun with polymers enabling a new scope of application.

Due to these intrinsic properties, it has been widely used in the field of photonics over the past few years.74,84,85 When discussing light emitting devices these have been widely used in LEDs8688 even though their presence in ACEL devices is not that significant. Vohra et al.86 blended a F8BT conjugate polymer with polyethylene oxide (PEO) and electrospun it into a single layer light emitting diode. Whereas in the work of Moran-Mirabal et al.88 they electrospun ruthenium(II) tris(bipyridine) onto gold electrodes and observed light emission at low voltages. Kim and Lee89 used hyperbranched conjugate polymers (HCP) in their electrospinning solution and prepared a full color emission fiber mat. By tuning the volume ratio of HCPs in the solution, they obtained emission over the visible spectrum. Yang et al.,90 electrospun co-axial nanofibers with ionic transition-metal complexes (iTMCs) as a luminescent material and fabricated a one-dimensional light emitting device with very low driving voltage.

Materials

The electroluminescence phenomenon has inseparable dependence on the materials used in it. During early stages, it was believed that sulfides possessed the luminance properties and exploration began based on different sulfides. Later, it was identified that the luminous impurity or the dopant material within a sulfide matrix makes the major contribution for electroluminescence.15 Researchers then developed a series of phosphor materials with different luminance properties. Not only the phosphor material, but also materials for other functional layers like dielectric and transparent electrodes also have been a subject of interest along the past few decades for better performances of ACEL devices.3,19,31 In this section, a detailed overview will be presented on the materials used in ACEL devices. The discussion will be organized according to the functional layer in which they have been used.

Phosphor materials

During the development of EL devices, the phosphor material is the center of interest. The phosphor material itself defines most optical characteristics of an EL device. Mostly, the phosphor is comprised with a sulfide host material doped with metals which act as the luminous centers during the excitation mechanism. One of the major requirements of choosing a host matrix is its band gap.3 Since the host matrix needs to be transparent to the generated light, it should have a band gap which is large enough to avoid any light absorptions. Metal sulfides (or IIIV compounds) have been widely used as the host matrix due to several lattice properties they can offer during operations.15,19 Apart from not absorbing luminance center emission, they should be great hosts for luminance centers.

Among a number of metal sulfides, zinc sulfide (ZnS,) is the most common host material.14,9193 One of major reasons for that is its chemical stability as a host matrix for EL.94 In addition to ZnS, ZnSe95 and CdS96 have also been used as the host matrix in different applications. In addition, ZnGaS97 has also been used as another sulfide based host material. As, an alternative for traditional sulfides, (gallium arsenide) GaAs,98 gallium nitride47 (GaN) and zinc silicate57 (Zn2SiO4) based host matrices also have been reported in ACEL applications.

Luminous centers are the real contributors the light emission of the phosphor material. As they get excited by highly accelerated electrons, they emit light corresponding to their respective band energies. A chapter by Yoshida et al. in ref. 3 highlights the requirements for a luminous center like a large cross-sectional area and suitability of an ionic radius. Doping and co-doping the host matrix with different transition metals are key approaches of fabricating inorganic phosphor materials. Copper34,45,56,64,97,99,100 is the most favorite host matrix dopant and has been used in a number of different applications. However, a copper doped luminance matrix has shown two different color outputs upon excitation.

Dielectric materials

In the ACEL working principle, the dielectric layer(s) bears an important role in the stability of these devices. In his review, Krasnov116 summarized some key roles a dielectric is supposed to play including protection against charge transport, charge storing, protection against leakage bound charges, stability in high electric fields, and stability against atmospheric conditions like humidity and many more. More importantly, the high dielectric constant of the material is of highest importance for lower driving voltages. Basically, two types of dielectric materials have been used over past years, inorganic and organic dielectric materials. Inorganic dielectric materials provide the highest dielectric constant but are crystalline in nature. On the other hand, organic dielectric materials provide comparatively low dielectric constant values and are suitable for flexible devices.31

In early device configurations with two dielectric layers, the one that lies between the transparent electrode and phosphor layer should allow the light to pass through with minimum absorption, while the other insulator should work as a mirror surface to reflect phosphor emission. This required additional engineering work for researchers and eventually they switched to a single dielectric configuration which lies behind the phosphor layer with reflective properties.3,116 This provided a few additional advantages including thinner devices, higher flexibility, one less processing step and cheaper fabrication with fewer materials and time.31

Inorganic dielectric materials can be further categorized into two sections as amorphous oxides/nitrides and ferroelectric materials. Silicon dioxide103,107,117 (SiO2), aluminum oxide48 (Al2O3), tantalum oxide51 (TaOx) and hafnium oxide108,118 are among some insulating oxides being used in ACEL applications. And for ferroelectric materials, barium titanate34,69,119123 (BaTiO3) has been the most popular choice among a number of groups. The extremely high dielectric constant (in the range of 20003000 at room temperature)19 is the main reason for being so popular. Interestingly, Gao et al.54 used a diphase polycrystalline material for their EL device where (Ba0.4Ca0.6)TiO3:Pr3+ acts as both the phosphor and dielectric material by itself. This has shown peak EL emission at 617 nm and made a significant process simplification with just a single functioning layer.

The next category is organic dielectric materials. When compared with their inorganic counterparts, they possess very low dielectric constants (in the range of 52031) but show remarkable flexibility. Poly(vinylidene fluoride)63 (PVDF), polydimethylsiloxane28 (PDMS) and cyanoethyl pullulan61 (CEP) are some reported organic insulators. To enhance the flexibility of the insulating layer while retaining its high dielectric properties, inorganic materials in composites with organic binders like BaTiO3PVDF124127 composite have been implemented in EL devices. The dielectric properties can be easily tuned by modifying the volume fractions of inorganic compounds and they have provided high flexibility over the fabrication process as well. Transparent electrode materials

The next important device layer is the transparent electrode of a device. The use of transparent electrode can be found in various electronic applications like light emitting devices, solar panels, and touch panels and hence it has been reviewed by several researchers as well.132134 He and Tjong133 listed a few factors that needed to be considered during the selection of a transparent electrode as typical transmission (at 550 nm), electrical conductivity or sheet resistance, haze, thermal stability and typical thickness. In addition, when processability is considered, the fabrication method and fabrication cost should also be considered. Transparent electrode materials can be divided into three major categories: semiconductors, carbon based materials and metals.132 Here, organic transparent materials are listed under a second category of carbon-based materials.

Indium tin oxide (ITO) has been used widely for a long time because of its transmittance characteristics and electrical conductivity. ITO coated on a glass substrate38,51,66,71,120 also has been used by number of researchers on different occasions. It typically offers conductivity over 4000 S cm1 and transparency values over 90%. Apart from ITO, tin oxide17 (SnO2) has also been used as a transparent electrode material. One major limitation of these semiconductor oxide materials is their poor flexibility which prevents them from being used in most flexible electronic applications. Further, a complex fabrication process can make them difficult to be used for large area lighting purposes. However, recent developments in organic chemistry have provided a chance to investigate the possibility of using conductive polymers as a transparent electrode material. Wang et al.134 suggested poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene), poly(styrene sulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS), polyaniline (PANI) and polypyrrole (PPY) as major candidates for organic transparent electrodes. These can offer very high mechanical flexibility and simple processing requirements despite being poor electrical conductors.143,144 Among them, PEDOT:PSS was the popular choice35,145147 and had been used in ACEL devices with different chemical performance enhancements like doping.

In addition to conducting polymers, carbon nanotubes32,148,149 (CNTs) have also been used as transparent electrodes in EL devices. They have shown high transmittance and high conductivity properties while offering various fabrication options to be used. Alonso et al.150 used graphene as the transparent electrode and investigated the flexibility of the resulting device against its luminous properties. Moreover, Sloma et al.151 used a screen-printed CNT electrode for a textile EL device.

Metal nanowires are the next choice of electrode materials. Among them, copper nanowires152 has been used over a vast range of applications in opto-electronic devices. Excellent electrical conductive properties, high transmittance properties and high mechanical flexibility has favored the choice of metal nanowire electrodes1,42 Among different metal nanowires, copper nanowires141,153157 (CuNW) can be seen more commonly due to the fact that it is cheap, widely available and bears very good electrical conductivity. Apart from that, silver nanowires21,34,158 (AgNW) and coppernickel nanowires159 (CuNi NW) also have been applied over EL devices.

Back electrode

Unlike in other functional layers, the back electrode or the opaque electrode has been given lesser interest with the development stages of EL devices. Even though we can see a number of innovative steps alongside of other functional layers, for back electrodes, there is hardly any innovation involved. That's because it's supposed to play very lesser important character in the device, being an electrode. In most cases, metallic materials are used as the electrode given that they have high conductivity and are not needed to be transparent. In reverse architecture, the metal is deposited on the substrate either by sputtering or pasting whereas in a forward approach the metal electrode becomes the top layer of the device.

Special attention has been given for some cases24 where graphene nanoparticles (GNP) were printed on top of a fabric material for the flexibility it can provide to the whole device. For screen printed devices, a special silver based screen print has been used25 but it requires low temperature curing. In addition to metallic materials, carbon based conductive ink mixed with silver nanoparticles has also been used by Lee et al.37

Performance enhancement

One of limitations that a typical ACEL device is subjected to is its limited color options and poor brightness properties, thus limiting them to a very restricted list of applications. As shown in Table 1, the color of an EL device is decided by the phosphor material used in it. Though different luminescent centers provide different color outputs in same host matrix, the synthesis process of those phosphor materials is complicated and can be expensive. When different luminescence principles like electroluminescence (ACEL, LED), mechanoluminescence and photoluminescence principles have been investigated, three approaches can be identified as possible color tuning approaches:73 obtaining a mixture of different emitting phosphors,160,161 use of color filtering layers after the original phosphor layer38,63,66,68 or use of active quantum dot based phosphor materials.51,73,162,163

Jeong et al.160,161 mixed ZnS:Cugreen phosphors and ZnS:Cu,Mnorange phosphors in different volume fractions to tune the color output. Though this principle has been applied in mechanoluminescent devices, it can be easily applied with the electroluminescent principle as well. For example, in Zhang's work38 an organic photoluminescent dye was drop casted on an inorganic ACEL device to manipulate color output. Blue emitting phosphor was used as the emitting layer while poly[(9,9-dioctylfluorenyl-2,7-diyl)-alt-(benzo[2,1,3]thiadiazol-4,7-diyl)] (F8BT) and 4-(dicyanomethylene)-2-tert-butyl-6-(1,1,7,7-tetramethyljulolidin-4-yl-vinyl)-4H-pyran (DCJTB) have been used for yellow and red down-conversion layers, respectively. Similarly, Xia et al.63 have used a yellow down-conversion layer of YAG:Ce (cerium doped yttrium aluminum garnet). Min et al.111 developed a poly-(9-vinylcarbazole) fiber mat with quantum dots as a filter layer for a blue light emission. Apart from use as a color rendering filter, quantum dots (QD) have been used as a light emitting material itself. Kobayashi et al.51 fabricated an ACEL device with a cadmium selenide/zinc sulfide (CdSe/ZnS) QD-based phosphor layer and proposed that its brightness can also be increased. Jeong et al.73 fabricated a white light emitting EL device where a QD embedded conversion layer was used with a ZnS:Cu phosphor layer and CdSe/CdZnS QDs was used within an elastomer matrix.

The second concern of a typical ACEL is its poor brightness, which limits its applications further. However, being a surface light emitting source has made maximum use out of poor brightness issues and turned it into uses for backlighting applications. When device architecture and working principles are considered, the electric field applied across the phosphor layer determines the brightness of the device. This is proved by the fact that the brightness or the EL intensity of a typical ACEL device is drastically increased with applied voltage and frequency of the signal. However, a high driving voltage can make the system complicated. As an alternative approach, Stauffer and Tybrandt34 prepared the phosphor layer as a composite of both phosphor and dielectric materials. Since dielectric materials were small in size compared with phosphor particles, they covered the phosphor particles effectively so that, upon application of driving voltage, phosphor particles were subjected to an extremely high electric field. They reported an impressive 700% improvement in ACEL brightness after their approach. Wen et al.64 embedded a conductive layer with zinc oxide whiskers to enhance the local electrical field. At the tips of the whiskers, the electric field becomes intensified and the field across the phosphor layer also is increased. This has eventually led to an enhanced luminous output. Carbon nanotubes (CNT) can also generate the same results and have been used by number of groups.46,61,103

Conclusion and future aspects

Light emitting devices have evolved greatly from the days of their discovery. With current trends on wearables and miniaturizing electronics, flexible light emitting techniques have been introduced as well. Among different techniques, LED (Light emitting diodes) and ACEL (Alternative Current Electroluminescnce) have been used as key light emitting techniques among a number of individual techniques. Applications of flexible light emitting devices are already spread over a number of different commercial platforms. Even though luminance output seems poor compared with LED, ACEL devices possess a number of other key advantages which over-shadow the performances of LEDs. Simple fabrication processes, high luminance efficiency, simpler device architecture, smooth and surface light emission and ruggedness during operation have clearly made them industry ready and thus suitable for flexible light emitting devices.

During the early stages of its development, luminescence was identified and developed around sulfides. Later, as we moved through the timeline, the invention of semiconducting materials lightened up the LEDs and more recently, organic materials entered into the play with outstanding performances. However, one of the key concerns at the moment lies with the performances of material systems being used in these lighting devices. Since the applications of luminescence devices have expanded into wearables and outdoor applications, the demand for rugged materials has increased. Even though organic phosphor materials have shown unparalleled luminescence properties, their poor behaviours during intense ambient conditions like high humidity and high temperature have opened up the necessity that robust material systems are still needed.

On the other hand, researchers have also been busy developing device fabrication techniques to incorporate market requirements. Early stage thick film electroluminescence devices have been replaced with thinner and more flexible devices. Especially for LEDs, vacuum-based processes and nano fabrication techniques have been developed targeting portable electronic devices like mobile phones. However, having complicated and controlled-environmental-based fabrication techniques are less favourable with industrial scale production lines, especially for large area fabrications. Hence, current trends on fabrication techniques are more oriented towards simpler and open-air fabrication techniques like printing and coating techniques. The electrospinning technique is a nano-fiber fabrication process with a huge potential to be incorporated into industrial scale light emitting device fabrication. Since it is a simple and open-air fabrication method, the process is less complicated and its massive scalability can expand size limitations of the devices. Even though some electrospun organic light emitting devices have been developed at laboratory scales, still there are some points to be clarified before introducing them into industrial level light emitting device fabrications. Their future is bright.
