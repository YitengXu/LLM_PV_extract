Abstract

Exploiting solar power for hydrogen production from seawater is a great challenge owing to the corrosive properties of seawater and inadequate visible-light conversion capabilities. Here we report an uncomplicated post-processing method to construct Ti3+ self-doped titaniumâ€“silicon material with corrosion resistance. This is a new experimental method to regulate the electrical, optical, and photocatalytic performances of titanium-containing photocatalysts in a controlled way. Moreover, we demonstrate that Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si materials with different calcination temperatures can serve as a highly efficient and convenient catalyst for photogeneration of hydrogen from water and simulated seawater. Consequently, the optimized Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) sample exhibits impressive enhancement in the photocatalytic hydrogen evolution performance, by nearly 10.0 and 43.1 times compared with TiO2 nanoparticles in water and simulated seawater. The Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) with substantial Ti3+ and oxygen vacancies exhibits an excellent photocatalytic H2 production performance due to the improved separation and transmission of the photogenerated electronâ€“hole, the extended visible light response, and corrosion resistance. Our work opens a new door to engineering the intrinsic properties of the titanium-containing materials.

Introduction

With increasing concerns regarding the prevalent energy crisis and environmental pollution, hydrogen (H2) as an alternate fuel is receiving considerable attention from around the world.1â€“3 As one of the prospective technologies, solar driven H2 generation plays a considerable role in H2 evolution strategies as a renewable and clean energy source that can compensate for rapidly depleting fossil fuels.4â€“6 In the past decade, many kinds of materials have been utilized for photocatalytic H2 production.7â€“9 Of these, titanium dioxide (TiO2) is famous as one of the most extensively utilized materials in the fields of photocatalytic and solar energy conversion.10,11 Unfortunately, the application of TiO2 to photocatalytic H2 production generally suffers from moderate photocatalytic activities due to a fast electronâ€“hole recombination and a wide band gap (3.0â€“3.2 eV).12â€“15 Over the last 40 years, various strategies have been explored to improve the performance of TiO2 including doping metals (Co, Ni, Mn, Fe, Cr),16â€“19 nonmetal elements (N, S, C, I),20â€“22 noble metal nanodots,23â€“25 dye-sensitized decorated TiO2 nanostructures,26,27 and constructing heterojunctions with other semiconductor materials.28â€“32

In particular, a lot of interest has been focussed on the Ti3+ (or oxygen vacancies) in self-doped TiO2.33 As is well known, the photoexcitation in stoichiometric anatase TiO2 comes from transitions from the O 2p states in the valence band (VB) to the 3d states of the Ti (formally Ti4+) cations in the conduction band (CB).34 Moreover, Ti4+ in TiO2 can very easily be reduced to Ti3+.35 As shown by the material's color, the d â†’ d transitions from the gap states promote the optical performances of Ti3+ self-doped TiO2.36 Consequently, owing to the band gap narrowing and enhancing of the separation efficiency of the photogenerated electronâ€“hole connected with the existence of the Ti3+ induced band gap state, a highly active Ti3+ self-doped photocatalyst has been implemented.37 Usually, Ti3+ self-doped TiO2 is obtained by generating oxygen vacancies from sodium borohydride (NaBH4), as well as additional reducing reagents (Al, Zn, and H2etc.).38â€“41 However, the reduction method is difficult because of the need for high temperatures or a high pressure sophisticated environment along with H2/N2/Ar treatments.42 It is thus important to develop a low-cost, environmentally-friendly, and reproducible method for the large-scale preparation of Ti3+ defects in photocatalysts with dramatically enhanced photocatalytic activity.43 Keeping these facts in mind, lately we have successfully synthesized a homogeneous titaniumâ€“silicon material with corrosion resistance by selecting the low cost and less toxic organosilane and titanium(IV) butoxide as the raw materials. Interestingly, we discovered that the commonly used thermal treatment strategy (i.e., direct heating of titaniumâ€“silicon material to 400 Â°C for 3 h) under atmospheric pressure and air atmosphere can also be used for the synthesis of Ti3+ self-doped in titanium (Ti4+)â€“silicon material. Furthermore, compared to the scarcity of fresh water, we first discovered that the Ti3+ defects in titanium (Ti4+)â€“silicon materials can be used for photocatalytic hydrogen evolution from simulated seawater under solar light irradiation.

In this research, we first report the uncomplicated synthesis and detailed characterization of Ti3+ defects in corrosion resistant titanium (Ti4+)â€“silicon materials to the best of our knowledge. This is the latest experimental method to regulate the electrical, optical, and photocatalytic performances of titanium-containing photocatalysts in a controlled way. Owing to the introduction of a certain amount of oxygen vacancies and Ti3+, the Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) and Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (500) materials showed excellent light absorption covering both the visible and ultraviolet light region. The obtained Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) material, with excellent optical properties, displayed much better capability in photocatalytic H2 production from fresh water and simulated seawater than pure TiO2 nanocrystals. Furthermore, a possible mechanism is also proposed whereby the Ti3+ doping and introduction of Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) synergistically improve the visible light photocatalytic activity due to a reduced band gap and restrain the recombination of photoinduced electronâ€“hole pairs.

Experimental

Reagents

Diethoxydimethylsilane ((CH3)2Si(C2H5O)2, 98.0%), p-xylene solution (C8H10, 99.0%), triethoxymethylsilane (C7H18O3Si, 98.0%), titanium(IV) butoxide (C16H36O4Ti, 99.0%), ethanol absolute (C2H6O, 99.8%), and triethanolamine (C6H15NO3, 99.0%) were purchased from Aladdin Co. Ltd. The materials were all utilized without further purification and were all analytically pure. Deionized water was utilized throughout the experimental work.

Preparation of TiO2, Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si, Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400, 500, and 600) materials

8.0 mL (0.04 mol) methyltriethoxysilane, 8.9 g (0.06 mol) dimethyldiethoxysilane, and 25 mL ethanol absolute were added to a 250 mL three-necked flask equipped with a mechanical stirrer, a reflux condenser, and a dropping funnel. The mixture was stirred at 140 rpm with a mechanical stirrer and heated to a temperature of 65 Â°C. After adding 0.17 mL of HCl, 4.41 g (0.25 mol) H2O was added drop by drop to the mixed solution allowing a hydrolysis reaction. After completion of the dropwise addition, the temperature was maintained at 65 Â°C for 2 h. Then, 6.81 g (0.02 mol) titanium(IV) butoxide dissolved in 20 mL p-xylene solution and 1.891 g (0.11 mol) H2O were added dropwise, respectively, to the mixture solution, and then the solution was maintained at 65 Â°C for about 2 h for the hydrolysis reaction (clear liquid). A three-necked flask equipped with a thermometer, a condenser, and a vacuum pump was used to perform a reduced pressure condensation. The pressure was 0.02 MPa. The hydrolysis solution was heated to 80 Â°C to distill out the ethanol, n-butanol, and p-xylene solution, and was then heating was continued the reaction solution to 120 Â°C for a decompression condensation for about 2 h. This solution was allowed to stand for a period of time (30 d) for curing. The obtained solids were respectively washed with ethanol and deionized water (labeled as Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si). The Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si material was heat treated at 400, 500, and 600 Â°C for 3 h under atmospheric pressure and air atmosphere (labeled as Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400), Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (500), and Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (600)). For comparison, a TiO2 sample was prepared using the same method and heat treated at 400 Â°C for 3 h without the addition of methyltriethoxysilane and dimethyldiethoxysilane. Results and discussion

Crystal structure and composition

Scheme 1 evidently shows that the Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si materials were obtained by a multi-step hydrolysis and condensation process, these are obviously distinct from white perfect TiO2 nanoparticles. The individual Ti3+ self-doped Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si materials obtained after the ordinary heat treatment method (air atmosphere and atmospheric pressure) change their color dramatically by causing partial Tiâ€“Oâ€“Ti bonds to break. More meaningfully, according to this color change we observe that they can be kept for up to 10 months unchanged, the defects in Ti3+ self-doped Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si materials exhibit excellent stability. As shown in Fig. 1, the crystallite sizes and crystal structures of the as-obtained samples were researched using X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns. The diffraction peaks of the TiO2 sample exhibit a distinct anatase structure without any other impurity components consistent with the JCPDS card No. 65-5714. By forming a uniform transparent Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si material, XRD characterization showed that the crystalline state of TiO2 was changed. Moreover, through gradually increasing the calcination temperature, the TiO2 diffraction peak becomes sharper and stronger owing to the increase of crystallinity. The maximum peak intensity was observed once the annealing temperature increased to 600 Â°C, which indicates that the Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si bonds are all destroyed by ultra-high temperature treatment. Moreover, these XRD results show that annealing at the highest temperature does not cause a crystal phase transformation, indicating that the carefully controlled synthesis of Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si materials with distinct bulk Ti3+ distributions can inhibit phase transformation from anatase to rutile.

To further confirm the presence of subsurface and bulk Ti3+, EPR spectroscopy at 110 K was performed on the as-prepared samples. For comparison, the pristine TiO2 sample has also been tested. As illustrated in Fig. 4(e), no obvious EPR signals were detected in the TiO2 nanoparticles, which indicate that the compositions of titanium elements in this sample are all in the Ti4+ 3d0 states. Ordinarily, the surface Ti3+ can easily absorb oxygen from the air, which could be reduced to superoxide radical anions with an EPR signal at a g-value of 2.02.54,55 Therefore, no signal attributed to surface Ti3+ is found, showing the absence of surface Ti3+. Furthermore, the Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) and Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (500) materials show all intense axially symmetry signals centered on the g value of 1.99 corresponding to bulk Ti3+ spins due to localized Ti3+ 3d1 states, which are consistent with the previously reported results of Ti3+ ions in titanium-containing materials.56 The above outcomes confirm the successful synthesis of Ti3+ self-doped Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) photocatalysts, and are in good agreement with the results of other tests.

Photocatalytic H2 production and stability

The photocatalytic H2 evolution abilities of TiO2, Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si, Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400), Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (500), and Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (600) were each investigated in 78 mL pure deionized water solution and 10 vol% TEOA (adjusted to pH 8.2) or 78 mL 5 wt% NaCl water solution and 10 vol% TEOA (adjusted to pH 8.2) under irradiation with full-sunlight. As shown in Fig. 8(a), the H2 production rates of TiO2, Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si, Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (500), and Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (600) samples were 163.2, 329.9, 952.1, and 465.4 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1, respectively, while the Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) could reach up to 1640.2 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1, which is more than 10.0 times higher than that of TiO2 (163.2 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1). It is easy to draw the conclusion that the photocatalytic H2 production and photoconversion of Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) materials can be significantly improved by Ti3+ self-doped Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si materials, which have perfectly conquered the two main shortcomings of TiO2 nanoparticles, containing poor visible-light utilization and high charge-recombination rates. In addition, for more than 40 years, photocatalytic hydrogen production was needed in fresh water, but the scarcity of freshwater resources led to the selection of new sources of water, and the richness of seawater resources may make it a great application in the field of photocatalysis. Therefore, 5 wt% NaCl water solution is used to simulate seawater.60 As compared to the photocatalytic H2 production of the TiO2 sample in pure water, the H2 production rate of TiO2 is reduced 3.6 times in simulated seawater. The results show that excessive NaCl hinders the separation and transmission of the photogenerated electronâ€“hole. On the contrary, the H2 production rate of Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) was almost 1.2 times that of the Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) material. Hence, these different photocatalytic H2 evolution activities shown by Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si materials may have resulted from the thick sheet morphology, which is beneficial in protecting the titanium element from simulated seawater corrosion. At the same time, compared to other materials, the Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) material with increased photocatalytic activity may be due to the introduction of NaCl affecting the surface interface properties of special materials, thus resulting in a higher carrier separation efficiency of the materials. Furthermore, by filtering out the incident ultraviolet light (Fig. S10â€ ), the Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) sample still showed higher photocatalytic H2 evolution activity. The apparent quantum efficiency (AQE) for Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si reached 8.9% at 420 nm in 5 wt% NaCl water solution. A photocatalyst must display a stable performance for its successful practical application.61 The stability of Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) was assessed in a 5 wt% NaCl water solution under irradiation with full-sunlight by carrying out a â€œtime-circleâ€ H2 evolution experiment. The reaction was stopped every four hours. As shown in Fig. 8(b), the photocatalytic H2 evolution was maintained for seven repeated runs without adding a sacrificial agent in the photocatalytic experiment period, indicating good stability and recycling performance by Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400). Furthermore, analysis of the XRD pattern, FTIR spectrum, EPR spectrum, and XPS spectrum of Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) after this experiment (Fig. S11 and S12â€ ) also indicated its good stability.62 The reason for the high stability of this photocatalytic activity is as follows: the prepared Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si complexes exhibited a thick flat morphology, which is beneficial in protecting the titanium element from simulated seawater corrosion. In addition, the EPR signals are not surface Ti3+ but bulk Ti3+ spins, which can effectively avoid the photocorrosion.

Clarification of the mechanism

To further demonstrate the charge separation efficiency levels, the transient photocurrent responses of the TiO2, Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si, Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400), Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (500), and Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (600) photoelectrodes were obtained by the Iâ€“t curves with seven onâ€“off cycles under full-sunlight irradiation (Fig. 9(a)).63 An implication can be drawn that the photocurrent value that rapidly appeared in all the photoelectrodes when the lamp was turned on, and then quickly returned to zero when the lamp was turned off, demonstrating that the photoelectrodes had great stability and repeatability. The order of the photocurrent value under full-sunlight illumination was Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) > Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (500) > Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (600) > Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si > TiO2, which certificated that the Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) sample could greatly improve the separation of the photoproduced electronâ€“hole. This experimental result shows that Ti3+ can efficiently enhance the transmission efficiency of photogenerated electrons. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was used to investigate the electrical conductivity of the samples (Fig. 9(b)).64 Considering the diameters of the semicircles were associated with the charge-transfer resistance of a photocatalyst, the electrical Nyquist plots indicated the information on the charge transfer process across the electrode/electrolyte interface. Generally, the smaller size of the semicircle arc diameter implies the fast separation of photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs and smaller charge-transfer resistance in an EIS Nyquist plot. Compared to other as-obtained samples, the smallest arc radius of a Nyquist plot for Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) suggests the smallest charge transfer resistance of Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400).

The photo-luminescence (PL) spectroscopy technique can be considered as a useful method to comprehend the transfer and recombination of the photoproduced electronâ€“hole in a composite.65 Thus, the PL spectra of as-obtained photocatalysts has been investigated as shown in Fig. 10(a). The PL spectrum of pure TiO2 displayed a sharp emission peak at 467 nm. The intensity of the emission bands of Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) and Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (500) were lower than that of TiO2, demonstrating that the recombination of the photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs through the Ti3+ was more suppressed for Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) and Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (500) than that for TiO2. We further obtained information about the charge-carrier transport by the time-resolved PL spectra collected at the wavelength of their maximum emission as shown in Fig. 10(b). After multi-exponential fitting, the radiative lifetimes of pure TiO2, Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400), and Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (500) were 0.34 ns, 2.78 ns, and 2.21 ns, respectively. The resulting PL lifetime (Ï„) is 2.78 ns for Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400), which is almost 8.18 times longer than that of TiO2. The lifetime prolongation of charge carriers had an indispensable role in improving photocatalytic reactions before recombination.66,67 It is believed that the increased lifetime of charge carriers is associated with electron transport improvement and electronic structure change induced by the Ti3+ defect effect in the Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) materials. This outcome is consistent with the transient photocurrent response, EIS, and PL spectrum.

Conclusions

In summary, Ti3+ self-doped titaniumâ€“silicon material constructed using a novel approach induced changes in defect distribution, defect concentration, and the band gap. Amongst the many factors, a non-uniform distribution of defects in the bulk plays a key role in terms of photocatalytic activity. Such as, the Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) sample gains enhanced photocatalytic activity not only in fresh water, but also in simulated seawater. The H2 generation of Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) is respectively 10.0 times greater in pure water and 43.1 times greater in simulated seawater than that of a TiO2 nanoparticle under full-sunlight irradiation, superior to the frequently reported titanium-containing materials in simulated seawater. The excellent photocatalytic activity of Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si (400) depends on the recombination center reduction, solar absorption enhancement, and corrosion resistance. We believe that the preparation method provides an effective way for the rational design of corrosion resistant Ti3+ self-doped Tiâ€“Oâ€“Si materials. Therefore, hydrogen can be produced from seawater, which would be an interesting and important research direction to boost the potential practical application ability of the photocatalyst.
