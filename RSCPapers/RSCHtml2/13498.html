Insights into the development of Cu-based photocathodes for carbon dioxide (CO2) conversion

Abstract

The ever-growing over-consumption of fossil fuels and the accompanying massive emissions of CO2 have caused a severe energy crisis and environmental issues. Photoelectrochemical (PEC) reduction of CO2 that can combine and optimize the merits of both photocatalysis and electrocatalysis has been considered as an attractive prospect for addressing the two problems. Herein, photocathode materials are cores of PEC CO2 reduction. Among various photocathodes, Cu-based photocathodes attract much attention due to their low toxicity, low cost, earth-abundant properties, appropriate range of bandgap values and favorable band edges for CO2 reduction. More to the point, Cu-based photocathodes possess unique advantages for hydrocarbon production such as CO, HCOOH, CH4 or, more importantly, C2 products (like C2H4, C2H5OH and C2H6) compared with other metallic-based photoelectrodes. An insight into the relationships between the properties and functions of photocathodes plays a vital role in the field of PEC CO2 reduction. This review presents the development of Cu-based photocathode materials and discusses future research directions. The photocathode systems mainly include Cu-based oxides, Cu-based chalcogenides and Cu-based electrocatalyst coupled photocathodes. Subsequently, the existing major advantages and disadvantages of each system are also discussed. In conclusion, combining our analyses of the reported photocathodes, we will offer future challenges for the rational design of photoelectrodes. Overall, this review will present a significant overview of Cu-based photocathodes for PEC reduction of CO2.

1. Introduction

The rapid expansion of the global population and modern industry provoked excessive utilization of fossil fuels (such as oil, coal, natural gas, etc.), which triggered anthropogenic emissions of CO2 and ultimately led to global warming and climate change.1 This called for the development of chemical feedstocks and fuels using earth-abundant resources (e.g. water, CO2etc.) via renewable energy.2 In recent years, direct reduction of CO2 into storable hydrocarbon fuel has gained increased attention.3,4 However, carbon dioxide (CO2) has been demonstrated as a quite stable linear molecule, which possesses two equivalent C=O bonds.5 The corresponding binding energy (750 kJ mol−1) is significantly higher than C–O (327 kJ mol−1), C–C (336 kJ mol−1) and C–H (411 kJ mol−1).6 Thus, it requires a higher activation barrier. Given this, various efforts have been working on CO2 conversion, such as biocatalysis,7,8 thermocatalysis,9,10 electrocatalysis,11,12 photocatalysis13,14 and photoelectrocatalysis.15,16 From the point of view of available energy resources, solar energy is clean, abundant and cheap. However, choosing photocatalysts for photocatalytic CO2 reduction is limited due to their complex synthesis methods, poor selectivity, unfavorable band positions and high recombination rates of photogenerated charges. The electrochemical reduction of CO2 requires a large amount of electricity due to high overpotentials for CO2 reduction. Compared with photocatalytic and electrocatalytic reduction of CO2 processes, the PEC system embodies several distinct merits: (1) the recombination of photogenerated electrons and holes at the photocathode and anode can be efficiently suppressed, driven by the applied external electrical bias; (2) the PEC cells can efficiently separate oxidative and reductive products produced by the anode and photocathode; (3) from the viewpoint of energy utilization, photoelectrocatalysis can utilize solar energy to supply additional charges, thus significantly reducing electricity consumption.17 In response to the merits, photoelectrocatalysis is considered to be an efficacious strategy for CO2 reduction. Besides this, compared with the Cu-based photocatalysts or electrocatalysts, designing a photoelectrochemical cathode also has distinctive features: (1) a photoelectrochemical cathode has to be a photoactive semiconductor that must have a reasonable bandgap and suitable conduction and valence band positions for a redox reaction. (2) A photoactive Cu-based thin film is required in the photoelectrochemical system, which can be obtained by directly synthesizing as a thin film or immobilizing the Cu-based semiconductor powder onto a supporting electrode surface. (3) The as-prepared Cu-based thin film needs to form close and high-quality contact with the charge-collecting substrate, which would promote effective charge transfer, thus achieving excellent PEC reduction of CO2 performance.18,19

The seminal investigation of PEC CO2 conversion was reported by Halmann in 1978.20 After that, a series of research investigations were carried out on various inorganic semiconductor materials. For a feasible CO2 conversion, the conduction and valence band position of semiconductors should be higher than the overpotential of CO2 reduction and lower than the overpotential of H2O/O2, respectively. Besides this, the product selectivity of CO2 conversion also depends on the choice of electrode materials.21 It follows that the choice of semiconductors is vitally important for realizing PEC CO2 conversion.

In the long term, implementing the PEC configuration that is made of an n-type photoanode and a p-type photocathode without bias voltage is the ultimate goal for efficient CO2 reduction. However, the prerequisite for no bias is the suitable photoanodes and photocathodes with matched band gaps and band edge positions. Over the years, more attention has been focused on the development of n-type semiconductors, which have the advantage of high stability and low cost. A large number of n-type semiconductors, such as TiO2, WO3, ZnO, Fe2O3 and BiVO4, show stable and excellent water oxidation performance, and CO2 reduction can be realized if the Pt of the counter electrode is replaced by other special metals.22–25 Although many advancements in the development of n-type semiconductor photoanodes have been achieved, the understanding of p-type photocathodes remains limited for CO2 reduction. The reasons are as follows. Firstly, most p-type materials require a high bias potential for realizing the overall CO2 reduction due to the negative VB potential. Secondly, the products of p-type photocathodes for CO2 reduction have a competitive reaction with the production of H2 owing to similar free energies. Besides this, many well-known p-type materials are either toxic, expensive (e.g., GaP, lnP and CdTe) or susceptible to photo-corrosion (e.g., Cu2O, CuO, Si).26,27 Among numerous photocathode materials, Cu-based photocathodes in particular are the focal system in the studies due to their inexpensive nature and high capability of catalyzing the conversion of CO2.28–30 Impressively, Cu-based catalysts have demonstrated promise for selective CO2 reduction to C1 and C2 products.31–33 As a result, developing Cu-based photocathodes is expected to achieve the selectivity to multi-carbon products (Fig. 1).

Nevertheless, up until now, a comprehensive review covering the rational design of Cu-based photocathodes for efficient PEC CO2 reduction is still missing, and it is imperative to obtain a better understanding of the state-of-the-art progress in the area of PEC CO2 reduction, especially for the development of Cu-based photocathodes. It concerns the realization of the optimal Z-scheme system that consists of an n-type photoanode and a p-type photocathode. In this review, we first illustrate the fundamental principle with respect to PEC CO2 reduction systems. After that, we summarize the Cu-based photocathodes for PEC CO2 reduction, including Cu-based oxides, Cu-based chalcogenides and Cu-based electrocatalysts coupled with photocathodes. Finally, the challenges and perspectives of Cu-based photocathodes for PEC CO2 reduction are proposed with an outlook of future research directions. We hope that this review will shed light on the research orientation of Cu-containing semiconductor photocathodes in the realm of PEC CO2 reduction.

2. The fundamentals of CO2 conversion

2.1 Thermodynamics and kinetics of CO2 reduction

As is well known, the fundamentals of photoelectrocatalysis are based on photocatalytic processes. Similarly, the whole process of PEC CO2 reduction can be divided into three steps: (i) photoelectrodes harvest solar-light and generate electron–hole pairs, (ii) charge is separated and transported to the surface of the photoelectrodes, (iii) the electrons that reach the surface can reduce CO2 into various hydrocarbon fuels, while holes oxidize H2O to form O2.34,35 During the process of PEC CO2 reduction, PEC cell also plays an extremely important role in obtaining high conversion efficiency. Generally, a PEC CO2 reduction cell can be configured in accordance with the following three types: (1) the photoanode driven system that is made of an n-type semiconductor photoanode and a metal cathode (Fig. 2a),36,37 (2) the photocathode-driven PEC configuration that combines a p-type semiconductor photocathode for CO2 reduction with a metal anode (Fig. 2b),38–40 (3) the multiple photo-system that is composed of an n-type semiconductor photoanode for O2 evolution and a p-type semiconductor photocathode for CO2 reduction (Fig. 2c).41–43 To this end, it is critical to devise photoelectrodes to accomplish high-efficiency PEC conversion of CO2. Firstly, photoelectrodes possess suitable bandgap and band positions for PEC reactions.44 Secondly, photoelectrodes can make full use of the photogenerated charge carriers for CO2 reduction.36,40 Beyond that, photocathode materials are also capable of favourable adsorption of CO2.45
Fig. 2 Photoelectrochemical CO2 reduction system: (a) photoanode-driven system, (b) photocathode-driven system, (c) photoanode/photocathode-driven system.

In general, the semiconductors generate and migrate electrons from VB (valence band) to CB (conduction band) under illumination conditions, which produces a photovoltage. This promotes the band position bending. Fig. 3 shows the band bending diagrams of a p-type semiconductor in the equilibration state (Fig. 3a), the photochemical system (Fig. 3b), the electrochemical (EC) system (Fig. 3c), and the PEC system (Fig. 3d).46 Often, the Fermi level of the solution depends on the concentration, but it is always constant. When the semiconductor is immersed in the electrolyte solution, charge transfer occurs at the interface of the electrode/electrolyte until the Fermi levels of the semiconductor and solution are equal, which produces a space–charge region at equilibrium, also known as the depletion layer. It changes the band diagram near the edges of the CB and VB. Typically, the Fermi level of a p-type semiconductor lies near the VB. Then, downwards band bending of p-type semiconductors promotes CB electrons to the interface to promote the reduction reaction along the potential gradient, thus accelerating the separation of the electron–hole pairs and increasing the CO2 conversion efficiency.

On the one hand, many catalysts are capable of reducing CO2 to CO with high activity and selectivity. Nevertheless, it is not easy to transform CO2 to higher added value products (C2 and C2+ products) due to a lack of effective catalysts. On the other hand, the moderate adsorption energy of CO on copper is currently regarded as the key step for its activation for further reduction and C–C bond formation of C2+ products.51,52 Specifically, for the first route, the *COH intermediate determines C2H6 and CH3COO−. Then, the selectivity determining intermediate (SDI) −*CH2 serves as the key intermediate for the formation of specific C2 products. To be specific, a C2H6 product is formed via *CH3 dimerization, while the formation of CH3COO− needs CO insertion into *CH2. For the second route, the precursor of the C2H6O2 product is *CHO, which is also the precursor of CH4. Subsequently, CO insertion and further proton-coupled electron transfer steps generate C2H6O2 products. Unfortunately, this pathway will yield very small amounts of C2 products due to high selectivity to the CH4 product. For the third route, C2H4, CH3CH2OH, and n-propanol (n-C3H7OH) formation possess the same *CO intermediate, in which C2H4 is considered as the most important C2 product. There are a variety of pathways for the formation of C2H4, such as *CO–COH coupling, *CO dimerization, CO insertion mechanism and “carbene”, etc.53 In actual fact, the generally accepted pathway is the *CO dimerization. Firstly, the C–C formation step as a nonelectrochemical step is the rate-determining step (RDS). Secondly, the *CO–CO dimer is formed by a series of electron transfer and protonation steps. It will form the *CH2CHO intermediate, which is the key intermediate for different C2 products.53,54 More specifically, for strong binding of CO–CO−, all C2 pathways are limited by 2*CO → *CO–COH, while for weak CO–CO− binding, the C2H4 pathway is limited by *CH2CHO → *O + C2H4, then *CH2CHO → *CH3CHO → *CH3CH2O limits the C2H5OH pathway.55 In addition, it can be observed that formation of C2H5OH is more difficult than reduction of *CH2CHO to C2H4, which is attributed to its higher energetic barrier (∼0.2 eV).56 The explanation can be further confirmed by Hori's report.57 To obtain high selectivity for C2H5OH formation, some nanotechnology strategies were introduced to improve the energetics of intermediate binding on the engineered Cu surfaces.58

3. Origin of Cu-based photocathode instability

According to earlier research by Allen Bard and others, the stability of the semiconductors depends on the alignment of the material's self-reduction (Φred) and self-oxidation potential (Φox) relative to E(H2O/H2) and E(O2/H2O) under PEC conditions.64,65 It is thermodynamically unstable for a material to have Φred below E(H2O/H2) and Φox above E(O2/H2O), as shown in Fig. 5a and b. While Φred is located between the conduction band of a material and E(H2O/H2), the stability of a material is decided by the relative split between the HER (kHER) and the electron consumption rates for material self-reduction, namely, the branching ratio. Similarly, there is a competitive reaction between the self-oxidation reaction and the consumption rates of photo-generated carriers for water oxidation (kOER).66

As for Cu-based photocathodes (such as Cu2O), the corresponding Φred and Φox are between the E(H2O/H2) and E(O2/H2O), as displayed in Fig. 5c. Thus, it is thermodynamically unstable. In addition to this, Ng et al. proved that Cu2O tends to occur more in self-oxidation reactions than self-reduction reactions. Namely, it is certain that the dominant photocorrosion pathway of Cu2O is the self-oxidation into CuO under photocatalytic conditions. When applying the external bias for PEC measurement, Cu2O favours self-reduction into Cu.67 Therefore, Cu-based photocathodes can undergo self-oxidation or self-reduction or both under PEC conditions. To this end, it is essential to understand the dominant photocorrosion pathway before undertaking any steps to prevent it, whether in the field of PEC water splitting or CO2 reduction.

4. Cu-Based oxides

Cu-containing oxide photocathodes have been widely studied due to their relatively inexpensive processing costs, ideal absorber materials and the promising production of C2+ hydrocarbons. Furthermore, most of the Cu-based oxides have more negative conduction band edges in comparison with the CO2 reduction potential. Thus, the CO2 reduction is thermodynamically favourable. And for Cu-based oxides, (1) the roughened morphology of Cu species can affect the local pH, favoring the C–C coupling pathway, and (2) many factors like high density of grain boundaries, defects, or uncoordinated sites determine the formation of *CO reaction intermediates, which is the key element for the C–C bond formation. Theoretical studies have shown that Cu+ can cooperate with Cu to promote CO2 activation and *CO dimerization. The Mulliken charge of C is postive in *CO when *CO attches to Cu+, and it is negative when *CO attaches to Cu.68 Therefore, it is accepted that the attractive electrostatics between C of OC–Cu+ and C of OC–Cu contributes to C–C bond formation.59 Significantly, it is still unclear whether the role of Cu+ serves as a direct active site or the precursor to modulate the electronic structure of Cu. Besides this, the serious photocorrosion of Cu-based oxide photocathodes limited further development in PEC CO2 reduction. Thus, it is essential to design photocathodes properly for PEC CO2 reduction. In this section, plenty of effective strategies proposed to improve the stability of photocathodes and enhance the CO2 conversion efficiency are summarized. For instance, coating a protection layer or integrating with a cocatalyst on the surface of photocathodes would significantly enhance the stability and efficiency.69,70 More importantly, modulating the valence state of Cu is also beneficial for the generation of C2 products with high value added.

4.1 Cu2O

Owing to its low toxicity, inexpensive and environmentally benign nature, cuprous oxide (Cu2O) has been one of the most widely studied candidates in the PEC CO2 reduction system.71,72 Furthermore, Cu2O possesses a relatively narrow band gap (ca. 1.9–2.2 eV), which is favorable for harvesting visible light. Even more importantly, Cu2O can provide ease of CO2 reduction due to its sufficiently more negative conduction band and abundant Cu active sites for CO2 reduction. Theoretically, the maximum photocurrent density calculated from its band gap can reach 14.7 mA cm−2 and the maximum solar-to-fuel conversion efficiency is 18% based on the AM 1.5 G illumination.73 However, the current practical photocurrent density and solar-to-fuel conversion efficiency of a Cu2O photocathode are much lower than the theoretical ones, which is mainly limited by the fact that Cu2O suffers from serious photocorrosion in aqueous solution.60,74 Although it is a common issue for most narrow band gap semiconductor materials to experience photocorrosion, many previous studies demonstrated that photocorrosion of the photocathode would greatly influence the conversion efficiency due to the redox reaction of Cu2O itself.75 As a consequence, intelligently designing a protective layer on the surface of Cu2O is the key factor for the long period of Cu2O in PEC CO2 reduction.
4.1.1 Depositing overlayer and conformal cocatalyst for improved stability. To relieve the photocorrosion of Cu2O photocathodes, a feasible solution is to introduce a protective layer. As early as 2011, Thimsen and co-workers put forward a protective layer to suppress the rapid photocorrosion of Cu2O photocathodes in the field of PEC water splitting for the first time.73 In the report, the authors introduced a TiO2 layer and an Al-doped ZnO layer in conjunction with a Cu2O photocathode as a protection and buffer layer, respectively. Then, a hydrogen evolution catalyst (Pt) was deposited on the surface of the TiO2 protective layer. Electrochemical test results showed that the photocurrent density of Pt/TiO2/Al-doped ZnO/Cu2O photocathode can maintain 78% of the original current density (5.7 mA cm−2 at 0 V vs. RHE) at the end of a 20 min stability test, while the photocurrent density of a Pt/Cu2O without the buffer and protection layers decayed to 0 within 5 min. This implies the importance of a protection layer.

For the same reason, Michael Grätzel and co-workers proposed that employing an amorphous TiO2 overlayer protected Cu2O with the molecular catalyst Re(tBu-bipy)(CO)3Cl for PEC CO2 reduction.76 As shown in Fig. 6a, the device can achieve a photocurrent density of 2.1 mA cm−2 for selective CO evolution with 100% faradaic efficiency and generate 560 mV photovoltage. More importantly, the photocurrent density of the device can maintain stability for 7 h (Fig. 6b). Furthermore, they demonstrated that the TiO2 protection layer is of great importance to obtain stable photocurrent density and selective CO evolution. In order to confirm the importance of the TiO2 protection layer, they carried out the stability test for unprotected and protected Cu2O. It was found that the photoactivity of unprotected Cu2O was decayed within a few minutes and the gas analysis indicated that the degraded Cu2O photocathode produced large amounts of H2 instead of CO (Fig. 6d). Scanning electron microscopy and X-ray diffraction analyses further revealed the emergence of metallic Cu on the unprotected Cu2O on account of corrosion under reaction conditions (Fig. 6c and e). In addition to this, a Cu+-incorporated crystalline TiO2 (TiO2–Cu+) shell acted as a protective overlayer and coated on the Cu2O nanowires for PEC CO2 reduction.77 The study showed that the photocurrent density of a Cu2O photocathode with the TiO2–Cu+ overlayer increased 5.2-fold in comparison with bare Cu2O after 30 min of PEC reduction of CO2. The faradaic efficiency (FE) increased to 56.5% from 23.6% when bare Cu2O photocathodes were modified by TiO2–Cu+ coating. Simultaneously, they found that Cu+ species embedded in a TiO2 layer enhanced the adsorption of CO2 on TiO2 surfaces and Cu+ state on a TiO2 surface became the dominating active site for selectively-produced methanol during PEC CO2 reduction. This work provides a theoretical guide for the photocorrosion issues in spite of relatively low faradaic efficiency compared with other reports mentioned above.

Besides the inorganic species overlayer, an organic thin film (oligoaniline) was also developed to protect Cu2O against photocorrosion for PEC CO2 reduction processes.78 It revealed that the oligoaniline over-coated Cu2O can adsorb CO2 on the surface. Moreover, the XPS analysis signified no signs of degradation to Cu or CuO, further confirming the better stabilizing effect of oligoaniline. Nevertheless, the oligoaniline-modified copper(I) oxide photocathode is not good for the selectivity of products. Generally, there existed various products, including methanol (predominant product), some ethanol and possibly formic acid. As mentioned above, addition of surface overlayers can relieve the photocorrosion of Cu2O efficiently, however, this is usually performed via an expensive atomic layer deposition method, and effective catalytic sites are hard to be kept. Under ideal conditions, the surface coating materials need to meet two criteria: improving the stability of Cu2O photocathodes and affording the active sites for CO2 activation. Recently, Xiong et al. loaded a metal–organic framework (MOF) layer on the surface of a Cu2O photocathode by a facile in situ method.28 In this regard, the coated MOF (Cu3(BTC)2) layer played an important role in PEC CO2 reduction by protecting a Cu2O underlayer from photocorrosion, offering active sites and facilitating electron transfer. Ultrafast spectroscopy analysis further confirmed that the interface between Cu2O and Cu3(BTC)2 could effectively promote charge transfer from Cu2O to MOF for CO2 reduction (Fig. 7).

Historically, it has been an effective approach to deposit a surface coating material to avoid photocorrosion. It is worth noting that a buffer layer (e.g. Al-doped ZnO layer) is necessary before depositing the protective layer (e.g. TiO2 layer) on the surface of Cu2O photocathodes, implying recombination loss at the Cu2O/TiO2 interface.73 The intermediate layer provided a more uniform hydroxylated surface for TiO2 to grow on, which favored the charge separation at the Cu2O/TiO2 interface. Furthermore, an atomic layer deposited (ALD) technique was developed to introduce the buffer layer on a Cu2O photoelectrode. However, ALD is difficult for large-scale applications due to its complexity and expense, and some materials are difficult or even impossible to use with the ALD technique as a result of the requirements for a precursor with high vapour pressure and good thermal stability.79

Very recently, Ng's group developed a pulsed electrodeposition method that can effectively control the morphology, thickness and distribution of the deposited thin films.79 The detailed synthesis process and spatial distribution of the TiO2/Cu2O and TiO2/ZnO/Cu2O photoelectrodes are exemplified in Fig. 8a and b. Herein, the uniform coverage of ZnO amorphous layers synthesized by pulsed electrodeposition can not only provide invaluable fine control but also prevent the corrosion of Cu2O at a continuous high bias. Then, time-resolved photoluminescence (TRPL) results showed that the energetic electrons from the excited Cu2O can be transferred to TiO2 by the ZnO layer (Fig. 8c). This novel strategy can greatly improve the stability and charge separation efficiency of Cu2O, leading to a 1.5-fold increase in photocurrent density compared with the TiO2/Cu2O photoelectrode. Although the CO2 reduction performance is not referred to in this report, it offers a versatile and effective method for PEC CO2 conversion.

4.2.2 Forming heterojunctions for improved stability and charge separation efficiency. In addition to the surface coating, the formation of heterojunctions is another possible approach to solve the stability issue and enhance the charge separation efficiency of Cu2O photocathodes. A Cu/Cu2O electrode prepared by electrochemical deposition was performed for PEC CO2 reduction in 0.1 M Na2CO3/NaHCO3 electrolyte.80 The experimental results indicated that the faradaic efficiency of CO2/CH3OH reached 75% under ultraviolet-visible (UV-Vis) irradiation for 2 h with an applied bias of 0.2 V vs. Ag/AgCl. The authors found that parameters including electrolyte, pH and applied potential affected the production of methanol and ethanol markedly. Besides this, they demonstrated that the stabilization of Cu(I) for the photocathode played an important role in the PEC CO2 reduction process. However, further studies were not conducted for the selectivity of products and electrode stability.

To further study the product evolution during CO2 photoreduction at Cu/Cu2O electrodes, Zanoni and co-workers focused on the product distribution (such as methanol, ethanol, formaldehyde, acetaldehyde, and acetone) at a Cu/Cu2O electrode (1 × 1 cm2) during PEC CO2 reduction processes.81 In the study, the authors observed that the products formed in turn are methanol, acetaldehyde and acetone for a short period (<30 min) and longer accumulation periods (>120 min). More importantly, they proposed a self-consistent reaction scheme responsible for the distribution of the products CH3OH, C2H5OH, CH2O, C2H4O, and CH3COCH3. As shown in Fig. 9, the products (e.g. CH3OH, C2H5OH, CH2O, C2H4O, and CH3COCH3) of CO2 conversion relied on the formation of radicals such as OH˙, H˙, and CH3˙ during the PEC CO2 reduction process.

Next, Xia and co-workers achieved a better conversion of CO2 by combining metal electrodes with semiconductor thin films.82 At first, the authors deposited p-type and n-type Cu2O thin films onto a Cu substrate, respectively. Additionally, the morphology and conduction type of Cu2O interfered markedly with PEC CO2 reduction activity. Under similar morphology, Cu/Cu2O (p-type) electrodes had a higher CO2 conversion efficiency than the Cu/Cu2O (n-type), which is attributed to Cu/Cu2O (p-type) electrodes supplying more electrons for CO2 reduction (Fig. 10a). To facilitate charge separation and control product selectivity, metals (i.e. Ag, Au, Cd, Cu, Pb, and Sn) decorating CuO/Cu2O hybrid electrodes were developed.74 After introducing these metals into CuO/Cu2O photocathodes, a more efficient electron transfer from semiconductor to electrolyte was achieved through reducing the recombination of photo-generated charges. At −1.6 V vs. RHE, Pb/CuO/Cu2O gave rise to 0.524 μmol h−1 cm−2 for formic acid and 0.102 μmol h−1 cm−2 for methanol with 40.45% of total faradaic efficiency among these metals. Considering this, the stability of p-type Cu2O photocathodes should deserve wide attention. Kulesza et al. constructed a bifunctional p–n junction made of copper(I) oxide (Cu2O) and titanium oxide (TiO2−x) capable of driving PEC reduction of CO2.83 The hybrid system is beneficial for increasing the electron density and improving the overall conductivity. In view of the hydrophilic and hydrophobic properties of Nafion, the outer-most ultra-thin film of Nafion can act as the protective layer and wetting (favoring proton mobility) medium. Finally, the Nafion-protected mixed-oxides (Cu2O–TiO2−x) system achieved a cathodic photocurrent density of 1.2 mA cm−2 at 0.1 V vs. RHE with remarkable stability. Here, TiO2 treated by the hydrogen flame had many oxygen defects, which would provide better charge mobility and lower extent of recombination. As such, the photogenerated charge carries can be better applied to the CO2 reduction path (Fig. 11b). Although the performance of PEC CO2 reduction can be enhanced by virtue of the heterostructure design, the reduction ability is often limited by inferior electron transfer, particularly for type-II semiconductor/semiconductor heterostructures. To overcome this limitation and retain high redox ability at the same time, Hsu and co-workers introduced the Z-scheme charge transfer mechanism into the heterostructure design, namely, TiO2–Au-supported Cu2O heterostructures.84 The synthetic procedures for preparation of TiO2–Au–Cu2O and TiO2–Cu2O are illustrated in Fig. 11c. Remarkably, the photocurrent density of −1.82 mA cm−2 can be reached at 0.11 V vs. RHE for TiO2–Au–Cu2O photocathodes toward PEC CO2 reduction (Fig. 11d). In the study, the embedded Au particles as a charge transfer mediator facilitated charge transfer from the conduction band of TiO2 to the valence band of Cu2O. The vectorial charge transfer pathway was beneficial to electron aggregation in the conduction band of Cu2O and holes at the valence band of TiO2, which made TiO2–Au–Cu2O photocathodes with a high redox ability for PEC CO2 reduction. For comparison, the severe interfacial charge recombination between Cu2O and TiO2 would lead to poor CO2 reduction ability (Fig. 11e). Even more importantly, the observation from time-resolved photoluminescence spectra and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy indicated that the Au-mediated Z-scheme also improved interfacial charge transfer significantly (Fig. 11f–g).

4.3.3 Loading surface cocatalysts for improved PEC CO2 conversion efficiency. Surface area and photogenerated charge carrier transfer mobility are also two key factors to improve the PEC CO2 conversion efficiency of the Cu2O-based photocathodes. The loading of surface cocatalysts (e.g. CO2-reduction cocatalyst) has been demonstrated as an effective approach to promote the charge transfer and thus the surface reaction kinetics.86 As mentioned above (Fig. 4a), molecular complexes have been widely employed as CO2-reduction cocatalysts to improve charge transfer and the selectivity of products. For instance, Grätzel's group adopted a TiO2-protected Cu2O photocathode coupled with a molecular catalyst Re(tBu-bipy)(CO)3Cl for the PEC reduction of CO2.76 In the system, they found a serious charge-transfer limitation from the surface of the TiO2 protective layer to the Re-based molecular cocatalyst. Interestingly, the simple addition of a protic additive such as methanol or n-propanol could overcome the limitation. The protic additive can avoid the electrostatic repulsion between a molecular cocatalyst and Cu2O photocathodes by changing the charge state of the catalyst intermediate. Nevertheless, the reaction with a protic additive is not an ideal CO2 reduction reaction and the cost of the protic additive is high. To figure out the issue, a rhenium-containing CO2 reduction catalyst was attached on the surface of a protected Cu2O-based photocathode in the following year.60 In order to eliminate charge-transfer limitations between the photocathode and the molecular cocatalyst without the addition of extra protic additives, a nanostructured TiO2 scaffold layer, formed at low temperature, was introduced on the surface of the protected Cu2O photocathodes for the first time. Afterwards, a covalent combination between the Re-based molecular cocatalyst and the TiO2 scaffold layer was formed using phosphonate linkers, which made the homogeneous catalyst heterogenized (Fig. 12a). This strategy could vastly facilitate charge transfer from the TiO2-modified Cu2O photocathodes to the surface cocatalyst, which gave rise to a 40-fold enhancement of the photocurrent density in comparison with planar devices (Fig. 12b).

In the report, the authors found that a prenucleation process was the key factor for reducing the Cu2O nanoparticles and narrowing the particle size distribution (Fig. 13c, d, and f). Then, a five-fold higher electrical conductivity and a dramatically increased photocurrent were achieved for the CNTs/Cu2O hybrid film compared with the pure Cu2O film. Moreover, the observation from long term photoelectrolysis measurements manifested that the hybrid film was more stable than the Cu2O alone (Fig. 13g). The enhanced photocurrent and charge transport property can obtain further evidence by the EIS and voltammetry results. In general, all these advancements were due to the introduction of the highly conductive carbon nanotubes and superior photoactive Cu2O nanoparticles, which is beneficial to transfer photogenerated electrons to the Cu2O/electrolyte interfaces and the photogenerated holes to the supporting electrode. But the detailed CO2 reduction product was not tested by gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC–MS) in this work, and only liquid products were detected (Fig. 13h).

4.2 CuO

Cupric oxide (CuO) is a promising p-type binary oxide with an energy bandgap of 1.3–1.6 eV, which has been confirmed as a photocathode for PEC CO2 reduction. Moreover, CuO possesses a wider photoresponse region over a substantial portion of the solar spectrum due to a narrower band gap compared with Cu2O. In theory, CuO should be capable of generating a higher photocurrent than Cu2O.89,90 Additionally, the plentiful and relatively less toxic component elements are additional advantages for CuO from a sustainability and toxicity perspective. More importantly, the negative conduction band edge positions provide a higher conversion efficiency for CO2 reduction.91 Despite these advantages, less attention has been paid to CuO than to Cu2O. On one hand, it can be attributed to the prior uncertainty in the thermodynamic feasibility. On the other hand, similar to Cu2O, rapid photocorrosion of CuO is also the main issue that restricts the application of CuO as a photocathode for PEC CO2 reduction.92
4.2.1 Constructing the heterojunction structure for enhanced stability and efficiency. Constructing the heterojunction structure with CuO and another material is a solution for avoiding photocorrosion. For instance, a Cu2O–CuO core–shell nanorod configuration was prepared on Cu substrates for the first time by a simple two-step hybrid thermal growth/electrodeposition approach (Fig. 15a–e).90 The hybrid CuO–Cu2O semiconductor nanorod arrays can drive PEC CO2 reduction into methanol at potential of ∼800 mV that are below the thermodynamic threshold value, and the corresponding faradaic efficiency can reach ∼95%. Furthermore, methanol photogenerated at −0.2 V vs. SHE could be detected by a gas chromatograph equipped with a mass spectrometer (GC–MS). The excellent PEC performance was mainly ascribed to the band-edge positions of both oxides (Fig. 15f). It can be observed that the differences in the band edges of the two oxides promoted the transfer of photogenerated electrons from the Cu2O shell to the CuO core. At the same time, the photogenerated electrons from Cu2O could also directly participate in PEC CO2 reduction since the Cu2O shell is in contact with the electrolyte. Compared with the single Cu2O film, this double pathway for the injection of photoelectrons into CO2 was beneficial to improve the PEC CO2 reduction performance of CuO–Cu2O nanorod arrays. It is important to note that, in this work, it is necessary for the electrolyte to be in contact with the tip of the CuO core. Only in this way can the double injection pathway be sustained.

In addition to this, in 2016, Brito and co-workers constructed a Ti/TiO2/CuO n–p junction semiconductor with high porosity by using a dip-coating technique for PEC CO2 reduction.94 The Ti/TiO2/CuO n–p junction semiconductor presented a charge transfer and reduction mechanism as follows (Fig. 17a). The photo-generated electrons from the surface of TiO2 were quickly transferred to the Cu2+ sites, reducing the Cu2+ species to Cu+. After that, CO2 reduction occurred on the Cu+ sites, along with the reoxidation of Cu+ to Cu2+. As a result of this behavior, the PEC CO2 reduction process can proceed smoothly. It should be noted that, in this study, the type of electrolyte influence not only the CO2 reduction performance, but also the product selectivity (Fig. 17b and c). When the supporting electrolyte was 0.1 mol L−1 K2SO4, the products were methanol, ethanol, and acetone. Among them, methanol was the primary product with a conversion efficiency of 91%. When 0.1 mol L−1 NaHCO3 was used as the electrolyte, methanol production was achieved with a higher conversion efficiency of 97% following 2 h of reaction at the applied potential of −0.6 V vs. Ag/AgCl. The enhanced performance in the NaHCO3 electrolyte was attributed to the better adsorption of CO2.

Inspired by the reports that nanoparticle films covered on the semiconductor surface can not only improve the kinetics of hole reactions with electrolytes on account of higher penetration of electrolytes, they also alter the material conductivity as well as the adsorption of analytes on the substrate. Given this feature, Brito's group constructed a p–n junction semiconductor based on CuO nanospheres (NsCuO) covering the outside of TiO2 nanotubes (NtTiO2).95 They found that the NtTiO2/NsCuO material possessed smaller charge transfer resistance, smaller flat band potential and a wider optical absorption range compared to the bare NtTiO2 or Ti/TiO2 nanoparticles modified by larger size particles of CuO, namely Ti/TiO2/CuO (Fig. 17d–f). And, under UV-vis irradiation, the NtTiO2/NsCuO material promoted the selective reduction of dissolved CO2 to methanol with 57% faradaic efficiency at a lower potential of +0.2 V vs. Ag/AgCl in 0.1 mol L−1 K2SO4 electrolyte (pH = 8). Surprisingly, they observed that the methanol formation was significantly increased in the presence of hydroxyls (0.62 mmol L−1) and hole scavengers (0.71 mmol L−1), such as p-nitrosodimethylaniline (RNO) or glucose in the supporting electrolyte. The corresponding conclusion indicated that partial reduction products can be reoxidized to CO2 and H2O if the product still came into contact with OH species. The nanostructured NtTiO2/NsCuO electrode obtained the maximum conversion to methanol (generated 0.71 mmol L−1 methanol) in 0.1 mol L−1 K2SO4 (pH = 8) containing 10.0 mmol L−1 glucose. The transfer mechanism of the NtTiO2/NsCuO was based on the Z-scheme mechanism (Fig. 17g). The electrons located in the conduction band of TiO2 can be captured by holes in the VB of CuO. Therefore, more electrons in the conduction band of CuO can participate in the CO2 reduction reaction. More importantly, they point out that the nanostructured heterojunction was beneficial to generate more efficient electrons for CO2 reduction. Furthermore, they also explained that the methanol was oxidized by a more efficient hole, which was further evidenced by the results of RNO discoloration (proportional to hydroxyl generation). Meanwhile, they also observed that the non-nanostructured electrode (Ti/TiO2/CuO) gave rise to fewer hydroxyl radicals. Whereas, the nanostructured NtTiO2/NsCuO semiconductor could generate the same amount of methanol with the non-nanostructured Ti/TiO2/CuO. The result could be indicative that fewer hydroxyl radicals do not interfere with the CO2 reduction when using the non-nanostructured electrode.

For a complete nanostructured electrode, electrons and holes were separated efficiently with fast charge transfer. At the same time, the reduction of products was also consumed rapidly. Although a large amount of heterogeneous catalysis has been done to improve the performance of CO2 reduction, the selective generation of products remains a challenge. A CuFeO2/CuO mixed binary oxide was prepared by an electrodeposition method for PEC CO2 reduction.96 The composite can selectively produce acetate or formate by varying the Fe:Cu atomic fraction, which is different from other reports. The experiment results demonstrated the CuFeO2/CuO mixed binary oxide could convert CO2 to acetate with 80% faradaic efficiency at −0.4 V vs. Ag/AgCl (pH = 7) under visible light illumination. In addition to this, the authors also found that formate is the primary reaction product when the concentration of Cu is higher than Fe in the catalyst. The primary reaction product is the acetate with a faradaic selectivity of 80% while Fe:Cu atomic fraction within the mixed Fe–Cu oxide catalysts is 1.3 (Fig. 18a and b). Kinetic studies reveal that the Fe–Cu mixed oxide catalyst mainly produces acetate in the PEC CO2 reduction process. Compared to bare CuO and Fe2O3 catalysts, the selective Fe–Cu mixed oxide catalysts displayed higher yield and faradaic efficiency of acetate production after 2 hours reduction reaction at −0.4 V vs. Ag/AgCl under UV-Vis light illumination (Table 2). Also, it can be observed that the CuFeO2/CuO mixed oxide was partially etched under the reaction conditions, which could be due to the reductive dissolution of photo-excited Fe3+ in reducing environments. Unfortunately, the loss of Fe would cause instability of the catalyst. Moreover, kinetic studies indicated that the formation of acetate deactivated rapidly in the first 10 minutes of the reaction. To this end, it demonstrates that understanding the competing kinetics of CO2 reduction and Fe3+ reductive dissolution better is crucial to stabilize the catalyst for long-term electrolysis.

4.3 CuFeO2

CuFeO2 is a p-type Cu1+-containing oxide that becomes more and more attractive in terms of easy obtainment of the raw material, favorable band gap (1.5 eV), non-toxicity, and high stability. Moreover, CuFeO2 possesses a delafossite structure. In the crystal structure, Cu 3d orbitals combined with the O 2p orbitals are more covalent compared with typical metal–oxygen bonds in other oxides, promoting the charge carriers to move freely. This feature can improve the photocatalyst efficiency. Thus, CuFeO2 is supposed to have great application prospects in the field of photoelectrocatalysis. For example, Bocarsly and co-workers synthesized CuFeO2 photocathodes applied for PEC CO2 reduction.100 Unlike prior materials synthesized by an electrodeposition method,98 the authors prepared the Mg-doped CuFeO2 material by a straightforward high-temperature solid-state method. 0.05% Mg-doped CuFeO2 electrodes yielded a photocurrent density up to −1.0 mA cm−2 (Fig. 20a), which was mainly attributed to the improved conductivity of the material by addition of a Mg2+ dopant. At an applied potential of −0.4 V vs. SCE, the incident photon-to-current efficiency of the Mg-doped CuFeO2 photocathode could reach 14% at 340 nm. Simultaneously, the conduction band edge of the Mg-doped CuFeO2 photocathode was calculated to be −1.1 V vs. SCE (pH = 6.8), which is higher than CO2 reduction potentials (Fig. 20b). Thus, the p-type Mg-doped CuFeO2 electrodes were capable of reduction of CO2 to formate at an unparalleled underpotential without a cocatalyst.

4.4 CuBi2O4

CuBi2O4 is a p-type Cu-based oxide that contains Cu2+. From a structural point of view, CuBi2O4 has a tetragonal crystal structure that is composed of square planar Cu(II)O4 groups and trigonal Bi(III)O6 polyhedra.102 The band gap of CuBi2O4 is 1.5–1.8 eV. Thus, CuBi2O4 served as a good light absorber and has been employed as a photocathode for PEC water splitting and CO2 reduction.103,104 However, CuBi2O4 can only utilize photons with energies above ∼1.7 eV.103,105 Consequently, a photocurrent smaller than 1 mA cm−2 for water reduction is obtained on CuBi2O4. Intrinsic instability, low carrier mobility and apparent surface recombination may also be responsible.106,107 Thus, several attempts should be focused to improve the above drawbacks of CuBi2O4.

According to Fig. 1, the negative conduction band potential of CuBi2O4 is capable of CO2 reduction. The first investigation in 2017 also confirmed that CuBi2O4 has the activity of CO2 reduction. But the reduction products have not been explicitly identified.108 To identify the relevant product and assess its performance in PEC CO2 reduction, He et al. reported a nanoporous CuBi2O4 photocathode with a series of film thicknesses.109 They found that the CuBi2O4-4 min photocathode reaches a maximum photocurrent value (Fig. 22a). IMPS results demonstrated that the CuBi2O4-4 min photocathode possesses a faster charge transfer rate compared with other samples (Fig. 22b). Then, the Nyquist plot of IMPS further shows that the electron transfer time of the CuBi2O4-4 min photocathode is shortest among all the samples (Fig. 22c). Finally, only CO is detected over p-type CuBi2O4-4 min during PEC CO2 reduction (Fig. 22d). Even so, photocathode corrosion is still a major obstacle for high efficiency of CO2 reduction. Given this, their research group introduced a protective coating layer (TiO2) on CuBi2O4 photocathodes.110 The influence of a TiO2 protective layer on the activity and selectivity of CO2 reduction was also studied. The highest CO2 reduction product yield is obtained when decorating a thin TiO2 layer on the surface of CuBi2O4 photocathodes (Fig. 22e and f). This phenomenon may be attributed to the electrode/electrolyte interface and efficient separation of photogenerated electrons/holes caused by the CuBi2O4/TiO2 p–n junction. Moreover, with an increase in thickness of the TiO2 coating layer, the spacing between particles decreases, as shown in Fig. 22g. Due to the limited mass transport, the reactants are easier to exhaust when narrowing the gap. Besides this, the local pH, exposed active site and different electronic energy band diagram are also key factors for the difference in CO production selectivity.

4.5 CuFe2O4

CuFe2O4 is also a p-type semiconductor with an inverse spinel structure, in which electron hopping can occur. The existence of a cation site can serve as the basic side for the CO2 adsorption in the CuFe2O4. These features make it attractive to be used as a photocathode. The p-type CuFe2O4 was first investigated as a photocathode for PEC CO2 reduction by Khan and co-workers in 2018.111 Compared with photocatalytic (PC) reduction of CO2, PEC CO2 reduction can reduce the electron/hole (e−/h+) recombination in the CuFe2O4 by applying a bias potential. They observed that methanol is the only product in the liquid phase. Moreover, methanol yield is higher in PEC systems than photocatalysis and electrocatalysis systems, implying the advantages of a photoelectric synergy effect. At −0.5 V vs. NHE, the faradaic efficiency was 62%, while the quantum efficiency was calculated to be only 14.4%. They explained that the formation of competitive gas products limited quantum efficiency during CO2 reduction. Further investigation should be done to improve the methanol yield in the future.

In order to further improve quantum efficiency, their group developed a hetero-structure composite of CdS–CuFe2O4 by ultrasound-assisted sol–gel methods.112 Compared to CuFe2O4, the CdS–CuFe2O4 photocatalyst presented a ∼6 times higher photocurrent (−0.35 V vs. NHE) under CO2 conditions. Product analysis showed that the CdS–CuFe2O4 catalyst can produce methanol selectivity with trace amounts of H2 and CO. The faradaic and quantum efficiencies were demonstrated to be 72% and 16.9%, respectively (Fig. 23a). Mechanism investigation revealed that the photogenerated electrons generated by CuFe2O4 can transfer to the conduction band (CB) of the CdS for CO2 reduction. The holes located in the valence band (VB) of CuFe2O4 promote water oxidation (Fig. 23b). In addition to this, graphene oxide (GO) with different GO content was also incorporated into copper ferrite (CuFe2O4) for PEC CO2 reduction in 2019.113 They found that the PEC activity for CO2 reduction can be improved when modifying with GO. In particular, a 5% GO–CuFe2O4 hybrid photocatalyst displayed the highest methanol yield, which increased by ∼90% compared with the CuFe2O4 (Fig. 23c). The improved performance could be attributed to inhibiting the charge recombination and ensuring the accessibility of free charge carriers to support the catalytic activity. The underlying mechanism is presented as follows. GO sheets can trap the photoinduced electrons from CuFe2O4, then GO could act as an active site for the reduction of CO2 (Fig. 23d), thus suppressing the e−/h+ recombination.

4.6 Other Cu-based oxide

Beyond that, most of the works focused on developing other p-type semiconducting Cu-based oxides as photocathodes for PEC reduction of CO2. For example, a new p-type metal–oxide semiconductor (Cu3Nb2O8) was developed by Ohno's group through a spin coating and metal organic decomposition method.114 In the report, they demonstrated that the band gap of Cu3Nb2O8 photocathodes was estimated to be 2.5 eV via extrapolation of the absorption band edge. Then, the conduction potential of p-type Cu3Nb2O8 was calculated to be −1.21 V vs. NHE at pH 7 according to the results of Mott–Schottky analysis. As shown in Fig. 24a, the conduction band edge of Cu3Nb2O8 photocathodes was located at a more negative potential compared with the redox potential of CO2, implying that the p-type Cu3Nb2O8 photocathode was feasible for PEC reduction of CO2. Additionally, a strong cathodic photocurrent density of −0.18 mA cm−2 was reached for the p-type Cu3Nb2O8 photocathode at −0.30 V vs. Ag/AgCl (Fig. 24b). Meanwhile, the authors also observed that the p-type Cu3Nb2O8 photocathode presented a gradually decreased cathodic photocurrent with time at −0.20 V vs. Ag/AgCl (Fig. 24c). Interestingly, the cathodic photocurrent of p-type Cu3Nb2O8 photocathode was recovered by thermal annealing in air (Fig. 24d). This phenomenon was usually ascribed to the recovered valence state of copper [from Cu (0) and Cu(I) to Cu(II) species]. In the process of PEC CO2 reduction, the valence state of copper varied from Cu(II) to Cu (0) and Cu(I) species, which could be confirmed by XPS measurement (Fig. 24e). CO was detected as the only reduction product and CO evolution increased with an increase in the applied potential. However, the faradaic efficiency was only 9% obtained by the calculation, suggesting that producing other chemical products or reducing the electrode itself consumed most of the cathodic photocurrent of the p-type Cu3Nb2O8 photocathode. In addition, no other chemical products (such as formic acid, methane, formaldehyde, methanol and ethanol) were observed, as illustrated in Fig. 24f. Thus, it can be inferred that part of the cathodic photocurrent was used in photocorrosion or reduction of the p-type Cu3Nb2O8 photocathode itself.

5. Cu-Based chalcogenide

Apart from the above-described p-type Cu-contained oxide semiconductors, there are many p-type Cu-containing ternary or polynary chalcogenide semiconductors (like CuInS(Se)2, CuZnSnS4, etc.) employed in the area of PEC CO2 reduction. For Cu-based chalcogenides, the corresponding bandgap value is smaller than those of ZnS and CdS. However, in the absence of a CO2 reduction cocatalyst, no reduction product was produced for bare Cu-based chalcogenides, implying that the active center of CO2 reduction was the CO2 reduction cocatalyst.116 Various experimental phenomena indicate that Cu species of Cu-based chalcogenide is not the main active site for CO2 reduction. Usually, the active center of CO2 reduction is the cocatalyst or metal In (Se).

5.1 Cu2ZnSnS4

Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) is a promising p-type Cu+-containing sulfide semiconductor for PEC devices, which is comprised of abundant and relatively inexpensive elements. In addition, CZTS possesses a direct bandgap of 1.5 eV and a large optical absorption coefficient in the order of 104 cm−1. Hence, it can be capable of absorbing visible light. The first study utilizing a CZTS as a photocathode for PEC CO2 reduction was conducted in 2011 by Arai and co-workers, who proposed a Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) photocathode modified by a metal-complex electrocatalyst ([Ru{4,4′-di(1H-pyrrolyl-3-propylcarbonate)-2,2′-bipyridine}(CO)2]n for selective CO2 reduction to formate (HCOO−) in water.116 In this system, the authors emphasized that the Gibbs free energy (ΔG) between the conduction band minimum of semiconductors and the CO2 reduction potential of the metal-complex electrocatalysts is a significant factor for realizing such photocatalytic CO2 reduction (Fig. 26a). The performance of CZTS photocathodes was greatly improved by introducing a metal-complex electrocatalyst, especially, a metal complex with a phosphate ester ligand. Furthermore, they also found that the CO2 reduction activity was enhanced when inserting Se into CZTS photocathodes, which could be attributed to higher carrier mobility compared to bare CZTS photocathodes. Therefore, the sulfide semiconductor modified with a metal-complex electrocatalyst polymer could generate formate with high selectivity (>80%) under visible light irradiation.

5.2 CuInS (Se)2

CuInS2 is another Cu-containing p-type semiconductor. The bandgap of CuInS2 is about 1.5 eV, which can utilize visible light fully and the photoelectric conversion efficiency can theoretically reach 28.5% in the visible light region. Given this, CuInS2 film as a photocathode was also explored for PEC CO2 reduction.118 To fabricate CuInS2 thin films, Yuan et al. deposited Cu–In alloy layers by using an electrodeposition method followed by sulfurization. The as-prepared p-CuInS2 thin film could be applied as the photocathode for the solar-driven PEC reduction of CO2 to methanol. It also maintained stability for 11 h. Even though the p-CuInS2 thin film possessed a lower overpotential of hydrogen evolution, the addition of pyridine could inhibit the hydrogen evolution reaction. After introducing pyridinium ion as the co-catalyst, the selectivity to methanol with a faradaic efficiency of 97% was produced at potential of −0.54 V vs. SCE. Furthermore, the p-CuInS2 thin film possessed a lower overpotential of hydrogen evolution. More remarkably, the addition of pyridine could also inhibit the hydrogen evolution reaction. In this work, however, the mechanism for the PEC reduction of CO2 was still unclear. Furthermore, although pyridine served as a cocatalyst capable of reducing the overpotential of CO2 reduction and restraining hydrogen evolution reactions, the low rate of CO2 reduction with the cocatalysis of pyridine impeded its development in the field of PEC CO2 reduction.

To solve the above-mentioned problem, Yuan et al. investigated the role of pyridine at a CuInS2 thin film electrode in PEC reduction of CO2 to methanol.119 In this study, they found that the molar ratio of Cu to In(Cu/In), crystal size, the applied bias and the coverage of pyridine at the CuInS2 thin electrode had a significant influence on methanol yield in the process of PEC CO2 reduction. More importantly, one reason for slowing down the reduction rate of CO2 was the mass transfer resistance derived from pyridine adsorption layers on CuInS2 photocathodes. Besides this, they also observed that the selectivity to methanol might be in connection with the electron configuration and electronegativity of active sites. The same research group fabricated CuInS2 and CuInS2/graphene hybrid thin films in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) by one-step electrodeposition at 30 °C for PEC CO2 reduction.120 It could be observed that the photocurrent density of CuInS2/graphene hybrid thin film electrodes increased by 2-fold compared to the bare CuInS2 electrodes. To date, the CuInS2 thin films can be fabricated by methods including chemical bath deposition, co-evaporation, spray pyrolysis, sputtering and electrodeposition. However, these methods need expensive equipment. For this purpose, Wang et al. adopted a simple and low-cost sol–gel route to construct CuS/CuO/CuInS2 thin film for PEC CO2 reduction.121 In this system, methanol was the only liquid product. Besides this, the gas products such as H2 and CO were also detected. It can also be observed that the concentration of methanol increases with increasing molar ratio of In/Cu in the thin films. The improved catalytic performance of the film electrodes can be ascribed to the suppressed recombination of electron–hole pairs. Furthermore, CuS can easily transform to CuO during CO2 reduction, which leads to a higher catalytic activity for CuS/CuO/CuInS2 compared to CuS/CuInS2 and CuO/CuInS2 photocathodes.

In addition to CuInS2, copper indium gallium diselenides (Cu(In,Ga)Se2) (CIGS) is an effective p-type semiconductor material, which has a tunable bandgap of 1.0–1.7 eV and a high optical absorption coefficient (105 cm−1). The absorption range can come up to 1100 nm, which is superior to many main-stream semiconductors.122,123 Recently, CIGS was first applied in PEC CO2 reduction. Yu's group constructed a CIGS-based electrode by stacking Mo/CIGS/CdS/ZnO/AZO layer-by-layer (named as CIGS/CdS/ZnO/AZO photocathode), as shown in Fig. 27a.124 A high photocurrent density was achieved on the CIGS/CdS/ZnO/AZO photocathode, which is 4.8 mA cm−2 and 11.5 mA cm−2 at −0.2 and −0.7 V vs. RHE, respectively. The onset potential of the CIGS/CdS/ZnO/AZO photoelectrode was 0.41 V vs. RHE (Fig. 27b). More importantly, the products of CO2 reduction could be controlled by adjusting the bias. At −0.2 V vs. RHE, CIGS/CdS/ZnO/AZO photocathode could produce CO with the faradaic efficiency of 99.3% (Fig. 27c and d). Additionally, density functional calculations indicated that the CIGS/CdS/ZnO/AZO photocathode could generate CO most easily in comparison to other products like HCOOH and H2. When a larger voltage was applied, the multicomponent products like CO, H2, HCOOH and CH3OH could be produced. The bias-dependent property was due to the different adsorption energy of the CO2 reduction intermediate on the surface of the photoelectrode (Fig. 28). This work revealed that the bias determined the products of CO2 reduction.

6. Cu-Based electrocatalyst coupled photocathodes

During CO2 reduction reactions, suitable electrocatalysts can lower the energetic barriers, especially for the multistep proton-assisted electron-transfer process. Among electrocatalysts, metal Cu-based electrocatalysts draw extensive attention due to high abundance, low toxicity and their capability of CO2 conversion into hydrocarbons. More importantly, Cu possesses the unique ability in generating C2 hydrocarbons compared to other metals.125 For Cu electrocatalysts, it possesses the medium adsorption strength for CO and the ability of C–C coupling, which would promote CO reduction to CH4, C2H4, C2H5OH and so on. Therefore, coupling Cu-based electrocatalysts with photocathodes would be a promising way for efficient PEC CO2 reduction reactions.

For instance, a photocathode composed of copper nanoparticles deposited on the TiO2-passivated InP nanopillar for PEC CO2 reduction was developed.126 Profiting from the surface cocatalytic effects of Cu, the as-prepared composite electrode (TiO2-passivated InP photocathode with Cu nanoparticles) improves the selectivity, which produced a methanol yield of up to eight times with a Faraday efficiency of 8.7% (Fig. 29a). Yang and co-workers exploited a synergistic catalytic effect on the Cu nanoparticles (Cu NPs) decorated with TiO2–Au nanotube photonic crystal multi-functional photocathodes for PEC CO2 reduction.127 In this system, the functions of Au-doped TiO2, TiO2 nanotube photonic crystal and Cu nanoparticles were a light harvester, optical channels, and a CO2 reduction electrocatalyst, respectively. Under simulated sunlight illumination, the reduction product was mainly formic acid with a selectivity of 98% and a Faradaic efficiency of 82.6% at potential of −1.0 V vs. Ag/AgCl. More importantly, the yield ratio of formic acid could reach 1019.3 μmol L−1 cm−2 after 8 h reduction reaction, which was nearly 6.3 times higher than the traditional TiO2 nanotubes (Fig. 29b). Such promotion was ascribed to the synergistic effect on this multi-functional interface. First, the exceptional localized surface plasmon resonance derived from Au nanoparticles broadened the light absorption region to the visible region. Second, the photonic crystal layer structure improved the multi-reflection of the incident light. Finally, the specific catalytic effect of the electrocatalyst Cu nanoparticles enhanced the selectivity and activity of PEC CO2 reduction. The enhancement proportions of intrinsic Au nanoparticles, photonic crystal layer and Cu nanoparticles can be obtained by the calculation, which were 91%, 813% and 2028%, respectively. The results indicated that Cu nanoparticles played a great role in promoting PEC CO2 reduction and effectively controlling the generation of formic acid. Simultaneously, the detailed mechanism for the multi-functional synergistic action of Au nanoparticles, photonic crystal layer and Cu nanoparticles for PEC CO2 reduction was proposed (Fig. 29c). In particular, a surface band bending caused by Cu nanoparticles contributed to the electron–hole separation and Cu nanoparticles rendered the adsorption of CO2 through the formation of O–Cu. Then, the carbon atom of chemisorbed CO2 could react with a proton in the solution, resulting in the formation of HCOOads˙. Previous literature has reported that the formation of a stable intermediate was conducive to produce formic acid.128 Accordingly, the multi-functional synergic system enhanced the performance and selectivity of PEC CO2 reduction.

Although many photocathodes have been applied in the field of CO2 reduction, much effort has been devoted to the conversion of CO2 into the 2e− reduced species (such as CO). It remained extremely challenging for the 8e− photoreduction of CO2 into methane (CH4), which was mainly attributed to the kinetic barrier relevant to the multiple protons coupled electron transfer (PCET) process.35 To address the bottleneck, Mi et al. reported a gallium nitride nanowire/Si solar cell photocathode combined with Cu as co-catalyst for the reduction of CO2 under simulated sunlight illumination.129 The composition of the hybrid photocathode is depicted in Fig. 30a. Then, a faradaic efficiency of 19% for CH4 was obtained by the Cu/GaN/n+–p Si photocathode with Cu as co-catalyst at −1.4 V vs. Ag/AgCl, which is more than 30 times higher compared with the 2e− reduced CO (0.6%) (Fig. 30b). It should be noted that the modification of Cu particles is necessary for CH4 generation.

7. Conclusion and outlook

To conclude, we have summarized the recent progress made on the most attractive Cu-based photocathodes, including Cu-based oxides, Cu-based chalcogenides and Cu-based electrocatalyst coupled photocathodes. Some progress has been made after a decade of research, as shown in Fig. 31. Through the discussions in this review, it is expected that newcomers to the field can obtain useful information about designing the Cu-based photocathodes for CO2 reduction. Table 7 summarises the relationship between the products and different Cu-based photocathodes. Note that the stability issue of photocathodes in aqueous electrolytes still exists. To this end, it is imperative to fundamentally understand the cause of instability. Firstly, these photocathodes need a stable chemical surface under illumination, namely, E(A+/A) < CBM < Ere or Eox < VBM < E(B−/B). Secondly, active substances should be avoid to direct contact with aqueous electrolytes. Doping and passivating the surface can improve the surface energy level. Adding a protection layer with appropriate thickness can also maintain the stability of Cu-based photocathodes. Simultaneously, the improvement in the selectivity of CO2 reduction, particularly, to C2 products still requires further research in the future.

For Cu-based oxides, Cu-based binary oxides have relatively simple preparation methods and various nanostructures for different applications. However, severe photocorrosion issues limited further development. To this end, remarkable advancements have been focused on the stability issues, especially for Cu2O, such as depositing surface overlayers, forming heterojunctions and loading cocatalysts etc. Among them, only surface overlayers formed by ALD can maintain stability for 8 h. Unfortunately, the ALD technique is costly and difficult for large-scale applications. Besides this, the stable Cu2O/AZO/TiO2 photocathode is unbeneficial for CO2 reduction. Considering this, a facile method which exposes more active sites of CO2 reduction should be developed to improve the long-term stability of these photocathodes. In comparison with Cu-based binary oxides, the rate of photocorrosion of Cu-based ternary oxide is slower. However, more severe electron–hole recombination results in inferior photoelectrochemical performance. As a consequence, it would be necessary to develop effective strategies to improve electron–hole separation. It is important to note that a ternary oxide is better than a binary oxide because it provides more possibilities for structure and composition tuning.

Even though the Cu-containing chalcogenides have been applied in PEC CO2 reduction, the relevant research concentrates on loading with a cocatalyst. Hence, future efforts in preparing, optimizing, and understanding Cu-containing chalcogenides will be beneficial for constructing the photocathodes with high activity and selectivity. Stable Cu-based electrocatalysts are promising catalysts since they can be formed on the Cu-based photocathode by in situ methods. Therefore, coupling photocathodes with stable Cu-based electrocatalysts can not only improve their photostabilities but also provide new thoughts to improve the selectivity of products.

In the end, we hope that this review of Cu-based photocathodes will offer direction for designing Cu-based photocathodes with high activity and selectivity of CO2 reduction products, especially for CO2 products with higher added value.
