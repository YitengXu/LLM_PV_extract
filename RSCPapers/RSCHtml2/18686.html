Chemical instability leads to unusual chemical-potential-independent defect formation and diffusion in perovskite solar cell material CH3NH3PbI3

Wenmei Ming a, Shiyou Chen ab and Mao-Hua Du *a
aMaterials Science and Technology Division, Oak Ridge National Laboratory, Oak Ridge, TN 37831, USA. E-mail: mhdu@ornl.gov
bKey Laboratory of Polar Materials and Devices (Ministry of Education), East China Normal University, Shanghai 200241, China

First published on 13th October 2016

Abstract

Methylammonium (MA) lead triiodide (MAPbI3) has recently emerged as a promising solar cell material. However, MAPbI3 is known to have chemical instability, i.e., MAPbI3 is prone to decomposition into MAI and PbI2 even at moderate temperatures (e.g. 330 K). Here, we show that the chemical instability, as reflected by the calculated negligible enthalpy of formation of MAPbI3 (with respect to MAI and PbI2), has an unusual and important consequence for defect properties, i.e., defect formation energies in low-carrier-density MAPbI3 are nearly independent of the chemical potentials of constituent elements and thus can be uniquely determined. This allows straightforward calculations of defect concentrations and the activation energy of ionic conductivity (the sum of the formation energy and the diffusion barrier of the charged mobile defect) in MAPbI3. The calculated activation energy for ionic conductivity due to diffusion is in excellent agreement with the experimental values, which demonstrates unambiguously that is the dominant diffusing defect and is responsible for the observed ion migration and device polarization in MAPbI3 solar cells. The calculated low formation energy of a Frenkel pair and low diffusion barriers of and suggest that the iodine ion migration and the resulting device polarization may occur even in single-crystal devices and grain-boundary-passivated polycrystalline thin film devices (which were previously suggested to be free from ion-migration-induced device polarization), leading to device degradation. However, the device polarization due to the Frenkel pair (which has a relatively low concentration) may take a long time to develop and thus may avoid the appearance of the currentvoltage hysteresis at typical scan rates.

I. Introduction

Halide perovskite solar cells, such as methylammonium (MA) lead iodide (CH3NH3PbI3) solar cells, have recently undergone rapid development.1 The power conversion efficiency (PCE) of halide perovskite solar cells has exceeded 22%.2 Currently, chemical and electrical instabilities are major challenges facing the development of MAPbI3 based solar cells.35 MAPbI3 starts to decompose at moderate temperatures (e.g., 330 K).3 The high density of defects and their diffusion lead to unusual phenomena (such as hysteresis in currentvoltage curves,58 giant dielectric constant,7,9 switchable photovoltaic effect,10 photon-induced phase separation,11etc.), which strongly affect the solar cell performance. Defect management is clearly critically important to the improvement of the solar cell efficiency.

Previous density functional theory (DFT) calculations of defect levels in MAPbI3 have provided important understanding on the efficient carrier transport and the high PCE in halide perovskite solar cells.1217 Hybrid density functional calculations show that, among native point defects (including vacancies, interstitials, and antisites), only the iodine interstitial (Ii) and its complexes introduce deep levels inside the band gap of MAPbI3.15 The shallow nature of most of the defects,15 especially the iodine vacancy (which is abundant),18 contributes strongly to the long carrier drifting length in MAPbI3.1922 The defect diffusion barriers have been calculated and iodine related defects were found to be fast diffusers,8,2326 which agrees with some experiments8,27 but is inconsistent with some others.28

Despite the recent progress, the understanding of the defect formation and diffusion in CH3NH3PbI3 is still incomplete. Calculated defect formation energies by different authors showed qualitative differences. For example, between the two most important donor defects, i.e., iodine vacancy and MA interstitial , Yin et al. showed that is more stable than (ref. 12) whereas Buin et al. showed the opposite;13 between the two low-energy acceptor defects, i.e., MA vacancy and iodine interstitial , Yin et al. showed that has a lower formation energy than (ref. 12) whereas Buin et al. showed that the formation energies of the two defects are close to each other and their relative stability depends on the chemical potentials.13 Moreover, the reported defect formation energies change substantially with the elemental chemical potentials,12,13,16 which makes it difficult to determine defect concentrations in MAPbI3. Comparison between theoretical and experimental results on defect concentrations is usually difficult because there is no good method to make a connection between theoretical elemental chemical potentials and experimental growth conditions. The chemical-potential-dependent defect formation energies also prevent unambiguous determination of the activation energy for ionic conductivity (which is the sum of the formation energy and the diffusion barrier of the charged diffusing defect)26 in MAPbI3. The accurate calculation of the activation energy for ionic conductivity in MAPbI3 will help determine the identity of the main diffusing defect in MAPbI3.

MAPbI3 is usually semi-insulating with low free carrier density.29,30 In this paper, we show that, in low-carrier-density MAPbI3, defect formation energies are nearly independent of elemental chemical potentials and the Fermi level. Thus, the defect formation energy and the activation energy for ionic conductivity can be uniquely determined. We demonstrate that the weak dependence of the defect formation energy on chemical potentials is due to the very small (negligible) enthalpy of formation of MAPbI3 with respect to MAI and PbI2. is unambiguously identified as the dominant diffusing defect based on the calculated defect diffusion barriers and the chemical-potential-independent defect formation energies. We further show that the formation of Frenkel pairs and their subsequent diffusion in MAPbI3 may lead to device polarization even in single-crystal and grain-boundary-passivated MAPbI3, which were previously suggested to be free from ion-migration-induced device polarization.

F. Ion migration and device polarization due to Frenkel pairs

Significant ion migration in MAPbI3 has been reported3,8,10,2325,27,28,44,51 and is likely the cause of the currentvoltage hysteresis.58 Ionic conductivity can be caused by the formation and diffusion of vacancy defects. The formation of a vacancy requires moving an atom from the bulk to the surface, interface, or grain boundaries. Thus, passivating grain boundaries should reduce the available vacancies and suppress the hysteresis. Indeed, there are many reports on the elimination of hysteresis by grain boundary passivation (by fullerenes,52,53 chlorine,54 or Spiro-OMeTAD55) and by reducing the area of the grain boundaries by increasing the grain size.56 Recently, Shao et al. showed that ion migration dominates at grain boundaries and that the currentvoltage hysteresis is nearly absent in the interior of the grain.57 These experimental results suggest that the device polarization may be suppressed in single-crystal devices and grain-boundary-passivated devices.

However, ionic conductivity can also be caused by the formation of Frenkel pairs (vacancyinterstitial pairs). A Frenkel pair is formed by displacing an atom from a lattice site to an interstitial site, which does not involve surfaces, interfaces, and grain boundaries. Thus, the ionic conductivity due to the formation of Frenkel pairs cannot be prevented by passivating grain boundaries or increasing the grain size. The formation energy of an isolated and an isolated is only 0.4 eV (see Table 1), which leads to a concentration of about 2 1015 cm3. The diffusion barriers of and are both very low (see Table 2), enabling their fast diffusion at room temperature. Although the concentration of the Frenkel pairs is relatively low, under an external electric field, it would just be a matter of time for a significant amount of Frenkel pairs to be formed and separated, followed by the diffusion of individual vacancies and interstitials and their accumulation near the electron- and hole-selective contacts. However, ion migration caused by the formation of Frenkel pairs and their diffusion may not cause the currentvoltage hysteresis at typical scan rates due to the low concentration of the Frenkel pairs and consequently a low level of device polarization within the time scale of the currentvoltage scan; but it may still cause significant device polarization over an extended period of time (e.g., hundreds of hours).

IV. Conclusions

We show that defect formation energies are nearly independent of elemental chemical potentials and the Fermi level in MAPbI3 if the defect concentration is much higher than the free carrier density. This is due to the negligible enthalpy of formation of MAPbI3 with respect to MAI and PbI2. As a result, the formation energies of are uniquely determined to be 0.08 eV, 0.08 eV, 0.16 eV, 0.32 eV, 1.03 eV, and 0.75 eV, respectively, by DFT-PBE calculations. The calculated concentration of at 300 K is very high (5 1020 cm3). The calculated activation energy for ionic conductivity due to diffusion (the sum of the formation energy and the diffusion barrier of ) is 0.340.42 eV, which is in excellent agreement with the experimental values (0.360.43 eV). This result shows unambiguously that is the dominant diffusing defect in MAPbI3. The calculated low formation energy (0.4 eV) of the Frenkel pair and the low diffusion barriers of and suggest that the iodine ion migration and the resulting device polarization may occur even in single-crystal devices and grain-boundary-passivated polycrystalline thin film devices, which were previously suggested to be free from ion-migration-induced device polarization. However, the device polarization due to the Frenkel pair (which has a relatively low concentration) may take a long time to develop and thus may avoid the appearance of the currentvoltage hysteresis at typical scan rates.
