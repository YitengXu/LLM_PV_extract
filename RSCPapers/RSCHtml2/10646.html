Abstract

Organic photovoltaics (OPVs) represent one of the potential candidates of next-generation solar cells for converting green and sustainable solar energy into electrical power. An OPV cell utilizes a blend of electron donor (D) and electron acceptor (A) materials as photo-active layers, where photogenerated excitons are separated into mobile electrons and holes. Relative to fullerene acceptors, nonfullerene small-molecule acceptors (NF-SMAs) have several advantages such as synthesis-facile chemical modifications and straightforward tunability in absorptivity, spectral coverage, optical band gap, and Frontier molecular orbitals. In the recent 3 years, progress in design and synthesis of fused-ring NF-SMAs with perpendicular side-chains on an electron-rich core, and again, on design and synthesis of wide/medium/low band gap polymer donors have led to realizations of over 13% power conversion efficiencies (PCEs). These rapid advances require timely review articles. In this review article, we focus on this type of fused-ring NF-SMAs reported in the past 3 years, with special attention on their molecular structure design and structureproperty relationships.

1. Introduction

Studies on organic photovoltaics (OPV) are in their 4th decade since the first invention in 1986 by Tang.1 In an OPV device, electron donor (D) and electron acceptor (A) materials are blended to create a bulk-heterojunction to fulfil efficient exciton separation and transport. Both D and A materials are of equal importance. If we focus on the A materials, then OPV history can be divided into two parts the fullerene age and the nonfullerene age after 2013. These include a series of interesting wide band gap (WBG) nonfullerene small-molecule acceptors (NF-SMAs); for example, perylene dimide28 and other types of electron-deficiency macrocycles.912 Reported 34% power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) were achieved from these types of nonfullerene acceptors when pairing with low band gap (LBG) polymer donors.1321 Particular successes came from the invention of the fused-ring NF-SMAs with a ladder-type electron-rich core, such as ITIC 3,9-bis(2-methylene-(3-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)indanone))-5,5,11,11-tetrakis(4-n-hexylphenyl)-dithieno[2,3d:2,3d]-s-indaceno[1,2b:5,6b]dithiophene,22 IEIC 2,2-((2Z,2Z)-((5,5-(4,4,9,9-tetrakis(p-hexylphenyl)-4,9-dihydro-s-indaceno[1,2-b:5,6-b]dithiophene-2,7-diyl)bis(3-(2-ethylhexyl)thiophene-5,2-diyl))bis(methanylylidene))-bis(3-oxo-2,3-dihydro-1H-indene-2,1-diylidene))dimalononitrile),23 and rhodanine flanked IDTBR.24 When pairing with wide/medium/low band gap polymer donors, over 13% PCEs2527 have been reported very recently, which makes OPVs to be a candidate competitive with other types of next-generation solar cells. It has been speculated that if the non-radiation recombination Voc (open-circuit voltage) loss can be reduced to about 0.21 V or the energy loss can be reduced to about 0.5 eV, then PCE with over 18% has a very high possibility within reach for OPVs.28

Although a large step has been achieved, successes with OPVs still largely lagged behind their perovskite counterparts from which over 20% PCEs have been straightforwardly realized. To further improve OPV performance, design and synthesis of new acceptor and donor materials are of particular importance. Relative to recent advances on NF-SMA, several critical reviews have been published including energy loss,29 physics,30,31 molecular design,28,3239 and full reviews. Additional reviews have been published on charge generation pathways31 and all types of nonfullerene acceptors.4043 Very rapid advances are being achieved in this field and this requires a timely review article. In this review article, we focus on successes achieved from the design and synthesis of fused-ring type NF-SMAs which involve an electron-rich core with side-chains perpendicular to its backbone plane and, in particular, achievements published in the past 3 years. In this article, we will shed light on the advances on their molecular structures and the relationship between structure and OPV parameters of Voc, Jsc (short-circuit current-density), and FF (fill-factor). Certainly, the pairing D materials are also included.

2. D:A energy levels alignments and spectral coverage complement

The active layer of an OPV cell is made of a blend of D and A materials. Both D and A materials absorb solar photons and the photogenerated D and A excitons are separated via an electron transfer (path A, Fig. 1a) and hole transfer (path B) under driving forces provided by the DA lowest unoccupied molecular orbit (LUMO) energy offset, ELUMO,DA, and the DA highest occupied molecular orbit (HOMO) energy offset, EHOMO,DA, respectively. To fulfil efficient charge separations, the DA energy levels must be aligned. Because organic semiconductors intrinsically show a narrowed absorption band, complementary spectral coverage between D and nonfullerene acceptor (NF-A) is generally required (Fig. 1b) to more fully cover the solar emission spectrum. Accordingly, LBG D vs. WBG NF-A (nonfullerene acceptor) or WBG D vs. LBG NF-A combinations are generally selected though there are cases reported with the D and A materials having close values of optical band gap (Eoptg).

4. ADA type NF-SMAs with a fused-ring ladder-type core

4.1. ITIC and studies on new donor materials

Since its invention by Zhan et al., ITIC has been used as NF-SMA to study photovoltaic properties of different polymers and small-molecule donor materials (Table 1). Initially, a PCE of 6.80% was obtained with PTB7-Th as the pairing donor. 4.2. Modifications on the terminal A unit

Modifications on the terminal A units were first made on the IC (2-methylene-(3-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)indanone)) unit. Hou et al. found that introduction of one and two methyl units at 5 and 6 positions upshifted the LUMO/HOMO energies from 4.02/5.61 eV (ITIC) to 3.98/5.58 eV (IT-M) and 3.93/5.56 eV (IT-DM), which led to a larger Voc: 0.94 and 0.97 V for IT-M and IT-DM, respectively, when pairing with PBDB-T. Among the three SMAs, the best film-morphology was obtained from the PBDB-T:IT-M system, which gave rise to the largest Jsc and FF, and hence, the highest PCE of 12.2%.64,66 Huang et al. introduced cyclobutane on the 5,6-positions of IC and synthesized the acceptor IDT-HN which gave a Voc of 0.93 V and an FF of 0.764 with a PCE of 10.22%.67 When a stronger electron-donating unit such as methoxyl was introduced at the 5 and 6 positions, the LUMO/HOMO energy levels of the resulting SMAs then were tuned to 3.80/5.52 eV (IT-OM3, at the 5 position) and 3.86/5.49 eV (IT-OM2, at the 6 position), respectively. A Voc of 0.93 and 0.97 V was obtained with PBDB-T as the donor. The IT-OM2 exhibited smaller -stacking distance, larger coherence crystalline length, and larger electron mobility, which was attributed to the larger Jsc and FF.68

Oppositely, introduction of an electron-withdrawing unit, for example, fluorine atoms at the 5,6-positions of the fused-benzene of the IC unit, led to a reduction of the acceptor Eoptg, by which the SMA more fully covered the near infrared solar emission spectrum, which was helpful to obtain a larger Jsc. However, this approach also shifted down the LUMO level and a reduction of Voc was generally observed. Zhan et al. reported that the introduction of fluorine at the IC's 4, 5, or 6, and both 5 and 6 positions in sequence down shifted the SMA's LUMO/HOMO energies, going from 3.88/5.45 eV (without any F substitutions) to 3.97/5.54 eV (one F at 4 position), 3.98/5.52 eV (IC-F), and 4.02/5.52 eV (IC-2F), and meanwhile, the Eoptg was reduced. With FTAZ69 as a polymer donor, the device Voc reduced from 0.957 to 0.929, 0.903, and 0.857 V, while the Jsc increased from 13.51 to 16.63, 17.56, and 19.44 mA cm2, and finally a PCE of 11% was obtained from the IC-2F-based acceptor system.70 Hou et al. reported the acceptor IT-4F which was synthesized by covalently linking IDTT and IC-2F. PCEs of 13% (ref. 25) and 13.3% (ref. 26) were reported from IT-4F with PBDB-T-SF and PBDB-TF (a conjugated polymer of 4,8-bis(5-(2-ethylhexyl)-4-fluorothiophen-2-yl)benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b]-dithiophene and 1,3-bis(thiophen-2-yl)-5,7-bis(2-ethylhexyl)benzo-[1,2-c:4,5-c]dithiophene-4,8-dione)71 as the polymer donor, respectively. Additionally, Li et al. used a PBDB-T analogue, PM7, with 4-chloro-5-(2-ethylhexylthienyl) as the benzodithiophene (BDT) side chains, and reported a PCE of 12.6% with a Voc of 0.88 V and a Jsc of 20.1 mA cm2.72 When pairing with a deeper-HOMO polymer donor, PDTB-EF-T (a 2,4,6,8-tetra(thiophen-2-yl)benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b]dithiophene (DTBDT) based polymer) (EHOMO = 5.50 eV, Eoptg = 1.93 eV), a Voc of 0.898 V, a Jsc of 20.77 mA cm2, a FF of 0.75, and a PCE of 14.0% was reported by Hou et al.73 Substituents at the IC-5,6-positions with one or two chlorine atoms yielded IC-Cl or IC-2Cl. Conjugation of IC-Cl and IC-2Cl with IDTT afforded the acceptors of IT-2Cl and IT-4Cl, respectively. When using PBDB-T-2F as the polymer donor, the fullerene-free PSCs with IT-2Cl and IT-4Cl as the blended acceptor, respectively, exhibited a Voc of 0.922 and 0.790 V, a Jsc of 19.08 and 22.67 mA cm2, and a PCE of 13.16% and 13.45%.74

Replacement of the fused-benzene ring on IC is another way to tune the optoelectronic properties. The naphthalene-fused SMA exhibited a much smaller Eoptg than the benzene fused acceptor, due to its relatively lower-lying LUMO and higher-lying HOMO. Its solar cell devices showed much larger Jsc and FF because of its stronger -interactions and more ordered face-on orientation75,76 and gave rise to a PCE of 12% (ref. 76) when using PBDB-TF as the donor. Interesting results were found from the thiophene-fused SMAs. Hou et al. synthesized the cyclopenta[b]thiophen-4-one-5-methylene-6-(1,1-dicyanomethylene) (CT[b]) and the related SMA conjugated with IDTT unit, ITCC.77 Almost at the same time, Yang's group, Zou's group, and Zhan's group reported the synthesis of cyclopenta[c]thiophen-4-one-5-methylene-6-(1,1-dicyanomethylene) (CT[c]) and the related SMA also conjugated with a IDTT unit, which was named as ITCT, or ITCPTC, or ITTC.65,78,79 ITCC showed a higher-lying LUMO and HOMO as well as a larger Eoptg than ITIC and the device with PBDB-T as the donor exhibited a higher Voc, while a smaller Jsc. Oppositely, ITCT showed enhanced quinoidal character, which down shifted the LUMO energy level and reduced the optical band gap, related to ITIC, and the device also with PBDB-T as the donor had a larger Jsc and FF, but a little smaller Voc.65 Nevertheless, both ITCC and ITCT gave rise to a PCE of >11%. Sun et al. again paired ITCT with PBT1-C with a HOMO energy of 5.43 eV and reported a PCE of 12.5% with a Voc of 0.94 V and an FF of 0.76.80,81

Hou et al. introduced a methyl on the 5-position of the fused-thiophene of CT[b] and synthesized ITCC-M which was paired with PBDB-T as the front sub-cell to fabricate a tandem cell with PBDTTT-EF-T:IEICO as the back sub-cell, showing a PCE of 13.9%.82 Yang et al. reported that introduction of a methyl at the 2-position of the CT[c] unit slightly upshifted the LUMO and HOMO of the resulting SMA, and a higher Voc and Jsc with a PCE of 12.1% was enabled from the cell with J71 as the donor.83 Cheng et al. reported the cyclopenta[b]thieno[3,2-b]thiophen-4-one-5-methylene-6-(1,1-dicyanomethylene) (CTT[b]) and synthesized the acceptor BDCPDT-TTC by conjugating CTT[b] with benzodi(cyclopentadithiophene) (BDCPDT). This acceptor had a LUMO energy of 3.78 eV and showed a Voc of 0.94 V with PBDB-T as the donor.84

If the IC terminals were replaced with weaker A units such as N,N-diethyl thiobarbituric acid, then the Eoptg was shifted to 2.0 eV due to the much higher-lying LUMO and the solar cell with PTB7-Th as the donor had a Voc of 1.01 V.85

4.3. Modifications on the central D unit

Indaceno[1,2-b:5,6-b]dithiophene (IDT)86 is another ladder-type D unit reported in 2008. A conjugated SMA with IDT and IC was reported by Zhan et al. which showed a Voc of 0.89 V and a PCE of 8.71% pairing with PBDB-T1. Its LUMO and HOMO levels were estimated to be 3.91 and 5.69 eV when measured with the acetonitrile solution, which was a little higher than the ITIC LUMO and a little lower than the ITIC HOMO energy, due to the relatively weaker electron-donating ability of IDT than IDTT.87,88 IDIC is another NF-SMA used for paring with newly synthesized polymer or small-molecule donors. Use of PTFBDT-BZS as the WBG polymer donor, a higher Voc than the PBDB-T1:IDIC system with a PCE of 11% was reported by Zhan et al.89 Recently, Li et al. reported that the pairing of IDIC with a newly synthesized WBG polymer donor, PTQ10, whose LUMO/HOMO energy levels were 2.98 and 5.54 eV, exhibited a PCE of 12.3% and a Voc of 0.97 V.90 Tang et al. reported a PCE of 13% from a FTAZ:IDIC system by using a nanostructured Al2O3 doped ZnO composite film as the cathode interlayer of the inverted cell.91 Yang et al. reported that a thin film of IDIC spin-coated atop the cathode interlayer before the deposition of the fullerene-free binary system enabled improving the device's performance.92 Other studies with IDIC as the blended acceptor material can be found in Table 1.

IDICs also have been utilized to fabricate some all small-molecule solar cells (all SMSCs).93,94 The Voc achieved from the All SMSCs devices were observed to increase with the lower-lying of the HOMO energies of the small molecule donor materials, in sequences of 0.84 V for DRBDT-TVT (5.14 eV)95 < 0.90 V for a BDT-based small-molecule (SM1) (5.24 eV)96 < 0.92 V for NDTSR (5.25 eV)97 < 0.96 V for a difluoro-benzotriazole (2F-BT) based small-molecule (H11) (5.31 eV)98 < 0.99 V for DRTB-T (5.51 eV).99 The Jsc observed from these studies was about 15 mA cm2 and the FF was about 0.65.

Its SMA conjugated with IC showed a higher-lying LUMO (about 3.83 eV) and also HOMO (about 5.41 eV) and a smaller Eg of 1.54 eV than ITIC and about 10% PCEs were obtained with PBDB-T as the donor.49,103 Forrest et al. reported that this acceptor gave rise to a smaller Voc but a larger Jsc than ITIC when pairing with J71.104 Thiophene is less aromatic so the incorporation of thiophene is helpful to enhance the quinoidal character of the molecule. The LUMO and HOMO energies of PTIC were of 3.85 and 5.67 eV with an Eoptg of 1.55 eV. The device with PBDB-T as the donor exhibited a Voc of 0.84 V and a Jsc of 14.2 mA cm2.112 Zhu et al. reported another acceptor, NBDTP, by conjugating this electron-rich core (with 2-ethylhexyl as the side chains) with CT[b], which gave rise to a Voc of 0.937 V and a Jsc of 15.72 mA cm2.113 In another work, Wang et al. synthesized an octyl substituted core and conjugated it with IC-M. The 2H-pyran based D unit exhibited much stronger electron-donating capability than the IDT. 7. Conclusions and outlook

The types of NF-SMAs reviewed in this article possess very high possibilities to tune their optical, electrochemical, and photovoltaic properties via modifications of their chemical structures. Although hundreds of acceptors have been reported via modifications on either their electron-rich (D) core, electron-withdrawing (A) group, side chains, or -bridge and combinations via covalently linking different types of D, A, side chains, and -bridge, a much large area is yet awaiting for you to design and synthesize new acceptors with device-performance directed molecular designing. The big issues remaining to be resolved include: (1) reduction of Voc loss via molecular design, (2) achievement of large electron mobility via molecular design, (3) achievements in large absorptivity and wide absorption band via backbone structure design, and (4) design of new structures, for example, in D, A, the -bridge, and the conjugation, which are available for high-efficiency OPVs.

As the OPV active layer includes both an NF-SMA and a donor material, the design of new polymer/small-molecule donors with strong capability to absorb solar light, large hole mobility, and suitable Frontier molecular orbitals are highly required. Another interesting direction in materials designing is the possibility of these types of SMAs as building blocks to design high-performance polymer acceptors. Li et al. reported a novel polymer acceptor of PZ1 by conjugation of IDIC-C16 with thiophene through the IC-5/6-positions of IDIC-C16 and the thiophene-2,5-positions. This polymer acceptor had a higher absorption coefficient than N2200 and a smaller Eoptg of 1.55 eV and upshifted LUMO and HOMO levels than the parent IDIC-C16. When blended with PBDB-T, a PCE of 9.04% with Voc = 0.829 V, Jsc = 15.78 mA cm2, and FF = 69.12% were obtained.287

For OPVs, the total Voc loss (V) between the band gap (Egap) and the experimental Voc value comes from three parts: the Voc loss from radiative recombination from absorption below the bandgap, Vrad.below gap, the Voc loss from nonradiative recombination, Vnonrad, and the energy difference between the Egap and qVSQoc according to the following equation:289

qV = (Egap qVSQoc) + qVrad.below gap + qVnonrad = E1 + E2 +E3(1)
where q is the elementary charge, E1 is from the radiation recombination above the band gap, which is present for any kinds of solar cells with a value of 0.250.30 V, and E2 is due to the presence of the donoracceptor charge-transfer state,40,290 which provides the driving force for the charge separation of the strong binding Frenkel exciton. For OPVs, E3, as originating from a non-radiation recombination, is much larger and it was suggested to be a major factor for the Voc loss in an OPV device.28 The total Voc loss is normally above 0.6 eV with some examples having a Voc loss value between 0.5 and 0.6 eV.

The large Voc loss and the large difference between the experimental FF and FFSQ mean that there is still a very large area to improve OPV efficiencies. Designing donor and acceptor materials with decreasing non-radiation recombination is required to reduce Voc loss. Improving the fill-factor of an OPV device can be achieved by: (1) designing organic donor and acceptor materials, for example, optimizing their side chains to improve hole and electron mobilities, (2) optimizing film-morphologies291,292 to improve the active layer electronic properties, and (3) engineering the active layerelectrode interfaces293297 to improve charge collections.

Normally, OPV device performance is very dependent on active layer thickness and the active layer thickness is normally 100 nm for optimal OPV devices. However, processing towards large-scale and large-area production of OPVs requires that device performance be independent of the active layer thickness. Therefore, designing new materials with device performance dependence on reduced active layer thickness is required.25,100,213,298,299 This can be possibly carried out by designing high hole mobility polymer donor materials and high electron mobility nonfullerene acceptors because the hole mobilities reported so far from fullerene-free solar cells are still low, typically on the order of magnitude of 105104 cm V1 s1, while electron mobilities are normally on the order of magnitude of 104 cm V1 s1.

Large-scale and large-area production of OPVs available for future commercial applications also required environmentally friendly processing organic solvents. Therefore, seeking non-halide organic solvents available for achieving high-performance OPVs is another topic.300303

Stability is another issue with OPVs. For example, a study by Hou et al. indicated that a PBDB-T:ITIC solar cell showed much higher thermal stability than its PBDB-T:PCBM counterpart the PCE of the former cell decreased from 11% to 10% when the active layer was heated at 200 C for 30 min, while the efficiency of the latter decreased from 7.4% down to 3.4%.55 Otherwise, the results by Krebs and Zhan et al. indicated a fullerene-free solar cell showed higher light stability than the corresponding fullerene device.304 Studies by McCulloch et al. indicated that nonfullerene SMA-based devices had longer storage lifetime and better photo-stability24 as well as oxidative stability243 than fullerene-based devices. Studies by Ma et al. indicated that PBDB-T:ITIC solar cells fabricated by the blade-coating method had excellent stability when stored in an ambient condition without encapsulation and when using a small amount of 1,8-diiodooctane as a solvent additive.63 Research by Choi et al. showed that the 3MT-Th:ITIC solar cell had a long-term stability the device performance was nearly maintained for 1100 h storage under ambient conditions.160
