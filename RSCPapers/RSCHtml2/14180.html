Abstract

The most effective synthetic methodologies currently employed for producing polymeric semiconductors are affected by cost, safety, and environmental issues which may seriously prevent their large-scale production. In this regard, the application of principles of green chemistry for the development of waste-minimized and cleaner synthetic approaches to semiconductor synthesis is essential for propelling the field of organic electronics. In this review, selected advances in the development of synthetic green strategies for the preparation of poly(arylene) families as well as their implication in the performance of selected opto-electronic devices such as organic thin-film transistors and bulk heterojunction solar cells are summarized.

Broader context

The synthesis of Ï€-conjugated organic semiconductors is a cutting edge research area, where the main challenge is to enable organic opto-electronic devices with optimal performance. Most synthetic methodologies currently used to access these systems are demanding from both raw materials/chemical costs and environmental point of view and their large-scale production may present serious limitations. Therefore, the development of waste minimized and clean synthetic methodologies, where minimal amounts of reagents, green reaction media, and solid catalysts are used, is essential for the successful commercialization of opto-electronic devices. Since Ï€-conjugated polymeric semiconductors are of prime importance in this field, this Review provides an overview of the synthetic approaches to these materials addressing several criteria relevant to green/sustainable chemistry.

Introduction

Ï€-Conjugated polymers are attractive for the fabrication of diverse optoelectronic devices such as organic photovoltaic (OPV) cells, light emitting diodes (OLEDs), thin-film transistors (OTFTs) as well as unipolar/complementary circuits, sensors and displays.1 These semiconducting polymers are not expected to overtake the performance level of the widely deployed inorganic semiconductors, but they hold the promise for cost-efficient thin-film processing, light-weight, (semi)-transparency and excellent mechanical flexibility.1,2 To date, most of the research efforts focused on the development of new organic semiconductor structures aiming at improving the device performance, such as maximizing field-effect mobility for TFTs, light emission/vertical conductivity in OLED/diodes, light absorption/transport of charge in OPVs with limited emphasis on the synthetic methodology employed and environmental impact associated with their synthesis. These problematics include toxicity of the synthetic reagents, catalysts, and byproducts to humans, animal and plants, electrical energy for their production, and transfer from laboratory to large scales.

Ï€-Conjugated polymers have been synthesized mainly by traditional metal catalyzed step-growth polycondensation3 and chain growth polymerization reactions.4 Despite their great versatility, these reactions have drawbacks including the need for several steps to prepare the monomeric precursors, the instability of several organometallic reagents employed, difficulties in controlling the polymer architecture, and the formation of stoichiometric amounts of toxic by-products. In addition, device-quality organic semiconductors generally require extensive purification processes to remove undesired byproducts and metals.5 Therefore, the development of economically attractive and ecologically benign alternative reactions is highly desirable. In this regard, the application of principles of green chemistry for the development of waste-minimized and clean synthetic approaches to semiconductor synthesis is essential for the field of organic electronics.5b,6

Briefly, the â€œ12 principlesâ€ for a greener chemical product or process were published by the Green Chemistry Institute in US7 and can be summarized as follows: (1) prevent waste; (2) design synthetic methods to maximize the incorporation of all materials used in the process into the final product (i.e. atom economy); (3) design less hazardous chemical synthesis; (4) design safer chemicals/products; (5) use safer solvents/auxiliaries; (6) increase energy efficiency; (7) use renewable raw materials/feedstocks; (8) avoid derivatization (e.g. protecting groups, temporary modification of physical/chemical processes), because it requires additional reagents and can generate waste; (9) use (selective) catalysts, not stoichiometric reagents; (10) design chemical products considering their degradation after use; (11) analyze in real-time to prevent pollution; and (12) minimize the potential for accidents, including releases, explosions, and fire.

Particularly, waste production should be reduced through the use of minimal amounts of reagents and by using easily recoverable/recyclable catalytic systems (e.g. heterogeneous catalysts) to simplify workup/purification procedures and reduce energy and production times.5,6 Particular attention should be directed towards solvents,5,6 which are a major input in the synthetic processes and typically comprise the largest fraction of the waste generated (âˆ¼90%) as well as a significant workplace hazards. For instance, highly polar solvents, i.e. N-methylpyrrolidone (NMP), N,Nâ€²-dimethylacetamide (DMA), N,Nâ€²-dimethylformamide (DMF), pyridine and chlorinated solvents, which are commonly used as media in condensation polymerization, increase air pollution and most of them are toxic/flammable. These solvents rank high in the list of harmful chemicals,8 because they are used in significant volumes, and often their volatility makes them difficult to handle. High-boiling point media are used to reduce workplace hazards in solution polymerization since these reactions can be carried out at elevated temperatures without excessive pressure buildup. However, a major drawback is related to the high energy cost of the solvent removal from the final products.

Despite its broad scope and reliability, the Stille reaction has several drawbacks which may increase in severity when implemented on a large scale,9e.g. the need to lithiate the monomers to achieve their conversion to trialkylstannanes, the formation of tin-containing stoichiometric amounts of toxic by-products, and contamination of metal reactor walls by tin. In some cases, some instability of the organometallic reagents upon purification attempts may also be observed.

A recent, environmentally relevant advance in the Stille polymerization methodology is the application of microwave-assisted heating. Indeed, microwave irradiation is efficient in reducing the reaction times, which is desirable for the generation of high-molecular weight materials and, consequently, increases reaction yields while reducing the amount of byproducts.11

The first examples of semiconducting polyarylenes prepared by microwave-assisted Stille cross-coupling reactions were published by Scherf et al.12 and Tierney et al.13 Thus, in the Sherf contribution12 the synthesis of two liquid crystalline 9,9â€²-dialkylfluorene/oligothiophene alternating copolymers P1 and P2 and poly(1,5-dioctoxynaphthylene-2,6-diyl-alt-2,2â€²-bithiophene-5,5â€²-diyl) P3 (Scheme 1) was reported. Suzuki polymerization

The Suzuki polymerization reaction of dihalo-terminated and bis(organoboron)-functionalized monomers18 is in some aspects sustainable, although it typically requires the presence of additives such as a base, water, and a phase transfer catalyst, which add significant complexity to the reaction setup. This is likely exacerbated at a large scale, where multiphase reactions are sometimes slower and more difficult to control. Fig. 4-top shows the catalytic cycle for this reaction. Furthermore, the stability of the boron-containing monomers may be an issue since commonly employed boronic acids tend to condense into trimeric cyclic anhydrides and, consequently, several synthetic protocols use an excess of boronic acid to ensure a complete conversion of the electrophilic component of the reaction.18 Furthermore, their purification on a large scale may be challenging as many of them are waxy solids. The use of boronates partially solves these problems, as pinacol or 2-methyl-2,4-pentanediol boronate esters are monomers but they are expensive and their use lead to a marked decrease atom economy, which may make them less appealing. Finally, most of the synthetic approaches to boronic esters still requires a lithiation reaction of the precursor, and their purification on a large scale are often challenging.

Metal catalyst-free oxidative coupling reactions

Even though their importance and indispensability is unquestionable, the replacement of (transition) metal catalysts by organic surrogates may have great advantages for large-scale polymer preparation. This is readily rationalized by the relatively high costs, the contingent toxicity and the demanding removal of (transition) metals from final products, especially in the case of polymers with complex side chains.70 In addition, minimization of metal leaching into the final problem will be also beneficial for the quality and performance of the target device (see above). Consequently, the development of efficient (transition) metal-free protocols for materials synthesis represents an important field of research with remarkable growth over the last few years. Examples of these approaches include controlled radical polymerizations (CRP),71i.e. atom transfer radical polymerization (ATRP), nitroxide-mediated radical polymerization (NMRP), and ring opening polymerization (ROP). All the CRP methods rely on forming equilibria between active species (radicals) and various types of so-called dormant species.71 Radicals may be reversibly trapped in a deactivation/activation process according to Fig. 8A, or they can be involved in a â€œreversible transferâ€ degenerative exchange process (Fig. 8B).

Conclusions

In this review we summarized the major findings for the development of methodologies for the green synthesis of poly(arylenes), which are semiconducting systems of prime importance for the realization of unconventional (opto)electronic devices.

It is now clear that during the last three years, outstanding results in the field have been achieved. Several studies have addressed and elucidated problematic aspects when using conventional Stille and Suzuki cross-coupling reactions for poly(arylene) preparation as well as the implications and benefits of employing alternative reaction conditions, solvents, and reactants. Particularly, direct arylation polycondensation schemes enable the preparation of a range of poly(arylenes) featuring characteristics/performance which may rival or exceed those of those achieved using conventional routes. For this approach, it remains a fundamental question, i.e. how to address selectivity as well as regioregularity issues for monomers lacking proper directing groups.

Tuning the reaction conditions in terms of temperature, catalyst loading and the use of bulky catalyst ligands enabled more accurate control of homocoupling, end-groups, residual metal and branching defects of polymers. Further efforts in these directions are certainly needed to achieve a more trustworthy and mature methodology. Among the possible routes, the proper design of catalysts and/or ligands appears to be the most promising strategy.

In this regard, the reader can also refer to a very recent highlight article by Thompson et al.29 where it nicely discusses the origin of the main chain defects of the polymer through direct (hetero)arylation, the dependence of polymer properties on these defects, and all available tools effective for their control.

For all the reviewed methods of polyarylene synthesis, future development should focus on further expanding the substrate scope, which will ultimately enable the access to the most efficient polymers for (opto)electronic devices, carefully optimizing the reaction conditions to gain a better control over the polymerization reaction, and obviously simplifying the reaction protocols in terms of elimination (or, at least, minimization) of expensive/toxic reagents. The ultimate aim of this review is to anticipate and stimulate a discussion for the need to efficiently synthesize organic semiconductors at a scale much greater than that used in the laboratories, and with minimal production of waste and toxic byproducts. Despite the impressive achievements in the field of poly(arylene) sustainable synthetic processes summarized in this review, to date there are no comprehensive reports focusing on the evaluation of their costs in terms of energy consumption, labour cost, waste disposal, also in comparison with conventional methods. Thus, it is essential to further develop the area in such a direction, as well as explore new synthetic approaches, for enabling the commercialization of Ï€-conjugated polymer-based devices.
