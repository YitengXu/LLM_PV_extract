Abstract

The most effective synthetic methodologies currently employed for producing polymeric semiconductors are affected by cost, safety, and environmental issues which may seriously prevent their large-scale production. In this regard, the application of principles of green chemistry for the development of waste-minimized and cleaner synthetic approaches to semiconductor synthesis is essential for propelling the field of organic electronics. In this review, selected advances in the development of synthetic green strategies for the preparation of poly(arylene) families as well as their implication in the performance of selected opto-electronic devices such as organic thin-film transistors and bulk heterojunction solar cells are summarized.

Broader context

The synthesis of π-conjugated organic semiconductors is a cutting edge research area, where the main challenge is to enable organic opto-electronic devices with optimal performance. Most synthetic methodologies currently used to access these systems are demanding from both raw materials/chemical costs and environmental point of view and their large-scale production may present serious limitations. Therefore, the development of waste minimized and clean synthetic methodologies, where minimal amounts of reagents, green reaction media, and solid catalysts are used, is essential for the successful commercialization of opto-electronic devices. Since π-conjugated polymeric semiconductors are of prime importance in this field, this Review provides an overview of the synthetic approaches to these materials addressing several criteria relevant to green/sustainable chemistry.

Introduction

π-Conjugated polymers are attractive for the fabrication of diverse optoelectronic devices such as organic photovoltaic (OPV) cells, light emitting diodes (OLEDs), thin-film transistors (OTFTs) as well as unipolar/complementary circuits, sensors and displays.1 These semiconducting polymers are not expected to overtake the performance level of the widely deployed inorganic semiconductors, but they hold the promise for cost-efficient thin-film processing, light-weight, (semi)-transparency and excellent mechanical flexibility.1,2 To date, most of the research efforts focused on the development of new organic semiconductor structures aiming at improving the device performance, such as maximizing field-effect mobility for TFTs, light emission/vertical conductivity in OLED/diodes, light absorption/transport of charge in OPVs with limited emphasis on the synthetic methodology employed and environmental impact associated with their synthesis. These problematics include toxicity of the synthetic reagents, catalysts, and byproducts to humans, animal and plants, electrical energy for their production, and transfer from laboratory to large scales.

π-Conjugated polymers have been synthesized mainly by traditional metal catalyzed step-growth polycondensation3 and chain growth polymerization reactions.4 Despite their great versatility, these reactions have drawbacks including the need for several steps to prepare the monomeric precursors, the instability of several organometallic reagents employed, difficulties in controlling the polymer architecture, and the formation of stoichiometric amounts of toxic by-products. In addition, device-quality organic semiconductors generally require extensive purification processes to remove undesired byproducts and metals.5 Therefore, the development of economically attractive and ecologically benign alternative reactions is highly desirable. In this regard, the application of principles of green chemistry for the development of waste-minimized and clean synthetic approaches to semiconductor synthesis is essential for the field of organic electronics.5b,6

Briefly, the “12 principles” for a greener chemical product or process were published by the Green Chemistry Institute in US7 and can be summarized as follows: (1) prevent waste; (2) design synthetic methods to maximize the incorporation of all materials used in the process into the final product (i.e. atom economy); (3) design less hazardous chemical synthesis; (4) design safer chemicals/products; (5) use safer solvents/auxiliaries; (6) increase energy efficiency; (7) use renewable raw materials/feedstocks; (8) avoid derivatization (e.g. protecting groups, temporary modification of physical/chemical processes), because it requires additional reagents and can generate waste; (9) use (selective) catalysts, not stoichiometric reagents; (10) design chemical products considering their degradation after use; (11) analyze in real-time to prevent pollution; and (12) minimize the potential for accidents, including releases, explosions, and fire.

Particularly, waste production should be reduced through the use of minimal amounts of reagents and by using easily recoverable/recyclable catalytic systems (e.g. heterogeneous catalysts) to simplify workup/purification procedures and reduce energy and production times.5,6 Particular attention should be directed towards solvents,5,6 which are a major input in the synthetic processes and typically comprise the largest fraction of the waste generated (∼90%) as well as a significant workplace hazards. For instance, highly polar solvents, i.e. N-methylpyrrolidone (NMP), N,N′-dimethylacetamide (DMA), N,N′-dimethylformamide (DMF), pyridine and chlorinated solvents, which are commonly used as media in condensation polymerization, increase air pollution and most of them are toxic/flammable. These solvents rank high in the list of harmful chemicals,8 because they are used in significant volumes, and often their volatility makes them difficult to handle. High-boiling point media are used to reduce workplace hazards in solution polymerization since these reactions can be carried out at elevated temperatures without excessive pressure buildup. However, a major drawback is related to the high energy cost of the solvent removal from the final products.

The present review article summarizes synthetic approaches to semiconducting polymers addressing several relevant criteria for green chemistry. Particularly, it focuses on poly(arylene)s (Fig. 1) because of the significant efforts devoted to their “sustainable” synthesis and their wide use for the fabrication of several opto-electronic devices including OTFTs and OPVs (Fig. 2).

Specifically, in Section 1 we first introduce the Stille cross-coupling polycondensation reaction featuring relevant environmental issues9 related to the employment of this methodology. Although it is known that tin-based reactions are not green and will be difficult to be implemented for commercial product synthesis, the Stille-protocol is a prominent methodology to prepare polymer semiconductors10 since it is the only one which typically enables an excellent control over polymer molecular weight, polydispersity and regiochemistry. Furthermore, polymers synthesized following this route typically enable the best performing devices. Therefore, we have decided to include relevant examples of Stille polymerizations to provide the reader a very clear indication of the state-of-the art performance for several polymers as well as to share important contributions where attempts have been carried out to achieve certain polymers in a more sustainable (acceptable) manner. Thus, we provide representative examples of Stille-based methodologies where certain issues were addressed. Next, we give an overview of the green methodologies for metal-mediated polymerization reactions, i.e. Suzuki cross-coupling, and direct (hetero)arylation polycondensation. Section 2 refers to the progress made in establishing metal-free oxidative coupling protocols, with an emphasis on nitroxide-mediated radical polymerization. We do not intend to provide an exhaustive list of examples of such chemistries, but rather identify key developments in the field that seem particularly suitable for large-scale synthesis. Limitations and problems associated with some polymerization protocols in terms of material physical properties and performance in OTFT/OPV devices will also be illustrated and discussed.

Metal-mediated polymerization reactions

Stille cross-coupling

The most common methodology employed to synthesize π-conjugated polymers is the Stille condensation polymerization of dihalo-functionalized and bis(trialkylstannyl)-functionalized monomers, with the catalytic cycle reported in Fig. 3.10

Despite its broad scope and reliability, the Stille reaction has several drawbacks which may increase in severity when implemented on a large scale,9e.g. the need to lithiate the monomers to achieve their conversion to trialkylstannanes, the formation of tin-containing stoichiometric amounts of toxic by-products, and contamination of metal reactor walls by tin. In some cases, some instability of the organometallic reagents upon purification attempts may also be observed.

A recent, environmentally relevant advance in the Stille polymerization methodology is the application of microwave-assisted heating. Indeed, microwave irradiation is efficient in reducing the reaction times, which is desirable for the generation of high-molecular weight materials and, consequently, increases reaction yields while reducing the amount of byproducts.11

The first examples of semiconducting polyarylenes prepared by microwave-assisted Stille cross-coupling reactions were published by Scherf et al.12 and Tierney et al.13 Thus, in the Sherf contribution12 the synthesis of two liquid crystalline 9,9′-dialkylfluorene/oligothiophene alternating copolymers P1 and P2 and poly(1,5-dioctoxynaphthylene-2,6-diyl-alt-2,2′-bithiophene-5,5′-diyl) P3 (Scheme 1) was reported. The reactions were carried out in degassed toluene under an argon atmosphere using the appropriate monomeric building blocks (i.e. bis(trimethylstannyl)bithiophene 1/9,9′-dialkyl-2,7-dibromofluorene 2 or 3) and Pd(PPh3)4 as a catalyst. At the microwave power level of 300 W (9 min), polymer P1 was obtained with Mn = 7.8 kDa (PDI = 2.7), and anyway a partial decomposition of the soluble catalyst occurred, as indicated by a detectable deposition of palladium metal on the walls of the reaction vessel. After Soxhlet extraction with ethyl acetate to remove oligomers, Mn increased to 14.0 kDa (PDI = 1.6; 55% yield). Similar results were obtained for copolymer P2 (Mn = 15.4 kDa, PDI = 1.4, 52% yield after extraction). Note that the investigated solvent/catalytic system gave appreciable degrees of polymerization only when the reactor power levels were >170 W. The copolymers P1 and P2 obtained by conventional heating, i.e. 72 h under toluene reflux, exhibited slightly lower molecular weights (Mn = 12.3 kDa and Mn = 12.0 kDa, respectively) with very similar polydispersities (PDI = 1.6). The lower yields of the microwave-assisted reactions relative to those under conventional heating (55% vs. 83% and 52% vs. 72%, respectively) were attributed to the smaller reaction scale, which led to more significant losses during the workup procedure. Next, the results for the Stille cross-coupling between bis(trimethylstannyl)bithiophene 1 and the electron rich 1,5-dioctoxy-2,6 dibromonaphthalene 4 under both microwave-assisted and conventional heating protocols were compared. Note here that 1,5-dioctoxy-2,6 dibromonaphthalene 4 is less reactive relative to 9,9-dialkyl-2,7-dibromofluorenes 2 and 3 in the Stille coupling reaction. Interestingly, the use of microwave heating allowed copolymer P3 to be prepared with a markedly higher molecular weight (Mn = 13.7 kDa, PDI = 6.4 vs. Mn = 5.1 kDa, PDI = 2.8) as well as reaction yield (52% vs. 34%).

Tiernay et al.13 investigated the microwave-assisted Stille coupling between 2,5-bis(trimethylstannyl) thiophene 5 and 5,5′-dibromo-4,4′-dioctylbithiophene 6 to yield poly(3,3′′-dioctyl-2,2′:5′-2′′-terthiophene) P4 (Scheme 2). Optimal conditions involved the use of the relatively inexpensive catalytic system Pd2(dba)3/P(o-tol)3 (1:2) as a palladium source, LiCl as an additive, microwave heating (200 °C, 10 min), and chlorobenzene as a medium. Note that P(o-tol)3 is an air- and light-sensitive ligand, thereby presenting serious issues for green chemistry and atom economy. Furthermore, while increasing the polymerization rate and, ultimately, P4 molecular weight, the presence of LiCl14 adds complexity to the reaction setup, which would be amplified on a large scale where multiphase reactions are more difficult to mix. A Mn as high as 16.8 kDa was obtained for P4 (PDI = 2.3). When polymer P4 was synthesized by a conventionally heated (132 °C) analogous polymerization reaction, a lower Mn was achieved after 24 h (Mn = 13.0 kDa) whereas there was no noticeable effect on the polydispersity index (PDI = 2.3). Organic field-effect transistors were fabricated using P4 prepared by the microwave-assisted Stille coupling protocol and field-effect mobilities (μ) ∼ 3 × 10−2 cm2 V−1 s−1 (Ion/Ioff = 2 × 102) were achieved. Unfortunately, no comparison with P4 obtained under conventional conditions was reported in terms of device response.

More recently, the microwave-assisted Stille coupling approach has proven to be successful in the preparation of a wider range of polyarylenes for high-performance organic electronic devices.15

For instance, Bazan et al.15i reported a straightforward, reproducible microwave-assisted synthetic methodology to two novel donor–acceptor copolymers, i.e. poly[(4,4′-didodecylcyclopenta[2,1-b:3,4-b′]dithiophene)-2,6-diyl-alt-(2,1,3-benzothiadiazole)-4,7-diyl], P5, poly[(4,4′-didodecyldithieno[3,2-b:2′,3′-d]silole)-2,6-diyl-alt-(2,1,3-benzothiadiazole)-4,7-diyl], P6, and the previously reported16,17 poly[(4,4′-bis(2-ethylhexyl)cyclopenta-[2,1-b:3,4-b′]dithiophene)-2,6-diyl-alt-(2,1,3-benzothiadiazole)-4,7-diyl], P7 (Scheme 3). The design rationale for P5 was to provide insights into the influence on the photovoltaic performance after replacing a branched (P7) for a linear side group. Copolymer P6 was synthesized to explore the isoelectronic substitution of silicon for carbon as the bithiophene bridge atom. Thus, the authors first explored conventional Stille cross-coupling polymerization between 4,7-dibromo-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole 7 and bis-stannylated cyclopentadithiophene (8 or 9) or silacyclopentadithiophene (10) derivatives, using Pd(Ph3)4 as the catalyst (5 mol%) and a 1:1 co-monomer molar ratio (∼14 mM concentration). These conditions were found to yield polymers with too low-molecular weights. For instance, by heating comonomers 2 and 4 in xylenes at 120 °C for 48 h, a maximum Mn value in the range 7–10 kDa was obtained for P6. Interestingly, when microwave heating was examined it was possible to obtain P6 with Mn = 14 kDa after 30 minutes of reaction. Furthermore, an increase in reactants' concentration (200 mM) resulted in a Mn enhancement up to 22 kDa. A stoichiometric adjustment was also examined to compensate for possible contamination of 10 by trace amounts of organometallic tin compounds which can hardly be fully removed. This led to P6 with Mn values of 34 kDa, 31 kDa and 18 kDa as the distannane equivalents increased from 1.00 to 1.15 (0.05 equivalent increments). Copolymers P5 and P7 prepared using an analogous procedure, exhibited Mn = 44 kDa and Mn = 34 kDa, respectively. The polydispersity indices for all polymers were found to be ∼2, and the reaction yields were greater than 80% after fractionation by Soxhlet extraction. Note that the previously reported P7 showed a comparable Mn value of 35 kDa (PDI = 1.3).

Bulk heterojunction OPV cells based on copolymer P5 exhibited lower performance characterized by a Voc of 0.37 V, a Jsc of 15.5 mA cm−2, and a FF = 0.48 under simulated standard solar light (AM1.5 G, 100 mW cm−2), giving a maximum power conversion efficiency of 2.7% (1:2 weight ratio with PC71BM). Copolymer P6 afforded a greater photovoltaic performance with a PCE of ∼ 6% (Voc = 0.57 V, Jsc = 17.5 mA cm−2, and FF = 0.61) from an analogous device configuration but with a different component ratio, i.e.P6:PC71BM = 1:1. The authors attributed the Voc difference between P5 and P6 to the difference in HOMO levels, −5.12 eV and −5.36 eV, respectively. A direct correlation was found between P6, Mn and device performance, thereby highlighting the influence of synthetic procedures. Indeed, going from Mn = 7 (conventional heating) to Mn = 14 to Mn = 34 kDa, a significant increase in Jsc from 4.2 to 17.3 mA cm−2, along with a large increase in FF from 0.35 to 0.61 was observed. These changes resulted in an increase in the PCE from 1.2 to ∼6%.

Suzuki polymerization

The Suzuki polymerization reaction of dihalo-terminated and bis(organoboron)-functionalized monomers18 is in some aspects sustainable, although it typically requires the presence of additives such as a base, water, and a phase transfer catalyst, which add significant complexity to the reaction setup. This is likely exacerbated at a large scale, where multiphase reactions are sometimes slower and more difficult to control. Fig. 4-top shows the catalytic cycle for this reaction. Furthermore, the stability of the boron-containing monomers may be an issue since commonly employed boronic acids tend to condense into trimeric cyclic anhydrides and, consequently, several synthetic protocols use an excess of boronic acid to ensure a complete conversion of the electrophilic component of the reaction.18 Furthermore, their purification on a large scale may be challenging as many of them are waxy solids. The use of boronates partially solves these problems, as pinacol or 2-methyl-2,4-pentanediol boronate esters are monomers but they are expensive and their use lead to a marked decrease atom economy, which may make them less appealing. Finally, most of the synthetic approaches to boronic esters still requires a lithiation reaction of the precursor, and their purification on a large scale are often challenging.

Recently, Chiechi, Hummelen and coworkers19 successfully addressed the latter two issues by using bis(pinacolate)diboron (BiPi) to generate in situ the active boronate species for Suzuki polymerization. Four dibromo-substituted thiophene-based monomers 11–14 (Scheme 4) were homo-polymerized by employing the relatively expensive 1,1′-bis(diphenylphosphino)ferrocenepalladium(II)dichloride dichloromethane complex (Pd(dppf)Cl2) catalyst (5 mol%), K3PO4 as the base (5 equiv.), a 1:1 monomer: bis(pinacolato)diboron molar ratio, and N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) as the reaction medium (110 °C, 24 h). It is important to remind here that the use of DMF, although the standard for Suzuki cross-coupling reactions, is clearly not compatible with the current drive towards more sustainable processes due to the issues listed previously.

However, these conditions yielded copolymers P8–P11 from moderate to high yields (37–74%) with molecular weights up to Mn = 9.46 (Mw = 25.8) kDa and PDIs ranging from 1.80 to 2.86. Subsequently, the authors demonstrated that these polymers comprised chains that were terminated by only two different end-groups, i.e. Br and H, which was clearly in agreement with the use of the Suzuki homo-polymerization in place of the standard co-polymerization.20 Interestingly, monomers 11 and 12 gave predominantly H/H terminated chains, whereas 13 and 14 led to polymers having a higher fraction of Br/H and Br/Br end groups, respectively. These findings correlated with the proposed mechanism for the BiPi method, relying on two palladium-mediated catalytic cycles, i.e. Miyaura, which generated the active Br/B terminated monomer, and Suzuki, growing the polymer chain (Fig. 4).

The electron-rich thiophene moieties in monomers 11 and 12 decelerated the oxidative insertion of palladium in the rate-determining Suzuki cycle, thereby explaining the predominance of the H/H terminated polymer chains. On the other hand, the presence of a more electron-withdrawing fluorene moiety into monomer 13 accelerated the oxidative insertion step in the Suzuki reaction and balanced the two cycles, which favoured the formation of the Br/H end-capped polymer. Finally, when the monomer incorporated a thienothiophene moiety, which is also more electron-withdrawing than thiophene, as in 14, the termination of a growing chain by deboronation in the Suzuki cycle was also accelerated, and consequently the amount of polymer Br/Br end-groups increased. The authors also compared the BiPi method to the commonly used Stille co-polymerization.

The polymers P8–P11 were prepared using the appropriate dibromo-substituted and bis-(tri-n-alkylstannyl)-functionalized monomers in 1:1 wt/wt ratio, 5 mol% Pd(PPh3)4 catalyst, and toluene/DMF 3:1 v/v as the reaction medium, at reflux temperature (24 h). The average P8Mn and polydispersity were comparable to those achieved using the BiPi method (Mn = 5.87 kDa vs. 5.20 kDa; PDI = 1.61 vs. 1.84) whereas the yield was higher (90% vs. 77%). Despite the structural similarity to P8, polymer P9 was produced with a lower yield (30%), Mn (8.73 kDa) and PDI (1.85) than under the BiPi conditions (yield = 48%; Mn = 8.92 kDa; PDI = 2.56). Copolymer P10 was prepared in a similar yield of Suzuki polymerization (71% vs. 74%), but with a much lower Mn (3.97 vs. 9.46 kDa) and a similar PDI (2.20 vs. 2.73). This result was ascribed to the stronger electron-withdrawing nature of fluorenes compared to thiophenes, leading to an increased Suzuki turnover rate (vide supra). An opposite behavior was found for P11, which was obtained in higher yields compared to using BiPi (87 vs. 67%) and featured a nearly double Mn (10.09 vs. 5.72 kDa) as well as a narrower polydispersity (1.99 vs. 2.86). Notably, Mw was generally higher for the BiPi method, thereby indicating that the latter produced longer chains than the Stille cross-coupling approach. Indeed, the BiPi method involves a homo-polymerization, which does not suffer from chain terminating reactions as much as co-polymerizations. Importantly, this method avoid the synthesis of boron- or tin-containing monomers, thus enabling the preparation of copolymers P8–P11 in fewer steps than through the equivalent standard Suzuki or Stille reactions.

A promising advance in Suzuki methodology is the replacement of boronic acids/esters with trifluoroborates.22 Trifluoroborate salts may be easily accessed from the readily available and inexpensive starting materials i.e., they are chemically robust/highly tolerant functional groups stable in moisture/air as well as in chromatography, and often readily recrystallizable. Additionally, they possess a relatively low molecular weight and the byproducts from cross-coupling reactions would be relatively benign inorganic materials. However, the introduction of trifluoroborate requires both lithiation and fluorination,21 thereby adding a step to those required to synthesize conventional boronic acids/esters. Thus, recently Ober et al.22 reported the successful Suzuki cross-coupling reaction between dipotassium [2-(heptadecan-9-yl)-2H-benzotriazole]-4,7-bis(trifluoroborate) 15 and dibromobenzothiadiazole 16 to yield polymer P12 (Scheme 5) composed of all electron-accepting units. Initially, the polycondensation involved a 1:1 co-monomer molar ratio, Pd(PPh3)4 as the catalytic system, tetraethyl ammonium hydroxide as the base, and toluene/water as the reaction medium (90 °C, 2 h), and afforded a low molecular weight polymer P12 (Mn = 4.6 kDa). When LiOH·H2O was added, the reaction resulted in a Mn value for P12 as high as 120 kDa (PDI = 1.5), which was ascribed to LiOH superior ability in hydrolyzing the trifluoroborate to boronic acid.23 These findings suggested that dipotassium heteroaryl bis(trifluoroborate) can be used as a viable alternative to boronic acid/esters in the Suzuki polycondensation.

Copolymer P13 (Mn = 29 kDa, PDI = 1.8) containing 20 mol% of a bithiophene unit was also synthesized under the same reaction conditions (Scheme 5). Both polymers P12 and P13 exhibit high decomposition temperatures (onset >400 °C), as indicated by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). The HOMO and LUMO energy levels for P12 (−5.4 eV and −3.4 eV, respectively) and P13 (−5.0 eV and −3.15 eV, respectively) were measured from ultraviolet photoemission spectroscopy and optical absorption data. Solution-processed OFET devices based on both polymers showed ambipolar behaviour with hole mobilities of 4 × 10−5 to 7 × 10−5 cm2 V−1 s−1 and respectable electron mobilities up to 2 × 10−2 cm2 V−1 s−1 (P12) and 8 × 10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1 (P13) (Ion/Ioff ∼ 1 × 104 and ∼1 × 102, respectively).

Recent advances in the development of greener Suzuki cross-coupling protocols to semiconducting polymers include the use of microwave radiation.24

For instance, Farrell, and Sherf et al.24b reported the microwave-promoted Suzuki coupling between 2,5-dihexyl-1,4-phenylene diboronic acid 17 and 4,4′-didecyl-2′,5′-dibromoterephthalophenone 18 to yield the polymer P14 precursor to ladder-type p-phenylene P15 (Scheme 6). Optimal conditions involved the use of a 1:1 co-monomer molar ratio (60 mM concentration) and of a relatively inexpensive catalytic system Pd(PPh3)2Cl2 (∼4%) in THF/4 M aq. Na2CO3 at a constant microwave power of 150 W (∼130 °C) for ∼9 minutes. Under these conditions, polyketone P14 with Mn = 12.6 kDa and a PDI of 1.8 was obtained in 61% yields. Notably, when compared to conventional synthesis (refluxing toluene/2 M aq. Na2CO3 for 1–3 days), the reaction time could be significantly reduced, although polymer molecular weights and yields were comparable. The authors also reported that by increasing the irradiating power (70 → 100 → 150 W) a corresponding progressive increase in the Mn values (3.7 → 4.2 → 12.6 kDa, respectively) was achieved.

Within the same study 1,5-dioctoxy-2,6-bis(tetramethyldioxaborolato)naphthalene 19 was employed as the Suzuki reagent in the optimal conditions identified above. In this manner a new structurally related precursor P16 (Scheme 6) was obtained in a 60% yield (Mn = 14.2 kDa; PDI = 1.8). On the other hand, copolymer P16 obtained by conventional heating, i.e. 3 days under refluxing toluene/4 M aq. Na2CO3, exhibited a slightly lower molecular weight (Mn = 12.3 kDa), with very similar polydispersities (PDI = 1.7) but higher reaction yields (70%).

In a further study, Sherf et al.25 reported the microwave-assisted Suzuki coupling synthesis of the sterically hindered 1,5-naphthylene polyketone precursors to the polymers P17 and P18 in good yields (89% and 78%, respectively) and with a reasonable molecular weight (Mn = 13.6 and 9.0 kDa, respectively) as well as polydispersity index (PDI = 2.4 and 2.9, respectively) (Scheme 6). Satisfactory coupling reactions could be achieved employing the appropriate monomeric building blocks i.e.18/2,6-dialkyloxynaphthalene-1,5-bis(4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-1,3,2-dioxaborolate 20 or 21) in a 1:1 molar ratio and using Pd(PPh3)2Cl2 (∼4%) as the catalyst. Microwave heating at 115 °C (300 W, 10 minutes) was realized under heterogeneous conditions by using powdered KOH (12 equiv.) as base in dry THF. Interestingly, it has been demonstrated that the polymer Mn values increased almost linearly with the quantity of base. Finally, very recently Ma et al.26 reported a simple, expeditious, energy saving microwave-assisted Suzuki polymerizationprotocol to poly(9,9′-dihexylfluorene) P19 (Scheme 7). The use of heterogenous catalysts may enable greener synthetic strategies to π-conjugated polymers via Suzuki cross-coupling.27 Indeed, by carrying out a reaction using an heterogeneous catalyst, purification of the desired reaction product can be simplified because of the facile recovery from the reaction mixture by filtration/centrifugation. In addition, in most cases the need for chromatography may be avoided for metal removal, thus reducing the operating costs. Importantly, the use of heterogeneous catalysts prevents metal contamination of the semiconductor which may substantially affect device performance5 (vide supra). Heterogeneous catalysts are also easier to handle, allowing us to avoid the formation of inorganic salts, and are recoverable/recyclable and safer to be stored/discarded.

H.-Y. Li, and H.-Z. Chen et al.27 reported for the first time the use of the low-cost, ligand free Pd/C catalyst for Suzuki coupling polymerization, obtaining very good results. Thus, N-(2-ethylhexyl)-2,7-(bis-4′,4′,5′,5′-tetramethyl-1′,3′, 2′-dioxaboralan-2′-yi)-9H-carbazole 24 (Scheme 8) was copolymerized18 at 85 °C for 72 h with the diketopyrrolopyrrole-based bis-bromo monomers 25 and 26, respectively (1:1 molar ratio), using Pd/C as the catalyst (5 mol% Pd), K2CO3 as the base (3 equiv.), and THF/DMF/H2O (1:1:0.5 v/v) as the reaction medium. These conditions led to copolymers P20 and P21 in good yields (∼70%) and with molecular weights of Mn = 14.5 kDa (PDI = 1.5) and Mn = 13.0 kDa (PDI = 1.8), respectively, after Soxhlet extraction. Similarly to the heterogeneous Stille polymerization protocol (vide supra), the insoluble Pd/C catalyst was easily recovered after solvent extractions from the crude P20 and P21 polymers, but the activity of the recovered catalytic system decreased resulting in low molecular weights.

Direct (hetero)arylation polycondensation

Transition metal-catalyzed coupling reactions of (hetero)arene C–H bonds with aryl halides, i.e. direct (hetero)arylation (Scheme 9), have undergone a rapid development in very recent years, and is becoming a viable sustainable alternative to traditional cross-coupling polymerizations.28 This class of cross-coupling processes does not require the preparation of organometallic reagents and needs fewer reaction steps as well as can generate less waste. However, control of the regioselectivity of this reaction can be controlled only when a given (hetero)arene exhibits preferential C–H reactive positions.29 Thus, achieving regioselectivity for unbiased substrates would greatly broaden the scope of direct (hetero)arylation reactions.
Scheme 9

In 1999, Lemaire and coworkers30 first employed the direct arylation protocols, originally used only for small molecules, for the synthesis of semiconducting polymers.31 Thus, they prepared a series of poly(3-alkylthiophene)s P22 and P23 by polycondensation of the corresponding 2-halo-3-alkylthiophene derivatives 27 or 28, respectively (Scheme 10). The general procedure implied the use of Pd(OAc)2(5 mol%)/nBu4NBr (1 equiv.) as the catalytic system32 and K2CO3 (2.5 equiv.) as the base in DMF at 80 °C (48 h). Although the polymers were obtained in good yields (∼90%), they featured low molecular weights (Mn ∼ 2.4–3.0 kDa, PDI ∼ 1.5–2) and degrees of polymerization (DP ∼ 12–16). 1H-NMR magnetic resonance at 6.98 ppm, which is characteristic for the proton at C4-position of the thiophene rings, was used to evaluate the degree of head-to-tail P22 regioregularity. Although the presence of some additional signals in the aromatic region were observed, the authors stated that regioregular polymer chains were achieved. A regioregularity of ∼90% was reported for polymer P23. Furthermore, unsubstituted polythiophene P22a and poly(3-methylthiophene) P22b showed a mixture of iodo and hydrogen end-groups, whereas poly-(3-octylthiophene)s P22c and P23, as well as poly[3-(3-methyl-pentyl)thiophene] P22d showed a halo functionalization at one chain end.

Recently Ozawa and co-workers33–35 successfully applied this strategy to the synthesis of a variety of conjugated polymers. For instance, the well-known head-to-tail poly(3-hexylthiophene)33 (P3HT, P24, Scheme 11) with a high molecular weight (Mn up to 30.6 kDa, PDI = 1.6) and high regioregularity (up to 98%) was prepared in quantitative yields (99%).


Scheme 11

Optimal direct arylation/polycondensation of 2-bromo-3-hexyl-thiophene 29 (2 M concentration) was carried out at 120 °C for 24 h employing Cs2CO3 (1 equiv.) as base, and the relatively expensive Herrmann's catalyst 30 was used to generate in situ a palladium catalyst (ca. 1 mol%) with a tertiary phosphine ligand, i.e. tris(o-Me2N)phosphine 31 (2 mol%), featuring a high coordination ability. Note here that the Herrmann's catalyst (30) converted only partially to a catalytically active species. However, this route is substantially more atom-economical than the conventional Grignard metathesis (GRIM) polymerization to P3HT which produces a stoichiometric amount of MgBr2. To ensure the solubility of P3HT throughout the reaction, THF was chosen as the solvent instead of DMF or N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMA) which are the reaction media commonly used for palladium-catalyzed direct arylation. Interestingly, it was found that the head-to-tail regioregularity improved together with the molecular weight. MALDI-TOF analyses revealed that P3HT with a low molecular weight (i.e. Mn = 1.1 kDa) had chains with Br/Br and H/H end groups, in addition to those with the expected H/Br termini. The authors proposed that homocoupling reactions between C–H and C–H bonds and C–Br and C–Br bonds were operative to a considerable extent at the initial polymerization stage, whereas the cross-coupling reaction between C–Br and C–H bonds dominated at a later stage.

In a further contribution34 the same group reported the synthesis of aryl- end-capped poly(3-hexylthiophene)s Ar-P3HTs (Ar-P24, Scheme 12) via direct arylation, using different p-substituted bromobenzenes (ArBr) as capping agents.


Scheme 12

These reactions were carried out in THF (125 °C, 36 h) with 2-bromo-3-hexyl-thiophene 29 (1 M concentration) as the monomer and Cs2CO3 (1 equiv.) as the base. Complex [PdAr(μ-Br)(P(o-tolyl)3)]232 was employed as the palladium source (1 mol%), either in isolated form or easily generated in situ by the oxidative addition of ArBr to the relatively expensive Pd[P(o-tol)3]2. In addition, KOAc (1 equiv.) and the phosphine ligands 31 (2 mol%) were introduced into the catalytic system to ensure high efficiency of the reaction by avoiding the formation of oligomers and deposition of palladium black. Under these conditions, polymers with Mn ranging from 4.7 to 11.8 kDa (PDIs ∼ 1.7–2.5), up to 98% end-capping efficiency, and 99% of regioregularity were obtained in nearly quantitative yields. On the basis of the observed correlation between molecular weight/PDI and conversion of 2-bromo-3-hexyl-thiophene 29, a two-stage mechanism was suggested for the capped polymer formation. Until complete consumption of the monomer, the chain elongation of end-capped and non-capped P3HTs are competitive, and the molecular weight increases linearly with monomer conversion. After monomer consumption, the resulting capped and non-capped chains coupled to afford highly end-capped Ar-P3HTs featuring a higher molecular weights.

Kanbara and coworkers37 reported the first Pd-catalyzed AA/BB type direct arylation polycondensation of 1,2,4,5-tetrafluorobenzene 33 with 2,7-dibromo-9,9-dioctylfluorene 3 (or 3,6-dibromo-N-octadecylcarbazole 34) as monomers (Scheme 14). The optimized reaction was carried out in the presence of Pd(OAc)2 (5 mol%), a phosphine ligand (PtBu2Me/HBF4, 10 mol%), and K2CO3 (2 equiv.) in N,N-dimethyl acetamide (DMA) as the reaction medium (100 °C, 24 h). Under these conditions, polymer P25 (or P26) was obtained at a high molecular weight (Mn = 17.6–31.5 or 8.3 kDa; PDI = 2.68–3.45 or 1.7), with the maximum Mn value being achieved for the monomer concentration of 0.5 M. Interestingly, P25 molecular weight was ∼10× higher than that for the same polymer synthesized via the conventional Suzuki cross-coupling (Mn = 3.2 kDa) reaction.38 The reaction byproducts of these polycondensations (KBr, CO2, and H2O) were easily removed from the polymers. NMR and MALDI-TOF-MS analyses indicated that P25 featured Br/Br terminals, whereas three Br-substituted carbazole terminals per copolymer chain were found for P26, thereby suggesting the presence of a cross-linked structure caused by the relatively high reactivity of carbazole C–H bonds. Indeed, direct arylation likely occurred at these C–H bonds as well as at the C–H bonds39 of monomer 33. The absorption and photoluminescence spectra of copolymers P25 and P26 were recorded, both in solution and as thin-films. Notably, P25 in solution featured a maximum emission wavelength of 385 nm as well as a quantum yield of 66% at room temperature in air, which was a value higher than that for the polymer prepared via Suzuki coupling (55%). Note that DMA used in this reaction is a highly polar solvent, which is high in the list of harmful chemicals, being volatile and toxic/flammable. The solvent effect was also examined and, unfortunately, no polymeric product was obtained when using toluene or dichlorobenzene.

In a subsequent work40 Kanbara and coworkers further investigated the direct arylation polycondensation of 1,2,4,5-tetrafluorobenzene 33 as the monomer by using 2,7-dibromo-N-octadecylcarbazole 35 and 2,8-dibromo-10,10-dioctyl-N-methylphenazasiline 36 as co-monomers (Scheme 14). Under the same conditions as those for the synthesis of P25 and P26, the polymerization reaction of 1,2,4,5-tetrafluorobenzene 33 with 35 gave an insoluble product (probably P27) which could not be further characterized. The authors claimed that the high reactivity of the C–H bonds at the 3- and 6-positions in the carbazole units were responsible for the cross-linking reactions. On the other hand, the polycondensation reaction of 2,8-dibromo-10,10-dioctyl-N-methylphenazasiline 36 provided a soluble polymer poly[(10,10-dioctyl-N-methylphenazasiline-2,8-diyil)-(2,3,5,6-tetrafluoro-1,4-phenylene)], P28, in 88% yield (Mn = 12.5 kDa, PDI = 1.65). The same group41 also reported the preparation of several related 3,3′,4,4′-tetramethylbithiophene-based alternating copolymers via direct arylation polycondensation. For instance, the direct arylation polycondensation of 3,3′,4,4′-tetramethylbithiophene 38 with 2,7-dibromo-9,9-dioctylfluorene 3 using a 1:1 co-monomer molar ratio (0.30 M concentration), Pd(OAc)2 (2 mol%)/PCy-HBF4 (4 mol%) as the catalytic system, and K2CO3 (2.5 equiv.)/PvOH (30 mol%) in DMA (100 °C, 24 h) afforded a high-Mn polymer P30 (Mn = 36.1 kDa, PDI = 2.76) in good yields (82%) (Scheme 15). Importantly, by removing the phosphine ligand allowed the reduction of the reaction time (3 h) and led to good molecular weight Mn up to 31.8 kDa (PDI = 2.46) in high yields (91%). NMR analyses did not reveal the presence of any branched structures, thereby suggesting that a negligible direct arylation reaction occurred at the aromatic C–H bonds of the fluorene units. Note also that the methyl groups on the 3,3′,4,4′-tetramethylbithiophene monomer 38 allow us to avoid the side reactions at its β-positions. Furthermore, the reaction of 3,6-dibromo-N-octadecylcarbazole 34 as a monomer under the above phosphine-free conditions proceeded to give polymer P31 in 81% yield (Mn = 7.3 kDa, and PDI = 2.13). The extension of the reaction time up to 6 h increased the yields (99%) as well as the molecular weight of the polymer (Mn = 11.7 kDa, PDI = 1.93). However the polycondensation of 2,7-dibromo-N-octadecylcarbazole 35 afforded polymer P32 in low yields (47%), even though a very high Mn of 32.9 kDa (PDI = 5.36) was achieved.

Very recently,42 Kanbara and coworkers reported the rhutenium (Ru)-catalyzed direct arylation of pyrrole derivatives (e.g., 41) with 2,7-dibromo-9,9-dioctylfluorene 3 (Scheme 16) bearing a pyrimidinyl directing group on the nitrogen atom. Replacing the additive for stronger pivalic acid (PivOH), while increasing the reaction temperature (140 °C), leading to higher molecular weights Mn (up to 19.8 kDa) and yields (86%). Importantly, GPC/1H NMR/MALDI-TOF-MASS data indicated minimal polymer decomposition and a nearly quantitative cleavage of the pyrimidinyl substituent. Furthermore, by comparing the optical properties it was evident that polymer P36 exhibited red shifted absorption maximum relative to P35, both in solution and in the solid state (Δλmax = 26 nm and 41 nm, respectively). Metal catalyst-free oxidative coupling reactions

Even though their importance and indispensability is unquestionable, the replacement of (transition) metal catalysts by organic surrogates may have great advantages for large-scale polymer preparation. This is readily rationalized by the relatively high costs, the contingent toxicity and the demanding removal of (transition) metals from final products, especially in the case of polymers with complex side chains.70 In addition, minimization of metal leaching into the final problem will be also beneficial for the quality and performance of the target device (see above). Consequently, the development of efficient (transition) metal-free protocols for materials synthesis represents an important field of research with remarkable growth over the last few years. Examples of these approaches include controlled radical polymerizations (CRP),71i.e. atom transfer radical polymerization (ATRP), nitroxide-mediated radical polymerization (NMRP), and ring opening polymerization (ROP). All the CRP methods rely on forming equilibria between active species (radicals) and various types of so-called dormant species.71 Radicals may be reversibly trapped in a deactivation/activation process according to Fig. 8A, or they can be involved in a “reversible transfer” degenerative exchange process (Fig. 8B).

Conclusions

In this review we summarized the major findings for the development of methodologies for the green synthesis of poly(arylenes), which are semiconducting systems of prime importance for the realization of unconventional (opto)electronic devices.

It is now clear that during the last three years, outstanding results in the field have been achieved. Several studies have addressed and elucidated problematic aspects when using conventional Stille and Suzuki cross-coupling reactions for poly(arylene) preparation as well as the implications and benefits of employing alternative reaction conditions, solvents, and reactants. Particularly, direct arylation polycondensation schemes enable the preparation of a range of poly(arylenes) featuring characteristics/performance which may rival or exceed those of those achieved using conventional routes. For this approach, it remains a fundamental question, i.e. how to address selectivity as well as regioregularity issues for monomers lacking proper directing groups.

Tuning the reaction conditions in terms of temperature, catalyst loading and the use of bulky catalyst ligands enabled more accurate control of homocoupling, end-groups, residual metal and branching defects of polymers. Further efforts in these directions are certainly needed to achieve a more trustworthy and mature methodology. Among the possible routes, the proper design of catalysts and/or ligands appears to be the most promising strategy.

In this regard, the reader can also refer to a very recent highlight article by Thompson et al.29 where it nicely discusses the origin of the main chain defects of the polymer through direct (hetero)arylation, the dependence of polymer properties on these defects, and all available tools effective for their control.

For all the reviewed methods of polyarylene synthesis, future development should focus on further expanding the substrate scope, which will ultimately enable the access to the most efficient polymers for (opto)electronic devices, carefully optimizing the reaction conditions to gain a better control over the polymerization reaction, and obviously simplifying the reaction protocols in terms of elimination (or, at least, minimization) of expensive/toxic reagents. The ultimate aim of this review is to anticipate and stimulate a discussion for the need to efficiently synthesize organic semiconductors at a scale much greater than that used in the laboratories, and with minimal production of waste and toxic byproducts. Despite the impressive achievements in the field of poly(arylene) sustainable synthetic processes summarized in this review, to date there are no comprehensive reports focusing on the evaluation of their costs in terms of energy consumption, labour cost, waste disposal, also in comparison with conventional methods. Thus, it is essential to further develop the area in such a direction, as well as explore new synthetic approaches, for enabling the commercialization of π-conjugated polymer-based devices.
