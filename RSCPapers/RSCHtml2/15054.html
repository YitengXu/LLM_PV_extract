Colloidal semiconductor nanocrystals: the aqueous approach

Abstract

This article summarizes the main achievements and challenges in the field of the aqueous synthesis of semiconductor quantum dots in colloidal solutions. Developments in the last two decades demonstrate the great potential of this approach to synthesize nanocrystalline materials with superior properties such as strong photoluminescence, long time stability and compatibility with biological media, and the variability in assembling and self-assembling into larger structures or on surfaces. Being relatively straightforward, the aqueous approach provides some advantages such as versatility, scalability, environmental friendliness and cost effectiveness, leading in summary to very attractive application perspectives.

Introduction

The aqueous colloidal synthesis of semiconductor nanocrystals (NCs) or quantum dots (QDs) is an advantageous alternative to the widely used organometallic route.1 In comparison to the latter, the aqueous method (1) employs the most widespread, environmentally friendly and biocompatible solvent, (2) is not restricted to an inert atmosphere, (3) is easily up-scalable, and (4) provides various functionalizations of the NCs by applying an appropriate capping ligand which in turn may be further functionalized by electrostatic or covalent linking.2 The formation of nanoparticles is a dynamic process which is usually explained by an Ostwald ripening (OR) mechanism mainly consisting of the growth of larger particles at the expense of smaller ones present in an ensemble. The history of this method begins 30 years ago starting with an early work of Henglein.3,4 Comprehensive historical overviews of the progress made in the field during this time may be found in recent publications.57 The aqueous approach has already yielded stable binary IIVI and IVVI compounds such as CdS,810 CdTe,1,1114 CdSe,1517 ZnSe,18,19 HgTe,20 PbS2125 as well as some alloyed particles such as CdSeTe,26 CdHgTe,2729 ZnSeS,30 ZnCdSe,19,3133 ZnHgSe,34 and ZnSeTe.35,36 Most of these materials exhibit strong photoluminescence (PL) (Fig. 1) whose efficiency is substantially determined by a proper choice of the stabilizer.37 The strong tuneable luminescence and the high extinction coefficients of the NCs define their application potential in the fields of light emitting diodes (LEDs),38 colour conversion,39 energy scavenging,40,41 fluorescence sensing,4244 bioimaging45 and biosensing.46 Without doubt, in the aqueous synthesis short chain thiols are the most used ligands for the stabilization of various semiconductor materials.

The mild synthetic conditions used in aqueous syntheses often disable perfect arrangements of the atoms in the crystal lattice of the nanoparticles which is more easily achieved by the syntheses in high boiling organic solvents. In order to improve the crystallinity, size distribution and the corresponding optical properties of the QDs the use of microwave irradiation48,49 or the synthesis in autoclaves50 has been reported to be applied to CdTe and ZnSe NCs. The microwave assisted synthesis has also resulted in alloyed ZnSeS48 and ZnCdTe51 NCs and even coreshellshell CdTeCdSZnS52 highly emitting particles which, to the best of our knowledge, appeared as a first successful example of such kind of sophisticated structure prepared by the aqueous method. Different coreshell semiconductor nanoparticles are achievable by a common aqueous synthetic protocol, e.g. ZnSeZnS,53 ZnCdSeZnS,54 CdSeCdS,5559 CdTeCdS,60 CdTeZnTe,61 CdSeTeZnS,62 CdHgTeCdS,63 and HgTeCdS.64 The obtaining of these coreshell and coreshellshell structures directly in water constitutes a large step forward towards a superior quality of NCs accessible previously solely from the organometallic synthetic route.

Interesting hybrid approaches for the synthesis of coreshell NCs combining aqueous and organic phase techniques have recently been developed. Depending on the accessibility of the corresponding core and shell materials, cores being synthesized in water (organics) have been transferred into organics (water) for subsequent shell formation. Thereby, CdTe and CdHgTe cores transferred from water were coated by ZnS shells via the hot injection technique.65 The opposite direction of the core being transferred (from organics to water) has been applied for the synthesis of InP/ZnS QDs.66 As examples of a shape control, the facile synthesis of CdTe67,68 and CdHgTe69 nanorods with controllable sizes and aspect ratios is mentioned. Nevertheless, it is accepted that the aqueous method usually does not permit a direct efficient shape control of the nanoparticles during their growth due to temperature limitations: temperatures of around 100 C are not sufficient to overcome the energy barrier of transforming the usually evolving zinc-blende to the wurtzite structure. Thus, the NCs synthesized exhibit in most cases an isotropic zinc blende cubic crystal structure and mainly spherical or quasi-spherical shape, since this shape is thermodynamically the most stable.70

In the following paragraphs we will review in more detail recent results of the aqueous synthesis of various semiconductor materials, arranging them in decreasing order of their band gaps, i.e. beginning with wide band gap compounds emitting in the ultra-violet (UV) region (ZnSe) via the visible (CdTe) to the near-infrared (NIR) and infrared (IR) narrow energy gaps (HgTe, PbS). The synthetic part including a search for less toxic materials and insights into the NC growth mechanism will be appended by processing approaches and application perspectives. We note that the applications of semiconductor NCs synthesized in water mostly relate to CdTe since this material has been best investigated. Nevertheless, our experience shows that the majority of the assembly and application approaches developed for CdTe NCs are also transferrable to other types of thiol-capped nanoparticles.

General synthetic protocol

In general, an aqueous synthetic protocol consists of the reaction between metal ions or their complexes and chalcogen containing precursors in the presence of an appropriate stabilizer with subsequent nucleation and growth of the NCs, typically controlled by heating. As metal precursors water soluble salts are used, commonly perchlorates and chlorides. As a sulphur source Na2S22,25 is widely employed instead of the less convenient H2S.71 Direct injection of sodium sulphide solution is done also for a CdS and ZnS shell formation.52 However, in our opinion, organic compounds which slowly decompose with a controlled release of sulphur ions, such as thioacetamide55,57 and thiourea53,72 are more appropriate for this purpose. Se2 and Te2 are introduced in the reaction in the form of H2Se30 and H2Te1 gases generated by the decomposition of the corresponding aluminium chalcogenide, or in the form of NaHSe15,73 and NaHTe7478 prepared by the reduction of elemental Se or Te by NaBH4.79 An electrochemical generation of H2Te has also been applied in the synthesis of CdTe13 and HgTe80 NCs. Mixed NaHSexTe1x81 or simultaneous injection of NaHSe and NaHTe solutions26 was employed for the one-pot synthesis of alloyed CdSeTe NCs. The slow release of selenium ions is realized by the decomposition of N,N-dimethylselenourea in the reaction mixture under microwave57 or laser55 irradiation. We note that H2Te injection for the precursor generation is not limited to only aqueous media and can be applied also in organic solvents (toluene,82 dimethylacetamide and dimethylformamide83) and even ionic liquids84 whereas NaHTe is useful solely in water.

Therefore the typical synthetic protocol for obtaining aqueous thiol-capped NCs is a one-pot process consisting of 3 main steps:

1. Formation of metalthiol complexes by dissolving a metal salt in water followed by the addition of an appropriate thiol with subsequent pH adjustment.

2. Injection of a chalcogen source into a thoroughly de-aerated (through purging by an inert gas (Ar, N2)) metal/thiol solution results in the formation of a MeX precursor (frequently referred to as a monomer).

3. Nucleation and growth of the NCs by heating or microwave irradiation, usually upon reflux under open air conditions. Note that the growth of HgTe and PbS particles occurs at room temperature. Fig. 3 presents an example of the aqueous synthesis employing aluminium chalcogenides to produce the hydrogen chalcogenide gas.


Fig. 3 Schematic presentation of the synthesis of aqueous thiol-capped binary semiconductor NCs (ZnSe, CdSe, CdTe, HgTe) using aluminum chalcogenides (Al2Se3, Al2Te3) as the chalcogen source. Courtesy of A. Dubavik (LMU Munich, Germany).

Recently Zhou et al. dramatically simplified the aqueous synthesis, having reproducibly prepared highly luminescent CdTe QDs in a conical flask at room temperature, without precursor preparation, pH adjustment, inert gas protection, and heating.85 In this protocol the QDs were synthesized through the stepwise addition of water, CdCl2, thiol, Na2TeO3, NaBH4, and N2H4. The adjustment of the feed ratio of the reagents allowed for an efficient control of the particle sizes. Hydrazine as a strong reducing agent protected the monomers from oxidation and, being a weak electrolyte, permitted the QD growth by altering the electrostatic environment of the growing crystals. At the same time, the employment of a large amount of highly toxic hydrazine (almost three fourth of the total reaction volume) and low concentrations of the monomers are serious drawbacks of this protocol.

ZnX NCs

Zinc chalcogenides attract scientific interest due to the demand for non- or low-toxic nanoparticulate semiconductor materials, decreasing the amount of released heavy metal pollutants such as Cd, Hg, Pb into the environment. The most developed materials via an aqueous approach are ZnSe NCs. ZnS, with a wide bulk band gap of 3.54 eV (2.58 eV for ZnSe and 2.26 eV for ZnTe, all of zinc blende crystal structure),99 is commonly used as a shell material for the creation of coreshell structures. The synthesis of ZnTe NCs, although reported,100 has not yielded high quality light emitting colloids, probably due to the high reactivity of ZnTe towards oxygen and water and its instability under ambient conditions.36,101

ZnSe

Nanostructured ZnSe exhibiting luminescence in the near-UV and blue spectral region is a promising candidate for the fabrication of blue emitting LEDs and laser diodes,102 and as a potentially low-toxic material also for biolabelling. The first successful aqueous colloidal synthesis of strongly emitting ZnSe NCs was reported by Shavel et al.30 A typical procedure comprised the reaction between Zn ions and H2Se gas generated by the decomposition of Al2Se3, carried by N2 or Ar to the reaction mixture. This nucleation step occurs in the presence of thiol-stabilizers (thioglycolic acid (TGA), 3-mercaptopropionic acid (MPA) or 1-thioglycerol (TG) (Fig. 2)) and is followed by the growth of the NCs under reflux. We note that the pH value of the reaction mixture plays a very important role in the aqueous synthesis of semiconductor nanoparticles, ensuring their stability and providing control of their growth. Since the as-prepared ZnSe NC colloids exhibited negligible photoluminescence quantum yields (QY) (0.1%), they underwent a photochemical treatment by an illumination with white or UV light in the presence of the Zn salt and the thiol-stabilizer.18,30,73 The latter was decomposed under the irradiation, releasing S ions which subsequently reacted with the Zn ions, forming a ZnS or a mixed ZnSSe shell on the surface of the ZnSe NCs.103 The evolution of the optical properties of the ZnSe NCs during white light illumination and a scheme of the ZnS layer formation are shown in Fig. 4. This photochemical treatment greatly enhances the QY up to 50%.18 In fact, due to the small size of these particles (23 nm), their structure is considered more as alloyed than as coreshell, possibly graded. Nevertheless, the escape of charge carriers or their nonradiative recombination in traps is reduced drastically. Application of microwave irradiation for the synthesis of ZnSe NCs leads to a simplification of the procedure, enabling the fast preparation of an emitting colloid in one stage, excluding additional photochemical treatments.48

L-Glutathione (GSH) capping allows the preparation of readily luminescing ZnSe NCs without additional post-preparative treatment, probably due to the quite labile ZnSC bond which decomposes by heating during particle growth, forming a ZnS shell layer, similar to the formation of the CdS layer during the growth of CdTe NCs as described in ref. 92. The thus-prepared NCs have an average size of 2.7 nm, emission with a maximum at approx. 370 nm and a QY of up to 20%.19,53 An additional reaction stage consisting of the heat treatment of the synthesized QDs in the presence of Zn ions, GSH and thiourea leads to the formation of a more pronounced ZnS shell and a corresponding QY enhancement of up to 65%.53 Nevertheless, in spite of the high QYs achieved in the synthesis of ZnSe and ZnSeS NCs, they demonstrate an ultimate PL peak position at a wavelength slightly above 400 nm, i.e. at the edge of the visible region, not including weak trap-related emission in the region of 400600 nm. Apparently, the conditions of the aqueous synthesis do not permit size tuning of ZnSe QDs over a wide range, thereby entrapping their luminescence in a very narrow corridor, typically 350400 nm. In this case, the formation of alloyed structures can provide an additional opportunity for controlling the material properties by changing their composition and consequently their band gaps.104

ZnSe based alloyed NCs

Although the aforementioned synthesis of ZnSe NCs accompanied by a thin ZnS layer surrounding may also be considered as an alloying procedure, the composition of the resulting particles is not fully tuned by the process conditions, i.e. such alloying is an occasional rather than a controllable one. In a more controllable way the incorporation of Cd atoms (ions) into a ZnSe matrix was carried out. Two basic strategies have been developed for the synthesis of alloyed ZnxCd1xSe NCs: incorporation of Cd ions into already synthesized ZnSe QDs,19,33 or direct synthesis of an alloyed structure by means of the partial replacement of a Zn precursor by a Cd salt in the reaction mixture.31,32,54 Incorporation of Cd into GSH-capped ZnSe NCs enabled variation of the alloy composition up to a molar ratio of Zn/Cd = 1/1.5, shifting the PL to longer wavelengths from 360 nm (for the initial pure ZnSe) to 500 nm (see Fig. 5a).19 A typical evolution of the absorption and fluorescence spectra of the GSH-capped ZnSe QDs after injection of the Cd precursor is illustrated in Fig. 5b. This approach has yielded alloyed ZnCdSe NCs with tuneable composition in the size range of about 34 nm and strong fluorescence with a QY of up to 50% (see Fig. 5c).19,33 Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) measurements revealed the gradual transformation of the ZnSe lattice to that of CdSe. The prepared GSH-capped ZnCdSe QDs underwent selective fluorescence quenching in the presence of Pb2+ ions.42 By coupling the GSH-capped QDs with a high throughput detection system, a simple tool for the quick and ultrasensitive Pb2+ detection was demonstrated. This nature-mimicking system is capable of Pb2+ detection even in the presence of an ionic mixture.

The other strategy, i.e. the direct synthesis of alloyed ZnCdSe NCs ensures the decrease of the Cd content, providing the same PL shift.31,32,54 The resulting composition of thus-obtained alloyed crystals depends mainly on the initial ratio of concentrations of the metal precursors and their intrinsic reactivities towards the chalcogen source. It was observed that the composition of TGA-stabilized ZnCdSe NCs determined by elemental analysis is very close to the feed ratio of the Zn and Cd precursors used in the synthesis, which enables efficient composition control.32 The same method of photochemical treatment as described for pure ZnSe was applied to alloyed particles in order to enhance their emission by means of a ZnS enriched layer formation, leading to QYs of 2030%.31,32 Injection of a Cd precursor to the Zn salt and TGA containing solution during the post-preparative photochemical treatment provides a further shift of the PL to longer wavelengths due to additional incorporation of Cd atoms into the NC surface layer. Obtaining alloyed GSH-capped ZnxHg1xSe QDs by a one-pot synthesis leads to further narrowing of the band gap and yields colloids emitting in the range of 550620 nm.34 XRD analysis shows a zinc blende cubic crystal structure for all aqueous ZnSe NCs and their alloys.

An alternative way of changing the band gap energies consists of the preparation of alloyed materials based on a ZnSe matrix by means of a partial replacement of Se atoms (or ions) by e.g. Te atoms (or ions) aiming at the formation of a smaller band gap material. Recently, this approach has been realized in a multistep synthesis of Cd-free water-soluble NCs.35 The application of GSH as a stabilizer for ZnSeTe NCs improved and simplified the synthetic method towards a facile one-step protocol, providing blue emitting QDs in the region of 370430 nm with QYs of up to 20%.36 In contrast to ZnCdSe alloys, only a very small amount of Te (relative to the initial feed ratio) is incorporated into the resulting NCs. The maximum amount of the injected H2Te was converted to Te nanoparticles. The incorporation of the obtained particles into a polymer matrix allows us to obtain stable NCpolymer composites directly from the as-prepared aqueous colloid by the addition of a prepolymer. Thereby the alloyed NCs in the dispersion are wrapped in a polymer shell, which serves as an additional stabilizer. Thus, the chemical and physical properties of semiconductor NCs can be varied by changing the particle size as well as their composition. Alloying paves the way for designing materials with electronic and optical properties, different from their corresponding constituting compounds.

CdX NCs

Although the first colloidal material synthesized via the aqueous approach and thoroughly investigated was CdS, the most successful example of the cadmium chalcogenides is CdTe owing to its unique optical properties, in particular, its strong and tuneable photoluminescence in the visible region. For an overview of the work on CdS colloidal synthesis we refer the reader to the corresponding chapters in the recently issued excellent books edited by Schmid5 and Rogach.6

CdTe

CdTe NCs have been obtained directly in water firstly by Resch et al. in the presence of hexametaphosphate,100 then Rajh et al. employed a mixed stabilizer system consisting of hexametaphosphate and 3-mercapto-l,2-propanediol.12 Later the optimization by Rogach et al. led to a synthesis of stable NCs with sizes ranging from 1.3 to 2.4 nm solely in the presence of thiols, viz. 2-mercaptoethanol and 1-thioglycerol.11 An important step towards strongly emitting CdTe NCs was taken by Gao et al. through the utilization of thioglycolic acid as the capping agent.105 State-of-the-art preparations yield aqueous CdTe NCs capped by mercaptoacids, mercaptoamines or mercaptoaminoacids, which have diameters of up to 6 nm and a PL covering most of the visible region and extending to the NIR with a QY of up to 7080%. Thus, they can compete with the best organometallically prepared materials.13 For instance, as-prepared NCs by microwave irradiation demonstrate a QY as high as 82% and a narrow size distribution.49 Here we would like to note that the very high QYs reported in the literature should be considered with care, since the QY value is strongly dependent on the method of determination, standards used, instrumental conditions, to name a few. A reliable and quite detailed procedure for QY determination has been reported recently by Grabolle et al.106

A variety of thiol capping ligands as summarized in Fig. 2 is applicable for the synthesis of CdTe NCs. The thiols determine the chemical functionality and the charge of the NC surface. This opens the possibility to manipulate these NCs, e.g. by the layer-by-layer (LbL) assembly, oriented attachment, electrostatic assembly or by electrophoresis. Specific chemical functionalities (e.g. amino- or carboxylic groups) are attractive for the covalent linking to macrosurfaces (e.g. electrodes or substrates for optical studies), to the surfaces of other NCs as well as to biomolecules. By this, proper thiol-capping may be considered as the key factor in determining the successful fabrication, assembly and application of the aqueous NCs. In addition to the common carboxylic- or amino-surface functionalities a variety of more specific capping thiol molecules was used. For example, 5-mercaptomethyltetrazole (5-HSCH2Tz) is one more recently developed thiol-containing stabilizer enabling reversible 3D assembly (gelation) of CdTe NCs98 and moreover the formation of hybrid CdTe/Au structures.107 This assembly, initiated in the presence of several metal ions, may be promising for optical sensing applications.

The chiral structure of the CdX surface was imparted by the application of enantiomeric thiols, L- and D-cysteinemethylester hydrochloride, bidentate R- and S--lipoic acids in the synthesis.75,89 These NCs retain their optical activity even after ligand exchange for an achiral stabilizer (so-called chiral memory effect).

As in the case of ZnSe and its alloys, aqueous CdTe NCs commonly display the zinc blende cubic crystal structure. It is noted that it is not possible to completely prevent the incorporation of sulfur into the largely grown nanocrystals either by the choice of the solvent or by a variation of the stabilizers.109 Nevertheless, this process leads to the formation of a CdS layer on the CdTe NC surface (or CdTe(S) NCs with a sulfur enriched shell), resulting in an improvement of the material's quality, e.g. higher stability and QY as well as longer PL lifetimes.13,60,110

Aqueous CdTe NCs display different stabilities in varied water based media, which strongly depends on their size and concentration. Thus, Boldt et al., having tested TGA- and MPA-stabilized CdTe particles in various buffers (in the pH range 511) commonly used for the indirect immunofluorescence labelling of cells, showed that a lower concentration of QDs results in the detachment of ligands from the particle surface and therefore a decreased stability.111 The control parameter being monitored during a period of one week was the PL of several CdTe NCs fractions derived via size-selective precipitation of the colloids (described in the section Postpreparative treatment of CdTe nanocrystals). As expected, larger semiconductor QDs have shown a greater resistance to photobleaching than smaller ones. However, smaller QDs displayed higher stability under acidic conditions and low buffer concentrations, which was most probably due to their cluster-like, more defined structure. The authors have observed pseudozero-order kinetics of the emission diminution, ascribing it to the oxidation of the particle surface by the dissolved oxygen.

Although, as mentioned above, the aqueous method does not permit the direct synthesis of semiconductor particles with a nonspherical shape, a few examples of the formation of 1D nanostructures during the CdTe NC growth have been reported.67,68,112 The key factors ensuring the CdTe growth in one direction and the subsequent stability of the obtained structures are the low concentration of the precursors, employing of a mixed ligand system and moderate temperatures in order to suppress OR of the NCs. Zhang et al. successfully prepared CdTe nanorods using TGA and TG as mixed stabilizers and incubating the precursor solution with concentrations of Cd2+ = 2.4 mM and a ratio of Cd2+/TGA/TG/HTe = 1/1.2/1.2/0.5 at 80 C for several hours (see Fig. 8).68,112 At the same time, in the sole presence of TGA, metastable 1D wurtzite structures have transformed under the present conditions into thermodynamically stable spherical zinc blende NCs. Furthermore, applying a mixture of TGA and 2-mercaptoethylamine allows for a control of the aspect ratio of the CdTe nanorods during their growth.112 The elongation causes a long-wavelength shift of the optical absorption, resulting in the dark colour of the nanorods suspension as seen in Fig. 8c. These nanorods exhibit a weak luminescence owing to the weaker quantum confinement of 1D nanostructures, relative to that of 0D QDs.

Therefore, shaped CdTe nanostructures, such as 1D wires,113,114 twisted ribbons,115 2D sheets,94 and 3D gel-like networks,107,116,117 are achievable via postpreparative self-organization of preformed QDs. Thus, the up to date developed assembly approaches yield a wide variety of nanoparticle-based structures starting from small QD clusters90,118,119 through 1D nanowires, 2D layer-by-layer films and highly porous 3D gels to closely packed solids120 (monoliths). Most of these approaches have been recently reviewed in ref. 47.

CdTe nanocrystal growth mechanism

Similarly to the organometallic route, the formation of aqueous NCs consists of a fast nucleation stage, followed by slow OR. Coalescence of the precursors monomers or even some magic-sized clusters is the main process accounting for nucleation, whereas OR comes into the foreground after monomer depletion. It is also considered that an oriented attachment (OA) mechanism coexists simultaneously with OR, especially in the early stages of the particle growth, when two crystallographically oriented nanoparticles combine together to form a larger one.121

Due to the lower temperature, different precursor reactivity and reaction media, the NC growth rate is much slower as compared to the organometallic approach and therefore the formation of aqueous NCs has its own peculiarities. The kinetics of the NC growth is determined mainly by the concentration of the precursors, the nature of the stabilizer, the Cd2+/Te2/thiol ratio, the pH of the solution, and its temperature. Yang et al. explained the growth of CdTe NCs by electrostatic effects occurring on the nanoparticles surface.122 According to this suggestion, the nature of the diffuse layer influences the ability of the monomer (i.e. CdTe precursor) diffusion and hence the growth rate of the NCs (see Fig. 9). Thus, the reaction conditions reducing the thickness of the electric double-layer foster monomer diffusion and a rapid growth of the NCs. At the same time, the diffuse layer also regulates a charge-selective transfer of Cd monomers. The neutral monomers, such as the complex Cd(MPA), migrate through the diffuse layer more easily than the charged ones Cd(MPA)22 or Cd(MPA)34, thus facilitating the growth of the NCs. Electrostatic effects play an important role not only in the monomer diffusion but also in the interparticle interaction, especially at the initial stage of the reaction, where CdTe NCs grow through the agglomeration of small clusters.123 At this step the decrease in the electrostatic repulsion facilitates aggregation and consequently the growth of the particles.

Experimental results and calculations employing stability constants of cadmium complexes with TGA performed by Shavel et al. also reveal the domination of the uncharged Cd(TGA) complex in the NC growth, gaining further insight into the factors governing the NC growth.124 A numerical calculation shows a clear correlation between the concentration ratio of Cd2+/thiol in the initial solution and the QY of the resulting CdTe NCs. An improvement of the emission efficiency of the CdTe QDs up to ca. 5060% was achieved through decreasing of the Cd/TGA ratio to 1:1.3, which is advantageous for increasing the concentration of the Cd2+thiol complex. Moreover, maintaining the solution pH at 12 allows the reduction of the full width at half-maximum (FWHM) and the Stokes shift. The decomposition of the Cdthiol complexes rather than the diffusion of the monomers is suggested as a reaction rate-limiting process.125 In this respect, the stability of the Cd(MPA) complex is lower than that of Cd(TGA), which leads to a faster release of the monomers and correspondingly to a faster particle growth. The key role of a ligand shell in the growth process and QYs of aqueous CdTe NCs has also been proven, considering its influence on the activation energy via the formation of a transition-state complex.126

As was proposed by Yin et al., coalescence of small particles plays a major role in the initial growth stages of TGA-capped CdTe NCs, while the later stages are mainly controlled by OR.121 As is seen from the TEM images presented in Fig. 10ad, after 20 min of the reaction most of the irregularly shaped NCs exhibited the characteristics of growth by the OA mechanism, such as retained lattice defects (twins and stacking faults) and clear coalescence profiles of small building blocks, whereas at 200 min the particles evolved into a relatively perfect lattice and a more regular shape with smooth edges, achieved via OR. Fitting the growth kinetics further confirms this assumption: the evolution of the particle size is better fitted by employing an OA mechanism in the early stage (first 40 min), whereas the OR theory provides a better convergence at the later stage (after 50 min) (see Fig. 10e). It was found that in the initial growth stage the increasing particle concentration accelerates the growth rate, providing more opportunities for collisions between the NCs while in the later stage the particle growth becomes slower in the system with higher particle concentration. At the same time, as is seen from Fig. 10fh, the increase in the particles concentration leads to a rise of the surface charge density, inhibiting OA by the NC repulsion, and consequently results in a smaller OA rate constant and correspondingly a higher activation energy. With the increase of the particle sizes, the OA rate constant largely decreases due to reduced diffusion. In addition, in the course of the growth, the electrostatic particleparticle repulsion increases, and thus the OA rate constant decreases abruptly.

Strong interactions between the adsorbed layer inside the diffuse layer and the Cd monomers enable accelerated NC growth and NCs with high QYs are gained. This assumption elucidates the difference between the growth rates of CdTe capped by MPA and TG. The deprotonated carboxyl groups of MPA strongly interact with Cd, assisting monomer adsorption. On the contrary, although a similar amount of monomers are transported through the diffuse layer formed by the hydroxyl groups of TG, only a few monomers are adsorbed on the NC surface due to the weak interaction of Cd and the OH groups, retarding the growth. In general, a relatively quick particle growth leads to an insufficient quality reflected in a low crystallinity and a large number of defects and surface states. Oppositely, a comparatively slow growth rate results in a high content of sulfur (as a product of the thiol decomposition) in the particles and a higher probability of NC oxidation.124

Although the NC growth is considered as the stage determining the quality of the material and consequently its optical properties, Zhou et al. have demonstrated that well separated and controlled nucleation greatly contributed to the luminescence efficiency of CdTe QDs.81 Accordingly, the reorganization of precursors via their aging over a period of 9 hours at room temperature resulted in the formation of highly crystalline nuclei which promoted further growth of defect-free particles. In this case nucleation is also determined by the interaction between precursor species, in particular via their electrostatic repulsion, leading to decomposition of amorphous nuclei aggregates formed at the very early nucleation steps, which in turn is governed by the particle surface.

Obviously, the formation and growth of any kind of NCs is a multifactorial process, whose analysis requires a complex approach, taking into account various aspects including the reactivity of the initial precursors, the stability of intermediate complexes, electrostatic interactions between monomers and growing species, their activation energy etc. In addition, a comprehensive description is further complicated by changing the role of certain factors at different stages of particle formation and growth. Moreover, a characterization of the kinetic regimes depends intrinsically on the chemical procedure adopted.127 Although a creation of an universal theory embracing different sides of the aqueous NC synthesis seems to be quite difficult, a large number of experimental investigations conducted up to date pave the way for the generation of an optimized synthetic protocol for at least the main systems. For instace, the basic conditions for the preparation of high quality CdTe NCs capped with TGA, MPA and TG are as follows: a molar ratio of Cd2+/Te2/stabilizer = 1/0.5/1.3, pH 11.512, and H2Te gas injection. At the same time, in order to obtain cysteamine stabilized particles the Cd2+/Te2/stabilizer ratio and the pH should be adjusted to 1/0.5/2.6 and 66.5, respectively.

Postpreparative treatment of CdTe nanocrystals

In certain cases the quality of the resulting NCs is not satisfactory even under optimal conditions. In order to improve the quality of the as-synthesized particles as well as to impart to them new properties and functionalities, postpreparative treatments are applied. Some of these methods are: size selection,1,128 surface modification by means of Cdthiol complex formation,78,105 photoetching,1,129 ligand exchange,130 and ligand modification.131,132
Size selection of CdTe nanocrystals. Since in the beginning of its development the aqueous synthesis usually yielded NCs with quite broad size distributions, the as-prepared colloids were often subjected to a size selective precipitation. This procedure consists of the gradual precipitation of the nanoparticles induced by portional addition of a nonsolvent (e.g. 2-propanol) into a preliminary concentrated NC solution.1,133 This technique, originally designed for narrowing the size distributions and the isolation of fractions showing sufficiently enhanced emission in comparison to the ensemble average, today is mostly used for the preparation of QD colloid series with very accurately tuned optical properties, as shown in Fig. 11. Note that in this example the size distribution is narrowed to only about 10% standard deviation.

Recently a more facile separation method was introduced by Ho et al. having applied free flow electrophoresis for the fractionation of TGA-capped CdTe NCs.128 Unlike the gel electrophoresis method134 validated mostly as an analytical tool, this technique is more convenient and operational on a preparative scale. Several variables such as buffer composition, buffer and sample infusion rate, potential field, and channel bed temperature play important roles in the successful size separation of nanoparticles. The FWHM of the PL peak is reduced by 50% as compared with the emission spectra of the separated samples with the original one, as is seen in Fig. 12. This technique not only separates the CdTe NC colloids into narrow fractions but also allows the removal of unreacted species. Moreover, it can potentially be used for a controlled self-organization of nanoparticles under the influence of an electrical field.

Phototreatment. Photochemical etching of colloidal NCs is a powerful tool for enhancing their quality. It has been shown that the PL efficiency of TGA-capped CdTe colloid increases upon irradiation with 400 nm light of a 450 W Xe lamp in oxygen saturated solutions. Presumably, the reason for this improvement of the PL is the etching of tellurium trap states which are responsible for the non-radiative recombination.1,135 In this case, the NCs having the most defect states dissolve primarily during photochemical etching. On the contrary, photochemical treatment of the CdTeTGA colloid under inert atmosphere retards its photodegradation and results in a CdTeCdS coreshell structure formation.129 The formation of this structure occurs via an intermediate Cd(TGA) complex shell followed by a slow generation of sulphide ions reacting with cadmium atoms on the CdTe surface, similar to the growth of a ZnS shell on the surface of ZnSe QDs as described above.129 CdS being a larger band-gap material passivates surface trap states and prevents nonradiative recombinations from reaching a QY of 85%. The crude aqueous colloids usually contain certain amounts of unreacted species (e.g. Cdthiol complexes) which may contribute to illumination induced photoreactions. Under the same conditions MPA does not release sulphide ions, leading to only a very limited influence on the PL intensity.
Ligand exchange and ligand modification. In order to impart solubility in organic solvents to aqueous particles, strategies of ligand exchange and ligand modification have been developed. Gaponik et al. employed a partial exchange of TGA with 1-dodecanethiol (DDT), allowing for an almost complete (90%) phase transfer of CdTe NCs synthesized in water to nonpolar organic solvents such as toluene, hexane, chloroform, etc.130 The optical properties of the transferred QDs exhibit only a moderate alternation in comparison to the particles in water. It was found that acetone plays an important role in an efficient phase transfer of the particles, reducing the surface tension at the H2O/DDT interface. Utilizing this phase transfer technique a hybrid aqueous-organic synthesis of coreshell CdTeZnS QDs has been developed.65 This approach affords a facile growth of a ZnS shell in organic solvents at high temperatures around the CdTe cores originally synthesized in water to yield small-diameter, highly luminescent material. Combining the treatment with DDT and amphiphilic octadecylamine-modified poly(acrylic acid) (PAA-ODA) in a one-pot process, Yuwen et al. have developed an interesting method of encapsulation of coreshell MPA-capped aqueous CdTeCdS QDs into micelles according to the scheme presented in Fig. 13.136 This polymer coating significantly enhances the photostability and the QY of the QDs, retarding the diffusion of oxygen as well as the detachment of thiol ligands and consequently preventing the oxidation of the particle surface and the release of Cd2+ ions into the environment. By controlling the molar ratio of PAA-ODA to QDs, the number of QDs in the micelles can be tuned from a single QD to multiple particles.

Ligand shell modifications via the interaction between COO groups on the surface of TGA-capped CdTe NCs and the NH2 group of 1-hexadecylamine ensures an efficient phase transfer of the QDs from water to chloroform.131 The adjustment of the pH of the water phase induces decomposition of COONa+NH2 bonds and results in the reversible transport of the particles back into water.

An interesting postpreparative method combining alkanethiols and an alkylammonium surfactant, allowing for a controllable band gap engineering of TGA-capped CdTe NCs, was proposed by Akamatsu et al.74 This method consists of a phase transfer of aqueous QDs to toluene, employing tetra-n-octylammonium bromide (TOAB) with subsequent injection of various alkanethiols, leading to selective dissociation of Te species from the crystal surface and a simultaneous attachment of the alkanethiol at the defect site thus formed. As a result, the PL experienced a significant blue shift whereas the size of the particles remained constant.

The utilization of QD colloids as dispersions of individual particles is restricted to applications in fluorescence sensing (e.g. discriminative detection of lead42 and manganese44 ions in solutions) or to bio-imaging.45 Since most of the other applications require assembled nanoparticles rather than individual QDs, diverse processing techniques have been developed until now. The immobilization of QDs onto a substrate surface by covalent linking or by LbL deposition leaves them partially exposed to an environment, whereas their encapsulation or embedding into a continuous matrix can provide efficient isolation. As intermediate structures in the nanowire formation linear pearl necklace aggregates were found which subsequently recrystallized into uniform nanowires having high aspect ratios (up to 500) and diameters equal to the diameters of the NCs.113 Importantly, these nanowires preserved the strong luminescence of their parental CdTe NCs to a large degree (QYs of up to 29%) whereas their PL maxima underwent a remarkable red shift due to a weakening of the quantum confinement and energy/charge transfer (see Fig. 14a). At the same time, nanocrystals assembled into 2D films retained less than 10% of the QY of the initial NCs (see Fig. 14b).94 The authors found that this 2D organization resembles the assembly of surface layer proteins. The sheets formed possess some physical integrity, and some pieces as large as 50 30 m remained after gentle stirring and drying.

Slow oxidation of tellurium ions in CdTe resulted in the assembly of 14 m long flat ribbons composed of several layers of individual NCs (see Fig. 14c).115 Twisting of the ribbons with an equal distribution of left and right handed helices was induced by illumination with visible light. Unusual shapes of multiparticle assemblies, such as ellipsoidal clouds, dog-bone agglomerates, and ribbon bunches, were observed at intermediate stages. Computer simulations revealed that the balance between attraction and electrostatic repulsion determines the resulting geometry and dimensionality of the nanoparticle assemblies. Instead of the typical red shift of the optical features found in the case of wires and sheets, a gradual blue shift of the luminescence and absorption peaks was observed during the formation of the ribbons (see Fig. 14c).

Despite the simplicity of the self-assembly approach, it requires quite a long time for the spontaneous reorganization and recrystallization of NCs. Moreover, the yield of the resulting nanostructures is not quantitative and their total amount is limited by the low concentration of the particles in the parent colloids. Therefore, in order to facilitate the assembly of NCs frequently external stimuli are employed, such as e.g. addition of a bad solvent or chelating charged species. Also by simple mixing of oppositely charged particles (e.g. TGA- and cysteamine-capped CdTe) it is possible to obtain colloidal QD-clusters whose constituents are capable of Frster resonance energy transfer (FRET).118 The same effect has been achieved by the addition of Ca2+ ions to negatively charged TGA-capped CdTe NCs.119 In these methods the assembly level can be controlled by the variation of the ratios between positively and negatively charged particles or by the amount of the chelating agent. Although these approaches are more controllable than the destabilization of the surface, without proper templates they produce rather small nano-micro-sized objects.

In this respect, the addition of a nonsolvent to an aqueous CdTe QD colloid leading to flocculation of NCs has been demonstrated as a route to large highly porous 3D structures preserving bright luminescence even after drying (see Fig. 15).117 As is seen from SEM and TEM images, the structure obtained consists of several micrometers long nanowires built up of CdTe@CdTGA complexes formed from as-prepared CdTe QD colloids by the addition of ethanol and sodium acetate, and by applying moderate heating. The in situ formed CdTGA complexes play the role of a mechanical support for the CdTe NCs and also act as spacers between the inner CdTe NCs, limiting their interactions. In this scenario almost all particles are incorporated in the final product.

Another type of 3D structures is NC-based gels where particles in solution form self-supporting highly porous networks either by aging, by chemical (via pH tuning140) or by photochemical treatment,116 as well as by metal ion-induced complexations of the nanocrystals.98,107 These gelation methods are applicable not only to NCs synthesized in water but also to those originally obtained in organic media and subsequently transferred to water by means of ligand exchange by thiols. Thus, oxidative loss of thiolate surface groups has already yielded nanoparticulate gels of ZnS, CdS, CdSe, CdSe/ZnS and PbS investigated in detail by the Brock group.141144 Being processed via supercritical drying, hydrogels transform into monolithic aerogels exhibiting densities hundreds of times less than those of the corresponding bulk materials.145

Recently, an interesting method of precisely controlled and highly reproducible gel-like assembly of NCs via the complexation of tetrazole capped particles by metal ions has been developed.98,107 In this approach, 5-HSCH2Tz-capped CdTe NCs form the entire network by interaction with transition metal ions (such as Cd2+, Zn2+, Ni2+) according to the scheme presented in Fig. 16. The gelation process embraces all the particles present in solution and is rapid, easily upscalable and does not involve any destabilization reactions. The versatility of the method has been demonstrated by the formation of hybrid CdTe/Au structures with controlled content.107 Owing to the equal capping, both types of particles are uniformly distributed within the network as is seen in Fig. 16. Moreover, this assembly method is fully reversible, i.e. by the addition of stronger electron donors than the tetrazole, e.g. ethylenediaminetetraacetate, the hydrogels formed dissolve, releasing the individual nanocrystals which, to a large extent, maintain their original optical properties.

Immobilization of QDs templated assembly. Although the template free self-assembly of aqueous semiconductor NCs described above offers a series of architectures with varied shape and composition, it is limited in terms of the size and robustness of the evolving structures. Therefore QDs are often scaffolded by microscopic or macroscopic substrates. In this scenario they still retain their surface features, which is crucial for catalytic and sensing applications. To attach QDs to a substrate chargecharge interaction and covalent linking strategies are mostly harnessed. In both cases a proper functionalization of the substrate surface is necessary prior to coupling. For instance, in order to covalently link carboxyl-functionalized CdTe NCs to a glass substrate147 or attach them via chargecharge interaction to a porous ZnO surface,148 the substrates have been aminated with 3-(aminopropyl)triethoxysilane. Subsequent covalent binding has been realized via water soluble carbodiimide mediated amidation.147 This reaction allows the formation of amide and ester bonds between carboxylic and amino or hydroxylic groups even at room temperature in a standard buffer medium and has already been successfully used for the conjugation of nanoparticles to biomolecules,149 the coupling QDs,90 and their attachment to micro- and macroscopic surfaces.147,150

Another interesting method for immobilizing CdTe QDs on thin, fluid films consisting of poly(acrylic acid) (PAA) polymer chains tethered by one end to an underlying substrate in a polymer brush configuration has been recently published.151 The protocol involves amide linkage between the amino groups on the surface of CdTe NCs and the carboxylic groups of PAA polymer chains. Exploitation of a thin polymer brush (4.5 nm) was found to suppress QD aggregation and facilitates complete surface coverage. A novel hybrid material based on monodisperse, thermosensitive poly(N-isopropyl acrylamide) (PNIPAM) microgels has been obtained via covalent immobilization of CdTe NCs as is shown in Fig. 17a.152 In this composite the chemical environment around the NCs was modified by a temperature-induced volume-phase transition in the microgel (see Fig. 17b). This variation of the chemical environment induced in its own turn a steep variation in the nanocrystal PL intensity. The PL was quenched when the temperature was under the low critical solution temperature of the polymer (36 C), whereas above this value the PL intensity was restored, as is seen from Fig. 17c.

One more interesting example of hybridization of QDs with an active substrate is the QD/graphene composite which has been recently developed by the group of Dirk Guldi.153 Two strategies for the immobilization of CdTe QDs onto the basal plane of graphene in either exfoliated graphite or nanographene were implemented as is schematically outlined in Fig. 18: a direct (left) and a sequential (right) approach. In the second, the combination of hydrophobichydrophobic/ stacking and electrostatic interactions resulted in hierarchical structures composed of nanographene, positively charged pyrene (pyrene+), and negatively charged TGA/2-mercaptoethanesulfonat-costabilized CdTe QDs. Layer-by-layer deposition. The LbL deposition is a well established economical method for the assembly of various charged species.154 Since the basic aspects of the application of LbL deposition to thiol-capped QDs have been recently reviewed by Gaponik47 and Shavel et al.,137 we will not focus on the basics of this method of assembly in this review. Nevertheless, we will show several novel examples of its application. The LbL assembly of QDs is applicable to a wide variety of substrates from polymer microspheres and carbon nanotubes to large scale planar, porous or curved surfaces. Apart from covering microbeads by luminescing nanoparticles,155158 recently the LbL technique has been extended to the deposition of different types of nanocrystals onto the surface of diatom biosilica, yielding a novel material combining the natural structure characteristics of diatoms with special properties of the nanoparticles.150 Another hybrid material has been developed in the group of Guldi by decoration of single wall carbon nanotubes (SWNTs) with TGA-capped CdTe QDs using poly(diallyldimethylammonium chloride) (PDDA) and poly((vinylbenzyl)trimethylammonium chloride) as polyelectrolytes.159 The authors have detected charge transfer between the SWNTs and the QDs by means of various spectroscopy techniques. This material is a suitable system for solar energy conversion.

LbL provides an opportunity of engineering of composite sandwich macro-structures containing different types of nanoparticles with fine tuning of the interlayer distance and thus controlling opto-electronic properties of the resulting systems. Thus, combining two differently sized CdTe QD layers well separated by several polyelectrolyte bilayers (PDDA/poly(sodium 4-styrene sulfonate) (PSS)), FRET from smaller to larger QDs with rates as high as 1.2 ns1 has been revealed by Lunz et al.138,139 The authors have shown the possibility of controlling the efficiency of the FRET process within the QD layers through variation in the QD concentration and/or careful selection of the inhomogeneous broadening and Stokes shift of the QD ensemble. They also demonstrated that the FRET rate rather than its efficiency more appropriately characterizes the donoracceptor energy transfer due to the input from intra-donor energy transfer.138 The utilization of the FRET phenomenon in LbL architectures allowed for highly sensitive tuning of their colour chromaticity.160 These structures are applicable as colour conversion layers for the fabrication of LEDs based on a blue emitting chip.161

Sandwiching of gold nanoparticles in between CdTe donor and acceptor monolayers resulted in an enhancement of the FRET rate by a factor of 80 and an increase of the Frster radius by 103% compared to a donoracceptor structure without gold NCs, thus providing a way to improve the performance of energy transfer exploiting devices.162 Furthermore, it was shown that the donor properties have a large impact on the probability of surface plasmon mediated FRET. Donors with highest QY and the emission spectra being in resonance with the surface plasmon peak of Au NCs showed a clear signature of surface plasmon mediated energy transfer, reflected in the largest acceptor emission.

Assembling CdTe QDs, as is presented in Fig. 19a, Ozel et al. have demonstrated sufficient enhancement of the QD emission in the presence of gold particles separated by a dielectric polyelectrolyte layer.163 At the same time, without a dielectric spacer the QD emission was quenched. For instance, as is shown in Fig. 19b, the PL increased by a factor of 3.6 in the case of N = 2 (two-time unit cell repetition), whereas its life time decreased. Three PDDA/PSS bilayers offer an optimal dielectric spacing for the enhancement of QD emission. In such a multilayered plasmonic resonator assembly of isotropic CdTe QDs, polarized light emission of 80% in the vertical polarization has been observed. This concept of plasmon coupled QD composites paves a new path for biosensors and highly efficient devices in nanophotonics. This example clearly demonstrates that although LbL based architectures are thin films, in many cases they contain several layers and cannot be treated simply as 2D objects.

Employing the specially designed water soluble conjugated polymer (poly(9,9-bis(3-[(N,N-dimethyl)-N-ethylammonium]-propyl)-2,7-fluorene-alt-1,4-phenylene] dibromide) as a positively charged polyelectrolyte and TGA-capped CdTe QDs Lutich et al. fabricated multilayered composites.164 In this structure the QD layers play a twofold role, acting both as very efficient energy acceptors (energy transfer efficiency 45%) and as insulating layers, effectively suppressing nonradiative recombination in conjugated polymer layers via the reduction of the exciton diffusion. The dual function of NCs in the LbL composites makes these structures very attractive for applications in optoelectronics.

We emphasize that even nonconjugated polyelectrolytes, such as PDDA, may serve as interparticle layers in the fabrication of a LED as was demonstrated recently.165,166 Thus, LbL assembled CdTe sandwiched between indium tin oxide and aluminium electrodes showed an electroluminescence turn on at 2.5 V and the maximum light output at 3.3 V, with a peak radiated power of 141 nW corresponding to an external quantum efficiency of 0.51%. The luminous efficiencies of the red-emitting LED reached 0.4 cd A1 and 0.81 Lm W1.165 To accelerate the LbL process and to avoid the use of a dipping robot, alternating solution spraying has been introduced as an improved technique for forming single layers.166 A typical volume fraction of the NCs in thus obtained CdTe/PDDA films was found to be around 30%.166 An improved stability of a LED for working under harsh operating conditions has been achieved using hydrotalcite as an alternate interparticle layer which acts both to support and to distribute the CdTe nanocrystals, thus comprising a novel all-inorganic based device.167 It exhibited high thermal stability and a maximum efficiency at 0.12 A cm2 operating continuously up to 90 C.

Combining two different kinds of QDs (i.e. CdTe and CdSe) in a multilayered type II aligned structure, Gross and co-workers have recently unambiguously demonstrated the directionality of charge separation by means of surface photovoltage spectroscopy.168 Within this LbL assembly the holes accumulated in the CdTe NC layers and the electrons in the CdSe NC layers. Separated charges were observed to diffuse over several layers of the same kind of NCs. By this, an increase in the thickness of the PSS/PDDA spacer strongly decreased the separation efficiency. The structures developed are promising for the use in photovoltaic applications.

A LbL deposited CdTe QD film on the surface of ZnO nanorods has been recently utilized as a light absorbing layer in the fabrication of excitonic solar cells.169 Solar cells were produced by filling the obtained CdTe-coated nanorods grown on fluorine-doped tin oxide substrate with the wide band gap p-type semiconductor CuSCN or by a hole-collecting layer consisting of poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) (PEDOT) and PSS. Increasing the number of CdTe layers resulted in a controllable enlargement of the absorption of the incident light: 50 layers of QDs coated onto the ZnO nanorods absorbed 80% of the incident light at their absorption peak. In this system annealing of the LbL films at 350 C was essential to reduce the series resistance of the cell. The annealed cells generated an open-circuit voltage of 120 mV and a short-circuit current density of 0.19 mA cm2 under AM 1.5 illumination.

Embedding into a continuous matrix. In order to improve the mechanical properties of the QDs and impart them with additional stability while maintaining their original bright emission, they are often embedded into a robust continuous matrix. In this respect, polymers are appropriate candidates to stabilize and protect the NCs and integrate their functionalities. Two main scenarios for the fabrication of NCpolymer composites are feasible in the case of aqueous nanoparticles. First, they can directly be mixed with water dispersible polymers (like polyvinyl alcohol) in solution or after the above-mentioned ligand exchange or surface modification the QDs become soluble in organic solvents and thus miscible with a larger number of polymer solutions. Second, the QDs may be combined with monomers followed by their polymerization/polycondensation. The latter approach also requires a ligand modification step in order to provide compatibility of the aqueous QDs with the organic monomer molecules.

Thus, a facile preparation of a hybrid GSH-capped CdTe QDenzymepolymer composite film has recently been demonstrated by Yuan et al. by simple interaction of the particles and enzymes with PDDA in water via electrostatic attraction with subsequent precipitation of the composite formed, as shown in Fig. 20a.46 This composite proved to be a stable and versatile material for biosensors and test paper development for catechol and glucose as example analytes. Its sensor response was based on the quenching effects of the enzymatic products on the emission of the QDs (see Fig. 20b). The detection limits of catechol and glucose were 1 105 and 5 106 M, respectively. In the obtained hybrid materials, the emission of the QDs was stable for at least 3 months, and the structure and activity of the enzyme was also well maintained.

Polycondensation of prepolymer in solution with subsequent evaporation of a solvent has been developed for the encapsulation of CdTe QDs into a polyurethane matrix.170,171 Thus, TG-stabilized CdTe NCs dissolved in DMSO formed composites with polyurethane via the reaction between the OH groups of TG and the NCO groups of the prepolymer.170 Water dispersible polyurethane has been exploited for the straightforward fabrication of the composite with cysteine-capped CdTe particles directly in water without any post-preparative treatment.171 In this case nanoparticles act as cross-linking centers, reacting with NCO terminated prepolymer molecules by the amino groups of cysteine, as is shown in Fig. 21. After water evaporation transparent solid compounds can be obtained, which retain and even enhance the QY of the CdTe QDs and impart their photochemical stability.

Since the number of water dispersible polymers is limited, in most of the cases the surface exchange step is necessary to obtain QDpolymer composites. In spite of imparting solubility in organic solvents the common hydrocarbon tail-containing ligands do not ensure uniform distribution into the polymer matrix due to phase separation, leading to the formation of large QD domains within the polymer. Therefore, a straightforward method for the modification of the thiol-ligand shell with polymerizable surfactant has been developed.132 Octadecyl-p-vinylbenzyldimethylammonium chloride (OVDAC) allows for the efficient transfer of CdTe NCs directly to styrene or styrene/methyl methacrylate with subsequent radical polymerization of the obtained QDsmonomer mixture induced by azobisizobutyronitrile (AIBN), yielding transparent highly emitting bulk NCpolymer composites containing up to 10 wt% of the nanoparticles (see Fig. 22).132 Alternatively, OVDACstyrene copolymers prepared in a first step can serve as a phase transfer mediator for aqueous CdTe NCs, avoiding an interaction of the particles with free radicals.172 Analogously, exploiting the copolymer of OVDAC with 9-vinylcarbazole showing blue fluorescence, in combination with green and red emitting CdTe QDs Sun et al. have demonstrated the fabrication of white LEDs.173

Encapsulation of individual QDs and small ensembles. Despite the main demand for large scale macroscopic assemblies of integrated QDs there are some fields such as biomedical applications requiring small nanomicro entities containing QDs which are capable of the transport in live cells and organisms being at the same time stable enough in the bio-environment. This task has been tackled by the incorporation of individual nanoparticles or their small ensembles into various micelles and beads based on specially designed polymers or silica. In addition to the example presented in Fig. 13, another innovative approach of the encapsulation of aqueous CdTe NCs into an amphiphilic polymer has recently been developed.174 The authors employed a two step phase transfer of the QDs from water to chloroform by ligand modification with dimethyldioctadecyl-ammonium-bromide (DDAB) and subsequently from chloroform back to water by encapsulation of DDAB-coated particles into amphiphilic poly(ethylene glycol) diglycidyl-grafted poly(maleic anhydride-alt-octadecene), as is illustrated in Fig. 23a.174 The obtained QDpolymer composites exhibited a spherical shape, with an average diameter of about 80 nm according to TEM investigations (see Fig. 23b) and a hydrodynamic diameter of 95 nm measured by dynamic light scattering. The wrapping of the CdTe NCs into composite microspheres greatly enhanced their stability under acidic conditions.

The application of the above shown polymerizable surfactants as both phase transfer agent (didecyl-p-vinylbenzylmethylammonium chloride) and emulsifier (OVDAC) resulted in a preparation of composite CdTe NCpolystyrene microbeads with an average size of 1.5 m upon mini-emulsion polymerization.175 The use of polymerizable surfactants as the emulsifier plays the most important role in the effective encapsulation of the aqueous colloidal particles into polymeric beads. At the same time, polymerizable surfactants as the phase transfer agents guarantee a homogenous distribution of the QDs within the polystyrene matrix.

Another material for encapsulation of various nanoparticles is silica.176 The fabrication of silica beads incorporating CdTe NCs analogous to those of polystyrene mentioned above has been developed recently by the group of Gao via the water-in-oil reverse microemulsion method using cyclohexane, Triton X-100, n-hexanol and tetraethyl orthosilicate (TEOS), as a medium, a surfactant, a co-surfactant and a silica source, respectively.177179 The method results in the formation of 45109 nm SiO2 spheres containing in the core one or several CdTe particles, depending on the process conditions (see Fig. 24). An additional incubation of the as-prepared CdTe colloid in ammonia solution enhances the QY of the QDs and moreover retains an excellent emission with an efficiency of up to 47% for the resulting CdTe@SiO2 coreshell particles.177 A silica shell impedes the diffusion of oxygen from the surrounding environment, preventing the CdTe QDs from photo-oxidation. Moreover, the shell blocks diffusion of Cd2+ ions, reducing the cytotoxicity of such coreshell particles and making them a promising material for bio-labeling. Further surface modification of the silica coated QDs with amino groups provides a route for covalent linking of biomolecules.179

CdTe NCs coated with thin hybrid silica shells containing CdS clusters have been shown by Murase's group to assemble into intensely luminescing fibers (QYs of up to 55% were reported).180,181 According to the method developed (see Fig. 25), reflux of a solution of CdTe NCs, cadmium salt, TGA, TEOS and NH3 yielded tubal, solid, or belt-like 1D structures, depending on the concentration of the precursors. Amphiphilic CdTe particles

In this short paragraph we focus on amphiphilic particles as a separate category of colloidal nanomaterials which have been emerging during the last decade owing to their versatile synthesis bridging the hot-injection and the aqueous approaches. For example, the application of thiolated poly(ethylene glycol) (mPEG-SH) as a capping ligand enables the direct synthesis of CdTe NCs not only in water but also in a variety of organic solvents (e.g. toluene, dimethylacetamide, dimethylformamide), as recently reported by Dubavik et al.82,83 Therefore, this technique steps towards an organic method, which, on the other hand, approaches the aqueous route by using a similar technique for the injection of the gaseous PH3 precursor in the synthesis of InP183 and Cd6P7 QDs.184 The solubility of the mPEG-SH-stabilizer facilitates the spontaneous direct and complete transfer of CdTe NCs across phase boundaries from toluene through water to chloroform without stirring or agitation or any other means and medium adjustments as is shown in Fig. 26a. The versatility of this approach has been proven also in a Au NC synthesis.185 Moreover, these particles have revealed an ability to penetrate through the lipid bilayer of model membrane systems as well as live cells, demonstrating nonendocytic uptake.83 As is seen from Fig. 26b, amphiphilic mPEG-SH-capped CdTe QDs were found to be distributed over the whole cell interiors with partial confinement in the endosomes whereas reference hydrophilic TGA-capped NCs were solely accumulated in the endosomes due to their aggregation in the presence of chelating metal ions (see Fig. 26c). This finding makes CdTe@mPEG-SH QDs promising agents for tracking whole cells and intracellular processes. Moreover, their high colloidal stability in various biological media and stable emission properties can be exploited for a better delivery of agents for therapeutic applications.

Coreshell CdTe based NCs

Microwave irradiation has been revealed as an advantageous technique for coreshell particle synthesis. Thus, using this method the first synthesis of coreshellshell MPA-capped CdTeCdSZnS NCs directly in water was recently introduced.52 This protocol comprises three steps: CdTe core preparation, coating the core by a CdS shell followed by a second shell (ZnS) formation (see Fig. 27). The intermediate CdS shell is formed in order to reduce lattice strain induced by the large lattice mismatch between the CdTe core and the outer ZnS shell. The NCs undergo purification after each step by reprecipitation which is necessary for the separation of unreacted species. The resulting QDs were reported to be very stable in biological media and non-cytotoxic, which makes them very promising candidates for biolabelling.
Fig. 27 Schematic illustration of the microwave-assisted synthesis of aqueous CdTeCdSZnS coreshellshell QDs. Reproduced from ref. 52 Copyright 2008 WILEY VCH Verlag GmbH & Co.

Another material for bioimaging applications, namely type II CdTeCdSe coreshell QDs with NIR fluorescence was prepared by applying a successive ion-layer adsorption and reaction (SILAR) technique.50 According to this recipe, L-cysteine-stabilized CdTe cores were covered with 1 to 6 layers of CdSe by the sequential injection of Cd2+/cysteine and KHSe solutions which, upon reflux, resulted in the preparation of NCs emitting in the red to NIR region of 600850 nm with the highest QY = 12%. The SILAR technique can also ensure an efficient control of the shell thickness, allowing the tuning of the behaviour of coreshell CdTeCdS QDs from type I to type II structures by gradual deposition of several shell layers.60 This transition was indicated by the change of the optical properties of the coreshell QDs including a sufficient enhancement of their PL life time compared with that of the initial core particles.60

In addition to microwave irradiation, an ultrasonic treatment has been applied for a CdS shell formation on CdTe NCs.72 In this case acoustic cavitation facilitates the decomposition of thiourea, which releases S2 to react with Cd2+ present in solution with subsequent creation of a gradated shell.

We have to admit, unfortunately, that the formation of coreshell structures is often not unambiguously proven. In our opinion some reports, especially those claiming a one-pot aqueous synthesis of coreshell NCs when the precursors for both the core and shell are added and are supposed to react simultaneously, give rise to logical criticism since in this case the formation of an alloyed structure rather than a classical coreshell is much more probable. One such example is the one-pot synthesis of CdTeZnTe NCs,61 a system which is particularly doubtful, taking into account the inherent instability of ZnTe36,101 in aqueous media.

Alloyed CdTe based NCs

All the above-mentioned approaches ensure the synthesis of NCs emitting mainly in the visible region. In order to extend the emission of CdTe to longer wavelengths one uses alloying of the NCs with materials having narrower band gaps which have quite similar lattice parameters as CdTe. Thus approaches for alloying via partial replacement of both the cation and the anion have been developed.
CdHgTe. An appropriate candidate for alloying with CdTe is HgTe which has a very similar lattice constant (cf. 6.477 and 6.462 for bulk zinc blende CdTe and HgTe, respectively99). The aqueous synthesis of NIR emitting alloyed CdHgTe and coreshell CdHgTeCdS along with pure HgTe NCs has been first reported by Harrison, Rogach and others.27,186,187 This approach based on the incorporation of Hg2+ ions into already prepared CdTe NCs, stabilized by 2-mercaptoethanol or TG, involves several steps: the synthesis of the initial NCs followed by the subsequent formation of a HgTe shell. In order to shift the PL to wavelengths longer than 1000 nm, this layer-by-layer procedure has to include the creation of 23 HgTe layers separated by CdTe layers upon addition of different amounts of Hg2+ ions to the CdTe colloidal solutions corresponding to 20%, 40%, 80% and 120% of the cadmium content of the aliquots, as is schematically shown in Fig. 28.27
Fig. 28 Scheme of the synthetic route for obtaining CdxHg1xTe NCs. Reproduced from ref. 186 Copyright 2000, with permission from Elsevier.

The thus-prepared alloyed QDs have been applied for the fabrication of field-effect thin-film transistors via their spin-coating onto oxidized Si substrates with subsequent sintering at 150 C, followed by the deposition of a top-gate dielectric alumina layer.188 This device exhibited a field-effect mobility of 0.21 cm2 V1 s1 and an Ion/Ioff ratio of 1.5 102.

A similar approach was applied for the preparation of CdHgTe nanorods using CdTe nanorods as a template.69 The authors have assumed that the redistribution of mercury and cadmium ions in the growing QDs most probably leads to the formation of CdxHg1xTe alloys rather than to discretely layered NCs. The shell formation is more obvious in the hybrid approach when CdTe or CdHgTe particles were transferred from water to organics with subsequent coating by ZnS shell employing an organometallic route yielding small-diameter, highly NIR-luminescent NCs.65 Water absorbs strongly in the NIR and IR spectral region starting from approx. 1150 nm (its transmission measured in a 1 cm cuvette at this wavelength is ca. 35%, see Fig. 29a), sufficient to obscure the luminescence of the samples.29 Therefore, solvents with lower absorption have to be used for the acquisition of PL spectra (e.g. D2O or some organics) or measurements have to be performed from dried NCs deposited on a transparent wafer.

The Hg incorporation is limited in terms of the achievable spectral position of the PL maximum, i.e. the tuneability of the PL band. The ultimate value reachable with this synthesis is 1100 nm for the PL maximum and 40% for the QY of the corresponding NCs. The newly developed facile one-pot synthesis provides a wide range of alloyed TGA stabilized CdxHg1xTe NC batches possessing a strong PL within the spectral range of 640 to 1600 nm (see Fig. 29bd).29 The recipe is very similar to the synthesis of pure CdTe and includes a partial replacement of the Cd salt by the Hg precursor. In contrast to the incorporation technique, the nucleation and the growth of alloyed NCs using the addition of only a small amount of the mercury precursor leads to a significant shrinkage of the band gap and correspondingly to a shift of the PL to longer wavelengths. The authors have suggested that the synthesis of the CdxHg1xTe particles follows approximately the same NC growth kinetics as the synthesis of undoped TGA-capped CdTe. Thus, the red shifts of the first absorption maxima and the PL maxima during the synthesis of the alloyed NCs occur only due to the incorporation of the narrower band gap material as was confirmed by an elemental analysis (see Fig. 29e). At the beginning of the growth the Cd content in the NCs is less than in the initial feed ratio (Cd/Hg = 95/5) and continuously decreases due to the approximately 20 times lower solubility of HgTe compared to CdTe in water. A similar relationship was observed by Sun et al., having obtained a composition of Cd0.23Hg0.77Te from the initial feed ratio of Cd/Hg = 1/1.28

At this point we would like the reader to note the necessity of reconsidering QY determinations of these NIR-emitting samples using IR-26 as a standard reference dye. Its quantum efficiency has been re-examined recently and revealed as 0.05% (at least in 1,2-dichloroethane), which is an order of magnitude lower than the previously reported value.189 Therefore, some of the previously reported QYs of NIR emitting QDs may have been overestimated by a factor of 10.

CdSeTe. Alloying via replacement of Te ions in the CdTe crystal lattice by another anion constitutes an additional possibility for band gap engineering. Analogous to the CdHgTe NC preparation, the incorporation technique has been applied for a synthesis of alloyed L-cysteine capped CdSeTe and CdSeTe/ZnS particles using CdTe as the initial material.62,88 Although CdSe has a wider band gap (1.74 eV for the wurtzite structure99) than CdTe, the alloyed QDs demonstrate a nonlinear dependence of the band gap on their composition, denoted as optical bowing effect. As a result, the optical band gap of alloyed particles decreases in comparison to CdTe QDs of the same size, which enables a shift of the PL up to 814 nm.88 An additional ZnS shell coating ensures a high QY = 70% of the resulting coreshell particles.62 Piven et al. developed a one-pot synthesis of alloyed TGA-capped CdSeTe and CdSTe NCs, employing a simultaneous injection of NaHSe (Na2S) and NaHTe into the Cd precursor solution.26 In this case the emission efficiency (24%) was approximately one order of magnitude lower than that for thiol-capped CdTe NCs, which is determined by the different energetic location of the thiol-related trap surface states for CdTe and CdSe serving as nonradiative channels for CdSe NCs (see below). The measured values of the Stokes shifts for CdSexTe1x particles with different compositions are comparable or even smaller than those for the reference CdTe NCs, indicating that the PL of the alloys is mainly determined by exciton recombination rather than by surface trap emission. The above-mentioned results suggest that L-cysteine is a beneficial stabilizer for alloyed CdSeTe QDs.

CdSe, CdSeCdS NCs

Another member of the cadmium chalcogenide group is CdSe. Whereas the organometallic synthesis of this material in the nanoparticulate form is very well developed, there are only a few examples of CdSe NCs obtained by the aqueous approach. The main reason is that CdSe capped with various thiols (viz. 2-mercaptoethanol, TG, TGA) usually exhibits only a weak trap related emission.15 As was explained by Wuister et al., hole trapping on a thiol molecule is responsible for the quenching of the excitonic (band-to-band) emission.190 This process is favoured by the position of the thiol redox energy level within the CdSe band gap, i.e., it is situated at a higher energy than the top of the valence band of CdSe, opposite to CdTe which has a valence band above the redox energy of most thiols. Nevertheless, a successful aqueous synthesis of highly luminescent CdSe NCs has been reported recently.16,17 Stabilization by GSH allows us to obtain small QDs with an average size of 2 nm and a QY of 16%, which has been further enhanced up to 25% by streptavidin coating, suggesting that GSH has a more appropriate energy alignment in contrast to other thiols.17 A QY of 40% was reported in the synthesis of CdSe NCs, employing MPA as a stabilizer and a hydrazine hydrateSe complex as a Se source, providing size control of the particles in the range of 1.63.4 nm and emission covering a wide range from blue to red.16

Analogous to the cases of ZnSe and CdTe described above a postpreparative photochemical treatment can be applied to citrate capped CdSe NCs to enhance their PL. Ambient light treatment of CdSeCdS coreshell NCs synthesized under microwave irradiation either in solution or in solid multilayer films (prepared by LbL deposition) for several days resulted in a drastic QY enhancement from 0.52% up to 2545%.59 The technique reported has yielded high quality photoactivated micropatterns from LbL NC/PDDA films using TEM copper grids as lithographic masks. The emission increase is accompanied by a blue shift of the PL and the absorption due to photooxidation of the particle surface which was observed for CdSe as well as for CdSeCdS NCs, assuming that the CdS shell coverage is not complete and allows access to the CdSe core.58 Interestingly, even a thick silica coating on these coreshell particles does not protect them from photooxidation; instead it facilitates an activation process owing to some amount of oxygen trapped within the pores of the silica shell. Laser illumination along with a common heat treatment in the synthesis of citrate capped CdSe and CdSe/CdS has resulted in stable particles in the size range of 3.76.3 nm, emitting from 560 to 615 nm with a reported ultimate QY of 60%.55 Citrate being a quite weak ligand relative to thiols permits fast growth of the CdSe NCs even at room temperature so that particles of 2, 2.4, 3 and 3.2 nm were prepared over 20 min, employing a common heat treatment at 20, 50, 75 and 94 C, respectively.56

HgX and PbX NCs

To the best of our knowledge, HgTe and PbS are the only narrow band gap semiconductor materials successfully prepared in their nanoparticulate forms by the aqueous approach thus far. Bulk mercury chalcogenides are frequently ascribed to the class of semimetals since they exhibit zero band gaps.191 The high reactivity of the metal ions towards their chalcogen counterparts is the main reason for their poor colloidal stability which strongly affects their application. Typically, an aqueous synthesis of both HgTe and PbS NCs is carried out at room temperature by simply mixing the respective metal salts with the corresponding chalcogen sources (H2Te(Se) or NaHTe(Se), Na2S) in the presence of thiol stabilizers. Particles obtained in this way proceed to grow and coalesce under storage even at quite low temperatures and after purification.

NIR and IR emitting aqueous semiconductor NCs carry a large potential for applications as optical amplifiers for telecommunication systems based on the silica fibre technology. Conventional silica fibres have optimal low-loss transmission windows (so-called telecommunication windows) at around 1.3 and 1.55 m.187 Other possible applications include biolabelling,156,192 especially for in vivo imaging studies,63 electronics193,194 and optoelectronics,195 photovoltaics193,196 and nanophotonics.197

HgX NCs

The aqueous synthesis is the only successful colloidal approach for HgTe nanoparticle preparation since the organometallic method suffers from either high toxicity of the mercury precursors (e.g., dimethylmercury) or severe difficulties in controlling the growth, especially at higher temperatures (>100 C).65 As mentioned in the previous paragraph, the first reports of a synthesis of HgTe NCs were published by Rogach, Harrison et al., having employed Hg(ClO4)2 and H2Te as the initial precursors in the presence of TG acting as a stabilizer.20,64,198 The particles obtained had a broad size distribution which could be narrowed by size-selective precipitation, their PL covered the spectral region from approx. 900 nm to 2000 nm.27 HgTe NCs with a mean particle size of 3.5 nm show an exceptionally high QY of around 50%. HgS and HgSe colloids prepared applying the same synthetic procedure exhibited even lower thermostability than HgTe with the PL centred at 1 m and a QY of 0.5%.199,200 In order to enhance the stability of these materials protection and passivation of the mercury chalcogenide core with a CdS layer were suggested.64,199

Another strategy for enhancing the stability as well as for further processing of the NCs is their encapsulation into an appropriate inert matrix, e.g., polymers. Thus, a LbL assembly of HgTe QDs with polyelectrolytes yielded a simple thin-film hybrid polymer/NC LED.187 A composite PDDA/HgTe NC film deposited on ITO-coated glass employed as a substrate/bottom electrode with an evaporated aluminum top electrode above the LbL film emits IR light when pumped electrically, albeit with rather low quantum efficiency (0.001%). To a large extent this is attributed to the poor carrier-transport properties of the insulating PDDA. Conjugated polymer matrices hosting semiconductor NCs may improve the characteristics of LEDs fabricated thereof. Koktysh et al. proposed the device architecture shown in Fig. 30ac, employing the methyl-substituted ladder-type poly(paraphenylene) (MeLPPP) as a host matrix and HgTe NCs transferred from water into toluene which formed a homogeneous coating with a thickness of 100150 nm on ITO glass.195 The inset of Fig. 30d shows the NIR electroluminescence (EL) spectra of a MeLPPP/HgTe NC device emitting at 1.3 m at a turn-on voltage of 10 V. The EL spectra closely match the PL spectra and exhibit no spectral shift in the presence of an applied field, revealing that in both cases the same emissive species are responsible for the NIR luminescence.

PbX NCs

As in the case of HgTe, thiols were successfully used for the stabilization of PbS NCs.2224 Best results in terms of stability of the particles and their optical properties were achieved by employing a mixture of two ligands: TG and dithioglycerol (DTG).22,23 The variation of the Pb2+/S2/TG/TGA molar ratio provides an opportunity for controlling the NC properties, e.g., decreasing the Pb2+/S2 initial ratio allows for tuning the PL maxima from approx. 1050 to 1350 nm (Fig. 33). The optimized procedure yields PbS colloids stable against aggregation for 23 months, having an average size of 4 1 nm and a QY of 710%.22 At the same time, being kept at 37 C in the dark they continually grow, which was indicated by a red shift of their PL. Neither TG nor DTG being used as a sole ligand leads to a sufficient stability of the NCs. The same tendency was observed in the synthesis of a PbTe NC colloid, employing TG as the stabilizer.199 However, dihydrolipoic acid as a bidentate ligand provides quite stable PbS colloids.24

Conclusions and outlook

As is clearly seen from the work reviewed, tremendous progress has been made during the last decade in the colloidal aqueous synthesis of semiconductor NCs including both its practical preparative aspects as well as theoretical insight into the processes and mechanisms responsible for the formation of nanoparticles. As a result of intensive investigations many straightforward synthetic techniques providing high quality materials have been developed. The prepared QDs feature excellent emission properties and owing to their size control they cover a wide spectral region from the UV to the IR. The recently depicted syntheses of coreshell and coreshellshell structures constitute large steps forward, delivering colloidally and chemically stable materials, especially promising for applications requiring continuous contact with oxidative environments, e.g. in vivo bio-imaging. Efficient strategies of the encapsulation of semiconductor particles into polymeric micelles or silica beads also provide robust nanoscaled materials, preserving and even enhancing the optical properties of the parental NCs. Obtaining amphiphilic NCs and their composites displaying versatile solubility and, thus, being capable of penetrating through cell membranes is an additional advantage of the aqueous synthesis. A vast variability of the functionalization of aqueous QDs achievable both directly in the synthesis during their growth and by means of post-preparative handling is a key factor for their processing and subsequent application in various fields ranging from life sciences to photonics. NCs being functionalized directly in the synthesis by the application of various thiol stabilizers are off-the-shelf building blocks for the fabrication of different hybrid structures assembled via self-organization, electrostatic interactions, covalent linking, etc. The assembly of nanoparticles into superstructures of greater complexity is a necessary step in the utilization of nanomaterials, which enlarges the spectrum of their properties. It is believed that the complexity of nanoparticulate architectures will grow not only with respect to spatial dimensions but also along the temporal coordinate, finally leading to dynamic nanometer-scale systems.

In spite of various successful synthetic approaches to several chalcogenide based IIVI and IVVI NCs, most of the assemblies and functional architectures reported in the literature have been obtained by using CdTe NCs. Indeed, CdTe NCs are the most successful and prominent members of thiol-capped family. Nevertheless, it is very important to stress that the developed processing methods are versatile and are also applicable to other types of thiol-capped NCs. Moreover, most of the NCs synthesized by hot-injection methods (amongst others CdSe NCs and nanorods, their coreshell combinations, CdTe, lead chalcogenides) may undergo surface exchange of the capping agent to short chain thiols, e.g. MPA.201 Such types of secondary thiol-capped NCs are well compatible with most of the processing methods described above. Taking into account the variety of materials available from both aqueous and hot-injection syntheses, each offering unique physico-chemical properties, the above-described methods as well as new assembly approaches have literally unlimited applicability.

Successful applications of aqueous QDs and their assemblies in optoelectronics include light-emitting diodes, microarrays of multicolored light-emitting pixels, energy harvesting nanostructures, and photosensitive films. This is closely connected to photonic applications in which these assemblies play the role of subwavelength emitters, tunable light-sources coupled to optical modes of photonic crystals and heterocrystals, spherical microresonators, photonic molecules, and waveguides. Biology related applications include temperature sensitive nanoassemblies, conjugates with smart polymers, luminescence coding of multifunctional microbeads and polymer microcapsules suitable for drug delivery systems and biosensors. The ability of the NCs to interact with adjacent nanoparticles or molecules gives rise to the assembly of FRET-based and nanoplasmonic devices as well as various sensors.
