Abstract

Non-precious-metal single atom (NPM-SA) confined nanomaterials with maximum metal atom utilization have recently attracted particular interest, providing opportunities for exploiting novel heterogeneous electrocatalysts with low cost and high efficiency. The applications of non-precious-metal single-atom catalysts (NPM-SACs) have been extensively developed in electrochemical energy technologies such as fuel cells, metalâ€“air batteries, and hydrogen production via water splitting. NPM-SACs can be confined on substrates with various structures and dimensions from 1D to 3D. However, the role of the structural dimensions of substrates has not been critically summarized. In this review, recent advances in NPM-SACs such as facile synthesis, characterization, performance in catalyzing oxygen redox reactions, and related applications are highlighted through a focus on different dimensional substrates (1D, 2D, and 3D). The general fabrication strategies and the catalytic mechanism of NPM-SACs in the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) are extensively discussed. Moreover, the opportunities and challenges in this emerging field are presented on the basis of its current development.

1. Introduction

As widely employed in electrochemical applications like fuel cells, metalâ€“air batteries, and electrolysis cells, oxygen redox reactions, including the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER), with fast kinetics are highly desired to reduce irreversible potential loss and thus improve the energy efficiency of electrochemical processes. Unfortunately, either the ORR or the OER suffers from a sluggish reaction rate due to multiple charge transfer processes and a high activation barrier.1 Electrocatalysts based on precious metals, such as Pt, Ir, Ru, Rh, and Au, show excellent catalytic performance for the ORR and OER. However, their shortcomings are also fatal, including high cost, limited durability, natural scarcity, and poor resistance to poisoning, which restrict large-scale commercial deployment.2,3 In order to migrate the drawbacks of precious metal catalysts, scaling down the strategy of developing nanoscale catalysts has been proven practical. Nanoscale materials of pure precious metals or alloys with diverse morphologies have been developed, and continuous efforts have been made in tuning the nanostructure and further decreasing the critical dimensions of catalysts. The breakthrough progress has been archived until the emergence of single-atom catalysts (SACs).4,5

SACs, defined as materials that only contain single atoms dispersed on supports without any appreciable interaction between them,6 have become a new research frontier for electrochemical reactions. SACs represent the ultimate form of the scaling down strategy, with the theoretically minimum metal size and maximum utilization efficiency. Since the presentation of the concept in 2011, SACs have been drawing intensive research attention.7,8 Recent studies have shown the viability of using SACs for catalyzing diverse elementary electrochemical reactions, such as the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), oxygen evolution reaction (OER), hydrogen evolution reaction (HER),9,10 CO2 reduction reaction (CO2RR),11â€“14etc.15,16 It is found that precious-metal-based SACs exhibited stunning catalytic activities for electrochemical energy technologies.17â€“19 For instance, Liu et al. reported a carbon black-supported Pt single-atom electrocatalyst for the ORR with a power density up to 680 mW cmâˆ’2 at 80 Â°C in acidic single-cells.20 Considering the shortcomings of precious-metal catalysts, a further step has been taken to eliminate the use of precious metal catalysts by developing non-precious metal single-atom catalysts (NPM-SACs), especially in the last five years. In 2019, Zhang et al. reported graphene supported single-atom Feâ€“N5 sites in catalyzing the CO2 reduction process with superior faradaic efficiency (ca. 99.7%) for CO production at a low overpotential of 0.35 V, outperforming reported traditional Feâ€“Nâ€“C catalysts.21 Non-precious metals, particularly transition metals such as Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, and Zn, combined with nonmetal dopants (N, P, S, B, and O), were found to show desirable catalytic activities for oxygen redox reactions and were regarded as a promising candidate to replace precious-metal catalysts. Compared with traditional non-precious-metal catalysts comprised of metal particles or clusters, NPM-SACs demonstrate reduced possibility to be oxidized and superior stability especially in acidic media as a consequence of the strong bonding between metal single atoms and supports. The utilization of metal precursors is relatively low. Thus, the fabrication of NPM-SACs would be more cost effective. Moreover, it is easier to probe the coordination of single atoms on various supports rather than the complicated structure of conventional metal-involved materials, thus allowing for further prediction of reaction dynamics in the catalytic process. To date, NPM-SACs have already been dramatically developed and presented a competitive or even better electrocatalytic performance compared with bulk precious-metal catalysts and traditional precious-metal free catalysts.9,10,22

The confinement of NPM-SAs relies on strong covalent bonds via either doping or anchoring on various supports, and NPM-SAs generally remain coordinatively unsaturated to provide active sites in an electrochemical reaction process. Thus, there is no doubt that the type of support is vital in the design of highly active NPM-SACs. The promising supports requires comparably large surface area, excellent conductivity, strong metalâ€“support coordination, and comparably high stability to ensure the confinement of NPM-SAs and superior electrochemical catalytic performance.23 The most reported carbon nanomaterials, especially graphene-like materials, well satisfy the above requirements, attracting extensive interest in the synthesis of NPM-SACs.24 Besides carbon-based supports, multiple 1Dâ€“3D substrates (Fig. 1, metals, metal oxides, non-metal oxides, etc.) have been widely explored for oxygen redox reactions in fundamental research and experimental application as well.15,25 In this review, we summarize various strategies employed in the facile preparation of recently developed NPM-SACs confined on different dimensional supports. Then we introduce the characterization methods used to identify active sites in NPM-SACs, including physical and chemical techniques. Advanced computational modelling and relevant simulation methods are used to find out the reaction mechanism of oxygen redox reactions catalyzed by NPM-SACs. The electrocatalytic activities of currently developed NPM-SACs for the ORR and OER, and bifunctional performance have been systematically evaluated. For the first time, the effect of the support morphology on catalytic performance has been extensively discussed and compared. Finally, the major challenges and the future prospects of NPM-SACs are presented based on our understanding of the recent development.

2. Synthesis

A variety of experimental techniques have been presented for the preparation of NPM-SACs (1D, 2D, and 3D), such as ball milling, electrochemical deposition, physical and chemical vapor deposition, wet-chemistry pyrolysis, and thermal and microwave pyrolysis. In spite of the diverse approaches, the facile fabrication methods have been extensively discussed. It is noticeable that high-temperature pyrolysis plays a dominant role in the synthesis process of NPM-SACs. In the following, detailed preparation methods are summarized and divided according to different dimensional supports of NPM-SACs.

Generally, the stable anchoring of NPM-SAs on 1D supports is difficult to achieve due to the intrinsic closed tubular structure of 1D nanomaterials. There is limited literature reporting the successful fabrication of NPM-SACs confined on 1D supports with excellent electrochemical performance. Confined NPM-SAs can be directly exposed to the surface of 1D supports or can be embedded inside 1D structures with the capability of modulating the electronic properties of the coordination environment into intrinsically inactive 1D materials to trigger catalytic activity. 2D nanomaterial supported NPM-SACs can more easily be accurately probed by advanced characterization techniques. The open structure on both sides on 2D substrates enables a more expedited mass-transfer process than those on 1D or 3D supports and significantly maximizes the catalytic reaction rates. 3D structures are comparably complex compared with low-dimensional supports, and thus have been intensively investigated as substrates of NPM-SACs. The complicated structure of 3D provides more anchoring sites for metal single atoms and the possibility of dual-metal or multi-metal confinement. However, it is difficult to further predict the local atomic structure as well as the local electronic states of single metal atoms on 3D supports.

2.1 NPM-SAs confined on 1D nanomaterials

One-dimensional nanomaterials, such as nanorods (NRs), nanotubes (NTs), nanowires (NWs), and nanofibers (NFs), have attracted considerable attention in electrocatalysis due to their intrinsic advantages compared to 0D nanoparticles.26 Among them, hollow carbon nanotubes (CNTs) show a much higher specific surface area as well as better mass transport properties than the other two structures, which were used as promising supports for various electrochemical applications.27â€“31 Moreover, carbon nanotubes also take the advantages of common carbon materials, like rich sources, low cost, and strong tolerance to acidic and alkaline environments.5,32â€“34 Heteroatom doped CNTs, especially N-doped CNTs, have been widely investigated by computational and experimental studies and been proven to enhance the electrocatalytic activities of pristine CNTs through changing the electronic state by replacing C atoms with N atoms.35â€“41 On the basis of N-doped CNTs, Liu and co-workers designed transition-metal-based catalysts (M/Nâ€“CNTs, M = Fe, Co, and Ni) via a solid-state pyrolysis process, providing a general fabrication strategy of 1D CNT supported electrocatalysts.42

The combination of NPM-SAs and CNTs has been conducted very recently. Generally, NPM-SACs supported on CNTs could be synthesized through two commonly used technical routes: (1) thermal decomposition of a pre-treated carbon nanotube matrix with metal-containing precursors; (2) self-assembly of carbon nanotubes through template-free pyrolysis with the addition of metal precursors. For the first route, Guo et al. demonstrated a 1D coreâ€“shell cable structure of Coâ€“P, Nâ€“CNT where the Co single atoms were embedded in a carbon sheath (shell structure in Fig. 2a).2 In their study, commercial multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) were firstly treated with acid and then coated with resorcinol, melamine, and formaldehyde on the surface. Following that, the obtained RMF@CNT mixed with cobalt nitrate hexahydrate underwent high-temperature pyrolysis under N2. In this route, the unique coating played a key role in preventing the structural changes and connecting the CNTs with transition metal precursors. This kind of â€˜coreâ€“shellâ€™ morphology with CNTs as the inner cores could also be observed in other studies, such as CNT@Feâ€“Nâ€“PC (polypyrrole as the coating)43 and M@N-doped MWCNTs (M = Co, Fe, or Cu, with PDA as the coating).44

2.2 NPM-SAs confined on 2D nanomaterials

2D materials confining NPM-SACs show particularly unique features compared with other dimensional structures.51,52 For instance, metal single atoms supported on 2D materials tend to be more coordinatively unsaturated and can more easily be exposed to reactants, which ensures maximum utilization of metal single atoms and maximizes the electrocatalytic reaction rates as well.
2.2.1 NPM-SAs confined on graphene-family nanomaterials. As the most widely used 2D material which approaches the physical thickness limit, graphene exhibits remarkable properties, particularly the highest surface area and conductivity among inorganic materials.53 A large number of graphene-like 2D materials have been increasingly developed, such as graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4), transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), and 2D metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs). Besides traditional graphene, other graphene-family nanomaterials, such as multi-layered graphene, graphene oxide (GO) and reduced graphene oxide (rGO), and materials fabricated from the above precursors, are highly favourable supports for oxygen redox reactions because they contain abundant edges, defects, or dopants which contribute to a higher density of active sites.54,55 Similar to CNTs, heteroatom doping on graphene-based materials brings about improved electrocatalytic activities. Moreover, the co-doping of a metal center and typical non-metal heteroatoms (N, S, O, P, etc.) enhances the electroactivity and electrocatalytic durability of the origin graphene due to the metal and metal-free dopants.56 N-doped graphene, especially, has shown potential for stabilizing metal atomic species as well as for catalysing electrochemical reactions.57

Universally, graphene-family nanomaterial confining NPM-SAs could be classified into single-atom confined and atomic metalâ€“Nx site confined catalysts. Single atoms could interact with the oxygen groups or C atoms in defects. For example, Ni2+ cations were directly dispersed in defective graphene (DG) and reduced in a nitrogen atmosphere as shown in Fig. 3a.58 The Ni nanoparticles were removed via hydrochloric acid latching treatment and only Ni single atoms were finally anchored into the DG with a high loading up to 1.24 wt%. For atomic metalâ€“Nx site confined graphene-family catalysts, there are various promising carbon precursors which are able to serve as supports. Graphene oxide (GO), which could be reduced to rGO through a thermal annealing process, could provide a tremendous number of vacancies for incorporating N and for anchoring metal single atoms. The intrinsic interfering species also prevent the formation of amorphous carbon and the configuration of the transition metal centers.44,59 Fei et al. employed a general route to prepare atomic metal embedded in nitrogen-doped holey graphene frameworks (Mâ€“NHGF) via facile hydrothermal and pyrolysis processes in the presence of GO, inorganic metal salts (e.g.: FeCl3, CoCl2, NiCl2, etc.), and H2O2 serving as precursors.60

2.3 NPM-SAs confined on 3D nanomaterials

Compared with 1D or 2D substrates, 3D configurations were developed in more recent years. Among all kinds of high-dimensional nanomaterials, 3D porous carbonaceous nanomaterials have attracted extensive attention in recent research. The intrinsic electrocatalytic activities of 3D materials such as a high surface area, extrinsic physicochemical activities, and good mechanical and chemical stability are crucial for serving as feasible supports in single-atom catalysts.76â€“78 Herein, we will demonstrate the synthesis strategies of 3D MOFs, carbon spherical nanomaterials, and other 3D nanomaterials confining non-precious-metal single atomic sites.
2.3.1 NPM-SACs derived from 3D metalâ€“organic-frameworks. Metalâ€“organic-frameworks (MOFs), composed of metal ions and organic ligands by strong coordination bonds, have attracted tremendous attention for energy conversion reactions. They exhibit intrinsic properties including a large surface area, large pore volume, uniform pore distribution, and tenable chemical structure, contributing to superior performance in both the ORR and OER.79,80 Not only can they directly act as templates for supporting metal composites,81 but also be used as the precursor of carbon skeletons via a high-temperature carbonization process. Since MOF-5 was first used as the template for synthesizing porous carbon with a high surface area (217 m2 gâˆ’1) in 2008, lots of research efforts have been made in developing MOFs as electrocatalytic catalysts.82 A Zn-based MOF, in particular, is a prevalent substrate for NPM-SACs. The addition of Zn helps to prevent aggregation during a high-temperature pyrolysis process through lowering the metal atom concentration and thus increasing the adjacent distance between target metal atoms. In terms of this aspect, ZIF-8 (zeolite imidazolate frameworks), a sub-class of MOFs constructed with Zn cations as metal nods, becomes an ideal platform to anchor NPM-SAs through a high-temperature pyrolysis process.83

During the fabrication process of NPM-SAs confined on 3D materials, two main functions of MOFs could be concluded: (1) the sources of carbon/nitrogen/metal, and (2) (in)direct template for supporting the single atoms. Dilpazir et al. mixed ZIF-67 with central cobalt metal and dicyandiamide (DCD) as precursors and then ZIF-67@DCD went through a carbonization period at 750 Â°C (Fig. 5a).84 The convoluted cobalt-atom-decorated carbon nanotubes (CoSAs@CNTs) showed a bamboo-like morphology where the metallic cobalt nanoparticles were embedded in each tip of CNTs, and the Co single atoms were homogeneously well dispersed throughout the network of nanotubes. The ZIF here only served as an atom provider (C/N/Co) and the formation of CNTs was depended on the self-reactions of DCD with the involvement of Co atoms which was mentioned before.

2.3.2 NPM-SAs confined on 3D carbon spherical nanomaterials. The successful application of monodisperse colloidal spheres in the electrocatalysis field strongly relies on the availability, tightly controlled sizes, surface properties, and self-assembling ability to form ordered superstructures.94 Most carbon sphere-like supports show a unique seedless-shell structure, resulting in the high efficiency of mass transfer based on the reduced diffusion lengths and resistance to aggregation.95 Two general formation categories of non-precious-metal single atoms supported on carbon spheres have been speculated: hard-template52,96,97 and template-free methods.98â€“100 To produce a spherical structure, SiO2 was often chosen as the hard template and finally removed through acid52,97 or alkaline etching.96 As demonstrated in Fig. 6a, metal sources herein were dissolved in DMF containing organic carbon sources to produce a composite, followed by a carbonization and alkaline etching process to form porous N-doped carbon nanospheres confining isolated single atomic sites (ISAS).96 In contrast to the hard-template synthesis process, the template-free method seems to be more facile and more likely to be conducted for large-scale fabrication. Additionally, there are some other unusual 3D structured materials acting as templates for NPM-SACs. For instance, Yan et al. designed low spin-state d0 W6+ single atom doped Ni(OH)2 nanosheets (Wâ€“Ni(OH)2) which showed a 3D layered structure.104 Moreover, sustainable biomass with a high concentration of carbon atoms could also serve as a support. Zhang et al. fabricated N-doped hierarchically porous carbon (NHPC) from Porphyra in the presence of a natural Fe source from hemin. After a high-temperature pyrolysis process, the composite transformed into the final Fe single atom confined NHPC (SA-Fe/NHPC).105 It is also found that biomimetic Co(II)â€“histidine complexes are able to realize reverse oxygenation and deoxygenation in solution. Based on this theory, Wan et al. successfully produced Co SAC coordinated nonplanar catalysts via pyrolysis and acid washing processes with L-histidine, carbon black and Co-inorganic salts as the precursors.106

2.4 NPM-SAs confined on heterogeneous dimensional supports

Apart from the single kinds of supports, several combined composites have been developed to serve as metal-single-atom supports. Most of these SACs exhibit a three-dimensional nanostructure, as a result of complex chemical bonds among different substrates. As shown in Fig. 6d, Chen et al. put forward a novel route combining carbon layers and CNTs as the supports for metal-single atoms.107 The 3D hierarchical network comprised a great number of carbon layers grown in situ on entangled CNT clusters. Additionally, the 3D carbon structure was co-decorated with N and S as the substrates for Feâ€“Nx atomic active sites. The resulting S,Nâ€“Fe/N/Câ€“CNT hybrid materials exhibited superior bifunctional catalytic activities for both the ORR and OER, as a result of the 3D conductive networks to expedite electron transfer, the unique hierarchical structure, and the abundance of atomically dispersed highly active metalâ€“Nx sites. Wang and co-workers fabricated atomic Fe embedded in a conceptual inter-layer between porous g-C3N4 and N-doped graphene meshes (Feâ€“NGM/Câ€“Fe).108 The 3D dual-mesh structure resulted from a two-step annealing process with exfoliated graphene and dicyandiamide as the precursors. The obtained complex coordination structure and homogeneous distribution of high density nanopores contributed to excellent electrocatalytic performance in the ORR process.

3. Characterization techniques for identifying active sites in NPM-SACs

Physicochemical characterization offers vital information of NPM-SACs to investigate their electrocatalytic performance and to explain the function of single atoms in the catalyst, providing guidelines for the rational design of NPM-SACs. Currently, the characterization of SACs could be examined by physical, chemical, and advanced computational modelling and related simulation methods such as density functional theory (DFT). High-angle annular dark-field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HADDF-STEM) has been widely used for finding out the atomic structure of such catalysts. Chemical characterization detection for NPM-SACs is mainly based on synchrotron-radiated X-ray absorption fine structure (XAFS) techniques, including X-ray absorption near edge structure (XANES) and extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS). DFT provides the binding energy, magnetic moment, and other parameters at the atomic level, which closely correlate with the electrochemical catalytic performance of NPM-SACs.

3.1 Electron microscopy imaging

In the first place, the morphology of materials would be detected by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). However, SEM and TEM can only provide information on the microscopical level. To investigate the atomic structure of NPM-SACs, more precise and high-resolution techniques are required. Compared with conventional TEM, a scanning transmission electron microscope (STEM) equipped with additional scanning coils, detectors, and necessary circuity is more effective in measuring compositional changes at buried interfaces as well as the electronic structure and bonding.109 Annular dark field (ADF) imaging using a STEM, which was first conducted by Crewe et al. in 1970, has been further developed to demonstrate single heavy atoms, especially on a thin carbon substrate.110,111 Based on the concept of ADF-STEM, high-angle annular dark-field imaging (HAADF)-STEM could optimize SAC images at the atomic level with the avoidance of structure-sensitive effects compared with conventional high-resolution microscopy.112,113 In recently reported studies, electron energy-loss spectroscopy (EELS) has generally been conducted simultaneously with HADDF-STEM to identify the elemental compositions of materials.114 Considering that light atoms mainly show weak signals in ADF-STEM, modified aberration-corrected scanning transmission electron microscopy (AC-STEM) has been developed to observe real space imaging and spectroscopy. More attractively, it can be used to obtain chemical information such as bonding, elemental valence, and dynamics of every single atom.115,116 Scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) and modified atomic force microscopy (AFM) are capable of realizing the distribution and atomic sites of SACs. Since STM is a kind of surface-science technology, only SACs with a flat structure (2D supports) can be characterized by STM. Non-contact AFM, in particular, could provide a high-resolution three-dimensional surface profile in ambient air or even liquid environments regardless of the sample conductivity, which remedies the defects of common STM.117â€“120

For instance, nickel SACs stabilized on nitrogen-doped carbon nanotubes (NiSAâ€“Nâ€“CNTs) presented a bamboo-like tubular nanostructure with an average diameter of 20â€“50 nm, which could be observed in Fig. 7a and b. STEM with elemental mapping in Fig. 7c and d showed a uniform distribution of N and Ni across the tubes.45 In another study reported by Yin et al., the precise structural characterization of NPM-SACs was performed by HADDF-STEM, which implied that the Co single atoms/nitrogen-doped porous carbon (Co SAs/Nâ€“C) derived from a MOF retained the initial rhombododecahedral shape of the MOF precursor (Fig. 7e). From the EELS mapping image (Fig. 7h), it is discerned that Co and N were homogeneously dispersed within the carbon structure.87

4. Electrocatalytic performance for oxygen redox reactions and applications

Most recently reported NPM-SACs have shown comparable or even better oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), oxygen evolution reaction (OER), or bifunctional catalytic activities compared with the commercial benchmark (Pt/C, Ir/C, etc.) and other precious-metal-involved catalysts. It is notable that limited NPM-SACs on 1Dâ€“3D supports exhibited OER or bifunctional catalytic activities. The most appealing non-precious-metal single atoms participated in these oxygen redox reactions are Fe, Co, and Ni, and there has been a small quantity of research focusing on the Cu, W, or dual-metal sites of the above elements. The atomic metalâ€“nitrogen structure (Mâ€“Nx) is the dominant active site for NPM-SACs and limited catalysts directly rely on the metal single atoms in electrochemical reactions which could be only observed in 1D nanomaterials supported NPM-SACs. For high-dimensional substrates (2D and 3D), Mâ€“Nx or Mâ€“Nxâ€“Cy molecules play significant roles in oxygen redox reactions with the infrequent involvement of other heteroatoms. Unusual dual-metal or multi-metal active sites in NPM-SACs could be observed due to difficult in stabilization without any aggregation, and only a few reported 2D and 3D substrates with a comparably complicated structure allowed this kind of confinement. The addition of other metal-free heteroatoms such as B, S, P, and O has been indicated to influence the metal atom valence state on different substrates and to potentially optimize the ORR and/or OER performance. DFT calculations have been further conducted to understand the reaction pathways, combined with various advanced characterization technologies.

4.1 Oxygen reduction reaction

Generally, the ORR proceeds with the adsorption of oxygen molecules onto the catalyst surface. There are two adsorption types: bidentate O2 adsorption (two O atoms coordinate with the catalyst) and end-on O2 adsorption (one O atom coordinates perpendicularly to the catalyst), which decide the reaction conducts a two-electron and four-electron transfer pathway, respectively.123 The 4eâˆ’ transfer pathway is preferred in fuel cell and metalâ€“air battery applications, and the 2eâˆ’ transfer pathway represents a facile and clean method for H2O2 generation. It is still in its initial state for selectively reducing oxygen to H2O2, while recently SACs have presented potential as electrocatalysts in the H2O2 generation process.124â€“126 For instance, Yang et al. prepared single Pt atoms supported on titanium nitride nanoparticles and the obtained Pt/TiN with a noble-metal content of 0.35% selectively changed the ORR toward a two-electron pathway.126 The high mass activity and selectivity relied on the atomic metal active sites with low loading. In addition, NPM-SACs also demonstrate potential in electrochemical H2O2 generation. Jiang et al. reported that transition metal single-atom coordination motifs (demoted as Mâ€“CNT) could act as electrochemical catalysts for reducing oxygen to H2O2 with an unprecedented selectivity of above 95% in both alkaline and neutral media.127 Theoretical analysis also highlighted that transition metal single atoms could efficiently tune the ORR pathways and product selectivity, especially Feâ€“Câ€“O coordination motifs.

The 4eâˆ’ transfer pathway is more commonly involved in the existing OER. To visually demonstrate the ORR catalytic activity, the Gibbs free energy (Î”G), defined as the difference between the initial and final states of intermediates, has been regarded as a significant parameter in the ORR process to show the adsorption energies in each step.128 It is widely accepted that the adsorption of O and OH might be the crucial step of the ORR, especially on metal catalysts. 4.2 Oxygen evolution reaction

In contrast to the ORR, studies about NPM-SACs possessing oxygen evolution reaction (OER) activity have been rarely reported. The OER mechanisms and pathways are relatively complex as the generation of oxygen molecules should undergo several proton/electron coupled processes which is related to the catalyst intrinsic characteristics.141 Transition metals, especially the earth-abundant first-row transition metals which have higher electronic conductivity, have shown the possibility to accelerate the OER catalytic process.142 It is remarkable that although acidic electrolytes potentially provide much lower ohmic resistance,143 recently reported NPM-SACs with different dimensions profited from the alkaline medium in the OER. The OER conducted on Ni-NHGF followed a dual-site pathway where the Ni single atoms and C sites were both favorable for intermediate adsorption. 5. Conclusion and prospects

In order to maximize the utilization of active sites of metal catalysts, single-metal-atom catalysts were developed coinciding with the rational design of more active sites at the atomic level. Non-precious-metal single-atom materials represent a new class of low-cost, highly efficient electrocatalysts for ORR and/or OER processes, and exhibit the great potential to replace precious-metal-based catalysts. In this review, we first compare recently reported NPM-SACs from the viewpoint of different dimensional supports. In particular, NPM-SAs confined on 3D nanomaterials have been extensively developed, especially those derived from 3D MOF frameworks. There has also been great interest in the experimental and theoretical investigation of 2D NPM-SACs. However, oriented NPM-SACs for OER and ORR/OER reverse reactions are inferior and need dramatic development. Clean energy storage and conversion applications, such as fuel cells and metalâ€“air batteries based on NPM-SACs, are still in the initial state.

Although the progress of NPM-SACs is impressive, many challenges and opportunities remain. The vast majority of as-prepared NPM-SACs are Fe-, Co-, and Ni-based materials, and these single atoms are mainly first anchored by non-metal heteroatoms such as N, S, B, and O through covalent or coordination bonds, and then the metal matrix is bound to various supports, especially to carbon-based supports. The difficulty of anchoring single metal atoms on supports narrows the range of NPM-SAs as well as supports. As a result, although co-doping or tri-doping of different metal atoms has allowed NPM-SACs to act as bifunctional and even multi-functional electrocatalysts, almost all of the reported NPM-SACs contained only one type of metal atom. Therefore, there is an urgent demand to expand the range of NPM-SAs and to develop various feasible supports. In addition, the loading of active metal sites in NPM-SACs is generally minimal due to the intrinsic restrict of NPM-SACs. Most atomic content is lower than 1%, which limits the increase of active sites and thus the electrocatalytic performance. Defects or vacancies on supports could provide probable sites for single atoms and reduce aggregation as well. As reported by Zhang et al., pre-treated graphene with defects successfully trapped Ni single atoms and exhibited excellent performance in the HER and OER.58 In the future, more research attention should be paid to enhance the stability of supports and break the trade-off between atom loading and agglomeration. Moreover, the performance of NPM-SACs is normally unpredicted due to the random anchoring location of single metal atoms. Developing an effective strategy to manipulate and control the growth of certain metal single atoms on selective supports can be significantly meaningful.

Exploring universal and facile high-resolution techniques is a continuous mission to precisely identify the active sites in NPM-SACs. Although the development of ex situ characterization techniques, such as HAADF-STEM and XAFS, could enable some identification of single atoms, techniques for effective in situ detection of single-atom active sites during reaction pathways are still in their infancy, and are essential to obtain a mechanistic understanding of electrocatalysis on NPM-SACs. A poisoning test with a specific molecular probe is also an imperative method for unequivocally revealing the role of the metal in electrochemical catalysis. A particular design of control experiments could identify the significance of single atoms as well as distinguish the difference between NPM-SACs and traditional non-precious-metal catalysts.

There is no doubt that modelling technologies are significant in the design and electrocatalytic activity analysis parts. Most electrochemical performance simulations of NPM-SACs supported on carbon-based materials have been built on 2D-graphene models via DFT calculations. The difference among 1D, 2D, and 3D supports is usually ignored to simplify the calculation process, while more specific computational work is needed to fully realize the roles of individual metal atoms, different-dimensional supports, and the interactions between them at the atomic level. Although previous studies have investigated bimetal catalysts, the mechanism of the confinement of different metal atoms on the same supports is not clear. The interaction of bi- and even tri-metal active sites and the electrochemical reaction mechanism on them need to be specifically investigated to accelerate the development progress of complex NPM-SACs. It is well known that the computational technique is a powerful tool and the proof-of-concept pattern could verify the feasibility of synthesis and provide the trends in catalyst optimization. The rational design of different functional NPM-SACs would be possible on the basis of more advanced modelling technologies.

The application of NPM-SACs for ORR and/or OER catalytic reactions is in its infancy. Especially for bifunctional NPM-SACs, liquid Znâ€“air batteries seem to be the most popular applications recently. All-solid metalâ€“air batteries and new kinds of fuel cells like high-temperature membrane fuel cells could be taken into account. Considering the commercialization possibility, metalâ€“air batteries in acidic medium seem to be more efficient than in alkaline electrolytes. The performance of most NPM-SACs is barely satisfactory in acidic media, and thus more effort is needed. The economic cost and the environmental effects of NPM-SACs are nonnegligible factors in the applications of NPM-SACs as well. To this end, the future of NPM-SACs will be bright in the electrochemical energy technology field. Taking advantage of advanced modelling technologies, high-revolution characterization methods, and novel synthesis strategies, NPM-SACs will undergo rapid development.
