Abstract

To date, all the lead halide based full-inorganic or organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskites have been synthesized from organic solvent, such as N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) or dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), by a solution method. Herein, water has been utilized as a â€˜greenâ€™ solvent to develop an efficient synthetic route to grow various kinds of lead halide perovskite nanowires (NWs). By controlling the proportion of the hybrid cations, Csx(CH3NH3)1âˆ’xPbI3 perovskite NWs were successfully synthesized. Every Csx(CH3NH3)1âˆ’xPbI3 perovskite NW demonstrated single crystal characteristics with uniform stoichiometric element distribution. Because of the controllable cation composition, the NW bandgaps could be finely tuned from 1.5 to 1.7 eV. Transient photoluminescence spectra showed superior NW quality when compared with those of the conventional DMF-based NWs. Based on the abovementioned high quality single Cs0.5(CH3NH3)0.5PbI3 perovskite NW, a reliable single-NW photodetector was fabricated to investigate the optoelectronic application. It demonstrated a responsivity of 23 A/W, exceeding most of the reported values in the perovskite nanowire photoconductive detectors, and the shot-noise normalized detectivity was 2.5 Ã— 1011 Jones comparable to the parameters of the commercial silicon-based nanowires. The green and robust synthesis method, finely tunable NW bandgaps, and superior optoelectronic properties are expected to open a new door for the development of perovskite optoelectronic devices.

1. Introduction

Organo-lead halide perovskite (OHP, CH3NH3PbX3) has been widely applied in optoelectronic devices, such as solar cells,1â€“3 light-emitting devices (LEDs),4 field-effect transistors (FETs),5 photodetectors (PDs),6â€“8 and lasers,9 due to its remarkable photoelectric properties. For optoelectronic applications, the absorption band edge and peak position play key roles in the device performance.3â€“9 Hybrid perovskites have attracted significant interest due to their tunable bandgaps and superior energy conversion properties. The bandgap tuning is generally implemented by changing the composition by halogen anion10â€“14 or cation15â€“17 exchange. Recently, Li et al. utilized FA0.81MA0.15PbI2.51Br0.45 as an organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskite, which not only realized the tunable bandgaps but also improved the device performance compared to that of their counterparts alone.18

Nowadays, the synthesis methods of perovskite NWs are ascribed to solvent evaporation assisted self-assembly methods19â€“24 and most reports utilized organic polar solvents, such as N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), in a solution method. The low-cost and large-scale perovskite optoelectronic devices, especially for solar cells, would consume large amounts of organic solvent, which is not only harmful to the operators but is also non-environmentally friendly. Once the organic solvent is replaced by water, it is of great benefit for the future of perovskite development. Inspired by the abovementioned considerations, we investigated the utilization of water as a solvent to synthesize the perovskite materials.

Different perovskite morphologies and structures, including nanoparticles,25 NW,19â€“21 nanosheets26 and quantum dots,11 have been realized by various synthesis routes. The perovskite NWs hold well-defined crystal structure and a novel one-dimensional enclosing surface,27,28 which offers fewer grain boundaries, lower defect concentration, and longer photocarrier lifetime.29,30 Recently, most groups have fabricated single cation perovskite (CH3NH3PbI3) NWs by solvent evaporation assisted self-assembly methods.27,28,30 In contrast, hybrid cation perovskite NWs with fine tuning bandgaps were expected to match better to the light emission or absorption spectra. Furthermore, the reported perovskite photodetectors (PDs) based on NWs and microwires (MWs) were all based on groups of them (hundreds of NWs) rather than a single NW, which may lead to the deviation from the NW's original properties. In this regard, single NW-based photodetectors were systematically investigated to understand their intrinsic physical properties, especially their photocarrier behavior, which is the basis of perovskite-based optoelectronic devices.

Here, we developed an aqueous solution method for Csx(CH3NH3)1âˆ’xPbI3 perovskite NWs. By tuning the hybrid cation ratio, Csx(CH3NH3)1âˆ’xPbI3 perovskite NWs with finely tunable bandgaps were realised. Single NW photodetectors with a single crystalline nature demonstrated high detection performance. The â€˜greenâ€™ solvent utilization, tunable NW bandgaps and high performance optoelectronic devices in the present work are expected to introduce new elements into perovskite research.

2. Results and discussion

It was well known that the polar solvent water could dissolve perovskite materials. Thus, it is a possibility to apply it in perovskite synthesis in order to avoid consuming organic solvent. The aqueous solution method for perovskite NWs mainly contained two parts, as shown in Fig. 1aâ€“f. One part was the preparation of PbI2 flakes in saturated aqueous solution (Fig. 1aâ€“c). Excessive PbI2 powder was dissolved in water at 75 Â°C as the starting precursor solution (Fig. 1b). Once the solution was cooled to room temperature (âˆ¼25 Â°C) the flashing PbI2 flakes could separate out from the oversaturated solution (Fig. 1c). The other part was the growth of perovskite NWs as shown in Fig. 1dâ€“f. The obtained supernatant was added with cation sources (Fig. 1d). By simply shaking for few seconds, the transparent solution became light grey (Fig. 1e). Then the newly grown porous grey materials were immediately filtrated and baked to remove residual water (Fig. 1f). The detailed process was described in the experiment section.

PbI2 flakes were obtained by recrystallization process (Fig. S1a and 1c, ESIâ€ ). The synthesized PbI2 flakes could easily react with CsI or CH3NH3I due to ultra-high surface area and high solubility of cation sources, and then be converted into perovskite molecules in solution. These molecules freely reacted with each other to grow into perovskite NWs or NW-like intermediate materials by oriented self-assembly method in aqueous solution. Excess CsI and MAI were added into the solution to enable the complete reaction with PbI2 flakes. For NW growth, the yellow CsPbI3 NWs could directly grow in aqueous solution, while the CH3NH3PbI3 NWs first existed in the form of precursor NWs (Fig. S1aâ€ ). After suction filtration and drying, the white precursor NWs would convert into black CH3NH3PbI3 NWs. The amount of perovskite NWs obtained by this method depended on the mass of PbI2 separated out from aqueous solution. The temperature dependent solubility of PbI2 in aqueous solution was shown in Fig. S1b.â€  We could get âˆ¼9.7 mg PbI2 flakes in 1 mL water when the temperature decreased from 75 Â°C to 25 Â°C. Therefore, one milliliter PbI2 saturated solution could contain 13.1 mg CH3NH3PbI3 or 14.7 mg CsPbI3 NWs. The X-ray diffraction (XRD) spectra of these perovskite NWs were shown in Fig. S1dâ€  and all peak positions were coincident with standard ones of CsPbI3 and CH3NH3PbI3 perovskite.15

And in our previous report,19,34 the growth direction of CH3NH3PbI3 NWs is [001]; the XRD pattern in Fig. S1dâ€  further confirmed their [001] preferential growth direction.19,20 From the high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) image of the Cs0.5(CH3NH3)0.5PbI3 nanowire (inset of Fig. S3hâ€ ), the interplanar spacing verified the [001] growth direction, and the SAED pattern of CsPbI3 proves that the growth direction is [001] also.

In addition to the single cation perovskite NWs, hybrid cation perovskite materials have obtained the highest state-of-the-art conversion efficiency in solar cells and light emitting diodes, ascribed to their superior optoelectronic properties.1â€“4,18 In this regard, three kinds of perovskite NWs (CsPbI3, CH3NH3PbI3, Cs0.5(CH3NH3)0.5PbI3) were conveniently implemented by addition of the corresponding cation sources. The optical microscope pictures of perovskite NWs in water were shown in Fig. 2aâ€“c. It was obvious that all the perovskite molecules grew into NWs in aqueous solution. The products in Fig. 2aâ€“c looked like floccules consisting of numerous thin and short NWs. However, the pure CH3NH3PbI3 perovskite NWs in Fig. 2b tended to grow larger diameter NWs of hundreds of microns in length due to the larger molecular volume31,32 and stronger orientation growth. The scanning electron microscope (SEM) images of three perovskites dispersed on SiO2 substrate were shown in Fig. 2dâ€“f. All obtained NWs were clean and smooth from the present aqueous synthetic route. The diameters of CsPbI3, CH3NH3PbI3 and hybrid perovskite NWs were about 300 nm, 1200 nm, 300 nm extracted from their corresponding insets in the single NW SEM images. The above pure NW growth highly depended on PbI2 flakes. We could only get short nanorods or non-uniform particles (Fig. S1fâ€ ) by utilizing PbI2 powder as a precursor. It was noted that the present aqueous solution method grew the NWs in aqueous solution rather than the reported solvent evaporation-driven method26 and it was expected that such natural growth could yield high quality perovskite NWs.

The high external quantum efficiency (EQE) and carrier transport mobility of perovskite NWs promise great potential applications in photodetectors (PDs). The previous work utilized groups of NWs to detect light source rather than a single NW, which maybe suffered from reported NW quality, and the high resistance led to thermal degradation.33 The present freely-grown NWs and inorganic cation incorporation was expected to realise single NW-based PDs. Single NW PDs attracted more attention due to the special superiorities such as small size, high performance and low cost.34 To test the photoelectric properties of hybrid perovskite materials, the single NW PDs were fabricated with Cs0.5(CH3NH3)0.5PbI3 perovskite NWs. Once the device was irradiated by a light source, the photo-generated carriers were rapidly separated and collected by electrodes under the applied electric field. 3. Conclusion

An efficient synthetic route to grow full-inorganic and hybrid cation perovskite single crystal NWs was successfully implemented by a new aqueous solution method. By controlling the proportion of CsI and CH3NH3I, Csx(CH3NH3)1âˆ’xPbI3 perovskite NWs successfully grew in water. The obtained Csx(CH3NH3)1âˆ’xPbI3 perovskite NW was single crystal in nature and their compositions was consistent with their corresponding stoichiometric quantities. Transient photoluminescence demonstrated the extension of photocarrier lifetime by one order when obtained from the present aqueous method. By changing the cation ratios, the bandgaps could be finely tuned from 1.5 to 1.72 eV. Furthermore, a stable and reliable PD based on single Cs0.5(CH3NH3)0.5PbI3 perovskite NW was fabricated with responsivity of 23 A/W and stable normalized detectivity of 2.5 Ã— 1011 Jones. The present green and robust tuning method combining with high performances of hybrid Csx(CH3NH3)1âˆ’xPbI3 perovskite photodetectors are expected to support a powerful platform for the development of perovskite-based optoelectronic devices.

4. Experimental section

Material preparation

All the materials, such as CsI, PbI2, methylamine alcohol solution and hydroiodic acid, were obtained from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co. Ltd and used without further purification. Methylamine iodide (CH3NH3I) was synthesized according to a previous study.1,2

Perovskite NW growth

Excessive PbI2 powder was added into 20 mL water. Then it was heated to 75 Â°C in aqueous bath for 30 min to get PbI2 saturated aqueous solution. After that, the supernatant (about 10 mL) was transferred to another beaker and cooled to room temperature and the PbI2 flakes were separated out. For NW growth, CsI and CH3NH3I with set molar ratio were added into the above PbI2 flake solution and it was violently shaken for a few times. After a short growth time (within 1 minute), the corresponding perovskite NWs would deposit at the bottom. Finally, we used suction filtration for 5 min to remove the water quickly and baked the NWs for 30 min at 90 Â°C. Numerous NWs were synthesized and stored for ready use.

Material characterization

The optical and structural characterizations were performed on UV-Vis absorption (Cary, Lambda 950) and X-ray diffraction spectra (XRD, XRD-7000S/L). The prepared perovskite NW morphologies and crystal structure were characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Nova NanoSEM 450) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM, Tecnai G20). The NW surface morphologies and emission properties were measured by atomic force microscopy (AFM, SPM9700) and photoluminescence (PL, LabRAM HR800).

Device fabrication and measurements

The Cs0.5(CH3NH3)0.5PbI3 perovskite NWs were homogeneously dispersed in aqueous solution and uniformly deposited on SiO2 substrate by drop-coating method. Assisted with shadow mask, ordered Au electrodes were fabricated on NWs by thermal evaporation. The active channel containing a single NW was 10 Î¼m in length and 300 nm in diameter. Photodetector performance was measured by Agilent B1500A semiconductor characterization system assisted with a probe station. The irradiation was generated from monochromatic light-emitting diodes (530 nm) controlled by a functional generator (Agilent 33210A). Light intensity was calibrated by a silicon optical power meter (Newport 818-UV).
