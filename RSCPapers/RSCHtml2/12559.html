Silicon based tandem cells: novel photocathodes for hydrogen production

Abstract

A photovoltaic tandem cell made of amorphous silicon (a-Si) and microcrystalline silicon (μc-Si) was investigated as a photocathode for hydrogen evolution in a photoelectrochemical device. The electronic and electrochemical properties of the samples were characterized using X-ray photoemission spectroscopy (XPS) and cyclic voltammetry (CV), whereas the morphology of the surface in contact with the electrolyte was investigated by scanning electron microscopy (SEM). The electric efficiency of the tandem cell was determined to be 5.2% in a photoelectrochemical (PEC) setup in acidic solution which is only about half of the photovoltaic efficiency of the tandem cell. A significant improvement in efficiency was achieved with platinum as a catalyst which was deposited by physical vapour deposition (PVD) under ultra-high vacuum (UHV) conditions.

1. Introduction

Photoelectrochemistry plays a pivotal role in converting solar energy to storable fuels. In comparison to molecular systems the main advantage of using semi-conducting photoelectrodes for light absorption and charge carrier generation and separation is the high quantum efficiency over a wide spectral range. Since the pioneering work of Fujishima and Honda1 in the 1970s, there has been a long lasting research effort to develop efficient photo-electrodes which is even enforced nowadays due to the fact that the climate change has been recognized as a true danger for mankind. The Gibbs free energy change ΔG° of the net water splitting reaction is 237 kJ mol−1 and its corresponding electromotive force ΔE° is 1.23 V. Since water splitting is an energetic uphill reaction, a working photoelectrode has to supply a minimum photovoltage of 1.23 V in order to drive water splitting without an external bias. In practice, a much higher potential of at least 1.6 to 2.0 V is necessary to split water due to cathodic and anodic overpotentials or other losses.2,3 In this respect, single bandgap photoabsorbers like e.g. silicon or gallium arsenide do not provide enough photovoltage to drive the water splitting reaction because their bandgap is too small. On the other hand, semiconductors with a larger band gap especially oxides (EG > 2.5 eV)4 may provide increased electrochemical driving forces but they have limitations in accessing the lower wavelength part of the solar spectrum, which in turn leads to lower efficiencies as well. Another important point is the stability of the considered materials in contact with aqueous solutions. Often photocorrosion effects form non-conductive oxide layers, which then give rise to strongly increased charge transfer resistances suppressing the water splitting reaction. Moreover, a lot of materials dissolve or degrade in the presence of the electrolyte which means reduced long term stability. Additionally, the energetic band alignment of promising semiconductor materials with respect to the redox potentials of the considered species in the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) or the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) or to suitable catalysts may be a limiting factor for an efficient water splitting process.5 For these reasons, the PEC devices investigated so far do not provide feasible solutions for efficient and economic H2 production.

In contrast to single band gap semiconductors, multiple band gap systems or tandem cells are able to suitably match the energetic requirements and effectively utilize the spectral distribution of the sun's light, which leads to higher efficiencies.5 Weber and Dignam calculated an efficiency of 10% to 18% for various tandem cell configurations.3 Gerischer predicted efficiencies up to 24% with tandem cells6 and Bolton even proposed 28% for a two photon system.2 In practice, Khaselev and Turner obtained 12.4% water splitting efficiency using a tandem cell made of p-GaInP and GaAs,7 whereas Licht and coworkers demonstrated a value of 18.3% with a tandem cell arrangement based on AlGaAs and Si.8 Recently, Reece and coworkers got efficiencies of 2.5% and 4.7% using silicon based triple cells in different geometries presented as an artificial leaf.9 In ref. 10 a comprehensive overview of the current status of tandem cells used for solar water splitting is given. However, none of the presented systems so far combine sufficient efficiency, reasonable stability and the use of inexpensive materials or device structures, which are developed towards technologically feasible solutions. In addition, the reported efficiencies are often not well defined or related to different measurement techniques (two-electrode or three-electrode geometry, supporting bias voltage from potentiostat, electric vs. fuel formation efficiencies).11,12

Herein, we report on a silicon based tandem cell structure as a photocathode for hydrogen evolution, which has been initially developed as a photovoltaic device and which may provide reasonable photovoltages. A possible arrangement and an energy diagram of a tandem device used as a photoelectrolysis cell are shown in Fig. 1. In this set-up dedicated catalysts for the HER and the OER are used to minimize losses of the photovoltage due to over-voltages. In addition, the semiconductor surfaces are in direct contact with the electrolyte which induces photocorrosion processes depending on the materials selection. In our case this problem is avoided by using a solar cell which is designed as a superstrate device13,14 and which is terminated by a silver layer acting as protecting back contact. Other approaches, where the semiconductor is in direct contact with the electrolyte, are based on special surface treatments as shown in ref. 15 using a Ni passivated Si photoanode. The tandem cell mainly consists of two p–i–n junctions made of a-Si and μc-Si, which are carefully adapted to each other in order to absorb most of the solar spectrum and in order to provide enough photovoltage without losing too much photocurrent. In general, photoelectrochemical devices based on thin film Si cells give the perspective to lead to a cost-effective and environmentally friendly technological solution. However, single absorber cells based on μc-Si (EG ∼ 1.1 eV) or a-Si (EG ∼ 1.7 eV)16 do not provide enough photovoltage for the water splitting reaction whereas triple cells with photovoltages beyond UPh > 2.0 V lose too much of the generated energy compared to ΔE° = 1.23 V which is needed for the water splitting reaction. As a consequence a-Si/μc-Si tandem cells with correctly adjusted photovoltages provide the most promising solution to achieve efficient and cost-effective photoelectrochemical devices.17–19

2. Experimental section

Aqueous solutions were prepared using deionized water from a Milli-Q (Millipore) system. All chemicals were purchased as analytical grade (Merck) and were used as received unless otherwise noted. Physical vapour deposition (PVD) of platinum (99.99%, Alfa Aesar) as a catalyst was done from a graphite crucible placed in an electron beam evaporator (EMF 3, Omicron/Focus GmbH), which was attached to a dedicated ultra-high vacuum (UHV) chamber located in the “Darmstadt Integrated System for Fundamental research (DAISY-FUN)”.20 The platinum deposition was accomplished at a growth rate of 0.05 Å min−1 (Sycon STM-100/MF) while the shutter was opened for 60 min under pressure conditions below 10−9 mbar. From these values the nominal thickness of the platinum layer was estimated to be 3 Å.

XPS analysis was done using a hemispherical analyser (Phoibos 150), which was operated together with a high-resolution monochromatic Al-Kα X-ray source at 1486.61 eV (SPECS GmbH). All photoelectron peaks were fitted by Gauss–Lorentz functions after subtraction of a Shirley-type background. For the evaluation of the spectra, a dedicated software package (CasaXPS, Casa Software Ltd.) was used with already built-in sensitivity factors for the quantitative analysis of the surface composition. The spectrometer itself was calibrated to the Fermi edge of a clean Ag foil assigned to a binding energy of 0 eV. The surface morphology was investigated using an SEM (Philips XL30 FEG).

Photoelectrochemical measurements were performed at room temperature utilizing a commercially available CIMPS-2 system (Zahner Elektrik GmbH), which combines a potentiostat, a light emitting diode (LED) light source and a modified PECC-2 cell as a specimen holder. The tandem cell was investigated as a photocathode in a three electrode arrangement with a 3 M Ag/AgCl reference electrode and a platinum counter electrode. Designed as a superstrate device, the reflecting back contact made of silver is in contact with the electrolyte consisting of 0.1 M H2SO4 solution. All potentials specified in the voltammograms of this work are referred to the normal hydrogen electrode (NHE). As a light source, a white light emitting LED with a power of 100 W m−2 was utilized and regulated by a feedback control system, which is based on a calibrated Si photodiode placed close to the working electrode. The illuminated sample area was 0.5 cm2 in size. In normal operation mode, the light source was switched on and off (chopped) with a frequency of 1 Hz while the scan rate for the polarisation was adjusted to a value of 20 mV s−1. In order to avoid disturbances from stray light, all photoelectrochemical experiments were carried out in a light blocking cabinet.

3. Results and discussion

A detailed description of the silicon based tandem cells acting as photovoltaic devices and which were used for this study is given elsewhere in the literature.21–23 The cells were used without any changes as described in the PVC research. A rough sketch of the device structure is shown in Fig. 2 for a better understanding of our experimental approach. The solar cell is illuminated from the left through the transparent glass substrate, which acts as the carrier of the complete tandem cell structure. The incoming light rays first transit the top cell, which consists of a p–i–n homojunction based on a-Si with a bandgap of 1.7 eV. They then reach the bottom cell consisting of a μc-Si based p–i–n structure, where remaining photons with hν > 1.1 eV are absorbed effectively. A highly reflective ZnO/Ag layer is used as back contact, which enhances the absorption probability further. The presented configuration, often called a superstrate tandem solar cell, provides a higher photo-voltage (∼1.3 V, see Fig. 3) than a single bandgap device. Due to a more effective capture of photons in two dedicated energy bands or wavelength regimes, the overall efficiency is also improved. As shown in the photocurrent–voltage (I–U) curve in Fig. 3 the photovoltaic efficiency of the tandem cell in the conversion of light into electric power reaches a value of η = 9.5 ± 0.1% under AM1.5 illumination (Lot Oriel GmbH).
Fig. 2 Schematic of the tandem cell used as a photocathode.

Fig. 3 Photocurrent–voltage (I–U) curve of the tandem cell measured as a PV cell under AM1.5 conditions. The active area is 1 cm2.

The cells used in our experiments provide stabilized efficiencies of about 9.5%; a typical PV current voltage curve is given in Fig. 3. We have used an arrangement for the photoelectrochemical studies as shown in Fig. 2.

Preliminary experiments were performed with ZnO or Ag terminated electrode surfaces which were modified with Pt layers either deposited by wet-chemical reduction of PtCl4 or by PVD (see the Experimental section). It was observed that within the preferential electrolyte (H2SO4) the Ag covered surfaces with Pt PVD deposits provide efficient devices so far.

The I–U curve of the tandem cell with a Ag surface layer under chopped light conditions is shown in Fig. 4a. The onset potential (Uoc) of the cathodic current starts at around 1.2 V versus NHE under illumination. This is equivalent to a reduction potential which is shifted by 1.2 V more positive with respect to the general onset potential of the hydrogen evolution taking place at a standard platinum electrode in the same solution. The measured short circuit current density (jsc) is −0.94 ± 0.05 mA cm−2. In the region from 1.2 V down to 0.5 V we observe severe spikes in the current transients as well as a slow increase of the photocurrent, which are assigned to surface trapping of charge carriers and slow charge carrier transfer at the electrolyte interface. The maximum power point (MPP) of the photovoltage is only around 0.46 ± 0.05 V vs. NHE indicating a loss of driving force of about 0.7 V in comparison to the photocurrent onset potential of 1.2 V in the PEC cell or UPh ∼ 1.3 V for the PV cell. The value of the maximum photocurrent in the PEC cell is close to the value measured for the PV cell (jsc = −9.77 ± 0.05 mA cm−2) as expected for the low light intensities under 1/10 AM1.5 conditions. The comparable photocurrents indicate that there is no further loss mechanism in the separation of charge carriers affecting the overall efficiency, and that the charge transfer resistance due to insufficient electrocatalysis is negligible. This formula represents the photon to electric power efficiency ηPEC of a light absorbing electrode which works as a PEC cell with the product of the values for photocurrent density jMPP and photovoltage UMPP at the MPP normalised by the incoming light power per area pLight.24 It is important to be aware that resistive losses e.g. at the counter electrode are not considered in the present case due to the fact that the experimental data used as the input for this calculation have been generated in three electrode configuration, thus neglecting potential relevant processes at the counter electrode. Furthermore, it has to be pointed out that the calculated efficiency is related only to the efficiency of a photoelectrochemical cell converting light energy into electrical energy thus not considering the conversion into chemical energy. Therefore, the only way to obtain a realistic value for the water splitting efficiency is to measure the current in two electrode configuration under short circuit conditions4 which however needs a higher photovoltage to drive the water splitting reaction.

Since Pt and noble metal containing materials have received more and more attention as electro-catalysts for hydrogen evolution in the last few years,25–29 pure platinum was deposited under UHV conditions onto the silver surface from an e-beam evaporator. The chopped light I–U curve of the platinum covered tandem cell is given in Fig. 4b. The onset potential (Uoc) of the hydrogen evolution is shifted to about 1.1 V versus NHE and the short circuit current density (jsc) is −0.86 ± 0.05 mA cm−2. Although these values are comparable to those of the tandem cell without platinum the photoelectric efficiency could be improved from 3.2 ± 0.1 to 4.8 ± 0.1% according to formula (1). This enhancement is mainly attributed to an increase in the fill factor (FF) shifting the MPP by 0.2 V to 0.64 ± 0.05 V. However, these calculated values and their comparison must be handled with care in both PEC cases namely without and with platinum. The occurrence of spikes and a non-zero photocurrent indicate that the band edges of the device shift with applied potential leading to an additional potential drop across the electrochemical double layer induced by trapped non-equilibrium charge carriers. Such effects have been often observed for hindered charge transfer e.g. at metal chalcogenide photoelectrodes.30 Evidently, the performance of the silicon tandem cell as a photoelectrochemical cell is strongly determined by the (photo) electro-catalytic properties of the surface exposed to the electrolyte.

For this reason the surface composition of the Pt modified tandem cell was studied by XPS. Survey spectra of the tandem cell as depicted in Fig. 5 clearly show a Pt 4f line at about 70 eV after platinum deposition. The Pt peak itself is very small due to the little amount of Pt which was deposited. As expected the Ag 3d line dominates both spectra, but further to this there is only less oxygen and carbon visible on the pure tandem cell indicating a non-oxidized silver layer which is obviously kept in its metallic state. In order to get more information about the impact of platinum on silver, the Pt 4f and the Ag 3d lines were analysed with higher resolution.


Fig. 5 X-ray photoelectron survey spectra of the tandem cells with and without Pt.

In Fig. 6, Ag 3d spectra are presented before and after the Pt deposition. From the analysis of the Ag 3d 5/2 peak component by means of a best least square fit using a single Gauss–Lorentz function we obtain a binding energy value of 368.19 ± 0.05 eV and a full width at half maximum (FWHM) of 0.69 ± 0.05 eV for the pure silver layer. With platinum these measurements are slightly changed to a binding energy value of 368.17 ± 0.05 eV, an intensity decrease of about 3% and a FWHM of 0.71 ± 0.05 eV. We conclude from these data that the silver layer is barely affected by the deposition of platinum, since the binding energy of both lines is more or less unchanged and that the Ag 3d 5/2 peak is only slightly damped due to the deposition of material on top of it. This value is in the same range as the nominal thickness of 3 Å attained with the quartz microbalance but still too low. We assign this to the fact that in contrast to the assumption of a layered system there is evidently the formation of small Pt nano clusters (up to 100 nm in lateral size), which cover the silver surface thus leading to a shadowing effect with only a slightly reduced Ag 3d line intensity as a consequence. A rough calculation on the signal attenuation induced by the coverage of about 30 round shaped Pt nano clusters with a diameter of 100 nm as counted on an area of 4 μm2 (Fig. 6) reveals an intensity decrease of about 6.3% which is close to the 3% damping of the Ag 3d line. The assumption of the occurrence of Pt nano-clusters is also in accordance with the binding energies of the Ag 3d lines before and after Pt deposition with signals arising in both cases from the uncovered areas. In addition the formation of Pt nano-clusters would explain the underestimation of the mean thickness of the Pt layer in the layered model. The increase of the FWHM value might be attributed to an increased roughness after the Pt treatment.

The formation of Pt clusters is also supported by the XP spectrum of the Pt 4f doublet shown in Fig. 7 with its Pt 4f 7/2 and Pt 4f 5/2 line components. Best least square fits considering only the Pt 4f 7/2 component reveal two species at binding energies of 70.63 ± 0.05 eV and 71.61 ± 0.05 eV, respectively. We assume that the first component (I) located at 70.63 eV belongs to bulk like clusters20 whereas the second component (II) at 71.61 eV belongs to surface species which may be modified by adsorbed oxygen on platinum as reported by Kim et al.33 Both species differ in their intensity by a factor of two with an increased FWHM of the second component (II) compared to the first component (I). For a better comprehension a summary of all XPS results is given in Table 2. The shown values support the model of small Pt clusters covered by adsorbed oxygen. This would explain the higher intensity of the more surface related component (II) with more platinum atoms coupled to oxygen than in the bulk and the higher FWHM of this component which is addressed to a higher disorder at the surface of the small clusters. It is assumed that residual oxygen which was not removed by the wet-chemical cleaning treatment of the tandem cell prior to the XPS analysis is responsible for this effect. The fact that the binding energy value of 70.63 eV for the first component (I) is slightly below the value for bulk platinum34 may be attributed to size effects of the platinum clusters.35,36 However, the formation of a silver platinum alloy could not be proved by our measurements but it has to be pointed out that the data basis from the literature with regard to this behaviour is rather inconsistent. Therefore we cannot exclude the partial formation of a Pt–Ag alloy in the sub-monolayer regime. In summary, we propose the following picture of the Pt coated silver surface based on the obtained data. Bulk-like Pt clusters up to several hundred nanometres in lateral size are covering the microcrystalline Ag substrate in the range of 5 ± 2% acting as electro-catalytically active areas for an efficient charge carrier transfer into the electrolyte upon illumination (H2 evolution).

4. Conclusions

We have shown in this paper that silicon based tandem solar cells have the potential to be used as efficient photocathodes for the hydrogen evolution reaction for photo-electrochemical water splitting. A remarkable enhancement of the efficiency of the photoelectrode was achieved by the deposition of platinum using a PVD process. Since platinum couples to the underlying silver metal surface by simple alignment of the Fermi level without affecting the energetic band alignment of the tandem cell, this procedure seems to be well suited for an enhancement of the overall efficiency. Besides the good electronic coupling of both metals to each other, the Ag layer plays an important role in protecting the underlying ZnO layer, which would be dissolved in contact with acid electrolytes suitable for water splitting. Even though the tandem cell produces enough photovoltage, an additional bias-voltage is still required to reach the maximum power point photovoltage and to split water.

In order to achieve bias-free water splitting in the future, a number of additional measures must be taken to improve these promising device arrangements further. First of all a tandem solar cell with adjusted photovoltages of UphOC of about 1.8 V and a UphMPP of about 1.6 V is needed. As already discussed above triple cells with strongly enlarged photovoltages beyond 2.5 V are not alternatives as they lead to large free energy losses. The counter electrode which consists of platinum so far has to be developed further and replaced e.g. by ruthenium oxide (RuO2) as anode material. In addition systematic experiments have to be performed on interface engineering at the photocathodes like e.g. the deposition of Pt as an electro-catalyst using different types of deposition processes, varying the Pt particle size and distribution as well as using different substrate surface/pretreatments to improve the PEC performance further.

In summary, first promising PEC results have been obtained by our group with a-Si:H tandem cells. Additional experiments are also necessary to deposit suitable hydrogen evolving catalysts onto the silver surface with specific size and particle distribution as alternatives to platinum in order to save costs and in order to improve our understanding of the water splitting process. The development of advanced HER or OER catalysts, which are designed on the basis of biomimetic principles, is of interest as they can be used as alternatives for precious metal materials for an efficient water splitting process using cheap and abundant transition metals as the primary catalytically active materials.
