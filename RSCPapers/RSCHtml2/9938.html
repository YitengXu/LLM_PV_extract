Abstract

Developing efficient photoanodes with higher charge separation and light harvesting ability remains a crucial challenge. For this purpose, we have used a reduced graphene oxide (rGO) electron-conducting scaffold to improve the charge transfer properties in the ternary transition metal chalcogenide Cu2WS4 (i.e., CWS). The photoluminescence (PL) studies reveal a weakened intensity of the PL band in CWS-rGO compared to CWS. In addition, fluorescence decay suggests an average lifetime of 1.6 ns and 1.16 ns for the CWS and CWS-rGO composite, respectively. The improved performance of the binary composite may be attributable to the remarkable electron transfer characteristics of the highly conductive rGO that facilitate the easy transport and more extended separation of charge carriers. Furthermore, the photoelectrochemical (PEC) measurements confirm the improved charge separation. The CWS-rGO composite exhibits a higher photocurrent density of 1.18 mA cmâˆ’2, while the bare CWS was around 0.45 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.98 V vs. RHE. Furthermore, a higher applied bias photon-to-current efficiency (ABPE), a better transient photocurrent response, and a lower charge transfer resistance in the binary composite confirm the results obtained from the linear sweep voltammetry plots. This work demonstrates that incorporating rGO in CWS is an efficient approach towards accelerating the separation of charge carriers and achieving a better PEC performance of the CWS photoelectrode.

1. Introduction

Photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting is an environmentally benign and scalable approach for converting solar energy to meet the increasing energy demands. Significant efforts have been devoted to fabricating low-cost, highly active, and stable solar-conversion photoanodes.1 Transition-metal dichalcogenide (TMD) materials such as WS2, WSe2, and MoS2 are known to be suitable Pt substitutes for PEC applications due to the cost-effective, environment-friendly, and intrinsic activity of the S or Se atoms on the edge sites.2,3 But the activity of these TMDs in the present form has room for efficiency improvement due to the inadequate electron transfer ability of these chalcogenides in the non-active basal plane.4,5 Cu2WS4 (CWS) is a layered ternary transition metal sulfide with a narrow band gap and excellent electrical conductivity, making it a promising candidate for electrocatalytic and visible-light-driven photocatalytic applications.6â€“8 CWS was first synthesized using a low-temperature route by Pruss et al. in 1993.7 Such ternary transition metal compounds are of great interest because of their appropriate optical and electrical properties. Many efforts have been devoted to examining the photocatalytic hydrogen evolution for this catalyst. However, not much has been explored in terms of photoelectrochemical studies. Jia and group have reported the catalytic properties of CWS for reducing aqueous Cr(VI).9 Jing and group have used CWS for photocatalytic hydrogen evolution applications.10 Peng et al. have reported the fabrication of CWS/NiTiO3 for photocatalytic hydrogen evolution and pollutant degradation.11 Li et al. have reported that the photocatalytic activity of CWS decahedra can be tailored by changing the ratio of (001)/(101) exposed facets. According to reports, the valence band and conduction band edge locations of CWS are around 1.71 eV and âˆ’0.47 eV, respectively. Nevertheless, the photoinduced oxidation and reduction reaction sites for CWS are in the (101) and (001) facets, where the reduction is quick while the oxidation process is delayed. When the photogenerated holes are consumed more slowly, (101) accumulates holes, which causes corrosion and reduced photocatalytic activity.12,13 To lower the corrosion and improve the charge separation in CWS, an effective route is the addition of electron-conducting scaffolds, which will improve the photoelectrochemical performance of CWS. Among the electron conducting scaffolds, reduced graphene oxide (rGO) has gained significant attention due to its distinctive properties, such as low-cost, large surface area, adequate mobility of charge carrier, and remarkable electrical and mechanical properties, which help in better collection and transport of electrons.14â€“16 rGO sheets have been known for their electron-accepting tendency that can be used to enhance the electron transporting properties of materials.17 rGO possesses sp2 hybridized carbon atoms, which tend to act as electron sinks and collectors from the semiconductor's surface, which makes rGO a suitable candidate for improving charge separation in semiconductors.18 For instance, coupling Cu2MoS4 hollow nanotubes with rGO sheets has proven to be an effective way to enhance the photocatalytic activity of the Cu2MoS4 photocatalyst.19 Wang et al. have coupled CuO/TiO2 with graphene for photocatalytic H2 evolution applications.20 Ojha et al. have investigated the photophysical properties of CdS supraparticles embedded on graphene sheets that give a much faster photocatalytic dye degradation when compared to bare CdS. The efficient charge separation achieved in the CdS-graphene composite was confirmed by the ultrafast transient absorption spectroscopic studies that suggest slower bleach recovery in the binary composite.21

In this study, we have designed a nanocomposite by coupling CWS with rGO, which has been used as a photoanode for photoelectrochemical water-splitting applications. This extends the lifetime of charge carriers in CWS and provides a better photoelectrochemical performance. The interactions in the excited state and the lifetime of charge carriers have been investigated using photoluminescence (PL) and time-resolved photoluminescence (TRPL) studies. The enhanced separation of charge carriers has also been reflected in the photoelectrochemical studies of the samples.

3.2. Mechanism of charge-transfer processes

The possible mechanism of charge transfer in the binary composite has been demonstrated in Scheme 1. When the CWS-rGO composite is exposed to visible light, CWS can quickly produce electronâ€“hole pairs in the conduction and valence bands, respectively. The valence band (VB) and conduction band (CB) of CWS are located at âˆ’6.21 eV and âˆ’4.03 eV, respectively, while the Fermi level of rGO is located at âˆ’4.72 eV, as reported earlier.13,28 The formation of the CWS-rGO composite leads to the migration of photogenerated electrons from the conduction band of CWS to the rGO sheets. The rGO sheets can efficiently transfer electrons from CWS to the counter electrode through an external circuit, and thus, this leads to the water reduction reactions to produce hydrogen. While the photoinduced holes located in the VB of CWS are scavenged by the electrolyte solution, where they drive water oxidation.
Scheme 1 Schematic illustration of the charge transfer mechanism in CWS-rGO.

rGO plays the role of electron acceptor and mediator that can adequately collect electrons from CWS and instantly transfer them to the counter electrode due to delocalized electrons in its carbon network.14 Generally, a Schottky barrier is formed between the metal and a semiconductor if the work function of the metal is greater than that of the semiconductor. To lower the electronâ€“hole recombination caused by the Schottky barrier, the metal under illumination can collect the photogenerated electrons from the CB of the semiconductor. For instance, the work function of rGO is larger than that of CWS. When rGO is combined with CWS, a Schottky barrier is formed at the interface of CWS and rGO, which helps transfer the photogenerated electrons from the CB of CWS to rGO. The Schottky barrier between rGO and CWS would reduce the electronâ€“hole recombination by effectively prohibiting the electrons from moving backward from rGO to CWS. The electrons transferred from CWS to rGO will be immediately transferred to the substrate to establish an external circuit because of the extraordinarily high charge carrier mobility in rGO.19,29,30 Thus, the formation of the Schottky barrier plays a vital role in lowering the recombination of the charge carriers in the CWS-rGO composite and hence deciding the overall photoelectrochemical performance of the material.

The estimated band gaps for CWS and CWS-rGO were found to be 2.23 eV and 2.30 eV, respectively. The increase in band gap with the formation of a binary composite indicates interaction amongst its combining entities, which may lead to a better charge transfer and altered electronic states.34 For instance, Royâ€˜s group have reported that the loading of a higher weight percent of TiO2 on Polypyrrole (Ppy) changes the band gap from 3.61 eV to 3.69 eV due to the aggregation of TiO2 at the Ppy matrix. The charge transfer and recombination processes can be revealed by the photoluminescence (PL) spectra of the sample, where a weaker intensity indicates a lower probability of recombination. The PL spectra shown in Fig. 6 depict a broad emission band centered around 522 nm for CWS and CWS-rGO at an excitation wavelength of 400 nm. The addition of rGO to CWS significantly lowers the intensity of the emission band indicating a faster transfer of photoexcited electrons from the conduction band of CWS to rGO sheets, which has also been supported by the PEC studies. Furthermore, this quenching of the PL intensity with the incorporation of rGO suggests a low recombination of charge carriers in the binary composite compared to CWS.

The charge transfer process between CWS and rGO has been understood using time-resolved photoluminescence (TRPL) studies (Fig. 7). 4. Conclusions

The composite CWS-rGO photoanode was designed to improve the charge carrier separation and the photoelectrochemical performance of CWS. The weakened PL intensity of CWS with the incorporation of rGO shows the effective inhibition of the recombination of charge carriers. Moreover, the shorter average lifetime of the CWS-rGO composite obtained from the time-resolved fluorescence decay also reveals a superior charge separation and migration compared to bare CWS nanosheets. The electron transfer property was further evaluated by photoelectrochemical measurements, suggesting a higher photocurrent density of 1.18 mA cmâˆ’2 in the composite case. In addition, a better transient photocurrent response and smaller EIS radius of CWS-rGO corroborate the results obtained from PL and TRPL studies. Thus, adding an electron-conducting scaffold to CWS improves the charge transfer properties and shows a better photoelectrochemical performance.
