Recent advances in the Suzuki–Miyaura cross-coupling reaction using efficient catalysts in eco-friendly media

Abstract

The ever-increasing interest in the Suzuki–Miyaura cross-coupling reaction (SMR) and its applications, with more than 40 years of history, has increased exponentially in the last decade, which speaks volumes about its efficiency and effectiveness. This widely used powerful method provides a practical synthetic route for the direct formation of carbon–carbon bonds, which has found considerable academic and industrial use for the production of polymers, fine chemicals and materials, in addition to total synthesis and pharmaceuticals. Green chemistry predicates the use of environmentally-benign media in organic transformations and in recent years, catalytic systems have been introduced in diverse and neoteric green media for SMR. In view of the widespread popularity, there is the need for a comprehensive understanding of the various greener strategies introduced for this important reaction encompassing diverse catalytic systems based on palladium, nickel, gold, ferrites, copper, silver, and cobalt. The present review embodies literature from 2008 onward that covers the SMR using efficient catalysts in various green media.

1 Introduction

1.1 Green chemistry: a necessity

The new sustainability-focused development plan outlined by the United Nations in 2015 is entitled “Transforming Our World: The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development” and is comprised of 17 sustainable development goals that address a broad range of topics, mainly recognizing the need for green and sustainable chemistry and engineering.1 Achieving the objectives of this plan is vital for the future of the planet and its biological species, which include humans, plants, and animals. A significant challenge and barrier to attaining this goal is the selection of appropriate media and solvents for use in industrial, hospital and research centers; the study and development of eco-friendly media have been under active scrutiny in diverse scientific and commercial fields. The term green chemistry has been defined by IUPAC as follows: “the invention, design and application of chemical products and processes to reduce or to eliminate the use and generation of hazardous substances”.2 Importantly, an ideal green medium should be safe for both human beings and the environment and its use and production should adhere to the sustainability theme. From a synthetic chemist's viewpoint, this emphasis on environmental and health issues is leading to a paradigm shift in the design of greener approaches for the preparation of all molecules without applying any toxic solvents and damaging substances; some of the traditional solvents used earlier by researchers in academia and the industrial community have been blacklisted by international regulations; for example, chlorinated hydrocarbon solvents, benzene and some of its derivatives have been severely curtailed.3 Consequently, scientists and researchers have been ceaselessly trying to find appropriate sustainable replacements for expensive, toxic and environmentally damaging organic solvents. In this context, many efforts have been expanded during the last decade for accomplishing SMR in environmentally benign media. These remarkable developments to conduct this noteworthy and widely deployed cross-coupling reaction in green solvents such as water and ethanol (EtOH) as well as neoteric media like biomass-derived solvents, ionic liquids, deep eutectic solvents, and supercritical carbon dioxide, etc., has encouraged us to review this field.

1.2 The Suzuki–Miyaura cross-coupling reaction (SMR)

Transition-metal-mediated cross-couplings, namely, Suzuki–Miyaura, Hiyama, Stille, Heck, Negishi, Kumada, and Sonogashira reactions, are powerful tools for C–C bond formation.4–11 The C–C coupling reactions have become truly fundamental routes in natural product synthesis, materials science, biological-, medicinal-, and supramolecular chemistry, and also in catalysis, coordination chemistry and polymer synthesis.12–14 The Suzuki–Miyaura reaction has been heavily exploited in this decade, especially reactions that provide rapid entry to biaryl frameworks via sp2–sp2 linkages.15,16 This reaction is widely used due to attractive properties such as the mild reaction conditions, the commercial availability of an array of precursors, the functional-group compatibility, the high stabilities towards air and moisture, and the deployment of nontoxic and easily synthesized boronic acids. A recent survey of the literature reveals many examples of palladium (Pd), nickel (Ni) and gold (Au) complexes as efficient catalysts for the cross-coupling reactions;17,18 the award of the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for 2010 to Ei-ichi Negishi, Akira Suzuki and Richard Heck is testimony to their valuable contribution to this research field.19 Several reviews on the SMR have appeared in the literature,20–23 with the initial one being written by Suzuki and Miyaura in 1995. Today, numerous papers have emerged on the SMR in a truly explosive growth pattern comprising diverse fields; the frequency of publications from 1995 to 2017 is illustrated in Fig. 1, showing an upward trend of about 4369 articles published in addition to over 3506 patents.

2 Water

Water, a plentiful resource with an admirable specificity, is an abundant, safe and green medium and has been applied to an array of organic reactions in the last two decades.24 Water plays a dual role as medium and as a co-catalyst in quite a few of these convergent synthetic routes due to its high surface tension and hydrophobic nature, which underscore the tendency of nonpolar molecules or their surfaces to aggregate in water; Breslow's hydrophobic effect in-water emphasizes that for decreasing the exposed organic surface area, the substrates must be aggregated.25 On the other hand, there are some salient properties of water including a large cohesive energy density derived from some characteristics of water molecules such as the small size and high polarity, along with the three-dimensional (3D) hydrogen bond networks of water. Often, water plays an important role in the rate acceleration of reactions by establishing extensive hydrogen bonding with the functional groups of reactants and activating them in the process.26 In recent years, the use of water as a greener sustainable reaction medium has been under focus for diminishing environmental pollution,27–30 and has been applied in the SMR coupled with different support systems for a wide variety of nanoparticles (NPs) as catalysts.20,21,31 Substantial considerations have been extended to the aqueous and heterogeneous switching of organic transformations by amphiphilic block copolymer-supported transition metal complexes or metal NPs.32 The Suzuki–Miyaura reaction (SMR) has been carried out in water via assorted catalysts, accommodating not only hydrophilic substrates but also hydrophobic substituents in aqueous medium, affording high to excellent yields. The driving force for the hydrophobic guest molecules to diffuse onto the sphere is mainly ascribed to the hydrophobic–hydrophobic interactions in water. These ensuing good yields might be due to a combination of the hydrophobic effect of water and the nature of the support,33 and stabilization of leached palladium reactive species34 in water.

Karimi et al. reported the design, preparation and characterization of a water-dispersible Pd catalyst on a Fe3O4@SiO2 anchored hydrophilic triethylene glycol (TEG)-imidazolium IL (Mag-IL-Pd, 7) for the SMR (Scheme 3);36 the core–shell structure had a narrow size distribution, with the thickness of the iron oxide core and the silica shell being ∼10 and 5–7 nm, respectively. The products were acquired in good to superior yields with high turnover numbers (TONs) (185-4000) using a very low Pd loading (0.025–0.5 mol% of catalyst), which could be recycled 10 times without significant loss of activity and no apparent decrease in Pd content; nearly the same as the fresh catalyst (0.084 mmol Pd g−1). The TEG tags of functionalized magnetic NPs led to a hydrophilic nanocatalyst that provided an excellent dispersion of the catalyst in water. Furthermore, supported IL not only stabilizes the Pd NPs during the reaction but also provides an IL-based nano-environment on the surface of magnetic Pd to increase their interaction with aryl halides, thus improving the overall reaction efficiency.

N-heterocyclic carbenes (NHCs) have aroused considerable attention in recent years as possible alternative ligands for metal-catalyzed cross-coupling reactions,37 exemplified by N-substituted main-chain NHC-Pd polymers 11a–c (Scheme 4) as reusable catalytic systems for the SMR of chloroarenes (Scheme 5).38 The catalyst 11c, bearing the more lipophilic n-dodecyl group, was found to be more reactive (91% yield, with extremely low Pd loading (0.0005 mol%)) for reactions of both deactivated and hindered aryl chlorides 4 with various arylboronic acids 5, exclusively in aqueous media. The results demonstrate that the electronic effects do not significantly affect the isolated yields for electron-deficient and electron-rich aryl chlorides. The lifetime and recycling of the catalyst were investigated; the obtained data showed that catalyst 11a could be consistently applied in 6 consecutive coupling reactions of 4-chlorobenzaldehyde with phenylboronic acid to afford the product in an average yield of 92%. The same coupling model reaction in the presence of 11c (0.05 mol%) could be successfully recycled in seven reaction runs. While the obtained data clearly show that virtually no significant deactivation occurred during these runs, it was necessary to increase the reaction times after the 4th run to ensure proper conversion.

In another variation of the methodology, Liu et al. described a polyethylene glycol (PEG)-functionalized imidazolium salts-based Pd-catalyst for the cross-coupling of aryl bromides 4 and arylboronic acids 5 in the presence of 0.5 mol% Pd(OAc)2 at 100 °C (Scheme 7),39 wherein several water-soluble imidazolium salts bearing PEG moieties directly linked to an N-atom of imidazole could serve as NHC precursors 16a–c (Scheme 6). For comparison purposes, a kinetic study of the SMR was performed in the presence of Pd(OAc)2 and triethylamine (Et3N) at 80 °C in aqueous media; 16c displayed a relatively higher catalytic activity in a shorter induction period than catalysts 16a and 16b. These results show that increasing the “steric-bulk” of the ligand attached to the metal center is crucial for attaining the Pd-catalyzed SMR; Et3N is the ideal choice among the other bases such as morpholine, CH3CH2ONa, piperidine, 1,2-diaminocyclohexane, DMEDA (N,N′-dimethylethanediamine) and TMEDA (N,N,N′,N′-tetramethylethanediamine). The superior performance of Et3N is presumably due to its inherent tendency to re-coordinate with the active NHC-Pd groups, thus elongating the lifetime in solution.40

Zhang et al. reported the SMR within the core–corona nanoreactor of poly(N-isopropylacrylamide)-grafted Pd NPs (Pd@PNIPAM) 18 with average size of ∼3 nm, which were synthesized by covalently grafting the thiol-terminated PNIPAM brushes to Pd NPs, in water (Scheme 8).41 The Pd NPs as core and poly(N-isopropylacrylamide) brushes as the corona provided the nanoenvironment to the guest molecules for the SMR. Both hydrophilic and hydrophobic reactants can be enriched in the nanoenvironment of 18 (Scheme 9). The hydrophilic substrates can be entrapped in the nanoreactor of PNIPAM brushes due to the strong hydrogen bonding at room temperature and therefore the SMR within the nanoreactor is carried out in water. For hydrophobic reactants, the substrates can diffuse into the nanoenvironment of 18 and the SMR is carried out efficiently above the phase-transition temperature of the nanoenvironment of the PNIPAM brushes. Furthermore, the catalyst can be reused due to the reversible phase transition of the PNIPAM brushes. At room temperature, the PNIPAM brushes are soluble in water and the 18 nanoreactor exists as a colloid; when reactants such as benzeneboronic acid, 4-bromophenol and K2CO3 are added and the temperature is increased above the phase-transition temperature, the PNIPAM brushes become insoluble and collapse onto the Pd NPs, rendering 18 as a fine flocculent suspension. After completion of the SMR, the organic compounds are extracted into the organic solvent phase and the catalyst 18 NPs, which exist as a fine flocculent suspension, remain in the aqueous phase; the suspended catalyst is then carefully collected and reused in next run of the SMR.

The reduction of Pd NPs occurs during the dehydratation step (Scheme 10). The virgin catalyst system is comprised of Pd spheroidal NPs whose average size is 3.4 ± 1.4 nm, and the material recovered after eight runs showed the NPs to be about 3.6 ± 1.7 nm in diameter. The catalyst could be reused several times without significant loss of activity, with the cumulated TON over eight runs being 68400.

The immobilization of Pd NPs directly onto various supports such as carbon,43 zeolites,44 silica,45 and polymers,46 and their application in cross-coupling reactions in green media has been emphasized lately due to the ever-intensifying demand for cleaner and sustainable chemistry; the choice of supports is shifting from petrochemical-based feedstocks to environmentally safe materials. Bacterial cellulose (BC) is an example of such a support due to its eco-friendly properties, possessing a large surface area and stable heterogeneous interface for developing semi-heterogeneous catalysts.47–49 In this context, Sun et al. introduced bio-supported Pd NPs (Pd/BC) as a phosphine-free catalyst for the SMR (Scheme 12)50via one-pot synthesis by simply mixing Pd(NO3)2 (or PdCl2) and the reducing reagent with BC nanofibres in a hydrothermal reaction; the ensuing Pd NPs are comprised of small polydisperse islands with dimension of about 20 nm on BC nanofibers. Higher yields of the products were attained in water in the presence of 0.05 mol% recyclable Pd/BC catalyst, with yields over 86% after five cycles; the Pd concentration was found to be 5.29% before reaction and 5.26% after five reaction cycles.


Scheme 12 The SMR of aryl halides 4 and arylboronic acids 5 by 0.05 mol% Pd/BC catalyst in water.

Pitchumani et al. reported the preparation and function of an ionic Pd(II) complex stabilized using water-soluble pyridinium-modified β-cyclodextrin (Pyr:β-CD) (supramolecular ligand) as an efficient catalyst for the SMR;51 this homogeneous catalyst is very soluble in water as compared to native β-CD and could be reused more than six times without significant loss of catalytic activity. The substrate scope, under the optimized conditions, was explored for a range of diverse 4-substituted aryl bromides, 4, containing both electron-withdrawing and electron-donating groups, which all furnished products 6 in good yields in a short time, with the exception of nitro-group substitution (0.5 mol% Pd(OAc)2 at 80 °C in 2 mL H2O) (Scheme 13).

Zou et al. developed the Pd@MIL-101Cr-NH2 catalyst via the immobilization of Pd NPs on an amino-functionalized metal–organic framework (MOF);52 four catalysts with different loadings of Pd NPs were introduced (denoted as 4-, 8-, 12-, and 16 wt% Pd@MIL-101Cr-NH2). The catalytic activity of these catalysts was evaluated for the SMR, with the best catalytic performance being achieved with the MOF that contained 8 wt% Pd NPs (Scheme 14). In recent years, oxygen-equilibrated aqueous solutions of Kolliphor EL, a well-known surfactant, have been noted to form nanomicelles with oxygen-free cores that provided an ideal green core environment for Pd-catalyzed cross-coupling reactions.53 The Lipshutz group recently showed that the high solubility of oxygen in an organic medium, including micelles, can be beneficially applied to perform the aerobic oxidation of water-insoluble arylalkynes to β-ketosulfones under mild conditions. In 2017, Beverina et al. showed that such micelles provide an appropriate medium for the SMR in an oxygenated environment at room temperature;54 Kolliphor EL can be used for the synthesis of popular molecular semiconductors relevant to the classes of diketopyrrolopyrroles,55,56 diarylanthracenes,57 isoindigos,58 diarylbenzothiadiazoles,59 and perylenediimides.60–63 The scope of the reaction is indicated by the coupling of aryl and heteroaryl bromides with boronic acids and esters. Huo et al. introduced a fullerene-supported PdCl2 [C60-TEGS/PdCl2] 30 as a water-soluble nanocatalyst via the coordination of water-soluble fullerene NPs 29 with PdCl2; the average particle sizes of 29 and 30 were 42.5 nm and 61.5 nm, respectively (Scheme 18).65 Both the electron-donating and electron-withdrawing groups of aryl halides 4 were coupled with phenylboronic acid 5, and the ensuing biphenyl compounds 6 could be obtained in excellent yields using 0.01 mol% of the catalyst at room temperature (Scheme 19); 4-chloroacetophenone was coupled using 0.5 mol% catalyst to afford 65% yield and the catalyst could be reused five times without significant loss in its catalytic activity. A heterogeneous biopolymer complex, wool-based Pd catalyst, has been reported for the SMR in water.66 Wool is an inexpensive natural biopolymer comprising amino acids cross-linked via S–S bonds, the main component being keratin. This biodegradable biopolymer, bearing numerous amino acids units with functional groups such as –NH–CO–, –NH2 and –S–S– etc.,67 serves as a green support for facile reactions and the broad scope of these reactions with respect to both coupling partners has been explored.

2.2 Gold/palladium NPs

Jutand et al. described the homogeneous origin of Au–Pd core–shell NPs in aqueous SMR through Pd leaching.68 The presence of the Au core allowed the careful evaluation of the surface status of the Pd NPs after a catalytic reaction using surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy (SERS); Au@Pd NPs turned out to be efficient catalysts for the SMR, where even a sluggish reagent69 reacted readily in water at room temperature with an average yield of 95% in roughly 4 h (Scheme 20).

2.3 Fe-ppm Pd NPs

Most of today's applications of transition metal-catalyzed cross-coupling chemistry rely on the expensive Pd. However, Lipshutz et al. in 2015 demonstrated that NPs from inexpensive iron(III) chloride (FeCl3), which naturally contains ppm levels of Pd,70 can be used directly for the SMR. Using several control reactions, it was clearly shown that successful couplings require the presence of both FeCl3 and Pd within these nanocomposites. All reactions were carried out using 2-dicyclohexylphosphino-2,6-dimethoxybiphenyl (SPhos) as a ligand in various ratios. The reduction of 5 mol% of pure FeCl3 with 10 mol% of MeMgCl in the presence of 5 mol% SPhos and 320 ppm Pd(OAc)2 generated a superior nanocatalyst that mediated the formation of the desired coupled product 6 in 95% isolated yield (Scheme 21). Doping pure FeCl3 with 500 ppm of other metals, such as Ni(acac)2, NiCl2, MnCl2, CoCl3, Cu(OAc)2 or CuBr2 produced catalysts that generated variable levels of product; in all cases, the yields were ≤38%.

2.4 Cu-ppm Pd NPs

The same research group reported copper-catalyzed Suzuki–Miyaura reactions under remarkably mild conditions, wherein ppm levels of Pd(OAc)2 worked synergistically with Cu catalyst to generate biaryls in the absence of organic solvent;71 the investment of significant energy in the form of typically harsh reaction temperatures could thus be eliminated. A new P(O),N-ligand generated in situ from a readily available precursor has been uncovered that allows couplings to occur with inexpensive Cu(OAc)2. The entire aqueous system containing nanomicelles as the reaction vessels, base, copper and palladium can be recycled, further attesting to the eco-friendly nature of this technology.

2.5 Nickel NPs

Nickel nanoparticles, formed in situ and used in combination with micellar catalysis, catalyze the SMR under mild aqueous reaction conditions;72 the proper choice of ligand on the metal, complementing the hydrophobic characteristics of aqueous nanomicelles achieves the objective. This newer technological development also showcases broader substrate scope, essentially stoichiometric levels of reaction partners, considerable flexibility in the choice of both leaving group and source of boron, very mild reaction conditions and notably, the option for recycling of the aqueous medium containing both the surfactant and the catalyst.

2.6 Ferrite NPs

The potential application of the glutathione-based organocatalyst, having uniform-sized particles with cube-like morphology with an average size range of 10–15 nm, was introduced in the homocoupling of aryl boronic acids 5 under the microwave (MW)-assisted aqueous synthesis of symmetric biaryls 6 by Varma and co-workers (Scheme 22).73 A wide range of substituents, regardless of their electron-withdrawing or donating nature, was accommodated and the catalyst was found to be reusable after 4 cycles; easy recovery from the reaction mixture was facilitated using an external magnet. The firm anchoring of the glutathione on the ferrite support via the thiol group is responsible for achieving the high reusability of the catalyst.

2.7 Gold NPs

Although the chosen catalyst for the SMR is invariably Pd NPs, Au(I) complexes can also be applied as efficient alternatives.74 Au NPs have gained significant prominence in catalytic applications75–77 as exemplified by the facile synthesis of highly poly(2-aminothiophenol) (PAPT)-stabilized Au NPs78 applied for the SMR. The proposed mechanism of Au NPs use in SMR may follow a homogeneous route, as the leaching of Au was observed in the first half-hour of the coupling reaction (5.5% of the initial catalyst charge).79 Importantly, the precise control over the size of Au NPs and the amount of PATP stabilizer are crucial for improving the catalytic activities of Au NPs; an increase in size from 1.0 to 2.0 and 5.0 nm decreased the yield from 87% to 76% and 10%, respectively. On the other hand, when the size of Au NPs was maintained at 1.0 nm but with considerably more stabilizer added, only a trace amount of product was formed. It is known that the smaller size of Au NPs will lead to higher catalytic activity, while the effect of polymer amount on the Au activity may be attributed to the fact that the PAPT polymer may act as a catalyst inhibitor. With only 0.05 mol% of Au catalyst loading, high yields of products from substituted aryl chlorides 4 and arylboronic acids 5 were attained (Scheme 23) and the recovered Au catalyst, by simple filtration, retained its activity without significant loss over six cycles, with yield loss being within 3% for six cycles.

The preparation of Au NPs and graphene oxide (GO)-incorporated strontium ion cross-linked alginate/carboxymethylcellulose (Sr/Alg/CMC/GO/Au) composites was accomplished using dissipative convective procedures that were followed by the freeze-drying method (Scheme 24). In the case of the Sr/Alg/CMC composite, capillary formation in the range of 50–80 μm with interconnected pores having a diameter of about 5–20 μm was observed. Incorporation of Au-NPs into the alginate/CMC composites resulted in organized capillaries of 25–50 μm with interconnected pores around 5–30 μm. Moreover, the Sr/Alg/CMC/GO/Au composite gel revealed capillaries and pores with diameter around 10–15 μm and 5–20 μm, respectively; its application for the SMR required only 0.005 mol% catalyst (Scheme 25).80 Higher yields were obtained in the case of substituted aryl iodides, while substituted aryl chlorides gave a lower yield. The composites displayed 6 cycles of recyclability and 98% isolated yield, the turnover frequency (TOF) value being 4900 h−1; ICP-MS analysis did not show any leaching of Sr2+-ions.

2.8 Cobalt

In recent years, considerable attention has been focused on Pd-free reactions as the awareness sets in that transition metal catalysts such as Ni,81 Cu,82 Fe83 and Co,84,85 may serve as substitutes for air-sensitive and expensive precious metals such as Pd. Although Co-catalyzed reactions have been relatively rare,86,87 they have attracted attention in organic synthesis owing to their superior reactivity for various C–C bond forming reactions in view of nontoxicity, low cost, accessibility and their interesting mode of action;88,89 both homogeneous and heterogeneous Co-based catalysts in combination with various ligands have improved the cross-coupling reactions.90–94 Akhlaghinia et al. introduced a green magnetic catalyst based on Fe3O4@Boehmite NPs 35 of very small size; the immobilization of Co(II) was accomplished via the reaction of Fe3O4@Boehmite-NH239 with ethanolic solution of CoCl2·6H2O (Scheme 26) and the ensuing catalyst used for SMR.95 Boehmite NPs 32, a component of the aluminum ore, bauxite, with octahedral double sheets centered with aluminum ions and extra hydroxyl groups on their outer surface, provide the ability for immobilization of metal ions.96 The particle sizes of the resultant Fe3O4@Boehmite-NH2-CoII NPs 40, in irregular geometric shape, were about 13–54 nm. Under the optimized reaction conditions, a variety of structurally divergent aryl halides 4 reacted with phenylboronic acid 5 to produce the desired coupling products 6 (Scheme 27) and the superior catalytic activity of the new nanocatalyst was not significantly decreased even after seven recycle runs. The hot filtration test demonstrated that a negligible amount of active species (less than 0.31 mol% by ICP-OES analysis) was leached out during the catalytic reaction.

The selection of an appropriate solvent or solvent mixture as the reaction medium is of paramount importance besides task-specific reaction catalysts and energy requirements. 3.2 Copper/palladium

A Suzuki-type C–H arylation of azoles was accomplished using Pd(OAc)2, Cu(OAc)2 and CuCl101 and the beneficial effect of CuCl was described in the Pd(OAc)2 catalyzed SMR with 2-heterocyclic boronates.102 Liebeskind et al. introduced a Pd catalyzed, Cu-mediated route for the reaction of aryl- and alkenyl halides and arylboronic acids with Cu(I) thiophene-2-carboxylate as a mediator in the absence of a base.103 Alginate-based nanocatalysts have been used for the C–C bond formation via a procedure that might be applied for gelling many metals Mn+ (Ca, Ba, Mn, Zn, Ni, Ce, Cu, and Co), allowing the straightforward preparation of a panel of alginate/Mn+/Pd NPs gels (Scheme 32).104 The main influence of the gelling metal nature on the surface areas of alginate/Mn+/Pd NPs aerogels has been disclosed. Carbon quantum dots (CQDs) make an interesting choice for supporting metals because of their high surface area, chemical stability and inertness.114 This was exemplified by the synergistically active bimetallic Pd–Ag hybrid NPs supported on CQDs (Pd-Ag@CQD) 45, which were formed by an expeditious UV-light-driven (365 nm) one-step wet chemical protocol via the in situ reduction of Pd(II) and Ag(I) ions (Scheme 40);115 CQDs served as both stabilizing agent and reducing agent. Today, poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) oligomers are efficient, eco-friendly and low-volatility liquids and are organic compounds with a broad spectrum of chemical, industrial and medical applications,132 although explored and produced independently in 1859 by Wurtz and Laurence.133 PEGs are readily miscible with water and polar organic solvents, but immiscible with non-polar organic solvents, aliphatic hydrocarbons and supercritical CO2.134 In recent years, relatively inexpensive PEGs have been widely used as greener media in several organic reactions as they are nearly harmless, being routinely used in drug formulations. 7 Ionic liquids

Ionic liquids (IL) are increasingly being used as popular media in a diverse range of scientific and technical applications; the first ionic liquid, ethylammonium nitrate, was described by Walden in 1914. The discovery of this new class of media did not have the expected impact until 1982 when dialkylimidazolium chloroaluminate melts were introduced by Wilkes and co-workers. A decade later, the new generation of air-stable 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium-based ILs was reported, thus allowing the successful use of ILs147 with unique task-specific features. 8 Deep eutectic solvents

Deep eutectic solvents (DES), introduced initially by Abbott et al., are important alternatives to room temperature ionic liquids (RTIL), due to their interesting properties and benefits such as low cost, ease of synthetic accessibility, benign and safe nature along with renewability and biodegradability.157 In general, DES are prepared via the complexation of quaternary ammonium salts as hydrogen-bond acceptors with a hydrogen bond donor such as urea, carboxylic acids, amino acids, sugars, and glycerol or M2+ ions such as Zn2+.158 The charge delocalization occurs via hydrogen bonding between the electronegative component, such as a halide ion, and the hydrogen-donor moiety, which is responsible for the decrease in the melting points of the mixture relative to the melting points of the individual components. One of the most characteristic properties of DESs is their high viscosity due to the presence of an extensive hydrogen-bonding network, relatively large ion sizes, and electrostatic forces within the liquid.159 Furthermore, they possess negligible volatility, a broader liquid temperature range than conventional molecular solvents, and are generally non-flammable. DESs as green media have been used for many chemical processes160,161 such as creating alloys, extraction, surfactant chemistry, electrochemistry, polymerization, gas adsorption, biomass processing, nanotechnology and various organic and inorganic transformations.162–164 The first reported use of DES in the SMR was described by König and co-workers when they synthesized biaryls with the Pd-catalyzed cross-coupling of aryl halides with boronic acids in a mannitol–DMU–NH4Cl mixture; phenylboronic acid 5 was coupled with aryl bromides 4 in the DES at 90 °C using 10 mol% of Pd(OAc)2 as a catalyst and 1.2 equiv. of Na2CO3 as a base, resulting in the nearly quantitative conversion of the starting materials after 6 h (Scheme 54).165

9 Supercritical carbon dioxide

Supercritical or liquid carbon dioxide (compressed CO2) has attracted enormous interest as an efficient and green alternative to many hazardous organic solvents. Supercritical CO2 is an abundant, cheap, nontoxic and non-flammable reagent that is readily available and inexpensive. Carbon dioxide emissions due to human activities, especially in densely populated areas, have greatly increased over the past century. With increasing awareness of its role in climate change, there is an ever increasing interest in finding applications for the captured carbon dioxide.167 At room temperature and pressure, CO2 is gaseous in nature, which facilitates the recovery of the analyte and provides solvent-free analytes. CO2, being a linear and small molecule, diffuses much faster than other conventional solvents168 and has been used to replace harmful Freons and certain organic solvents. Supercritical CO2 (scCO2) has ideal properties for the extraction of non-polar compounds, for example hydrocarbons, while its large quadrupole moment also enables it to dissolve some polar compounds such as aldehydes, ketones, alcohols, and esters.169 For the first time, Holmes and co-workers reported the application of scCO2 as a solvent in carbon–carbon bond formation via the Pd-catalyzed SMR,170 followed by Feng et al. who reported the use of SBA-15-supported Pd catalyst for the SMR in scCO2.171 The use of supercritical CO2 as an eco-friendly medium not only preserves the simplicity but also prevents the leaching of the Pd metal from their support. Furthermore, to expand the repertoire of the new synthetic methodology using scCO2, Wang and co-workers explored an environmentally benign and efficient access to C–C bonds in good to high yields, which comprised the use of Pd2(dba)3 under [hmim]NTf247 and a supercritical CO2 biphasic system employing K2CO3 as a base (Scheme 56).
Scheme 56 The SMR under the [hmim]NTf2/supercritical CO2 biphasic system.

The combination of scCO2 and ILs may provide a unique synthesis strategy in which reaction and separation can occur consecutively. Owing to the strong electrostatic forces between cations and anions, scCO2 dissolves well in ionic liquids, but ILs actually do not dissolve in supercritical CO2.172 This catalytic system has several advantages such as operational simplicity, ease of isolation of products, and high yields. The catalytic system could be easily reused for up to six consecutive cycles without noticeable loss in activity. A plausible mechanism for this reaction is shown in Scheme 57. Initially, Pd2(dba)3 can react with ionic liquid [hmim]NTf2 to form the metal carbene complexes Pd (NHC) NTf2, followed by the oxidative addition of bromobenzene 4 to Pd (NHC) NTf2 to obtain the aryl palladium intermediate A. Phenylboronic acid 5, activated by K2CO3, reacts with intermediate A, which generates the intermediate B upon transmetallation. Finally, the reductive elimination of intermediate B produces the desired product 6, which was separated from the reaction mixture by scCO2 extraction during the course of the reaction.

10 Biomass-derived solvents

Nature provides us with a generous supply of molecules that can be applied as media either directly or after purification or processing (valorization). In addition to the aforementioned water, pioneering investigations are currently underway exploiting the biomass-derived solvents as green and plentiful resources. Biomass-derived solvents, which satisfy the seventh principle of the green chemistry protocol, are emerging as favourable alternatives as feedstock and renewable resources for solvent production. This has been a burgeoning field of interest within the fields of green and sustainable chemistry and the following section describes several biomass-derived solvents that have been used in the SMR, e.g., glycerol, 2-methyltetrahydrofuran (2-MeTHF), ethyl lactate and Cyrene, etc.

10.1 Glycerol

The etymology of the word ‘glycerol’ is from the Greek word ‘glykeros’ which means ‘sweet’. Since its discovery in 1779 by the Swedish chemist Carl Scheele, the polyol has been used in several disciplines.173 Glycerol, also known as glycerin, or as 1,2,3-propanetriol, is an environmentally benign, hygroscopic, colorless, and sweet-tasting viscous liquid that is the principal by-product of the biodiesel industry; 100 kg of glycerol is produced for every ton of biodiesel. Glycerol finds use in various fields such as the pharmaceutical, cosmetic, and food industries, where it is mainly employed as a humectant, thickener, lubricant, sweetener or anti-freeze, besides being a green medium for a wide range of chemical processes. This eco-friendly medium can dissolve a lot of organic and inorganic substances, including transition-metal complexes, with several advantages of high efficiency, low cost, biodegradability, non-toxic nature (does not metabolize to toxic products, unlike ethylene glycol) and nonflammability. In addition, its high boiling point enables the conduction of reactions at elevated temperatures without losses via evaporation.174 In this regard, glycerol as an effective medium has been used in many organic syntheses as well as various cross-coupling reactions,175,176 particularly for the SMR where the glycerol was initially deployed as an efficient medium in 2007 when Wolfson and co-workers reported their results with several aryl halides.177 Gómez et al. prepared Pd NPs stabilized by tris(3-sulfophenyl)phosphine trisodium salt (TPPTS) in glycerol, starting from Pd(II) and Pd(0) precursors and other reactants; this nanocatalyst was used for the SMR in glycerol and t-BuOK as a base at 100 °C with excellent yields.178 Also, Cravotto et al. demonstrated the use of microwave (MW) radiation and ultrasound (US) energy and combinations thereof (MW/US), instead of conventional heating to produce C–C bond formation in glycerol; Pd salt or the corresponding amount of solid catalysts (5% Pd/C or Pd-cross-linked chitosan) were used.179 The use of alternative energy input systems, such as US and MW as substitutes for conventional heating and classical methods for a variety of chemical processes, has received noticeable attention in the past two decades.118,120,180 US-promoted SMR in glycerol has been reported under mild conditions (Scheme 58)181 and four new Pd N-heterocyclic-carbene (NHC)-based complexes were prepared with 3,4,5-trimethoxybenzyl, alkyl and sulfonate N-substituents. The catalytic activity of catalysts was investigated in the synthesis of various biphenyls in a short reaction time at 40 °C; aryl iodides and bromides 4 reacted successfully with arylboronic acids, naphthalene-1-boronic acid or potassium phenyltrifluoroboronate 5 with good to excellent yields.
Scheme 58 The SMR process in glycerol under ultrasound irradiation.

Recently, Grison et al. disclosed the usefulness of Eichhornia crassipes, commonly known as water hyacinth, to prepare recyclable EcoPd.182 Based on the natural ability of Eichhornia crassipes to bioconcentrate Pd in its roots, the authors reported the transformation of plant-derived Pd metals as eco-friendly catalysts. This biobased EcoPd has been used for the SMR of various heteroaryl rings with good yields and enabled the expeditious synthesis of a broad spectrum of heterocyclic biaryl and heterocyclic-heterocyclic biaryl scaffolds, in the presence of a small amount of catalyst in glycerol and in the absence of ligands or any additives. Nagarkar and co-workers reported the Ni-catalyzed SMR of phenylboronic acid 5 with various aryl diazonium salts 22 in glycerol with good to excellent yield without using any base (Scheme 59);183 this greener protocol can be successfully repeated for up to five consecutive cycles with no considerable loss in catalytic activity.

10.2 2-Methyltetrahydrofuran (2-MeTHF)

2-Methyltetrahydrofuran (2-MeTHF) is accessible from natural resources from biomass-derived compounds such as furfural or levulinic acid and is a good alternative medium for the development of green synthetic methodologies. Its chemical and physical properties, namely its low miscibility with water, boiling point, significant stability compared to other cyclic-based media such as THF, 1,4-dioxane and others, renders it more attractive for processes involving olefin metathesis,184 organocatalysis, and organometallics.185 Also, 2-MeTHF being an environmentally benign and harmless medium, may be used in the pharmaceutical chemical process.186 Garg and co-workers reported an efficient and inspiring concise synthesis of various biaryls 6 using boronic acid derivatives 5 and aryl halides 4 or phenol-derived substrates 48via the Ni-catalyzed SMR in 2-MeTHF (Scheme 60);187 1–10 mol% of commercially available bis(tricyclohexylphosphine)nickel(II) dichloride NiCl2(PCy3)2 complex was used as an efficient catalyst for this reaction. Additionally, several bis(heterocyclic) frameworks are easily accessible and so is the potential for these cross-couplings to be performed for the preparative gram scale assembly of bis-heterocyclic frameworks with low catalyst loadings.
Scheme 60 Ni-catalyzed SMR in 2-MeTHF.

The impressive influence of the biosolvent, 2-MeTHF, in the SMR was demonstrated by the Bora research group, where arylboronic acids 5 were allowed to react with acid chlorides 49 to produce aryl ketones 51 using 0.4 mol% of oxime-palladacycle 50 as a catalyst in excellent yield in open air (Scheme 61).188 Gratifyingly, the reaction under a N2 atmosphere provides a slightly lower yield of the coupling product. This suggests that the presence of dissolved air in 2-MeTHF accelerates the catalytic cycle by enhancing the rate of activation of arylboronic acid to aryl ion species, vital for the catalytic transmetalation step. A wide variety of aromatic acid chlorides have been applied in this catalytic system but aliphatic acyl chlorides were not reactive under otherwise identical reaction conditions. The salient advantageous feature of this approach is the application of water-immiscible 2-MeTHF as the medium, which provides convenient isolation of the crude reaction mixture via the separation of 2-MeTHF–water layers, followed by the evaporation of 2-MeTHF. Moreover, oxime-palladacycles are stable to heat, air and moisture, and are easily accessible from low-cost starting materials.


Scheme 61 Synthesis of aryl ketones 51 in 2-MeTHF.

The mechanism proposed by the authors shows that initially, Pd(0) is formed by the reduction of the Pd–C bond of oxime-palladacycle 50 and then the oxidative addition of acid chloride 49 to Pd(0) produces the acylpalladium(II) chloride intermediate A. Under alkaline conditions, the subsequent transmetallation of ArB(OH)25 to the acylpalladium intermediate A occurs at the Pd–Cl bond to generate (acyl)(aryl)palladium(II) species B, which upon reductive elimination delivers the oxidative coupling product 51; the active Pd(0) species were regenerated and the catalytic cycle resumed (Scheme 62).

10.3 Ethyl lactate

Ethyl lactate, a lactic acid ethyl ester, is an important monobasic ester that is found naturally in small quantities in a wide range of foods including wine, chicken, and some fruits. Its industrial preparation, in the form of a racemic mixture, occurs via a reversible reaction between lactic acid and ethanol. It is a slightly yellowish liquid, has a pleasant odor and is a suitable solvent for many organic reactions due to attractive properties such as nontoxicity, high solvency power, low vapour pressure, high boiling point (154 °C), and low surface tension. Furthermore, ethyl lactate as a sustainable medium has been used in the pharmaceutical and cosmetic industries.189 Wan and co-workers used ethyl lactate as a solvent in the SMR190 where the reaction occurred at 60 °C with Pd(OAc)2 as a catalyst and K2CO3 as a base in the mixture of H2O/ethyl lactate under ligand-free conditions. Later on, the Chalker research group developed another system that used pure ethyl lactate for the SMR (Scheme 63),191 where a melamine–Pd complex 53 was used as an effective catalyst to provide direct access to biaryl 6 structures in high yields. Furthermore, this catalyst is soluble and active in common tap water. Next, the melamine–palladium catalyst can also be cross-linked by reaction with formaldehyde to generate an insoluble polymeric catalyst that can be recovered after cross-coupling. The melamine–palladium system is inexpensive, easy to handle, bench-stable and effective in catalysis in the presence of a variety of impurities. The authors, via this melamine–palladium system, could successfully synthesize Felbinac, a topical anti-inflammatory drug, on a gram-scale in a single step in nearly quantitative yield using unfiltered Arkansas River water as the solvent.
Scheme 63 The SMR catalyzed by melamine–Pd complex 53 in pure ethyl lactate.

10.4 Cyrene

Dihydrolevoglucosenone (Cyrene) 54, obtainable from cellulose in two steps, has been demonstrated to have physical properties similar to dimethylformamide (DMF) and other dipolar aprotic solvents and, so far, has not shown any adverse effects. In 2018, Watson et al. reported a greener route for the SMR, which employs 54 as an eco-friendly and efficient alternative to conventional solvents such as DMF, THF, and 1,4-dioxane (Scheme 64);192 this benign method offers simplicity and excellent functional group tolerance and generality with appropriate yields in both small and larger scale synthesis.

11 Solvent-free conditions

According to the principles of green chemistry and addressing sustainable development from both economic and environmental perspectives, organic reactions under solvent-free conditions have garnered much attention in the recent years in an attempt to curb the production of pollutants, reduce the utilization of or eliminate hazardous and expensive organic solvents, as well as operational simplicity. The solvent-free synthetic route is in compliance with the philosophy of green chemistry since that can improve the E-factor (defined as the ratio of the weight of waste to the weight of product) and the process mass intensity, defined as the total mass of materials used to produce a specified mass of product.193 Solvent-free reactions have gained considerable attention as green methodologies that are alternatives to the traditional procedures,194 often using supported reagents under MW irradiation conditions.195 These include grinding methods (performance of the reaction in standard glassware using a magnetic stirring bar for mixing) and high-speed ball milling.196 in recent years, the SMR has been conducted under solventless conditions.197,198 Schneider et al. demonstrated a greener protocol for solvent-free SMR in the presence of an inorganic solid support (KF–Al2O3) and the development of alternative energy forms such as mechanochemical treatment (grinding with mortar and pestle and ball milling) and MW irradiation (Scheme 65).199
Scheme 65 Solvent-free synthesis of biphenyl 6 under MW and ball-milling.

Tolerance toward atmospheric oxygen, desirable safety aspects, higher yields and shorter reaction times make this strategy a facile and attractive version of the SMR. Very recently in 2018, Baran et al. reported a magnetically recoverable sporopollenin-based Pd catalyst for the direct synthesis of biaryl compounds via SMR with high turnover frequency and turnover numbers;200 the recyclability of the catalyst was inspected to ten consecutive cycles for the synthesis of biaryls with only minor decreases in the reaction yields from 99 to 93%. In an interesting work, Borchardt et al. described the mechanochemical Suzuki polycondensation as a greener and more effective alternative to the conventional media-based polymerization transformation of poly(phenylene)s.201 Under optimized conditions, this synthetic route can produce linear polymers with an outstanding degree of polymerization, under ligand-free conditions in 30 minutes. Furthermore, the strategy was broadened to a hyperbranched system with high-temperature resistance in moderate to good yields in a short span of time.

12 Conclusions and future prospects

After nearly three decades of enthusiastic green chemistry activity in the face of increasing worldwide pollution and the global warming crisis, green and sustainable protocols have turned out to be commonplace strategies in every aspect of human activities both in academia and industry. In this regard, comprehensive discussion and analyses of recent advances in cleaner approaches will help us to gain better insight into the holistic aspects of green metrics. This article spotlights the most relevant advances in eco-friendly and environmentally-benign media for the synthesis of organic compounds via the SMR as a key step and a practical approach to total synthesis and organic transformations. A wide spectrum of homogeneous and heterogeneous catalytic systems, especially nanocatalysts with metals such as palladium, nickel, gold, ferrite, copper, silver, and cobalt for the SMR have been summarized. Diverse classes of heterogeneous catalysts based on graphene, zeolites, Fe3O4, carbon quantum dots, cellulose, wool, polymers, MCM-41, and chitosan, etc., have shown their usefulness in friendly vehicles ranging from water, ethanol, glycerol, PEG, and ionic liquids to newfound media such as DES, aqueous extracts, supercritical carbon dioxide, and biomass-derived solvents such as ethyl lactate, Cyrene etc. Notably, in all the aforementioned efforts, the researchers have tried to improve the important outcomes such as yield, time, amount of catalyst, TON, TOF and generality with a wide substrate scope. It is important to evaluate the number of effective catalysts used and their prowess under both homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysis, which may differ as has been shown very recently.202 In the near future, the emergence of the neoteric biocompatible catalytic systems for SMR will prompt the utilization of catalysts supported on biomass such as sugarcane bagasse, silk, carrageenan, casein, pullulan, hyaluronan, glucan as well as nanocomposite materials based on the chitosan, cellulose and alginate. Furthermore, the option to explore a combination of alternative energy output systems such as microwave, ultrasound and mechanochemical bill-mill operation or solar and visible light-assisted reactions in conjunction with renewable biomass-derived greener media such as Cyrene, ethyl lactate or γ-valerolactone (GVL) present exciting opportunities. In addition, the predominant use of earth-abundant base metals that are cheaper and safer should be examined for this protocol, which will open new avenues. Moreover, the newer developments in flow chemistry as a key enabling technology along with greener media described in this review can forge an effective alliance in this cross-coupling reaction, capitalizing on the use of newer substrates other than aryl halides. Further improvements in benign by design methodologies are expected in the coming decades and hopefully, this review will stimulate the design of more innovative, efficient, biocompatible and biodegradable, non-metallic systems to help circumvent any limitations of this widely-used coupling protocol.
