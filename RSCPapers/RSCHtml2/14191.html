Abstract

Developing efficient noble metal-free multifunctional electrocatalysts is highly effective to dramatically reduce the overall cost of electrochemical devices. In this work, we demonstrate for the first time a facile strategy for in situ coupling of ultrafine Co0.85Se nanocrystals and N-doped carbon (Co0.85Se@NC) by directly selenizing zeolitic imidazolate framework-67 (ZIF-67) polyhedra. Benefiting from the synergistic effect of the coupling between Co0.85Se and NC, the Coâ€“Nâ€“C structure, and the porous conductive carbon network, Co0.85Se@NC affords excellent oxygen evolution reaction (OER) performance with a small overpotential, remarkable stability, and high faradaic efficiency. Furthermore, Co0.85Se@NC can also efficiently catalyze hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), and we therefore investigated its applications as a trifunctional electrocatalyst for overall water splitting and Znâ€“air batteries. When used as both the anode and cathode for overall water splitting, a low cell voltage of 1.76 V is sufficient to reach the current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2; the obtained Znâ€“air batteries exhibit a very low dischargeâ€“charge voltage gap (0.80 V at 10 mA cmâˆ’2) and long cycle life (up to 180 cycles). These results not only demonstrate a facile strategy for the synthesis of affordable Co0.85Se@NC but also present its huge potential as a trifunctional electrocatalyst for clean energy systems.

Introduction

Growing global energy consumption and associated environmental issues have motivated considerable interest in clean energy systems, such as water splitting devices, rechargeable metalâ€“air batteries, and fuel cells. The electrocatalytic reactions involved in these energy systems [e.g. hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), especially core oxygen evolution reaction (OER)] all require high-performance electrocatalysts to promote their commercial applications.1â€“3 Nowadays the state-of-the-art electrocatalysts for OER, HER, and ORR are still noble metals, such as Ir, Ru, Pt and so on.4â€“6 However, the prohibitive costs and scarcity of these noble metals seriously hinder their large-scale commercial use. Therefore, much effort has been devoted to searching for noble metal-free electrocatalysts with performances comparable to those of the noble metals. Recently, cobalt selenides, as a type of highly active cobalt (Co)-based electrocatalysts,3,7â€“16 have attracted considerable attention due to their low cost, environmental friendliness, and high thermal and chemical stability. A variety of strategies have been developed to improve their performance. For instance, Xie's group designed and prepared vacancy-rich ultrathin CoSe2 nanosheets with enhanced OER activity.12 Yu's group reported that CoSe2/Mn3O4 and CoSe2/CeO2 hybrids display significantly improved OER performances compared to pristine CoSe2.11,13 Zhang's group achieved bifunctional overall water splitting by anchoring CoO domains on CoSe2 nanobelts.14 Cui's group constructed an efficient and stable HER electrocatalyst by growing CoSe2 nanoparticles (NPs) on carbon fibre paper.15 A Co0.85Se/graphene hybrid was also used as a highly efficient catalyst for ORR.16 Although the cobalt selenides mentioned above show remarkable electrocatalytic behavior in a separate OER, or HER, or ORR, they can scarcely function well toward all OER, HER and ORR in the same electrolyte because of their unstable or inactive nature in an unfavorable pH environment.14,17 Therefore, constructing cobalt selenides with integrated performance for clean energy systems such as overall water splitting and rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries is still a challenging work.

Recently, a new family of metalâ€“organic framework (MOF)-derived materials, including porous carbon and metal-based/(heteroatom-doped) carbon hybrid nanocomposites, have drawn fast-growing interest, and they have been widely used in electrocatalysis, electrochemical energy storage, and other fields.2,3,18â€“21 The MOFs as both the precursor and template can afford abundant channels to facilitate electron/ion transport or provide (heteroatom-doped) carbon species to combine with metal species by in situ carbonization to further increase the relevant properties (i.e. active sites, conductivity, and adsorptivity).22â€“24 Besides, the huge families of MOFs, which can be obtained easily by changing the metal species and organic ligands, also enrich these precursors and templates. On the other hand, novel and high-performance electrocatalysts like MoCx,2 especially Co-based groups such as Co3O4-carbon hybrid,3 Coâ€“C@Co9S8 double-shelled nanocages,25 and CoxFe1âˆ’xP,26 have been successfully obtained by using the corresponding MOFs. Nowadays, zeolitic imidazolate framework-67 (ZIF-67), a common type of MOFs, has been used as the precursor to prepare some functional materials, especially electrocatalysts like Co@Co3O4/NC,27 Coâ€“C@Co9S8,25 CoP,28,29 and ZIF-Co0.85Se.30 However, these materials suffer from a time-consuming, multi-step synthesis process, or cannot work well as trifunctional electrocatalysts in OER, HER, and ORR. Also, cobalt selenide derived from MOFs as an electrocatalyst has rarely been reported so far.

Materials with a metalâ€“nitrogenâ€“carbon (Meâ€“Nâ€“C) structure, i.e. metal cations coordinated with nitrogen functional groups provided by organic ligands or N-doped carbon, have been demonstrated to be high-performance electrocatalysts because of their excellent electroactivity and strong durability.5,31â€“36 Thus, extensive studies have been directed to those electrocatalysts with the Meâ€“Nâ€“C structure for OER, HER, and ORR. For instance, Coâ€“Nâ€“C species originating from a N-doped Co9S8/graphene hybrid31 and Co encapsulated in nitrogen-doped carbon nanotubes36 can boost both OER and ORR performances; NiO combined with Fe(Co)â€“Nâ€“C5 and Tiâ€“Nxâ€“C32 constructed by the interaction between the carbon nitride and the titanium carbide nanosheets can enhance the OER activity. However, these strategies all suffer from a multi-step, time-consuming preparation process, or a high-temperature thermal treatment. Therefore, the above cases inspire us to seek for an advanced yet simple method to obtain catalysts with the Meâ€“Nâ€“C structure for efficiently enhancing the electrocatalysis performance. Thus, a promising fabrication strategy is devised by using MOFs with a N-rich organic ligand followed by one-step carbonization to ensure that the resultant electrocatalysts have the Meâ€“Nâ€“C (e.g. Coâ€“Nâ€“C) structure and a controlled microstructure.

Herein, for the first time, we demonstrate in situ coupling of Co0.85Se nanocrystals and N-doped carbon (denoted as Co0.85Se@NC) via a one-step carbonizationâ€“selenylation procedure by using zeolitic imidazolate framework-67 (ZIF-67) as both the precursor and template. The resultant Co0.85Se@NC can be used as a trifunctional electrocatalyst for overall water splitting and Znâ€“air batteries. Importantly, the carbonization of ZIF-67 can not only enable a synergistic effect of the combination of N-doped carbon and Co0.85Se nanocrystals, but can also facilitate construction of the Coâ€“Nâ€“C structure to further enhance the multifunctional electrocatalysis performances. Besides, the in situ carbonization of ZIF-67 can also ensure a smaller size of Co0.85Se nanocrystals to increase the catalytic sites, thus resulting in high electrocatalytic activity. Additionally, the resultant Co0.85Se@NC also inherits the porous structure of the ZIF-67 template, which can increase the solid/liquid contact surface area and promote the efficient transfer of the electrolyte and the rapid diffusion of gas products. As a consequence, Co0.85Se@NC with the Coâ€“Nâ€“C structure, short diffusion pathways for electron/ion transport, and synergistic effect between the ultrafine Co0.85Se nanocrystals and NC exhibits excellent activity as a trifunctional electrocatalyst for overall water splitting and rechargeable Znâ€“air batteries.

The electrocatalytic OER activity of Co0.85Se@NC was first evaluated using the polarization linear sweep voltammograms (LSVs) recorded in O2-saturated 1.0 M KOH aqueous solution with a scan rate of 2 mV sâˆ’1 using a standard three-electrode configuration (see the Experimental section for details). For comparison, CoSe2@NC, Co@NC, Co0.85Se (Fig. S4â€ ), NC (Fig. S5â€ ), ZIF-67, Pt/C and IrO2/C were also tested under the same conditions. First, as shown in Fig. S3b,â€  the OER activity of Co0.85Se@NC is obviously better than those of Co@NC and CoSe2@NC. As we know, the Coâ€“Se bond can lead to the Co in Co0.85Se@NC with a high valence state,30,44 and this is able to boost the OER adsorption process, which can be further supported by its Tafel slope value (Fig. S3câ€ ) (75 vs. 101 mV decâˆ’1 for Co0.85Se@NC vs. Co@NC). In addition, Co0.85Se@NC has a higher Co/Se atomic ratio than CoSe2@NC, and this can enhance the adsorption process in the OER reaction as Co0.85Se@NC has a small Tafel slope value (Fig. S3câ€ ), which is determined by its inherent phase structure, commonly observed in other studies.44,45 Moreover, NC and ZIF-67 show negligible current densities below the potential of 1.60 V vs. reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE), while the pristine Co0.85Se presents the OER response with an onset potential at 1.59 V (Fig. 4a), indicating that the catalytic sites come mainly from Co0.85Se. In contrast, Co0.85Se@NC exhibits significantly enhanced OER catalytic activity. Specifically, Co0.85Se@NC reveals a sharp onset of OER current at approximately 1.49 V, indicating that the synergistic effect between Co0.85Se and NC is responsible for the excellent OER performance. Noticeably, although the IrO2/C affords an onset potential of approximately 1.47 V, slightly smaller than that of Co0.85Se@NC, its OER current densities drop obviously below those of Co0.85Se@NC at high biases, indicating the excellent OER catalytic activity of Co0.85Se@NC. Moreover, we further compared the operating potentials required for all the catalysts to deliver 10.0 mA cmâˆ’2 current density, the approximate current density expected for a 10% efficient solar water-splitting device under 1 sun illumination.46,47 The operating potential of Co0.85Se@NC at the current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 is 1.55 V, comparable to that of the state-of-the-art IrO2/C catalyst (1.55 V), but much lower than those of Co0.85Se (1.65 V), NC (1.72 V), ZIF-67 (1.76 V), Pt/C (1.74 V), CoSe2@NC (1.64 V), Co@NC (1.69 V), and many other reported catalysts, for example, IrO2/C (1.60 V, 0.1 M KOH),48 Pt/C (1.83 V, 0.1 M KOH),49 CoSe2 ultrathin nanosheets (1.55 V, 0.1 M KOH),12 CoSe2/N-graphene (1.596 V, 0.1 M KOH),50 Mn3O4/CoSe2 hybrids (1.68 V, 0.1 M KOH),11 CoS2/N,S-GO (1.61 V, 0.1 M KOH),51 and TCCN (1.65 V, 0.1 M KOH).32 A detailed comparison of various OER catalysts reported recently is presented in Table S1,â€  further confirming the outstanding catalytic activity of Co0.85Se@NC. The OER kinetics of the above catalysts were further examined by Tafel plots, as shown in Fig. 4b. The Tafel slopes are found to be 75, 88, 90, 97, 94, and 73 mV decâˆ’1 for Co0.85Se@NC, Co0.85Se, NC, ZIF-67, Pt/C, and IrO2/C, respectively. To obtain further insights into the reaction mechanism, we used the rotating ringâ€“disk electrode (RRDE) technique with a Pt ring electrode potential of 1.50 V to oxidize the peroxide intermediates formed on the Co0.85Se@NC surface during the OER process. As shown in Fig. 4c, it can be clearly seen that a fairly low ring current of 6 Î¼A is detected and this value is 3 orders of magnitude lower than that of the disk current (mA scale), which is so small that it can be neglected. So it can be reasonably concluded that Co0.85Se@NC favors a desirable four-electron pathway for water oxidation (4OHâˆ’ â†’ O2 + 2H2O + 4eâˆ’). Moreover, in order to prove that the observed anodic current results from the OER process rather than other side reactions and also to further measure the faradaic efficiency, an RRDE with a ring electrode potential of 0.40 V (a value to ensure that the ORR can take place) in N2-saturated medium was employed to reduce the generated O2 from the OER process (Fig. 4d), thus guaranteeing a continuous OER (disk electrode) to ORR (ring electrode) process (the inset of Fig. 4d).3,44 When the disk current was fixed at 200 Î¼A, a constant ring current of about 39 Î¼A (collection efficiency 0.2) can be detected, indicating that the anodic current catalyzed by Co0.85Se@NC is attributed to the OER process with a high faradaic efficiency of 97.5%.10,51,52

Another critical criterion for evaluating the OER performance of the electrocatalysts is the long-term durability, which is significant for energy conversion and storage systems. As shown in Fig. 4e, the chronoamperometric response curve for Co0.85Se@NC at a low potential of 1.55 V exhibits an anodic current attenuation as small as 6.6% within 12 h, demonstrating high stability. This insignificant activity decrease of Co0.85Se@NC may be caused by a small amount of the Co0.85Se@NC catalyst peeling off the electrode during the evolution of a large amount of O2 gas for the long process.45 In contrast, Co0.85Se displays not only 3.1 times larger current attenuation (20.7%) than Co0.85Se@NC, but also 100 mV larger potential to afford the same current density response within 10 h (Fig. S7â€ ), indicating the synergistic effect between Co0.85Se and NC. The TEM image of the Co0.85Se@NC electrocatalyst after a 12 h reaction shows a good polyhedron structure, almost completely same as that of the fresh Co0.85Se@NC (Fig. S8aâ€ ). Besides, the HRTEM image gives the interplanar distance of the lattice fringes to be 0.26 nm, which corresponds to the (101) planes for Co0.85Se (Fig. S8bâ€ ), suggesting the fact that the Co0.85Se phase does not change during the OER process. All these findings indicate the superior stability and excellent OER catalytic activity of Co0.85Se@NC. Furthermore, from the chronopotentiometric curve tested at the current density of 10.0 mA cmâˆ’2 (the inset in Fig. 4e), we can see that Co0.85Se@NC has a nearly constant operating potential at 1.55 V with a trend of continuous potential decrease corresponding to its current increase in the iâ€“t curve in the first 3 h, which is owing to the diffusion of the electrolyte into the electrode, while the potential of Co0.85Se increases about 13 mV from 1.650 to 1.663 V as a striking contrast within 5000 s, again revealing the superior durability of Co0.85Se@NC. Additionally, accelerated degradation studies are carried out by taking continuous cyclic voltammograms (CVs) at an accelerated sweep rate of 50 mV sâˆ’1 for 1000 cycles to probe the stability of the catalyst; only 2.6% anodic current loss (4 mV increased) is observed for Co0.85Se@NC, further confirming its highly stable performance (Fig. 4f).

The outstanding OER performance of Co0.85Se@NC originates from the synergistic effect between ultrafine Co0.85Se nanocrystals and highly conductive N-doped carbon, the Coâ€“Nâ€“C structure constructed by the interaction between the high N content and Co0.85Se species, and unique porous architecture. Firstly, the synergistic effect between the highly conductive N-doped carbon and ultrafine Co0.85Se plays an important role in enhancing the OER performance of Co0.85Se@NC. As discussed above, the pre-edge of the Co K-edge XANES spectrum of Co0.85Se@NC (Fig. 2a) has shifted towards higher energy compared to Co0.85Se due to the negative charge transfer from Co to carbon,41,42 indicating a strong coupling effect between Co0.85Se nanocrystals and N-doped carbon species. The in situ incorporated N-doped carbon causes impaired electron density of Co atoms, which can make the electrocatalytically active sites of Co more electrophilic,42,53 thus facilitating the adsorption and reaction of OHâˆ’ groups with Co0.85Se@NC,54 resulting in outstanding OER performance. Besides, the uniform anchoring of Co0.85Se in situ on the N-doped carbon, assured by the use of ZIF-67 as the precursor, can greatly improve the conductivity and charge transfer capability of the Co0.85Se catalyst. The strong coupling effect between them can take full advantage of the superior electric conductivity of the N-doped carbon backbone, which can facilitate the charge transfer in the hybrid catalyst, thus resulting in enhanced OER activity and stability. For instance, compared to the physically mixed Co0.85Se and NC, and carbon-free Co0.85Se, Co0.85Se@NC has a smaller onset potential with continually enhanced current density, suggesting its much higher OER activity (Fig. 5a). Moreover, Co0.85Se@NC has a smaller semicircular diameter in electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) than Co0.85Se (Fig. 5b), revealing its smaller contact impedance and faster charge transport along with favorable reaction kinetics. The higher OER electrocatalytic activity and smaller charge transfer impedance of Co0.85Se@NC both benefit from the strong coupling between Co0.85Se and N-doped carbon. Additionally, the N-doped carbon matrix might also play an important role in prohibiting the growth of Co0.85Se nanocrystals, thus increasing the catalytic sites and stabilizing the polyhedral structure of Co0.85Se@NC to contribute to the microstructure effect such as the porous structure.

Secondly, as high as 5.26 wt% N content of Co0.85Se@NC can be achieved, as determined by CHN elemental analysis. Such a high N content can not only directly act as OER active sites but can also facilitate incorporation of metals (e.g. Co species) forming a Coâ€“Nâ€“C structure (as proved by XPS and XANES) to boost the OER performance. Recently, Qiao32 reported that Tiâ€“Nx motifs constructed by the interaction between carbon nitride and titanium carbide nanosheets can act as OER electroactive sites. Dai31 reported that N-doping into both Co9S8 and graphene could efficiently enhance the electrocatalytic performance for ORR and OER by tuning the electronic properties of Co9S8 and graphene. Others also assumed that the metalâ€“Nâ€“C structure can boost oxygen electrochemistry performance. Based on these facts, we conjecture that the Coâ€“Nâ€“C structure in our case also contributes to increasing the OER performance.

Thirdly, the unique porous architecture of Co0.85Se@NC, on the one hand, can provide abundant and short diffusion pathways for electron/ion transport during the electrochemical reactions, and on the other hand, can afford a large active surface area for increasing catalytic sites. Noticeably, for the OER polarization curves of Co0.85Se@NC, the current density is not susceptible to the scan rate increasing from 2 to 15 mV sâˆ’1, while Co0.85Se acts as a striking contrast (Fig. 5c). This result indicates that the high porosity of Co0.85Se@NC favors the efficient transfer of the electrolyte and the rapid diffusion of the O2 product. Furthermore, the mesoporous Co0.85Se@NC polyhedra afford a large active surface area, which has been confirmed by the electrochemical double-layer capacitance (Cdl) and the calculated slope from the linear relationship of the current density against the scan rate (Fig. S9â€ ). Co0.85Se@NC shows a larger Cdl than Co0.85Se (3.2 vs. 2.4 mF cmâˆ’2), indicating an enlarged catalytic solidâ€“liquid contact surface area for Co0.85Se@NC (Fig. 5d). Thus, the rich channel and large solidâ€“liquid contact surface area of Co0.85Se@NC provided by its unique porous architecture can obviously increase its OER electrocatalytic performance.

The opposite reaction initiated on the oxygen evolution electrode that can catalyze the corresponding cathode hydrogen evolution process as well is an important criterion to pursue the fabrication of a bifunctional water electrolysis electrode and simplify the installation. Hence, the catalytic capacity of Co0.85Se@NC for HER is assessed in the same electrolyte as OER (1.0 M KOH). As shown in Fig. 6a, Co0.85Se@NC has a small onset potential of around âˆ’140 mV at the current density of 2 mA cmâˆ’2 (Fig. S10â€ ), beyond which the cathodic current increases rapidly at higher biases, indicating outstanding electrocatalytic activity. The overpotential at the current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 for Co0.85Se@NC is 230 mV, which is far smaller than those of Co0.85Se (424 mV), NC (442 mV), ZIF-67 (599 mV), and some other reported non-noble metal HER electrocatalysts. Co0.85Se@NC also possesses favorable HER reaction kinetics with a small Tafel value of 125 mV decâˆ’1, which is comparable to that of commercial Pt/C (104 mV decâˆ’1), but much smaller than those of Co0.85Se (197 mV decâˆ’1), NC (224 mV decâˆ’1), and ZIF-67 (135 mV decâˆ’1) (Fig. 6b). This result suggests that Co0.85Se@NC has favorable HER reaction kinetics corresponding to its high catalytic activity. As expected, Co0.85Se@NC displays excellent stability (tested by the chronoamperometric model) in basic solution within 10 h only with a minor current drop at the beginning of the HER measurements, which may be due to the loss of the electrocatalyst from the electrode on rapid rotation (Fig. 6c). Furthermore, the polarization curves before and after operating for 10 h in basic medium were also tested (the inset in Fig. 6c), which show similar robust durability, indicating the super stability of Co0.85Se@NC. We also studied the relationship between the HER current density and the scan rate. As shown in Fig. 6d, the HER current density for Co0.85Se@NC is not susceptible to the scan rate increasing from 2 to 20 mV sâˆ’1. So, the unique porous architecture of Co0.85Se@NC may contribute to the enhanced HER performance. Besides, the synergistic effect between the highly conductive N-doped carbon and ultrafine Co0.85Se also contributes to the enhanced HER activity by decreasing the charge transfer impedance (as evidenced by the EIS test in the inset of Fig. 6d). Encouraged by the excellent OER and HER performances, we accordingly assembled a water electrolyzer with a two-electrode configuration in 1.0 M KOH, in which the as-synthesized Co0.85Se@NC was coated on Ni foam as both the anode and the cathode (the inset in Fig. 6e). As shown in Fig. 6e, a catalytic current is detected when the applied potential is larger than 1.42 V, and the current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 can be achieved at a cell voltage of 1.76 V, representing a combined overpotential of 530 mV for full water splitting. The overall water splitting performance of Co0.85Se@NC also outperforms those of commercial Pt/C and the Ni foam substrate. In addition, the stability test indicates that Co0.85Se@NC has high stability with good rate performance as the current density increases from 5 to 10, and 20 mA cmâˆ’2 during the 35 h electrocatalysis process (Fig. 6f). Co0.85Se@NC exhibits low overpotentials and superior stability, which demonstrates its promising practical application for overall water splitting.

Interestingly, apart from the excellent overall water splitting performance, Co0.85Se@NC also shows remarkable ORR activity, making it a promising application for metalâ€“air batteries. Specifically, in cyclic voltammograms (CVs, Fig. 7a), one characteristic ORR peak centered at 0.817 V is observed in 0.1 M O2-saturated KOH solution, showing the electrochemical reduction of oxygen initiated on Co0.85Se@NC, while, in sharp contrast, no significant peak is obtained in N2-saturated solution. This potential (0.817 V) is close to that of the commercial Pt/C catalyst, but far more positive in comparison to those for Co0.85Se (0.617 V), NC (0.73 V), ZIF-67 (0.732 V) (Fig. S11â€ ), and previously reported Co-based ORR catalysts (e.g. âˆ’0.3 V vs. Ag/AgCl for Co0.85Se/graphene hybrid nanosheets,16 0.704 V vs. RHE for Nâ€“Co9S8/G,31 and âˆ’0.21 V vs. SCE for Co3O4/ONâ€“CNW55), indicating an easier ORR process on Co0.85Se@NC. Accordingly, Co0.85Se@NC displays a sharp onset potential at 0.912 V in the polarization LSV curves (Fig. 7b), and its limiting current density increases with increasing the rotation speed (e.g. from 400 to 2025 rpm) because of the increased diffusion rate at high speeds.56 The corresponding Koutechyâ€“Levich (Kâ€“L) plots of Co0.85Se@NC are obtained from LSVs with potentials ranging from 0.272 to 0.572 V vs. RHE (Fig. 7c), and all plots show fairly good linearity, suggesting first-order reaction kinetics toward dissolved oxygen and similar electron-transfer number (n) for the ORR process at different potentials. The n is calculated to be in the range of 3.98 to 4.03 at the potentials ranging from 0.272 to 0.572 V (Fig. 7d), indicating that the Co0.85Se@NC electrode favors a four-electron oxygen reduction process. Thus, Co0.85Se@NC favors a desirable four-electron pathway for reversible OER and ORR processes (4OHâˆ’ â†” O2 + 2H2O + 4eâˆ’), which is of great significance, especially for rechargeable metalâ€“air batteries and regenerated fuel cells involving these two reactions.

Moreover, we also evaluated the ORR performance of Co0.85Se@NC, Co0.85Se, NC, ZIF-67, and commercial Pt/C catalyst using RDE technology at the rotating speed of 1600 rpm in 0.1 M O2-saturated KOH (Fig. 7e). Remarkably, Co0.85Se@NC displays significant ORR performance, which is not only better than Co0.85Se, NC, and ZIF-67 in both the onset potential and the reaction current density, but also comparable to the commercial Pt/C, revealing the high ORR activity of Co0.85Se@NC. As expected, Co0.85Se@NC also shows strong durability as revealed by the chronoamperometric response (Fig. 7f), retaining 85.2% of the initial current even after 10 h, while the Pt/C catalyst loses 28% of its initial current. The high activity and good durability of Co0.85Se@NC may be due to the Coâ€“Nâ€“C structure, the porous structure and the synergistic effect between the highly conductive N-doped carbon and the ultrafine Co0.85Se nanocrystals. These excellent ORR features of Co0.85Se@NC, especially together with its outstanding OER performance, are particularly desirable for clean energy systems, such as rechargeable metalâ€“air batteries and alkaline fuel cells.

Next, proof-of-concept experiments were conducted to demonstrate the potential application of Co0.85Se@NC in rechargeable Znâ€“air batteries since OER and ORR are two fundamental steps in this application. A home-made rechargeable Znâ€“air battery device was constructed by pairing a zinc plate (the anode), the Co0.85Se@NC loaded on carbon fiber paper (the cathode), and O2-saturated KOH/Zn(Ac)2 electrolyte (Fig. 8a). The battery has an open circuit voltage of âˆ¼1.4 V and a small internal resistance (Fig. S12â€ ). The peak power density of Co0.85Se@NC (Fig. 8b) is as high as 268 mW cmâˆ’2 at 0.64 V, which is also superior to those of Co0.85Se (139 mW cmâˆ’2), Pt/C (173 mW cmâˆ’2) and some previously reported materials on Znâ€“air primary batteries (Table S3â€ ). Fig. 8c shows the charge and discharge polarization curves of the rechargeable Znâ€“air battery using the galvanodynamic method with Co0.85Se@NC coated on carbon fiber paper as both the ORR and OER electrodes. Conclusion

In summary, we have achieved in situ coupling of Co0.85Se nanocrystals and N-doped carbon via one-step selenization of metalâ€“organic frameworks. The resultant Co0.85Se@NC was used as a trifunctional electrocatalyst for overall water splitting and Znâ€“air batteries for the first time. When used as an electrode for overall water splitting in alkaline solution, Co0.85Se@NC shows a low cell voltage of 1.76 V at the current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 and good stability. At the same time the obtained Znâ€“air batteries based on Co0.85Se@NC exhibit a very low dischargeâ€“charge voltage gap (0.80 V at 10 mA cmâˆ’2) and long cycle life (up to 180 cycles). Its excellent electrocatalytic performance can be attributed to the synergistic effect of the coupling between Co0.85Se and NC, the Coâ€“Nâ€“C structure, and the porous conductive carbon network. Our work establishes a facile strategy for the preparation of the Co0.85Se@NC hybrid as an efficient and stable trifunctional electrocatalyst for overall water splitting and Znâ€“air batteries and this strategy can be extended to fabricate other nanomaterials for energy fields.
