Intelligent control of nanoparticle synthesis through machine learning

Abstract

The synthesis of nanoparticles is affected by many reaction conditions, and their properties are usually determined by factors such as their size, shape and surface chemistry. In order for the synthesized nanoparticles to have functions suitable for different fields (for example, optics, electronics, sensor applications and so on), precise control of their properties is essential. However, with the current technology of preparing nanoparticles on a microreactor, it is time-consuming and laborious to achieve precise synthesis. In order to improve the efficiency of synthesizing nanoparticles with the expected functionality, the application of machine learning-assisted synthesis is an intelligent choice. In this article, we mainly introduce the typical methods of preparing nanoparticles on microreactors, and explain the principles and procedures of machine learning, as well as the main ways of obtaining data sets. We have studied three types of representative nanoparticle preparation methods assisted by machine learning. Finally, the current problems in machine learning-assisted nanoparticle synthesis and future development prospects are discussed.

1. Introduction

With the deepening of the research progress of nanoparticles, researchers have found that nanoparticles play a vital role in many research fields, for example, the fields of electronics, sensing, chemical catalysis, physical components and medical treatment,1–4 and it also shows great potential in some emerging research directions. Nanoparticles refer to particles with a particle size between 1 and 100 nm (nanoparticles are also called ultrafine particles). They belong to the range of colloidal particle size.5,6 They are located in the transition zone between clusters of atoms and macroscopic objects, and are groups composed of a small number of atoms or molecules. There are many methods for preparing nanoparticles, and some of the traditional methods include vapor deposition, precipitation, and hydrothermal synthesis.7–9 With the gradual maturity of microfluidic technology, people have focused on the use of microfluidic technology to prepare nanoparticles.10,11 Compared with traditional methods, this technology has the advantages of less reagent loss, sufficient mixing reaction, and cheap preparation of microreactors. These advantages make the use of microfluidic technology to prepare nanoparticles have good development prospects.12,13

Artificial intelligence is a branch of computer science. Its main functions are making decisions and solving problem tasks. In many cases, it is also a multi-parameter fitting and scoring system. Artificial intelligence can simulate the information process of human consciousness and thinking. Machine learning is the core of artificial intelligence and it is one of the more cutting-edge research areas.14,15 It is the use of data or previous experience as a way to optimize the performance criteria of a computer program in order to acquire new knowledge or skills. And it can reorganize the existing knowledge structure to continuously improve its performance. Machine learning is a branch of artificial intelligence that uses a large number of known datasets to predict or respond to unknown data.16 The main purpose of machine learning is to use historical data and specific conditions to train the model to build complex dependencies between independent variables and dependent variables, so as to infer and predict new data.17 Machine learning was put forward for research as early as the 1950s. However, due to the immature technology development, it is far from being able to meet the needs of mankind. In recent years, the research and development of machine learning has suddenly exploded, which has brought new research prospects for some tedious experiments and unknown conditions. At the same time, it also brings hope of breakthrough to some areas with bottlenecks.

The properties of nanoparticles in electronics, optics and sensing are often directly affected by the size, shape and surface chemistry of the nanoparticles.18 For example, polystyrene nanoparticles with a size of 50 nm and 100 nm have different properties. And when their surface chemistry is different, they also change the properties of the biologically active protein in the corona to varying degrees. The nanoparticle size and polyethylene glycol graft density have a direct effect on the relative density of adsorbed proteins.19 Changes in serum protein adsorption are related to differences in the mechanism and the efficiency of the macrophage uptake of nanoparticles. Macrophages lead to the inefficient delivery of nanomaterials to diseased tissues. Therefore, it is a key challenge that the synthesis of nanoparticles can be precisely controlled.20 The current microfluidic control technology for preparing nanoparticles cannot accurately synthesize the expected nanoparticle size and shape. In order to achieve the control of nanoparticle synthesis, it takes repeated experiments to estimate the approximate reaction conditions. This is not only time-consuming but also laborious. Therefore, it is imperative to combine machine learning to assist the synthesis of nanoparticles. Since machine learning has the advantage of being able to make predictions by building models, this will solve the challenge of efficient and accurate synthesis of nanoparticles with the desired properties.

In this paper, we review the methods and principles of nanoparticle preparation adopting microfluidics in recent years and describe the implementation steps, functions of machine learning and its applications in nanotechnology. We summarize the approaches and advantages of using machine learning to prepare nanoparticles in recent years. Finally, we provide an outlook on the use of transmission electron microscopy to acquire nanoparticle images online to identify the centers and contours of nanoparticles to determine their size, and the use of convolutional neural networks to characterize aspects of nanoparticle size and morphology.

2. Synthesis of nanoparticles by microfluidic technology

Microfluidics is the technology for controlling, manipulating and detecting complex fluids at microscopic dimensions. It is a new interdisciplinary discipline developed on the basis of microelectronics, micromechanics, bioengineering and nanotechnology.21 Microreactors typically contain small channel sizes (equivalent diameter less than 500 μm) and channel diversity. Fluids flow in these channels and are required to undergo the required reactions in these microchannels. This leads to a very large contact area and excellent reactions between different fluids in microconstructed chemical devices.22,23 Microreactors have excellent heat and mass transfer capabilities. They can achieve instantaneous homogeneous mixing of materials and efficient heat transfer, so many reactions that cannot be achieved in conventional reactors can be achieved in microreactors.24,25

Nanoparticles have become one of the most important frontier applications in the development of modern analytical chemistry. Due to their unique optical, electrical, thermal, chemical and mechanical properties, they have a wide range of applications in many fields of analytical chemistry such as chromatography, electrochemical analysis, spectroscopy, imaging analysis, vital analysis, pharmaceutical analysis, etc., and especially they play an important role in enhancing the sensitivity, selectivity and analytical speed of analytical methods.26–28

There are many methods used to prepare nanoparticles, including traditional process methods and emerging microfluidic technologies. Traditional processes for preparing nanoparticles are mainly classified into two types according to their nature: physical and chemical methods.29,30 The physical method involves the crushing or grinding of large material blocks into micron or nanoscale size particles by some mechanical devices. It also called mechanical crushing.31,32 Chemical methods are used to synthesize nanoparticles through the reaction or polymeric assembly between atoms and ions in reagents, which mainly include: solution-gel method, hydrothermal method, thermal decomposition method, high-temperature combustion synthesis method and vapor phase deposition method.33–35 However, the conventional method of preparing nanoparticles has some drawbacks. It takes a long time or even more than one hour, and the reaction temperature and the mixing of reagents are not well controlled. Microreactors, however, have the ability to control a wide range of reaction conditions. Moreover, its reaction time is short, mixing is sufficient and fast, and heat transfer is uniform. The reaction also requires less chemical reagents, thereby saving costs. Furthermore, microfluidic chips are highly integrated and can achieve adequate mixing of reagents before performing the reaction according to the reaction conditions.36–39

Nanoparticle preparation by microreactors can be divided into three main categories: continuous laminar flow, segmented flow, and droplet-based microreactors.40–42 The continuous laminar flow microreactor is currently the most common and it is relatively the simplest in design and operation.43,44 It works on the principle of continuous flow of several different fluids in full contact and reaction within microscale channels, followed by nanoparticle nucleation and growth (Fig. 1b). In recent years, it has been studied and reported by many research scientists for the synthesis of nanoparticles. In general, high-quality biochemical reactions require certain reaction conditions and adequate mixing to be achieved.45,46 In the preparation of Cu2O nanoparticles, two chemical reagents, Benedict's solution and glucose buffer, can be reacted using an AC electrothermal continuous laminar flow micromixer. Only a certain temperature can be reached to trigger the reaction conditions. Cu2O is a semiconductor nanomaterial, which has excellent absorption properties for visible light and can reach a theoretical energy conversion of up to 20%. The Cu2O particles are all cubic in shape, but the particle size is different at different voltages.47

A segmented flow microreactor is typically composed of multiple inlets (Fig. 1c). The inlets include a multiple liquid phase and a single gas phase input. Due to the presence of the gas input, bubbles are created during the fluid flow, thus blocking the continuous flow of the fluid. The reason for creating bubbles between continuously flowing fluids that restrict the fluid from reacting is the presence of surface tension between the gas and liquid phases.48–50 In order to control the range of the particle size distribution to be narrow, segmented flow methods are often employed in the synthesis of Au nanoparticles. In the microreactor for synthesizing Au nanoparticles, the HAuCl4 chemical reagent of NaBH4 is usually employed for the rapid generation of Au nuclei by the reduction reaction, followed by the growth of Au nuclei by agglomeration, and the growth of Au nuclei has an interactive connection with the fluid flow in the microchannel. It has been found that the particle size distribution of Au nanoparticles is influenced not only by axial diffusion in segmented flow but also by the fluid circulation-induced mixing within the microchannel.51

Droplet-based microreactors are composed of a continuous phase (oil) and several dispersed phase (chemical reagents) inlets (Fig. 1d). Due to the unmixable effect of the continuous phase, the dispersed phase is wrapped to produce droplets.52,53 The reagents inside the droplets allow for adequate mixing and reaction. The advantage of this method is that it allows for a relatively clean and healthy reaction environment, but it is necessary to control the two-phase flow by adjusting parameters such as surface tension, viscous effects, and capillary number so that the two-phase flow has conditions such that it does not stick to the walls of the microchannel thus avoiding problems such as diffusion of the fluid and droplet rupture.54,55 In experiments investigating the synthesis of nanoparticles from recombinant Escherichia coli cell solutions and metal ion solutions, the continuous phase inlet was fed with FC40 and the surfactant polyethylene glycol perfluoropolyether (PEG-PFPE). The dispersive phase inlet is fed with the reactants. Droplets were generated by means of co-axial flow focusing, which led to the synthesis of nanoparticles in the droplets.56

It has been found through previous studies that the reaction conditions for preparing nanoparticles (for example, reaction temperature, reaction time, concentration ratio of chemical reagents, reaction precursors, reaction ligands, type of solution reagents, channel structure of the microreactor and external stimuli) have a direct influence on the properties of nanoparticles such as size, shape and surface chemistry.57,58 In turn, these properties often have a controlling effect on the electronic, sensing and optical properties of nanoparticles as well as on some applications. However, the precise control of nanoparticle properties with the current microfluidics methods is still a key challenge. Machine learning is an intelligent algorithm with predictable functions. It can use a large amount of data to establish complex linkages for the desired nanoparticle properties and reaction conditions to achieve the accurate prediction of nanoparticle properties and applications.59,60

Machine learning is a powerful tool for the efficient and accurate development of nanoparticle synthesis solutions with anticipatory properties and the potential synthesis of novel nanoparticles with complex properties.

3. Machine learning

3.1. Algorithm

Machine learning prediction models are divided into linear regression and non-linear regression. The relationship between reaction conditions and nanoparticle properties in experiments for preparing nanoparticles is generally nonlinear. Machine learning algorithms can achieve precise control of the properties of the synthesized nanoparticles. Machine learning models include supervised learning, unsupervised learning, and reinforcement learning.61–63 Among them supervised learning is widely used in prediction tasks for nanoparticle synthesis, and both build models can represent the relationship between input conditions and output attributes.64 The dataset used to train the machine learning model is called the training set and the dataset used to verify the accuracy of the machine learning model is called the test set. Generally, they are in the ratio of 7:3.65 There are many kinds of predictable machine learning models being selected, but we have to match the selection by weighing between its predictive accuracy, computational cost and feasibility.66,67 The predictive power is usually affected by the amount of data in the test set, the choice of kernel function and the definition of loss function in the data set. The larger the dataset the more accurate the prediction of the machine learning model, but it may substantially increase the computational cost and lead to a long computation time. There is also a risk of overfitting due to too much data. Before making predictions, machine learning models need to be validated and evaluated, and the selection of machine learning model algorithms based on actual conditions also needs to have strong scientific insight.68–70

3.2. Supervised learning

The principle of supervised learning is actually an attempt to explore an unknown function where a linkage exists between the input variables and an unknown output target.71 It includes the following typical algorithms.
Support vector machine algorithm (SVM). SVM can be used for classification and regression, and in nanoparticle synthesis techniques we use support vector regression algorithms that can solve prediction problems. The essence of the regression problem is to be able to find a straight line or curve so that it fits our data points perfectly.72 The traditional linear regression algorithm solves the problem by minimizing the mean squared loss function corresponding to the predicted straight line to the data points. The SVM regression model, on the other hand, is different from the traditional regression algorithm. Its method to solve the fitting data is to first define a constant ε. For a certain point (xi, yi), if |yi − ω·ϕ(xi) − b| ≤ ε, there is no loss at all. If |yi − ω·ϕ(xi) − b| > ε, the loss is |yi − ω·ϕ(xi) − b| − ε (Fig. 2a). The SVM algorithm has a large number of kernel functions available, which makes it flexible to solve various nonlinear classification regression problems. More often, when the sample size is not massive data, the classification accuracy is high and the generalization ability is strong. However, SVM is computationally overloaded and less suitable for use when the sample size is very large and the kernel function mapping dimension is very high, and there is no general standard for the selection of kernel functions for nonlinear problems, making it difficult to choose a suitable kernel function.73 Khatibinia, Mohsen, et al. focused on the prediction of the compressive and flexural strength of self-pressurized mortars containing SiO2 nano, Fe2O3 nano, and CuO nano using a wavelet-based weighted least squares support vector machine approach. The complex relationship between the input and output in the database is constructed by the support vector machine thus the properties of the nanofluid can be predicted. Similarly, we can use the above wavelet-based weighted least squares support vector machine method for predicting the properties of the synthesized nanoparticles.74

K-nearest neighbor algorithm (KNN). When using the KNN algorithm to solve the prediction problem, we first have to choose the k value, which is generally a smaller value based on the size of the data set, and then select a more appropriate final value by cross-validation. The decision method in regression problems generally uses the mean method or the weighted mean method (Fig. 2b). The weighted mean method is calculated by using weights and distances inversely proportional to each other.75 Among all the machine learning algorithms we have encountered, KNN is the easiest to get started with. It has the advantages of simple model, good results without much tuning and fast when the data set is small. But in fact it is very limited in solving prediction problems. It is prone to generalization, over-fitting, and under-fitting. In addition, it is not suitable for datasets with many features when the dataset is large, and it is slow and ineffective. Chau et al. examined the potential of using quantitative nanostructure–activity relationship models to predict the properties of nanoparticles prior to synthesis. In this study, it was shown that if k = 7 and 5 of its nearest neighbors showed good cellular uptake while 2 showed poor cellular uptake, the nanoparticle would therefore be predicted to have good cellular uptake. The model developed using the K-nearest neighbor algorithm was used to predict the uptake of 105 single metal core nanoparticles by pancreatic cancer cells. The above prediction model using the K-nearest neighbor algorithm can be investigated for application to synthetic nanoparticles in order to predict their properties.76
Decision tree (DT). The calculation principle of the DT algorithm is to determine the segmentation variable according to the data set, and then consider the segmentation point. In this way, the loss function value of each segmentation point is calculated and the optimal segmentation point is found. Then two segments before and after the optimal segmentation point, namely D1 and D2, are found. And then while continuing the above operation steps, the optimal cut point is selected. Finally, the regression tree is generated (Fig. 2c). It has the advantage of basically no preprocessing, no advance normalization, and handling missing values.77 However, it is very prone to overfitting, resulting in poor generalization, and it is difficult to learn for some of the more complex relations. Trinh et al. used in vitro characteristics as input parameters (for example, cell line, source, type, and so on). Decision trees and example-based algorithms were used to manage and analyze the data on metal nanoparticles (AuNPs and AgNPs) to predict cytotoxicity. We can use the decision tree prediction principle from the above work to apply it to predict various aspects of nanoparticle properties.78
Artificial neural network (ANN). An ANN is composed of an input layer, a neuron layer, and an output layer. The neuron layer includes multiple artificial neurons. Artificial neurons can be implemented to construct a nonlinear relationship between the input conditions and the predicted properties, which can determine an unknown nonlinear function. In order to achieve this relationship there may be a particular degree of complexity, it can be solved by stacking multiple neurons and adjusting the weights and activation functions. This will complete the computation through the combination of multiple nonlinear functions.79 The neurons can be flexibly arranged and connected to each other, thus constituting a neural network (Fig. 2d).

ANN has powerful computational power. It can approximate arbitrarily complex nonlinear relationships very well. All quantitative or qualitative information is equipotentially distributed and stored in each neuron within the network, so it has strong robustness and fault tolerance. This powerful predictive capability further proves that it is more robust for application to assist in the preparation of nanoparticles.80,81 Youshia et al. determined the effect of molecular weight, hydrophobicity and surface activity of polymers on the particle size by measuring the viscosity, contact angle and interfacial tension of polymers. An artificial neural network was used to develop a model with the particle size and polydispersity index as the input and the particle size and polydispersity index as the output. Finally, the effectiveness of the artificial neural network in predicting the particle size and polydispersity of polymer nanoparticles was confirmed. This method of constructing artificial neural network models for predicting the nanoparticle particle size is fully applicable for us to study in microfluidics. This combines the advantages of microfluidics with the application of neural network models to predict nanoparticles, thus making the neural network-assisted synthesis of nanoparticles technology more efficient and the device more integrated.82

3.3. Unsupervised learning

Unsupervised learning is concerned with finding similar classes in unlabeled data, for instance, clustering in a dataset. The most typical example in unsupervised learning is clustering. The purpose of clustering is to group similar things together (Fig. 2e). Clustering algorithms work based on the calculation of similarity.83 The main algorithms included in the typical partitioning clustering are: the K-means algorithm, the K-medoids algorithm, and the CLARANS algorithm.84 Even though unsupervised learning is a remarkable technique for class partitioning of similarity in datasets, the technique is very narrowly involved in the problem of preparing nanoparticles. We do not need to cluster the properties of nanoparticles based on similarity, what is important is the problem of prediction of their properties.

Unsupervised algorithms rely mainly on visibility to process images.85 For the application of nanoparticles on unsupervised learning dabble we can have a scientific outlook on it. The lack of technology for this area is something we should consider to compensate. Bi, Xiangyu, et al.86 used the K-means clustering algorithm for the first time in conjunction with the signal processing of raw SPICP-MS data. Compared with currently used data processing methods, the K-means algorithm improves the distinction between particle signals and background signals. It provides a sophisticated statistical-based method for quantitatively solving the different large groups contained in nanoparticle suspensions. Finally, the function of classifying nanoparticle suspensions according to their large groups is achieved.

3.4. Reinforcement learning

Reinforcement learning deals with the problem by finding the best or better action among many plans to satisfy the maximization of the reward. In a nutshell, its essence is learning in interaction to achieve optimization. Reinforcement learning builds a convenient work system for intelligent work that can intelligently learn to plan the optimal work path thus achieving the defined goals.87

On the one hand, strategies of genetic algorithms as well as heuristic and Bayesian optimization algorithms88,89 can be used to optimize the factors influencing the process of response experiments. By defining the fitness function and thus optimizing the fitness factor, the experiment is optimized with increasing number of iterations of the population through a scheme similar to the genetic evolution strategy (Fig. 2f). On the other hand, the global optimum can be found by defining an objective function, which may be a loss function, and then selecting some independent variables affecting the objective (for example, reaction time, reaction temperature, ligand concentration ratio and so on) and thus optimizing them90 (Fig. 2f). What's more, stable branching and fitting noise optimization algorithms can also be used to establish the three-dimensional spatial relationship between the reaction conditions and the properties of the nanoparticle objective, so that the global optimal value can be searched for.91

3.5. Working steps of machine learning

Dataset preparation. The dataset in machine learning models generally consists of input variables and output properties. Moreover, it is usually related to the reaction conditions and properties of the nanoparticles. The output properties are generally determined by the input variables. Preparation of the dataset is the first step of the machine learning model. The quality of the prepared data directly affects the superiority of the predictive function of the machine learning model. The more the dataset satisfies the normal distribution, the easier it is to be trained by the machine learning model. However, in the real data, the datasets we get often suffer from missing data, noise and errors, so the collected data can be de-duplicated, normalized, and error corrected, and subjected to other operations.92 Machine learning models are greedy for data, and larger datasets represent more powerful computational prediction capabilities. The final dataset is generally saved as a database file or a file in csv format.
Analysis of independent and dependent variables. Before the algorithm is chosen, we have to determine the independent variables (x1…xn) and the dependent variable (y1…yn). Then it is necessary to find out the correlation between the dependent and independent variables. In nanoparticle preparation techniques the independent variables are generally selected as reaction conditions (for example, reaction temperature, reaction time, concentration ratio of chemical reagents, reaction precursors, reaction ligands, type of solution reagents, channel structure of the microreactor, external stimuli and so on) while the dependent variables are generally selected as the size, shape, and electronic and optical properties of the nanoparticles.
Feature selection. After preparing the dataset for analysis, we need to filter the data and meaningful features are a worthy choice. It is crucial to select good features to seat the input of machine learning models for model training. In general, we choose features from two aspects. Splitting the data set. The dataset is generally divided into two components, namely the training set and the test set. The part used to train the machine learning model is the bulk of the dataset. We are careful in algorithm selection to avoid generalization, overfitting and underfitting. Machine learning models have excellent performance not only on the training set, but also have excellent predictive power for new data. This effect is called machine learning with better generalization.96,97 The reasons for the poor predictive power of machine learning algorithms also include overfitting and under fitting. 4.1. Au and Ag nanoparticles

The preparation of metal nanoparticles is mainly obtained by reducing metal salt precursors using reducing agents. This section covers the application of machine learning approaches in the synthesis of Au and Ag nanoparticles via reduction. There is a significant relationship between the size and morphology of metal nanoparticles and their optical properties, but this relationship is not visible from the crystalline matter of the nanoparticles. The size and morphology relationship of plasma can be found by resonance frequencies.110,111 Metal nanoparticles are the main substance for many emerging fields of research. The size of nanoparticles has a direct impact on optical and other properties, and being able to control their size is a skill we should have to master. Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) is one of the most important properties of metal nanoparticles. The wavelength and intensity of SPR depend on the shape and size of the nanoparticles. Metal nanoparticles are mainly used in sensing, solar cells, photothermal therapy, and so on. So the ability to precisely control the size of metal nanoparticles can lead to nanoparticles with specific applications.3

The reaction rate can affect the Au nanoparticle size distribution.112 For example, important parameters for Au nanoparticle synthesis include the switching frequency of the microfluidic inlet syringe pump, which can regulate the reaction rate of the metal chemical reagents and reducing agents. The reaction time is generally inversely related to the diameter of the nanoparticles. Tetrachloroauric acid (HAuCl4) and sodium citrate (C6H5Na3O7) are used as the reaction system for the reduction synthesis of Au nanoparticles as reactants. The particle number of Au nanoparticles can be detected by scanning electron microscopy. The switching frequency of the micropump can control the reaction rate of the fluid. When the frequencies are 50 Hz, 100 Hz and 200 Hz, respectively, the particle size of the synthesized Au nanoparticles can be found to be different by scanning electron microscopy113 (Fig. 4a). In order to precisely control the particle size, we can build machine learning models to construct the relationship between them. SVM algorithms or ANN are often chosen as prediction models. 4.2. Quantum dot

Nanoparticles with dimensions close to or smaller than the Bohr exciton radius (2–10 nm) and with continuous energy bands confined to discrete energy levels (quantum confinement effect) are called quantum dots.120,121 Generally speaking, quantum dots are synthesized by thermal injection and heating methods. Thermal injection means that the reagent is rapidly injected into a hot solvent containing a surfactant. However, the heating method refers to an intermittent process that is accomplished by heating the reaction mixture until it reaches the temperature of crystallization. In recent years, the development of research breakthroughs in quantum dots in the fields of optical sensing, optics and light wave emission has led to more attention to their performance. In particular, aspects such as solar cell optimization and laser threshold optimization are still mainly influenced by improving nanoparticle monodispersity.122–126 We can use machine learning to train experimentally collected datasets and construct neural network structures for the reaction parameter space with synthesized monodisperse PbS quantum dots.127 This strategy allows PbS quantum dots to have well-defined exciton absorption peaks. The results show exciton absorption peaks of 55 meV and 24 meV at nanoparticle particle sizes of 950 nm and 1500 nm, respectively.

Because there is always some error in the prediction made by neural networks (Fig. 7a), a new strategy is considered that is less affected by local noise and provides a broader range distribution of the effect of each factor. In order to optimize the neural network model, the prediction points can be searched more precisely. A Bayesian algorithm optimization model is proposed. Once the neural network predicts the optimal position in the dataset for new data, the Bayesian algorithm samples around the optimal position until the closest actual data is accurately searched for. The scatter distribution of the training and test sets in the neural network for various combinations of influences (Fig. 7b and c) is reported. This method does not require a large dataset to be collected experimentally, and requires only a small number of combined factors to make accurate predictions for a specific label. This would be a meaningful improvement, substantially reducing the time needed for the number of trials. Most of the activation functions of neurons in neural networks are nonlinearly related, so that they can better simulate real-world problems. Nonlinear regression has potential for application in quantum dot synthesis.

5. Conclusion and outlook

In this paper, we mainly review the methods used in recent years to prepare nanoparticles using microfluidic techniques, and mainly describe the working principle of machine learning, as well as review the progress of work on nanoparticle preparation assisted by machine learning. The research on machine learning in aiding nanoparticle synthesis can be summarized in two main areas:

(1) Supervised learning in machine learning predicts the specific optical, electronic and sensing properties of nanoparticles after synthesis. In general, we can take reaction conditions (for example, reaction temperature, reaction time, concentration ratio of chemical reagents, reaction precursors, reaction ligands, type of solution reagents, channel structure of the microreactor, external stimuli and so on) as input variables and nanoparticle properties as output targets (for example, nanoparticle size, morphology, surface chemistry and so on). Through the selection of different machine learning algorithms to construct the mapping relationships between them, we are able to precisely control the nanoparticles with the desired functions. Currently, there are still challenges in controlling nanoparticle synthesis on microreactors. However, the use of machine learning to assist nanoparticle synthesis will bring new breakthroughs and advances.

(2) In the neural network models for the prediction of nanoparticles, hyperparameters such as the number of neurons and the number of hidden layers have a direct impact on the predictive power. On the one hand, Bayesian optimization of hyperparameters can be performed by defining loss functions to make the prediction model more authoritative. On the other hand, “unsatisfactory coefficients” can be defined according to the experimental conditions. Finally the control algorithm can be optimized iteratively until the optimization is satisfactory and the target emission wavelength is reached.

(3) The principle of fitting functions with mathematical modeling is least squares, which is a mathematical optimization technique. It finds the best function match for the data by minimizing the squared error. However, the fitting accuracy of this method is not high, and the time cost of fitting is huge for large data sets. Compared to traditional modeling, machine learning-assisted preparation of nanoparticles takes much less time to train the data, and the accuracy can be improved by optimizing the model. Among the various algorithms of machine learning, artificial neural networks are the most frequently applied compared to other algorithms. It can approximate arbitrarily complex nonlinear relationships very well. All quantitative or qualitative information is equally distributed among the neurons within the network, so it has strong robustness and fault tolerance. This powerful predictive capability further proves that it is more robust for application to assist in the preparation of nanoparticles.

However, with the current technology, even machine learning-assisted synthesis of nanoparticles generally has some problems. On the one hand, it is difficult to achieve precise synthesis control. The method of characterizing nanoparticle size by collecting the emission spectra of nanoparticles through an online spectrometer introduces some errors. For example, emission spectra with similar shapes and wavelengths may correspond to nanoparticles with different particle sizes.140–142 This also represents a possible bias in the nanoparticle size predicted by neural networks trained on large datasets. On the other hand, the issue of how to address the large time and effort investment required for the machine learning-assisted synthesis of nanoparticles needs to be explored and addressed in depth.

To address the error problem mentioned above, devices such as Fourier transform ultraviolet spectrometers and infrared spectrometers are used to characterize the size of nanoparticles are worth being considered. They can be combined with online spectrometers to characterize nanoparticles, and thus the data generated for training neural networks crossover should greatly improve the accuracy of their predictions.143 Secondly, dynamic light scattering is one of the promising methods for estimating optical dimensions in a manner commensurate with fast measurement timescales. Therefore, the dynamic light scattering approach is used to estimate the particle size of nanoparticles which would make high throughput valuable. Moreover, to solve the problem of being able to accurately measure the size of nanoparticles, transmission electron microscopy is used to acquire nanoparticle images online,144 and then the method of processing the images by computer to identify the centers and contours of nanoparticles to determine their size is also expected. Convolutional neural networks145–147 can process, identify, and segment images very well. If convolutional neural networks can be used to measure the size of nanoparticles, this will also provide advances for the effective and accurate control of machine learning-assisted preparation of nanoparticles.

In the research of machine learning-assisted synthesis of nanoparticles, the problem of reusing machine learning to assist in the construction of feature attribute models when training different kinds of nanoparticles is faced. This would again require a significant amount of time to usher in the training model. To solve the above commonly faced time cost problem, migration learning can be used to help train new models by migrating the trained model parameters to the new model. This can substantially reduce the time cost of training the model. Since most of the data or tasks are related, migration learning allows us to somehow share the learned model parameters to the new model, thus accelerating and optimizing the learning efficiency of the model without the need to learn it from scratch as most networks do. Finally, the current immaturity of the machine learning-assisted preparation of nanoparticle technology means that many challenges remain, but this does not hinder its novelty and great research potential.
