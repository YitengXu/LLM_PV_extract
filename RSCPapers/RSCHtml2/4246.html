Dual-mode infrared laser-excited synergistic effect in NaGdF4:Er3+ nano-glass ceramics: a kinetic model

Abstract

Realization of the absorption and conversion of wide band infrared light have been a challenge in the field of upconversion luminescence. Herein, a facile physical approach is reported to realize the cooperative absorption and conversion of dual-band infrared light by NaGdF4:Er3+ nano-glass ceramics by employing a dual-mode excitation source (980 nm + 1545 nm). A synergistic effect of infrared photons induced by dual-wavelength infrared excitation is observed. The dual-mode excited red emission intensity is 2.76 times the total red emission intensities from 980 nm and 1545 nm single excitation. This upconversion synergistic effect can be modulated by adjusting the single excitation power, and it is proved to originate from ground and excited state absorption, in which the Er3+ ions in metastable states excited by 980 nm (or 1545 nm) photons are excited again by the 1545 nm (or 980 nm) infrared photons. A rate equation model is established to simulate the dynamic process in the dual-mode infrared upconversion process. The synergistic effect provides us with a way to convert two low-energy infrared photons into middle-energy visible photons to enhance the upconversion efficiency of rare earth ion doped glass ceramics.

1. Introduction

In recent years, the scope of investigation of rare earth ion doped materials has been extended to the absorption and conversion of infrared photons for potential applications in solar cells, infrared-pumped visible lasers, color displays, optoelectronics, photocatalysis, and bioimaging,17 since upconversion materials can convert lower-energy photons into higher-energy ones. The response wavelength of amorphous silicon ranges from 350 nm to 750 nm, which means that most of the infrared energy (over 25%) is lost.810 Current research on photocatalysis has been focused on converting light from the ultraviolet and visible regions into longer-wavelength emission.1113 However, few researchers have addressed the problem of how to manufacture luminescent materials that can simultaneously absorb and convert infrared photons with different frequencies. It is necessary to develop a phosphor that shows an optimized broad and efficient absorption band in the infrared region.

As reported, for the absorption of infrared light, the Yb3+ ion mainly absorbs 960-1100 nm infrared light since it consists of only two levels and has only one electronic excited state, 2F5/2, that is located in the 980 nm near infrared region.14,15 The Er3+ ion has a 4f11 electronic configuration and dense energy levels located over a wide wavelength range from ultraviolet to infrared, which is suitable to absorb ultraviolet, visible and infrared luminescence.16 For example, the energy gap between the ground level 4I15/2 and the first excited level 4I13/2 is about 6400 cm1, which matches well with the absorption of about 1.54 m photons.17,18 The energy gap between 4I15/2 and 4I11/2 matches well with the energy of 980 nm photons. Thus, the Er3+ ion is a good candidate to achieve the cooperative absorption of 980 nm and 1545 nm infrared photons. It was reported that the upconversion process relies on the excitation power of infrared light.19 After the absorption of infrared light at 980 and 1545 nm by the 4I11/2 and 4I13/2 states of Er3+ ion, respectively, a competition upconversion process will occur under dual-mode excitation light due to the different absorption cross-sections of the different energy levels.20 Er3+ ions in the 4I13/2 long-lifetime state from the nonradiative relaxation process in the 980 nm upconversion route will be excited again by the 1545 nm infrared photons in the upconversion route. The infrared photonphoton interactions will occur by manipulating the order and the excitation power of the single infrared photon. The study of this competition process has been reported rarely,6,7 and it will provide a method to realize the upconversion of dual-mode infrared light.

Hexagonal NaGdF4 was reported to be another efficient upconversion material with low phonon energy, as a compound isostructural with -NaYF4.2126 The synergistic effect of infrared photons of 980 nm and 1545 nm will be achieved in Er3+ doped NaGdF4. Glass ceramics containing fluoride nanocrystals can protect the fluoride luminescent centres from oxidization at high temperature, and also from non-radiative decay caused by some surface defects.23,24 Thus, in this work, a NaGdF4:Er3+ nano-glass ceramic is synthesized to study the properties of upconversion luminescence and the modulation mechanism of fluorescence intensity under single 980 nm, single 1545 nm and dual-mode (980 nm + 1545 nm) excitation power. The photon synergistic enhancement effect was pleasantly found. The competition mechanism of dual-mode infrared light is analyzed by simulating the process of luminescence kinetics with a rate equation model.

2. Experimental

2.1 Fabrication of samples

All starting materials are SiO2 (AR), Na2O (AR), Al2O3 (AR), AlF3 (AR), NaF (AR), Gd2O3 (99.99%) and Er2O3 (99.99%). All the chemicals were used as received without any further purification. The nominal mass composition of a glass sample was 70.1SiO24.3Al2O31.8AlF32.3Na2O18.5NaF2.7Gd2O30.3Er2O3. 20 g of the analytical grade raw materials was well mixed and melted at 1550 C for 45 minutes in a furnace. Then, the melt was poured onto cold stainless steel to obtain a transparent glass. The glass was heat-treated at 700 C for 2 h. Then, the obtained Er3+ doped glass samples were polished for optical measurements.

2.2 Measurements and characterization

The structures of the samples were investigated by X-ray diffraction (XRD) using an X'TRA (Switzerland ARL) instrument provided with a Cu tube with K radiation at 1.54056 . The size and shape of the samples were observed by using a JEM-2100 transmission electron microscope (JEOL Ltd, Tokyo, Japan). Luminescence spectra were recorded using an Acton SpectraPro Sp-2300 Spectrophotometer with a photomultiplier tube equipped with a dual-mode infrared laser of 980 nm and 1545 nm as the excitation source.

2.3 Optical setup

To investigate the dual-mode optical modulation of up-conversion fluorescence, we used a coaxial optical path coupling two laser beams with a pump combiner to excite samples in the sample chamber, as shown in Fig. 1. The fluorescence signal was collected through an optical lens and sent to the spectrograph and the data collector. All the measurements were performed at room temperature.

As shown in Fig. 10(a), when P980 is fixed, Er3+ in the ground state will absorb energy and transfer to a high energy level, leading to various degrees of population of Er3+ in various energy levels through radiation relaxation and non-radiation relaxation processes. When P1545 is adjusted step by step, Er3+ ions need to absorb energy from not only the ground state to the high energy level, but also the other energy levels that are populated owing to 980 nm excitation, as long as the energy difference between the energy levels is satisfied. Under low 1545 nm excitation, firstly, the few Er3+ ions in the ground 4I15/2 state can be excited by the three 1545 nm INR photons in the up-conversion route. The 4I11/2 state was proved to be the long-lifetime intermediate metastable state to store energy.30,31 Secondly, the Er3+ ion in the long-lifetime 4I11/2 state from the 980 nm excitation can be excited again to 4F9/2 and 2H11/2 by one and two 1545 nm photons, respectively, in the up-conversion route. Thirdly, the Er3+ ion in the 4I13/2 state from the nonradiative relaxation process in the 980 nm up-conversion route can be excited again by two 1545 nm photons in the up-conversion route and induce the 4I13/2 4I9/2 2H11/2 and 4I13/2 4I9/2,4I11/2 4F9/2 transitions. The energy consumption induced by nonradiative relaxation in the 980 nm excitation process is eliminated, achieving one plus one is greater than two results. The coexistence of the above three processes results in the upconversion process involving less than two photons on average to contribute green and red emissions under low 980 nm and 1545 nm excitation. When the 980 nm excitation power is increased, the ground state absorption will be enhanced. Thus, the two-photon up-conversion process induced by 980 nm excitation is enhanced, leading to the increase of slopes at high P980. On the other hand, the intermediate excited states 4I13/2 and 4I11/2 will be populated by more and more Er3+ ions with the increase of 1545 nm excitation power. Thus, the one-photon up-conversion processes 4I11/2 2H11/2 and 4I13/2 4F9/2 are also enhanced, leading to a decrease of the whole slopes at high P1545, as shown in Fig. 8.

4. Conclusions

In summary, we demonstrated an effective approach to achieve absorption and conversion of dual-mode infrared light at 980 nm and 1545 nm in NaGdF4:Er3+ nano-glass ceramic. The upconversion route can be modulated by controlling the 980 nm or 1545 nm infrared excitation power. The synergistic effect of 980 nm and 1545 nm dual-mode infrared upconversion is observed. Under dual-mode infrared excitation, a 172% absolute enhancement of the 661 nm red emission can be realized. The photon synergistic enhancement effect was the major dynamic evolution reason for this optical modulation of up-conversion fluorescence. A rate-equation model was developed to determine the relation between and 980 or 1545. This work offers a method to widen the absorption of the solar spectrum from the visibleinfrared waveband in photovoltaic conversion.
